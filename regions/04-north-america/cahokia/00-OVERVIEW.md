# Cahokia — The Sun People of the Great River

They did not leave a name for themselves in any surviving record. "Cahokia" is the name of an Illiniwek sub-group who lived near the site centuries after its abandonment. The city's own name, the people's own name — both are lost. What remains is the city itself: Cahokia Mounds, at the confluence of the Mississippi, Missouri, and Illinois rivers near modern St. Louis — the largest pre-contact urban center north of Mexico, a city at its height around 1050-1100 CE of perhaps 10,000-20,000 residents surrounded by a larger metropolitan zone of perhaps 50,000 people. Monks Mound, the principal platform mound at the city's center, is the largest earthen structure in the Americas: taller than the Great Pyramid of Giza, built of earth basket-carried to the site over generations by the people whose political and cosmological world it anchored.

The world they built was organized around the cosmic axis: the great mound as the mountain at the world's center, the Birdman — the falcon-warrior who was simultaneously a political and a sacred identity — as the person who stood at the world's top and held the sky and earth in right relationship. The Chunkey game (a disc-and-pole game played on the large central plaza) was not sport; it was a cosmological enactment that drew crowds from the entire metropolitan zone and from distant communities connected to Cahokia by the trade networks that brought copper from the Great Lakes, shells from the Gulf, and chert from Ozark quarries to the river city. The Birdman iconography — the warrior with falcon markings, clutching a severed head in one hand and a mace in the other — spread along these same trade routes to become the dominant sacred imagery of the entire Mississippian world that extended from the Mississippi delta to the Appalachian foothills.

Key places:
- **Monks Mound** — the central platform mound; the largest earthen structure in the Americas; the site of a large building (the chief's or priest's structure) on its summit
- **Grand Plaza** — the large open space at Monks Mound's base; the site of the Chunkey game and major public ceremonies
- **Woodhenge** — the series of large timber circles near the central precinct; solar calendar markers; aligned to the solstices and equinoxes
- **Mound 72** — a burial mound near the central complex; contains the famous "Beaded Burial" — a young man laid on twenty thousand shell beads arranged in the shape of a bird, surrounded by sacrificed women

## What Defined Them

**Monks Mound as the world's axis.** A platform mound is a claim: this elevated place is the world's center, the junction between the sky and the earth, the place where the person at the top mediates between the cosmic forces and the human community. Monks Mound's size — built over multiple construction episodes, each layer encasing the previous — was a measure of the commitment that the community invested in maintaining this claim. The building on the summit, visible for kilometers across the flat river plain, was the house of the person who stood at the world's center. That person's presence there was not symbolic. It was the practical, ongoing maintenance of the cosmic order.

**The Birdman — the falcon-warrior identity.** The Birdman figure — the warrior with the under-eye markings of a peregrine falcon, the falcon feathers on his arms, the severed head in one hand — was the defining sacred-political identity of the Cahokia world. It expressed a specific claim: that the person who held political authority also held the power of the falcon — the fastest, most precise killer in the sky — and that this power was cosmologically legitimate because the falcon was the bird that flew between the world of the living and the world of the dead, carrying messages. The Birdman was the political leader and the cosmic messenger simultaneously.

**The Chunkey game and the public cosmos.** The Chunkey stone — a carefully shaped disc of polished stone, rolled across the packed-earth plaza — was tracked by players who threw poles to land where the stone would stop. The game required skill, practice, and a deep knowledge of the disc's particular roll. But it was also a cosmological act: the rolling disc was the sun, and the game was an enactment of the sun's movement through the sky. The public ceremony of the Chunkey game, conducted before large crowds in the Grand Plaza, was the community's collective enactment of the solar order. The plaza was not a sports field. It was a stage for the maintenance of the cosmos.

**Shell and copper as the sacred materials.** The trade goods that flowed through Cahokia and along the Mississippian networks were not randomly chosen. Gulf Coast shell (particularly Busycon whelks and Marginella shells) carried the white purity of the spirit world; Great Lakes copper, hammered cold into geometric forms, carried the red vitality of the living world. Together, they expressed the fundamental duality of the Mississippian sacred order — the white path (peace, ancestral connection, the spirit world) and the red path (war, fertility, the living world). The objects made from these materials — the shell gorgets worn at the throat, the copper plates embossed with Birdman imagery — were the portable expressions of the cosmic order that Monks Mound expressed in earth.

**The city that vanished.** Cahokia's abandonment by 1400 CE is one of the most discussed questions in North American archaeology. The city that housed tens of thousands of people was empty within two centuries of its peak. The causes remain debated: agricultural soil exhaustion, flooding, deforestation, political fragmentation, epidemic disease, climate change (the Medieval Warm Period that may have enabled Cahokia's rise ended around 1200 CE), or some combination of all of these. What is clear is that the Mississippian tradition that Cahokia anchored did not end with the city's abandonment — it continued in the mound-building communities of the southeast (Moundville, Spiro, Etowah) for another century or more, and its cultural descendants were still building platform mounds when the Spanish arrived in the 1540s.

## The Chapters

**1 — The River Confluence Formation:** At the confluence of three rivers, a community is choosing where to build. The site has flooded before and will flood again. It is also where three continental drainage systems meet, which means it is where the trade networks of the Great Lakes, the Plains, and the Gulf converge. The mound they are beginning to build will be the largest structure north of the Rio Grande.

**2 — The Big Bang Horizon:** Something happens around 1050 CE that archaeologists call the "Cahokia Big Bang" — a rapid transformation of the site from a substantial town into something that looks planned, deliberate, and sudden. The Grand Plaza is created by scraping and filling several hectares of land to a level surface. Monks Mound's first major expansion phase is completed. Thousands of people arrive. What happened to make this transformation occur so rapidly is the central mystery of Cahokian history.

**3 — The Height and the Network:** A trader from a Mississippi delta community arrives at Cahokia carrying Busycon shells and gossip from the southern towns. He has made this journey four times. He will leave with copper and Cahokia-style pottery. The networks that connect the Gulf to the Great Lakes pass through this city, and the city knows it.

**4 — The Long Decline:** The stockade wall is going up around the central precinct. Something has changed in the city's political environment — the wall that was not needed at the peak is needed now. Population is declining. The Grand Plaza ceremonies are smaller. By 1400, the last residents will have moved, and the mounds will stand alone in the river bend for five hundred years before a French monastery is built on top of the largest one and gives it its permanent name.
