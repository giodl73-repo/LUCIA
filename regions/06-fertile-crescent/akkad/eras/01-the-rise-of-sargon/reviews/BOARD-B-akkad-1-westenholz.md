## B-akkad-1 — Joan Goodnick Westenholz — Board Review of The Rise of Sargon

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Critical editions of Sargonic royal inscriptions and literary texts; transmission history of the Sargon Legend and related compositions.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The Sargon Birth Legend is correctly handled as a literary tradition transmitted through later copies, not as biography. The phrase "whether the story is true or useful does not matter here" is an appropriate narratorial stance.
- The "thirty-four battles" claim is correctly attributed to the royal inscriptions and appropriately qualified ("whether it represents thirty-four distinct engagements or a literary convention for totality").
- The treatment of "weapons washed in the Lower Sea" as an inscriptional formula is correct.
- The "five thousand four hundred soldiers" is from the later Sargon Legend tradition, correctly attributed to "the later tradition."
- Enheduanna's dating is acknowledged as potentially straddling Sargon's reign and his successors'. This is honest. Her major compositions are generally dated to the reign of Naram-Sin, when she was removed from office and then restored, and the "Exaltation" may relate to that crisis rather than to Sargon's era. The chapter's placement of her in the context of the bilingual synthesis is defensible but should note more clearly that her literary career's peak may postdate Sargon.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The dual legitimation framework (Enlil's institutional grant + Ishtar's personal bond) is a valid reading of the inscriptional evidence. The emphasis on Ishtar's personal bond as non-transferable is an interpretive choice, not a scholarly consensus, but it is a productive one and does not contradict the evidence.
- The governor system is accurately described in broad strokes. The term "ensi" for Akkadian-appointed governors is correct for this period.
- The bilingual reality is correctly represented. The empire was indeed a bilingual operation from the beginning.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The handling of source uncertainty throughout. The chapter consistently distinguishes between what the inscriptions claim, what the literary tradition adds, and what the archaeological record confirms.
- The treatment of Akkade's unknown location as an honest gap rather than a romantic mystery.
- The Sumerian scribes' perspective in Section III: "the conqueror needed the script, and the script did not need the conqueror." This is a strong and accurate reading of the power dynamic.

**What needs correction:**
- The Enheduanna quotation ("Lady of all the me, Queen of all given powers, unveiled clear light, unfailing woman") is a paraphrase/translation that merges elements from different lines of "The Exaltation of Inanna." Consider noting that this is a translation rendering, not a direct quote, or standardizing against a published translation (e.g., Hallo and van Dijk 1968, or more recently Zgoll 1997).
- The claim that Sargon "pulled down" the walls of Unug is from one recension of the inscriptions. Other sources are less specific. Consider qualifying: "The inscriptions say he pulled down its walls" or similar.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (two minor corrections noted above)
