# Malla — The City as Temple

We are the Malla kings of the Kathmandu Valley, and our achievement is the valley itself: the three city-kingdoms of Kathmandu, Patan, and Bhaktapur, each ruled by its own Malla dynasty, each competing with the others not primarily through war but through architecture. The pagoda rose in our valley to its definitive form; the multi-tiered wooden temples that became the model for temple architecture across the Himalayan world were built under our patronage. When a Malla king built a temple in the central square of his city, he was not merely exercising piety; he was making a political claim about whose city was most beautifully realized, whose patron of the sacred arts was most generous, whose kingdom most fully expressed the ideal of the sacred polity.

The competition among the three Malla city-kingdoms produced the finest medieval art in the Himalayan world. The Nyatapola at Bhaktapur — five stories, rising on five diminishing platforms — is the tallest pagoda in Nepal and an argument in stone about what royal patronage can achieve. The Patan Durbar Square's concentration of temples and palaces in one urban space has no parallel. The Kathmandu Kumari tradition — the living goddess selected from among the Newari Shakya clan, who embodies the divine feminine for the valley's population — shows a religious culture of extraordinary complexity, one that absorbed Shaivism, Vaishnavism, Vajrayana Buddhism, and Newari indigenous traditions into a single ceremonial system.

## What Defined Them

**Three cities, one competition.** The political fragmentation of the Malla era into three kingdoms was culturally productive precisely because it was competitive. Each Malla king knew that his rival in the neighboring city was building a new temple, commissioning a new bronze, refining a new festival. The result was an urban environment of extraordinary density: every square meter of the Durbar Squares is filled with temples, pillars, shrines, and the records of competitive royal patronage. The architecture of competition produced more than the architecture of a single unchallenged center would have.

**The Newari artisanal tradition.** Beneath the Malla courts, the Newari craftsmen — organized into castes of painters, sculptors, carpenters, metalworkers — produced the works that made the valley famous. The Newar woodcarvers' designs — the struts, the torana arches, the peacock windows — traveled to Tibet and Bhutan with the architects the Malla kings occasionally sent to build temples for their neighbors. Arniko, a Newar architect sent to the Mongol court in the thirteenth century, built the White Pagoda in Beijing and influenced Chinese Buddhist architecture.

**Indra Jatra and the living festival cycle.** The Malla festivals — Indra Jatra, Bisket Jatra, Rato Machhindranath's chariot procession — were not celebrations separable from governance. The king's legitimacy was renewed each year by the Kumari's blessing during Indra Jatra. The chariot festivals were civic events at which the king appeared publicly to receive the sanction of the divine. The festival cycle was the Malla state's calendar, its theology, and its social binding simultaneously.

**Refuge from the north and south.** The Malla period coincided with the fall of Nalanda and the disruption of Buddhist institutions in India (early 13th century) and with the Mughal expansion in the south. The valley became a repository: Buddhist manuscripts and scholars fleeing the Mongol and Turco-Afghan destructions found refuge in the Malla monasteries. This gave the valley's Buddhist institutions an unexpected accumulation of texts and traditions from destroyed institutions elsewhere.

**The Kumari — the living goddess.** The tradition of the Kumari — a pre-pubescent girl from the Newari Shakya clan who serves as the living embodiment of the divine Taleju goddess — is a Malla-era institution that combines Shaiva, Shakta, and Vajrayana Buddhist traditions in a single ritual figure. The Kumari's blessing was essential to Malla royal legitimacy, and the tradition persisted through the Shah conquest and continues today. No external power that governed the valley found it possible or desirable to abolish the living goddess.

## The Chapters

**1 — Early Malla:** The first king to call himself "Malla" holds a festival in the Durbar Square. The title means "wrestler" — a reference to royal strength. The Durbar Square is beginning to fill with the monuments of successive kings.

**2 — Unified Kingdom:** The valley has one king. The trade routes to Tibet flow smoothly. The temples are being built faster than at any previous time. The competition, though not yet between three courts, is already with the past.

**3 — Three-Kingdom Period:** The king of Bhaktapur commissions the Nyatapola. Five platforms, five stages, the goddess at the top. His cousins in Kathmandu and Patan see the foundation being laid. Each begins planning what will answer it.

**4 — Prithvi Narayan Shah:** The Gorkha king's forces have cut off the valley's trade routes. The three Malla kings have failed to unite against him. The siege of Bhaktapur is the last. He enters Kathmandu on the night of Indra Jatra, 1768. The Malla king is watching the festival when the Gorkha forces arrive. The Malla era ends at a festival.
