# P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of The Great Hajj (Opening)

**Overall:** This opening is closer to right than most attempts at this story will ever get. The voice begins inside the Mande world and mostly stays there. But it slips — three or four times, quietly, in ways the author may not have noticed — and each slip reveals a writer who has not fully surrendered their vantage point.

**What works:**

- The opening sentence places the jeli first. Before Musa, before the gold, before Cairo — Bala Faseke Kouyate stands at the gate and sings. This is correct. In the Mande world, the event does not begin until the jeli begins it. The author understood this and built the structure around it. That is not a small thing.

- The genealogy passage — "He sang Sundiata, the Lion, the hungering lion, who crawled and then walked and then ran and then conquered" — carries the cadence of the jelilu's oral recitation. The repetition is not ornament; it is method. The rhythm of accumulation, name after name, is how a jeli works. The prose has absorbed that rhythm rather than merely described it.

- "A jeli does not choose which ancestors to remember. A jeli remembers them all." This is stated as self-evident truth, not as anthropological explanation. The author does not say "In Mande culture, griots are expected to..." — they simply let the principle stand as fact. Good.

- The logic of the gold is rendered from inside: "So the gold was not extravagance. The gold was the point." This is Musa's logic, the Kurukan Fuga's logic, not a modern defense of an ancient practice. The author does not apologize for the display. They do not call it "conspicuous consumption" or soften it for a reader who might find it excessive. They let the culture's own framework explain itself.

- The Kurukan Fuga is treated as operative law, not as charming tradition. "The Kurukan Fuga demanded generosity. A king who does not give is not a king." The covenant is a living political reality in this text, which is what it was.

- "Musa raised his hand, and the caravan began to move." Then the text stops and returns us to the morning. The restraint here is significant. A lesser telling would have followed the caravan to Cairo immediately. This telling understands that the departure is the scene.

**What doesn't:**

- "The sources agree on this." This sentence, referring to Musa's height, is a rupture. Who is speaking? Not Bala Faseke. Not anyone in Niani. This is a modern historian stepping into the frame to assure us that the claim is documented. It positions the reader outside the story, looking at sources rather than standing at the gate. A member of the Mande world does not need the sources to agree. The jeli said it; that is the source. If the author wants to convey that Musa was tall, they should convey it as the people at the gate saw it — through his presence, his bearing, how the crowd looked up at him. The parenthetical scholarly voice must go.

- "The Arab scholars who later recorded this could not agree on the number. Eight thousand, some said. Sixty thousand, others said. What they agreed on was that they had never seen anything like it." This is the most significant problem in the opening. The author has shifted the authority for this story from the Mande world to Arab observers. The jelilu carried this story for centuries. The numbers the jelilu sang are the numbers. But the text bypasses the Mande oral tradition entirely and hands the verification to external chroniclers — al-Umari, Ibn Khaldun, Ibn Battuta. The effect is subtle but unmistakable: the story is not fully real until an outsider confirms it. This is the precise mechanism I spent my career identifying. The culture's own testimony is treated as insufficient. The Arab sources matter to historians, yes. But this is not a historiography seminar. This is Musa's morning. The crowd at the gate knew what they saw. Let them be the witnesses.

- "Mapmakers in Majorca would draw Musa on a throne, holding a nugget of gold, and write beside it: the richest king in the world." Again the validation comes from outside. The story leaves Niani, leaves the Mande world, and travels to Majorca to tell us that Europeans were impressed. Why does this matter at the gate? It does not. The author includes it because the Catalan Atlas is the most famous image of Mansa Musa in the Western historical imagination, and the author is, in this moment, writing for a reader who shares that imagination. A member of the Mande world would not end their story of departure by noting that a Catalan cartographer was later impressed. They would end it at the gate, with the dust. The author almost does this — the final sentence returns to Niani — but the damage is done. The penultimate paragraph has told the reader: the real significance of this event is what it meant to the outside world.

- "The price of gold in Egypt would fall and not recover for twelve years, because one man had been generous in the way the Kurukan Fuga required, which is to say: completely." The clause "which is to say: completely" is the author's voice, not the culture's voice. It is a writerly flourish — clever, satisfying, and external. The Kurukan Fuga does not require generosity "completely" as an abstraction. It requires specific acts of giving in specific social contexts. The compression into an aphorism serves the prose but not the worldview. It makes the covenant sound like a philosophy rather than a law.

- "Five hundred men carried gold. Each bore a staff of the metal weighing six mithqals." The word "men" here is doing unexamined work. The Overview document notes that these were enslaved men. The opening neither names them as enslaved nor names them as anything else. They are simply "men" who carry. A text that inhabits the Mande worldview would understand these people within the social categories the Kurukan Fuga itself established — the jon, the distinct social station that the covenant regulated. By rendering them as anonymous "men," the text avoids both the Mande framework and the modern discomfort. It avoids, that is, the truth from any direction. This is the kind of evasion that happens when a writer is trying to be respectful rather than trying to be honest.

- "He was going because he was Muslim, and the hajj is the fifth pillar, and a mansa who can make the journey and does not has failed before God." The phrase "the fifth pillar" is a textbook gloss. Every Muslim knows this. Saying it in a text that is supposed to be inside Musa's world is like a novel set in Rome pausing to explain that Catholics take communion. It is written for a reader who may not know what the hajj is. That reader exists, but serving them here costs the voice its interiority.

**The question I'd push on:**

Whose witnesses does this story trust?

The opening is built on a tension the author may not see. The Mande world had its own historians — the jelilu — whose authority was institutional, hereditary, and sacred. The Arab scholars who recorded the hajj were outsiders writing for outside audiences. The Catalan mapmakers were further outside still. Yet when this opening reaches for proof that the event was extraordinary, it reaches outside. It reaches to al-Umari's numbers, to Cairo's gold markets, to Majorca's maps.

I would ask the author: if the jelilu are the true historians of this world — and you clearly believe they are, because you open with one — then why do you abandon their testimony the moment you need to establish scale? Why does Bala Faseke sing the names but the Arab chroniclers count the camels?

The fix is not to remove all external reference. The fix is to let the Mande world be the primary witness to its own event, and to let the external world appear only as it appeared to Musa — which is to say, as a place he was about to astonish, not as an authority he needed to validate him.

The final image — the dust that did not settle for hours — is the right ending. It is local, physical, witnessed by the people of Niani. Build the whole opening on witnesses like that, and the voice will hold.
