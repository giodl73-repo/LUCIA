# The Parima World
## Era 1: c. 1000–1750 CE

---

Aramo had been calling since midday, and the waterfall's hekura had not yet come.

He crouched at the edge of the shabono's open plaza, his back to the families settling in for the evening — fires being coaxed up, the smell of plantain soup thickening in the air, children chasing each other between the roof posts where the hammocks hung. The yakoana powder was still in his nose, the bright bitterness of the Virola resin that opened the way. He felt the familiar dissolution: the edges of his body going soft, his chest becoming a place where something else could live. He breathed and waited and breathed again, and then — a shimmer in the air just past the line of trees where the stream fell off the edge of the slope — the hekura of the waterfall was moving.

It came in small and quick, the size of a child's fist, luminous. Aramo had been learning the hekura since his teacher's teacher showed him how to build the internal landscape that they needed to settle in — a clean space, swept and tended, scented with the right plants. A shapori who neglected this upkeep would find the hekura restless, difficult to direct, prone to wandering away. Aramo had been diligent. His inner landscape was in order. The waterfall's hekura came in and settled.

He did not shout. He did not make theater of it. Behind him, an old woman looked up from her fire to note that he was kneeling in the plaza, and then looked back down. She knew what he was doing. Everyone knew what he was doing. The shapori's work was not a mystery to be observed from outside; it was a maintenance task, like mending a roof or smoking a fish — essential, regular, requiring skill, belonging to the community rather than to the man performing it.

The hekura of the waterfall was one of hundreds Aramo had learned to call. Each had its own song, its own appetite, its own relationship to the human ailments it could diagnose and the forest conditions it could read. He had spent two years eating almost nothing, the deliberate starvation of initiation, while his teacher sang the hekura into him one by one. He had been young then and the hunger had been a kind of burning. Now he was old enough to carry the weight without the burning, though the yakoana still opened the same door.

Tonight he was looking for something specific. One of the children of the family in the eastern section of the shabono had been feverish for three days. Aramo had already visited twice. He had called the hekura of the wind and the hekura of the forest spider, and they had shown him something persistent at the edges of the child's body — not a fever that would break but something holding on. The waterfall's hekura knew water-carried sickness. It was the one to ask.

The Parima highlands had their own weather. The streams that came off the slopes fed both the Orinoco moving north and the Amazon tributaries turning south; Aramo's people had been in this upland forest long enough that the distinction between the two river systems was a fact of geography the way the sun's path was a fact of geometry. The forest here was different from the lowland forest — cooler, denser, the tepui formations rising above the treeline in flat-topped columns that caught the morning clouds. The hekura of the tepuis were ancient and powerful and not easily called; they required experienced shapori working together, the calling shared between two or more, the weight of the ancient creatures distributed. Aramo had worked the tepui hekura alongside only two other shapori across his entire life, and one had died in the previous rainy season.

He sang quietly, the hekura song that addressed the waterfall spirits, and felt the creature in his chest respond with information. Water coming from somewhere outside the usual — the child had drunk from a stream farther upstream than the community usually went, during the recent hunt. A different spirit territory. A different negotiation would be needed. Aramo filed this away and began the process of sending the hekura back, carefully, the way you would lower a small flame into a lamp — you did not simply stop, you managed the transition.

Behind him, the old woman had finished her fire preparation and was watching the soup. Her granddaughter was trying to get a puppy to chase a palm frond. Her son-in-law had returned from the forest with two birds, and the quiet discussion of who would eat what was underway in the eastern section. The shabono's sounds were the sounds of an ordinary evening — the particular ordinary of the Parima world, the world that Aramo's shapori work was keeping in condition. The child's fever and the family's meals and the hunt and the forest's condition and the hekura who lived in it were not separate things, and the man kneeling in the plaza was not separate from the woman watching the soup and the birds coming in and the puppy with its frond. It was all one system, and Aramo was attending to the part of it that no one else in the shabono could reach.

He rose slowly, his knees aching in the way of a man who had spent many years on the ground. Tomorrow he would go to the family and tell them what the hekura had shown him. There was a spirit at the upstream water source that needed addressing. He would need to find a shapori from the neighboring community who knew that stretch of forest, whose hekura held jurisdiction there. A visit would need to be arranged. There would be food to share, conversations to have. The healing of one child would require the careful tending of an alliance.

The waterfall's hekura had gone. The soup was ready. The puppy had caught the palm frond and was pulling it to pieces in the dust.

Aramo walked back under the thatched roof and hung himself in his hammock, and the Parima world continued its evening.

## The House That Is the People

The shabono deteriorated on a cycle of several years. This was not a failure of construction but a feature of the design: the palm thatch and pole framework that made a good roof also made a temporary one, and the community that built it knew from the first beam that it would eventually need to build again. The decision about when to build, and where, was the community's most significant political act.

Every such decision was also a social renegotiation. Family sections within the shabono were not fixed by any authority higher than consensus and the willingness to occupy space. When a new building was planned, families might shift their positions relative to each other — a family that had been near the central plaza finding themselves at the periphery, an alliance newly cemented expressed as adjacent sections under the same section of roof. The new shabono was the community's current understanding of itself, rendered in wood and thatch and the arc of the communal structure's circular form. Walk the interior and you read the political situation. Space was not neutral.

The open plaza at the center held the life the shabono protected. Children played there in the afternoons. Visitors from other communities were received there. The shapori's work happened there, in view of everyone, because it was everyone's business. The plaza was the place where nothing was hidden — the community's arguments, its celebrations, its grief. The circular roof around it was the membrane that defined who was inside and who was not, but the membrane was permeable: there were no doors in a shabono, only gaps between sections where any member of the community could enter or leave.

The gaps were the point. Entry happened through invitation, through relationship, through the acknowledgment that passing through another family's section required the tacit permission of that family. A stranger who did not know this would blunder through without the proper acknowledgment and would be recognized immediately as someone who did not understand where they were. A community member who knew the shabono's social grammar moved through it with care, reading the physical space as a map of obligations.

When the shabono was finally abandoned — when the roof had decayed past the point of repair, when the garden plots around it had been used long enough that the soil needed rest — the community moved to a new site, usually within a distance manageable by foot. They cleared a new space, built a new structure, and re-constituted themselves in the new building's physical form. The old site decomposed back into forest. The people remained.

This cycle of building and moving was not instability. It was the Parima world's relationship to permanence: a community was permanent; a building was not. The Yanomami carried their continuity in themselves, in the relationships that held among families across dozens of communities, in the songs the shapori knew and the knowledge of which hekura lived where. When a shabono fell, nothing that mattered was lost.

## The Method of the Shapori

The yakoana took preparation. Aramo had been doing it for forty years, but he had not shortened the process. The Virola theiodora bark had to be stripped in the cool of the morning, when the sap ran closest to the surface. The inner bark was scraped and the resin collected carefully — enough for the work at hand, not more. Other materials went into the preparation: the ash of a specific tree bark, the seeds of a secondary plant, depending on what hekura were being called and what the occasion required. The mix was dried slowly, ground to fine powder. The grinding required attention. Powder that was too coarse would not carry the full opening. Too fine and the first inhalation was an assault, not an invitation.

A shapori he had known in his youth had taken shortcuts. He had dried the bark too quickly, over high heat, and the preparation had been adequate for ordinary work but insufficient for the demanding calls — the hekura of the mountain, the old ones who needed the full channel. Over the years, this shapori's range of communication had narrowed. The hekura he could reliably reach grew fewer. He was still useful; he was no longer great. The community he belonged to knew the difference.

When the yakoana opened the way, what came in first was the disorientation of passage: a brightness at the edges of vision, a physical sensation in the chest and throat, the body's ordinary sense of its own boundaries becoming provisional. But this was the beginning, not the destination. The shapori who stopped at the disorientation was a shapori who had not learned to move through it. The initiation's two years of starvation, the repeated callings, the sustained learning of the songs that invited specific hekura — all of it was training to move through the passage without stopping at the bright edges.

The songs were the key technology. Each hekura had a song that corresponded to it — not a description of the hekura, not a prayer addressed to it, but a kind of acoustic fingerprint that the hekura recognized as an invitation specific to it. Aramo carried hundreds of these songs, each learned from his teacher or from other shapori during alliance visits, each tested against the actual experience of whether the corresponding hekura arrived. A song that worked in one community's territory sometimes had to be adjusted for another's — the hekura of a waterfall in one watershed were not the hekura of the waterfall in the next. The knowledge was local. The songs were local. The shapori who had spent his whole life in one community knew the hekura of that community's forest with a depth that no visiting shapori could match, and was correspondingly ignorant of the next community's spirit landscape.

This localism was the system's great strength and its structural requirement for alliance. No community could be spiritually self-sufficient because no shapori could know all the hekura of all the relevant territories. When the child drank from the upstream stream, in the spirit jurisdiction of a different community's forest, the hekura there were creatures that Aramo's songs did not fully reach. He needed a shapori whose knowledge mapped that ground.

The hekura of the jaguar, the hekura of the harpy eagle, the hekura of the wind: these were among the most powerful, and the shapori who carried them were correspondingly respected. Aramo had learned the jaguar's song from a shapori who had died four seasons ago, and he still felt the weight of the learning — not pride exactly, more the feeling of carrying something that had been entrusted. The jaguar's hekura was useful for sickness that moved fast, that struck suddenly, that needed to be addressed with decisiveness. The harpy eagle's hekura was used for diagnosis at distance, for cases where the child or the patient could not be examined directly. The wind's hekura traveled through the canopy and returned with information about weather conditions and the movement of spirit forces across large areas.

The diagnostic process had a grammar. First, the general survey: calling the hekura whose jurisdictions covered the patient's history and the probable sources of disturbance. Then, the narrowing: following what the first hekura showed toward more specific causes. The hekura communicated through image and sensation more than through anything that translated easily into speech — Aramo experienced the waterfall's hekura's report as a visual impression of the upstream stream and a physical sensation in his left hand, which corresponded to a spirit disturbance in water approached from the east. He interpreted this through forty years of learning what those sensations meant in this forest. The interpretation was the art; the calling was the craft.

## The Spirit Landscape of the Parima

The tepuis rose above the canopy in formations that the forest could not soften. These flat-topped mountains, their vertical walls dropping hundreds of meters into the surrounding forest, were genuinely other worlds: the plant communities on their summits had evolved in isolation long enough to be unlike anything at forest level, the cloud forest that clung to their upper slopes different again, the exposed rock plateau at the top a third ecosystem entirely. Water fell off their edges in unbroken falls that could be heard from distances impossible to judge. The Parima highlands were full of them.

The hekura of the tepuis were old in a way that the forest hekura were not. Aramo understood this not as a fact he had been told but as a quality he had experienced in the one tepui hekura he had successfully called, years ago, with the guidance of the shapori who had since died. The tepui hekura moved differently in the chest — slower, heavier, with a quality of endurance that the smaller forest hekura did not have. The knowledge they carried was correspondingly older. When a tepui hekura showed the landscape, it showed a landscape of longer cycles: the flood histories of the rivers, the movements of animal populations across generations, patterns of plant distribution that had settled over centuries.

Marahuaka mountain, the highest in the Yanomami territory, was in some accounts the mountain from which certain plants and forces had first entered the world. The shapori who knew Marahuaka's hekura were the most knowledgeable about origins — the origins of cultivated plants, of certain diseases, of the rivers' courses. This knowledge was not always practically useful in the way that the waterfall's hekura was useful for a child's fever. It was differently useful: the knowledge of origins was the knowledge of the correct relationships between things, the understanding of why the world was organized as it was, which was prerequisite to knowing when something had gone out of alignment.

The xapiri — the word Kopenawa would later use for the hekura — were not simply healers. They were the ecology's memory. A shapori who had spent decades learning their landscape was a shapori who held a map of the forest's condition that no other technology could produce. When the game animals moved or disappeared from a territory, the hekura who had accompanied the animals were the first to register this absence. When a plant that had been abundant became scarce, the hekura associated with it showed the scarcity before the hunters and gatherers encountered it directly. The shapori's ecological knowledge and the shapori's spiritual knowledge were the same knowledge.

This understanding shaped the practical decisions of the community without ever being named as "environmental management" or any equivalent abstraction. When Aramo said, after a session of calling, that the community should not return to a particular area of forest for several months, the family groups listened not because Aramo had authority to command them but because the hekura he carried were understood to know what they were saying. The compliance was voluntary and the information was trusted because it had been reliable. The system worked because it was a reliable information system, and it remained so because shapori who gave unreliable information lost the community's trust and became less central to its decisions.

## The Feast and the Alliance

The neighboring community arrived in late morning, before the heat. Fifteen people, most of them family groups, with the community's shapori at their center — a man named Towaho, younger than Aramo, whose reputation for the wind's hekura was strong. They had walked two days from their own shabono, and the community that received them had prepared: plantain soup in large quantities, the garden's best smoked game, the children dressed in their best ornaments.

The reception was formal without being stiff. The visiting community was led to the center plaza, where the host community's headman offered food and the visiting headman received it with the acknowledgment that the offering was seen. This was not a perfunctory ceremony. The quantities mattered. The quality mattered. A host who offered too little, or food of poor quality, was offering an assessment of the relationship — that it was not worth full effort, that the guest community's presence did not require the host's best. The guest community filed this assessment. It would be remembered.

Towaho and Aramo had worked together before. The previous rainy season, when one of Aramo's people had developed a sickness that moved from the lungs into the blood — a sickness Aramo's hekura showed him as coming from a territory far to the east, outside any community's direct knowledge — Towaho had provided the hekura of the wind, which traveled farther than most and had returned with a pattern that helped Aramo identify the source. The patient had recovered, slowly, over months. The recovery was understood by both communities as a shared achievement, and this feast was in part the continuation of that acknowledgment.

The feast's logic was clear and its demands were not: the host community gave generously, the guest community received graciously, and the exchange created an obligation that would be discharged when the guest community hosted in turn. But "discharge" was not quite the right word for what happened, because the obligations did not cancel each other out. Each successful feast deepened the relationship rather than settling it. Communities that had been feasting with each other across many cycles were communities bound by a weight of mutual obligation that was a form of security — they would help each other in emergencies, provide marriage partners from each other's families, share shamanic knowledge, support each other in conflicts with third communities. The feast was not a transaction. It was a thickening of the thread.

When the thread thinned — when a community could not feast adequately, or refused an invitation, or failed to reciprocate over several cycles — the consequences were not immediate. But they accumulated. A community that became a poor feast-partner became an isolated one: fewer marriage options, less shamanic cooperation, less support in conflicts. Isolation in the Parima world was precarity. No community that lived without strong alliance ties could manage the spirit landscape without inter-community shamanic cooperation, could weather a major sickness without external support, could defend its territory against communities that had alliances the isolated one lacked.

The feast's second day was when Aramo and Towaho worked. The two shapori sat at the plaza's edge, the yakoana prepared, and the calling began in sequence. Aramo first called the waterfall's hekura and showed Towaho what it had revealed about the child's fever. Towaho listened in the way shapori listened to each other — not hearing words but attending to the quality of the hekura's presence, the accuracy of the calling, the information coming through. Then Towaho called the hekura whose territory included the upstream stream — an older hekura, one associated with the watershed above the usual hunting grounds, a creature that had jurisdiction over that particular stretch of water and the spirits that moved through it.

What the upstream hekura showed was a disturbance at a place where a tree had fallen into the stream several seasons ago, changing the water's course. The change had displaced a small water-spirit from its established location, and the displaced spirit was moving downstream and entering bodies that came in contact with the water there. Not malice; disruption. The treatment was a realignment: a ceremony at the site, songs addressed to the displaced spirit acknowledging the change and its right to a new establishment, and a specific plant preparation for the child that would help the displaced spirit recognize that this was not the territory it was seeking.

Towaho described this as naturally as a person describing a route through familiar country. Aramo understood it as a confirmation and an expansion of what the waterfall's hekura had suggested. Together, the two shapori's knowledge mapped the problem accurately. Separately, neither had the complete picture.

The child recovered over the following week. The two communities would feast again.

## The Ash of the Dead

When someone died in the Parima world, the body was cremated before the end of the day. The community gathered the bones from the fire and began the careful work: grinding the bones completely to ash, storing the ash in a decorated gourd in the family's section of the shabono. The ash was kept for months. The invitation to the reahu went out to the allied communities, and they came when the preparations were complete — the food accumulated, the space prepared, the family ready. Then the ash was mixed with plantain soup from the dead person's garden. The community drank.

This was the reahu — not only the feast of alliance but the feast of the dead, and the two were not separate practices. The dead became part of the community's body. The ash in the soup was not metaphor; it was the physical substance of the person who had lived among them, and drinking it was the completion of a relationship that death had interrupted but not ended. The dead did not leave. They were incorporated.

The shapori understood the reahu in cosmological terms: the hekura of the dead person remained in the world for a period, still connected to the community, and the reahu was the ceremony that acknowledged this continued presence while also providing the hekura with a proper transition. To incorporate the ancestor's substance into the bodies of those who loved them, to refuse the separation that death imposed by making it literal — the community drank the ash and the hekura settled.

The preparation was careful and slow. The bones had to be ground completely — fragments were not acceptable; the family took care with this over days. The gourd that held the ash was kept with the family through the months of preparation, present in the section of the shabono where the person had lived. Older family members wept and sang to it. The crying was not the same as an expression of loss so much as an address to the departed — a speaking to them that the months of preparation made possible, because the dead were still present as long as their ash remained with the community.

Communities that lived in alliance with each other attended each other's reahu. To be invited to a community's reahu was to be recognized as close kin — close enough to incorporate the dead person's substance into your own body. To not be invited was to be marked as distant. The feast and the ash were the same measure of relationship: how much did these communities share? Enough to eat from the same pot, enough to carry the dead in their bodies, or less than that?

The ash of the dead was the most intimate measure the Parima world had.

## What the Knowledge Held

The shapori's learning was not written down. It could not be. The hekura were not recorded in any medium outside the shapori's own body — the songs were held in memory, the inner landscape was maintained in practice, the knowledge of which hekura lived where was accumulated through calling and was stored nowhere except in the living tissue of the person who held it.

Knowledge held in bodies could be lost when bodies died. Aramo had thought about this. He had taken two apprentices, both younger men who showed the capacity — the patience, the tolerance for the hunger of initiation, the particular kind of attention that the hekura required. He had been teaching for twelve years. The first apprentice was now competent across much of Aramo's range; the second was still early in the learning. When Aramo died, which would happen eventually, much of what he knew would survive in these two. But not all of it. The songs specific to the tepui hekura that the dead shapori had taught him — there was only one other person in the community who carried those, and he was aging too.

The knowledge was always one generation from going silent. This was cause for attention. A community that recognized it tended its shapori carefully, supported the initiation process, valued the apprentices who were enduring the hunger. A community that did not recognize it found, over the generations, that its shamanic range narrowed — that fewer hekura could be reliably called, that the spirit landscape became less legible, that the community's ability to maintain its world diminished without any single moment of crisis marking the change.

Some generations produced a shapori of extraordinary range — men and women who called hekura that most shapori only heard described, who mapped territories of spirit knowledge their communities had not visited before. Some generations lost knowledge through a single death at the wrong moment: the songs specific to one territory, the method for calling one ancient hekura, going silent with the person who carried them. The practice grew and contracted and held its continuity through the chain of teacher and student, song to song, generation to generation.

The chain held. Across centuries, the Parima world maintained its capacity to negotiate with the hekura, to call the specific creatures whose knowledge mapped the specific forest, to keep the maintenance of the community's relationship with the spirit world ongoing and adequate. This was not inevitable. It required, every generation, the willingness of someone to undergo the initiation, to endure the hunger, to learn the songs, to take on the weight of the knowledge and the obligation it created. It required the community's support for this person. It required the alliance networks that allowed shapori to share knowledge across community boundaries. All of this had to be continuously re-chosen.

## The Evening Continues

The Parima highlands kept their own time. The rainy season came when it came; the dry season followed. The tepuis gathered their clouds on whatever schedule the clouds were keeping. The game moved through the forest on paths that the shapori's hekura tracked but did not control. The rivers rose and fell.

In the evenings, when the day's work was finished and the cooking fires were settling, the Parima world turned inward. Families in their sections of the shabono talked quietly, the children falling toward sleep in their hammocks, the adults reviewing the day's transactions. Which family had been generous with a catch, which had been less than they might have been. Which of the alliance visits coming up would require preparation, which could be managed with what the community already had. The headman moved through the sections, listening more than speaking, keeping the temperature of the community's internal relationships in his awareness the way Aramo kept the internal landscape of his body in his — daily maintenance, steady attention, the work of keeping something complicated functioning.

The shapori's work and the headman's work were both maintenance, and the maintenance was the civilization. There were no monuments in the Parima world, no stone structures that would survive the community by centuries, no written record of what had happened here. What survived was more fragile and more lasting: the songs the shapori carried, the relationships the feasts sustained, the children who grew up knowing which hekura lived in which part of the forest, the ash of the dead folded into the living bodies of the community.

The Parima world would continue this way for a long time. The highlands were remote; the lowland forests kept the outside world at distance. The communities turned in their cycles of building and moving, feasting and mourning, calling and healing.

The knowledge was alive because people were alive who held it. While they held it, and passed it on, and held it again, the world it described held together.

Aramo, in his hammock, slept. His apprentice, across the plaza in the younger men's section, was awake and listening to the forest's sounds, beginning, without quite knowing it yet, to learn which sounds meant something and which did not. The instruction was always ongoing. The hekura were always there, in the waterfall and the wind and the mountain and the root systems of the old trees, waiting for someone with the songs to call them in.

The soup had been good. The birds had fed the family. The puppy had fallen asleep in the dust with the palm frond still in its mouth.

The Parima world continued its evening, as it had, as it would.
