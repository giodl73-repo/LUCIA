# Board Review — Anthony Reid

**Chapter:** Chinese Overseas Era 1: The Tang-Song Nanyang
**Reviewer:** Anthony Reid (Southeast Asian context specialist)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Overall Assessment

The chapter is stronger on the Southeast Asian context than I expected. The Malay figures (Ismail, the pilot, the harbor master) are present as people with their own knowledge and agency, not as extras in a Chinese story. The local rulers' calculation (revenue vs. risk) is correctly characterized. But the chapter could go further in representing the Southeast Asian side of the relationship.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

1. **Ismail and the camphor trade.** The Malay camphor trader who knows which trees produce the finest grade, which upriver collectors to trust, and what the Chinese will pay is exactly the kind of figure the chapter needs. His knowledge is presented as equal in commercial sophistication to Lin's maritime knowledge. The chain of negotiation from forest canopy to ship deck correctly represents the multi-layered nature of the interior trade.

2. **The local ruler's conditional tolerance.** The chapter's formulation that "the Chinese presence was tolerated, not guaranteed" and that the permission to build in timber rather than stone was a deliberate political choice by the local ruler is historically accurate and structurally important. The Chinese quarter existed at the ruler's pleasure, and the chapter never forgets this.

3. **The Malay pilot.** The orang laut pilot who reads shoals by ripple patterns and whose knowledge of the strait exceeds Lin's is a small but important detail. It correctly establishes that the strait's maritime knowledge belongs to its own people, and that the Chinese navigated it with local help.

## What Needs Correction or Clarification

1. **Srivijaya's characterization.** The chapter describes Srivijaya's "grip on the strait" as having "loosened," which is a significant understatement of a complex and debated process. Srivijaya's decline was not a simple loosening; it involved the Chola raids of 1025, internal fragmentation, the rise of competing polities (Jambi-Melayu, Singhasari), and shifts in the maritime trade routes. The chapter should either give Srivijaya's decline a sentence of specificity or acknowledge that the process was complex and debated.

2. **The interior trade networks.** The chapter mentions upriver collectors and farming communities in the interior but does not give them any specificity. The forest product collection systems of interior Borneo and Sumatra were complex social arrangements with their own logic, their own intermediary figures, and their own relationship to the coastal trade. Even a single sentence about the interior collectors' own interests and agency would strengthen the chapter.

3. **The Malay market paragraph.** The paragraph about the two markets being "separate but not sealed" is good but brief. The interaction between Chinese and Malay commercial communities in the Nanyang ports was more extensive than the chapter suggests. Malay merchants were not only sellers of forest products; they were active participants in the maritime trade as well, with their own shipping networks connecting the Malay coast ports to the eastern Indonesian islands. The chapter's focus on the Chinese maritime network risks suggesting that the Nanyang's maritime commerce was primarily Chinese, which it was not in this period.

4. **The absence of Indian traders.** The Nanyang in the Tang-Song period also hosted significant Indian trading communities, particularly from Tamil Nadu (the Chola connection) and Gujarat. These communities interacted with both the Chinese and the Malay traders, and their absence from the chapter leaves the impression that the Nanyang's foreign trade was a Chinese-Malay affair. A brief mention of the Indian and Arab trading communities in the same ports would correct this.

## Recommendation

The chapter is respectful of the Southeast Asian context and avoids the common error of treating the Nanyang as a passive stage. The corrections above would strengthen the regional balance. **Approve with minor revisions.**
