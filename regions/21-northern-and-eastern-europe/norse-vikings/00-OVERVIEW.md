# Norse/Vikings — Overview

They called themselves Nordmenn, the people of the north. Or they called themselves by the name of their farm, their fjord, their jarl. The word vikingr meant something a man did, not something a man was. A farmer went i viking the way a farmer went to the summer pasture: it was a season, an activity, a choice made when the fjord ice broke and the wind turned fair and the ship was sound.

The world they built was not an empire. It was a web of routes, a network of ships and kin-ties and seasonal movements that stretched from the river systems of the Rus to the volcanic shores of Iceland, from the markets of Constantinople to the cod-drying rocks of the Lofoten. They measured wealth in arm-rings and ship-timber and the loyalty of men who had sailed with you and come home alive. They measured time by winters. They measured distance by days of sail.

## The Eras

### Era 1: Futhark and Fjord — The Early Norse World (c. 500-793 CE)
The centuries before the raids. Chieftains consolidating power in the fjord country. The ship technology evolving from coastal vessel to deep-water craft. Trade networks forming along the Baltic coast. The hall culture: feasting, oath-giving, the skald's recitation. Vendel-period wealth. The world that made the expansion possible.

### Era 2: Vikingalond — The Viking Age Opens (c. 793-850 CE)
Lindisfarne, 793. The raid that announced the Norse to the wider world. But from inside: farmers and shipbuilders expanding outward because the fjords were narrow, the younger sons were many, and the ships could go anywhere the water went. The first generation of raids and the logic behind them. Charlemagne's coastguard. The transformation from seasonal raiding to sustained presence abroad.

### Era 3: The Great Heathen Army and the Settlers (c. 850-954 CE)
The shift from raiding to conquest. The Great Heathen Army in England. The Danelaw. Norse settlement in Normandy, Ireland, Scotland. The founding of Dublin as a Norse city. Harald Fairhair and the unification of Norway. The thing system and the law.

### Era 4: The North Atlantic — Iceland, Greenland, Vinland (c. 870-1030 CE)
The settlement of Iceland. The Althing. The Greenland colonies. Leif Erikson and Vinland. The farthest reach of Norse navigation. A society that governed itself without a king, through assembly and law and the threat of outlawry.

### Era 5: The Christian Kings (c. 960-1100 CE)
Conversion. Harald Bluetooth's rune stone. Olaf Tryggvason. Olaf Haraldsson. The old gods do not vanish overnight. The stave churches rise where the groves were. The Scandinavian kingdoms take shape as Christian monarchies, and the Viking Age ends not with a defeat but with a transformation.

## Voice Note

The Norse left two kinds of record: the sagas (written down in the thirteenth century, from older oral tradition) and the runes (carved in stone, bone, wood, metal). Both are spare. The sagas do not explain feelings. They record what people did and what they said, and the reader must find the meaning in the space between action and speech. The runes are terser still: a name, a deed, a boast, a grief, compressed into stone.

The voice for these chapters belongs to the saga tradition. Short declarative sentences. Forward motion. Understatement where a lesser tradition would emote. The weight carried in what is not said. When a saga says "he took that badly," a man is about to die.
