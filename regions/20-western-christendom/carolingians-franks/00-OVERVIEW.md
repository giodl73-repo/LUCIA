# Franci — "The Free Men"

We inherited a world half in ruins and decided to rebuild it entire. The Roman roads still ran. The Latin still served as the tongue of law and learning. The Church held the fragments of a civilization together across the wreckage of the migrations — and we, the Franks, had conquered more of that wreckage than any other people. Charles — the one the chronicles would call Magnus, the Great — understood that conquest was not enough. He rebuilt the palace school at Aachen and gathered the best minds of the age: Alcuin of York, Theodulf the Visigoth, Paul the Deacon from the Lombards. He reformed the coinage. He standardized the script. He corrected the liturgy. He insisted that priests be able to read and write. None of these acts were simply practical. They were theological: to govern well was to participate in the divine order. A kingdom run on ignorance was not merely inefficient — it was a sin against the Creator who had placed Charles in authority.

We called ourselves Franci — "the free men" — a name that carried from our origins as a confederation of Germanic peoples west of the Rhine, unconquered by the later Roman Empire. But by the time of Charles the Great, we were no longer simply Germanic warriors. We were the custodians of a restored order that claimed to be both Roman and Christian — the empire that Charlemagne's coronation in Rome on Christmas Day 800 CE embodied in a single act of calculated theater and genuine religious conviction. Pope Leo III placed the crown on the king's head, but every witness understood that Charles had already made himself the most powerful man in the Latin West. The question the coronation opened — who held ultimate authority, pope or emperor? — would haunt every successor state that came after us.

Major centers:
- **Aachen (Aquisgranum)** — the palace complex and chapel; "the new Rome"; seat of the palace school
- **Reims** — the city of royal anointing; archbishopric that crowned Frankish kings
- **Tours** — Alcuin's scriptorium; the Carolingian minuscule developed here
- **Paderborn** — the frontier palace; meeting point with papal legates and Saxon hostages

## What Defined Them

**The reform as kingship.** Charlemagne understood governance as fundamentally educational. His *capitularies* — the royal directives sent to the missi dominici, the royal envoys who carried the emperor's will to the counties — are full of commands about literacy: every monastery must have a school; every bishop must be able to explain the creed; every priest must be able to read the text he chants. This was not humanist sentiment. It was a theory of administration: a kingdom administered by literate, doctrinally correct clergy would be predictable, uniform, and answerable to the emperor. The reform of the Church and the reform of the state were the same project.

**The palace school and the circulation of learning.** Alcuin of York brought the intellectual tradition of Northumbrian monasticism — the tradition of Bede — to the Frankish court. The palace school at Aachen was simultaneously a training ground for the royal children, a scriptorium that corrected and copied Latin texts, and an intellectual salon where questions of theology, grammar, and philosophy were debated as royal entertainment. The Carolingian minuscule script developed in this environment: cleaner, more legible, more portable than the scripts it replaced. Most of what we have of classical Latin literature survives because Carolingian monks copied it.

**The missi dominici — the king's eyes and ears.** The Carolingian empire had no permanent bureaucracy in the modern sense. It was administered through the personal loyalty of counts, bishops, and abbots, checked by the missi — pairs of a layman and a clergyman sent in circuits through the regions to hear complaints, correct abuses, and report to the emperor. This system required constant royal attention to work, which meant it began to fail as soon as the emperor's successors proved less capable than Charles. The empire was held together by personal authority, not institutional structure. It could not be inherited without degradation.

**The dream of renewed Rome.** The coronation of 800 was more than political theater. It was a statement about history: the Christian Roman Empire had not ended; it had only lapsed, and now Charles restored it. The Aachen palace chapel was modeled on the Byzantine church at Ravenna — the Franks knew exactly what Rome looked like and built it again in the north. This claim to Roman continuity shaped every subsequent Holy Roman Emperor and haunted every pope who had to decide whether to honor or undercut the claim. The dream of a unified Christian empire under a single emperor and a single pope was a Frankish legacy — and so was the permanent conflict between those two claims of authority.

**The vernacular begins.** The *Strasbourg Oaths* of 842, in which Charles the Bald and Louis the German swore loyalty in the other's language, are the earliest surviving documents in Old French and Old High German. The partition of the Carolingian empire did not merely divide territory — it divided language. The western kingdom became France not just politically but linguistically. The eastern became the German-speaking lands. The Carolingian project of a unified Latin Christendom began almost immediately to produce its own undoing: the peoples of the empire were becoming distinct peoples with distinct tongues.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Merovingian Foundation:** Clovis stands at the edge of a battle he is losing and makes a bargain with the Christian God of his wife Clotilde — if I win, I convert. The conversion of the Franks is not a moment of spiritual illumination. It is a military calculation that reshapes a continent.

**2 — The Great Correction:** Alcuin of York, summoned from Northumbria to Aachen, receives a manuscript so corrupted by generations of careless copying that the Latin has become nonsense. He begins to correct it, word by word, and realizes that what he is actually correcting is the empire itself.

**3 — The Coronation and Its Aftermath:** Christmas morning, 800. Charles leaves the church in Rome wearing an imperial crown he did not ask for — or so he will later claim. The question of who placed it on his head, and what that placement means, is the question that will divide Europe for six hundred years.

**4 — The Partition:** Three brothers divide their grandfather's empire at Verdun. The cartographers have no maps adequate to the task. The borderlands between the three kingdoms will be fought over for eleven centuries, and the lines drawn in 843 will still be legible in the wars of the twentieth century.

**5 — The Unraveling:** A count in the Seine valley, watching the Viking longships come upriver for the third time in a decade, stops waiting for the emperor's army and builds his own bridge and his own militia. The Carolingian empire does not fall in a battle. It dissolves into a thousand local decisions that the center can no longer prevent.
