# BOARD Review — African History Specialist (Gold Coast / Akan)
## The First Empire: Atlantic Commerce and Plantation

**Domain:** Akan history; Gold Coast political economy; Atlantic slave trade from the African coastal perspective  
**Review type:** Cultural accuracy and African-perspective representation

---

**General assessment:** The chapter navigates a difficult problem correctly. It is written from inside the British commercial-legal perspective, and it does not pretend otherwise. Within that constraint, the Fante section is accurate and appropriately presented — the Fante as commercial actors with their own institutions, not as passive suppliers. The chapter earns the confidence it extends.

**Specific factual and cultural notes:**

1. **Fante at Anomabo.** The chapter describes the Fante at Anomabo as running "a sophisticated commercial operation: they set prices, they maintained the barracoons... they negotiated terms. They required advance payment in goods." This is historically accurate and consistent with Robin Law's work on the slave coast and the broader historiography of African participation in the Atlantic trade. The Fante did maintain significant commercial leverage over European traders at the Gold Coast during the eighteenth century. VERIFIED.

2. **Akan-speaking people and Tacky's War.** The chapter identifies Tacky as "a Coromantee man" — "Coromantee" or "Coromanti" was the term British colonists used for enslaved people from the Gold Coast (specifically from the area near the Kormantin trading post). It is a British-derived exonym, not an Akan self-designation. The chapter's use of it is historically appropriate given that the colonial records use this term and the chapter is working from those records. The identification of Tacky as likely a Fante chief ("some accounts say a chief of the Fante") is one historical reading; other accounts identify him as Asante or more broadly Akan. The chapter correctly hedges: "some accounts say." APPROPRIATE HEDGE — VERIFIED.

3. **The Fante "concept of pawning and debt-slavery" vs. Atlantic chattel slavery.** The chapter notes "the distance between the Fante concept of pawning and debt-bondage and the Atlantic concept of chattel slavery was considerable, and the British trade systematically exploited that distance, purchasing people within one framework and holding them under another." This is accurate and represents the scholarly consensus on the ideological gap between African forms of dependent labor and the Atlantic chattel system. The use of "exploited" is the chapter's only near-evaluative word in this passage; it is defensible as descriptive rather than moral. VERIFIED.

4. **"The Fante knew this."** The chapter asserts that some Fante leaders "protested the conditions of the British trade and negotiated for protections that were not always honored." This is accurate — there are documented cases of Fante and other Gold Coast leaders raising objections to specific practices (notably, the enslavement of free persons who should have been redeemable, and the mistreatment of debtors-turned-captives). The chapter does not overstate Fante agency or portray them as abolitionists before abolitionism; it correctly places them as actors within a system with its own interests and constraints. VERIFIED.

5. **One concern:** The chapter refers to Tacky as having "his own knowledge of war" from the Gold Coast without specifying what that knowledge was. The historiography of the 1760 Jamaica uprising (Vincent Brown's *Tacky's Revolt*, 2020) identifies the specific Akan military traditions Tacky drew on, including the use of ritual preparation, the organization of fighters by cultural networks, and the strategic selection of the moment of attack. The chapter's reference to "military precision" is accurate, but the Akan origin of that precision could be more specific — even one sentence grounding Tacky's Gold Coast knowledge in its specific character would honor him more fully than "military precision" does.

**Recommendation:** APPROVED. One suggested addition: a single sentence in the Tacky's War passage grounding his military knowledge in its Akan-specific character (ritual preparation, cultural-network organization). This does not require a long explanation; even "the organization of fighters by the cultural networks that cut across estate boundaries, a form of mobilization the Akan knew from the Gold Coast" would suffice.
