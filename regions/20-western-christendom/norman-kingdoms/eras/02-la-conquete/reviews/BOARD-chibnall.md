## Overall Assessment

My domain is Orderic Vitalis, and Orderic is central to this chapter. The chapter uses him well: as a narrative source, as a characterization tool, and as himself a figure whose doubled identity embodies the Conquest's ambiguity. I have one required correction and several observations about how Orderic could be more fully present in the chapter without disrupting its structure.

## Orderic Vitalis — Accuracy

**The deathbed speech characterization is correct.** The chapter accurately describes Orderic's speech as a reconstruction in the penitential tradition rather than a transcript. This is exactly right. The speech in Book XIII of the *Historia Ecclesiastica* is modeled on classical and medieval conventions for deathbed speeches; Orderic probably drew on accounts from men who were present, but what he gives us is his own literary construction of what William would appropriately have said.

**The quote "massacred by slow famine" is correctly attributed.** It appears in the deathbed speech in Book VII (concerning the Harrying), not in the same passage as the death itself, but the attribution to Orderic is accurate.

**Correction — Required:**
The chapter states "Orderic put the dead at 100,000." Orderic does not give this number. He does not enumerate the dead. The figure 100,000 appears in Symeon of Durham, not Orderic, and even there it is an estimate of a different kind. This correction was also flagged by Bates; it is necessary and strengthens the passage.

**Orderic's father's origin:**
The chapter describes Orderic's father as "Norman." His father Odelerius was of Norman family but born in Orléans and had come to England in the service of a Norman lord. This is minor; for the chapter's purposes, "Norman father" is acceptable shorthand. I would suggest "a father of Norman descent, born in Orléans" if the author wants precision, but this is optional.

## Orderic's Presence in the Chapter

The chapter uses Orderic's words extensively but keeps him somewhat at a distance as a person. In the Harrying section, the chapter identifies him (monk at Saint-Évroul, born in England, Anglo-Norman parentage, heard survivors relate it) and then moves directly to what the survivors said. This is effective. But I want to flag that Orderic's own emotional register in the Harrying account is unusual for a Norman chronicler: he is not neutral. He is troubled. His phrase "I was careful not to be present" is not a logistical note; it is a moral statement. He is distancing himself from something he finds disturbing even as he records it.

The chapter could make this more explicit without breaking the voice: "Orderic was careful, he said, to have avoided being present himself in that winter. The phrase is not logistical. He does not mean he could not travel north. He means he did not want to see it." This is a small addition that deepens the chapter's most difficult passage and honors what Orderic actually tells us about himself.

This is a suggestion, not a correction. The chapter is not wrong as it stands.

## The Chronicle Tradition and Source Asymmetry

The chapter handles the source asymmetry well. The English-language chronicle tradition after 1066 is correctly noted as diminishing. The chapter does not overclaim from the sparse English record. The contrast between Orderic's rich narrative and the terse post-1066 Anglo-Saxon Chronicle is implicit in the chapter's structure, which is the right way to handle it.
