# Nafarroako Erresuma — The Kingdom of Navarre
## Opening

The pass does not care who crosses it.

Orreaga in August: the light comes in hard and flat from the west, throwing long shadows into the gorge below. The scrub grass between the rocks smells of dry earth and the particular coolness that comes off the stone even in summer, the smell of a place that holds winter inside itself. Eneko son of Aizarna lay flat on the upper ridge, where he knew the stone loosened near the lip and a careful man could see down without being seen. He had walked this ridgeline since he could walk. He knew which rocks moved in the thaw and which ones would hold a man's weight in August.

He heard the rearguard before he saw it. Metal on metal, the slow creak of wagon wheels on the stone track below, the sounds of an army not expecting trouble — spread too thin through the narrow gorge, the baggage train strung out and vulnerable, the end of it still not visible around the southern bend. He listened and counted what he heard. He had been counting since dawn.

Below, Charlemagne's rearguard was crossing.

The army of the Franks had spent the summer in Iberia. They had come south at the invitation of a Saracen governor who needed allies and then thought better of the offer. The campaign had accomplished little. Zaragoza had not opened its gates. The emperor had turned north, impatient, ordering the walls of Pamplona torn down — Iruñea, city of the Basques of Navarre — for reasons the Basques found sufficient to remember. Now the rearguard was strung out through Orreaga, the narrowest place in the Pyrenees, loaded with what the army had managed to take. The baggage train. The supply wagons. Twelve thousand, some said, or fewer, or more — the counts vary by who is counting and what they want the number to mean.

The men on the ridge had no interest in the count.

What happened next was quick. The Basques rolled boulders into the pass from the heights on both sides. The Frankish knights in their armor — useless armor, in a gorge, against stone falling from above — could not form a line, could not charge uphill into the broken terrain, could not bring their cavalry advantage to bear on enemies who stood above them and then did not stand there at all. The rearguard was destroyed. The commander died. The baggage was taken. The Basques withdrew into the mountains from which they had come, leaving the pass empty and quiet, the way mountain passes are always quiet after the difficult business is done.

The Franks called the dead commander Roland.

The poets would later have much to say about Roland. They would say the enemy was Saracens, not Basques, because Saracens were the proper enemy of the *chanson*, and the Christians dying against Saracens made a story that served the courts of France. The poem grew to 4,000 lines. It became one of the foundational works of French literature. The Basques, who had actually won at Orreaga, do not appear in it. This is not a complaint. It is simply the record: what happened, and what the poem said happened, are two different things, and the Basques have always lived in the distance between them.

---

This is the distance from which the story of Nafarroako Erresuma begins.

The Kingdom of Navarre was not simply a Basque kingdom. It was the form the Basques found to survive in a world that was beginning to organize itself into kingdoms, and that found people without kingdoms easier to absorb. The Kingdom of Pamplona was declared — if declaration is the right word for what was more a recognition of existing power — around 824 CE, when Iñigo Arista, whose name suggests the Basque-Iberian mix the region had always been, held Iruñea and the surrounding valleys against both Frankish pressure from the north and Umayyad pressure from the south. He did not hold it through armies that could compare with either power. He held it through the mountains and through the *foru*.

The *foru* required that any ruler who wanted Basque cooperation travel to the Basque lands, stand under the oak at Guernica or in the assembly of each province, and swear — before the gathered community — to honor the prior customs of the valley people as the price of their acceptance. Not a privilege the king granted. A covenant the king entered. The oak had been there before any of the names of the kingdoms that pressed against the mountains. The people gathered under it had been managing their pastures and their water rights and their iron-forge inheritances by assembly long before anyone thought to call them a kingdom. The king, when he came, came as a newcomer to an old agreement.

The *foru* is the difficult word to translate, and that difficulty is itself informative. It is rendered in Castilian as *fuero*, in French as *fors*, in English as nothing, because English has no word for it. Customs. Usages. The way things are done here. The agreement between the community and whoever wishes to govern it, in which the community's prior claim to its own law is recognized as the condition of governance. Every king who wanted to rule the Basque country had to swear to the *foru*, not in his capital but in the Basque lands, standing under the oak at Guernica or in the assembly of each province in turn, before the gathered people, making the oath that was the price of their acceptance. If he violated the oath, the covenant was broken. The Basques have a word for this: *foruaren haustea*. The breaking of the *foru*. After which the obligation of loyalty was no longer obligatory.

No other people in the medieval Iberian world operated on these terms. The Basques did not invent this principle in the ninth century. It was already there, already old, already assumed to be the natural order of things, by the time Navarre became a kingdom with a name the kings of France and Castile recognized. The kingdom was the latest vessel for something much older. The vessel would crack. The thing it held would not.

By the reign of Sancho III the Great — Sancho Garces III, who died in 1035 and whose territory at his death stretched from the Atlantic to the borders of Castile and Aragon — the Kingdom of Navarre had briefly become the largest Christian state in Iberia. It would fragment among his heirs within a generation. But the *foru* remained. The language remained. Euskara, which had survived the Romans and the Visigoths and the Franks, which carries in its vocabulary traces of a world before metal, before writing, before any of the kingdoms that pressed against its edges — Euskara remained.

The mountain does not care who crosses it. But the people who know the mountain do.
