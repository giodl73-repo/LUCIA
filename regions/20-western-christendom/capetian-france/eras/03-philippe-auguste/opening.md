# Philippe Auguste et la Grande Expansion
## Opening — Era 3, Capetian France

---

The rider came in from the east at dusk, his horse's flanks dark with sweat, and the men standing in the courtyard at Péronne knew before he dismounted that it was over. You could read it in how he sat the saddle — loose, exultant, the particular looseness of a man who has lived through something he was not certain he would survive. He was carrying news from a river crossing called Bouvines, and the news was this: Otto was broken.

The twenty-seventh of July, in the Year of Our Lord 1214. Philip, by the grace of God king of the Franks, had not himself led the charge at Bouvines — he was fifty years old and he knew his limitations — but he had chosen the ground, and he had held when the line broke around him, and he had watched his knights do what knights were bred to do. Otto IV, Holy Roman Emperor, had brought to that field everything the enemies of France could assemble: the imperial host, the count of Flanders and his cavalry, the mercenaries of John of England who had spent silver he did not have to put in the field an army he could not control. They had the numbers. They had the coalition. They had, they believed, the momentum of a war that had been bleeding France for a decade.

They had, it turned out, a riverbank at their back and a French cavalry that did not stop.

Philip rode back to Paris. The city had been waiting. The streets from the Porte Saint-Antoine to the Île de la Cité filled, a chronicler would write, with torches and singing that went on through the night and into the next day without ceasing. Students from the university came out of their colleges. The cathedral canons processed from Notre-Dame. The prisoners Philip brought with him — the great lords of Flanders, the counts and commanders who had staked everything on the wrong side — were paraded through the same streets, and the Parisians who had been taxed and conscripted for this war lined the road to look at them. No one needed to explain what had happened. Everyone understood.

France had, in the space of one afternoon, become something it had not been before.

Philip had begun his reign thirty-four years earlier as a sixteen-year-old king whose crown was largely ceremonial. His father had left him the Capetian domain: Paris, Orléans, the towns of the Île-de-France, and the feudal loyalty, in theory, of vassals who held more land than he did and obeyed him when it suited them. The duke of Normandy, the count of Anjou, the duke of Aquitaine — these were the great men of the west, and the fact that they owed homage to the king of France had not, in Philip's grandfather's time, meant very much. Homage was words. Words were not land.

Philip had spent his reign learning the lesson his predecessors had not, or had not been able to act on: that words became land when you enforced them. The king of England, Henry II and then Richard and then John, held Normandy, Anjou, Maine, Touraine, Poitou as vassals of the French crown. They held these territories by inheritance and by the sword. But they held them by feudal tenure, which meant they held them on conditions, and conditions could be violated, and violations had legal consequences that a determined king could press to their logical end. When John killed his nephew Arthur, Philip pressed. When John refused to answer the charge in the royal court of France, Philip declared his French fiefs forfeit. And then he went and took them.

Normandy fell in 1204. Philip had stood in Rouen and received the keys. The fortress of Château Gaillard — Richard's masterpiece, the castle Richard had said could be held against any army that God Himself commanded — had fallen to a siege of eight months. Philip's engineers had found the latrine shaft. The story was told with relish at the French court: God, it seemed, had not been given the assignment.

But Normandy and Anjou were reversible if John could reassemble his coalition. Bouvines was not reversible. Bouvines ended the argument. The coalition was broken and its members imprisoned or fled, John's English barons were in revolt against him at home, and Philip rode back to Paris knowing that the territories he had taken were now, in the permanent way of facts established by God's manifest judgment on a battlefield, his.

The sacred anointing at Reims had told Philip what he was. Twenty-seven years on the throne had shown him how to be it. His clerks were already building what would become the archives of the French crown — the *trésor des chartes*, the collected documents of a kingdom that was learning to administer itself. His court in Paris was drawing scholars, lawyers, and architects. The university that had grown from the cathedral school of Notre-Dame was becoming the greatest in Christendom, and the king who sat over it, the rex Christianissimus, the Most Christian King, looked out from the Île de la Cité at a city that had been a town when he was born and would be, before he died, a capital.

He was fifty years old. He had eighteen months left.
