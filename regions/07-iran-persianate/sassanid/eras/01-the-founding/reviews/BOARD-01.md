# Board Review 1 — Sassanid Era 1: The Founding

**Reviewer:** Touraj Daryaee (specialist, Sassanid Iran; UCI; author of *Sasanian Iran (224–651 CE)*)
**Chapter:** Sassanid Era 1 — The Founding (c. 224–272 CE)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Domain Accuracy

**Ardashir's origins and the Battle of Hormozdgan:** Accurately represented. The scholarly uncertainty about the exact location of Hormozdgan is properly acknowledged ("some sources say" construction for the single-combat tradition). The dating of 224 CE for the battle is the standard scholarly date. No corrections required.

**The three sacred fires:** Adur Gushnasp at Shiz/Takht-e Suleiman in Azerbaijan — correct. The volcanic lake setting is accurate; the natural gas seepage feeding the fire is historically attested and correctly stated. Adur Farnbag in Fars — the note about uncertain precise location is correctly reflected in the chapter's phrasing ("in the heartland province"). Adur Burzen-Mihr in Khorasan — correctly placed. The three-estate correspondence (priests/Adur Farnbag, warriors/Adur Gushnasp, farmers/Adur Burzen-Mihr) is the standard Zoroastrian framework. Accurate.

**Kartir's titles and career:** The progression from *hrb* to *mobadan mobad* is accurate and well-established in the primary inscriptional record. The four inscription sites (Naqsh-e Rostam, Naqsh-e Rajab, Ka'ba-ye Zartosht, and Sar Mashhad — the chapter mentions four without listing all four) are correct. The mystical journey inscription at Ka'ba-ye Zartosht is real and correctly characterized.

**Mani and the Sassanid court:** The scholarly consensus on Shapur's patronage of Mani is accurately represented. The dating of Mani's death (274 or 277 CE under Bahram I) — the chapter uses "274 or 277 CE" which correctly reflects scholarly uncertainty. The Manichaean tradition calling his death a martyrdom is accurate. Mani's spread into the Roman Empire (Augustine's Manichaean period, 373–382 CE) and east to China is historically documented. Accurate.

**Ka'ba-ye Zartosht inscription:** The chapter's description of the inscription as trilingual (Middle Persian, Parthian, Greek) is correct. The content described — listing of provinces, Roman war accounts, grants to multiple religious communities — is accurate. The quoted passage (paraphrased, not directly cited) is representative of the inscription's content. The chapter does not overstate what the inscription says.

**Valerian's capture:** Correctly dated to 260 CE at Edessa (or near it; the precise location is debated but Edessa is the conventional identification). The tradition about Valerian being used as a mounting block is correctly flagged as possibly invented. The five commemoration sites listed (Naqsh-e Rostam, Bishapur, Darabgird, Salmas, Taq-e Bostan) — minor note: Salmas is a less commonly cited site; Naqsh-e Bahram at Darabgird is more standard; this is a minor issue of site classification, not an error in substance.

**Band-e Qaysar / Shapur Bridge:** The Roman prisoner labor tradition for this bridge is historically attested and the dating is correct. The bridge's survival "in its essential form" — accurately hedged, as parts have been rebuilt or reinforced in the Islamic period but the Sassanid structure underlies the current form.

**The Gordian III question:** The chapter correctly presents the ambiguity — "some sources say" construction, noting both battle death and possible post-capture execution. The Sassanid relief at Bishapur shows Gordian under Shapur's horse (indicating death in defeat) while the Mishiche inscription suggests he died in battle. The chapter handles this uncertainty correctly.

---

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter's decision to treat the rock reliefs as the Sassanid primary archive — "a civilization that carves its history into vertical limestone faces rather than writing it on papyrus is making a statement about durability" — is a valid and sophisticated reading of Sassanid historical epistemology. The royal reliefs were not merely propaganda; they were the official record of cosmic events. The chapter captures this correctly.

The Zoroastrian cosmological framework (asha/druj, Ahura Mazda/Ahriman) is handled with accuracy and with appropriate seriousness. The fire maintenance practice described — the covered mouth, the prescribed woods, the five watches of the Zoroastrian day — is consistent with the Avestan tradition and later Zoroastrian practice.

One enhancement suggestion: the chapter mentions the *Khwadaynamag* (Book of Lords) in the overview but not in the chapter itself. This would be appropriate to reference in the chapter, as Ardashir's investment in the Iranian legendary-historical tradition was part of his founding project. Not a factual error — an enhancement.

---

## Summary

The chapter is historically accurate on all major claims. The source uncertainty issues are handled with appropriate scholarly caution. The Sassanid world as presented — its religious framework, its legal-sacred connection between kingship and divine authority, its relationship to the Achaemenid past, the Shapur-Kartir tension — is represented in a way that specialist scholarship would recognize and endorse.

**Board Recommendation:** PASS with one enhancement suggestion.

**Required corrections:** None.
**Enhancement (optional, not required):** Consider a brief reference to the *Khwadaynamag* tradition in the chapter to ground Ardashir's historical consciousness in its own literary form.
