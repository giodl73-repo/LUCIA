# Opening — The Classic Taíno (c. 1200–1492 CE)

---

She has been working on this areíto for three days.

The lead lines are settled now: the opening invocation of Atabey, the earth mother who holds the island in her body, and then the passage through the four directions, and then the ancestor-naming that links the cacicazgo of Xaragua back through seven generations to the one who first emerged from the cave at Cacibajagua, when the world was still being sorted into its proper forms. These lines belong to the tradition. Anacaona has not invented them; she has inherited them, as she inherited the duho she sits on, the carved wooden seat whose slight backward tilt places the cacica in the posture of someone who is always on the verge of receiving. A cacica receives. She receives the zemís' guidance, the community's need, the ancestors' instruction. She receives the lines from those who came before and passes them forward, changed a little, charged with the particular weight of her understanding.

What she has been working on is the middle section. The new part. The part that must be hers.

She is composing in the way that all areíto composition works, which is through the body first. She has been singing the phrase to herself in the rhythm of the ceremony, moving her weight from foot to foot as the community will move it, feeling where the phrase wants to land and where it resists, where the body's knowledge says yes and where it says not yet. The words are not separate from the motion. The motion is not separate from the sound. An areíto that is only words is a lesson. An areíto that carries the right rhythm in the right body makes the zemís present, which is the whole point.

Outside the bohío, the late afternoon is cooling toward the hour when the fireflies emerge in the plantain groves along the river. In the conucos, the manioc mounds rise in their rows, their broad leaves still holding the day's heat. The women who tend them are returning to the yucayeque, their hands carrying the particular smell of earth and broken stems that means the ground is in good order. The zemís in the central batey court are quiet now, their carved faces turned toward the sky that is slowly shifting from the color of guanábana flesh to something deeper. The land is in its proper evening arrangement. Everything is doing what it is supposed to do.

This is what the areíto must hold.

Anacaona's cacicazgo of Xaragua is the largest and most prosperous on Ayiti. Her brother Bohechío led it before her, and before him their father, and the lineage runs back and back into the time when the zemís were still learning how to speak through the people they had chosen. Xaragua is known across the island for its areítos. Caciques from Maguana, from Marién, from the smaller communities in the eastern mountains send word when they want a ceremony that will hold: send us one of Xaragua's singers. The reach of Xaragua's ceremonial tradition is the reach of its authority. It is not coercion. It is something more durable than coercion. It is the fact that when you hear an areíto from Xaragua, performed correctly, the ancestors are present, and everyone in the gathering knows it.

Anacaona composes. The community will learn. Other communities, hearing it performed by Xaragua's singers, will learn from them, and so the areíto will travel outward through the island like water finding its level, carrying whatever knowledge she has placed in its middle section. This is how knowing works. You cannot write it down. You do not need to. The body is a better archive than any material object, because the body remembers by repeating, and repetition makes the knowledge alive. The zemís attend when the knowledge is alive. They are not present for information; they are present for living knowledge, which is knowledge in motion, knowledge with breath in it.

The phrase she is working on concerns the batey. The ball game and what the ball game means. Not in the way a description would mean it, but in the way that ceremony means things: through the body's participation, through the moment when the rubber ball rises from a hip's smooth strike and the players and the watching community are one body tracking one motion through the air that holds everything together, the living and the dead and the zemís who are both and neither. The phrase must make that moment happen in the telling of it. This is the difficulty. This is why she has been working for three days.

Outside, her daughter is learning a previous areíto from one of the elder singers. The child's voice, still finding its placement, threads through the evening air. She is only six years old. By the time she is twelve she will have this entire ceremony in her body. By the time she is twenty she will compose her first addition. The tradition is continuous. It has always been continuous. This is not a thought that Anacaona has in so many words; it is simply the texture of things, the way the world is organized, the same way the manioc grows in mounds and not in flat beds, the same way the rain comes from the north in certain seasons and from the east in others. The world has a structure. The areíto is one of the things that maintains it.

She stands up from the duho. She begins to move.

The phrase has found its landing.

She will teach it tomorrow morning, while the dew is still on the conuco leaves and the day is fresh enough for difficult work. The community will learn it in sections. By the next full moon, they will perform it together, and the zemís will be present, and the cacicazgos that have sent their representatives will hear it, and Xaragua's knowing will extend its reach again, quietly, the way roots extend through soil that doesn't notice them until the tree stands firm.

She does not know that she has eight years.

This is not irony. It is simply the condition of being alive: you are always working for a world that will outlast you, and you cannot know by how much, and so you work carefully, the way she works on this middle section, finding what is true and making it repeatable, which is the only gift you can give to the ones who come after.

The phrase is ready. She moves through it once more in the gathering dark.

The zemís are listening.
