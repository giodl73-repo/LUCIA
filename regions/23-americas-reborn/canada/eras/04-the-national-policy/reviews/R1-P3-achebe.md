# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Review: Opening — Canada Era 4, The National Policy and Its Discontents

I have read this carefully. I want to say what is right before I say what concerns me, because what is right matters here.

The opening begins inside the child's experience, not above it. "Her name was Qwenqwent" is the right first sentence. Not "Indigenous children across Canada," not "The residential school system," not a date followed by a generalization. A name. A specific person. Her name leads because her name is the thing the system will take. This is the kind of structural intelligence that comes from actually understanding what is at stake.

The grandmother's song, the teaching about water moving through the land, the braids as the physical form of transmitted knowledge: these are handled with care and without sentimentality. The author has understood that the braids are not symbolic — they are actual, a real medium for actual cultural transmission. The scissors are not symbolic either. They are a real instrument doing a real thing. This is the difference between rendering a culture's loss from inside and displaying it for a reader's sympathy.

"The word for water moving through land did not appear in any federal ledger." This sentence is doing important work. The ledger does not contain what the grandmother's song contained. The two systems cannot see each other. This is told from inside the logic of what was lost, not from the position of someone tallying the loss.

**Where the voice is most at risk:** The second movement, the National Policy section. "Canada in 1867 was a confederation of four provinces with a design for the west and a problem with the east." Whose language is this? "A design for the west" is the colonial government's language. "A problem with the east" is the colonial government's language. These are not the Secwépemc's categories, nor the Ojibwe's, nor the Cree's. This is the moment where the author's position as someone trained in Western historical narrative is most visible.

I understand why this passage exists: the chapter must hold the dual track, and the dual track requires entering the colonial narrative to show its assumptions. But the phrasing must be more deliberately at arm's length from those assumptions. "The design for the west was settlement": from whose position is this a design? The child in Kamloops is part of what was being settled. The sentence treats the colonial government's framing as the neutral default.

This is a craft problem, not a failure of intent. The fix is modest: the framing language can be marked as the colonial narrative's own self-understanding rather than neutral description. "What the National Policy called settlement" rather than "settlement." "What Macdonald's government understood as the design for the west." The reader should feel the author's consciousness of the perspective they are inhabiting when they enter the second movement.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter will cover a century of residential schools. 150,000 children, 139 schools, 1880s to 1996. This is not a single story; it is many stories held in one system. How does the chapter maintain the Achebe Test across this scale? The opening works because it has one child. The chapter risks becoming a chapter about the system rather than the people inside it. I would watch carefully for the moment when "residential school" becomes an abstraction and the children inside it become data.

The close is strong. "The mountains were still there. They had not been waiting for Confederation to name them. They did not recognize the name." This is the land speaking in its own temporal register, indifferent to the colonial act of naming. It passes the test I apply to every sentence: could a thoughtful Secwépemc reader find their story told with dignity here? Yes. This sentence is told from inside.
