# Figures — The Great Hajj

## Mansa Musa (Musa Keita I)

He came to power by accident. Abu Bakr II, his predecessor, assembled a fleet and sailed west into the Atlantic and did not return, and Musa, who had been Abu Bakr's deputy, became mansa. He did not seek the throne. What he did with it once he held it was deliberate.

The witnesses who saw him described a tall man, deliberate in speech, who wore white pilgrim's cloth even when the gold around him said otherwise. When the Mamluk sultan's court in Cairo expected him to kiss the ground, Musa found a way through: he kissed the ground before God, not before the sultan. This was a man who understood that he moved between systems of authority and that each system had to be navigated on its own terms without conceding the terms of his own.

He ruled the Manden Kurufaba for roughly twenty-five years, from about 1312 to 1337. He expanded the empire's reach, but what the jelilu carried forward was not the expansion. It was the hajj, the giving, the return, the building. He left with gold and came back with scholars and architects and books, and the covenant that demanded he give everything was fulfilled, and the cities he built into survived him by centuries. He died around 1337. The jelilu are still singing his name.

## Inari Kunate

She rode at the head of her own column. The indigo of her wrapping cloth was dark against the morning haze, and behind her came five hundred women of her household, the provisions train, the food stores and grain and the water vessels counted and recounted at every halt.

The historical record preserves her name and the scale of her retinue but little else. What she said, what she decided at the dry wells and the difficult crossings, what she thought as the savanna turned to rock and the rock turned to sand, the sources do not say. What they do say is that she was there. She traveled with her own household and her own attendants and her own column, and this was how a mansa's senior wife traveled: not as a consort trailing behind the king but as a figure of authority commanding her own section of the caravan. The Manden did not send its senior women into the desert as passengers. Inari Kunate organized, commanded, and crossed the Sahara at the head of her people. That the details are sparse does not diminish the fact. It only means the jelilu carried more than the written record preserved.

## Bala Faseke Kouyate

The name Bala Faseke belongs to the Kouyate jeli lineage, and it reaches back to the founding of the Manden. In the Sundiata Epic, Bala Faseke is the jeli who accompanied Sundiata himself, the first keeper of the covenant's memory. The name recurs across generations of the Kouyate lineage, not because each bearer is the same person but because the function persists. The jeli who sang at the gate of Niani when Musa's caravan departed carried a name that was itself a genealogy.

His kora held the low notes while his voice climbed. He sang the names of the dynasty, the successes and the failures alike, because a jeli does not edit the past for the comfort of the present. He sang because that was his obligation under the Kurukan Fuga, as binding as the mansa's obligation to give. The nyamakalaw, the craft specialists of the Manden, held their knowledge as a sacred trust, and the jeli's knowledge was the most sacred of all: the memory of who the people were and where they came from and what they owed each other. Bala Faseke sang at the departure, and the tradition he embodied is still singing seven hundred years later.

## Abu Ishaq al-Sahili

He was from Granada, from the Islamic west, from a world that was itself contracting under the pressure of the Christian Reconquista. He was a poet as well as an architect, a man of letters as well as a man of buildings. When Musa met him in Cairo and offered a commission, al-Sahili said yes. He said yes to twelve thousand mithqals of gold, which was a fortune, and he said yes to a city he had never seen on the edge of a desert he had never crossed.

In Timbuktu, he built. The Djinguereber Mosque rose under his direction: mud-brick and cut stone, the protruding toron timbers that served as scaffolding and ventilation, lime plaster on the interior walls, a minaret visible from the river. He did not replace the Sahelian building tradition. He entered it, bringing what he knew from al-Andalus and adapting it to what the climate and the materials and the existing practice demanded. The building that emerged was a conversation, not an imposition. Al-Sahili lived out his life in Mali. He chose to stay. The buildings he raised have been repaired and replastered by generations of hands since his, but the conversation he began with the mud and the stone is still legible in the walls.

## Al-Umari's Informants

The scholar al-Umari never met Mansa Musa. He arrived in Cairo years after the hajj and interviewed the people who had been there: court officials, merchants, members of the Mamluk administration who had witnessed the visit firsthand. His account, the Masalik al-Absar, written in the 1340s, is the most detailed surviving written record of Musa's time in Egypt.

The informants themselves were people caught inside a story they did not fully understand. A court official named Ibn Amir Hajib remembered the protocol confrontation, the mansa's refusal to kiss the ground, the compromise that satisfied both courts. A merchant in the gold souk watched his inventory lose value day by day as Musa's gold flooded the market. They described what they saw: the scale of the caravan, the volume of the giving, the disruption of the gold price. What they could not describe was the logic that drove it. The Kurukan Fuga was not their covenant. The generosity that baffled them was, to Musa, simply what a mansa does. Al-Umari compiled their testimony faithfully. The bewilderment he recorded is theirs, and it is honest, and it is the bewilderment of people standing inside someone else's story.

## Ibn Battuta

He arrived in Mali in 1352, fifteen years after Musa's death, a Moroccan traveler with strong opinions and a low tolerance for customs that departed from his own. He found the court functioning, the mosques standing, the jelilu singing, the scholars at Sankore teaching. He also found things that irritated him: the freedom of women at court, the serving customs at royal meals, the casual mixing of social categories that his Moroccan-Islamic sensibility found improper.

His account is valuable precisely because of its limitations. He describes what he saw with the eye of a man who has traveled from Morocco to India to China and back, and his descriptions of the physical world, the routes, the cities, the daily texture of life, are vivid and grounded. His judgments are his own, shaped by the cultural certainties of a well-traveled but narrow-minded observer. What he confirms, without intending to, is that the world Musa built persisted. The mosques still stood. The scholars still taught. The jelilu still sang. Ibn Battuta came and went. The tradition he witnessed did not need his approval to continue. It had been continuing since before he was born, and it would continue long after his account became a manuscript gathering dust in a library in Fez.
