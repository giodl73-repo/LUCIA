# PANEL-1 — Chinua Achebe
## The First Empire: Atlantic Commerce and Plantation

This opening passes the Achebe Test, and I want to be precise about why, because the test is harder here than in almost any chapter in the Chronicle.

The chapter's subject is the British Empire. The "inside" perspective belongs to the British. The chapter is written from inside British self-understanding: the legitimate manifest, the chartered company, the Parliamentary endorsement. And yet the opening does not endorse the system. It records it. The distinction is in the grammar: "The law recognized each transaction as legitimate" is not the author's verdict that the law was right; it is the system's own self-description. "Both things were true: the ledger, and the crossing" is not a moral equivalence; it is a structural observation about how the city organized itself. This is honest prose.

Equiano arrives and passes his own test. He is not romanticized. He is not victimized. He is an agent who uses the system's own vocabulary to dismantle the system — "using the arguments they had taught him to value, dismantling the system from inside the vocabulary it had given him." This sentence is the chapter's clearest statement of purpose, and it belongs in the chapter because it is accurate: Equiano's *Interesting Narrative* was a work of strategic appropriation, not just testimony. The opening honors his agency.

**What I would push on:** The forty-six dead. "The remaining forty-six were entered in the accounts as loss." This is a devastating line, and it earns its impact without editorial comment. My concern is not with this line but with what follows. The chapter immediately pivots to Norris's testimony, then to Liverpool's institutions. The forty-six do not have names. They do not have a world. The chapter is, by design, the story of the empire's self-understanding — but the Achebe Test requires that the humanity of the people in the hold not be collapsed into accounting categories even when the accounting categories are the chapter's dominant mode. The Middle Passage chapter in Region 25 holds those people fully. This chapter cannot do the same — it has a different subject. But one sentence grounding even one of the forty-six in particularity, in the same way the *Thetis* manifest is grounded in particularity, would honor them without betraying the chapter's voice.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter is about the British arguing with themselves. The abolitionists were British. The critics used British libraries. This is all true and important. But does the chapter acknowledge, even briefly, that the argument was also being made from outside the British self-understanding — from the enslaved people themselves, from the Akan and Fon and Yoruba traders who had their own calculations, from the planters in Jamaica who had their own version of the story? The danger of "the empire arguing with itself" as a frame is that it makes abolitionism a British intellectual achievement rather than a response to sustained African and enslaved Caribbean resistance. The opening should plant the seed that the argument had more than one side.
