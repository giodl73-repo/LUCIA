## Assessment of the Loya Jirga Rendering

The chapter renders the 1747 Loya Jirga with essential accuracy. The gathering was convened under conditions the chapter correctly describes: the power vacuum following Nader Shah's assassination, the Pashtun khans' need to reconstitute their collective authority. The chapter's portrayal of the Loya Jirga as a mechanism of consensus — not a "democratic" institution in any modern sense but a gathering of clan chiefs whose agreement carried the force of collective obligation — is consistent with the best current scholarship.

The Sabir Shah episode is historically attested and correctly placed. The holy man's sanction served a specific political function: it allowed the assembled khans to reach a decision without any individual khan bearing personal responsibility for proposing it. This is a feature of many consensus-based systems in situations of factional uncertainty, and the chapter handles it correctly by presenting it within the assembly's own logic rather than as an exotic ritual.

## Terminology Concern

I concur with the concern raised by my co-reviewer about the Mohammadzai/Barakzai terminology. The chapter should consistently distinguish: the Barakzai are the tribe; the Mohammadzai are the dominant clan within the Barakzai. At the founding jirga, the figure would be identified primarily by his tribal affiliation (Barakzai) and his clan position (Mohammadzai chief). The conflation creates a small but potentially confusing anachronism — by the nineteenth century, "Mohammadzai" was being used as a dynastic name for the Barakzai rulers, which is the meaning most readers will encounter in other sources.

**Required correction:** Revise references to be consistently: "Hajji Jamal Khan of the Barakzai" (first instance) and "the Barakzai" thereafter when referring to the tribe. The Mohammadzai clan designation can appear with an explanatory phrase on first use.

## On the Sadozai-Barakzai Structural Tension

The chapter correctly identifies this as the founding deal and the empire's eventual structural weakness. I want to add one historical note that the chapter might carry lightly: the "deal" at the 1747 jirga was not formalized — it was understood within the logic of Pashtunwali rather than written out. This makes it exactly the kind of arrangement that the code can sustain as long as each party's expectations are met and exactly the kind of arrangement that the code cannot resolve when those expectations diverge. The chapter's terminal condition analysis reflects this correctly.

## Poetry Fragment Note

I note the poetry fragment used in the court section. The chapter's note that this is a "composite rendering rather than a specific attributed text" (from the board recommendation) should be reflected in figures.md with a brief note about the limits of the attribution. Ahmad Shah's *divan* is partially preserved; the existing scholarship on his verse is thin. The period-consistent composite is a legitimate approach but should be acknowledged as such.

## Minor Factual Note

The chapter describes the army marching "through the Khyber Pass" on the route to the Punjab. This is generally correct for the main route, though Durrani forces also used the Khyber Pass alternately with the Bolan Pass depending on the campaign's objectives (the Bolan route was used for campaigns into Sindh). For the Punjab campaigns the Khyber is correct.

**Board recommendation: APPROVED with terminology correction required before final lock.**
