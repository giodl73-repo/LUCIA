# Notes — Era 1: The Parima World
## Production Bible

**Era:** The Parima World (c. 1000–1750 CE)
**People:** Yanomami
**Region:** Amazonia, Caribbean & Lowland South America
**Voice:** Shamanic-Observational — patient, maintenance-paced, present-tense attentiveness; cosmos and ordinary life in the same register
**Rubric version:** v1.5

---

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** The hekura as continuous civilizational maintenance — the shapori's negotiation with spirit forces is identical to the shapori's negotiation with political alliance. Spiritual maintenance and diplomatic maintenance are not parallel systems; they are the same system seen from two angles.

**Mechanism:** Each healing, each crisis in the hekura world, requires a shapori from the community with appropriate spirit jurisdiction. Because no single shapori or single shabono controls all the hekura of all the relevant forest territories, healing is constitutively intercommunit — it requires alliance. Feasting (reahu) creates and maintains the alliances that make healing possible. Alliance enables healing; healing demonstrates the value of alliance. The system is self-reinforcing as long as it functions.

**Terminal condition:** The engine does not break within Era 1 — this is the era of the world before sustained external disruption. The terminal condition for the broader Yanomami civilization comes in later eras: when epidemic disease kills the shapori (who carry the hekura knowledge) faster than apprentices can be trained, the maintenance system collapses not because the cosmology failed but because the transmission chain broke. Era 1 should end with the engine fully operational and the hint that its vulnerability is precisely its transmission dependency — knowledge held in human bodies is fragile.

**Non-chronological organizing principle (v1.5 relevant):** The chapter does not have a linear chronological arc — Era 1 spans c. 750 years of a world without significant external disruption. The organizing principle is spatial and cosmological: the chapter moves through the Yanomami world concentrically, from the shapori's body outward to the shabono, outward to the alliance network, outward to the spirit landscape of the Parima highlands, and back inward to the community as the place where all these circles converge. Time passes within this structure but does not drive it.

## Sources

Primary / closest to primary:
- **Davi Kopenawa and Bruce Albert, *The Falling Sky: Words of a Yanomami Shaman*** (2013) — the most authoritative inside account; Kopenawa is a Yanomami shapori and spokesman; Albert is a French anthropologist who spent decades with the Yanomami; the text is explicitly Kopenawa's voice translated and framed with Albert's scholarly apparatus. Tier 1/2 hybrid — Kopenawa is as close to primary as the project gets for an era without written records.
- **Helena Valero, *Yarima: A Venezuelan Woman Among the Yanomami*** (1984, various editions) — Valero was a Venezuelan woman captured by Yanomami in the 1930s and lived with them for decades; her testimony, whatever its complications, provides detailed ethnographic observation of pre-intensive-contact Yanomami life.
- **Napoleon Chagnon, *Yanomamö: The Fierce People*** (1968, multiple editions) — heavily contested source; Chagnon's framing of the Yanomami as uniquely violent has been disputed extensively (see Tierney, *Darkness in El Dorado*, 2000); the ethnographic detail is valuable but requires the source-asymmetry technique (v1.5): Chagnon as structural evidence of the anthropological encounter, not as characterization of the Yanomami.

Academic:
- **Bruce Albert, "Yanomami Violence: Inclusive Fitness or Ethnographer's Representation?"** (1989) — direct rebuttal of Chagnon's "fierce people" thesis; Albert's work provides the corrective framework.
- **Kenneth Good with David Chanoff, *Into the Heart*** (1991) — another anthropologist who spent years with Yanomami; more sympathetic than Chagnon but also an outside observer.
- **Jacques Lizot, *Tales of the Yanomami: Daily Life in the Venezuelan Forest*** (1985) — detailed daily-life ethnography; Lizot spent three decades with Venezuelan Yanomami.
- **Gary Urton (ed.), *Animal Myths and Metaphors in South America*** (1985) — broader context for Amazonian cosmological systems.
- **Neil Whitehead and Robin Wright (eds.), *In Darkness and Secrecy: The Anthropology of Assault Sorcery and Witchcraft in Amazonia*** (2004) — provides context for inter-community spiritual conflict and alliance.

Source situation (v1.5 source asymmetry): The written sources for the Yanomami are almost entirely from outsiders. The one inside-voice source (Kopenawa) is from the twentieth century and reflects both traditional knowledge and the experience of crisis. The chapter should use Kopenawa as the interpretive frame for the era, even though his testimony comes from later — the oral tradition he represents is continuous from Era 1. Chagnon and other anthropologists appear as structural evidence of the outside encounter, not as characterization of the inside world. This is the "outsider-as-structural-evidence" technique from v1.5 applied proactively to a pre-contact era.

## Key Figures

**Aramo** (composite / fictional-typical) — the shapori of the opening. His specificity is established in the opening: old, experienced, trained in an extended apprenticeship, maintaining a community's cosmic balance. Not named in any historical record (there are no written records of pre-contact Yanomami individuals), but grounded in the phenomenology of shapori experience as described by Kopenawa and others. The composite figure technique (v1.1 Figures amendment) applies.

**A second shapori from a neighboring community** — appears in the middle section during the alliance visit; represents the network of shamanic authority that spans community boundaries. Also composite.

**A woman of the eastern family** — the grandmother of the sick child; her presence in the opening (looking up, looking down) grounds the chapter's claim that the shapori's work is ordinary. Developed slightly in the chapter's middle section.

**Davi Kopenawa** — though he is a figure of Era 3-4, his voice structures the chapter's interpretive frame. He is not a character in the chapter but his understanding of the shapori's work is the chapter's epistemological authority.

On honest limits (v1.1): The chapter cannot name pre-contact Yanomami individuals. This is stated in the figures.md and turned into a technique: the anonymity of the figures is the anonymity of oral tradition, of a world that kept its records in the bodies of the shapori rather than in written names. The figures are composite and this is acknowledged.

## Narrative Arc (Section Plan)

**Section 1 — The Shapori at Work (~900 words, integrates opening)**
The opening scene: Aramo calling the hekura, diagnosing the child's fever, receiving information that requires an alliance visit. Establishes the engine. The shapori's work as maintenance.

**Section 2 — What the Shabono Is (~700 words)**
The shabono as political and cosmological center. The architecture is the community's social structure made physical: family sections, the open plaza, the shared roof. How the shabono constitutes the political unit. How the decision to build a new shabono — made every few years as the structure deteriorates — is the decision to re-constitute the community. The physical object (shabono) as structural principle (v1.5 material object engine). What changes and what continues when the community rebuilds.

**Section 3 — The Hekura Landscape (~900 words)**
The spirit world as the environmental map of the Parima highlands. Hekura of specific places — waterfalls, mountains, animals, plants. The tepuis as ancient, powerful spirit territories. The shapori's work as navigation of this landscape. The yakoana's method: not escape from the physical world but deeper entry into it. Davis's challenge answered here: show the knowledge system at the level of method. What the shapori does with the information the hekura provide.

**Section 4 — The Feast and the Alliance (~900 words)**
The reahu as political maintenance. A feast in detail: the preparation, the arrival of guests from a neighboring shabono, what is exchanged (food, goods, songs, dances), what is obligated by the exchange. How the host community's generosity constitutes both diplomatic obligation and shamanic alignment — the community that can feast is the community that is in good standing with the hekura of its territory. Ibn Khaldun's challenge answered: what is the causal logic, what are the stakes of failure.

**Section 5 — The Ash of the Dead (~700 words)**
The reahu's most charged element: the consumption of the dead's ashes. Not as a climax but as a continuation of the chapter's argument. The dead are consumed so they can continue to be present. The ashes in the plantain soup are not ceremony — they are the most complete form of refusing the separation of death. This section should inhabit the practice from inside, without announcement, without the brace that signals to the reader that something unusual is coming. Achebe's challenge answered.

**Section 6 — What Will Not Break (~500 words)**
Landing. The chapter closes at the level of the engine's durability: the shapori's knowledge, held in bodies, transmitted across generations through the demanding apprenticeship, constitutes the civilization's continuity. The Parima highlands have sustained this system for long enough that no living person can remember when it was not in place. The world continues. The hint at vulnerability: the chain of transmission is exactly as long as the shapori who carry it. The opening's image returns — the evening, the ordinary sounds, the maintenance continuing.

## Panel Pressure Points Anticipated

**Tuchman:** The outsider-misunderstanding passage in the opening. Consider revision: trust the scene more, the explicit naming of the hypothetical outsider less. The chapter must maintain narrative momentum through the more complex middle sections (hekura landscape, alliance mechanics) without retreating to exposition.

**Ibn Khaldun:** Show the failure stakes, not just the successful maintenance. The chapter should include at least one moment of alliance stress or shamanic failure — a hekura that proves difficult to call, a feast that produces tension rather than smooth exchange — so the system's stakes are visible.

**Achebe:** The reahu section is the test. No announcement, no preparation of the reader, no distancing. Simply inhabit it as the community does.

**Davis:** Show the yakoana at method level: what plants, what preparation, what bodily indicators of success or failure. Show the hekura knowledge system as rigorous, not atmospheric.

**Kapuscinski:** Every section needs its sensory anchor. The reahu needs the texture of ash and grief. The feast needs the sounds and smells of a community hosting. The hekura landscape needs the physical reality of the Parima highlands.

## Open Questions

1. The specific Virola species used in yakoana preparation — is it V. theiodora or V. elongata? (Chagnon and Lizot record different species for different communities.) The chapter should use the more widely documented one or hedge honestly.

2. The role of the nochile (community headman) relative to the shapori — are these always separate roles, or can the same person hold both? Kopenawa's account suggests they are separate but co-dependent. The chapter should reflect this without over-systematizing.

3. Whether the reahu always involves ash consumption or only when the death is recent — Lizot's account suggests the ash is prepared and consumed months after death, not immediately. This affects the section's placement.

4. The tepui cosmology's specific content — Kopenawa describes Marahuaka as a central cosmological reference; the chapter should be careful not to over-generalize from one community's account to all Yanomami.

## Voice Notes

**Confirmed voice register: Shamanic-Observational**
- Moves at the speed of attention, not urgency
- Cosmos and ordinary life occupy the same register
- Sensory anchoring at small, specific scale
- Patient sentences that observe without interpreting
- Present-tense maintenance rhythm
- Zero or near-zero em-dashes: pauses are periods
- The analytical argument lives inside the narration, not alongside it

**Risk to monitor:** The chapter's analytical content (how the engine works, what the stakes are) must be carried by the same voice as the sensory passages. Do not slip into explanation when the material becomes more complex. Let the scenes do the analytical work.
