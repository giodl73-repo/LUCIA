| Scene     | 5/5   | The opening drops into a wholly specific moment: muskets arriving in a courtyard, being unloaded from camels, counted by a named chronicler. "Ahmad ibn Fartuwa, imam and chronicler to Mai Idris Alooma, counted them as they were unloaded in the courtyard of the royal compound." A named person, a named place, an action in progress. | World     | 5/5   | The culture's own logic permeates every sentence. The mai watches from behind a curtain because "The mai's body was the body of the state." The muskets are instruments, not symbols of modernization. The culture is so fully itself that no external vantage is available. ## Overall Assessment

This is a strong opening that succeeds most fully in its scene-setting, its world-building, and its pull. The musket demonstration is a vivid, specific entry point that simultaneously establishes the era's defining innovation (firearms), the culture's political structure (the curtained mai), and the narrative's anchor figure (Ahmad ibn Fartuwa as chronicler). The decision to place the imam-chronicler at the center, counting and recording, gives the opening a self-aware historical consciousness that belongs to the culture itself. The main area for improvement is ensuring that the middle expository passages (Ottoman diplomacy, Ahmad's legal training) maintain the same sensory density as the opening and closing paragraphs.
