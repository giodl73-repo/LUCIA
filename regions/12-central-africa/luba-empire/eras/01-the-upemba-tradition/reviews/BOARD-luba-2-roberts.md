## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

**The lukasa treatment:** The chapter introduces the lukasa memory board carefully: as a technology of the kingdom period, not of the Upemba tradition, while correctly noting that the conceptual foundation — political knowledge encoded in portable material form, legible only to trained specialists — is visible in the earlier burial practices. This is exactly the right relationship to establish between Era 1 and the system I documented in *Memory: Luba Art and the Making of History*. The chapter does not anachronize the lukasa by placing it in a period before it existed; it plants the seed without claiming the flower.

**The bana balute:** They are correctly identified as trained specialists in the overview context and in the note about the lukasa. They do not appear in the Era 1 chapter itself, which is appropriate — they are a feature of the kingdom period.

**The knowledge-as-governance framing:** The chapter's argument that the Upemba tradition's burial record documents a form of governance carried in communal memory — specifically in the knowledge of who is owed what recognition — is consistent with my analysis of the Luba memory system. The Luba governance tradition made political authority legible through material encoding and specialist interpretation; the chapter correctly identifies the early grave-goods tradition as a precursor to this practice.

**Interior approach to the grave goods:** The question "What did the community know about her?" (Section 3) is the right question to ask. This is the approach I have tried to take in my own scholarship — not treating the burials as passive evidence awaiting interpretation by outside scholars, but as artifacts of an active political knowledge system held by the community itself. The chapter does this correctly.

## Corrections Required

**Correction 1 (clarification):**
The chapter states: "The bana balute who read the lukasa were trained specialists — the keepers of the kingdom's institutional memory." This sentence appears in the broader overview context (the overview document, not the chapter itself, which I also read). In the chapter, the lukasa reference is correctly hedged ("the beaded and carved objects that would eventually encode the Luba kingdom's political genealogies..."). No correction needed in the chapter; I note this for the overview's review.

**Correction 2 (minor emphasis):**
The chapter could more explicitly note that the lukasa is not simply a record but a performance — it must be read aloud, in context, by a trained specialist, to be meaningful. The object alone is not the governance technology; the reading is. This distinction matters because it positions the Luba memory system as an embodied practice rather than a written equivalent, which is exactly what Davis is asking for and what the v1.5 Knowledge System amendment requires. I recommend adding one sentence to the lukasa passage in the chapter: something to the effect that the boards carried meaning only in the hands of those trained to read them aloud.

## Cultural Representation

The chapter represents the Upemba tradition with dignity and intellectual seriousness. The Luba world is not treated as a primitive precursor to the kingdom but as a sophisticated society organized according to its own principles. The anonymous-builder technique honors the fact that the people of the Upemba shore were real human beings with knowledge and agency, even when the record cannot preserve their names.

**Recommendation: PASS with Correction 2 as a recommended enhancement (not a required fix).**
