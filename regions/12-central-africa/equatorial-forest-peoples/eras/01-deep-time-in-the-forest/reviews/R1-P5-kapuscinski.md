# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski
## Panel Review, Round 1 — Opening

**Chapter:** Deep Time in the Forest — Era 1
**Reviewer:** Ryszard Kapuscinski (P-5)
**Stage:** PANEL-1

---

I have spent a great deal of time in forests and in the places between forests and the world that comes after forests. The Congo Basin is like no other place. The author knows this, and knows how to render it.

"The smell of loam and decay and new growth is so dense it is almost a thing you could hold." Yes. That is the smell. That is why you cannot write about the equatorial forest without that smell — everything else is a different place. The light through the canopy in the morning, moving as the leaves move — yes. The hornbill to the north. Yes. The author has been in this forest, or has read the accounts of those who have, with great attention.

I want more of this. The chapter ahead will need the physical forest at every turn. The forest peoples' knowledge is inseparable from the physical experience of being in that specific place — the way your feet read the ground, the way sounds come from different directions and heights, the way you track an animal by smell and disturbed vegetation and the direction the dew has been moved. This is a world in which the human body is a precision instrument, calibrated to one environment over many generations. The chapter must make the reader feel this calibration.

What is missing: the night. The opening takes place in the morning, and the equatorial forest morning is magnificent. But the night in the Ituri is something else — vast, dense, with sound rather than vision as the primary sense. The molimo ceremonies happen at night, around a fire, with singing that is directed at the forest itself. If the chapter includes the molimo, it must include the night forest, because the ceremony and the place are inseparable.

Also missing: the specific sound of polyphonic singing in the forest. The ethnomusicologist Simha Arom described Aka polyphony as matching the structural complexity of the forest itself — many independent lines weaving into a coherent whole without a conductor, exactly as the forest operates. This is not just a beautiful metaphor. It is a description of how the music functions as a way of knowing. The chapter should find a way to render this sound, not describe it. "Complex polyphony" is a description. The sound itself — how it moves between voices, how the voices interweave without ever quite resolving — that is the thing.

The ending works. "Three trees ahead. It has always been three trees ahead." The present tense at the end is right — this is happening now, has always been happening, the tense makes the point without making the point.

**The question I'd push on:** Where is the night? Where is the sound of the forest at night, which is the context in which the molimo ceremony happens, which is when the forest becomes most itself, when vision fails and hearing becomes everything, when the human body must use a different set of instruments? The chapter needs both the morning forest and the night forest to be complete.
