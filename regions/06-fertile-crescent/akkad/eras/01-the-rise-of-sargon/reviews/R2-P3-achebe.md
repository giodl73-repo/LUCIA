# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Targeted Review of Notes

**Date:** 2026-04-16
**Artifact:** notes.md

## Cultural Voice Risk

The composite governor figure (Section II) is a good solution to the Achebe Test concern from R1, but it must be handled carefully. The governor in a conquered Sumerian city is both conqueror and student. He imposes Akkadian authority but writes in Sumerian. He replaces the local king but maintains the local temple. This duality must be felt as tension, not as enlightened multiculturalism. The Akkadians did not preserve Sumerian culture out of respect. They preserved it because they needed it. The scribal system, the religious vocabulary, the agricultural management, the canal administration. All of it was in Sumerian, and the empire could not function without it. This is not tolerance. It is dependency. The chapter must be honest about the difference.

## Conquered Perspective

Section III (the bilingual synthesis) is the section most at risk of presenting Akkadian conquest as cultural enrichment. The note that "the Akkadians do not destroy the Sumerian system; they graft onto it" is accurate but must not read as praise. A graft is still an act of taking. The Sumerian scribes who now served Akkadian governors did not choose the arrangement. Their cities had been taken in thirty-four battles. The bilingual tradition is an achievement, but it is an achievement built on conquest, and the chapter must hold both truths simultaneously.

## Recommendation

Include at least one moment where the bilingual reality is experienced as loss, not synthesis. A Sumerian scribe teaching Akkadian words to students who will use those words to administer a Sumerian city on behalf of an Akkadian king. The scribe's knowledge is valued. The scribe's autonomy is not. This is not a verdict. It is a fact. Present it.
