# B-hausa-2 — Paul Lovejoy — Board Review of "The Manufacturing Peak"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** The commercial economy of the Hausa states, the trans-Saharan trade, the kola nut trade, the role of enslaved labor in the manufacturing economy.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The characterization of Kano as a manufacturing center exporting finished goods (not raw materials) is correct and important. The distinction between Hausa manufacturing and the trade intermediation of earlier Sahel empires is historically sound.
- The "Moroccan leather" claim is well-attested in secondary scholarship. Hausa-tanned leather was indeed exported to North Africa and reached European markets labeled as Moroccan. The chapter's handling of this is accurate.
- The indigo dyeing process described is consistent with the ethnographic and historical record. The fermentation, the alkaline solution, the oxidation process are all correctly rendered. The guild secrecy around recipes is documented.
- The description of the enslaved-labor economy is honest and largely accurate. The differentiation of enslaved roles (workshop labor, porters, domestic labor, farm labor, trans-Saharan trade) is correct. The system was not uniform, and the chapter's portrayal of these gradations is historically responsible.
- The connection between Zazzau's military campaigns and the supply of enslaved people and raw materials to the urban economy is accurate. The southern frontier trade was the supply chain for the manufacturing system.
- The kola trade is correctly placed: kola nuts moved north from the forest zone to the Saharan traders. However, the chapter gives the kola trade only a passing mention. The kola-salt-cloth triangle was a more significant commercial structure than the chapter implies.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter avoids romanticizing the manufacturing economy while also avoiding moralization about its extractive base. This is the correct approach.
- The trade routes and their management by the Tuareg are correctly attributed.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The integration of production, extraction, and trade into a single system is the chapter's strongest analytical achievement. The dye pits and the slave caravans as the same economy is historically correct and too often separated in popular accounts.
- The "Moroccan leather" passage is a genuinely effective way to convey the reach and the invisibility of Hausa manufacturing in the outside world's understanding.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter mentions the Songhai Empire's fall (implicitly, through the redirected trade routes) but does not explicitly identify the Moroccan invasion of 1591 as a factor in the Hausa states' commercial expansion. This is an important causal link: the destruction of the Songhai state redirected trans-Saharan trade routes eastward through Hausa territory, significantly boosting Kano and Katsina's commercial position. This should be made explicit.
- The kola trade deserves slightly more attention. It was not just a commodity that "traveled north to the Saharan traders." It was a stimulant with ritual and social significance across the Sahel and the Sahara, and its trade was a major component of the commercial economy that the chapter describes.
- The trans-Saharan slave trade component of the economy could be more specifically placed within the broader trans-Saharan commercial system. Enslaved people were not just labor for local workshops; they were a commodity traded alongside cloth and leather in the Saharan exchange.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS
Conditions: (1) Explicitly mention the Songhai collapse (1591) as a factor in the Hausa commercial expansion. (2) Give the kola trade slightly more weight. (3) The enslaved-people-as-commodity dimension of the trans-Saharan trade should be acknowledged more specifically.
