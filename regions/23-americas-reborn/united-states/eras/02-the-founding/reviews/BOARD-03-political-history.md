# Board Review — B-03: Early American Political History
## United States Era 2: The Founding — The Experiment Begins

**Reviewer profile:** Specialist in early American political history and the Federalist-Republican rivalry; has published on Hamilton's Treasury program, the Alien and Sedition Acts, and the election of 1800.

---

### Factual Accuracy Assessment

**Hamilton's Treasury program:** The chapter describes Hamilton's economic program accurately: assumption of state war debts, Bank of the United States, "Report on Manufactures" (1791), protective tariffs, subsidies for manufacturing. The characterization of Jefferson's opposition — "a class of financiers dependent on government favor," driving farmers into factories, reproducing England's social conditions — is consistent with Jefferson's actual stated positions. The chapter does not caricature either side.

**The Alien and Sedition Acts:** The chapter correctly dates these to 1798 and correctly identifies Matthew Lyon of Vermont as a prosecuted journalist. The description of Lyon's sentence (four months) is correct. The detail that "he ran for reelection from his jail cell and won" is historically documented. The characterization of Jefferson and Madison's response (Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions, arguing states could nullify federal laws) is accurate. The observation that "the argument about states' rights and federal power that would eventually contribute to the Civil War had its first formal statement in Jefferson's response to the Alien and Sedition Acts" is historically justified — scholars including James Morton Smith (*Freedom's Fetters*, 1956) make this connection.

**The election of 1800:** The chapter states "thirty-six ballots across six days." The precise count is thirty-six ballots over six days (February 11–17, 1801). The chapter's characterization of Hamilton's intervention — letters to Federalist representatives, the argument about Burr's character versus Jefferson's — is historically documented in Hamilton's correspondence. The phrase Hamilton "chose the institution over his faction" is an interpretive characterization, but it is a defensible one based on Hamilton's letters, which emphasize that Burr represented a threat to the constitutional order rather than merely a political opponent.

**One factual correction required:** The chapter states that Adams "left Washington before dawn on a stagecoach." This is accurate in spirit — Adams did leave before the inauguration — but the detail "before dawn" is not conclusively documented. Some accounts have Adams leaving in the early morning; others simply note he left before the ceremony. Recommend changing to: "Adams left Washington before the inauguration. He did not attend." This is unambiguous and documented.

**Hamilton's duel:** The chapter states Hamilton "fired into the air, or perhaps he simply missed." This hedging is historically appropriate: the question of whether Hamilton deliberately wasted his shot (fired into the air, or held his fire until Burr shot) versus whether he simply missed is genuinely unresolved. The chapter's "or perhaps he simply missed" is the right scholarly caution.

---

### Political-System Assessment

**The founding paradox as structural engine:** The chapter's thesis — that the Three-Fifths Compromise and the slavery protections were not errors but structural features of the constitutional machine — is consistent with the best current constitutional scholarship. Edmund Morgan's *American Slavery, American Freedom* (1975) and David Brion Davis's *The Problem of Slavery in the Age of Revolution* (1975) both argue versions of this position. The chapter does not overstate by claiming the founders "designed" the paradox; it correctly states that the compromise was the price of union and that the founders knew the price they were paying.

**The Constitution-as-scripture observation:** The chapter describes the Constitution's quasi-religious authority in American political culture ("constitutional" and "unconstitutional" as moral categories). This is a well-documented phenomenon in American political science, analyzed by scholars including Robert Bellah (*The Broken Covenant*, 1975) and Sanford Levinson (*Constitutional Faith*, 1988). The chapter handles this observation accurately and at the right level of abstraction for its format.

**The peaceful transfer of 1801:** The chapter's treatment of Jefferson's inaugural ("We are all Republicans, we are all Federalists") and Adams's departure is historically accurate. The characterization of the transfer as "not inevitable" and "not easy" is appropriate — historians including John Ferling (*Adams vs. Jefferson*, 2004) have argued that the republic's survival of the 1800 election was not guaranteed and represented a genuine constitutional achievement.

---

### Recommendation

**Factual correction required:** Replace "before dawn on a stagecoach" with "before the inauguration" — the dawn detail is not conclusively documented.

**The chapter's political history is accurate, well-sourced, and analytically sound.** The treatment of the Federalist-Republican conflict, the Alien and Sedition Acts, and the election of 1800 is consistent with the best current scholarship. The Hamilton characterization is fair: he is presented as brilliant, combative, and capable of choosing the republic over his faction, without being romanticized. Jefferson is presented as politically brilliant and personally contradictory, without being made into either a hero or a villain.

**Endorsement with one required correction.** Approved for FINAL after correction.
