## Assessment of the "Franchise" Model

The chapter uses "franchise" as its organizing metaphor for the Durrani confederation's consensus-distribution mechanism. I want to address this directly because it is doing substantial analytical work in the chapter.

The metaphor is not one I use in my own scholarship, but it captures something real and important: the conditional nature of the khans' loyalty, the expectation of returns, the fact that the arrangement functioned without a bureaucratic state structure. In *Afghanistan: A Cultural and Political History*, I describe the Durrani system as a confederation in which Ahmad Shah served as "first among equals" rather than as a state executive — the franchise metaphor is a different language for the same observation. It works as a narrative device precisely because it is not an academic term: it makes the conditional-loyalty mechanism legible to readers who have no background in tribal political theory.

My only caution: the franchise metaphor should not imply that the arrangement was primarily commercial in character. The khans' loyalty was rooted in the code of Pashtunwali (the obligations of nang, the calculation of what their participation entitles them to) as much as in instrumental calculation about returns. The chapter handles this correctly by grounding the metaphor in Pashtunwali from the beginning — the franchise language appears AFTER the Pashtunwali section has established the code's logic, so the reader understands the accounting system the franchise operates within.

**No correction required. The metaphor is appropriate and well-grounded.**

## Pashtunwali Rendering

The chapter renders Pashtunwali correctly in its core provisions (melmastia, nanawatai, badal, nang). The characterization as "the operating system of a society that had no effective state enforcement" is accurate and consistent with how I and other anthropologists understand the code's function. It is not romantic (it does not present the code as a pristine ethical system) and it is not condescending (it does not present the code as a primitive substitute for state law). It is an accurate description of a functional legal and social system.

## The Terminal Condition

The chapter's identification of the terminal condition — the legitimacy gap between military reach and administrative capacity — is the analysis I develop at length in *Afghanistan: A Cultural and Political History* (particularly Chapters 3-4). The chapter has arrived at the same conclusion and rendered it in narrative terms that are more accessible than my academic prose while losing none of the analytical precision. The observation that "the assembly had not built a mechanism for choosing the next right man" is exactly right and is the structural explanation for why the Durrani confederation's succession crises were so destructive.

**One addition I would recommend:** The chapter should note, even briefly, that Ahmad Shah himself recognized this problem and made some attempts to solve it — he tried to formalize succession norms and had multiple sons who were groomed for leadership. The attempts failed not because he ignored the problem but because the problem was structural: no formal succession mechanism could override the khans' right to validate (or refuse) the next ruler through the jirga mechanism. The chapter's terminal condition analysis would be strengthened by acknowledging Ahmad Shah's awareness of the problem.

## Factual Assessment

The chapter is historically sound. The concurrence with my co-reviewers on the Barakzai/Mohammadzai terminology correction is noted. The Panipat analysis (Vishwasrao's death as the structural pivot) is consistent with my reading of the sources.

**Board recommendation: APPROVED. Add one sentence on Ahmad Shah's awareness of the succession problem before final lock.**
