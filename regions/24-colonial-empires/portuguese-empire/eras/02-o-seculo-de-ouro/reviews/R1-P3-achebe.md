# PANEL-1 Review — P-3 Chinua Achebe

**Chapter:** Portuguese Empire, Era 2 — O Século de Ouro
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (cultural voice, dignity, authenticity)
**Stage:** R1 — Opening review

---

## First Reading

The opening does something important: it makes Calicut the assessor, not the assessed. The Zamorin's officials who examine da Gama's gifts and find them inadequate are exercising professional judgment within their own competence. The Arab merchants who watch the Portuguese arrivals with "the specific wariness of people who had heard what the cross meant in Spain and North Africa" are reading the situation accurately. These people are inside their own world, not outside da Gama's.

This is the correct starting position for a chapter about the Portuguese Empire from the empire's self-understanding: the self-understanding must include what the empire's presence meant to the worlds it entered. Not as external judgment imposed by the chapter, but as part of what the empire actually was — a system that disrupted existing systems, and those disrupted systems had their own logic.

## On the Empire's Self-Understanding Versus Its Reality

The chapter assignment specifies that this is the empire from "the empire's self-understanding." That framing is carefully chosen and needs careful handling. The Portuguese empire understood itself as Crusade, Christian mission, and legitimate commerce. It did not understand itself as disruption, extraction, and forced displacement — even when it was doing all three. The chapter must tell the empire's story from within this self-understanding without endorsing it and without stepping outside to judge it.

The Lusotropicalism problem is the test case. The opening does not address it; that is correct. But the chapter must. The Lusotropicalism myth — that Portuguese colonialism was more humane because of historical Iberian mixing — was genuinely believed by the Portuguese who maintained it. The chapter should name it from inside the belief system that produced it, not from outside as a correction. What did it feel like to believe you were doing something different? The chapter can show the belief and let the evidence do the rest.

## What the Opening Gets Right

The saudade passage is honest without being sentimental. It does not romanticize the departing sailor. It names the structural condition — departure under Crown command, the economics of fortune or death — and only then names the feeling. This is the correct approach. The empire's emotional cost was real; treating it as mere romanticism would be a different kind of distortion.

The opening is careful about what it does not say. It does not say the Zamorin's system was better than the Portuguese. It does not say da Gama's arrival was a disaster. It shows a functioning world encountering a new and disruptive presence and leaves the assessment to the reader.

## The Question I Would Push On

The chapter must give real presence to the people who are being displaced or extracted from at the feitorias. The Gujarati merchants who lost the Hormuz route when Albuquerque took it. The Malay merchants who lost Malacca. The people at these points are not background; they are the measure of what the empire's maintenance logic actually cost. The chapter can show this from the empire's own records — da Gama's own accounts, Albuquerque's letters — because the empire recorded what it encountered even when it was not reflecting on what it was doing to it. Let the empire's own records be the evidence. Do not step outside to editorialize.
