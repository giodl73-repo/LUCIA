# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Panel-2 Review (Targeted)

**Focus:** Kalingan voice; the "Nile to Tigris" comparison; notes plan for cultural ownership

## On the Kalingan Voice

The notes plan to give Kalinga presence through landscape and through the Separate Kalinga Rock Edicts, and to acknowledge honestly that their story is told only through their conqueror's words. This is the right approach. It is honest, and honesty about the record's limits deepens trust rather than breaking it (per the rubric's Honest Limits amendment).

But I want more precision. SRE 1 opens: "Beloved of the Gods commands thus: all men are my children. As for my own children I desire that they may be provided with all the welfare and happiness of this world and of the next, so do I desire for all men." This is Ashoka speaking TO the Kalingans. The voice is paternalist. The conquered are "my children." The chapter must let this paternalism be visible without editorializing about it. Let the edict speak. Let the reader hear what the Kalingans heard.

**Suggested technique:** Present the Separate Kalinga Rock Edict in the same way the opening presents RE 13. Let the edict's own voice reveal the power dynamic. "All men are my children" is simultaneously an expression of care and an assertion of ownership. The reader will see the tension without being told.

## On the Nile-to-Tigris Comparison

The notes plan to fix this. Good. My suggestion: replace the external comparison with an internal one. Within the Indic tradition, kings carved prashastis (praise-poems) and rajashasanas (royal decrees). Ashoka's edicts break from this tradition by confessing failure rather than celebrating victory. The comparison should be: within the grammar of Indian royal inscription, what Ashoka did was unprecedented. Not "no king from the Nile to the Tigris," but "no rajashasana before this one had carved a king's sorrow."

## On the War Section Plan

The notes propose 900 words for the war. This is enough. But the Kalingans must be more than casualties. They were an independent people who had resisted Maurya incorporation. The Arthashastra's mandala theory would have classified them as an ari (enemy) in Magadha's strategic calculus. Present Kalinga through Maurya strategic categories: the ari who must be subdued. Then let the edicts reveal what the categories could not contain.

## Remaining Concern

The succession question (Ashoka's possible violence against his brothers) is noted as an open question. My counsel: include it briefly. It grounds the turn. A man who came to power through violence and then conquered through violence and then carved his grief into stone is more human than a man who came to power cleanly and was simply shocked by his first war. The Divyavadana tradition may be legendary, but the edicts' silence on his succession is itself eloquent.
