# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Panel Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Arameans — Era 2: The Age of the Aramean States

## What Works

The voice is inside. The opening does not explain the Arameans to an outside audience. It places the reader at the gate of Dammasq and lets the world's logic emerge through a specific transaction. The bazaar concession is described as the Arameans and Israelites understood it: a commercial arrangement, not an exotic custom requiring anthropological framing.

The use of Dammasq rather than "Damascus" throughout is correct. The culture's own name leads. The parenthetical Western name does not appear because the opening does not need it. This is exactly right.

"The language is the language of traders. Not of warriors, not of priests." This sentence works because it is an observation that the people themselves would have recognized. The Aramean merchants knew their language was the language of commerce. They did not need a historian to tell them. The sentence belongs to the world it describes.

The Assyrian annals are used as evidence without allowing the Assyrian perspective to colonize the narrative. "The Assyrians noticed. They recorded it because it mattered." The Aramean military capacity is established through the fact that Assyria took it seriously, but the story remains Aramean.

## What I Would Push On

The Israelite text (1 Kings 20:34) is a valuable primary source, but the opening relies on it heavily for the framing of the negotiation scene. This is an Israelite source, recording an Israelite king's perspective. The Arameans left no equivalent text for this event. The chapter should acknowledge this asymmetry rather than using the Israelite source as if it were a neutral record. The Hebrew Bible is a source, but it is a source with a perspective, and that perspective is not Aramean. The author uses it well, but the opening would be more honest if it noted, even briefly, that we are hearing this negotiation through Israelite memory.

There is a subtle risk in the closing paragraph. "The people who spoke it were just beginning to understand that the road they had built... was more durable than anything they could have built in stone." This attributes a historical consciousness to the Arameans that they may not have had. Did the Aramean merchants of the tenth century BCE understand that their language would outlast their states? Almost certainly not. The observation is the author's, projected onto the culture. It is a mild form of stepping outside, and it should be cut or reframed.

## The Question I'd Push On

**Is the closing paragraph the author's observation or the culture's own understanding?** If the Arameans did not think of their language as their monument, the chapter should not tell us they did. Show the language spreading. Let the reader draw the conclusion. The culture does not need the author to explain its own significance.
