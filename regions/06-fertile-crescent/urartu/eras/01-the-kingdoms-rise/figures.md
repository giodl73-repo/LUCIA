# Figures — The Kingdom's Rise

## Sarduri I, Son of Lutipri

He exists in one inscription. The Akkadian text on the Van rock names him, names his father, and gives him the titulature of an Assyrian king. No other text from his own kingdom mentions him. The Assyrian annals of Shalmaneser III refer to a king of Urartu whom the Assyrians fought in the mid-ninth century, and the identification with Sarduri is probable but not certain.

What the single inscription reveals is a decision. The highland had kings before Sarduri, unnamed in the stone, known only through Assyrian campaign reports that describe raids against "the lands of Nairi" and "the kings of Uruatri." Sarduri was the first to carve. The act of inscription was the act of founding: not the first ruler of the highland peoples, but the first to claim the highland as a kingdom in a medium that would last. He chose the enemy's language because no other was available for writing, and the choice tells us that he understood what writing did. It made a claim permanent. It placed a name in stone where weather and memory could not erase it.

The citadel walls at Tushpa are attributed to his reign by the archaeological stratigraphy and by the inscription's placement on the lower courses of the wall construction. He was a builder before he was a writer. The cyclopean masonry of the earliest phase at Tushpa is the physical record of a king who understood that a kingdom begins with a defensible position and a wall that holds.

No image of Sarduri survives. No personal detail. He is the inscription and the wall: a voice in borrowed words, a foundation in native stone.

## Ishpuini, Son of Sarduri

The king who gave the kingdom its language and its god. Ishpuini's inscriptions appear in both Akkadian and Urartian, and the Urartian texts are among the earliest surviving examples of the language in written form. The transition from his father's purely Akkadian inscriptions to his own bilingual and then Urartian texts spans perhaps a single generation, suggesting that the adaptation of cuneiform to Urartian was either a rapid innovation of his court scribes or the formalization of a system that had been developing without surviving records.

Ishpuini's most significant act was the organization of the pantheon. The inscription at Meher Kapisi, carved jointly with his son Menua, lists the gods of Biainili in order of precedence and prescribes the sacrifices due to each. Haldi stands first. The hierarchy was not merely religious. It was political: by placing Haldi above all other gods, Ishpuini placed the king who served Haldi above all other authorities in the highland. The divine order reflected the political order, and both were carved into the same stone.

Ishpuini campaigned, as all Urartian kings campaigned, and his inscriptions record victories in the territories around Lake Urmia to the southeast. But his campaigns are less significant than his inscriptional innovations. He gave the kingdom the tools of its own self-description: a written language, a divine hierarchy, an inscriptional formula. Every king who followed him used what he built.

## Menua, Son of Ishpuini

The canal builder. Menua's inscriptions are the most numerous of any early Urartian king, and they record a reign of energetic construction: fortresses, temples, canals, storerooms. The canal that bears his name, running over fifty kilometers from the mountains south of Lake Van to the agricultural plain on the lake's eastern shore, is the most significant hydraulic engineering project in the Urartian record and one of the most substantial in the ancient Near East.

The canal survived Menua. It survived the kingdom. It survived, in some form, for nearly three thousand years, carrying water to the Van plain long after the people who built it had been forgotten. Local tradition attributed it to Semiramis, the legendary Assyrian queen, because the name Menua had been lost. The misattribution speaks to the canal's durability: the builders were forgotten, but the water still ran.

Menua also expanded the fortress network and campaigned against the highland peoples to the north and northwest, in the territories the inscriptions call Diauehi and Eriahi. His campaigns extended Urartian territory and produced the captive populations that would be resettled as agricultural laborers on newly irrigated land. The pattern of campaign, deportation, and resettlement that Menua practiced and his son Argishti extended was the mechanism by which the fortress-canal cycle expanded: each new territory required workers; each campaign produced them.

What the record does not reveal is the man behind the building program. The inscriptions are formulaic: "By the greatness of Haldi, Menua, son of Ishpuini, built this." The formula repeats across dozens of inscriptions. Menua is visible as a builder and a campaigner. Whether the relentless construction was driven by strategic calculation, by personal ambition, by Haldi's theologians, or by the fortress-canal logic that rewarded expansion, the stone does not say. The stone says: he built.

## Argishti I, Son of Menua

The founder of Erebuni. Argishti's reign represents the organizational peak of the kingdom's founding phase. His inscriptions record campaigns across a wide territory, from the lands of Hatti and Tsupani in the west to the Araxes valley in the north, and the founding of fortress-cities that extended Urartian control into the Ararat plain, over two hundred kilometers from the Van heartland.

The Erebuni foundation inscription is the most complete Urartian building text from this period. It names the fortress, names the king, records the god's authority, and specifies the 6,600 deportees settled at the site. The number is precise, which suggests an administrative apparatus capable of counting, recording, and managing large population transfers. Argishti's kingdom was not a raiding polity but a state: it counted its people, assigned them to places, stored their surplus, and recorded the transactions in stone.

Erebuni itself, excavated in the twentieth century near modern Yerevan, reveals a fortress-city of considerable sophistication: a citadel with a temple to Haldi, administrative quarters, workshops, and the massive storerooms that are the signature of Urartian architecture. The wall paintings at Erebuni, fragmentary but recoverable, show geometric and figural designs in red, blue, and white, the earliest known examples of monumental painting in the Armenian highland.

Argishti's inscriptions are confident. The kingdom under his rule controlled territory from Lake Van to the Araxes, held a network of fortresses connected by maintained roads, fed its population through a canal system that transformed the highland landscape, and recorded all of this in its own language on its own stone. The founding was complete. What followed, the expansion and the reckoning with Assyria, would test what the founders had built.

## The Masons and Canal Builders

They have no names. The inscriptions name kings and gods. They do not name the hands that cut the stone.

The evidence of their skill is the work itself. The cyclopean masonry at Tushpa: basalt blocks weighing several tons, cut to fit without mortar, stable after nearly three thousand years. The canal: a gradient maintained over fifty kilometers of broken terrain, the channel cut through volcanic rock with bronze and iron tools, the aqueducts spanning ravines. The storerooms: hundreds of clay jars sunk into the floors, sealed with stamped lids, capable of holding wine and grain through the highland winter.

The tool marks survive. On the basalt blocks at Tushpa, the parallel grooves of the point chisel are still visible, the signature of an individual hand that held a tool against a rock three thousand years ago. The technique required knowledge passed from mason to mason: which angle of strike splits basalt cleanly, which fractures it; how to read the grain of a volcanic rock that has no grain in the way that limestone does; how to dress a contact surface flat enough that two multi-ton blocks can bear their combined weight without shifting.

The canal builders possessed a different knowledge: how to read terrain for gradient, how to calculate the slope needed to move water at a useful rate without eroding the channel, how to cut a trapezoidal cross-section that resists the outward pressure of the water. This was engineering without writing. No Urartian text describes the method. The method is in the canal: in its gradient, its cross-section, its stone lining, its aqueducts. The builders wrote their knowledge in water and stone, and the water and stone held it.
