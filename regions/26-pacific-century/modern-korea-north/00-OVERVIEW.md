# 조선민주주의인민공화국 — Joseon Minjujuui Inmin Gonghwaguk: The Democratic People's Republic of Korea

The DPRK understands itself as the legitimate Korea. Not North Korea — the term the outside world uses — but Joseon, the name of the Korean dynasty that ruled the peninsula for five centuries before Japanese colonization, the name that Koreans themselves had used before the Japanese replaced it. The Democratic People's Republic of Korea is, in its own self-understanding, the state that continued the Korean resistance to Japanese colonization, that defeated American imperialism in the "Fatherland Liberation War" (what the rest of the world calls the Korean War), and that has maintained Korean national sovereignty against the encirclement of hostile powers ever since. The Kim family dynasty — three generations of Kims who have governed the country since its founding — is not, in this framework, a dynastic anomaly. It is the guardian of the revolution.

The North Korea visible to the outside world — the nuclear program, the famines, the prison camps (kwan-li-so), the elaborate personality cult, the mass spectacles, the defectors' testimonies — is a real and documented system. The North Korea that exists from inside — the people who sincerely believe in the Juche philosophy of self-reliance, who experienced the "Arduous March" famine of the 1990s and survived it, who have built their lives around the structures the state provides, who may genuinely revere the Supreme Leader or may perform reverence as a survival strategy that has become indistinguishable from belief — is also real, and less visible. Both are necessary to understand what the DPRK is.

## What Defined Them

**Juche — the philosophy of self-reliance.** Juche (주체 — literally "subject," "main body," "self-reliance") is the official ideology of the DPRK, codified in the Juche Idea attributed to Kim Il-sung. In its official formulation, Juche holds that man is the master of everything and determines everything; that the motive force of social development is the independent, creative, and conscious activity of the masses led by the Great Leader; and that self-reliance in politics, economy, and national defense is the correct principle for socialist construction. In practice, Juche has served as the ideological justification for the Kim family's absolute authority, for the country's isolation from external influence, and for the nuclear weapons program that its proponents describe as the ultimate expression of national self-reliance. Whether Juche is a genuine philosophy, an ideological cover for dynastic rule, or some combination of both is a question the outside world cannot fully answer from outside.

**The Kim dynasty — songbun and the loyalty state.** The DPRK organizes its 25 million citizens into 51 categories of songbun (성분 — social origin and loyalty classification), ranging from the "core" class (those with impeccable revolutionary lineage and reliable loyalty) through "wavering" to "hostile" classes. Songbun classification determines access to food, housing, education, employment, and geographic mobility. It is inherited: the grandchildren of "hostile" classification people — landlords, religious practitioners, those with South Korean relatives — remain classified as hostile. The system is the technological infrastructure of the loyalty state: a society organized around the production of observable loyalty, where disloyalty is punished across generations. It is also, from the regime's perspective, rational: the system produces a population whose survival depends on the appearance of loyalty, which is, for most practical purposes, indistinguishable from loyalty itself.

**The nuclear program as national sovereignty.** The DPRK's nuclear weapons program — which has produced an estimated 40–50 warheads and the ballistic missiles to deliver them — is not irrational in its own terms. The Kim regime's analysis is that Muammar Gaddafi gave up Libya's weapons of mass destruction program in 2003 and was killed by NATO-backed forces in 2011; Saddam Hussein had no WMD program and was invaded and killed; the lesson is that nuclear weapons are the only guarantee of regime survival against US military force. Whether or not this analysis is correct about US intentions, it produces a coherent strategic posture: the nuclear program is non-negotiable because it is the insurance policy. The six nuclear tests and successive missile tests are the proof of concept; the diplomatic engagement (the Singapore Summit with Trump in 2018) is the negotiation over what it costs to keep the insurance policy in place.

**The prison camps — the kwan-li-so.** The DPRK operates a system of political prison camps — kwan-li-so — that current estimates place at 4–6 functioning facilities holding 80,000–120,000 people. Satellite imagery confirms the existence of the facilities; survivor testimony describes the conditions (forced labor, inadequate food, arbitrary execution, systematic torture). The camps hold not just political offenders but their families — the principle of collective punishment extends to three generations. The kwan-li-so system is not peripheral to the DPRK's governance; it is central to it: the threat of the camps is what makes the loyalty system function. A society in which disloyalty means the destruction of your family is a society with a powerful incentive for performed loyalty.

## The Chapters

**1 — Kim Il-sung Returns:** September 19, 1945: a 33-year-old man steps off a Soviet ship in Wonsan harbor. He is introduced to the North Korean population as Kim Il-sung — the legendary guerrilla commander who fought the Japanese. Some who knew the historical Kim Il-sung doubt this is the same man. The Soviet-backed installation of a compliant Korean communist leader is not presented to the Korean people as a Soviet decision; it is presented as the homecoming of a hero. The state that follows will be organized around this founding fiction.

**2 — The War:** December 1950: US Marines are retreating from the Chosin Reservoir in temperatures of 40 below zero. Chinese forces have entered the war. The UN advance that had nearly reached the Yalu River is being reversed. The war that was supposed to end by Christmas will last another two and a half years. When the armistice is signed in July 1953, every city in North Korea has been destroyed. Pyongyang has been bombed more completely than Dresden. The "Fatherland Liberation War" has ended not in the reunification Kim Il-sung began it to achieve but in the same division where it started, with three to five million dead.

**3 — The Personality Cult:** Visitors to the Kumsusan Palace of the Sun in Pyongyang, where the embalmed bodies of Kim Il-sung and Kim Jong-il lie in state, must dress appropriately, must bow before the bodies, must not speak. The palace is the most elaborate expression of the DPRK's state religion: the deification of the Kim family as the embodiment of Korean national consciousness. The thousands of statues, the portraits in every room of every building, the daily study sessions devoted to Kim Il-sung's teachings — all of these are not merely propaganda in the Western sense. They are the liturgy of a state religion in which political and sacred authority are identical.

**4 — The Arduous March:** 1996: a family in North Hamgyong Province has eaten their last food. The state distribution system has collapsed since the withdrawal of Soviet support in 1991. The father has already left to search for food in China. The mother is deciding what the children can eat. The estimates of the number who died in the famine of 1994–1998 range from 600,000 to 3 million — perhaps 3–13% of the population. The state that had organized every aspect of life could not feed its people. The private markets (jangmadang) that emerged from the famine — the informal economy that let people survive despite the state — are the most significant structural change in North Korean society since the founding.

**5 — The Singapore Summit:** June 12, 2018: Kim Jong-un and Donald Trump meet at the Capella Hotel on Sentosa Island, Singapore. The handshake is unprecedented: no sitting US president has met with a North Korean leader. The photograph is extraordinary. The agreement is vague. The denuclearization process that the summit is supposed to begin does not begin. By 2019, negotiations have stalled. By 2022, North Korea has resumed missile testing at an unprecedented rate. The summit was real; its consequences were not what either side hoped.

**6 — The Closed State:** 2022: North Korea has been more closed to the outside world than at any point since the Korean War. COVID-19 became the justification for sealing the border with China — the country's economic lifeline — in January 2020. The economy has contracted severely. The jangmadang markets have been suppressed. Information from inside is extremely limited. What is known: Kim Jong-un has consolidated power absolutely; his sister Kim Yo-jong has emerged as a significant figure; the nuclear program continues; the people are suffering. What is not known is the internal state of a society that has survived worse and continues to survive.
