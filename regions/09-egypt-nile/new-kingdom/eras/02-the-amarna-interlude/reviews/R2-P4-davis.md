## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of Notes (Panel-2)

**Focus:** Inside-voice rendering of Atenist theology and sensory plan.

**Assessment:** The notes correctly identify the Great Hymn as the primary text for inhabiting the Atenist vision. The plan to render it "from inside, not as comparative religion" is the right instinct. But the execution plan is vague.

**What needs attention:**

1. The Great Hymn to the Aten should not be summarized. It should be quoted (in translation) and rendered through sensory experience. The Hymn describes animals, birds, fish, the fledgling in the egg, the ships on the river. It is not an abstract theological statement. It is a sensory catalogue of the world as the Aten illuminates it. The chapter should let the Hymn speak in its own register and build the Atenist worldview from its images, not from the author's exposition.

2. The open temple at Akhetaten is a critical sensory fact. Traditional Egyptian temples were dark, enclosed, mysterious. The Great Aten Temple was open to the sky: no roof, no sanctuary, the sun falling directly on the altars. This is not an architectural footnote. It IS the theology made physical. The temple's form must be rendered as the Aten's worldview in stone.

3. The Amarna art section needs more than "elongated figures, intimate family scenes." The art shows Akhenaten and Nefertiti kissing their daughters, holding them on their laps, the rays of the Aten touching all of them. This is unprecedented in Egyptian royal art. It is a visual argument that the royal family IS the model of the Aten's relationship with humanity. The art must be read from inside that argument, not as an aesthetic curiosity.

4. The workers' village at Akhetaten: Kemp's excavation shows that the workers who built the new city maintained traditional religious practices in their homes. Small Amun amulets, Bes figurines, Taweret statuettes. The official theology was the Aten. The lived theology was mixed. This is essential evidence and it should appear somewhere in Sections III or V.

**Verdict:** The production bible needs sharper sensory planning for Akhetaten and a commitment to rendering the Great Hymn from inside the Atenist vision. The workers' village evidence of private devotion is a crucial counterpoint that the chapter must include.
