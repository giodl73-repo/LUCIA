## Historical Accuracy Assessment

The chapter's representation of Aka medicinal plant knowledge is accurate at the structural level. The claim that Aka women's knowledge specifies which part of the plant, in which season, prepared how, in what quantity — this reflects the fieldwork record. The knowledge is indeed held primarily by women, though men hold significant botanical knowledge as well; the chapter's use of Ekali as the carrier is appropriate.

The description of the forest-farming exchange relationship is accurate. The exchange is correctly described as a relationship of ongoing obligations rather than market exchange, and the structural asymmetry is accurately characterized without importing modern political-economy framing. The note that the iron tools the Baka needed were harder to substitute than the forest products the farmers needed is a defensible claim given the historical record.

**Factual flag:** The chapter describes the farming arrival as "gradual, across generations that none of the Baka alive in any single moment could trace from beginning to end." The Bantu expansion and its interface with forest peoples is more complex than "gradual" suggests — there were periods of rapid movement and periods of stability, and the contact situations varied enormously across geography. The chapter's use of "gradual" is not wrong, but it should perhaps say "across generations" without "gradual" specifically, since the pace varied considerably.

**Minor flag:** "Specific Baka families developed ongoing obligations with specific farming families." This is accurate for the exchange relationship as it eventually developed, but the specific kinship-like ties (called by various names in different areas) are a developed form of the relationship, not necessarily its earliest form. The chapter might specify "as the exchange deepened" or "over time" to avoid implying these specific structures were present from the first contact.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

The composite figure method is disclosed honestly in figures.md. The use of Aka and Mbuti material for figures within their respective sections is appropriate.

The Ekali figure is built from Aka material (Bahuchet fieldwork) but carries the ekila concept from Lewis's Mbendjele work. This composite requires the disclosure made in figures.md; the chapter itself does not explain the distinction, which is correct editorial practice (the chapter should not step outside to address its sources), but the figures.md disclosure is essential and is present.

The chapter correctly identifies the polyphony as Aka; does not incorrectly attribute it to the Mbuti or Baka as a shared practice. The diversity within forest peoples is handled with appropriate care.

**Endorsement:** The chapter represents the Aka and Baka traditions with accuracy and dignity. The relational epistemology framing — knowledge as right relationship with the forest, maintained through correct practice — is an accurate representation of how the forest peoples themselves understand their knowledge systems, insofar as that understanding can be conveyed through outside scholarship.

---

## Recommendation

Accept with minor note: consider "across generations" rather than "gradual arrival" to avoid implying a uniform pace. This is a small textual adjustment, not a factual error.
