# PANEL-1 — P-4 Wade Davis
## R1-P4-davis.md

**Chapter:** Aragon Era 2 — La Reconquesta Aragonesa
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (opening review)
**Reviewer:** Wade Davis

---

The opening identifies something real: the Moors who remained in Valencia after 1238 held a knowledge system that the Aragonese could not replicate. The acequia irrigation network — fed from mountain sources through hundreds of kilometers of channels, distributing water according to schedules and rights that had been negotiated over generations — is one of the great hydraulic achievements of medieval Europe. The silk trade, the orange cultivation, the rice paddies: these are not generic "agricultural knowledge." They are a specific, sophisticated, technically complex set of practices built on particular soils, particular water regimes, particular species selected over centuries of cultivation.

The opening gestures at this. "Acequia systems," "Moorish agricultural knowledge," "silk merchants" — the vocabulary is present. My concern is that the chapter, as it develops, must render this knowledge at method level. Not just that the Moors had valuable knowledge, but what that knowledge was. How the acequia amin — the water judge — managed distribution disputes. What the planting calendar looked like. What the silk cultivation required in terms of mulberry groves and specific temperature management. The knowledge must be shown operating, not asserted as existing.

**What is working:**

The opening avoids the trap of presenting the mudéjar arrangement as European tolerance. It does not say James was enlightened or unusually humane. It says he was calculating, and the calculation was correct. This is the right frame. The Moorish agricultural and commercial knowledge was maintained for centuries under Aragonese rule because the Aragonese system of compact — the *Furs de València*, the mudéjar courts — protected the conditions under which that knowledge could continue to operate. This is a knowledge-preservation story as much as a political story, and the opening has its eyes on that.

The specific detail of the acequia engineers and the Catalan commercial intelligence as parallel knowledge systems — one agrarian and hydraulic, the other financial and navigational — is exactly the kind of parallel that the chapter should develop. Two different epistemological traditions operating simultaneously under the Crown of Aragon's umbrella. That is genuinely interesting.

**What I'd push on:**

The chapter must be careful not to present the Moorish agricultural knowledge simply as a resource that the Aragonese chose to preserve because it was useful. That framing — however accurate in political terms — risks rendering the mudéjars as a living tool rather than as people who continued to maintain a complex, living knowledge system according to their own understanding of what it was for. The acequia system was not just irrigation. It had cosmological and social dimensions. Water rights in Islamic agricultural communities were governed by specific legal traditions. The water judges operated within a jurisprudential tradition that understood water distribution as a form of justice, not merely efficiency.

**The question I'd push on:**

If we removed the Aragonese political framework entirely — if we simply followed one acequia amin through a single day's work in Valencia in 1240 — what would we see? What decisions would he make? What knowledge would he draw on? That figure — his body, his expertise, his understanding of his own practice — is the knowledge system made visible. The chapter should find him.

The opening is clean and earns its passage. The chapter's intellectual challenge is to honor the mudéjar knowledge system with the same depth it gives the Aragonese constitutional system.
