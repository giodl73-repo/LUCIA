# BOARD Review — Piotr Steinkeller
## Assyriology, Sumerian Civilization, Ur III Period
## Harvard University

---

I am reviewing this chapter from the vantage of a specialist in Sumerian civilization and the Ur III period — the culture that the Elamites destroyed in 2004 BCE and whose own sources provide much of what we know about Elamite actions in this era. I come to this as someone who has spent decades with the Sumerian materials and who therefore brings a particular interest in whether this chapter handles the Elamite-Mesopotamian interface honestly.

**The source asymmetry treatment:**

The chapter's handling of the Kindattu episode is, in my judgment, the most important thing it does, and it does it correctly. "The victim's voice is preserved. The conqueror's is silent." This is accurate. The Lament for the Destruction of Ur is our primary source for what the fall of Ur III looked like from the Mesopotamian side. The Elamite sources that might have given us the other side do not survive in readable form. The chapter acknowledges this directly rather than filling the gap with inference dressed as fact.

What I want to add from the Assyriological side: the Lament for the Destruction of Ur was composed not immediately after the destruction but in the decades following, during the Isin-Larsa period when Mesopotamian city-states were competing to claim Ur's legacy. This means the Lament is as much a political text as a memorial one — it was composed by people with an interest in preserving the memory of Ur's greatness as a claim for their own legitimacy. The chapter does not need to include this complexity, but I note it so the reader understands that the "victim's voice" is itself already a curated, politically motivated preservation. Not a lie — the destruction happened — but a literary construction with its own agenda.

**On the relationship between Elam and Ur III:**

The chapter states that Ur III had been "demanding tribute, imposing its administrative conventions, integrating Elamite regions into its own redistributive network on Ur's terms rather than Elam's." This is broadly correct. Ur III's administrative apparatus included Elamite regions — there are Ur III tablets from sites in southwestern Iran. The relationship was not one of equals. The chapter's characterization of Kindattu's campaign as "the dual kingdom's engine asserting itself against a constraint" is an Elamite-centric reading that is appropriate for this project, but I want to note it as a reading rather than a established historical consensus. The Assyriological community would more commonly describe the Ur III collapse as multi-causal, with the Elamites being one significant factor among several.

**On the contemporaneity of proto-Elamite and early Sumerian writing:**

The chapter states that "the Mesopotamian world to the west, where Sumerian scribes were also pressing clay in this same era, was not the only place on earth where human beings had discovered that a mark could outlast a memory." This is accurate. The relationship between proto-Elamite and early Sumerian writing is still debated — whether they developed independently or in contact — and the chapter's formulation is appropriately agnostic. It does not claim Elamite priority over Sumerian (which would be contentious) nor Sumerian priority over Elamite (which would be unfair). The "also pressing clay in this same era" is the correct level of precision.

**Factual clarification on the sukkalmah:**

The chapter describes the sukkal of Susa as one of the three offices, with the sukkalmah of Elam and Simashki above him, and the sukkal of Elam and Simashki as intermediate. This is accurate for the standard model. I would add for precision that the "Simashki" in the title refers to a geographic entity in the Zagros highlands — it appears to be a highland polity or region that was brought into the Elamite orbit during the Old Elamite period. The title "king of Elam and Simashki" thus names both the lowland Elamite heartland (centered on Susa/Khuzestan) and a highland component. This reinforces the chapter's central argument about the highland-lowland axis as structural: the title itself encodes the dual geography.

**Verdict: APPROVED. The Elamite-centric framing is appropriate and explicit. The source handling is accurate and honest. The Sumerian materials are used correctly. One note on the Lament's political context for the author's awareness. Factual content is sound.**
