Physical Batavia in 1619–1625:

The Ciliwung River ran through the city, and the Dutch built their warehouse district along its banks. The warehouses smelled of pepper, cloves, and the particular mildew of tropical timber that has absorbed too much humidity. The river itself would have smelled of the city's waste and the cargo being loaded and unloaded at the quays. The heat was relentless — Batavia sits six degrees south of the equator, and the humidity in the wet season made cloth rot in the warehouses if it was not properly sealed. European VOC employees died of tropical fever at extraordinary rates; the company factored these deaths into its staffing models.

Coen's Batavia was built on the ruins of the Javanese city of Jayakarta, whose population had been expelled. The Dutch built a canal-grid in the Amsterdam pattern, which proved catastrophically inappropriate for a tropical environment: the canals became vectors for disease. Malaria, dysentery, cholera. The men who maintained the VOC's Asian operations died faster than they could be replaced from the Netherlands, and the company's staffing files at the Nationaal Archief are full of death notices.

This is the sound of the VOC's operations: not only the cannon fire at the Banda Islands but the sound of men dying of fever in the warehouse district of a city they had built on a pattern that did not work for the climate they were in.

The distance mechanism requires both to be present.
