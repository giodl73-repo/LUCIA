# B-Wayúu-3 — Michel Perrin

## Background

Michel Perrin is a French anthropologist who spent decades in fieldwork with the Wayúu, producing the foundational studies of Wayúu cosmology and oral tradition in French (*Le chemin des Indiens morts*, 1976) and English (*The Way of the Dead Indians*, 1987). His work on the yoluha (soul), the Jepira afterlife, and the second burial ceremony remains the primary scholarly reference for Wayúu cosmological practice. He also documented the ecological knowledge systems of the Guajira pastoral economy.

## Key Question

**"Does the chapter represent Wayúu cosmology, the relationship between the material world and the Jepira cycle, and the ecological knowledge of the Guajira pastoral tradition with accuracy?"**

## Review

The chapter's engagement with Wayúu cosmology is present but light, which is appropriate for a chapter focused on the cattle economy and the pütchipü'üi system. The Jepira reference in the mochila section ("a color combination that had come to her in a dream of Jepira") is correctly placed. In Wayúu understanding, the boundary between the living world and Jepira is permeable in dreams; a woman receiving pattern inspiration from a Jepira dream is not making an exceptional claim but a normative one. The chapter does not over-explain this, which is correct.

The ecological knowledge sections are this chapter's strongest contribution from a cultural documentation standpoint. The behavioral observation of cattle vs. goats, the reading of the sky for rain, Seyuuya's knowledge of "the temperature of the east wind, the quality of light over the Serranía, the behavior of the cattle when real moisture is coming" — all of this is consistent with documented Wayúu pastoral knowledge as recorded in my own fieldwork and in the agricultural studies of the Guajira region. The Guajira's pastoral knowledge is empirical, accumulated, and highly sophisticated. The chapter treats it as exactly that.

The dry well is correctly used as a political fact. In my fieldwork, disputes over water access were consistently identified as the most significant sources of inter-eirruku conflict, more significant even than cattle disputes (though cattle disputes and water disputes are usually connected). The chapter's claim that "water becomes the thing that every other transaction is secretly about" is not an outside framing; it reflects the Wayúu's own understanding of the Guajira's organizing scarcity.

One addition the chapter might consider: the Guajira has two wet seasons, not one. The minor wet season (the "little rains" in April-May) and the major wet season (September-November). The chapter refers to the wet season as singular. For a chapter covering 1700-1900, this is a minor ecological imprecision; the "short wet season" mentioned in the opening could specify which one. This is a small point and does not affect the chapter's argument.

The composite figure methodology is sound and honestly presented in figures.md. The use of composite figures for cultures without extensive named individual records is a legitimate ethnographic practice, and the chapter's handling of this is transparent.

**Verdict:** Cosmologically and ecologically accurate. One minor ecological detail (dual wet season) worth noting for precision but not requiring revision. No factual errors in the cultural content.
