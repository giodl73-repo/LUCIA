# Figures — Haebang Era

## Kim Il-sung (or: The Man the Soviets Called Kim Il-sung)

The fundamental mystery of North Korean history begins with this man. On September 19, 1945, he stepped off a Soviet ship and was presented to North Korean communists as Kim Il-sung — a name that carried the weight of resistance, of legend, of hope. Whether he was the actual Kim Il-sung who had fought the Japanese in Manchuria or a carefully selected Soviet proxy remains unknowable. Soviet records are inaccessible. Korean memory is filtered through mythology.

What can be said: the man who became known as Kim Il-sung was born in Mangyongdae in 1912 (by the official North Korean biography) or 1913 (by other accounts). He spent the 1930s fighting in Manchuria, where he may have been killed or captured. He spent the 1940s in the Soviet Union or in Yan'an with the Chinese communists. When he arrived in Korea, he was thirty-three years old.

What he was not: he was not immediately credible. The schoolteacher Im Hae-jo noticed the wrong cheekbones, the trained movement. Others may have noticed. Others may have had doubts. But within five years, through the installation of mythology and the machinery of state repetition, the man became the name. By 1950, it was not possible to separate them. He was Kim Il-sung because the state had decided he was, and the state had the power to make that decision true.

His interior — what he thought, what he believed about himself, whether he knew he was a proxy or whether he had internalized the mythology himself — is unavailable. He left no diaries. He gave no intimate accounts. He lived inside the mythology he represented, and the mythology he represented left no room for an authentic human being underneath. Whether this was tragic or simply the price of survival is not a question the historical record permits us to answer.

By 1950, Kim Il-sung had been transformed into something more than human: a symbol, a father, a leader. The man beneath the symbol, if he ever existed as a separate entity, had been absorbed into the mythology entirely. What remained was the figure that appeared in statues, in photographs, in the lessons of schoolchildren. That figure was real. The man was not.

## Park Hon-yong (The Communist)

Park Hon-yong was the authentic revolution — the Korean communist who had survived underground during the occupation. He was born in 1900 and had been organizing communist cells in Korea since the 1920s. He had watched the Japanese occupation destroy everything. He had kept the Party alive in secret. When the Soviets arrived, Park was in his mid-40s: experienced, committed, and realistic about what communism could mean in a divided peninsula.

Park understood the geometry of power. He knew that the Soviets would not leave unless they had an obedient Korean leader. He knew that the Korean people needed hope. He understood that the name of Kim Il-sung carried that hope. He made the decision — pragmatic, cold, necessary — to accept the man the Soviets offered and to spend the next five years making that man real through state machinery.

Park was effective. He was also subordinated. He would remain the Number Two figure in North Korea throughout the Kim Il-sung era, always below the mythology, never able to transcend it. In the 1950s, after the Korean War, he would lose power and eventually face execution in 1955 — officially for being a South Korean spy, actually for being a living reminder that communism in Korea had come from Koreans, not from the Soviets, and that Kim Il-sung's authority was therefore contingent, not total.

Park's tragedy was that he was real. He had lived through the occupation. He had organized the resistance. He had political legitimacy that did not depend on mythology. But the state that was being built could not have a Number One figure with any legitimacy that was not mythological. And so Park was gradually erased, then executed, then written out of the official history. The Korean communist who had made the revolution possible became a non-person, an unperson, someone who had been decided out of existence.

## Im Hae-jo (The Doubter)

Im Hae-jo was a schoolteacher who had grown up near the village of the original Kim Il-sung. When the man stepped off the ship in 1945, Im Hae-jo recognized that something was wrong. Not obviously wrong. Not provably wrong. But something. The cheekbones. The movement.

This observation — a small, private act of attention — became the founding crime of North Korea. Im Hae-jo spoke his doubt aloud. He said to others what they may have felt but did not voice: this might not be the same man.

The state could not tolerate this. A state built on mythology cannot permit the question that would destroy it. Im Hae-jo was imprisoned. He would spend years in prison for the crime of noticing the contradiction between the mythology and the sensory evidence of his own eyes.

Im Hae-jo's trajectory illustrates the first principle of the North Korean state: doubt is not permitted. Not strategic doubt, not internal debate, not the kind of intellectual disagreement that might strengthen a government. Any doubt about the mythology is a threat to the state. Therefore, Im Hae-jo must be removed. His doubt must be erased. And everyone who might have asked the same question must learn, from his example, not to see what their eyes show them.

Im Hae-jo is known to us only because some accounts survive of his imprisonment. He never wrote a memoir. He never published analysis. He was simply erased and then, long after, half-remembered as a cautionary tale: the man who asked the question the state could not tolerate.

## Soviet Colonel (The Occupier)

The Soviet commander in North Korea — whether we call him Chistiakov or another name — remains a functional figure in this history. He gave an order. A man was presented. A name was announced. The machinery was set in motion. He did not create the mythology, but he created the conditions for it.

The Colonel understood his task: install a communist government, secure the Soviet sphere, prepare for the confrontation with the Americans that was coming. He needed an obedient Korean leader. The man who stepped off the ship would be obedient because he owed his position to Soviet power. The machinery would work.

The Colonel left no record of his personal thoughts. He gave orders. He oversaw the installation of the new state. He reported to Moscow. He was doing his job. From the perspective of Soviet geopolitics, he was correct: Korea would become communist, would remain in the Soviet sphere for four decades, would serve Soviet interests.

The Colonel is a representative figure — the external power that structured Korean choice. The Koreans made the decisions (to accept the mythology, to build the state, to organize the revolution) but they made them within parameters set by Soviet occupation. This is not to say the Korean choices were not real, or that Korean communism was entirely artificial. It is to say that the machinery of mythology, the machinery of belief, was installed under the supervision of an occupying power that needed obedience more than authenticity.

## Korean Mother (Collective Figure)

During the Japanese occupation, a mother taught her daughter Korean words in whispers. The danger was real: teachers had been beaten for speaking Korean. The state had declared war on the language itself. To speak Korean was to resist, but to resist too obviously was to endanger your family.

The mother's generation had lived through the occupation. They knew what language meant. They knew that the occupiers could suppress speech but they could not suppress memory. In whispers, in private, in the spaces the occupiers did not control, Korean continued. The mother kept it alive because she understood that once the language died, the nation was truly dead.

After liberation, this mother emerged into a world where Korean could be spoken openly. The relief was physical, visceral. Her daughter could go to school. The language could be taught. The nation could be rebuilt.

But when the Communist state was established, the mother faced a new requirement: her daughter must now speak Korean in a new way, with new words, new concepts, new meanings. The language had survived occupation. Now it must be reshaped to serve the revolution. The mother watched as her daughter learned the mythology in school — the stories of Kim Il-sung, the lessons of the Party, the grammar of loyalty.

The mother had not been a communist. She had been a resistant, a survivor, someone who kept the nation alive through the occupation. But she recognized that her daughter's survival now depended on accepting the new mythology. She encouraged her daughter to study hard, to learn the lessons, to show loyalty. The mother was making the same bargain that Park Hon-yong was making, that every Korean was making: the mythology is the price of survival. Accept it. Live as if it is true. Teach your children to believe.

By 1950, the mother's daughter was fully formed in the mythology. The daughter did not remember the occupation. The daughter did not remember that language had been suppressed. For the daughter, Korean was revolutionary language, the language of liberation, the language of Kim Il-sung. The mother had given her daughter what she thought was survival. She had also given her daughter a mythology in place of memory.

## Sources and Access

The figures in the Haebang era are characterized by the limitations of the source record. Kim Il-sung's identity is uncertain. Park Hon-yong's eventual erasure means that first-hand accounts of his thinking are sparse. Im Hae-jo is remembered only through the record of his imprisonment. The Soviet colonel is a functional figure whose personal agency remains invisible. The Korean mother is a composite figure, built from oral histories and defector accounts of daily life during the period.

What is available is the mythology itself — the official North Korean records, the state media, the school texts, the accounts of what people were taught to believe. This is both limitation and opportunity: the mythology is primary source material for understanding how belief was installed and maintained.

Access to interior experience — what people actually thought, felt, doubted, believed — is thin. Defector accounts provide some access, but they are filtered through trauma and through the experience of people who ultimately left. Those who remained have not, in most cases, written accounts. What they believed, how they lived inside the mythology, whether they performed or genuinely inhabited it — these questions remain largely closed to us from outside the system.

The challenge of this era is to present the mythology as the Haebang Koreans had to experience it: not as propaganda to be skeptical of, but as truth embedded in institutions, repeated in schools, represented in statues, required by survival. To feel from inside what it means to live inside a founding fiction without the luxury of doubting it.
