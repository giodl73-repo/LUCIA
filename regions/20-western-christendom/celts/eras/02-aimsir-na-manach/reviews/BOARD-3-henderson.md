# BOARD-3 — Irish Manuscript and Visual Tradition Specialist
## Modeled on: George Henderson, *Vision and Image in Early Celtic Christianity* (T&T Clark, 1999) and Bernard Meehan, *The Book of Kells* (Thames & Hudson, 1994)

**Domain:** Irish illuminated manuscripts, the Book of Kells, material culture of the scriptorium, the interlace tradition
**Assignment:** Accuracy of the manuscript and material culture sections; the Book of Kells attribution; the pigment sourcing; the interlace as theological argument

---

## Assessment

**On the Book of Kells attribution:** The chapter states that the Book of Kells was carried from Iona to Kells in Meath following the Viking raids. This is the majority scholarly position, held by Françoise Henry and subsequent Kells scholars. It is not uncontested: some scholars have proposed an origin at another Columban monastery, and the movement from Iona to Kells is recorded in the *Annals of Ulster* (under 806 CE, after a particularly severe raid). The chapter's formulation "the manuscript that would become the Book of Kells" is appropriately hedged, acknowledging that the manuscript's production may have been split between locations. This is accurate.

**On the multiple hands:** The identification of at least three distinct artists in the Book of Kells is the current consensus position, established by Françoise Henry's detailed stylistic analysis and confirmed by subsequent scholars. The "Goldsmith" designation for the artist of the Chi Rho and related pages is Henry's own term and is standard in the literature. The chapter uses this accurately.

**On the pigments:** The identification of ultramarine (lapis lazuli from Badakhshan) as a source for the blue pigment in the Book of Kells is correct, though it should be noted that the blue in the Book of Kells is primarily ultramarine and indigo rather than ultramarine alone. The chapter's formulation is a simplification but not an error. The identification of kermes as the insect dye for red is accurate: kermes vermilio was the source of crimson in Insular manuscripts. The description of glair (beaten egg white) as the adhesive for gold leaf is correct for the period.

**On the interlace as theological argument:** The chapter states: "every line in the pattern folds into every other line without beginning or terminus, and the whole cannot be traced to a source." This is an accurate description of the formal properties of the carpet-page interlace, and the theological reading (the divine name/Word as without beginning or end) is a legitimate interpretation supported by scholarship on early medieval visual theology, including the work of Roger Stalley and Hilary Richardson. The chapter presents this as an interpretation ("The page is an argument, made in the visual logic of interlace") rather than as established fact, which is the correct epistemic stance.

**One factual correction:** The chapter states that the light "fails by half past three in the afternoon" in December at the latitude of Iona or Clonmacnoise. Iona is at approximately 56.3 degrees north latitude, Clonmacnoise at approximately 53.3 degrees north. At 53 degrees north in December, sunset occurs around 3:55 to 4:10 PM (depending on the specific day and cloud cover). At 56 degrees north, it is somewhat earlier, around 3:35 to 3:45 PM. "Half past three" is accurate for Iona but slightly early for Clonmacnoise. Given that the chapter hedges with "roughly 53 degrees north" and that the point (the scriptorium's brief winter daylight) is accurately conveyed, this is a minor imprecision rather than an error. No revision required.

**Overall verdict:** Accurate. One minor imprecision on the December light timing, not requiring revision. No corrections required.
