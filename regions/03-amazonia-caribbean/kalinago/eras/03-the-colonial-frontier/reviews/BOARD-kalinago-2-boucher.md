# Board Review — Philip Boucher
## Kalinago, Era 3: The Colonial Frontier
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Factual Assessment

**Breton's biography and the dictionary:** The chapter states Breton "arrived in the Caribbean in 1635 and spent most of the next twenty years on Dominica." This needs minor calibration. Breton arrived in Martinique in 1635 as part of the first French colonization effort. He subsequently moved between Martinique, Guadeloupe, and Dominica over the next decade. His most sustained contact with Kalinago communities on Dominica occurred in the 1640s, not from 1635. The statement "spent most of the next twenty years on Dominica" slightly overstates his Dominica residence. A more accurate framing: "arrived in the Caribbean in 1635 and spent the better part of the following decade in the Lesser Antilles, his most sustained contact with Kalinago communities occurring on the leeward coast of Dominica in the 1640s."

**The 1627 French expedition:** I can confirm that French attempts to establish a presence on Dominica in the 1620s were met with organized Kalinago resistance and failed. The specific year "1627" should be verified against the colonial records — my recollection is that the relevant expeditions were in the late 1620s and early 1630s, with no single definitive 1627 date. The chapter should treat this as "c. late 1620s" rather than a specific year.

**The failed Spanish colony on Guadeloupe:** The chapter references "a Spanish colony on Guadeloupe, abandoned in 1604." This is approximately correct — Spanish attempts to maintain a presence on Guadeloupe in the early 17th century were repeatedly disrupted by Kalinago resistance, with effective Spanish withdrawal by around 1604–1605. The date is close enough for a narrative chapter but should be noted in SOURCES.md.

**French attempt on St. Vincent in 1626:** The chapter references "a French attempt on St. Vincent in 1626, abandoned after two years." This is consistent with French colonial history, though the precise dating of early French attempts on St. Vincent is difficult to pin down exactly. The 1626 date is within the range of what the sources support.

## The Breton Dictionary Treatment

The chapter uses Breton's dictionary as its primary inside-voice source, presenting dictionary entries as direct evidence of Kalinago attention and knowledge. This technique is appropriate and consistent with how scholars in my field use the dictionary. However, the chapter should note (in SOURCES.md rather than the chapter itself) that Breton's dictionary reflects the Kalinago community of Dominica in the 1640s — a community that had already been significantly affected by European contact and was not representative of the pre-contact Kalinago world. The words are Kalinago words, but the community producing them had already adapted to a generation of colonial pressure.

This is not a reason to avoid using the dictionary — it is the best source we have. But the chapter's phrase "preserves Kalinago vocabulary" should be held alongside the understanding that what is preserved is one community's vocabulary at one moment in time, not a timeless Kalinago lexicon.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

**The African-Kalinago meeting on St. Vincent:** The chapter correctly handles the African-Kalinago encounter as imperfectly documented and contextually significant for the next era. The historical record supports the presence of African communities on St. Vincent from at least the mid-17th century, and the relationship with the Kalinago that produced the Garifuna is documented as a long-term process rather than a single event. The chapter's framing is appropriately hedged.

**The resistance strategies described:** The tactical picture — attack early, use mountain interior, maintain inter-island communication — is consistent with the French and English colonial records of the period. The chapter correctly captures the strategic sophistication of Kalinago resistance rather than presenting it as random violence.
