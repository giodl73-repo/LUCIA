# Panel Review — Round 1
## P-4 Wade Davis | Opening Review

**Era:** The Island Expansion | **Culture:** Arawak / Lokono
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

What strikes me most in this opening is the precision with which the canoe is presented not as a vessel but as an epistemological system. The knowledge of the Orinoco delta's currents — "the channel that ran fast beneath the second sandbar where the anaconda had taken his grandfather's dog — that current was local knowledge, passed through three generations of paddlers who knew it by feel in the dark" — this is not presented as quaint indigenous lore. It is presented as what it actually was: a sophisticated navigational knowledge system that accumulated over generations, was transmitted through bodily practice, and could not be learned from any source other than the experience of paddling those specific waters. The grandparents' dog's death is not colorful detail. It is a datum in the dataset.

The opening understands that indigenous knowledge systems are not simpler than Western knowledge systems; they are differently organized and differently stored. River knowledge, in this case, is stored in the body, in the practice of paddling, in the named features of the landscape that carry the history of what happened there. The "feel in the dark" is not mysticism. It is the endpoint of an empirical process that had been running for three generations.

The transition from river knowledge to sea knowledge — from the brown-green water of the delta to the blue-green of the open sea, from the named channels to the unnamed crossing — is the chapter's deeper subject, announced here. Kurima is not just crossing water. He is moving between two epistemological regimes, two knowledge systems, and the question of whether he is "a river person or an ocean person" is a question about which system of knowing he will live inside.

**My concern:** The opening does not yet show us the island knowledge system. We know the mainland knowledge from inside; the island is still a darkness on the horizon, a "low darkness, a thickening of the line." The chapter will need to enter the island knowledge system with the same depth that the opening gives to the river knowledge. If the island becomes merely a destination rather than a distinct epistemological world, the chapter will have lost its deepest question.

**The question I'd push on:** The manioc cultivation knowledge is mentioned briefly — "the knowledge of how to make cassava bread could be carried to any new location." But cassava processing is one of the most technically sophisticated food-preparation systems in the Americas: the bitter manioc's cyanide must be removed through a specific sequence of grating, pressing with the matapi (the fiber press), and extended cooking or drying. This is not simple. It is a knowledge system of considerable complexity. Does the chapter intend to render this knowledge at method level — showing the technique, not just naming it — and if so, where? The opening gestures at it but the chapter needs to follow through at the level of physical practice, not generalization.
