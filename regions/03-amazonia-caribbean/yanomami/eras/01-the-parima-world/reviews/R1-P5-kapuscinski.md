# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski
## Panel Review — Opening, Round 1

**Chapter:** Yanomami — Era 1: The Parima World
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The smell of the plantain soup. The bitterness of the Virola resin in his nose. The puppy with the palm frond. The aching knees of a man who has spent many years on the ground.

This is where I live as a reader: in the body, in the specific physical moment. And this opening has done what almost no Amazonian chapter manages — it has made the forest's body present without making it exotic. The tepuis catching morning clouds. The shabono's particular sounds. The birds the son-in-law brought back. I am there.

The detail I treasure most: "the familiar dissolution: the edges of his body going soft, his chest becoming a place where something else could live." This is phenomenologically precise. It does not describe the yakoana's effects from outside (it "induces visions," "causes hallucinations," the standard clinical glosses). It describes from inside what the body experiences. The edges going soft. The chest opening. This is the right way to render a bodily knowledge that the outside gloss destroys in the act of explaining.

The hekura of the waterfall arriving — "small and quick, the size of a child's fist, luminous" — is exactly specific enough. Not generic spirit-light. A specific creature, a specific size, a quality of luminosity. The chapter sees clearly.

What the full chapter needs to do is sustain this specificity across five thousand words. The risk in chapters like this is that the opening achieves sensory presence and then retreats into narrative exposition for the body of the chapter. Every section should have its physical anchor. The reahu, when it appears, should have the weight of ash, the consistency of plantain soup, the heat of grief made tangible. The feasting that constitutes alliance should have the smell of food, the sounds of many families sleeping under one roof, the particular social tension of a community being watched by guests who are also judging you. The forest should be felt in every section — its humidity, its density, the specific acoustic quality of a Highland Amazonian forest at different times of day.

**The question I'd push on:** Does the chapter trust its sensory anchors to carry the argument, or does it retreat to explanation when the evidence gets complex? The opening trusts its body. The chapter must keep that trust through the most difficult passages — through the reahu, through the feasting diplomacy, through the shapori's initiation. The body is the chapter's most honest witness. Don't lose it.

— RK
