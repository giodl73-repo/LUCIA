# Panel Review R1 — P-4 Wade Davis
## The High Wagadu — Opening

**Voice:** Wade Davis — Anthropological wonder, hidden condescension, worldview depth

---

The opening does what very few chapters manage with sacrificial practices: it enters the worldview without flinching and without aestheticizing. The girl's cold feet, the tight cotton, the awareness of the cloth as "both familiar and final" — these are the details of someone inside an experience, not someone observing it from a safe anthropological distance.

What strikes me most is the sentence about how the Dausi is carried: "the jeli who carry it." The chapter understands, at the level of vocabulary, that this tradition is alive — that it lives in human keepers who carry it as they carry their breath, not as an artifact in a museum case. The oral tradition is not described as having preserved something; it is described as carrying something, which is a different ontology entirely.

The world-building in the twin-city section achieves genuine anthropological depth. The key insight — "Each required the other. Neither could absorb the other without destroying what made it function" — is not stated as analysis. It is stated as fact, from inside the system's own logic. The six miles between the towns is not an accident of urban planning; it is the state's structure made physical. A reader who comes away from this opening understanding that the space between the two cities IS the government has understood something true and deep about how some political systems work.

The one place where I'd press harder: the description of the kola nuts in woven baskets, the brass scales, the layered sound of languages — this is strong sensory work, but it arrives in a list. The chapter will need to find ways to make these sensory details do analytical work, not just atmospheric work. In the Hausa chapter, the smell of the dye pit IS the economic argument. The opening of this chapter gestures toward that fusion; the chapter must achieve it.

**The question I'd push on:** Bida is named but not sensed. The serpent is the covenant's body — it lives in the well, or beneath it, or IS the well. How will the chapter render Bida's presence in the world? Not as a legend the Soninke believed but as a reality the Soninke inhabited? The girl at the well can feel the serpent's proximity. Can the chapter find the sensory correlate for divine presence in a Soninke world?
