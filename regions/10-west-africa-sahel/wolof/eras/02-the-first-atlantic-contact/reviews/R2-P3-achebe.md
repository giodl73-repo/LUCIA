# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Targeted Review of Notes

**Chapter:** Wolof / Era 2: The First Atlantic Contact
**Round:** 2 (Notes review, focused on cultural voice risks)

## What the Notes Get Right

The notes correctly identify that every Portuguese source must appear as a character inside the Wolof story, not as an authority on it. The plan for Bemoim in Section 3 is sound: his story is told as a Wolof political calculation, not as a tale of African encounter with Europe.

The gewel is planned as framing voice throughout. This is essential.

## What Needs Strengthening

**The jon must be present as human beings, not only as a trade category.** The notes mention them as a legal status ("defined and bounded under Wolof law") and as a trade commodity. But the jon were people. They had been captured in wars or raids. They had names, families, lives that were disrupted. The chapter need not moralize about this, but it must give the jon physical presence. At least once, the chapter should let the reader see a person being traded: not as spectacle, not as judgment, but as the human reality inside the economic calculation. The Wolof world had its own understanding of the jon's place, and the chapter should inhabit that understanding. But it should not render the jon invisible by treating them only as an abstraction.

**The gewel must DO something, not just BE invoked.** The notes plan the gewel as "framing voice throughout." But the gewel tradition is not a framing device. It is a performance tradition. At least once in the chapter, the gewel should perform: a phrase of praise song, a line of genealogy, a proverb that illuminates the political situation. Let the reader hear the tradition, not just be told it exists.

**Bemoim's story must be told from inside.** The notes correctly plan this, but the risk is high. The Portuguese sources (Zurara, Barros) are the primary written accounts of Bemoim's journey to Lisbon. These sources describe his baptism, his reception, his return, and his death from the Portuguese perspective. The chapter must invert this: Bemoim is a Wolof man making calculations. The baptism is a diplomatic tool, not a spiritual transformation. The Portuguese court is a place Bemoim has come to use, not a place that has honored him with an audience. When the Portuguese officer kills Bemoim on his return, the Portuguese narrative frames this as a misunderstanding or a betrayal. The Wolof narrative should frame it as what happens when you entrust your power to people who have their own agenda.

**Watch for "balance" language.** The notes use "equals" language that I flagged in Round 1. The production bible should reinforce: the Wolof did not think of themselves as Portugal's equals. They thought of themselves as the Wolof. The Portuguese were one more group to be assessed and managed. The concept of diplomatic equality is a European framework. The Wolof framework was sovereignty: the buur governed his territory, and anyone who came to trade did so within his terms.

## The Question I'd Push On in the Full Draft

The chapter is told primarily from the perspective of the ruling class: the Burba Jolof, the buur of Cayor, the ambassadors, the ceddo. This is partly a source problem (the gewel tradition and the Portuguese chronicles focus on elites), but the chapter should at least acknowledge the wider Wolof society. The farmers who grew the millet. The fishermen who first saw the ships. The women who managed the compounds. The chapter cannot tell their stories in detail without inventing them, but it can signal their presence. A Wolof noble's calculation about horses had consequences for every person in his territory. The chapter should let those consequences be felt, even if the specific people remain unnamed.
