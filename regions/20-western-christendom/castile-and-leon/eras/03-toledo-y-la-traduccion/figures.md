# Figures — Toledo y la Traducción

**Era:** Toledo and the Translation (1085–1212 CE)
**Culture:** Castile and León

---

## Gerard of Cremona (c. 1114–1187)

He came from a city known for its marble and its markets, from a Lombardy that produced merchants and scholars with equal facility, and he spent his working life in a different city entirely. The forty years he gave to Toledo are documented in the biography his students wrote after his death — a short text, precise as a translation, that lists what he translated and why he came. He came for Ptolemy's *Almagest*. He found more than Ptolemy. He found Euclid's *Elements*, which in Arabic was more complete than any Latin version then circulating. He found Avicenna's *Canon of Medicine*. He found al-Farabi on the sciences, al-Kindi on optics, Galen in Arabic translations that were better annotated than the Latin versions he had known. He translated eighty-seven texts — mathematicians count, scholars list — and by the time he died the question of whether there was more to do in Toledo had been answered: there was always more.

What the biography does not tell us is what forty years in Toledo did to the man who arrived there from Cremona. He learned Arabic thoroughly. He continued to use intermediaries even after he no longer needed them, which suggests either habit or the recognition that Abubacer's ear for inflection in the original texts was an instrument he would not replace by learning. He did not return to Lombardy. He did not seek appointment or preferment in Toledo's cathedral hierarchy. He was a translator, and Toledo was where translation happened, and that was enough.

His students found him in Toledo at his death in 1187, and they wrote down what he had done, and they took it with them, and the curriculum of the universities their generation was building included, because of what he had done, a body of knowledge that Latin Europe had not previously possessed.

## Dominicus Gundissalinus (fl. 1150–1190)

The Archdeacon of Segovia spent his career doing two things that his contemporaries might have expected him to keep separate: translating Arabic philosophical texts into Latin and writing original philosophical works in Latin. He understood they were the same project.

His *De divisione philosophiae* — written around 1150, which is to say during the years he was working with Gerard and Abubacer in the cathedral workshops — is the first attempt in Latin to give the Arabic Aristotelian tradition a systematic Latin account. It is not a translation. It is a synthesis that required Gundissalinus to understand not just what al-Farabi and Avicenna argued but what categories they were working in, which meant he had to understand those categories well enough to find Latin equivalents, which meant he had to understand Latin categories well enough to know where they didn't fit. The *De divisione* is what happens when a scholar does translation seriously enough that it changes how he thinks.

He also translated al-Farabi's *On the Intellect*, Avicenna's *On the Soul*, and al-Ghazali's *Aims of the Philosophers* — texts that between them provided Latin Europe with a philosophical psychology it did not previously possess. The questions those texts raised — about the nature of the intellect, about the relationship between individual soul and universal form, about the capacity of reason to reach conclusions about divine matters — would structure the debates of thirteenth-century scholasticism. Thomas Aquinas would wrestle with them. So would Duns Scotus. So would everyone who thought carefully about what it meant to be a rational creature in a world made by God.

Gundissalinus was the first person in Latin Christendom to wrestle with them rigorously, and he did it not by receiving a finished tradition but by reaching across a language barrier to take hold of a living one.

## Abubacer — and the question of his name

The Latin sources call him Abubacer or Ioannes Avendeuth or, in some documents, Avendehut. He appears in the dedicatory prefaces of several translations as Gerard's interlocutor — the Arabic-speaking, Hebrew-knowing, Mozarabic-fluent scholar who read the manuscripts aloud, rendered them into the spoken vernacular, and made the chain possible.

His identity is uncertain in ways the record cannot resolve. Abu Bakr is a common element in Arabic names; Ioannes is a common Latin rendering of multiple Arabic and Hebrew names; Avendehut (son of Daud) in Hebrew would be Abraham ibn Daud, which is also the name of the Toledo Jewish philosopher who wrote the *Sefer ha-Qabbalah* in 1161. Some scholars believe these are the same person. Others believe they are different people and the confusion is the Latin record's error.

The uncertainty is itself historical evidence. These scholars appear in the record when they were useful to the patron's dedicatory preface — when a bishop or a canon wanted credit for commissioning a translation and needed to name the instruments of the work. They disappear from the record when the text was done. The Arabic-speaking scholar who made Gerard's forty years possible was named often enough to allow his name to survive in variant forms across several manuscripts; he was not named enough to allow us to be certain which variant is correct or whether it refers to one person or several.

What we know: someone — one person or a collaborative group working across the same decades — made the trilingual chain function. He read Arabic with the precision that allowed him to identify when an Arabic term carried a mathematical meaning that a simpler rendering would lose. He read Hebrew and Mozarabic and Latin well enough to navigate between them. He spent years in the cathedral workshops. His successors did the same work after him. The translation school did not stop when Gerard stopped; others continued, and they needed the same trilingual competency, and the Jewish aljama of Toledo produced it, generation after generation, because that was what the community had been doing in that city for longer than the Christian administration had existed.

The gap in the record is not a gap in the history. It is a gap in who the record was written to remember.

## Abraham ibn Daud (c. 1110–1180)

The philosopher and historian of Toledo's Jewish community wrote the *Sefer ha-Qabbalah* — the Book of Tradition — in Hebrew in 1161. It is a chronicle of the Jewish scholarly tradition: a list of the chain of transmission by which Jewish learning had passed from generation to generation from the time of Moses to the time of ibn Daud's own teachers in Toledo. It is not a history of the translation school. It is a history of a different project entirely — the maintenance of a continuous intellectual tradition within a community that had been, in various centuries, under Roman governance, under Visigothic governance, under Islamic governance, and was now under Christian governance, and intended to remain a community of scholars regardless of who held the city.

Ibn Daud's Toledo is not the Toledo of Gerard's biography. In the *Sefer ha-Qabbalah*, Toledo is not a place where Arabic knowledge is transmitted to Latin Europe. It is a place where the chain of Jewish learning has been maintained unbroken, where the yeshivot have sustained their scholars, where the community has navigated successive political changes without losing what it was. The translation school appears in the *Sefer ha-Qabbalah* as something that happens in Toledo without being the point of Toledo — a service the community provides to Christian patrons, useful for sustaining the community's economic position and legal status, not the community's definition of its own purpose.

Ibn Daud is also the author of *Emunah Ramah* — the Exalted Faith — a philosophical treatise that uses Aristotelian categories to address questions of Jewish theology. He read Avicenna in Arabic. He engaged with Arabic Aristotelianism directly, not as a relay to Latin Europe but as a living tradition he needed to argue with because it raised questions his own tradition had to answer. His philosophical project was independent of the translation school even when it used the same sources. He is not a bridge. He is a thinker.

## Alfonso VIII (r. 1158–1214)

He came to the throne as a child and spent his minority in the custody of warring noble factions and spent his reign rebuilding from the consequences of that minority. When the Almohad armies destroyed his forces at Alarcos in 1195, he was thirty-seven years old and had been governing Castile for the better part of two decades, and the disaster was of a kind that puts everything in question. Alarcos was not a setback. It was a catastrophe. The army that had been the shield between Castile and the south was gone. The frontier was open.

He spent the next seventeen years doing what was necessary. He negotiated with Aragon. He appealed to the papacy. He managed his nobility. He raised the resources for a new army without the resources to do it easily. And in 1212 he crossed the Sierra Morena with a coalition army and at Las Navas de Tolosa broke the Almohad force sufficiently that it never again threatened Castile as a military power.

He did not live to see Córdoba fall — that would be his son Fernando III's achievement. He died in 1214, two years after Las Navas. The kingdom he left behind was intact and had a future, which was not guaranteed in 1195. Whether he understood that the world he was fighting to preserve was already, in some of its most essential features, beginning to transform — this is the kind of question kings who are fighting for survival do not have time to ask. He fought. He won. Toledo kept its libraries.

## Ibn Tumart (c. 1080–1130) and the Almohad dynamic

He was born in the mountains of Morocco, traveled to Baghdad to study at the great centers of Islamic learning, and came home convinced that the Islam practiced in the Maghreb had drifted from its proper form. He preached reform. He gathered followers. He built a movement. By the time he died in 1130, the movement he founded had become a military force powerful enough to overthrow the Almoravid dynasty that controlled Andalusia and eventually to threaten Castile at Alarcos.

Ibn Tumart is not a figure in Toledo's story in any direct sense. He never visited Toledo; the city had been Christian for thirty years before he preached his first sermon. But the movement he created determined the conditions under which Toledo's translation school operated. The Almohad theology was doctrinally opposed to the three-community arrangements that Toledo embodied; the cities of Andalusia under Almohad governance were not hospitable to the overlapping cultural life of the taifa period. Jewish and Christian communities that had functioned under the taifa kings with their own internal governance faced starkly different conditions under Almohad authority.

The scholars who had been working in Seville and Córdoba moved north. Toward Toledo. Toward the city where the conditions that had existed in the taifa south still existed, protected by distance and by the practical governance decisions of the Castilian crown. The Almohad movement, which sought to end the kind of coexistence that made Toledo work, inadvertently strengthened it by concentrating in Toledo the people most committed to sustaining it.

History does not make simple trades. Ibn Tumart's reform movement produced both the military crisis at Alarcos and the scholarly concentration that made Toledo's workshop more productive in the 1150s and 1160s than it might otherwise have been. Both outcomes followed from the same cause.
