## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Review of Meroe Era 3 Opening

**Overall:** I can feel the bronze head. I can feel the weight of it, carried south through the cataracts. I can feel the sand beneath the temple steps. The opening puts me in a physical place for its first three paragraphs. Then it lifts off the ground and does not fully return until the close.

**What works:**
- The bronze head is magnificently physical. "The size of a man's head but heavier." The soldiers carrying it. The blank eyes watching the river. And then its burial: "face down in the sand, still watching nothing." This is an object with a body, and the body of the object does the narrative work. I can feel it in my hands.
- The final paragraph is one of the best landings I have read. "The furnaces burned, the river carried iron and ivory north, and the kandake walked into the temple of Apedemak over the face of the most powerful man in the Mediterranean world." This sentence puts multiple bodies in motion simultaneously: the furnaces, the river, the kandake, Augustus's face. I am there.
- The Garstang discovery is physical: "face down in the sand." Three words that make me feel the archaeological moment. The head has been watching nothing for seventeen centuries. Absence as a physical presence.

**What doesn't:**
- The paragraph beginning "What made the kandake's position possible" loses the ground entirely. Iron furnaces, trade routes, the Butana steppe, the Red Sea ports, Arabian incense, Indian pepper. These are words about geography. I cannot feel the heat of the furnace. I cannot smell the iron. I cannot hear the hammer. The furnaces at Meroe are among the most significant industrial sites in the ancient world, and this paragraph gives me slag heaps as a fact rather than as a physical presence. I want to stand next to those furnaces.
- The Meroitic landscape is absent. What does the Island of Meroe feel like? The territory between the Nile and the Atbara: what is the steppe like? The light? The heat? The chapter tells me about the agricultural heartland but does not put me in a field. A reader should feel the landscape that produced this kingdom.
- Amanirenas herself is only partially embodied. We know she lost an eye (from Strabo). We know she commanded in person. But what did she look like? The temple reliefs at Meroe and Naga show the kandakes as physically powerful women, large, depicted striking enemies. The chapter should let us see her as the Meroites depicted her, not as a name with a title.

**The question I'd push on:**
The full chapter will need to sustain physical presence through 5,000 words of military campaign, treaty negotiation, and cultural analysis. The danger is that the middle sections (the treaty at Samos, the border arrangement, the trade analysis) will float above the ground. How will the chapter keep its body? The bronze head is grounded. The treaty terms are not yet grounded. Can the chapter find physical anchors for its political and economic analysis, or will those sections drift into the abstract?
