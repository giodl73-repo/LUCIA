# Panel Review — R2 / P-5 / Ryszard Kapuściński (PANEL-2, targeted)
## French Empire Era 2: La Révolution et Ses Contradictions — Notes Review
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

The production bible addresses my concern about sensory anchoring. The planned sensory nodes are right: the archive smell in Section 1, the burning cane in Section 3, the Jura cold in Section 5, the ledger's arithmetic in Section 6. I want to confirm these are in the right places and give some guidance on what each should carry.

**The archive (Section 1):**

A colonial administrator's office in Port-au-Prince in 1789 smells specific. The paper imported from France would already be damp by the time it arrived — the Atlantic crossing, the tropical humidity, the incompatibility of the medium with the climate. The ink was iron gall, which corrodes paper from within over time. Moreau de Saint-Méry's own memoirs record the heat and the insects. He was working in conditions that the Enlightenment apparatus was not designed for. That friction — the rational system in the wrong climate — should be in the first sensory note.

**The burning cane (Section 3):**

Sugar cane burns wet before it burns dry. The mills were wooden and ran day and night during harvest; the smell of the boiling house (the *sucrerie*) was already present in the air of the northern plain before the insurrection. The insurrection's fires added something else: the burning of the buildings themselves, the great houses, the warehouses. Le Cap-Français was downwind of the northern plain. The smell reached the harbor before the dispatches did. Governor Blanchelande wrote that the sky over the plain "was orange for three nights." This is the detail the chapter needs. Orange sky. Three nights. Then the dispatches.

**The Fort de Joux cold (Section 5):**

The Fort de Joux is in the Jura mountains, at altitude, in winter. It is as far from Saint-Domingue as it is possible to be within France — climatically, geographically, epistemologically. Toussaint arrived in August 1802 and died in April 1803. He had never experienced a European winter. His letters from the fort (several survive) complain of the cold, the damp, the darkness. He asked for his papers, which were not returned to him. He was 59 or 60 years old, and the French administration kept no record of his precise birth date because the system that classified him had not originally intended to need one. This is the chapter's most intimate sensory scene: a man in a cold cell, asking for papers that would not come, dying of something that was not quite cold but not quite anything else either.

**The ledger (Section 6):**

The 150 million franc indemnity has a specific physical form: the ordonnance royale of Charles X, April 17, 1825, delivered to Haiti under the guns of French warships in Port-au-Prince harbor. The document is a masterpiece of administrative compression — it reduces an empire's defeat, a people's liberation, and a century of enslaved labor to a number and a signature. The chapter's final sensory note should be this document: the paper, the guns, the harbor where the fires of 1791 once lit the sky orange.

**The note I'd add:**

The chapter should have one scene in the *northern plain itself* — not from the harbor, not from the governor's office, but from the ground. Even a paragraph. The roads, the plantation layouts, the distance between the great house and the quarters, the visibility lines (why the insurrection could coordinate without being seen). Leclerc's dispatches describe an enemy that moved through the landscape like water, appearing and disappearing. That description is in the dispatches — use it. The French military mind encountering a knowledge of the terrain that it could not map.
