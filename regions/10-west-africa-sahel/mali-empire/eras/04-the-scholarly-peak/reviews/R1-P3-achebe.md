# P-3 -- Chinua Achebe -- Review of "The Scholarly Peak" Opening

**Overall:** The voice is right. The scholar is not a specimen. He is a man working, in his city, in his tradition, at his desk. I recognize the world. It belongs to itself.

**What works:**
- "This was not unusual. This was what Timbuktu did." This is the single most important sentence in the opening. It refuses the temptation to present the intellectual tradition as exceptional or surprising. It is ordinary. It is work. This is exactly the tone the chapter needs.
- "No one in Timbuktu called it a university. The word is ours, not theirs." This is honest and precise. It neither imposes the Western term nor denies the reality. It names the gap between the reader's vocabulary and the culture's own categories, and it lets the culture's categories stand on their own terms. This is how it should be done.
- The commentary tradition is rendered from inside. Al-Kabari is not writing a response to show that Timbuktu was intellectually active. He is writing because a jurist in Fez was wrong about the third proposition, and someone has to say so. The motivation is intellectual, not representational.
- The students sitting together -- "Berber and Songhai and Hausa and Fulani and Soninke and Arab" -- are not a diversity tableau. They are people who came for the knowledge. The knowledge is the subject, not their origins.

**What doesn't:**
- The phrase "Mansa Musa was ten years dead, perhaps more" creates a slight dependence on the previous chapter. A reader entering here should not need to have read Era 3 to understand the world. The Musa reference is necessary for continuity, but it should feel like background the current world takes for granted, not like a bridge from the previous chapter.
- "The dates are not certain -- the Arabic sources say he died around 737 of the Hijra (1337 CE), but the jelilu carried a different reckoning" -- this is honest, but it briefly shifts the voice from the scholar's world to the historian's study. Al-Kabari does not care about the disagreement between Arabic sources and jelilu reckoning. He knows when Musa died, or he does not care. The historiographic frame should not be visible here.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening handles the "university" question with admirable precision. But the full chapter will face a harder version of this test. When Ibn Battuta arrives, the temptation will be to let his account validate the scholarly tradition -- "even a Moroccan traveler was impressed." Ibn Battuta is a character in this story, not a witness whose approval matters. His observations are evidence of HIS perspective, not evidence of Timbuktu's worth. The chapter must handle him as a visitor with limited understanding, not as an outside authority whose testimony confirms what the reader should already know from inside.
