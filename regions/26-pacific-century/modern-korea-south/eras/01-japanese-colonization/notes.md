# Production Bible — Era 1: Iljegangjeongi (Japanese Colonization, 1910–1945)

**Culture:** Modern Korea (South) | **Period:** 1910–1945 CE | **Voice:** Discover (han — compressed sorrow as productive force)  
**Structural Engine:** Cultural persistence through erasure — what the system cannot fully control

---

## **The Story**

The Japanese annexation of Korea in 1910 was not merely a political conquest. It was a *systematic attempt to erase Korean identity and replace it with Japanese identity*. The soshi-kaimei policy (1939–1945), the suppression of Korean language in schools, the requisition of rice and resources, the coercion of comfort women into sexual slavery, the forced labor of Korean workers — these were not random cruelties. They were *parts of a unified system designed to absorb Korea into Japan as a racial and cultural satellite*.

The era's arc is not **Korean resistance defeated**. It is **Korean culture persisting through every mechanism the system attempts**. The distinction matters. Resistance implies active rebellion. Persistence implies *the refusal to be fully erased despite everything*.

The haegeum player carries this: in the quiet choice to keep playing, in the choice to teach the next generation despite the schools being required to teach in Japanese, in the way Korean ways of naming, thinking, and being survive the administration's attempt to erase them. The era closes with Korea in 1945 — the administration's system collapsed, but not by Korean rebellion (though there was rebellion). The system collapses because Japan loses the Pacific War. Korea is liberated by external defeat of the colonizer, not by successful internal resistance.

This is the *discovery* the era makes: **liberation from external event, not earned by internal agency**. The chapter must honor that complexity. The Koreans fought. They suffered. They persisted. And in the end, they were freed by something beyond their control. The landing must sit with this: gratitude and incompleteness simultaneously.

## **Key Figures**

### **1. The Haegeum Player (Kim Min-jeong / 김민정, b. 1869)**

**Korean name and meaning:** Min-jeong (민정) means "brightness and equity" — a name that carries Confucian ideals of luminous virtue and fairness. After soshi-kaimei (1939), she will be registered as "Tanaka Minako."

**Entry point:** She is already established in the opening scene. She is a musician of the pansori tradition — a living archive of Korean song. She is 43 years old when the Japanese administrator arrives in 1912. She carries the music of forty generations in her fingers.

**Her arc through the era:**

- **1910–1920:** She continues playing, becomes a teacher of younger musicians, begins to understand the full scope of the soshi-kaimei policy that is coming
- **1921:** After the March First Movement (Korean uprising, March 1, 1919), the suppression intensifies. She sees some of her students arrested
- **1939:** The soshi-kaimei mandate forces her to adopt a Japanese name. She complies publicly (Tanaka Hana). But she continues teaching under her Korean identity.
- **1940–1945:** As war intensifies, food is rationed, young people are drafted or forced into labor. She keeps teaching pansori in secret — a form of cultural persistence that cannot be documented
- **1945:** When Korea is liberated, she is 76 years old. She plays at a celebration in Seoul. The pansori, banned for 35 years, is sung openly again.

**Her characteristic:** She is not a revolutionary. She is a *keeper*. She understands that the haegeum's power lies in its *continuity*, not in its noise. She teaches in the traditional way: one student at a time, by listening and repetition.

**Historical accuracy note:** This is a composite figure, based on the historical reality of Korean musicians during the colonial period. Specific pansori masters like Choi Ok-sun and Sin Bulgeum were documented as continuing the tradition despite suppression. The character draws from their reality but is not identical to any one person.

---

### **2. The Japanese Administrator (Tanaka Reiji, b. 1882)**

**Entry point:** He arrives in the opening scene as a functionary of the system.

**His arc:**

- **1910–1920:** Tanaka is a young administrator, part of the machinery of cultural assimilation. He participates in the cataloguing of Korean cultural practices — which ones can be assimilated, which must be suppressed.
- **1925:** He is promoted. He becomes involved in the implementation of the soshi-kaimei policy.
- **1939:** He oversees the name-change registrations in his district. He keeps meticulous records.
- **1940–1945:** As the war turns against Japan, he becomes more brutal, partly from desperation, partly from ideology.
- **1945:** When Japan surrenders, Tanaka flees Korea. He lives out his life in Japan, never returning, haunted by his role.

**His characteristic:** Tanaka is not evil. He is *systematic*. He genuinely believes he is bringing "civilization" to Korea. He is not sadistic; he is bureaucratic. This is the most dangerous kind of colonizer: the one who sees his work as benevolent modernization.

**The complexity:** The chapter should show both Kim-sa and Tanaka as *people operating within a system*, not as Good vs. Evil. The evil is the system itself, not the individuals who execute it.

---

### **3. Young-Sook (Kim-sa's granddaughter, b. 1925)**

**Entry point:** She is Kim-sa's student and the bridge between the traditional music and the modernizing world.

**Her arc:**

- **1932–1939:** She learns pansori from her grandmother under the radar. It is not a legal activity. It is something women do in private.
- **1939:** The soshi-kaimei mandate forces her to adopt a Japanese name (Tanaka Yuki). At school, she is this name. At home, she is Young-Sook.
- **1940–1943:** She is 15–19 years old during the intensifying war. She is conscripted into factory work. She works 12-hour days. She no longer has time to learn pansori.
- **1943:** She is offered a "choice" — become a comfort woman or continue factory work. She "chooses" factory work, knowing it might cost her her life anyway.
- **1945:** She survives. She is 20. She has lost five years to the war. She will spend decades processing this loss.

**Her characteristic:** She is the generation that experiences *both* the colonization and the liberation. She is old enough to have learned Korean identity but young enough to have been schooled in Japanese. The chapter must show her in both languages, both names.

---

### **4. Park Min-jun (Korean nationalist, b. 1887)**

**Entry point:** He is a secondary figure but carries the arc of *Korean resistance that is ultimately crushed*.

**His arc:**

- **1919:** He participates in the March First Movement. He is arrested, tortured.
- **1921–1939:** He works in an underground network, printing Korean-language materials, helping people escape to Manchuria and Shanghai.
- **1940:** He is betrayed. He is arrested again.
- **1945:** He is released from prison when Japan surrenders. He is in his late 50s, broken by torture. He survives but never fully recovers.

**His characteristic:** Park represents the *active resistance* that the era requires. Without him, the chapter would be only about passive survival. With him, the chapter shows the *full spectrum of Korean responses to colonization*.

**Historical accuracy note:** There were multiple underground Korean independence movements. Park is a composite figure drawing from documented historical activists like Yi Sang-jae and Kim Gu.

## **Narrative Arc & Section Plan**

**Opening integration:** The haegeum player scene (~800 words) becomes the chapter's first section, introducing the era's central question: *what can the system not control?*

**Section 2: "The System of Names" (~900 words)**

The section develops how the soshi-kaimei policy works and what it means. **Crucially, it shows the machinery: the policy targets genealogy.** A Korean family name carries lineage and inheritance — it is how the Confucian system knows you. By replacing it with a Japanese name, the system *severs you from your own genealogical chain*. You are no longer legally a descendant of Korean ancestors. The records will show you as descended from Japan.

We follow Kim Min-jeong through the process of registration (1939), the bureaucratic machinery, the moment she learns her name will be "Tanaka Minako." The section shows *what is being taken*: not just a label, but her continuity. We see Tanaka at his desk with meticulous care, recording the names, documenting the erasure, believing he is building an orderly system. We see Young-Sook learning to navigate both names, becoming bilingual not just in language but in identity.

**Section 3: "What the Schools Cannot Teach" (~1000 words)**

The chapter narrows to education. Korean language is banned from schools. Japanese becomes mandatory. But Kim Min-jeong continues teaching pansori in secret — it is oral tradition, impossible to fully suppress because it leaves no written record. The section shows two epistemologies in collision: the Japanese schoolteacher (introduced here) teaches with genuine pedagogical care, believing she is educating her students in civilization and modernity. Her way of knowing is systematic and sophisticated. But the haegeum teacher teaches with equal care, transmitting knowledge that cannot be written down, that lives in the body and the ear.

Young-Sook is caught between them. She learns to code-switch: one language at school, another at home. One self when she is "Tanaka Yuki," another when she is Young-Sook. The section should *embody* her bilingualism in the prose itself — sometimes thinking in Japanese structures, sometimes in Korean ones.

**Section 4: "The March First Spirit" (~800 words)**

We move to the March 1, 1919 uprising. The chapter shows how the initial liberation movement was suppressed militarily, and crucially, *how the suppression triggers the system to intensify*. This is the causal mechanism: Korean resistance provokes not abandonment but *deeper entrenchment* of the colonial machinery. The Japanese fear Korean nationalism. They respond by accelerating the soshi-kaimei policy (which will be mandated in 1939), by intensifying surveillance, by militarizing the police.

Park Min-jun is introduced here as a participant in March First, arrested, tortured. He is released broken — he represents the cost of open resistance. His broken body carries the message: *overt rebellion is possible, and it is crushed*.

The section shows that *Korean resistance changes the colonial system itself*, making it more brutal and systematic.

**Section 5: "The War That Reaches Into Everything" (~1000 words)**

By 1937, Japan is at war with China. By 1941, Japan is at war with the United States. Korea becomes a *supply base* for the war. Rice is requisitioned. Young people are conscripted for labor. The comfort women system begins to operate. The section shows how the colonization becomes war-driven, more brutal.

**Section 6: "What Cannot Be Written Down" (~900 words)**

The final section covers 1943–1945. The war is turning against Japan. The administration becomes desperate. The suppression intensifies. But here, the chapter shows what *persists*: the haegeum still played (though more secretly), the language still spoken (though more quietly), the Korean identity still taught (though in whispers).

Young-Sook is offered the "choice" to become a comfort woman. The section handles this with the gravity it requires, but without sensationalism. This was genocide dressed as military necessity.

The section closes with August 15, 1945: Korea is liberated. Japan has surrendered. The system that tried to erase Korea is gone.

**Landing (final section, ~700 words):**

The landing does not close the era triumphantly. It acknowledges what Korea has won (sovereignty, the haegeum can be played again) and what it has lost (35 years of development stolen, millions of people dead or traumatized, the economy destroyed, the country about to be divided).

Kim-sa, now 76, plays at a celebration. Young-Sook, now 20, listens. Both women have survived. Both carry the war in their bodies. The chapter closes with the knowledge that liberation is not the end of the story — it is the beginning of the next chapter (division, war, reconstruction).

**Total chapter length: 5,400–5,600 words.**

## **Voice & Structural Engine**

**Voice specification:** The chapter uses what might be called a *discovery voice* — a prose that moves through compression and revelation, never explaining, always showing. Short sentences alternating with longer, complex ones. The rhythm mirrors the haegeum's question-and-answer structure. Dense sensory detail.

**Key constraint:** Zero to minimal em-dashes. The voice finds its pauses through periods and commas, not through em-dashes.

**Structural engine:** The **haegeum as persistence mechanism**. 

The chapter's narrative logic is not chronological. It is organized around what cannot be erased:
- Section 1: The haegeum survives the administrator's visit
- Section 2: Kim-sa's name is taken, but the haegeum remains her own
- Section 3: The schools teach Japanese, but the haegeum teaches Korean
- Section 4: The uprising is suppressed, but the memory of it (and Park's sacrifice) persists
- Section 5: The war requisitions everything, but the haegeum keeps being played
- Section 6: The war ends. The haegeum can be played openly again.

The engine is not "Korean resistance wins." The engine is "Korean culture persists through every attempt to erase it." The chapter traces what the system cannot control: the embodied practice of music, the oral transmission of history, the quiet teaching of young people who will carry the tradition forward.

**Terminal condition:** The system collapses not because of internal Korean uprising (though there is uprising), but because Japan loses the war. This is the tragic complexity of the era: Korea is liberated by external event, not by earned freedom. The chapter must sit with this.

**Primary sources:**

**Academic sources:**
- Bruce Cumings, *Korea's Place in the Sun*, Chapter 3 (Japanese colonization)
- Gi-Wook Shin, *Ethnic Nationalism in Korea*, Chapters 2–4 (identity formation under colonization)
- Yoshimi Yoshaki, *Comfort Women* (documented genocide)
- Korean Council reports (TRC Korea documentation of colonial period crimes)
- Park Kyong-rim, *Korean Music: A Brief History* (pansori tradition under suppression)

**Source strategy:** The chapter will use the *source asymmetry as narrative technique* (v1.5 rubric amendment). Korean oral testimony will be the primary lens; Japanese administrative records will be shown as the colonizer's perspective; pansori texts and music will be used as primary evidence of cultural persistence.

## **Panel Pressure Points — Anticipated**

### **Tuchman will push on:**

The landing. Does it earn its weight, or does it just stop? The chapter must close at a moment that opens onto what is coming (division, war), not try to contain the entire Korean story in one landing.

### **Ibn Khaldun will push on:**

The machinery of the system. The chapter must show *how* the colonial system actually worked — not just its cruelty, but its *logic*. The soshi-kaimei was not arbitrary; it was systematic. The comfort women system was not ad-hoc; it was bureaucratic. The section on "The System of Names" must demonstrate the machinery.

### **Achebe will push on:**

The woman's name. By the closing of the section where her name is taken, the reader should *feel* what is lost. The opening gives us a woman without a name. By the moment the soshi-kaimei is implemented, the reader should have named her (internally, at least) and should feel the erasure.

### **Davis will push on:**

The haegeum's epistemology. The chapter must show, throughout, that the way of knowing embedded in pansori is *equally sophisticated* as the administrator's system. It is not "less educated" or "more emotional." It is *differently knowing*. The section "What the Schools Cannot Teach" should make this explicit through contrast.

### **Kapuściński will push on:**

The bodies in the war sections. When the chapter moves to the comfort women, the requisitions, the forced labor — the sensory detail must increase, not decrease. The reader must feel the heat of the factory, the cold of the barracks, the body of the woman being conscripted. This is where sensory immersion is most crucial and most difficult.

## **Open Questions for the Chapter**

1. Should the chapter name Kim-sa before the soshi-kaimei takes her name? (Achebe suggests yes; what is her Korean name?)
2. How explicit should the comfort women section be? (The chapter must be honest without sensationalizing. The War and Women's Human Rights Museum is the guide.)
3. Should Park Min-jun's torture be depicted? (Yes, but through implication and aftermath, not description.)
4. What is the exact moment of liberation in the chapter? (August 15, 1945, when the radio announces Japan's surrender.)
5. How does the chapter handle the coming division (1945)? (The landing acknowledges it without narrating it.)
