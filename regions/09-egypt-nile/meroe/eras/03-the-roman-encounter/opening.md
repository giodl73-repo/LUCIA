# The Roman Encounter

They brought the head back to Meroe in the fourth year of the kandake's war.

It was bronze, the size of a man's head but heavier, and the soldiers who had carried it south through the cataracts said it had watched them the whole journey, the blank eyes of the Roman emperor fixed on the river as though he could still command it. Augustus. The man who called himself son of a god, who had taken Egypt and renamed it a province, who had sent his prefect Petronius south to teach the Kushites what Rome meant. The head had stood on a pedestal in the forum at Aswan before the kandake's army pulled it down. Now it was here, in Meroe, and the kandake Amanirenas had decided what to do with it.

She had it buried beneath the entrance steps of the temple of Apedemak.

Not hidden. Buried. Placed deliberately in the earth at the threshold where the lion god received his petitioners, so that every priest, every soldier, every ambassador who walked into the temple would tread on the face of the emperor of Rome. The placement was not symbolic in the way a later age might read it, not a gesture meant for historians. It was operational. In Meroitic practice, to place something beneath a threshold was to master it, to bind its power beneath the feet of those who passed above. Augustus's bronze gaze, which had watched the Nile from Aswan, now watched the dark earth beneath the steps of Apedemak's house, and everyone who entered pressed him further down.

Amanirenas had earned this. Her forces had struck north in the twenty-fourth year before the common era, into the territory Rome believed it had settled. Aswan, Philae, Elephantine. The Roman garrisons were not expecting an army from the south. The Meroites took prisoners, pulled down statues, and withdrew with the head and with something more valuable: the knowledge that Rome could be hit, that its southern frontier was a line drawn in sand, not in stone. When Petronius counterattacked, pushing south past the second cataract to the fortress at Qasr Ibrim, the kandake did not collapse. She regrouped. She negotiated.

The treaty that followed, struck at Samos before Augustus himself, gave Meroe almost everything it wanted. The border was set at Maharraqa, south of the first cataract, which was closer to Egyptian territory than Rome would have liked. The tribute Rome had demanded was cancelled. The Dodekaschoinos, the twelve-schoenoi strip of borderland between the cataracts, became a shared zone rather than a Roman possession. Roman historians recorded this as a reasonable peace. Meroitic records, to the extent we can read them, appear to have recorded it as a victory. The head beneath the temple steps suggests which reading the kandake preferred.

What made the kandake's position possible was not merely military daring. South of the capital, the iron furnaces burned day and night, the charcoal glow visible across the Butana steppe, the slag heaps growing darker and taller with each season's smelting. The furnaces made tools and weapons that supplied the kingdom and fed the trade routes running east to the Red Sea, where Meroitic iron met Arabian incense and Indian pepper in the same commercial current. The Nile and the Atbara watered the fields between them. The kingdom's scribes carved inscriptions in their own script, twenty-three signs that owed their shapes to Egypt but served a language that was Meroe's alone. And Apedemak, the lion-headed lord whose temple now held Augustus's face beneath its steps, had no Egyptian equivalent. He was Meroitic. His temples at Naga and Musawwarat es-Sufra, carved from the yellow sandstone of the steppe, answered to their own aesthetic and their own theology.

Amanirenas, who had lost an eye in the fighting according to Strabo, who commanded her forces in person, who sent her envoys to Augustus's court and got the terms she wanted, knew what she held. She knew what it was worth.

The bronze head stayed beneath the steps. Seventeen centuries later, the British archaeologist John Garstang would find it in 1910, face down in the sand, still watching nothing, still pressed beneath the threshold of a god it had never known. By then the temple was ruin and the kingdom was memory and the script was silence. But in the year the head was buried, none of that was visible. In the year the head was buried, the furnaces burned, the river carried iron and ivory north, and the kandake walked into the temple of Apedemak over the face of the most powerful man in the Mediterranean world, and the lion god said nothing, because there was nothing that needed to be said.
