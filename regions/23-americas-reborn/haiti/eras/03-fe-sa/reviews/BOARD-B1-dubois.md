# BOARD Review — B-1: Laurent Dubois
## Haiti, Era 3: Fè Sa — The Revolution

**Role:** Scholar of the Haitian Revolution; Professor of History, Duke University; author of *Avengers of the New World* (2004) and *Haiti: The Aftershocks of History* (2012)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Factual Assessment

The chapter's factual skeleton is accurate. The Bois Caïman ceremony on August 14, 1791 (some sources give August 22 as the date of the uprising's beginning, with August 14 as the planning meeting — the chapter does not over-specify this distinction, which is correct given scholarly uncertainty). The seven-year ledger calculation is historically documented. Boukman's capture and execution, the display of his head — accurate. The British evacuation of 1798 and Maitland's salute to Toussaint — accurate. The 1801 Constitution and its structure — accurate. Leclerc's arrival with approximately thirty-three thousand men — accurate. Toussaint's capture through treachery and death at Fort de Joux in April 1803 — accurate. The Battle of Vertières on November 18 — accurate. The independence declaration on January 1, 1804 — accurate.

**One factual clarification:** The chapter states Toussaint's last words were spoken "leaving Saint-Domingue." The more commonly cited location of this statement is either at the point of his arrest (before being shipped to France) or during his captivity at Fort de Joux, not definitively at the moment of departure. The phrase attributed to him is real and documented, but the precise moment is disputed. The chapter should note "according to the tradition" or "in his own account from Fort de Joux" rather than placing the statement at a specific scene the record does not precisely support.

**One factual addition to consider:** The chapter mentions the British defeat of 1798 but does not note that Toussaint successfully negotiated with Maitland a secret clause allowing American trade to continue with Saint-Domingue — a piece of the geopolitical maneuvering that the chapter's structure handles well at a macro level but might be enriched at the micro level. This is optional, not a correction.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

The treatment of Vodou as organizational technology rather than belief system is historically accurate and represents the current scholarly consensus (following Fick, Brown, and my own research). The description of the lwa as real — not as metaphors for the practitioners — is correct and appropriately rendered.

**One concern:** The chapter uses the term "houngan" for Boukman's role. This is commonly used but is technically a post-independence term that may not have been the vocabulary of 1791. The more historically precise formulation would be to acknowledge that we describe his role using later Vodou terminology because the contemporary vocabulary is not preserved. The chapter's note ("something the French would later call a *houngan*") does this partially but not fully. For full accuracy, I would recommend a small adjustment: "something the Haitian tradition would later call a *houngan*" — attributing the term to the tradition, not to the French.

## Source Use Assessment

The use of Toussaint's captivity account as primary source is appropriate and correctly attributed as primary. The planter's ledger calculation is real but the specific ledger reference would benefit from a footnote-level citation to Mintz (*Sweetness and Power*) or Dubois (*Avengers*) in a final manuscript notes section.

**Recommendation on Bois Caïman sources:** The chapter handles source asymmetry correctly by noting that the French accounts carry the biases of the observers. I would strengthen this by naming, in the notes, the specific accounts the chapter draws from — primarily the account of Antoine Dalmas (published 1814) and the later accounts that appear to draw on him. Dalmas was a royalist planter whose description of the ceremony was designed to discredit the uprising. Acknowledging him by name makes the source situation visible.

## Overall Assessment

This chapter is among the most accurate and most carefully considered treatments of the Haitian Revolution I have encountered in narrative history. The engine (libète as absolute) is the right framing. The treatment of Dessalines is appropriately complex — neither heroized nor condemned. The composite figure in figures.md is methodologically sound and acknowledged.

**Board recommendation:** APPROVE with two minor revisions (the "houngan" attribution; the placement of Toussaint's last words).
