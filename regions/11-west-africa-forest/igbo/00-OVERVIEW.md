# Igbo — "Ọ dị n'ọnọdụ / In Their Own Terms"

The Igbo governance question is the question the history books ask wrong. "Why didn't the Igbo have kings?" assumes that kingship is the normal state and the Igbo are the exception. But the question from inside the Igbo world is different: how do you govern a large, dense, prosperous population without the vulnerability that comes with centralizing power in a single person? The Igbo answer — worked out across two thousand years in the densely farmed yam and oil palm territory of the lower Niger — was institutional pluralism: village assemblies, age grades, title societies, the Ozo and Eze title systems, the masquerade institutions through which the ancestral world governed the living, and the Arochukwu oracle network that provided a quasi-judicial authority across the entire southeastern Nigerian territory. You did not need a king if you had all of these working together.

The Igbo world at the time of European contact was one of the most densely populated rural territories in Africa. The lower Niger and Benue confluence region supported population densities comparable to agricultural zones in East and South Asia. This density was sustained by intensive yam cultivation on mounded ridges, by oil palm cultivation for cooking and trade, by iron-working, by cloth production, and by a commerce that connected Igbo communities to the Niger Delta trade routes and thence to the Atlantic world. The Igbo were not isolated forest villagers; they were participants in a regional economy of considerable sophistication, and when the Atlantic slave trade created its terrible demand, the Arochukwu oracle network was one of the mechanisms by which that demand was met — at catastrophic cost to Igbo communities themselves.

## What Defined Them

**Governance without kings.** The Igbo village assembly — the oha — was the primary decision-making body for most Igbo communities. Decisions were made through discussion, through the weight of elders' opinions, through the authority of title-holders who had purchased membership in title societies (Ozo, Eze) through wealth and merit. Age grades organized collective labor and community responsibility. The masquerades — ancestral figures made present in the world through costumed performers who were simultaneously themselves and the ancestors — constituted a judicial institution: a masquerade's pronouncement carried the authority of the ancestral world. This was not a weak system; it was a system designed to distribute power broadly enough that no single person or lineage could seize and hold it.

**Igbo-Ukwu and the deep commercial connections.** The excavations at Igbo-Ukwu in the 1950s and 1960s revealed ninth-century bronzes and copper-alloy objects of extraordinary technical sophistication — vessels cast in forms that imitate organic objects (a vessel that looks exactly like a snail shell with a bronze fly on the rim), a priest-chief's regalia buried intact. The objects contain copper and bronze; the copper came from sources far from Igboland. Igbo-Ukwu also contained glass beads from India or the Middle East. In the ninth century CE, a Igbo ritual leader in a forest community was being buried with objects that connected his world to the Indian Ocean trade. The assumption that forest Africa was isolated from wider commercial networks is simply wrong.

**The Arochukwu oracle as institution.** The Ibini Ukpabi oracle at Arochukwu — the "Long Juju" of colonial accounts — was one of the most important institutions in southeastern Nigeria from the seventeenth century onward. People came from hundreds of miles away with disputes, questions, and offerings. The Aro traders who maintained the oracle also maintained commercial networks across the entire lower Niger and Cross River region. The oracle's pronouncements had the weight of divine authority backed by the Aro traders' commercial power. When the oracle pronounced that someone should be "eaten by the Long Juju" — taken away, apparently consumed — they were actually sold into the Atlantic slave trade. The oracle was the institutional mechanism by which the Atlantic slave trade was fed from the Igbo interior. This is not an external imposition on the Igbo world; it was an internal institution that was captured and corrupted by the trade's incentives.

**Chi and the individual cosmology.** The Igbo concept of chi — the personal spirit, the individual divine essence — places the cosmological at the center of personal identity. Your chi is your personal god, your fate, your companion in the journey from the spirit world to the living world and back. The famous Igbo proverb "When the time comes for a man to die, his chi agrees" is not fatalism; it is a statement about the relationship between personal destiny and divine order. Chinua Achebe's *Things Fall Apart*, the most widely read African novel, is built entirely on the logic of chi and its encounter with colonial disruption — it is Igbo philosophical anthropology in fictional form.

**The masquerade as governance.** The masquerades — Mmọ, the ancestral presences that appeared at festivals, at funerals, at judicial proceedings — were the institutional form of ancestral governance. When the masquerade appeared, it was the ancestor made present. Its pronouncements were not the pronouncements of the performer inside the costume; they were the voice of the dead speaking to the living. This was not theater, though it used theatrical means; it was the mechanism through which the community maintained its relationship with those who had gone before and through whom the laws and customs of the community were enforced. The masquerade tradition continues in Igbo communities today.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Village Assembly:** An elder in an Igbo village is speaking in the oha. He is not the chief; there is no chief. He holds the Ozo title and he has been speaking well for forty years. The assembly will decide this matter — a land dispute — through his words and the words of others. This takes time. It is supposed to take time.

**2 — The Igbo-Ukwu Burial:** A priest-chief is being interred with his regalia. The copper-alloy vessel that looks like a snail shell is placed with him. The glass beads from the far east are laid out. Whoever made these decisions about what mattered understood something about connection to the wider world.

**3 — The Oracle:** At Arochukwu, a supplicant has traveled four days to ask the oracle about his dispute. The Aro guide is explaining the procedures. The oracle speaks from within the cave. The pronouncement comes out. The supplicant does not know that the "oracle" has commercial agents. He knows only that the answer is authoritative.

**4 — The Slave Trade's Logic:** An Aro trader has a coffle of captives for the coast. Some are criminals sold by the oracle. Some are prisoners from a war. Some are people who were simply in the wrong place. The Atlantic market does not distinguish between these categories. The interior pays a price it will spend a century counting.

**5 — Chinua Achebe Reads His Notes:** In the 1950s, a young man from Ogidi is writing a novel about his grandfather's world. He is not mourning a lost civilization; he is asserting its reality against the colonial accounts that denied it. The Igbo world he describes was complex and had its contradictions. He knows this. He is not making a monument; he is making a portrait.
