# Čechové — Království České — "The Czechs — The Kingdom of Bohemia"

We are the pivot between Latin West and Orthodox East, between German culture and Slavic culture, between the reforming impulse of the conciliar movement and the institutional weight of the Roman Church. The Crown of Bohemia — the kingdoms of Bohemia, Moravia, Silesia, and Lusatia — was one of the most coveted prizes in medieval central Europe: rich in silver, positioned at the crossroads of the major trade routes, and governed by a people who had been, at their height under Charles IV, the center of the Holy Roman Empire itself. Prague under Charles IV was the largest city north of the Alps, the seat of the first central European university (founded 1348), and the capital of an emperor who genuinely attempted to be both a Czech king and a universal sovereign.

The Hussite reformation is the first great break within Western Christianity — a century before Luther. Jan Hus's challenge to the institutional Church, his execution at Constance in 1415, and the Hussite wars that followed demonstrated that a religious reform movement, given sufficient popular support and sufficient military organization, could hold off the combined military power of Catholic Europe for fifteen years. The Hussites did not win permanently — Bohemia remained Catholic and Protestant, contested and divided, for the next two centuries. But they demonstrated that the Church could be challenged from within its own territory, by its own people, using the same theological tools the Church had provided. The question Hus raised — about the authority of Scripture versus the authority of the institutional church — would not be answered until 1648, and even that answer was violent.

## What Defined Them

**Charles IV and the imperial vision.** Charles IV (r. 1346–1378) was simultaneously Czech king and Holy Roman Emperor, and he was the only medieval ruler who genuinely attempted to govern both roles from a central European base. His Prague was the most ambitious urban project of the fourteenth century: the New Town (Nové Město), the Charles Bridge, St. Vitus Cathedral expanded, the university founded, all in a decade. His *Golden Bull* of 1356 codified the imperial electoral system in law. His *Maiestas Carolina* attempted to codify Czech law. He was a patron of Czech literature (he wrote his autobiography in Latin and Czech), a promoter of the Czech language in the royal chancery, and a genuine believer that the Czech lands, at the center of Europe, deserved to be the center of European civilization. For one generation, they were.

**The university and the reform tradition.** The University of Prague, founded by Charles IV in 1348, was the first university in central Europe. Its theological faculty became the hotbed of the reform movement that led to Jan Hus: the English reformer John Wycliffe's texts circulated at Prague through Czech students who had studied at Oxford, and his arguments — that the Church had no authority not grounded in Scripture, that corrupt clergy had no valid sacramental powers — found a receptive audience in a Bohemian theological culture already critical of ecclesiastical wealth. The university was the intellectual engine of the Hussite revolution, and its existence is why Bohemia, rather than England where Wycliffe originated, became the site of the first successful reformation.

**Jan Hus and the cost of principle.** Jan Hus (1369–1415) was not primarily a political revolutionary. He was a university preacher who taught at the Bethlehem Chapel in Prague in Czech — not in Latin — to audiences of thousands, arguing from Scripture that the Church's wealth and institutional corruption contradicted its own teaching. When the Council of Constance summoned him and promised him a safe conduct, he went — believing that he could defend his positions intellectually. The council burned him anyway. His death created the Hussite movement more effectively than his life had: a man who had preached about clerical corruption died as a martyr to clerical injustice, and Bohemia went to war.

**Žižka and the Hussite military revolution.** Jan Žižka (c. 1360–1424) was, along with Hus, the other founder of the Hussite movement — but where Hus was a theologian, Žižka was a military innovator. He developed the *Wagenburg* — the wagon fortress, a mobile defensive line of chained war-wagons mounted with artillery and handgunners — that defeated every crusading army sent against Bohemia between 1420 and 1434. Žižka had never commanded an army before the Hussite wars. He went blind in 1421 and continued to command. He died of plague before the wars ended, having never lost a battle. His army used field artillery as an offensive weapon decades before any other European force had worked out how to do it.

**The Czech language as a theological statement.** The decision to preach, teach, and eventually govern in Czech rather than Latin was not a practical convenience — it was a theological position. The Hussite insistence that the Bible must be available in the vernacular, that the liturgy must be understood by the people who participated in it, that the *utraquist* communion (receiving both bread and wine, not just the bread reserved for laypeople in Catholic practice) was a right, not a privilege: these were arguments about what the church was for and who it served. The Czech Bible, the Czech hymns, the Czech legal texts of the post-Hussite period: these were the institutional expression of a theology of the common person.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Přemyslids:** Václav (Wenceslas) I of Bohemia, the "Good King Wenceslas" of the carol, is murdered by his brother Boleslav in 929. He is canonized within a generation and becomes the patron saint of Bohemia — the sacred king whose martyrdom founds the Czech Christian tradition. The kingdom is built on a murder and a saint simultaneously.

**2 — The Golden Age of the Luxembourgs:** Charles IV walks across the bridge he has just commissioned — the Charles Bridge is not built yet; he is looking at the old Judith Bridge and imagining what he will replace it with. He has been emperor for three years. He has twenty more years to build. Prague will be the greatest city north of the Alps by the time he is done.

**3 — The Hussite Revolution:** Žižka receives the report that another crusade is approaching. The papal army is thirty thousand strong; he has fifteen thousand. He orders the wagons chained and the artillery loaded. He has never lost a battle. The crusaders have never faced war-wagons and handguns used in this way. The outcome is already determined in his mind.

**4 — Post-Hussite Bohemia:** George of Poděbrady is elected King of Bohemia in 1458 — the only elected Czech king. He is a Hussite (utraquist), and the Pope refuses to recognize him. George spends his reign trying to govern a kingdom that the papacy considers illegitimate and that the Catholic powers around him want to crusade against. He manages it for thirteen years.

**5 — Under the Habsburgs:** The Defenestration of Prague, 1618: three Catholic royal officials are thrown from the windows of Prague Castle by Protestant Czech nobles. They survive — the Catholic side claims they were saved by angels; the Protestant side says they fell into a midden. The Thirty Years War begins. Two years later, at White Mountain, the Czech Protestant nobility is destroyed and Bohemia is re-Catholicized by force. The Czech language retreats from the court for two hundred years. It does not disappear.
