# Srivijaya — The Mandala of the Sea

We are Srivijaya, and we were not a state in the way that a land empire is a state. We were a mandala of the sea — a center of radiant commercial and religious authority surrounded by tributary port cities that acknowledged our prestige and shared in our prosperity. Our capital at Palembang, on the Musi River in southern Sumatra, commanded the Malacca Strait — the narrow passage through which the trade of the Indian Ocean world moved toward China and back. Whoever controlled Palembang could tax that trade; we controlled Palembang for six centuries.

We were also the greatest Buddhist kingdom of the maritime world. Our king Jayanasa, in the late seventh century, sent a monk named Yijing to India and kept him at Palembang for several years when he returned, where he translated Buddhist sutras into Chinese with Javanese, Malay, and Srivijayan scholars assisting. Yijing's account tells us that Srivijaya had over a thousand Buddhist monks; that the city was a center of Buddhist learning visited by scholars from across Asia; that a monk traveling from China to India was well advised to spend time studying in Palembang first. We did not just trade; we were a Buddhist university through which the East-West dharmic exchange flowed.

## What Defined Them

**The mandala polity.** Srivijaya was a mandala state: not a territory with defined borders but a center of prestige and power from which authority radiated outward, acknowledged by surrounding ports in proportion to their distance from the center. Port lords acknowledged Srivijayan suzerainty through tribute missions; in return they received the right to trade under Srivijayan protection and access to the Buddhist learning centered at Palembang. The mandala was real power disguised as prestige — or prestige that generated real power.

**The Malacca Strait as the engine.** The Strait of Malacca between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra is 800 kilometers long and at its narrowest less than 60 kilometers wide. Through it passed the bulk of the maritime trade between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea. Srivijaya controlled the southern end of the strait and taxed everything that moved through it. This was the simplest and most reliable revenue system imaginable: position yourself at the world's most important maritime chokepoint and collect a percentage of the world's trade.

**Buddhist university.** Yijing's account of Palembang (671–695 CE) describes a city with over a thousand monks and a center of Buddhist scholarship that he recommends to any Chinese monk heading to India for texts. The Srivijayan court sponsored the translation of Buddhist texts, maintained the monastic community, and used the prestige of Buddhist patronage to attract scholars from across Asia. The Buddhist university of Palembang was not incidental to Srivijayan power; it was part of the same prestige economy that made the tributary trade work.

**Javanese competition and the Chola expedition.** The Chola naval expedition of 1025 CE — launched by the south Indian Chola dynasty against Srivijayan port cities on the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra — was not a conquest but a disruption: the Chola were protecting Tamil merchant networks that Srivijaya was taxing too heavily. The expedition demonstrated Srivijaya's military vulnerability and accelerated the shift of commercial power toward the Javanese states and, eventually, toward the Majapahit model of more territorially grounded power.

**The mandala's dispersal.** When Srivijaya's commercial dominance ended, it did not collapse suddenly; it dispersed. The port cities that had acknowledged Srivijayan suzerainty gradually transferred their recognition to other centers — Majapahit in its territorial phase, and then the Islamic port-states that emerged from the fifteenth century onward. The mandala model itself was not abandoned; it was inherited by Melaka, Brunei, and the other Islamic sultanates that organized the maritime world on the same commercial-prestige principles, now combined with Islamic religious authority.

## The Chapters

**1 — Jayanasa:** He stands at the prow of his ship entering the Musi River. He controls the Malacca Strait. Every ship that passes pays a percentage. He is building a state out of geography. The state is the chokepoint.

**2 — Yijing at Palembang:** The Chinese monk has arrived from India. He has the texts he went to copy. He sits in the monastery at Palembang and begins translating. There are a thousand monks here. He will spend years. The city is a Buddhist university.

**3 — The Tributary Visit:** A Malay port lord sends tribute to Palembang. He has brought cloth, camphor, and the acknowledgment that Srivijaya is supreme. He receives in return: the right to trade under Srivijayan protection, access to the Buddhist learning, and the prestige of the relationship. The mandala functions.

**4 — The Chola Ships:** The Chola fleet approaches Kadaram (Kedah). The expedition has been sent from South India specifically to break Srivijayan control of the trade routes. The port city falls. The message is sent: the Malacca Strait is no longer solely Srivijaya's domain.

**5 — The Dispersal:** The great mandala of the sea does not collapse; it dissolves. The center of gravity shifts east to Java, where the Majapahit will build something different. The model of the port-state mandala is not forgotten; Islam will inherit it.
