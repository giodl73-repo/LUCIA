# B-wolof-1 — Jean Boulegue — Board Review of The First Atlantic Contact

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** The political history of the Grand Jolof from the thirteenth to sixteenth centuries; the Portuguese-Senegambian encounter; the dissolution of the Jolof Confederacy.

## Historical Accuracy

- **The chronology is sound.** The Portuguese first arrived at the Senegal River in the 1440s. Lancarote de Freitas is correctly identified as an early Portuguese captain in the Senegambian trade. The Bemoim embassy to Lisbon in 1488 is correctly dated. The conventional date of c. 1549 for Cayor's breakaway is used appropriately, with the qualification that the dissolution was a process.
- **The horse-for-captive exchange ratios (7-15 captives per horse) are within the range reported in Portuguese sources.** The variation reflects actual market conditions: breed quality, season, and the buyer's urgency all affected the price.
- **The Bemoim episode is accurately narrated.** The baptism, the military support from Joao II, the return expedition, and the killing by Pero Vaz da Cunha are all attested in Barros. The chapter correctly notes that the circumstances of the killing are disputed.
- **Minor concern:** The chapter refers to a "Njaay Sall" as part of the gewel lineage that kept the Burba Jolof's memory. The Sall lineage is indeed associated with gewel functions in the Wolof tradition, but the specific name is not firmly attested for this period. The text presents this cautiously ("they would say"), which is appropriate.

## Cultural Authenticity

- The chapter correctly represents the Jolof Confederacy as a loose federation, not a centralized state. The Burba Jolof's authority as mediation-based rather than coercion-based is accurate.
- The treatment of the jon within the Wolof legal framework is appropriate. The chapter avoids modern apologetics while presenting the Wolof understanding of the status.
- The gewel tradition as institutional memory and political authority is correctly represented.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The structural analysis of the confederacy's dissolution is the chapter's greatest strength. The argument that the Atlantic trade undermined the Burba Jolof's mediating role by giving coastal states independent access to the horse trade is a correct and important insight that aligns with the scholarly consensus.
- The treatment of Bemoim as a Wolof political actor using the Portuguese as tools in an internal power struggle is historically sound. This reframes the episode from the standard Portuguese narrative.
- The coexistence of the Saharan and Atlantic trade systems in Section IV is an important nuance that many popular accounts miss. The Saharan trade did not disappear; it was marginalized.

## What Needs Correction

- **The description of the Burba Jolof's compound.** The chapter places the Burba Jolof "in the shade of his receiving compound in the Jolof heartland." Archaeological work on the Jolof heartland is limited, and the specific character of the Burba Jolof's seat is not well documented. The description should remain general or be flagged as inference.
- **The tsetse fly and horse lifespan.** The chapter states horses lasted "perhaps three years. Five if the rains were kind." The actual range in the historical literature is broader: some sources suggest horses could survive somewhat longer in the drier Sahelian zones away from riverine tsetse habitats. The Senegambian coast, however, was more heavily affected. The chapter's estimates are defensible for the coastal and riverine zones but should not be generalized to the entire Wolof territory.

## Verdict: APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

Conditions: (1) Acknowledge that the Burba Jolof's compound description is inferential. (2) Qualify the horse lifespan figures as applying particularly to the coastal and riverine zones.
