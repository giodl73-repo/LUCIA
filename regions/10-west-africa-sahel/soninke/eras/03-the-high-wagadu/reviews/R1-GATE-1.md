# GATE-1 Scorecard — The High Wagadu

**Chapter:** Soninke / Era 3: The High Wagadu (c. 700-1076 CE)
**Scorer:** GATE-1
**Date:** 2026-04-16

| Dimension | Score | Evidence |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| Scene     | 4/5   | Opens with a specific moment: the virgin walking toward the well. A person, a place, a purpose. Not a single named individual, but the opening anchors in a ritual moment that is vivid and specific. The shift to Al-Bakri's twin-city description provides a second scene. Loses the top mark because the opening figure is archetypal (the virgin) rather than individually named. |
| World     | 5/5   | The Bida covenant is presented as operating logic, not myth. The twin-city structure emerges naturally. The culture's own categories (sacred kingship vs. merchant Islam, intermediary position, covenant-as-contract) shape the entire piece. The world has its own gravity. |
| Honesty   | 5/5   | No outside judgment. The Bida serpent covenant is presented as political economy, not superstition. No forbidden words. No comparisons to Western systems. The culture is so fully itself that no outside vantage is available. The "covenant with Bida was a theory of political economy expressed in the language of the sacred" is inside-voice honesty: it uses the culture's own logic to explain itself. |
| Texture   | 4/5   | Gold-plated harness, drum hollowed from a single trunk, calabash pans, rust-colored water, harmattan wind, millet and sorghum, shea clay soil. Al-Bakri's details are well-deployed. Could use more on smell and sound beyond the drum and muezzin call. The well at the opening needs more physical grounding. |
| Voice     | 5/5   | The prose rhythm is measured, deliberate, building in layers like the intermediary economics it describes. Paratactic construction in key moments ("The covenant held. The virgin walked. The gold moved."). The voice belongs to a trade culture: patient, accumulating, structural. Not steppe rhythm, not court rhythm, but Sahelian crossroads rhythm. |
| Pull      | 4/5   | The Bida covenant creates genuine tension: the prosperity depends on sacrifice, and the reader wants to know what happens when the covenant breaks (which the era title's end date of 1076 implies). The twin-city structure raises questions about how the balance held. Strong structural pull, though the lack of a single named protagonist slightly reduces the personal pull. |
| **Total** | **27/30** | |

**Notes:** Passes GATE-1 comfortably. Strengths: the covenant-as-political-economy framing is the chapter's distinctive intellectual contribution. The twin-city structure as "the architecture of interdependence" is well-conceived. Voice is excellent. Two areas to watch going forward: (1) the opening could benefit from more physical specificity at the well, and (2) the chapter will need named figures. Al-Bakri's account provides the king Tunka Manin; the oral tradition provides the Bida legend with specific names. The panel should push on whether the archetype-opening (the virgin) needs to become a named-individual opening, and whether the Bida covenant should be grounded in more specific sensory detail at the well.

**Innovations flagged:** The "intermediary economics as structural engine" framing, if sustained through the full chapter, may represent a novel application of the Structural Engine rubric amendment. Trade-position cultures have not been written yet, and the logic of "wealth from position, not production" could become a reusable engine for other intermediary peoples (Phoenicians, Sogdians, Swahili).
