## Analysis

**Strengths:**
1. **Perfect scene anchoring** — The opening is rooted in a single moment (July 8, 1853, morning, Uraga fortification, Tōjirō) and never drifts from it. Every detail serves the scene.
2. **Cultural worldview emerges naturally** — Sakoku is not explained; it is the unseen protagonist. The boundary's two-century hold is palpable through what Tōjirō expects and what the ships violate.
3. **Authority uncertainty as narrative engine** — The shift from Tōjirō (civilian harbor master) to Nakajima (military commander) shows power realigning in real time. Who decides? The scene presents the question without answering it.
4. **Material object as voice** — The black ships are not described as "advanced" or "powerful." They are described as moving "with an ease that defied the mathematics of its size." The prose lets the ships' properties carry the argument.
5. **Sparseness as strength** — No unnecessary explanation. The reader understands sakoku through Tōjirō's knowledge of it, not through editorial definition.
6. **Honesty at the margins** — The hesitation in Tōjirō ("In thirty years, no one had fired. In thirty years, the edict had been enough.") is not judgment; it is genuine uncertainty about whether the system has the capacity to defend itself.

**Potential development areas for panel:**
- The parallel between "What do we do?" (Tōjirō) and "What does this mean?" (the empire) — is there more to explore about the speed of decision-making in a rigid system?
- The voice's silence about Tōjirō's interior fear — is that intentional, or worth adding in the full chapter?

**Rubric innovations detected:**
- None. This is strong, traditional application of v1.5 standards. The scene-anchoring is textbook Gate-5 work.
