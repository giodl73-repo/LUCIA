# Figures — Era 2: The Island Expansion
## Arawak / Lokono | c. 500 BCE–700 CE

---

## On the Figure Problem

No named individual from the Arawak/Lokono community of c. 500 BCE–700 CE appears in any surviving source. The written record of this era consists entirely of archaeological assemblages: Saladoid ceramic sites, settlement patterns, botanical remains, linguistic reconstruction. The figures in this chapter are composite constructions, built from the archaeological and ethnographic record and disclosed here as such.

This is not a limitation that requires apology. It is the source situation of any chapter written about a non-literate culture in this era, and the chapter uses the anonymous figure technique confirmed in the v1.4 rubric amendment: a figure does not need a name, but a figure needs a body. Each figure in this chapter is described through their own sensory experience — the hands that know the matapi, the eyes reading the reef — not through outside observers. The anonymity is the collective voice.

---

## Kurima

The chapter's opening figure. A young man of the Orinoco delta community, making his first crossing to the Lesser Antilles. He is named — a Lokono word for "bridge" or "crossing place," constructed to be plausible in the Arawak language family — but he is not a historical individual. He is the composite of the archaeological evidence: the Saladoid-tradition communities that moved from the Orinoco delta into Trinidad and then up the island chain, beginning approximately 500 BCE.

What the archaeology tells us about him: he was part of a community with established ceramic traditions (Saladoid white-on-red ware), manioc agriculture (cassava-press equipment archaeologically attested), and canoe-based trade (inter-island movement of ceramic and lithic objects). He moved into a world that was not uninhabited — Trinidad had pre-Saladoid Archaic populations — and the evidence suggests that the Saladoid arrivals and the earlier populations existed in proximity, with cultural exchange in both directions. The chapter does not address the prior populations in detail, which is a scope decision (Era 2 focuses on the expansion itself); the figures.md notes it for context.

His reef moment — the moment of halting before the unfamiliar water and following a local guide — is not attested in any source. It is constructed as a plausible scene from what we know: the crossing was skilled labor, not accident; the reef navigation required local knowledge; knowledge was passed between individuals as a practical necessity. The scene is honest to the conditions without claiming historical specificity it cannot have.

## Aiyana

The manioc-processing figure. Named with a word used across several Arawak-family languages for "eternal blossom" or "flowering forever," chosen for its wide distribution in the language family rather than as a claim to specific community of origin.

She represents the evidence class that the archaeology preserves more reliably than the canoe crossings: ceramic assemblages, botanical remains, and the manioc-processing equipment (stone griddles, ceramic griddles, evidence of the fiber-press technique) found at Saladoid sites from the Orinoco delta to Puerto Rico. The matapi itself (the fiber squeeze basket) is not preserved in the archaeological record — organic materials rarely survive — but it is attested ethnographically in every Arawak-speaking community studied from the colonial period onward, and the starch residues on stone griddles from Saladoid sites confirm manioc processing throughout the expansion.

The chemistry described in her section — linamarin, the hydrolysis to hydrogen cyanide, the juice as the danger rather than the fiber — is biochemically accurate. The knowledge that this was the danger was demonstrably held by the processing communities: the specific technique of extraction under pressure, followed by extended drying or high-temperature cooking, would not have developed without understanding that the liquid was toxic. Whether the Arawak communities of 500 BCE conceptualized this in terms we would recognize as "chemistry" is a different question; the chapter presents her knowledge as embodied and practical, which is historically honest.

Her presence in the chapter addresses the PANEL-1 flag about women's knowledge. The archaeological evidence is unambiguous: manioc processing and ceramic production in Saladoid communities were predominantly female activities. The knowledge that made the expansion possible — the portable food technology, the ceramic tradition — was carried primarily by women. The chapter's architecture reflects this: Aiyana's section is not a supplement to the "main" story of canoe crossings; it is the chapter's causal argument about how the expansion worked.

## Waya

The ceramic figure. Named with a word meaning "water" in several Arawak-family languages, chosen for the coastal significance. She represents the potters of the Saladoid tradition — the makers of the white-on-red painted ware and zone-incised crosshatch decoration that archaeologists use as the migration signature.

The Saladoid ceramic tradition is the chapter's primary archaeological evidence. Its distribution — from the lower Orinoco through Trinidad and the Lesser Antilles to Puerto Rico, with the earliest dates around 500–400 BCE and the tradition persisting through several centuries — traces the island expansion in material form. Irving Rouse's synthesis of the Saladoid sequence (1992) remains the standard reference; Arie Boomert's study of the Trinidad-Orinoco interface (2000) adds detail on the gateway zone.

The zone-incised crosshatch pattern described in her section is a real decorative convention in the Saladoid assemblage. It appears consistently enough across sites that archaeologists use it as a diagnostic marker. The chapter's rendering of it as a "network certificate" — a mark of belonging that the maker applied consciously to trade-moving vessels — is an interpretive claim, not a documented fact. The claim is consistent with what we know about how network goods were marked in analogous trade systems; it is presented as interpretation, not certainty.

The detail that the island clay required different tempering (crushed shell rather than organic temper) is attested: island Saladoid ceramics show temper composition adjustments that reflect locally available materials while maintaining the decorative and formal conventions of the mainland tradition. The hands adjusted; the tradition persisted.

## The Unnamed Lonko

The governance figure is deliberately unnamed and undescribed in physical terms. What the archaeology and the later ethnographic record give us is functional description: the lonko (used here as the generic term; the specific term varies across Arawak-family communities) was the leader whose authority derived from network relationships rather than coercive force or hereditary title.

The network-management knowledge attributed to the lonko in Section 4 is consistent with the evidence from later Arawak communities documented by colonial-era observers, and with the anthropological literature on chiefdom societies more broadly. The specific claim — that a lonko's authority depended on knowing the network's social geography, and that leaders who lost this knowledge slid toward the edges — is an inference from the comparative literature on kinship-based trade systems, consistent with the Lokono evidence but not directly attested from this era.

The enforcement mechanism (social exclusion rather than punishment) is documented in ethnographic accounts of Lokono and Taíno communities and is consistent with the network's lack of coercive infrastructure. It is applied here to the Saladoid-era communities as a conservative inference: the network of this era was simpler than the later Taíno chiefdom system, but the reciprocity mechanisms were analogous.

## The Fisher at the Reef

Unnamed, undescribed beyond his function: the person who knows the reef channel and gestures for Kurima to follow. He is not a Lokono community member — he is a member of the island's pre-existing population, or of an earlier Saladoid-arrivals community already established. His presence in the chapter makes a specific historical claim: that the Saladoid expansion did not enter a void. Trinidad and the Lesser Antilles had Archaic-tradition populations before the Saladoid arrivals, and the two groups coexisted and exchanged knowledge. The fisher's reef-channel gesture is the chapter's image of this exchange: knowledge moving from the established population to the arriving one, in the same direction that the network's goods moved from the arriving Saladoid communities to the wider island world.

This is historically honest. The archaeological evidence for Archaic-Saladoid coexistence on Trinidad and some Lesser Antilles islands is documented in Boomert (2000) and earlier work by Harris (1973). The chapter does not develop the Archaic populations' own story — that would require a different chapter, possibly a different people-entry — but it does not erase them.
