## Corrections

**1. The Gratian marriage example**
The chapter uses the following example for Gratian's distinctio: "A canon from an early council permitted a slave to marry without his master's consent. A decretal from a later pope appeared to require consent for any marriage to be valid." The chapter then resolves this as a distinction between consent required for validity (the parties' own consent) versus consent required for licitness (social permission).

The example is plausible as a Gratian-type case, but the specific resolution in the chapter conflates two distinct categories. In Gratian's canon law, the validity of a marriage depended on the free consent of the parties — and "free consent" was precisely what was at issue for a slave, since a slave's consent given under fear of punishment might not be freely given. The resolution the chapter proposes (consent for validity vs. consent for licitness) is correct as a general Gratian technique, but applied to this specific example it is slightly off: the issue for a slave marriage was whether the slave's consent could be "free" consent, not whether the master's consent was "required for validity." Gratian's actual position was that a slave could contract a valid marriage without the master's consent, because consent is a matter of the soul, not the body, and the master's authority over the body does not extend to the soul.

The example is close enough to be illustrative, but the resolution should be: the canon permitting slave marriage without master's consent rested on the principle that consent is a spiritual act beyond the master's jurisdiction; a later decretal requiring certain formalities did not override this because the formalities addressed a different context (publicly contracted marriages among the free). The distinction is between the forum internum (the internal, spiritual domain) and the forum externum (the public, legal domain).

This is a technical correction that does not require rewriting the passage substantially, but the resolution should more precisely state: the early canon addressed the slave's own consent as a spiritual act; the later decretal addressed formalities of public marriage contracts. They were not in conflict because they governed different forums.

**Action required:** Minor revision to the Gratian resolution paragraph to clarify the forum distinction.

**2. The confession canon — penalty clause**
The chapter states, regarding the confession canon (Fourth Lateran, canon 21): "He was to be deposed from his priestly office and confined to a strict monastery to do penance for the rest of his life." This is accurate. Canon 21 states exactly this as the penalty for a priest who violates the seal of confession. Confirmed.

**3. The Raymond of Penafort commission**
The chapter states: "Gregory IX commissioned Raymond of Penafort, a Dominican lawyer, to compile and organize them. Raymond finished in 1234." This is accurate. Raymond was a Dominican, trained in canon law at Bologna; he completed the compilation in 1234. Confirmed.

## Clarification

**The distinctio as a general technique**
The chapter is correct that Gratian's fundamental method was the distinctio, and that this method allowed the system to resolve apparent conflicts between canons. However, it should be noted (perhaps in figures.md or notes.md, not necessarily in the chapter) that Gratian did not invent this method — he borrowed it from the scholastic dialectical method being developed at the same time in theology (Peter Abelard's Sic et Non used a similar approach). What Gratian did was apply the method systematically to the entire accumulated canon law tradition. The originality was in the comprehensiveness, not the technique.

This is not a required correction for the chapter's purposes — the chapter correctly describes what Gratian did without claiming he invented the method — but worth noting for future reference.
