# Ērānshahr — The Empire of the Iranians

We called our empire *Ērānshahr* — the empire of the Iranians — and in that name we declared something the Achaemenids and Parthians had not declared quite so directly: that Iran was a civilization, not merely a territory, and that its civilization had a specific religious content. The Sassanid kings were the guardians of Zoroastrian orthodoxy, the defenders of the sacred fires, the protectors of the *magi* who maintained the ritual tradition that Zarathustra had established. The king of kings was not merely a political sovereign — he was the defender of *asha*, the cosmic truth, against *druj*, the Lie, against the Romans, against the Arabian desert, against the Central Asian steppe peoples, against every power that stood outside the light of Ahura Mazda.

We restored what the Arsacids had allowed to become partial and informal. The Zoroastrian sacred texts — the *Avesta* — were compiled under Sassanid patronage into something approaching a canonical form. The fire temples were reorganized, their hierarchy established, their revenues guaranteed by royal endowment. The *magi* became a proper priesthood with a defined relationship to the royal court. And the art of the Sassanid court — the great rock reliefs at Naqsh-e Rostam and Taq-e Bostan, the silver plates with their hunting scenes, the textiles that spread across the medieval world — declared in image what the inscriptions declared in word: this was Iran, this was the center of the world's order, and its king ruled by divine right.

## What Defined Them

**Zoroastrian kingship — the king as cosmic defender.** The Sassanid king of kings was not merely a political leader. He was the earthly representative of Ahura Mazda's order, the defender of the sacred fires that embodied the divine presence in the world. The investiture scenes carved on the rock faces of Iran — at Naqsh-e Rostam, at Taq-e Bostan — show the king receiving his crown directly from Ahura Mazda or from the divine yazata Mithra, establishing his legitimacy not through human institutions but through divine appointment. The sacred fires at the great temples — Adur Gushnasp (for warriors), Adur Burzen-Mihr (for farmers), Adur Farnbag (for priests) — were the empire's spiritual infrastructure, maintained by royal endowment and connected to the three estates of Zoroastrian society.

**The book of kings — the historical tradition.** The Sassanids were intensely conscious of their place in a long Iranian historical tradition. The *Khwadaynamag* — the "Book of Lords," a Pahlavi prose history of the Iranian kings from mythological origins to the Sassanid present — was compiled and maintained at the Sassanid court and became the primary source for Ferdowsi's *Shahnameh* a thousand years later. The Sassanid kings positioned themselves as the heirs of the legendary Iranian hero-kings — Jamshid, Feridun, Kay Khosrow — and their court maintained the oral traditions about these figures in literary form. This historical consciousness — the sense that Sassanid Iran was the culmination of a continuous Iranian civilization stretching back to the beginning of time — was the ideological complement to the Zoroastrian religious framework.

**Khusrow I and the translation movement.** The reign of Khusrow I Anushirvan (531–579 CE) was the Sassanid cultural peak: the king who reorganized the tax system on more rational principles, who defeated Justinian's Byzantium and forced a humiliating peace, and who made the Sassanid court the collection point for the Greek philosophical tradition that Byzantium was closing down. When Justinian closed the Platonic Academy in Athens in 529 CE, the philosophers who were displaced found welcome at Khusrow's court. Indian scholars arrived with chess (which Khusrow introduced to the Iranian world) and mathematical texts. The Sassanid court translated Greek and Indian texts into Pahlavi — the Sassanid literary language — creating a bilingual scholarly tradition that would feed into the early Islamic translation movement at Baghdad.

**The cataphract cavalry and the strategic balance.** The Sassanid military was built around heavy armored cavalry — the *asavaran*, the horsemen — whose combination of lance and composite bow made them the most effective cavalry force of the late ancient world. The perpetual Roman/Byzantine-Sassanid border wars along the Euphrates and in Armenia were essentially a four-century standoff between two powers that could wound each other badly but not deliver the decisive blow. The Sassanids conquered Roman Armenia and held it; Byzantium retook it; the wars went on. Neither side solved the stalemate militarily. The Islamic expansion of the 630s solved it from outside, by destroying the Sassanid empire in a decade and reducing Byzantium to its Aegean core.

**The legacy carried into Islam.** When the Arab armies took Ctesiphon in 637 CE and the last Sassanid king fled east, Persian civilization did not end. It went underground into the administrative class, the scholarly class, the courtly culture that had built the Sassanid state, and reemerged within a century as the Persian synthesis that transformed the Abbasid caliphate. The Abbasid court at Baghdad was, in many of its forms, a Sassanid court that had converted to Islam: the same administrative structures, the same courtly etiquette, the same literary genres, the same interest in philosophy and medicine, now conducted in Arabic and oriented toward Quranic scholarship. Iran absorbed Islam while simultaneously Persianizing it.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Founding:** Ardashir defeats the last Arsacid king and carves his investiture scene on the cliff at Naqsh-e Rostam, placing himself in direct visual dialogue with the Achaemenid tombs above him. The message is explicit: the Sassanids are the Achaemenids returned, with a Zoroastrian clarity the Arsacids lacked.

**2 — The Classical Sassanid State:** Shapur I captures the Roman emperor Valerian in battle — the only Roman emperor ever taken prisoner by a foreign enemy — and has the scene carved on the rock face at Naqsh-e Rostam. The humiliation of Rome is made permanent in stone.

**3 — Khusrow I and the Golden Age:** The philosophers from Athens arrive at Ctesiphon. Khusrow receives them and asks to hear their arguments about Plato and Aristotle. The Indian ambassador arrives with a chess set. Someone is writing all of this down in Pahlavi. The court at Ctesiphon is, for a moment, the most intellectually active place in the world.

**4 — The Final Wars and the Collapse:** Khusrow II conquers Syria and Egypt — nearly destroys Byzantium — and then loses everything in the Byzantine counterattack. The empire is exhausted. The treasury is empty. The army is depleted. When the Arab armies cross the Euphrates in the 630s, there is not enough left to stop them. The last king of kings flees to Khorasan and is assassinated by his own people. The sacred fires are not extinguished — they persist for centuries — but the empire that maintained them is gone.
