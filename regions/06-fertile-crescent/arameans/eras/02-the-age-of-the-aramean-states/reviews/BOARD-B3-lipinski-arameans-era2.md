## B-3 — Edward Lipinski — Board Review of Arameans Era 2

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Aramean religion, Aramaic language history, West Semitic cultural studies.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The treatment of Hadad as the principal Aramean deity is correct. The Aramean storm god was worshipped under various forms (Hadad, Baal-Hadad, Baal-Shamayn) across the Aramean states. The chapter correctly notes both Hadad (in the Tel Dan Stele) and Baal-Shamayn (in the Zakkur Stele) without conflating them.
- The description of Atargatis and her cult at Hierapolis (Manbij) is appropriately placed as a developing phenomenon. The great sanctuary at Hierapolis is primarily attested in later periods (Hellenistic-Roman), but the cult's roots in Iron Age Aramean goddess worship are well-established.
- The Sfire treaty curse formulas are accurately quoted from the published inscriptions.
- The characterization of Aramaic as alphabetic (using the Phoenician-derived script) and its contrast with cuneiform is accurate. The chapter correctly identifies the script's accessibility as a key factor in Aramaic's spread.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The description of Aramean religion as household-based and portable is a sound characterization. The contrast with the monumental temple-based religions of Mesopotamia is legitimate, though it should be noted that the Aramean states did have urban temples (the temple at Tell Halaf, the temple at Gozan). The household cult coexisted with urban sanctuaries rather than replacing them.
- The passage on the portability of Aramean religion mirroring the portability of the language is an elegant analytical parallel that is supported by the evidence of Aramean religious practices spreading along the same commercial routes as the language.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The theological language of the inscriptions (Hadad going before the army, Baal-Shamayn answering through seers) is accurately represented and correctly contextualized as royal theology, not folk religion.
- The Sfire treaties' use of divine witnesses and curse formulas is correctly identified as juridical rather than merely literary.

**What needs correction:**
- Minor: The chapter states that Aramean religious practice "did not require a permanent temple or a professional priesthood." This overstates the case. The Aramean states did maintain temples with professional cult personnel. The household-level practice was an additional layer, not a replacement for institutional religion. The chapter should acknowledge the temple dimension alongside the household dimension.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (one correction as noted above)
