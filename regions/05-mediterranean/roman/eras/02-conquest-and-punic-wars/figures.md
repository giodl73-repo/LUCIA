# Figures — The Conquest of Italy and the Punic Wars (Era 2)

## Hannibal Barca (c. 247-183 BCE)

Carthaginian general. Commander of the Carthaginian army in the Second Punic War. Crossed the Alps, won three major battles on Italian soil, occupied Italy for sixteen years, and lost the war.

**What we know:** Hannibal is well-attested in Polybius (who had access to both Roman and Carthaginian sources), Livy, and Appian. His tactical brilliance is not in dispute. His Alpine crossing, his victories at Trebia, Trasimene, and Cannae, and his defeat at Zama are established historical events. After the war, he served as a suffete (magistrate) in Carthago, attempted political reform, and eventually fled to the Seleucid court and then to Bithynia, where he poisoned himself to avoid capture by Roma in 183 BCE.

**What we cannot know:** Hannibal's interiority. The sources are Roman or pro-Roman. The Carthaginian written record is almost entirely lost. The chapter presents Hannibal from within his world as far as the sources allow: a Carthaginian serving his city's interests, a professional soldier from a military family, a man whose oath against Roma (whether literal or literary) defined his career.

**Sources:** Polybius, *Histories* 3, 9-11, 15; Livy 21-30; Lancel, *Hannibal* (1998); Hoyos, *Hannibal's Dynasty* (2003).

## Publius Cornelius Scipio Africanus (236-183 BCE)

Roman general. Defeated the Carthaginian position in Iberia and Hannibal at Zama. The first Roman to carry a cognomen earned in war.

**What we know:** Well-attested in Polybius and Livy. His command in Iberia (210-206 BCE) and his African campaign (204-201 BCE) are documented in detail. His relationship with the Senate and the tension between his individual fame and the Republic's collective ethic are themes in the ancient sources. He retired to Liternum and his tomb inscription reportedly asked that Roma not receive his remains.

**What we cannot know:** Whether his self-exile was voluntary or forced, bitter or philosophical. The chapter presents the structural tension (individual glory vs. senatorial collective) without psychologizing the individual.

**Sources:** Polybius 10, 14-16; Livy 26-30, 38; Scullard, *Scipio Africanus: Soldier and Politician* (1970).

## Quintus Fabius Maximus Verrucosus (Cunctator) (c. 280-203 BCE)

Roman senator and dictator. Architect of the delay strategy against Hannibal after Cannae.

**What we know:** Fabius's strategy of avoiding pitched battle while shadowing Hannibal's army is well-attested. The strategy was controversial at the time but effective: it prevented Hannibal from winning the decisive political victory (breaking the alliance system) that he needed. Fabius was elected dictator in 217 BCE after Trasimene.

**Sources:** Polybius 3.87-94; Livy 22; Plutarch, *Fabius Maximus*.

## Marcus Porcius Cato (the Elder/Censor) (234-149 BCE)

Roman senator, censor, and orator. The voice of the old Republic confronting the wealth of the new. Famous for ending every Senate speech with "Carthago delenda est."

**What we know:** Cato is well-attested in his own writings (fragments of speeches, *De Agri Cultura*), in Plutarch's biography, and in Livy. His censorship (184 BCE) was marked by efforts to tax luxury and maintain old Roman austerity. His insistence on Carthago's destruction is in multiple sources. He wrote the first major historical work in Latin, the *Origines*.

**Sources:** Cato, *De Agri Cultura*; Plutarch, *Cato Maior*; Astin, *Cato the Censor* (1978).

## The Italian Ally (institutional/composite figure)

A soldier from an allied Italian community serving in the Roman army.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual allied soldier from this period can be named or characterized. The chapter references the allied system's contribution to Roman military manpower without attributing specific experiences to specific individuals. The allies' perspective is present through the system's structural logic (the dual promise of protection and participation) rather than through individual narrative.

**Sources:** Brunt, *Italian Manpower 225 BC-AD 14* (1971); Baronowski, *Polybius and Roman Imperialism* (2011).

## A Note on Sources

The Punic Wars are among the best-documented events of the ancient Mediterranean, thanks primarily to Polybius, a Greek hostage in Roma who wrote his Histories with access to both Roman and Carthaginian (through intermediaries) sources. Polybius is a careful historian who states his methods and evaluates his sources. Livy, writing later, is more literary and more invested in the moral narrative of Roman virtue. The chapter uses Polybius for factual claims and Livy for the tradition's self-understanding, distinguishing between the two where they diverge.

The Carthaginian perspective is largely lost. Carthago's own archives were destroyed in 146 BCE. What survives of the Carthaginian view comes through Greek and Roman intermediaries. The chapter presents Hannibal from within his world as far as possible while acknowledging that the surviving record is overwhelmingly Roman.
