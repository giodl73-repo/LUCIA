## Random Paragraph Test (v1.4 amendment)

Three paragraphs selected at random, read in isolation. Can the voice identify the culture without context?

**Paragraph 1** (Section 2, tracking passage): "He stops. Something has passed here recently. The undergrowth on the north side of the trail is disturbed — broken stems, the kind of break that bends before it snaps, which is an animal moving slowly, not a panicked run. The height of the disturbance is higher than a forest pig, lower than a bongo. He reads the impression in the soft ground — three toes forward, one back, a spread that tells him the animal's weight without telling him its fear."

Result: Unambiguously forest culture. The specificity of the tracking reading (bending vs. snapping, three toes forward, one back) is not portable to any other setting. The voice is intimate and observational. PASSES.

**Paragraph 2** (Section 3, Arom passage): "In the 1970s, the French musicologist Simha Arom traveled to the Central African Republic and set up his recording equipment near an Aka camp. He was a careful, serious scholar with decades of work in African music. He had notation systems capable of rendering rhythmic complexity that no Western composition had ever required. He began to record. He listened to the recordings. He could not write down what he heard."

Result: This is the borderline paragraph. It reads like a historical account rather than the Forest-Intimate voice — the scholar, the dates, the recording equipment. However, this is the "outsider-as-structural-evidence" technique (v1.5): the paragraph exists to establish the outsider's encounter as evidence of the inside system's distinctiveness. The voice is cooler here than in surrounding paragraphs, but the paragraph's function is to demonstrate the encounter, not to inhabit the forest world. The coolness is appropriate to the technique.

BORDERLINE — technically permissible but should be examined for whether the Forest-Intimate voice can carry more of the analytical work. The following paragraphs ("The Aka men and women who sing...") recover the register immediately. PASSES with note.

**Paragraph 3** (Section 5, farming exchange): "The exchange that developed was not a market. It did not have prices in any fixed sense, or contracts, or mediating institutions. It had relationships. Specific Baka families developed ongoing obligations with specific farming families — not transactions, but the same kind of relationship that governed the Baka's relationship with the honeyguide or the structure of the ekila system."

Result: The Forest-Intimate register is maintained even through structural analysis. The voice analyzes the exchange by rendering it in the relational terms the Baka themselves would use (obligation, relationship, the same logic as the honeyguide). No academic commentary intrudes. PASSES.
