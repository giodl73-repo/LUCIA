# Lan Xang — The Million Elephants

We are Lan Xang — the Kingdom of a Million Elephants — the Lao kingdom of the Mekong midlands, the great river valley between the Annamite Cordillera and the highlands of the Thai world. Our kingdom was named for the elephants that were our symbol of power and our export to every neighboring court: Lan Xang elephants worked the teak forests, carried armies, and served as the living measure of royal prestige from Angkor to Ayutthaya to the Ming tribute missions. A king of Lan Xang who presented the Chinese emperor with a white elephant was making the most eloquent diplomatic statement available.

We were also the kingdom of Theravada Buddhism in the Mekong midlands. Fa Ngum, who founded Lan Xang in 1353 with the military support of the Khmer and the cultural resources of the Khmer court (his wife was a Khmer princess who brought the Pha Bang Buddha image that became the kingdom's palladium), established a state in which the Theravada sangha was the institutional framework of governance: the monks educated the population, managed the records, and provided the ritual legitimacy that the king required. The Pha Bang — the gold Buddha image — is still the religious center of Luang Prabang and gives the city its name.

## What Defined Them

**The Mekong as civilization's axis.** Lan Xang was a river kingdom in the most literal sense: the Mekong defined its extent, its trade, its communications, and its spiritual geography. The kingdom's power and character derived from control of the river corridor, and the towns that matter in Lao history — Luang Prabang, Vientiane, Savannakhet — are all Mekong towns. The river's annual flooding determined the agricultural calendar; the river's seasonally navigable stretches determined who traded with whom.

**The Pha Bang and Buddhist sovereignty.** The Pha Bang — a standing gold Buddha image said to have come from Lanka through Cambodia — was the palladium of Lao sovereignty: the image whose presence sanctified the capital and guaranteed divine protection. When Fa Ngum brought it from Angkor, he was not merely carrying an art object; he was transferring divine protection to his new kingdom. The image's current location in Luang Prabang, the city named for it, makes the city itself the living center of Lao Buddhist sovereignty.

**The elephant economy.** Lan Xang's trade position was built substantially on elephants: trained war elephants and work elephants that the kingdom's forests produced and that neighboring courts required. The elephant was simultaneously a symbol of royal power, a weapon of war, a working animal for the forest industries, and an export commodity. A Lan Xang ambassador to the Ming or to Ayutthaya typically arrived with elephants as gifts. The kingdom's strategic value to its neighbors was partly military (elephants could tip a battle) and partly prestige (white elephants were cosmic symbols of sovereignty).

**Pha That Luang — the national stupa.** The great stupa built by Setthathirath at Vientiane (1566) is the defining monument of Lao national identity: a gold-covered tower rising 45 meters from a series of decorated platforms, representing Mount Meru and housing Buddhist relics. When Vientiane was sacked by the Siamese in 1779 and by the Siamese again more thoroughly in 1828, Pha That Luang was damaged each time and restored each time. The stupa persists as the symbol of Lao sovereignty through all the political changes that followed.

**Souligna Vongsa's golden age.** The reign of Souligna Vongsa (1637–1694) is remembered as the golden age of Lan Xang: the kingdom at peace with all its neighbors, productive trade on the Mekong, the sangha flourishing, the arts at their height. European Jesuit missionaries visited his court and left favorable accounts. Dutch traders were active in the Mekong valley. The calm was partly his achievement and partly luck — the surrounding kingdoms were occupied with their own problems. When he died without a clear successor, the fragmentation that ended Lan Xang began within a generation.

## The Chapters

**1 — Fa Ngum:** He arrives with the Khmer armies and the Pha Bang. He is bringing Buddhism to the Mekong valley at the point of a spear. His Khmer wife brought the image; he brought the sword. Both are necessary. He unifies the Lao principalities into one kingdom.

**2 — The Pha Bang Arrives:** The gold Buddha image is carried in procession through Luang Prabang. The city has been renamed for it. The monks chant. The people prostrate. This image traveled from Lanka to Angkor to here. It is now the heart of the kingdom.

**3 — Pha That Luang:** Setthathirath surveys the new stupa rising at Vientiane. He has moved the capital south, away from the Burmese pressure. He is building the symbol of Lao sovereignty in gold. No matter what happens to the kingdom, the stupa will stand. He is right.

**4 — Souligna Vongsa's Court:** The Dutch trader sits in the audience hall in Vientiane. He is writing his report. He says: this king is just and the people are prosperous. The Mekong trade is orderly. The temples are magnificent. He means it. This is the Lan Xang that will be remembered.

**5 — Fragmentation:** The three claimants to the throne after Souligna Vongsa's death each take a portion of the kingdom. Luang Prabang, Vientiane, and Champasak are now separate kingdoms. Lan Xang is over. The river still flows. The Pha Bang still sits in Luang Prabang. The great stupa still stands in Vientiane. The kingdom is gone.
