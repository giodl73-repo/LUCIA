# Göktürks (Kök Türük) — Tengri and the Khaganate

We are the Kök Türük — the Celestial Turks, the people of the Blue Sky — and we are the first Türkic-speaking empire to stretch from the eastern steppes to the Black Sea, from the Great Wall of China to the gates of Byzantium. We take our name from Tengri — the Sky, the supreme divinity of the steppe world — and from the color of the sky itself: kök means blue, and blue means celestial authority. When our khagan raises his yak-tail standard and rides to war, he is not merely a warlord but the earthly representative of Tengri's mandate over the steppe world.

We emerged from the Altai Mountains in the sixth century CE, a people who had worked iron for the Rouran confederation before we overthrew our masters and established our own khaganate in 552 CE. Within a generation our territory ran from the Yellow River to the Caspian Sea, making us the largest empire the world had seen since the Han dynasty fell. We negotiated as equals with both the Chinese and Byzantine emperors simultaneously — our envoys in both capitals at the same time, our khagans receiving ambassadors from both. We also left something no previous steppe people had left in their own language: the Orkhon inscriptions — stone stelae in the Orkhon Valley of Mongolia, written in the runiform script that our people developed, recording our history in our own words. We are the first Türkic voice in the written record.

Key places:
- **Orkhon Valley** — the sacred heartland of the Kök Türük khaganate; site of the great inscribed stone stelae
- **Altai Mountains** — the ancestral homeland; the source of the Türkic origin myths and the iron-working tradition
- **Ötüken** — the sacred mountain of Türkic sovereignty; the location of the khagan's authority in steppe ideology

## What Defined Them

**Tengri — the Sky as sovereign.** The Türkic religious system centered on Tengri, the Sky deity, who granted the khagan his mandate to rule: a khagan who succeeded was favored by Tengri; one who failed had lost Heaven's favor. This was not a simple theology but a sophisticated political language: the khagan legitimated his authority by invoking Tengri's endorsement, and rivals could claim that Heaven was withdrawing that endorsement when the khagan's power faltered. The same logic — heavenly mandate granted and withdrawn — appears in Chinese political thought as the Mandate of Heaven, and the parallel is not coincidental; the steppe and Chinese political traditions were in constant dialogue.

**The Orkhon inscriptions — Türkic writing.** The stelae erected in the Orkhon Valley by the khagans of the Second Khaganate (particularly the Bilge Khagan stele and the Kül Tigin stele, early 8th century CE) are the earliest substantial texts in any Türkic language. Written in a runiform script adapted from Sogdian writing, they record the history of the khaganate in the first person — the khagan speaking directly, warning his people against Chinese cultural seduction, celebrating Türkic military victories, mourning for fallen warriors. The inscriptions are simultaneously historical records, political propaganda, and expressions of Türkic identity against the cultural gravitational pull of the Tang dynasty.

**The silk road khaganate.** The Göktürk control of the steppe meant control of the overland routes between China and the West. The Sogdian merchants who carried goods along these routes operated under Türkic protection and paid for that protection with commercial intelligence, diplomatic services, and financial acumen. The Göktürk khagans actively managed the Silk Road trade: they sent embassies to Byzantium explicitly to open direct silk trade that bypassed the Sasanian Persian Empire, which had been taxing the trade as it passed through Iranian territory. The Türkic khaganate was not just a military power; it was the political infrastructure of Eurasian commerce.

**The dual khaganate structure.** Almost from the beginning, the Göktürk empire split into Eastern and Western Khaganates, a structural tendency toward division that would reappear in every subsequent Inner Asian empire. The eastern khagan controlled the Mongolian steppe and the Chinese frontier; the western khagan controlled Transoxiana and the Caspian steppe. The division was the steppe form's weakness and its adaptation: too large to manage as a single nomadic polity, the empire divided along its natural administrative lines while maintaining a theoretical unity under the senior khagan.

## The Chapters

**1 — Bumin's Revolt:** The Türkic smiths who have been forging iron for the Rouran confederation have grown too numerous and too organized to remain subordinate. Bumin Khagan refuses the Rouran leader's insult and declares independence. The Rouran cannot hold the Altai against the iron-workers. In 552 CE, the Kök Türük khaganate begins.

**2 — The Embassy to Byzantium:** The Göktürk envoys have crossed the entire Eurasian steppe to reach Constantinople. They carry silk — and a proposal. The Türks will supply silk directly to Byzantium, bypassing the Sasanian Persian Empire that taxes the trade as it passes through. The Byzantine emperor is interested. The Silk Road's political architecture is being renegotiated at the highest level.

**3 — The Orkhon Stele:** Bilge Khagan is composing his inscription. He has the text in mind: the history of his people's subjugation under China, the heroic restoration under his father Ilterish, the victories of his brother Kül Tigin, and — most urgently — the warning to the Türkic people not to be seduced by Chinese gold and silk into forgetting who they are. The stone will stand in the Orkhon Valley for twelve hundred years.

**4 — Tang Service:** The Türkic warriors who submitted to Tang Emperor Taizong now serve in his armies. Some of them rise to high command. Tang military culture absorbs Türkic cavalry techniques; Türkic nobles absorb Chinese administrative methods. The Second Khaganate's inscriptions are partly a reaction against this absorption — the khagans calling their people back from Chinese cultural seduction to Türkic identity.

**5 — The Uyghur Victory:** The Uyghur confederation, allied with other steppe peoples, defeats the Second Khaganate in 744 CE. The Göktürk ruling house is destroyed. The Türkic political tradition — the khagan's authority, Tengri's mandate, the Orkhon Valley as sacred center — passes to the Uyghurs. The Türkic language and the forms of steppe sovereignty survive the collapse of any particular dynasty. That survival is the Göktürk legacy.
