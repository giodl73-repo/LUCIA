**What I want to see in the writing that is not yet in the notes:**

The *quo warranto* case — if the author takes Ibn Khaldun's recommendation, it should happen in a specific place. A county town. A hall. A lord standing before a royal judge with his ancestors' charters, or without them. I want the lord's face when he is told that hereditary custom is no longer enough. I want the judge's manner — was he apologetic? Bureaucratic? Did he know what he was doing was historic? He probably did not. He was applying a writ. History was doing something else entirely.

**On Evesham:** The notes say "de Montfort anchored in the physical — Evesham, August, rain." I want to confirm this will be felt, not summarized. August 1265 at Evesham was a trap — de Montfort was caught in a bend of the Avon, his escape cut off, the young Edward's forces positioned on the hills above. It was a massacre more than a battle. The chroniclers noted that de Montfort's body was mutilated on the field before it was quartered. The man who invented parliamentary form was unmade with maximum physical violence. The contrast between the bodily end and the institutional survival is the chapter's central image. It should be felt.

**On the parchment:** The parchment point is important but the notes frame it as a question ("what did the knights carry home?") when it should be a certainty. The answer is: the enrolled statutes, the responses to the common petitions, and in some cases letters from the king addressed to the county confirming what had been agreed. Find one such letter from a post-1295 Parliament. Let it be the closing image of the "machine learns" section — the parchment arriving at the county, being read aloud at the county court, producing its local consequence. Parliament reaching backward into the shires. The institution made material.
