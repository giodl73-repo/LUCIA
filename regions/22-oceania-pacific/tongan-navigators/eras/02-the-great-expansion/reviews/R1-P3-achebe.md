## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of The Great Expansion (Opening)

**Overall:** The voice is right. This culture owns its own story. I find no sentence where an outsider is looking in.

**What works:**
- The opening inhabits the navigator's body. The knowledge is not described from outside. It is felt from inside. "His body reading the water through the wood the way a hand reads skin." This is not metaphor for decoration. This is how the knowledge works. The simile is earned.
- No forbidden words. No hidden hierarchy. No comparison to any other navigation system. The knowledge system is simply real, simply functional, simply the way the world works for the people who live in it.
- The grandfather teaching scene is perfectly calibrated. The knowledge is transmitted through touch, through presence, through the old man's hand on the boy's wrist. This is how oral-kinesthetic traditions work, and the opening renders it without explaining it.
- "The ocean is not an obstacle. It is the road." This is the Achebe Test in one sentence. Hau'ofa said exactly this in "Our Sea of Islands." The opening has absorbed the culture's own framing so completely that the insight arrives as the navigator's reality, not as the author's thesis.

**What doesn't:**
- I want to hear a Tongan word for the navigation system or for the navigator's role. The text says "the songs" and "the knowledge" but does not name them in Tongan. If there is a specific term for the star-path songs, the swell-reading practice, or the navigator's title, it should appear. Names in their language is Principle 5, and this culture has names for these things.
- The final paragraph's "as it always had, as it always would" is the only moment that feels slightly authorial rather than inside. A Tongan navigator would not think in terms of "always will." He would think in terms of the next voyage and the next and the knowledge his grandson will carry. The eternal framing is the author's, not the culture's.

**The question I'd push on:**
Where are the women? The women wove the sails. The women wove the fine mats. The opening mentions this in passing, but the women of Tongatapu are not present as people. In Tongan culture, women's production of textiles was not secondary to men's navigation. It was the other half of the system. The mats the kalia carries are months of women's labor, and those mats are the highest-value exchange items in the network. The chapter must give the women presence, not just mention.
