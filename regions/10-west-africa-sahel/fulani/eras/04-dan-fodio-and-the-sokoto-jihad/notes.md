**Structural engine:** discover — the scholarly network as revolutionary infrastructure; the transformation of a knowledge community into a governing order
**Status:** Pre-draft

---

## 1. Chapter Arc

The opening establishes the mechanism: a letter, a flag, a scholarly network activated for political action. The full chapter must trace the arc from the activation of this network through the military victory to the founding of the Caliphate, making visible the central paradox: the Shehu built a community of learning, and when he transformed it into a state, he changed its nature in ways he could not fully control.

**The structural engine:** The Torodbe scholarly network is simultaneously the jihad's greatest strength and the source of its deepest tension. The network was built over decades to transmit Islamic scholarship. It connected scholars across the Hausa states through correspondence, shared teachers, and a common legal tradition. When the Shehu activated this network for political purposes, the flags went up across a territory larger than most European states, coordinated not by military command but by scholarly consensus. This is the "discover" engine: the chapter must make the reader discover, progressively, that the same network that made the jihad possible also carried within it the tensions that would shape the Caliphate. Scholars who agreed on theology disagreed on governance. Flag-holders who rose in the Shehu's name had local agendas the Shehu could not control. The community of books became a community of emirs, and the Shehu, who wanted a reformed Islamic community, got a state.

**The paradox in the Shehu's own terms:** Dan Fodio himself articulated the tension. He withdrew from governance after the jihad's military success, leaving administration to his son Muhammad Bello and his brother Abdullahi. He continued writing, producing theology and ethical treatises, while Bello built the institutions of the Caliphate. The Shehu did not want to be a sultan. He wanted to be what he had always been: a teacher. But the jihad he called into being required a state, and the state required a kind of authority the teaching tradition had not anticipated. The chapter must show this transition without verdict: the Shehu's withdrawal is neither failure nor wisdom. It is a man confronting the consequences of his own success.

## 2. Sources and Evidence

### Primary
- **Dan Fodio, *Kitab al-Farq*:** The legal distinction between Islamic and un-Islamic governance. Specific violations named: unlawful taxation, syncretism, treatment of women, enslavement of free Muslims. Use for the specific content of the critique.
- **Dan Fodio, *Ihya al-Sunna wa-Ikhmad al-Bid'a*:** The theological foundation. Use for the Shehu's intellectual method and his vision of reformed community.
- **Muhammad Bello, *Infaq al-Maysur*:** The son's account of the jihad. Use for military details, the flag-holder system, and the founding of Sokoto.
- **Abdullahi dan Fodio, *Tazyin al-Waraqat*:** The brother's account. Use for the western campaign and the founding of Gwandu. Also for internal disagreements within the movement.

### Academic
- **Last, Murray. *The Sokoto Caliphate* (1967):** The foundational modern study. Use for the emirate system, the administrative structure, the relationship between Sokoto and the emirates.
- **Hiskett, Mervyn. *The Sword of Truth* (1973):** Dan Fodio's biography. Use for the pre-jihad period, the teaching career, the specific provocations by the sarki of Gobir.
- **Lovejoy, Paul E. *Transformations in Slavery* (2012):** Use for the Caliphate's slave economy and the tension between the Shehu's anti-slavery arguments and the Caliphate's practice.

### Key Facts
- The hijra from Degel: February 21, 1804 (21 Dhu al-Qa'da 1218 AH). The Shehu and his community left Gobir after the sarki Yunfa threatened the Torodbe.
- Battle of Tabkin Kwotto (Lake Kwotto): June 1804. The first military victory, where the Shehu's followers defeated a larger Gobir force.
- Fall of Alkalawa (Gobir capital): October 1808. Sarki Yunfa killed.
- Fall of Kano: 1807. Sulaiman's flag-holders took the city.
- Founding of Sokoto: 1809. Muhammad Bello established the new capital.
- Dan Fodio's death: 1817. He had been largely retired from governance for years.

## 3. Key Figures

### Usman dan Fodio (the Shehu)
Torodbe scholar, born 1754 in Maratta (Gobir). Studied under Jibril ibn Umar, who had himself studied in North Africa. Began teaching in the 1770s. His community at Degel grew to include thousands of students. He wrote prolifically in Arabic and Fulfulde. His critique of the Hausa sarakuna was specific, juridical, and grounded in the Maliki tradition. He called the hijra and the jihad, led the community through the early military campaigns, and then withdrew from governance, leaving administration to Bello and Abdullahi. He continued writing until his death in 1817.

**Panel note (Kapuscinski):** Give the Shehu a body. Ink-stained fingers. The sound of his voice. The teaching compound at Degel.

### Muhammad Bello
The Shehu's son. The administrator, the state-builder. After the military victory, Bello founded Sokoto, organized the emirate system, managed the relationship with the flag-holder emirs, and wrote his own account of the jihad (*Infaq al-Maysur*). He was both scholar and politician in a way his father was not.

### Abdullahi dan Fodio
The Shehu's brother. Led the western campaign that established the Gwandu emirate. Also a scholar and poet. His *Tazyin al-Waraqat* provides the most candid account of tensions within the movement. He disagreed with some of Bello's administrative decisions.

### Nana Asma'u
The Shehu's daughter. Scholar, poet, educator. Wrote in Arabic, Fulfulde, and Hausa. After the jihad, she organized the *yan taru* (women's association) system that sent female teachers into rural communities. She is the most important female intellectual figure of the Sokoto period and must be substantially present in the chapter.

**Panel note (Achebe):** The Torodbe intellectual world was not male-only. Nana Asma'u must appear early and substantially.

### Umaru Dallaji (opening figure)
Flag-holder for Katsina. Torodbe scholar. Received the flag and later led the jihad in Katsina. Provides the human entry point.

### Sarki Yunfa of Gobir
The Hausa king whose provocations triggered the hijra. He had once been Dan Fodio's student. This detail matters: the man who forced the Shehu out had been taught by him. Use sparingly, from the Torodbe perspective.

### Section II — The Hijra and the First Battle
**Word target:** ~800 words
**Anchor:** The Shehu and his community leaving Degel; the battle of Tabkin Kwotto

The triggering event: Sarki Yunfa of Gobir threatened the Torodbe community at Degel. The Shehu made the hijra, the conscious echo of the Prophet's hijra from Mecca to Medina. This was not a retreat; it was a declaration. In Islamic political theology, the hijra marks the moment when a community separates from an illegitimate order and constitutes itself as a new polity. The Shehu understood this, and so did his followers.

**Scene:** The community leaving Degel. Not soldiers marching. Families. Scholars carrying books. Women carrying children. Cattle moving with them. The pastoral network in motion, but this time not for seasonal migration. This is permanent departure. The physical experience: the heat, the dust, the weight of manuscripts wrapped against the journey.

**Scene:** Tabkin Kwotto. The Shehu's followers, outnumbered, defeat a Gobir force at a lake. This is the moment where the scholarly community discovers it can fight. Muhammad Bello is present. The battle should be rendered from inside, with the specific physical experience of the fighters, not as a military report.

**Panel risk (Ibn Khaldun):** Why did the jihad succeed militarily? The answer is in the pastoral Fulani's mobility and the Hausa states' inability to coordinate a response. Make the structural explanation visible through the narrative.

### Section III — The Flags Rise
**Word target:** ~900 words
**Anchor:** The flag-holders across the Hausa states

The simultaneous (or near-simultaneous) rising across the Hausa Bakwai. This section must make the distributed nature of the jihad visible. Each flag-holder assessed local conditions and chose his moment. Kano fell in 1807 under Sulaiman. Zaria fell under Musa. Each city had its own story, its own local dynamics.

**Scene:** Kano. The dye pits, the leather market, the commercial wealth that made Kano the economic heart of the Hausa zone. The flag rises here not because of theology alone but because the taxation grievance is sharpest where the commerce is greatest. The cattle Fulani in Kano's hinterland provide the fighters; the Torodbe scholars provide the legitimacy. The alliance of pen and herd in action.

**Panel risk (Ibn Khaldun):** The alliance between scholarly authority and pastoral military force is the structural mechanism. Show how it worked in at least one city.
**Panel risk (Davis):** Show the pastoral Fulani knowledge system in motion. The seasonal migration routes that became military supply lines. The ecological intelligence that became tactical advantage.

### Section IV — The Shehu Withdraws
**Word target:** ~900 words
**Anchor:** Dan Fodio retiring from governance; Bello building Sokoto

The military victory is complete. Alkalawa has fallen. Sarki Yunfa is dead. The Hausa states are under Fulani control. And the Shehu withdraws. He does not take the title of sultan. He returns to writing, producing treatises on ethics, on the duties of scholars, on the obligations of the faithful. His son Muhammad Bello builds the city of Sokoto and the administrative system that will govern the Caliphate.

**Scene:** The Shehu at his writing desk in the new community. Bello organizing the emirate system. The contrast: the father writes about how a community should live before God; the son builds the institutions that will actually govern. Nana Asma'u is present here: she is writing, teaching, organizing the yan taru. The intellectual tradition continues, but now it operates inside a state rather than outside one.

**The structural engine made visible:** The network that carried the flags now carries administrative authority. The flag-holders become emirs. The scholars become judges. The correspondence network that circulated treatises now circulates orders. The transformation is complete, and the tension is present: the Shehu wanted a community of the faithful, and he got an empire.

**Panel risk (Tuchman):** This section must not become an administrative survey. Anchor it in the physical: the construction of Sokoto, the Shehu's writing hand, Nana Asma'u's teaching voice.

### Section V — The Book and the Flag
**Word target:** ~600 words
**Anchor:** The Shehu's death, 1817; what remains

The Shehu dies in 1817. He has been writing to the end. His last treatises address the very abuses he sees developing in the Caliphate he created: emirs who govern like the sarakuna they replaced, taxation that exceeds Islamic limits, the gap between the jihad's ideals and the state's practice.

**Landing:** The landing must find the culture's own persistence mechanism. The Shehu's writings survive. The *Kitab al-Farq* is still taught. Nana Asma'u's poetry is still recited. The emirate system, for all its imperfections, governed Northern Nigeria until the British conquest in 1903, and its institutions persisted under indirect rule and beyond. The scholarly tradition that the Shehu built did not end with the state that consumed it. The books are still on the shelf. The flag is beside them.

**Panel risk (Ibn Khaldun):** The landing must carry the paradox to its conclusion. The Shehu's own writings critique the state his jihad created. This is not failure; it is the structural consequence of transforming a knowledge community into a governing order. The landing should let this paradox resonate without resolving it.

## 6. Open Questions

- **The Shehu's specific provocations:** Hiskett describes the sarki of Gobir's threats in detail. Need to verify: was there a specific incident (the demand to disarm the community? the threat to convert them back to "Hausa custom"?) or a gradual escalation?
- **Nana Asma'u's chronology:** She was born in 1793. In 1804 she would have been eleven. She may not be directly present at the jihad's inception, but her later work is inseparable from the movement. How to handle her presence in the chapter without anachronism?
- **The slave economy tension:** Lovejoy documents that the Sokoto Caliphate became one of the largest slave-holding societies in Africa. The Shehu's *Kitab al-Farq* specifically criticized the Hausa practice of enslaving free Muslims, but the Caliphate's own slave economy was extensive. How to address this from within without imposing modern judgment? The Shehu's own legal framework distinguished between lawful and unlawful enslavement; the tension is WITHIN the Islamic legal tradition, not between the tradition and a modern standard.
- **The Shehu's voice in Fulfulde:** Dan Fodio wrote in both Arabic and Fulfulde. His Fulfulde poetry reached audiences his Arabic treatises could not. Should the chapter incorporate Fulfulde terms beyond pulaaku and munyal?
