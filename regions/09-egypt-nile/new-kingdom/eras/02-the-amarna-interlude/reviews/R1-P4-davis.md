## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of The Amarna Interlude (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening demonstrates what this project does at its best: it makes a theological system tangible through the hands of a person who lives inside it. The stonecutter does not explain his cosmology. He inhabits it. The reader enters through his muscle memory and arrives at theology.

**What works:**
- The rendering of hieroglyphic carving as a knowledge system at method level. The stonecutter knows the signs "the way a weaver knows thread, by feel, by repetition, by the particular resistance each curve offered to the copper edge." This is embodied knowledge. The reader understands what it means to carve a hieroglyph not through description but through the sensation of resistance. This meets the v1.3 evidence standard.
- The theological system is treated as operative reality, not as belief. The names ARE the gods. The flood COMES because ma'at holds. There is no distance between the cosmology and the world. This is the standard every chapter should meet but few do this cleanly.
- The chisel-as-instrument is not a metaphor imposed by the author. It is the culture's own understanding of how power moves through material objects. The Egyptians understood their tools as ritual instruments. The opening honors this.

**What doesn't:**
- The landscape is thin. Ipet-Isut is one of the most specific and overwhelming architectural spaces in the ancient world: massive columns, painted surfaces, the smell of incense, the filtered light through the clerestory. The opening gives us the hypostyle hall but not the experience of standing in it. A stonecutter who has worked here for eleven years knows this space with his body. He knows which columns hold the morning light, where the stone is cooler, where the dust collects. This sensory map is absent.
- The opening does not engage with the revolutionary quality of Akhenaten's theology from the inside. For the Aten worshippers, this was not destruction. It was purification. The rays of the Aten extending the ankh "only to the king and his queen" is mentioned, but the opening does not inhabit the Atenist worldview the way it inhabits the traditional one. If both theologies are to be treated from inside, the chapter will need to render the Aten's reality with the same gravity.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening treats the stonecutter as a man of the old theology caught in the new one's wake. But the Amarna period was also a revolution in artistic and intellectual expression. The Amarna art style broke with millennia of convention. The Great Hymn to the Aten is one of the most remarkable theological texts in the ancient world. The chapter must find a way to inhabit the Atenist vision as a lived reality, not merely as something imposed on unwilling stonecutters. Otherwise the Aten becomes the villain and Amun the victim, which is a verdict.
