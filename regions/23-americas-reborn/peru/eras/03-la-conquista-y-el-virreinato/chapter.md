# La Conquista y El Virreinato
## Peru Era 3 — 1533–1821 CE

---

## I. The Name He Chose

Tungasuca, November 1780.

The corregidor's body was still warm when José Gabriel Condorcanqui put on the name.

He had the corregidor arrested on a Saturday — Antonio de Arriaga, collector of the mita, drainer of the village stores, the man who sent Andean men down into the Potosí mines and waited, not always, for them to come back. The trial lasted as long as it needed to. On Sunday morning, in the plaza of Tungasuca, in the high valleys above Cusco where the grass goes gold in the cold, Arriaga was hanged. The gathered crowd — Quechua-speaking, wool-cloaked, watching — heard the sentence read in their language. Condorcanqui had seen to that.

Then he put on the Inca's name.

Túpac Amaru. The last Sapa Inca by that name had been beheaded in this same Cusco province, in the plaza of the city itself, in 1572, by order of Viceroy Francisco de Toledo, because Toledo had decided that a living Inca heir, however powerless, was an idea the colony could not afford. Toledo had been right. The idea had waited two hundred years. Now it had a new body to wear — a kuraka, a village chief, a man who had spent years petitioning the colonial courts about the abuses of the mita and the repartos, who knew the laws, who had read the records, who had tried every channel the viceroyalty offered and found each one blocked or bought.

The name was not nostalgia. It was a theory of legitimacy.

In Quechua, tupaq means royal, sovereign, brilliant. Amaru is the great serpent — the snake that moves between worlds, between the inside of the earth and the surface, between Ukhu Pacha below and Kay Pacha here. The name meant something cosmological. It meant the world was turning. The Andean word for this turning is pachakuti: pacha, the world-and-time, and kuti, the return, the reversal, the coming-back-around. Every Andean person who heard Condorcanqui take that name in November 1780 understood that he was not declaring a rebellion. He was declaring a pachakuti. The world had turned before. It was turning again.

Micaela Bastidas Puyucahua, his wife, had been planning longer than he had.

She was thirty-five years old. She had been corresponding with every sympathetic kuraka in the southern highlands for months, timing the uprising to the fiestas when the corregidores' guards would be scattered and drinking. When Condorcanqui hesitated — when he waited, when he deliberated, when he sent one more letter to one more official who would not reply — it was Micaela who wrote to him with the Quechua word that does not translate gently: qonqarqanki. You are forgetting. She meant: you are forgetting what we are. She meant: the moment is now. She meant all of this in a language that the Spanish administrators of the Viceroyalty of Peru had spent two hundred and fifty years trying to subordinate, tax, convert, and contain, and which had survived all of it, intact, in the mouths of the people who planted the potatoes and wove the cloth and carried the silver down from the mountain that ate men.

Potosí. The mountain.

In Quechua they called it Sumaq Urqu — beautiful mountain. In the two hundred and thirty years since the Spanish had found the silver vein inside it, somewhere between eight and nine million people had worked in its galleries. The number of dead is not agreed upon. The mercury used to process the silver — azogue, they called it, the quick silver — was poured over the crushed ore in amalgamation patios and then the men breathed it. The men who came back from Potosí breathed differently. Everyone in the highlands knew what a man who had worked the mercury processing looked like. The mita sent them there whether they wanted to go or not. The mita was the labor obligation the Spanish had borrowed from the Inca — the word was Quechua, the mechanism was Andean, the silver went to Madrid.

Ayni is the word the Andes uses for reciprocity. You help me plant, I help you harvest. You give to Pachamama — the earth, the living mother — and Pachamama gives back. The universe runs on return. This was not metaphor. This was physics, Andean physics, the deep grammar of how the world worked. The mita was not ayni. The mita was a river that flowed one direction and did not return. The Andean body understood this as a violation at the level of cosmology, not merely economics. You could say: the tax is unjust. You could also say: the world is broken. In 1780, in the valleys above Cusco, both sentences were the same sentence.

## II. What the Inca Built, and What Came After

Three hundred years before Condorcanqui put on the name, the Sapa Inca Pachacuti built something the Andes had not seen: an empire organized around altitude.

The Andean world is not one world. It is a vertical stack of worlds. At the coast: the desert, the cold Humboldt Current, the anchovy grounds. Rising east through fog: the lomas, the quebradas, the river valleys. Higher: the puna, the high plateau grassland where the camelids graze and the cold at night can split stones. Higher still: the glaciers that feed every river below. The Quechua-speaking peoples who built Tawantinsuyu — the empire of the four suyus — understood that no single altitude zone could sustain a complete life. The potato grew in the highlands. The coca grew in the cloudforest. The dried fish came from the coast. The cotton came from the warm valleys. A household's survival required access to multiple ecological zones simultaneously, and the state — the Inca — provided this access through the mit'a.

The mit'a was the obligation. Every household in Tawantinsuyu contributed labor to state projects: roads, terraces, bridges, temples, military campaigns. In exchange, the Inca state provided from the qollqa — the storehouses of freeze-dried potato and dried meat and woven cloth that lined every road on the Qhapaq Ñan, the royal highway. A mita worker did not bring food. The state fed him. He did not bring tools. The state supplied them. When he finished, he returned to his fields, his labor obligation met, the cosmic ledger balanced. This was ayni at state scale: labor given, provisioning returned.

The quipu carried the record. A quipucamayoc — a trained keeper of knotted strings — could read a quipu the way a scholar reads a page: the color of the fiber told the category (census, tribute, store inventory, calendar), the type of knot and its direction of twist told the value, the position along the subsidiary string told the hierarchical level of the account. The quipucamayocs had been trained for years. They read by touch as much as by sight, running their fingers along the strings in low light, the knots speaking into the fingertips what the storehouses had given and what the highlands had produced and how many households had sent how many workers in what year. The empire of thirty million people, spanning from present-day Ecuador to central Chile, was administered through a technology of knotted strings.

Then the world ended.

November 16, 1532. In the plaza of Cajamarca — high, cold, the Inca army encamped on the hillsides surrounding the town — Atahualpa, the Sapa Inca, came to meet the strangers. He had won a civil war against his brother Huáscar just months before. He controlled an empire from one horizon to the other. He had not yet understood what the strangers' weapons could do.

What happened in the plaza of Cajamarca entered the Andean memory as a wound that did not close. The Spanish priest Valverde came forward with a Bible — a bound object the Inca had never seen — and spoke words that Felipillo, the interpreter, rendered imperfectly, a theology arriving as noise. Atahualpa took the book. He put it to his ear, as one would put a speaking object to the ear to hear it. It said nothing. He set it aside, or threw it down — the Spanish accounts differ, and the difference matters to them, not to the Andean record. Then Pizarro gave the signal, and the cannons fired, and the cavalry charged into the plaza, and in the space of two hours, the Sapa Inca — the axis between the human world and the cosmic order, the son of the sun, the one whose health was the world's health — was a prisoner in a room.

The Andean understanding of what had happened was not political. It was cosmological. The sun had been cut off from the earth. The axis that ran from Inti above through the Sapa Inca's body down into Pachamama had been severed. The world was not in political crisis. The world had structurally failed.

Atahualpa's ransom: a room filled with gold to a line marked high on the wall, and two rooms filled with silver. His people brought it from every corner of Tawantinsuyu — the gold masks from the temples, the silver vessels from the storehouses, objects that had taken generations to make. The Spanish melted them into ingots. The gold and silver arrived. The rooms were filled. Atahualpa was strangled anyway, on July 26, 1533, in the plaza of Cajamarca, because the Spanish had decided they could not afford a living Inca.

The cord had been cut. The cosmos had no center. What came next was not merely conquest. It was an attempt to reorganize the world around a different axis entirely — the one that ran through the king in Madrid and the pope in Rome and the silver in the mountains — and the Andean world's long reply to this attempt is what the next two and a half centuries are made of.

## III. The Songs That Carried the Rupture

The Spanish tried to destroy the wak'as.

Wak'as were the sacred — the living presences that inhabited specific places and objects and beings. A spring that had fed a valley for a thousand years was a wak'a. An unusual stone on a high pass was a wak'a. The mummified body of a dead Sapa Inca, carried out for festivals, consulted on matters of state, fed and clothed and held in his palace among his descendants, was a wak'a. The entire landscape of the Andes was a network of wak'as, each one a node in a reciprocal relationship between the human world and the living world around it. To tend a wak'a was to maintain the ayni contract with a specific presence. To destroy a wak'a was to break a specific reciprocal cord.

The Spanish priests called this idolatry and broke what they could find. The Andean people hid what they could hide. They buried the smaller wak'as in fields the priests did not inspect. They built churches on top of the larger ones, so that the sacred place remained sacred even as its form changed. They participated in the mass and maintained the agricultural ceremonies underneath. This was not cynicism. It was a sophisticated epistemological response: the new religion was legible to the powerful, and the old cosmology was real, and both things could be simultaneously true.

Then, in the 1560s, the Andean world did something the Spanish could not contain.

The taki onqoy: literally, the dancing sickness. Beginning around 1564, in the regions around Huamanga (present-day Ayacucho), Andean people began having seizures. They shook. They danced. They fell. They spoke in voices that were not theirs. When they recovered enough to speak, they said: the wak'as are returning. The wak'as have not been destroyed. They have been building strength. They are coming back. And when they come back, the world will turn. The Sapa Inca will rise. Pachamama will expel the Spanish and their god from the Andean world. A pachakuti is coming.

The taki onqoy was a song. Onqoy means both sickness and the Pleiades star cluster — the stars whose rising in the Andean calendar marked the planting season, whose disappearance marked the potential failure of the harvest. The dancing-sickness was also the rising of the Pleiades: a signal that the season was changing, that something that had gone underground was coming back up. The priests who organized the movement traveled between communities teaching the signs: how to read the wak'a's presence in a shaking body, how to respond, how to prepare for the pachakuti that was coming.

A harawi accompanied the teaching. The harawi is a Quechua song form — a lament, a love song, a song of longing for what is absent. The taki onqoy harawis carried the theological argument in the form of grief. A Spanish priest listening at the edge of a village heard mourning. The Andean listener heard, embedded in the mourning's structure, a specific message: the wak'as are not gone. They are in the body of the earth, waiting. The song is the record. The song travels where the quipu cannot.

The harawi's form: a lead voice that states the loss — the sun's child is taken, the cord is cut, the field is cold — and a responding voice that carries the return — but the seed is in the ground, the water is in the glacier, the wak'a is in the stone. The Spanish ear heard the lead voice. The Andean ear heard both. The form was the protection mechanism. For two hundred years, the harawi carried what the archive refused to carry, because the archive could not hear the responding voice.

The taki onqoy was suppressed by 1572 — the same year Toledo executed Túpac Amaru I. Toledo moved against both simultaneously, understanding that the cosmological resistance and the political resistance were the same thing. He was correct. They were the same thing. The dancing stopped in the villages because the dancers had been arrested and the priests had been flogged and the community leaders had been brought before the inquisitors.

The harawi did not stop. You cannot arrest a song form.

## IV. The Petitions and Their Failure

Condorcanqui was born around 1738, in the valleys the taki onqoy had traveled through 170 years before. He was educated at the Colegio San Francisco de Borja in Cusco — the school for the sons of kurakas, the indigenous intermediary class, the colonial system's essential link between the Spanish administration and the Andean communities. He read Spanish law. He read Inca history in the chronicles, including Guaman Poma de Ayala, who had written, around 1615, a 1,200-page illustrated letter to the King of Spain explaining in precise detail what the colonial system had done to the Andean world.

The King of Spain had never read Guaman Poma's letter. The manuscript sat in the Copenhagen royal library, discovered by a Danish archivist in 1908, and Philip III, to whom it was addressed, had never seen it. Guaman Poma knew the Spanish administrative system from inside — he had worked as an interpreter and assistant to colonial inspectors — and his letter named every abuse with documentary precision: the corregidores who did not pay for the goods they took, the priests who raped indigenous women, the mita laborers who walked to Potosí and did not come back. He addressed the king as the ultimate authority who could correct what his servants had done. He asked for justice. He received no reply.

Condorcanqui had read the letter, or knew its tradition. He began his own petitions in 1776 — to the Audiencia of Lima, the colonial high court. He presented evidence of Arriaga's abuses specifically and the reparto system generally. The reparto de mercancias was the mechanism by which corregidores enriched themselves at the expense of the communities they administered: they were granted monopoly rights to sell goods to the indigenous population at prices the corregidor set, and the obligation to buy was not voluntary. Condorcanqui documented the amounts. He named the goods: mules at three times their value, silk stockings nobody needed, playing cards at a hundred times their cost. He had the receipts.

The Audiencia told him to wait. The petition process moved at the speed of correspondence across the Atlantic, of officials who had purchased their positions and had no interest in investigating themselves, of a system whose structural function was extraction and whose structural interest was continuation. Condorcanqui waited. He filed again. He petitioned to have his lineage as a descendant of the original Túpac Amaru recognized legally, which would have given him a different status within the colonial hierarchy and a different standing to pursue the reform. The petition was denied.

He tried every channel. Every channel was blocked.

A man who has tried every legal instrument a system offers and found each one serving the system rather than the law arrives at a specific threshold. It is not rage that brings him there. It is something colder: the recognition that the system does not recognize his premises as binding. He is arguing from the assumption that the law applies equally. The system is operating from the assumption that it does not. The conversation cannot happen. No further petition will change what is structural. The decision that follows this recognition is not simple, and the man who makes it carries the weight of what it will cost.

Condorcanqui made the decision in late 1780. Arriaga was arrested in November.

## V. The Uprising and Its Geography

The rebellion spread faster than the Spanish expected because the network Micaela had built was real.

For months before November, she had been writing to kurakas across the southern highlands — the communities south and east of Cusco, through the altiplano, down toward the shores of Lake Titicaca and beyond into what is now Bolivia. The letters were specific: the timing of the corregidores' feast rotations, the Spanish garrison strengths in each town, the routes the rebel columns would need to travel, the supply lines the communities would need to provide. Micaela was not organizing a spontaneous uprising. She was coordinating a military operation across terrain that took weeks to cross on foot.

The kuraka network was the uprising's infrastructure, and its unevenness was the uprising's structural weakness. A kuraka was a man in two worlds. He was the head of an Andean community — with the obligations of ayni, the obligations to Pachamama, the obligations to maintain the reciprocal relationships the community depended on. He was also a functionary of the colonial administration — collecting tribute, certifying mita workers, administering repartos, appearing before Spanish authorities to report on his community. These roles were not inherently contradictory. Many kurakas had managed to hold them in a kind of productive tension for generations.

But when Condorcanqui declared a pachakuti, the tension became a choice. Some kurakas — those whose authority within the Andean community still rested primarily on Andean legitimacy, on their fulfillment of the reciprocal obligations, on the community's recognition of their right to lead — joined the uprising. They had felt the ayni violation in the same cosmological terms as the people they led. The rebellion was, in their understanding, the correct Andean response to a broken cosmos.

Other kurakas had built their authority primarily through the colonial network. Their local standing came from their Spanish appointments, their access to colonial courts, their ability to mediate between the administration and the community. Their material interests were tied to the colonial system's continuation. One such kuraka — Mateo García Pumacahua, of Chinchero — would become the uprising's most consequential opponent. Pumacahua commanded the indigenous auxiliary troops that the Spanish relied on to suppress the rebellion, and he did so with knowledge of Andean terrain and Andean strategy that the Spanish themselves could not have applied. He was a man whose ayni ran through a different network.

The rebellion's military geography: from Tungasuca, the rebel forces moved in several directions. To the north and west toward Cusco — Micaela pressed repeatedly, in letter after letter, for a rapid march on the city. To the south, the altiplano rose and spread, and the Aymara-speaking communities of the shores of Lake Titicaca joined in their own uprising under Túpac Katari (Julián Apaza), who declared himself a viceroy in his own right and besieged La Paz for 184 days. The rebellion was not one thing; it was a network of simultaneous eruptions, each with its own leadership, its own local cosmological claim, its own relationship to the Andean physics of ayni-violation.

Cusco did not fall. Condorcanqui's forces reached its outskirts in February and March 1781. The city held because the Spanish had reinforcements, because the indigenous population of the city itself was divided — some of the urban indigenous nobility sided with Spain, seeing the uprising as a threat to their own carefully negotiated positions — and because Condorcanqui himself hesitated. Micaela's letters at this point are the most urgent in the archive. "We are going to lose everything we have gained," she wrote. "The soldiers are dying. The provisions are running out. Why won't you march?" Why he waited is not certain. Whether he believed the city could actually be taken, whether he feared the bloodshed of an urban battle, whether something in him still hoped for a negotiated settlement that would avoid the final reckoning — the records do not answer. They show only that he waited, and that the waiting was fatal.

He was captured in April 1781, betrayed by Francisco Santa Cruz, one of his own men, who led the Spanish to his position. The uprising did not end with his capture — Diego Cristóbal Túpac Amaru, his cousin, continued the rebellion in the south, and Túpac Katari continued the siege of La Paz until October — but the Spanish had what they needed to perform the deterrence they had been planning.

## VI. The Plaza of Cusco, May 18, 1781

The plaza at the center of Cusco had been the navel of the world. The Inca had called it Huacaypata — the place of weeping, the place of assembly, the place where the empire gathered. It was here that the mummified bodies of the dead Sapa Incas were brought out for the great festivals, set in their thrones in the sun, offered food and drink, consulted by their descendants. It was here that Túpac Amaru I had been beheaded in 1572 while, according to the accounts, thousands of Andean people in the plaza wept. Toledo had used the plaza for its deterrence value. So now did the incoming Viceroy Agustín de Jáuregui.

The Spanish brought to the plaza on May 18, 1781: Condorcanqui's wife Micaela Bastidas, his two sons Hipólito and Fernando, his uncle Francisco Túpac Amaru, his cousin Andrés Túpac Amaru (a teenager who had commanded the southern theater of the uprising), Tomasa Titu Condemayta the kuraka of Acos who had fought in the rebellion's earliest battles, and others. The Spanish designed the executions as a sequence, each one witnessed by the surviving prisoners, the final prisoner being Condorcanqui himself, who was to watch everything before he died.

They cut out Micaela's tongue first. Then they garroted her. Then they killed the others in order — the uncle, the cousin Andrés, the other prisoners. Condorcanqui watched. Then they brought him forward.

The Spanish tried to quarter him — to tie each of his four limbs to a horse and drive the horses in four directions, separating his body and sending each part to a different province as warning. The horses pulled. The body held. He was too strong, or the horses were too few, or the cordage failed — the accounts differ — but the quartering did not work. They cut off his tongue instead, then beheaded him, then cut the body apart by hand and sent the parts to the four corners: the head to Tinta, the province he had governed; the arms and legs to other towns that had supported the uprising; the torso to Piccho, to be burned. His wife's head was sent to Tungasuca, the town where Arriaga had been hanged, where the uprising had begun.

The Andean crowd in the plaza did not see what the Spanish intended them to see.

What the Spanish intended: the utter annihilation of the Túpac Amaru body, the demonstration that no Andean claim to legitimacy would survive, the message sent to every community that had supported the uprising — your leader is broken and scattered, the pachakuti is canceled, the world will not turn.

What the Andean crowd witnessed: the horses could not break him. The colonial system required his total destruction — required it so urgently, with such spectacular public violence, that it took everything they had and still the body held for a moment before it was cut apart. The Spanish feared him so completely that they cut out the tongues of the women and burned the bodies and sent the pieces to the four corners. The taki onqoy had said: the wak'as will return. What kind of man requires the Spanish to do this in the center of the world, in the place of weeping? The Huacaypata told the crowd everything they needed to know about what the uprising had meant.

The plaza was cold that morning. The crowd did not speak.

## VII. What the Banning Could Not Reach

After the executions, the Viceroy issued a series of prohibitions.

Henceforth: no Andean person was to wear Inca dress. No Andean person was to use the title "Inca." No images of the Inca were to be displayed — no portraits, no textiles with Inca iconography, no commemorative objects. The quipu — already restricted by anti-idolatry campaigns in the previous century — was now forbidden outright in some jurisdictions; the harawi was to be reported to the priests. The name Túpac Amaru was prohibited. No child was to be given that name. No community was to use it.

The list of prohibitions is a map of what the Spanish feared most.

What you cannot take from a culture is its embodied knowledge. The harawi lived in the throat. The quipu lived in the hands of the quipucamayoc who had trained for years and continued to read in private, running the strings in firelight, reading what the storehouses had given and the mita had taken. The agricultural calendar lived in the bodies of the farmers who knew when the Pleiades rose and what it meant and who told their children and who did not stop. The name Túpac Amaru traveled through the Quechua-speaking world like water through the irrigation channels the Inca had built — quietly, without spectacle, finding the low places and moving through them.

Diego Cristóbal Túpac Amaru continued the rebellion in the south until 1782, when he negotiated a peace with the Spanish commander. The Spanish promised amnesty. He surrendered. In 1783, the Spanish arrested him anyway, tried him, and executed him in Lima. The Andean world added this to the record of what the colonial system's word was worth.

The uprising changed what came after, even in failure. The Spanish colonial administration, frightened by how far the rebellion had spread and how long it had held, abolished the corregidor system — the office that had given Arriaga his power — within a few years, completing by 1784 a reform process the Bourbon reorganization had already begun but had not yet urgently pursued. The repartos were eventually suppressed. These were not gifts; they were the colonial system's recognition that the extraction model had generated a rebellion that could have taken Cusco. The reforms were acts of fear. But fear is also a record. Fear tells you what the uprising had done.

The name was already moving before the executions were complete. The harawi that would carry it had no author and every voice. In the valleys where Arriaga had collected the mita, where the men had breathed the mercury and come back different or not come back at all, the song knew what the song was for. It was for the returning of the world. It was for the going-around. It was for what the Quechua language has always known: that the universe runs on return, that what is given does not disappear, that the cord cut in the plaza is still attached at both ends.

The plaza of weeping held everything that had been given. The taki carried it. The quipu knotted it. The name, forbidden in every province and spoken in every valley, was already arriving at the ears it was meant for.

Pachamama holds what the archive refuses. This has always been true. The Inca knew it when they built the qollqa along the royal highway — stores against the future's cold — and the Andean world knew it when the Spanish arrived with their god and their silver and their belief that a room filled with gold could be taken and the world would not eventually ask for it back.

The world asks. It always asks. The asking is what ayni means.
