# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman
## Panel Review, Round 1 — Opening

**Chapter:** Deep Time in the Forest — Era 1
**Reviewer:** Barbara Tuchman (P-1)
**Stage:** PANEL-1

---

The opening sentence does what an opening sentence must: it places you in motion. "Three trees ahead" — and you are already following. I approved of this immediately. The author understands that narrative begins in the middle of something, not before it.

The structural gambit here is that the honeyguide relationship carries double weight: it is both the specific scene and the thematic argument. That is efficient. I have seen chapters where the opening scene and the chapter's real subject are almost unrelated — the scene is a decoration, and the actual chapter begins on page three. This one earns the connection. The honeyguide moment is not an anecdote about ecology; it is ecology as knowledge-system in practice. By the time the author names that idea explicitly, the reader already understands it from what Mambu did.

Where I want to push: the chapter ahead faces a structural challenge that the opening does not yet resolve. "Deep Time" is a span of tens of thousands of years, which resists conventional narrative arc. The opening wisely evades this by focusing on a single morning — good — but the full chapter cannot be a single morning. I want to know what the arc is. The honeyguide relationship as the structural engine works for the opening, but the chapter must find a progression: not "the forest peoples' knowledge system exists" but "something about this knowledge system changes, reveals itself, or comes under pressure." A chapter that simply describes the forest world, however beautifully, is a tableau, not a narrative.

The description of Mambu's ecological knowledge — the forty-three tree species, the caterpillar emergence, the medicinal bark — is vivid but risks becoming a list. Lists do not move. In the chapter, each element of that knowledge should arrive as a specific act or scene, not as inventory.

The close of the opening is elegant. "It has always been three trees ahead." That is the right weight for an ending — returns to the beginning, opens outward, does not summarize.

**The question I'd push on:** What is the chapter's narrative engine? "They had deep knowledge" is not an engine. A relationship that changes, a practice that reveals its logic under pressure, a moment where the knowledge proves itself or fails — that is an engine. The chapter needs to find that before the writing begins.
