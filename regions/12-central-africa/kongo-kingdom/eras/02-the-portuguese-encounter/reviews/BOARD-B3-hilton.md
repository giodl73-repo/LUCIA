# BOARD Review — B-3: Anne Hilton

**Chapter:** Kongo Kingdom / Era 2: The Portuguese Encounter — Afonso I
**Reviewer:** Anne Hilton (Specialist in Kongo political history and social structure)
**Primary work:** *The Kingdom of Kongo* (1985)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Political Structure

The chapter's account of the tributary system — provincial governors sending tribute to Mbanza Kongo in exchange for recognition, protection, and access to goods — is accurate. The chapter correctly identifies this as the political ligament whose optionality was created by the slave trade's alternative goods supply.

The mpu is correctly identified as the symbol of legitimate authority at every level of the political hierarchy. The chapter's landing image — "the mpu still on his head" — is historically apt: the mpu was not merely symbolic but was the material evidence of political legitimacy. No amount of Portuguese commercial pressure could legally remove it.

**The committee of inspection:** Correctly characterized. My research confirms that Afonso established this mechanism and that it failed for structural reasons — the governors could not be compelled to cooperate with an inspection system that regulated their primary alternative income source.

---

## The Succession War

The chapter mentions that Afonso "fought a civil war to claim the throne from a brother who had not converted." This is accurate. The brother's name was Mpanzu a Kitima. The chapter does not name him, which is a choice — the narrative moves quickly past the succession war. This is defensible at the chapter's current length and focus, but the notes.md should have the name for reference.

**Recommendation:** Flag Mpanzu a Kitima for the notes; no need to add him to the chapter.

---

## Afonso's Early Captive Trade

The chapter correctly and courageously acknowledges that Afonso engaged in captive trade early in his reign. This is historically accurate and important. The chapter's characterization — that he rejected not the institution but its extension to free subjects of the kingdom — is the correct scholarly reading of his letters.

---

## Concern: The Soyo Province

The chapter does not mention the province of Soyo, which was the first point of Portuguese contact and a significant semi-autonomous power throughout Afonso's reign. The relationship between Mbanza Kongo and Soyo was central to how the Portuguese presence was managed in the kingdom — the Portuguese traders operated primarily through Soyo, and the Soyo governors had their own interests in the Atlantic trade that were not always aligned with the Manikongo's. The chapter's account of the governor's calculation is accurate in general; a brief mention of Soyo as the specific coastal province where this calculation first played out most intensively would strengthen the historical specificity.

**Recommendation:** Add one sentence naming Soyo as the coastal province through which the Atlantic trade primarily entered the kingdom. This is a minor addition but increases the chapter's historical precision.

---

## Overall Assessment

The chapter is historically accurate in its account of Kongo political structure, the succession, the tributary system, and the slave trade's effects. The recommended additions are enhancements rather than corrections. The chapter's fundamental argument — that Afonso was a sophisticated political actor whose strategy was internally coherent and whose failure was structural rather than personal — is well supported by the scholarship.

**BOARD PASS** with the minor enhancement regarding Soyo.
