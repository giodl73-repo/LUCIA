# Diné — "The People"

They call themselves Diné — "the people," or more precisely "the five-fingered earth-surface people" — in Navajo (Diné Bizaad), the language that is today the most widely spoken Native American language in the United States. Their homeland is Dinétah — "among the Diné" — defined by the four sacred mountains that mark the boundaries of the world the Holy People (Diyin Dine'é) gave the Diné: Blanca Peak (Sis Naajinii) to the east, Mount Taylor (Tsoodzil) to the south, the San Francisco Peaks (Dook'o'oosliid) to the west, and Mount Hesperus (Dibé Nitsaa) to the north. The four mountains are not metaphors. They are the physical posts of a world, the cornerstones of the Diné universe, and to live between them is to live in the homeland that the Holy People prepared.

Hózhó — usually translated as "beauty," but encompassing harmony, balance, wellness, and right order — is the governing concept of the Diné world. The goal of human life is to walk in hózhó: to maintain the balance between the male and female principles, the dark and light, the living and the dead, the human and the Holy People, that constitutes the right order of the cosmos. When hózhó is disturbed — by illness, by conflict, by violation of taboo — it must be restored through ceremony. The Navajo healing ceremonies (chantways) — the Beautyway, the Nightway, the Enemyway, and dozens of others — are the technologies of hózhó restoration. Each chantway addresses a specific kind of imbalance and restores it through the combination of song, sand painting, prayer, and the re-establishment of the patient's proper relationship with the Holy People.

Key places:
- **Canyon de Chelly (Tséyi')** — "within the rocks" — a canyon system in Arizona; sacred to the Diné; the site of Spider Rock (Ts'é Na'asdzáán), home of Spider Woman who taught the Diné to weave; also the site of the 1864 Kit Carson campaign
- **Monument Valley (Tsé Biiʼ Ndzisgaii)** — "valley of the rocks"; the iconic red-rock spire landscape; deeply embedded in Diné sacred geography
- **Hwééldi (Fort Sumner / Bosque Redondo)** — the location of the Long Walk internment (1864-1868); the defining trauma of modern Diné history; the return from Hwééldi is central to the Diné national narrative
- **Black Mesa** — the high plateau that is both a sacred landscape feature and the site of ongoing coal mining disputes with Peabody Energy

## What Defined Them

**Hózhó and the beauty way.** The Diné concept of hózhó is the most frequently misunderstood and the most fundamental element of Diné thought. It is often translated as "beauty," but its full meaning encompasses harmony, balance, order, wellness, and the right relationship between all aspects of the universe. The Beautyway chant concludes with "Hózhó nahasdlíí'" — "beauty is restored" — and the restoration of hózhó is the purpose of every healing ceremony, every major life ceremony, every prayer. Walking in beauty is not an aesthetic aspiration; it is the governing principle of a life lived in right relationship with the Holy People, the land, the community, and the self.

**The Holy People and the chantway tradition.** The Diyin Dine'é — the Holy People — are not distant deities but active presences who gave the Diné their world, their ceremonies, and their teachings. The chantways (healing ceremonies) were given by the Holy People to specific ancestral figures, who passed them to human practitioners. Each chantway — the Nightway, the Mountainway, the Enemyway, the Beautyway, and dozens of others — addresses a specific kind of imbalance and requires a specific sequence of songs, sand paintings, and prayers to perform. A hataalii (singer/ceremonialist) spends years learning a single chantway; the most knowledgeable practitioners know several. The chantway is not a rote ritual; it is a living relationship between the practitioner, the patient, and the Holy People whose teachings the ceremony enacts.

**Spider Woman and the weaving tradition.** The Diné weaving tradition — the Navajo blankets and rugs that became famous worldwide in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries — traces its sacred origins to Spider Woman (Ts'é Na'asdzáán), who taught the first Diné woman to weave on a loom that her partner Spider Man described. The technical sophistication of Diné weaving — the complex geometric patterns, the tight weft count, the regional pattern traditions — developed from this sacred foundation into one of the most technically accomplished textile traditions in North America. A Diné weaver is not simply making a product; she is enacting the teaching that Spider Woman gave, maintaining the relationship between the human community and the Holy People through the practice of a sacred skill.

**The Long Walk as the defining wound.** In 1863-1864, Kit Carson destroyed the Diné's sheep herds and orchards and forced the people's surrender through starvation. Approximately 8,000-9,000 Diné were marched 300 miles to Bosque Redondo (Hwééldi) in New Mexico, where they were held in internment conditions that killed hundreds each year from disease, cold, and hunger. The Long Walk to Hwééldi and the Long Walk home — following the 1868 treaty that established the Navajo Reservation — are the central events of modern Diné national memory. The homeland they returned to was reduced; the four sacred mountains were still visible from it; the people were alive. The 1868 treaty and the return are celebrated as the foundation of the modern Navajo Nation.

**Sheepherding and the landscape of relationship.** The sheep that the Spanish introduced and the Diné adopted became the foundation of the Diné economy and the medium of their relationship with the landscape. The sheep required following across the seasonal range — the summer pastures in the high country, the winter pastures in the lower valleys — in a pattern that embedded Diné families in a specific landscape over generations. This was not simply pastoral economy; it was a relationship practice. The families who moved with their sheep across the same ground over generations knew every water source, every shelter, every danger, and every sacred feature of that ground. The sheep made the family's knowledge of the land intimate and specific.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Emergence and the Early Homeland:** The Holy People gave the Diné their world between the four mountains. Spider Woman taught the first woman to weave. The chantways were given to the first practitioners. The Diné are learning their world — its boundaries, its obligations, its beauty. Hózhó is the name for the balance they are trying to maintain.

**2 — The Spanish Period:** A Diné community is negotiating with Spanish traders — horses and sheep in exchange for woven goods and the captives from a recent raid. The transaction is conducted in a mixture of Spanish and Diné Bizaad. Each side is managing the other. The relationship is commercial and adversarial simultaneously, which is not unusual for the borderland world.

**3 — The Mexican and Early American Period:** The American soldiers who arrive in New Mexico in 1846 are a different kind of outside force. They are making agreements they do not intend to honor. The Diné leaders who negotiate with them are trying to read the new political landscape. The raids continue; the counter-raids continue; the situation is not stable and everyone can feel it.

**4 — The Long Walk and Return:** The orchards at Canyon de Chelly are burning. Kit Carson's soldiers are burning everything that can be burned. The Diné families are surrendering because there is nothing left to eat. The walk to Hwééldi is 300 miles. The people who survive it arrive at a place that is not between the four mountains. The four mountains are still there, in the distance. The treaty is signed in 1868. The people walk home.

**5 — The Navajo Nation:** A Diné Code Talker in the Pacific in 1944 is transmitting orders in a language that the Japanese cryptographers cannot break — not because Diné Bizaad is secret, but because there are not enough people in the world who know it well enough to decode it in real time. He is using the language his grandmother spoke at the fire. After the war, the Code Talkers will not be publicly acknowledged for thirty years, because the code is still classified.
