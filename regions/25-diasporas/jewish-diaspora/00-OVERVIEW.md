# עם ישראל — Am Yisrael: The People Israel

We are a people. Not a race, not merely a religion, not simply a culture — though we are all of these, and the argument about which is primary has been going on for two thousand years in the diaspora and continues. What we are is a people defined by a covenant — the relationship between the God of Abraham and the people who accepted the Torah at Sinai — and by the text through which that covenant is maintained, interpreted, argued with, and transmitted. The remarkable fact about the Jewish diaspora is not that Jews survived two millennia of dispersal; it is that they maintained a continuous civilizational conversation across that entire period, in dozens of languages, in dozens of countries, under dozens of different forms of persecution and accommodation, while remaining recognizably the same argumentative, text-centered, memory-organized people. The Talmud is the record of that conversation: rabbis debating across centuries, arguments from third-century Babylon cited alongside arguments from second-century Palestine, the living and the dead in perpetual dialogue about what the Torah requires.

The diaspora did not begin in 70 CE when the Romans destroyed the Second Temple. It began in 586 BCE when the Babylonians destroyed the First Temple and carried the Judean leadership to Babylon. The Psalmist's question — "How shall we sing the Lord's song in a foreign land?" — is the founding question of diaspora existence, and the answer developed in Babylon (and refined in every subsequent diaspora) was: you make the text into the land. The Torah becomes the portable homeland. The synagogue becomes the portable Temple. The Shabbat becomes the portable sacred space. The community becomes the portable Jerusalem. This is the cognitive technology of Jewish survival: the ability to reconstitute a complete civilization from its texts and practices wherever the next expulsion has sent you.

## What Defined Them

**Torah as portable homeland.** The foundational innovation of rabbinic Judaism, developed after the Temple's destruction, was the transformation of religious practice from a Temple-centered, sacrificial system to a text-centered, community-based system that could be practiced anywhere. Prayer replaced sacrifice; the rabbi replaced the priest; the synagogue replaced the Temple; the study house replaced the sacred precinct. This transformation — accomplished in the two centuries between the Temple's destruction (70 CE) and the completion of the Mishnah (c. 200 CE) — made Judaism diaspora-proof. A community with a Torah scroll, a minyan (ten adult Jews), and a rabbi could reconstitute complete Jewish religious life in any location on Earth.

**The argument as sacred activity.** Jewish intellectual culture is organized around argument. The Talmud records not just decisions but the disagreements, the minority opinions, the questions that were asked and not fully resolved. The Talmudic formula "Teku" — used when a debate cannot be resolved — does not mean the question is abandoned; it means the answer is deferred to the arrival of the prophet Elijah, who will settle all unresolved questions in the messianic age. In the meantime, the argument continues. This culture of argument — which produced the yeshiva, the responsa literature, and eventually the disproportionate Jewish presence in law, philosophy, and the sciences — is not incidental to Jewish civilization; it is its method.

**Memory as identity.** Jewish communal identity is organized around collective memory in an unusually systematic way. "In every generation, each person is obligated to see themselves as if they personally left Egypt" (Passover Haggadah). The liturgical calendar re-enacts the central events of Jewish history — the Exodus, the destruction of the Temple (Tisha B'Av), the wandering in the wilderness — not as historical commemoration but as present experience. "Remember" (zachor) is one of the most frequently occurring commandments in the Torah. The relationship between Jewish identity and Jewish memory is constitutive: to know who you are is to know what your ancestors experienced and what it requires of you.

**The Shoah and its aftermath.** The Holocaust (Shoah — "catastrophe") was the systematic murder of approximately six million Jews by the Nazi state between 1941 and 1945 — approximately one-third of the world's Jewish population. It was the worst of many catastrophes in Jewish history, but its scale, its systematic nature, and its use of industrial technology made it categorically different from the pogroms and expulsions that preceded it. The Shoah transformed Jewish civilization: it ended the Yiddish-speaking Ashkenazi world of Eastern Europe, traumatized the survivors and their descendants in ways that are still being understood, and accelerated the founding of the State of Israel. It has also created an obligation of memory — "Never Again" — that has become central to both Jewish identity and the broader ethical framework of post-war international law.

## The Chapters

**1 — Galut Bavel:** The rivers of Babylon. The Psalmist sits beside them and weeps. His people have been carried from Jerusalem; the Temple is destroyed; the holy city is rubble. The question is whether this is the end of the covenant or a new form of it. The rabbis of the next six centuries will answer: a new form. The answer is everything.

**2 — The Talmud:** In the academies of Babylon, the rabbis are arguing. Rav and Shmuel, Abbaye and Rava — the names of those who debated points of Jewish law in the fourth century are known to every yeshiva student in the twenty-first. The arguments themselves are the record of Jewish intellectual civilization's most formative period, and they are still alive in the sense that they are still studied, still argued with, still cited.

**3 — Maimonides:** Moses ben Maimon — Rambam — is born in Córdoba in 1135 and dies in Cairo in 1204. He is the physician to Saladin. He is the author of the Mishneh Torah (the most systematic codification of Jewish law) and the Guide for the Perplexed (the attempt to synthesize Aristotelian philosophy with Jewish theology). He is one of the most influential thinkers in any intellectual tradition. His synthesis will be debated for eight centuries after his death.

**4 — The Expulsion:** March 31, 1492: the Catholic monarchs Ferdinand and Isabella sign the Alhambra Decree expelling all Jews from Spain. The community that has been in Iberia since Roman times — in some cases for 1,500 years — must leave within four months or convert. Perhaps 40,000 convert; perhaps 100,000 leave. Those who leave take with them the Sephardic liturgy, the Ladino language (Judeo-Spanish), and their keys — the keys to their Spanish houses, kept by their descendants for five centuries against the day of return.

**5 — The Pale:** In the Pale of Settlement — the western borderlands of the Russian Empire — millions of Jews are living in the conditions of the shtetl: the small Jewish towns of Poland, Lithuania, Ukraine, and Belarus. The Pale is simultaneously the world's greatest concentration of Jewish learning (the great yeshivot of Vilna, Volozhin, and Mir), the most vibrant expression of Yiddish culture (theater, literature, journalism, political movements), and a world of grinding poverty, periodic pogroms, and systematic legal discrimination. Between 1880 and 1924, two million Jews will leave this world for America. Between 1941 and 1945, the Germans will murder most of those who remained.

**6 — Yad Vashem:** In Jerusalem, the national Holocaust memorial institution holds the names of three million identified victims. The other three million have not yet been named. The obligation — to name each person, to restore the individuality that the genocide attempted to erase — is still being fulfilled, one name at a time.
