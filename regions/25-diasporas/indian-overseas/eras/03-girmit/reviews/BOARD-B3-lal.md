## BOARD — B3 (Lal) — Review of Girmit Chapter

**Domain:** Community formation, cultural reconstitution, comparative indenture experience

**Overall assessment:** The chapter's strongest achievement is the mandali section. The rendering of the Ramayan mandali as a living practice of cultural reconstitution, not a museum piece, is exactly right. The mandali was the girmitiya's primary institution of cultural survival, and the chapter shows it operating as it actually operated: informally, in the evening, without official sanction, built on memory and voice.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The jahaji-bhai system is accurately described as a kinship system forged in transit, and its persistence in the colony is noted.
- The evolution of Fiji Hindi as a distinct dialect is mentioned, though briefly. This is correct: Fiji Hindi (or Fiji Baat) is a significant linguistic development, a creole-like contact language that emerged from the mixing of Bhojpuri, Awadhi, and other Hindi dialects with English and Fijian elements.
- The Tamil girmitiya are acknowledged as a distinct group with their own traditions (Mariamman, Kavadi, fire-walking). This is important: the tendency to treat all girmitiya as North Indian Hindi-speakers erases the significant South Indian component.
- The chutney music reference is accurate and culturally significant: the fusion of Bhojpuri biraha with Caribbean calypso rhythms is a genuine cultural creation of the Indo-Trinidadian community.

**What could be stronger:**
- The comparative dimension across colonies is thin. The chapter is primarily a Trinidad story with Fiji additions. The experiences in British Guiana, Natal, and Mauritius were structurally different: different plantation economies (sugar in Trinidad and Fiji, sugar and tea in Natal, sugar in Mauritius), different relationships with indigenous populations, different political outcomes. Even a sentence or two acknowledging these differences would prevent the reader from generalizing from the Trinidad case.
- The Mauritius experience deserves a mention: Mauritius is the colony where Indians became the majority, and the community there developed the most autonomous institutional life, including Hindi-medium schools and a political party system organized partly along Indian community lines.

**Corrections needed:**
- The chapter states the gender ratio regulation was "no fewer than forty women for every hundred men." This is the most commonly cited figure, but the ratio was amended several times during the indenture period (from 25:100 in the early period to 40:100 after 1868). A note that the ratio was raised over time would be more precise.

**Verdict:** The cultural reconstitution is the chapter's strength. The comparative geographic coverage is adequate but not as strong as the Trinidad material. The girmitiya's agency is visible throughout.
