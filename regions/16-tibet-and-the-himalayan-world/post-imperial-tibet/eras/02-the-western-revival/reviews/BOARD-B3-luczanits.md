# B-3 — Christian Luczanits — Board Review of "The Western Revival"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Art and architecture of western Tibet; the Tholing and Tabo temple complexes; Kashmiri artistic influence in the Himalaya; pigments, techniques, and iconographic programs of the phyi dar period.

## Historical Accuracy

- **The murals at Tholing and Tabo:** The chapter correctly identifies these as major artistic achievements of the phyi dar and accurately attributes their commission to Rinchen Zangpo's patronage program. The description of the murals as "teaching tools" rather than decoration is exactly right. The statement that "A student who walked into the temple at Tabo entered a text as surely as if he had opened a manuscript" captures the essential function of the artistic program.

- **Kashmiri artistic influence:** Correctly characterized. The artists who came to western Tibet from Kashmir brought a distinctive style, and the chapter accurately notes the "flowing robes" and "Kashmiri style" of the figures. The characterization of the artistic program as a joint project between Kashmiri artists and Tibetan apprentices is consistent with what the evidence suggests.

- **Pigments:** The chapter mentions lapis lazuli, malachite, and gold leaf. The lapis from Badakhshan is correctly noted and accurately described as having traveled significant trade routes. Malachite from "central Asia" is a reasonable generalization. However, the pigment palette at Tabo and Tholing was broader than these three: vermillion (cinnabar), orpiment (yellow), and lamp black were also important. The chapter does not need to catalogue every pigment, but "lapis and malachite and gold" as the only named pigments gives a somewhat incomplete picture of the actual palette's range.

- **"Mud plaster" walls:** The chapter describes pigments applied to "walls of mud plaster." This is generally correct for the Tabo murals, though the ground preparation was more complex than "mud plaster" suggests: the walls were coated with multiple layers of progressively finer plaster, with the final painting surface carefully prepared. "Mud plaster" captures the material but understates the technique. Minor point.

- **The murals "still glow in the half-darkness":** This is true of the Tabo murals, which are remarkably well-preserved. The Tholing murals have suffered more damage over the centuries, particularly during the Cultural Revolution. The chapter should be aware that "still glow" applies more accurately to Tabo than to Tholing, though conservation work has revealed significant surviving material at both sites.

- **Mudra descriptions:** "This gesture means fearlessness, this one means teaching, this one means meditation." These correspond to the abhaya mudra, vitarka/dharmachakra mudra, and dhyana mudra, which are indeed prominent in the Tabo murals. Correctly identified.

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter treats the artistic program as an integral part of the translation/transmission project, not as a separate aesthetic endeavor. This is historically and culturally accurate. In the Tibetan Buddhist context, the creation of religious art was itself a meritorious act and a form of teaching. The chapter's parallel between "rendering in pigment" and "rendering in syllables" correctly captures the relationship between the artistic and textual programs.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The murals as a parallel transmission system to the textual translations.
- The Kashmiri origin of the artistic style.
- The lapis lazuli trade route from Badakhshan.
- The didactic function of the mudras.
- The physical preservation conditions (cold, dry air) that protected the murals.

## What Needs Correction

1. **Pigment palette:** Consider adding at least vermillion to the named pigments alongside lapis and malachite. Vermillion (cinnabar red) is one of the most striking colors at Tabo and its omission leaves the reader with only blues, greens, and gold. Suggestion, not requirement.

2. **"Walls of mud plaster":** Could be refined to "walls of prepared plaster" or "walls of layered plaster" to better represent the careful ground preparation. Minor.

3. **Tholing vs. Tabo preservation:** The chapter groups them together with "still glow." If precision matters, the Tabo murals are the better-preserved example. The chapter could say "the murals at Tabo are still there" (which it does in Section IV) and be accurate. The opening's reference to both Tholing and Tabo as places where murals "still glow" is slightly generous for Tholing's current state, though historically the murals were present at both.

**Verdict:** APPROVED

The descriptions of art and material culture are accurate in all essential respects. The corrections noted are refinements rather than errors. The chapter's treatment of the artistic program as integral to the transmission project is its most important art-historical contribution, and it is handled correctly.
