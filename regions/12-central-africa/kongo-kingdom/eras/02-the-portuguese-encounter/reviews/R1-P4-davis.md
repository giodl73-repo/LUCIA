## The Worldview Question

Every culture is a unique expression of the human imagination. My role is to ask: does this chapter treat the Kongo knowledge system as fully equal in sophistication, internal coherence, and explanatory power to the knowledge system of the Portuguese? Not as exotic, not as worthy of admiration from above, but as one of many ways the human species has organized meaning and power?

The opening passes this test in most respects. Where it needs work is in the depth of the Kongo epistemological system — specifically, the nkisi and the cosmogram, which are gestured at but not inhabited.

## The Cosmogram: Going Deeper

The chapter's claim that the Kongo recognized the crucifix because it resembled the cosmogram is historically accurate (Wyatt MacGaffey and Cécile Fromont have established this). But the chapter explains the recognition rather than showing it operating. What is the cosmogram's logic? It is not merely a symbol; it is a map of the universe. The horizontal line is the kalunga line — the boundary between the world of the living above and the world of the dead below. The vertical line is the path of the individual soul as it moves between these worlds. The intersection point is where power is available — where the living can contact the ancestors, where healing can happen, where the covenant between the human world and the spirit world is renewed.

When a Kongo person looked at the crucifix — a human figure on a crossed structure — they were seeing a being positioned exactly at the intersection point of the kalunga line and the vertical path. They were seeing a figure that, in Kongo cosmological terms, was precisely where power resided. The Christian story of death and resurrection — a being who crossed the kalunga line and returned — mapped onto the Kongo theory of power in ways that no other religious system the Kongo had encountered had done.

This is not similarity-as-accident. It is a fundamental congruence of two different but equally coherent cosmologies. The chapter should show this at this level of depth, not gesture at it.

## The Nkisi System

The opening mentions nkisi correctly — "an entire system of law and social contract." But the epistemological richness of the nkisi needs more. The nkisi nkondi (the nail-figures) work through a specific mechanism: each nail driven into the figure records a covenant made in its presence. The figure witnesses the agreement. If one party violates the covenant, the nkisi acts. This is not magic in the European sense; it is a legal recording system whose enforcement mechanism is distributed through the community's shared belief in the figure's power. The effectiveness of the nkisi depends not on supernatural intervention but on the certainty that every member of the community knows the covenant was witnessed and will hold each other accountable.

When missionaries burned the nkisi, they were not destroying superstition. They were destroying a contract registry. The full chapter must show this from inside the system — not as an observation that "the nkisi served as a form of social contract" but as a lived reality in which covenants were binding precisely because the nkisi witnessed them.

## The Risk of Condescension by Admiration

One place the chapter is close to a problem: "He is asking, politely and precisely, for justice — not as a subject petitioning a master, but as one Christian king asking another to honor what they both profess to believe." The "not as a subject petitioning a master" phrasing is defensive — it anticipates a reader who might misread the scene and preempts them. But this anticipation reveals an anxiety. A fully inside perspective would not need to clarify that Afonso is an equal. It would simply show him as an equal, without the defensive framing. The chapter should trust its own rendering.

## The Question I'd Push On

**"What does the Kongo theory of power have to say about the Portuguese failure to honor the covenant?"**

In the Kongo cosmological system, a covenant witnessed by a nkisi has consequences when violated. The person who violates it calls the nkisi's power against themselves. If Afonso's Christian covenant with the Portuguese was understood, within Kongo theology, as a version of this — a covenant witnessed by God — then the Portuguese violation was not merely political treachery. It was, in Kongo terms, cosmological transgression. The violators were calling destruction on themselves. The chapter should develop whether Afonso or his court understood the situation in these terms, and what the Kongo interpretation of subsequent Portuguese calamities (they had many) would have been.
