# La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
## Era 2 (1789–1815 CE)

---

Moreau de Saint-Méry had a system.

In his library in Port-au-Prince, in the year the Estates-General convened in Versailles, he was completing his *Description topographique, physique, civile, politique et historique de la partie française de l'isle Saint-Domingue*: a monument of Enlightenment classification, the most thorough census of a colonial world ever assembled in the French language. He had catalogued the colony's soil types, its rainfall patterns, the varieties of cane grown on the northern plain, the tonnage of sugar loaded at Le Cap-Français each season. He had catalogued its people with the same precision.

There were one hundred and twenty-eight racial categories.

Each required its own designation. Each designation corresponded to a legal status, a set of rights, a set of restrictions, a position in the social architecture that produced the sugar that made Saint-Domingue the wealthiest colony in the history of the Atlantic world. *Sacatra. Griffe. Marabou. Quarteronne. Métis. Mamelouque. Quarteron. Sang-mêlé.* The classifications descended through the fractions with the logic of a mathematical table: an eighth part, a sixteenth, a sixty-fourth. At some denominator small enough that the eye could not see it, a man became white. The law knew where that line was. Moreau de Saint-Méry knew where it was. The line was not nothing. It was everything.

This was the administrative genius of Saint-Domingue: a system so precise in its classifications that it could manage 500,000 enslaved laborers, 30,000 free people of color, and 32,000 white colonists as distinct legal communities with distinct legal fates, held in an equilibrium that produced 40 percent of Europe's sugar and half of its coffee, year after year, without failing. From the vantage of the colonial administration, the system worked. The ledgers balanced. The ships departed full. The numbers added up.

The office where Moreau de Saint-Méry worked was itself a product of the tropics doing what the tropics do to European things: the paper imported from Bordeaux was already warping at the edges, the iron gall ink corroding the pages from within, the shelves of the archive soft with humidity, everything in the office engaged in the slow tropical project of returning the order it had been given to the disorder from which it came. He wrote in the evenings, when the heat broke slightly, by the light of tallow candles that smelled of the rendering vats in the port. Outside, the town of Port-au-Prince moved at the tempo of a colonial capital: the unloading at the quay, the shouts of overseers on the roads, the bells of the cathedral, the grinding of the sugar mills running day and night during the harvest months. He could hear all of this and write at the same time, because a man trained in Enlightenment method learns to separate the signal from the noise.

Moreau de Saint-Méry was not a cruel man. He was, by the standards of his world, an educated one: a lawyer, a man of letters, a Fellow of learned societies, a reader of Montesquieu and Raynal. He had no difficulty holding, simultaneously, the proposition that men possessed natural rights and the proposition that the system of Saint-Domingue was rational and necessary. The Revolution assembling in Paris would produce the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen within weeks of his completing his description of the 128 categories. The Rights of Man were universal. The categories were practical. Both could be true, and indeed both had to be true, because the economy of France depended on it.

The contradiction had a geography. It ran between Paris and Port-au-Prince, across 7,000 kilometers of Atlantic Ocean, and across the legal fiction that what happened in Saint-Domingue was a separate species of event from what was being declared in the Salle du Jeu de Paume.

## I. The Amis des Noirs

In Paris, in February 1788, the month before the Estates-General was called, a society was founded: the Société des Amis des Noirs. Its members included the Marquis de Condorcet, who had written *Réflexions sur l'esclavage des nègres* in 1781 under a pseudonym; Jacques-Pierre Brissot, journalist and future Girondin leader; the Abbé Grégoire, who would spend the next decade advocating for the legal equality of Jews and free people of color with the same theological conviction that he applied to everything; and Mirabeau, who lent his eloquence to the cause before his death in April 1791 removed him from the debates that would follow. The society met in private. It advocated publicly. It drafted petitions to the Estates-General, the Constituent Assembly, the Legislative Assembly, in succession.

The argument the Société des Amis des Noirs made was not sentimental. It was Enlightenment argument, built from the same premises as the Declaration itself: if men possessed natural rights by virtue of being human, then the distinction between free men and enslaved men was an artificial one, a creation of law rather than of nature, and what law had created law could abolish. Condorcet had laid out the arithmetic: the colonial economy produced profit for approximately 10,000 French citizens and the productive labor of 500,000 Africans and their descendants, at costs the ledger did not record. The calculation was not difficult. The ratio was not ambiguous.

The colonial lobby, les colons as they presented themselves to the Assembly, were equally logical. Saint-Domingue produced 40 percent of French trade revenue. It was the most productive colony in the Atlantic world. It produced more sugar than all the British Caribbean combined. To abolish slavery was to abolish the colony. To abolish the colony was to abolish a significant fraction of the French national economy. The Assembly was not prepared to abolish the French national economy on behalf of people who were not, at present, French nationals. The calculation was not difficult. The ratio was not ambiguous.

The Société des Amis des Noirs lost the argument. In 1790, the Constituent Assembly explicitly excluded Saint-Domingue from the provisions of the Declaration of the Rights of Man. The following year, a limited decree extended political rights to free people of color born of free parents: a fraction of the 30,000 gens de couleur libres, and not a word about the 500,000 enslaved. The grand blancs in the colonial assembly rejected even this. Vincent Ogé, a free man of color who had been in Paris for the debates and returned to Saint-Domingue expecting the decree to be implemented, led an insurrection of the gens de couleur libres in October 1790. He was captured, broken on the wheel in the public square of Le Cap-Français in February 1791, and his execution was witnessed by approximately 20,000 people.

The system had processed Ogé through its machinery and produced an outcome. This was what it was designed to do.

What Raynal had seen clearly enough: in his *Histoire des deux Indes*, published in 1770 and expanded through 1780, the abbé had written a passage that he could not quite believe he had written — that the enslaved of the colonies were waiting for a great man to raise the standard of liberty. He wrote it. It was read. It circulated in Saint-Domingue in the same edition as the rest of Raynal: catalogued in Moreau de Saint-Méry's description of the colony's intellectual life, present in the libraries of the plantation managers and the literate overseers, available to anyone who could read. Toussaint Louverture, who was managing a plantation's enslaved population in the northern plain, could read. He had read Raynal. He had read Épictète. He had read Montesquieu on the laws. The literate enslaved and the free men of color read the Enlightenment alongside their enslavers, with more urgency, because the stakes for them were not theoretical.

## II. The August of 1791

On the night of August 22, 1791, in the northern plain of Saint-Domingue, the plantations went up.

The fires began around eleven in the evening: not all at once, not chaotically, but in a coordinated sequence that moved across the plain from east to west, plantation to plantation, great house to great house, mill to mill. The northern plain was the most productive agricultural region in the Atlantic world: 800 sugar plantations packed into the lowlands below the Massif du Nord, their layout visible from the roads as a grid of fields, mills, and workers' quarters that extended to the horizon. The fields burned first. Sugar cane burns wet before it burns dry; it smolders, its juice caramelizing, and the smell is sweet and thick and catches in the throat differently from wood smoke. Then the mills, and the smell changed: raw wood and grease and burning machinery, the mechanisms of refinement going up. Then the great houses.

Governor Blanchelande's dispatch, sent to Paris three days after the insurrection began, recorded that the sky over the northern plain was orange for three nights. From the harbor at Le Cap-Français, captains of merchant vessels already loaded for France watched the horizon. The dispatch reached Paris six weeks later, by which time the burning had consumed 180 sugar plantations, 900 coffee plantations, and a calculation that would take Moreau de Saint-Méry years to fully assess.

What the French colonial administration could not account for, in its dispatches and its analyses and its subsequent meetings of the colonial assembly, was the coordination. The insurrection had moved across the plain with a precision that the administration's categories had not anticipated. The enslaved of Saint-Domingue were classified as property; property did not coordinate. Property did not communicate across plantation boundaries. Property did not know the roads. But the enslaved knew the roads: they had built them, maintained them, moved along them every day under the supervision of overseers who did not accompany them into the cane fields. The insurrection used the landscape with an intimacy that the plantation system had itself created. The French administration had no map for this. The 128 categories had no entry for it.

The ceremony at Bois Caïman, held on the night of August 14, eight days before the fires, was not recorded by the French colonial administration in real time. What they recorded was the outcome. The ceremony itself was known only through fragmentary and hostile witnesses: a gathering in the forest of the northern plain, rain and lightning, a pig sacrificed, an oath taken. The details are contested and elaborated. What is not contested: across a landscape of 800 plantations, where communication between enslaved people was surveilled and restricted, something moved that the French system had no vocabulary for — a shared commitment, a coordinated decision, a political act performed through means the colonial archive did not know how to record.

## III. The Convention's Answer

The insurrection of August 1791 created an emergency that the Revolution in Paris had not anticipated and was not equipped to manage. Saint-Domingue was now a military problem: 500,000 enslaved people in arms, the colonial militia disorganized, the grands blancs in open conflict with the petit blancs, the gens de couleur libres fighting for their own legal status on a third front, and the most pressing condition of all: British and Spanish forces moving to occupy the territory and absorb its labor into their own colonial systems. Britain invaded from Jamaica. Spain invaded from Santo Domingo on the eastern part of the island.

Léger-Félicité Sonthonax arrived in Saint-Domingue in September 1792 as a civil commissioner with three principal assets: a mandate from the Legislative Assembly to restore order, a conviction that the rights of the gens de couleur libres had to be respected, and an army of 6,000 soldiers. By August 1793, facing a military situation that was deteriorating in every direction, he made a unilateral decision: he declared the general emancipation of the enslaved in the northern province of Saint-Domingue. It was a military decision as much as a humanitarian one. He needed soldiers. The people he was emancipating were soldiers. The calculation was not difficult.

In Paris, on the 4th of February, 1794, in the year II of the Republic, the National Convention voted the universal abolition of slavery in all French territories. The vote was unanimous. The proclamation was read to the Convention by three newly elected deputies from Saint-Domingue: a white colonist, a mixed-race man, and a Black man born in Africa — a composition the Convention had arranged so that the vote would be visible as well as formal. The three men stood at the podium. The Convention applauded. Jean-Baptiste Belley, the third deputy, had been enslaved as a child, served as a soldier in the American Revolutionary War on the French side, purchased his freedom, returned to Saint-Domingue, been elected to the Convention in the chaos of 1793. He was approximately 48 years old. He had voted in the Salle de la Convention.

Belley had been painted, or would shortly be painted, by Anne-Louis Girodet de Roucy-Trioson: a formal portrait, the kind reserved for men of significance. In the painting, Belley leans against a marble pedestal on which sits a bust of Raynal. He is looking directly at the viewer. He is wearing the tricolor cockade and the uniform of a French deputy. The painting exists. It is in Versailles, in the collection of the national museums. Belley is looking at us, leaning on Raynal's pedestal, wearing the uniform of the republic that had, finally, applied its own principles.

The practical consequence of the February 4 decree was significant: Toussaint Louverture, who had been fighting for Spain since 1793, switched his allegiance to France. He had been fighting for Spain because Spain had offered freedom to the enslaved who joined their forces; France had not yet done so. The decree changed the calculation. He brought his soldiers, already experienced, already organized, already in possession of the northern plain's geography in ways the Spanish army was not, into the French service. By 1798, the British occupying forces had been expelled, the Spanish forces had been pushed back, and Saint-Domingue was, effectively, under Toussaint's control.

The Enlightenment, applied consistently, had produced a general.

## IV. The Constitution and Its Refusal

On July 8, 1801, Toussaint Louverture promulgated the constitution of Saint-Domingue.

It was an Enlightenment document: well drafted, internally consistent, drawing on the French legal tradition and the principles of 1789. It abolished slavery. It established Catholicism as the state religion. It granted citizenship to all inhabitants regardless of race or color. It created a legislature, an executive, a judiciary. It acknowledged French sovereignty nominally; Article 91 specified that the metropolitan government retained the authority to modify the constitution, while making Toussaint governor-for-life with the power to appoint his successor. The document was sent to Paris in early September.

Napoleon read it. His reading has not survived in the form of annotations, but his response was administrative: he organized an expedition. In December 1801, a fleet of 21 warships and 20,000 soldiers sailed from Brest under the command of General Charles Leclerc, Napoleon's brother-in-law. The fleet carried, in a sealed envelope, a set of instructions from Napoleon that Leclerc was not to open until he had been in Saint-Domingue for three weeks. The sealed instructions ordered the restoration of slavery.

What Napoleon thought about Toussaint's constitution has to be inferred from what he did about it. His *Mémoires*, dictated at Saint-Hélène, describe Toussaint as a man he could have worked with, given different circumstances. The different circumstances Napoleon did not specify. What is visible in the record: Napoleon had concluded, by December 1801, that Saint-Domingue without slavery was not economically viable within the French imperial framework; that the February 1794 abolition decree had been an emergency measure of the Committee of Public Safety, produced by emergency conditions, and not a permanent constitutional principle; and that the correct order of things, the rational order of things, required the restoration of the system Moreau de Saint-Méry had catalogued. The law of 20 floréal, Year X, dated May 20, 1802, restored slavery in Martinique, Guadeloupe, and French Guiana. The restoration of slavery in Saint-Domingue was the expedition's unstated purpose.

Leclerc arrived in January 1802. He was a capable officer, 29 years old, who had distinguished himself in the Italian campaign. He was given a military problem with a political dimension he did not fully understand. His dispatches to Napoleon over the following months trace a curve from confidence to bewilderment to desperation. In February: "I believe the operation will be completed within six weeks." In March: "I lack the forces to pursue the insurgents into the mountains; they do not hold positions, they appear and disappear." In May: "This is not a war as I have known it. The terrain is against us, the climate is against us, the population is against us, and the enemy knows all three better than we do."

What Leclerc's army encountered was a knowledge of the northern plain built over generations: the trails that did not appear on French maps, the water sources that did not appear on French surveys, the sight lines that allowed movement between plantation positions without being seen from the roads, the coordination of armed groups across distances that the French assumed required the kind of written orders the French army used, because the French army knew no other kind. Yellow fever killed 2,000 French soldiers in March, 3,000 in April. Leclerc himself died of yellow fever on November 2, 1802. By then, 50,000 French soldiers had died in Saint-Domingue: more than Napoleon would lose at Waterloo.

Jean-Baptiste Belley, back in Saint-Domingue by this point, opposed the restoration of slavery. He was arrested on Leclerc's orders in August 1802, transported to France, and imprisoned on Belle-Île, off the Brittany coast. He died there in 1805, in captivity, still wearing, one imagines, the tricolor cockade he had worn in the portrait at Versailles.

## V. The Fort de Joux

Toussaint Louverture was captured in June 1802, not in battle but by deception: invited to a negotiation with a French general, arrested at the meeting, put on a ship the same day. He had no trial. He was transported to France and deposited in the Fort de Joux, a mountain fortress in the Jura near Pontarlier, at an elevation of approximately 850 meters. He arrived in August 1802. He died in April 1803.

The Fort de Joux is as far from Saint-Domingue as it is possible to be within France. Its winters are the winters of the Franche-Comté: cold, damp, the limestone walls conducting cold inward rather than holding warmth. Toussaint was approximately 59 years old when he arrived. He had spent his life in a tropical climate at sea level. The cold was not a weapon the French administration deployed against him deliberately; it was a condition the French administration did not consider, because they had not needed to consider it. He asked for his papers: the documents that would allow him to defend himself in writing, to appeal his detention, to put the administrative case for his release through the channels the French system provided. The papers were not returned to him.

His letters from the fort survive. They are formal documents, addressed correctly, citing the relevant legal provisions, arguing that his arrest violated the terms of agreements the French government had made with him. They are the voice of a man who had read Montesquieu and believed the rule of law applied to him as it applied to others: who had organized a state, drafted a constitution, argued in the idiom of the French Enlightenment that his people had the same rights as any other people, and who was now asking the French administration to be consistent with its own principles. The letters received no answer.

He died on April 7, 1803. The cause was recorded as pneumonia, though his letters describe a combination of cold, hunger (his rations had been reduced), and a respiratory illness that had begun weeks earlier. Napoleon received the news at Saint-Cloud and is recorded to have said: "That man is dead, and France is well rid of him." The war in Saint-Domingue was still ongoing.

## VI. Louisiana and the Ledger

In April 1803, Napoleon sold Louisiana to the United States for 15 million dollars.

The sale was an administrative decision made on administrative grounds. Without Saint-Domingue as the agricultural base, without the sugar and coffee revenues that were to finance the Western Hemisphere empire Napoleon had projected, Louisiana was strategically indefensible. It was also militarily indefensible: France was about to resume war with Britain, the British navy controlled the Atlantic, and an overseas territory 7,000 kilometers from Paris, accessible only through waters the British could close, was a liability rather than an asset. The American negotiators had come to Paris to buy the port of New Orleans. They left with 2.1 million square kilometers of territory, because Napoleon needed the cash and had no use for the land.

Thomas Jefferson's response to the offer, communicated by his ambassador James Monroe, was astonishment. "We have lived long," Monroe wrote, "but this is the noblest work of our lives." The American republic doubled its territory in a single transaction. What neither Monroe nor Jefferson said, in any document that survives, was what the transaction represented from the French side: the formal acknowledgment that the Atlantic empire was finished, that Saint-Domingue's revolution had not merely defeated an army but had destroyed a strategic architecture.

On January 1, 1804, Jean-Jacques Dessalines declared the independence of Haiti: the name taken from the Taíno word for the island that the French called Saint-Domingue. The declaration cited, directly, the French Declaration of the Rights of Man. The 128 racial categories were not abolished by the Haitian constitution; they were declared legally irrelevant, which is different and, in its way, more precise. All Haitians were Black under the constitution of 1805: not as a racial category but as a political statement: the republic was constituted by those the French system had classified as Black, and the classification itself was the declaration's target.

France did not recognize Haiti for twenty-one years.

In 1825, Charles X sent a fleet of fourteen warships to Port-au-Prince harbor. The fleet was not there to attack. It was there to negotiate. France would recognize Haiti's independence. In exchange, Haiti would pay 150 million francs in indemnity to the former slaveholders of Saint-Domingue: compensation for the loss of their "property," that is, for the loss of 500,000 human beings who had freed themselves. The sum was eventually reduced to 90 million francs, which Haiti would finance through loans from French banks at interest rates that ensured the debt would compound for generations. Haiti made its final payment on the indemnity in 1947.

The document that recorded this agreement, the *ordonnance royale* of April 17, 1825, was delivered to the Haitian government in Port-au-Prince harbor while the French warships sat at anchor outside. It was a work of administrative precision: specific amounts, specific payment schedules, specific legal language designating the transaction as settlement of claims arising from "the former colonists of Saint-Domingue." It made no reference to the Declaration of the Rights of Man. It made no reference to 1794. It made no reference to what the ledger had absorbed over the previous decades. It was, in its own terms, a clean document. The system was very good at clean documents.

## VII. The Man in Philadelphia

Moreau de Saint-Méry fled Saint-Domingue in 1793, when the insurrection made his position untenable. He fled Paris in 1794, when Thermidor made his position dangerous. He arrived in Philadelphia in the spring of 1794, where he opened a bookshop on South Front Street that became, briefly, a center of émigré French intellectual life in America. He sold books. He wrote his memoirs. He corresponded with the French scientists and administrators who had scattered across the Atlantic world in the years of revolutionary upheaval.

His library in Port-au-Prince had been burned. The census he had compiled: the soil types, the rainfall patterns, the varieties of cane, the tonnage, the 128 categories, existed now only in the manuscript he had sent to his publisher before the fires. It was published in 1797, two volumes, from his own manuscript, the most thorough description of a world that no longer existed. He had described Saint-Domingue at its peak: the most productive colony in the Atlantic world, the system that worked, the ledgers that balanced. The description appeared in print in the same year that Toussaint was negotiating the withdrawal of the last British forces from the territory he had, in fact, already won.

Moreau de Saint-Méry returned to France under Napoleon, was appointed to administrative positions, served competently, and died in Paris in 1819. He lived long enough to see Haiti declared, long enough to see Napoleon's empire collapse, long enough to see the restoration of the Bourbon monarchy whose world his work had tried to rationally describe. He did not, as far as the record shows, write about what the Declaration of the Rights of Man had produced in Saint-Domingue. He wrote about administration. He had always written about administration. The categories were what he knew.

The Declaration had not failed. It had, in fact, worked exactly as its words required.

Someone had read it.

And in the Fort de Joux, in the last winter of his life, Toussaint Louverture had said to his jailer what survivors report he said: "In overthrowing me, you have done no more than cut down the trunk of the black liberty in Saint-Domingue. It will spring back from the roots, for they are numerous and deep."

The roots were the Declaration. The French Empire had planted them in every library it had ever built, in every text it had ever circulated, in every archive where the universalist principle was stored alongside the categories that excluded it. The roots were numerous. They were deep. In Port-au-Prince harbor, in April 1825, under the guns of fourteen French warships, the indemnity document was signed. The ledger found its way to balance, as ledgers do. The system, even in defeat, knew how to extract.

What it could not do — what no system of 128 categories can do — is unread a text.
