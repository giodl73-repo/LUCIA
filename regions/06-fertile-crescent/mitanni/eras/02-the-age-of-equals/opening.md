# The Age of Equals

The messenger came from Egypt with gold.

Not loose gold, not raw gold, not gold weighed in a scale at the gate. The gold came shaped: earrings, a set of armlets, a necklace whose links the Egyptian goldsmiths had worked in a pattern of papyrus flowers. Beside the gold sat vessels of precious oil, bolts of linen so fine that a scribe in Washukanni would later note its thread count against the Mitanni standard and find it acceptable. The gifts filled several containers. They had been inventoried before departure, sealed with the pharaoh's cartouche, carried across the Sinai by donkey and then by ox cart through the Levantine corridor, past Megiddo and Qadesh and the cedar forests of Amurru, northward for weeks along roads that were kept open because the great kings needed them open.

The messenger carried a tablet. The tablet was clay, like every tablet in the world of letters between kings, but it was written in Akkadian, the language of diplomacy, in the careful cuneiform that pharaoh's scribes had learned for exactly this purpose: to speak to the other great kings as equals. The tablet began, as such tablets always began, with greetings. The pharaoh greeted the king of Mitanni. He greeted the king's household. He greeted his horses. He hoped that all was well with the king, with his household, with his horses, with his chariots, with his land. And then, past the greetings, past the formulae, came the purpose.

The pharaoh wanted a woman.

He wanted a daughter of the king of Mitanni, of the house that had ruled from Washukanni for three generations, whose charioteers the Egyptians had once fought on the banks of the Orontes and then, wisely, had chosen instead to marry. The request was not new. Shuttarna, the king's father, had sent his own daughter Gilukhipa to Egypt a generation before, with three hundred and seventeen attendants whose names were recorded on a scarab the pharaoh issued to mark the occasion. Three hundred and seventeen women, crossing the distance between the Khabur and the Nile, carrying with them the dresses and the oils and the implements of a Mitanni court household, because a princess did not travel light and a gift that arrived diminished was an insult, not an honor.

Tushratta read the tablet in the painted hall at Washukanni, where the Khabur wind came through the courtyard gate carrying the smell of the river and the horse paddocks beyond it. The walls were deep blue and red, Hurrian geometric bands running at shoulder height, and the light through the high windows fell in slabs across the clay floor. He could hear his grooms working the court horses in the yard, the measured rhythm of hooves on packed earth, the low commands in Hurrian. These were the pairs that drew the royal chariot in procession, bred from lines the horse-trainers traced through five generations of recorded pedigree. In the stables, the grooms followed the training methods the *maryannu* had used since before the kingdom took shape: interval work, controlled feeding, the precise daily regimen that the master trainer Kikkuli had set down in a manual whose technical vocabulary was not Hurrian, not Akkadian, but an older language from the east, from the Indo-Aryan tradition that the chariot aristocracy's ancestors had carried across the mountains before memory.

Tushratta set the tablet down. He would send his daughter, Tadukhipa. But first, the terms.

A daughter sent to Egypt was a bond. The bond committed the pharaoh to Mitanni's interests in the ongoing balance among the great powers: Egypt, Hatti, Babylon, Assyria, and Mitanni itself. The pharaoh would send gold. The gold was not payment. It was friendship rendered material, brotherhood made visible, and its quantity mattered precisely because it was not a price. Too little gold and the brotherhood was thin. Too much and the request had been a purchase. The exchange had to be calibrated, and Tushratta would calibrate it, and the letters that passed between them would record every discrepancy, every shortfall, every gift that arrived lighter than promised, because the great kings kept accounts of brotherhood the way temple scribes kept accounts of grain.

This was the system. Five great kings, five great courts, bound by marriage and correspondence and the ceaseless circulation of gifts whose value was diplomatic rather than material. The king of Babylon wrote to the pharaoh complaining that the gold statues he received were not solid gold but wood covered in gold leaf, and that he had tested them, and that between brothers such deception was unacceptable. The king of Hatti wrote to Tushratta warning that his western territories were not his to promise. Tushratta wrote to the pharaoh reminding him that the previous shipment of gold had been twenty minas short.

Every letter began: "To my brother."

The brotherhood was careful. It was elaborate. It was, in its own terms, sincere. And it rested on a foundation that every king in the system understood and none discussed openly: the chariot. The *maryannu* of Mitanni, the chariot warriors of Hatti, the chariot corps of Egypt, the chariot teams of Babylon had transformed warfare in the preceding century, and the kings who held the largest chariot forces held the largest territories, and the territories required the pastures and the grain and the copper and the tin that built more chariots, and the chariots required the trained horses and the trained warriors and the trained wheelwrights who maintained them. The system of brotherhood rested on an equilibrium of force, and the equilibrium held because no single king could defeat all the others, and so they wrote letters, and they sent daughters, and they counted gold, and they called each other brother.

Tadukhipa would go to Egypt. Tushratta would dictate the terms of her departure, and the scribes would press them into clay, and the clay would dry on the roof of the palace in the Khabur wind, and the letter would travel south by the same roads the gold had traveled north. The pharaoh would read it and reply, and Tushratta would read the reply and note what was promised, and the bond between the two courts would hold for another generation, and the letters would accumulate in the archive rooms of both kingdoms, a record of brotherhood maintained by arithmetic.

In the courtyard, the grooms brought the horses to a walk, cooling them after the morning's work. The animals steamed in the early light. Everything in the system ran on the same logic: measured, balanced, reciprocal. A gift for a gift. A daughter for a promise. A brother for a brother. The equality was the fiction that made the system work, and every king maintained it, because the alternative was war, and none of them wanted war, and all of them prepared for it, and in the western mountains, a Hittite king named Suppiluliuma was preparing more than most.
