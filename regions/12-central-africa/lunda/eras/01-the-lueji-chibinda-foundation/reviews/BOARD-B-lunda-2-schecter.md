## B-lunda-2 — Robert Schecter — Board Review of The Lueji-Chibinda Foundation

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** History and mechanics of Lunda political expansion; the titled-subordinate system and how daughter states maintained connection to the Lunda center.

---

**Historical accuracy:**

- The chapter's characterization of the franchise mechanism is accurate in its core claim: that titled subordinates carried Lunda political culture to new territories and established daughter states that acknowledged the Mwaant Yav's ceremonial supremacy while governing themselves independently. This is the documented pattern.

- The chapter is correct that what the titleholder carried was "the titles, the ceremonies, the political vocabulary" — not an army, not an administrative appointment. The binding was cultural, not coercive. This is the chapter's strongest historical contribution.

- The chapter correctly notes that the daughter states were "autonomous political entities" — they did not submit to the Mwaant Yav's day-to-day authority. This distinction between ceremonial acknowledgment and administrative subordination is crucial and often misrepresented in secondary accounts.

- The chapter's claim that the franchise was "not designed" but "discovered" is historically interesting and is broadly consistent with the evidence: the early Lunda expansion appears to have developed organically from the pattern of departures and installations rather than as a planned policy. The chapter handles this correctly by showing the franchise logic emerging from Katema's departure rather than from a deliberate decision at the center.

**Cultural authenticity:**

- The musumba as a compound that can be moved is correctly characterized. The Lunda capital's mobility is a structural feature, not a sign of political weakness. The chapter's rendering of the musumba as designed to be moved is consistent with the evidence.

- The handa (cross-shaped copper ingot) is correctly introduced as both currency and prestige object. The chapter is right that the exchange of handa was a socially significant act, not merely a commercial transaction.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The franchise mechanism is correctly characterized throughout. The Progression dimension's reliance on this mechanism as the structural engine is historically sound.
- The musumba's description in Section V is accurate and sensory in the right way.
- The copper network is introduced at the right level of specificity — present and grounded, not elaborated beyond what this founding era can support.

**What needs correction:**

- No factual corrections required beyond what Vansina has already flagged (the Imbangala hedge and the "grandson/son" correction).
- One observation: the chapter states that the musumba's barrier "was not simply physical — it was the visible form of the spiritual boundary between the holder of the lukanu and those who did not hold it." This is consistent with the evidence for later Lunda practice, but may be slightly more elaborated than the founding period's evidence can strictly support. It is not inaccurate, but a qualifying phrase ("in the tradition that developed from this period") would be appropriate.

**Verdict: APPROVED**

The franchise mechanism is handled with historical intelligence. Minor hedge recommended for the musumba's spiritual-boundary characterization.
