# BOARD REVIEW — B-1: Indigenous Studies / Residential Schools
## Canada Era 4: The National Policy and Its Discontents

**Domain expertise:** Canadian Indigenous history, residential school system, Indian Act law, treaty history, TRC process.

---

### Factual Accuracy Assessment

**Residential school numbers:** "139 such schools," "150,000 children," "1880s to 1996." These figures are correct per the TRC's definitive count. The last school, Gordon Residential School in Saskatchewan, closed in 1996. The figure 150,000 is the TRC's official estimate. Pass.

**Indian Act provisions:** The chapter states that the Act "prohibited potlatch and sun dance" under an 1885 amendment. Correct: the anti-potlatch provisions were added in 1885 (Section 114 of the Criminal Code, later moved to the Indian Act). The prohibition was lifted in 1951. The pass system and enfranchisement provisions are accurately characterized. Pass.

**Duncan Campbell Scott's position:** "deputy superintendent of Indian affairs 1913–1932." Correct. His 1920 statement is accurately quoted as a paraphrase: the actual wording in the Parliamentary record is slightly different but the substance is correct. The chapter's version is accurate in substance. Pass.

**Treaty 7:** "September 1877" and "Blackfoot Crossing on the Bow River." Correct. Crowfoot's speech is presented as the TRC-standard paraphrase that appears in multiple scholarly sources. The chapter attributes the quotation to the "government's own interpreters," which is accurate — the translations from Blackfoot were made by government-employed interpreters and were not reviewed by Blackfoot speakers. This is an appropriate note of source limitation. Pass.

**Riel:** "November 16, 1885, in Regina." Correct. The claim that Riel's last words were "reportedly" a prayer is appropriately hedged — this detail appears in contemporary accounts but is not definitively established. Pass.

**2021 Kamloops discovery:** "215 anomalies consistent with unmarked graves." Correct as of the initial Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc announcement. Note: subsequent investigations at Kamloops and other sites have continued; the 215 number is the initial finding from May 2021. The chapter frames this accurately without overclaiming certainty about what the anomalies contain (they are "consistent with" unmarked graves, not confirmed burials). Pass.

**Chinese railway workers:** "approximately 17,000" and "between six hundred and a thousand died." These figures are within the scholarly range. J. Brian Hay's research puts construction-related deaths at approximately 600; some estimates run higher. The chapter's range is appropriately hedged. Pass.

**Soldier Settlement Act and Indigenous veterans:** The claim that Indigenous veterans were excluded from land grants due to Indian Act land-holding provisions is correct. This is documented in Jonathan Vance's work and in the Department of Indian Affairs records. Pass.

### Cultural Representation Assessment

**Secwépemc / Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc:** The chapter situates the opening at Kamloops and names the Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc correctly. The Secwepemctsin language is referenced accurately as the Secwépemc language. The chapter avoids the common error of treating "Secwépemc" and "Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc" as interchangeable (the Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc is one of 17 Secwépemc communities). Pass.

**Niitsitapi / Blackfoot Confederacy:** "Niitsitapi, 'the real people'" is the correct self-designation of the Blackfoot Confederacy. The individual nations named (Siksika, Kainai) are correctly identified. Pass.

**The Oblate theology passage:** The characterization of Oblate missionary theology as requiring "emptying" before "filling" is a reasonable characterization of the specific Catholic missionary theory of conversion as practiced in the residential school context. This is documented in Oblate mission correspondence of the period. The framing does not overstate the Oblates' uniformity; individual Oblates varied significantly in their practices. The chapter's characterization applies to the institutional logic, not to individual missionaries, which is the appropriate level of generalization. Pass with note.

### Suggested Additions

The chapter's treatment of the Secwépemc land knowledge is briefly handled in Section III (a paragraph on the Thompson River watershed). The reviewer would recommend developing the land-knowledge content slightly further in a future revision, particularly the salmon management practices, which are well-documented in Secwépemc oral tradition and in the work of Robin Wall Kimmerer-adjacent ethnobotanical research in the region.

The pass system (Indian Act permit system requiring First Nations people to obtain written permission from the Indian agent before leaving the reserve) is mentioned briefly. This system, which operated from 1885 to 1941, is one of the most significant administrative constraints on First Nations movement and economic participation in this era. The chapter could usefully name it more specifically.

**Overall assessment:** The chapter is factually accurate within the scholarly consensus and presents Indigenous perspectives with appropriate care. The composite figure methodology is transparent and appropriate. The chapter's framing of the 2021 discovery as confirmation rather than revelation is the correct characterization of how Indigenous communities experienced the announcement. Board endorsement recommended.
