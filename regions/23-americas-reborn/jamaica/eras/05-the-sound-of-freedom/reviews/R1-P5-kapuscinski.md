# Panel-1 Review — P-5 Ryszard Kapuściński
**Era:** Jamaica Era 5 — The Sound of Freedom
**Date:** 2026-04-17

I can smell the tenement. This is the test, and the opening passes it.

The "breeze that was not cool enough to call relief" — I know that air. I have stood in similar air in Port-au-Prince, in Lagos, in Havana, in the cities that were built to produce wealth for someone else and where the people who remained after the wealth was extracted lived in what the wealth left behind. The opening places us in that air in the first paragraph and does not let us out of it. This is what I require.

**What works at the sensory level:**

The sound system speakers "taller than a man" — I can see them in the yard at night, the yellow light on the crowd, the music pressing against the zinc fences. The chalice described as "the long clay pipe" — specific, material, smelling of use. The guitar chop and the piano skank described through their physical landing: "they landed where the Western ear expected rest." The body of the listener is present in this description.

The scene of the three young men in the yard works because we are given their exact ages. Twenty, nineteen, not yet eighteen. These are not names in a history book. These are boys. Boys from the hills who came to a city that did not hold what they expected and who in a room that was not much of a room were doing something that would change how millions of people understood freedom. The gap between the room and what came from it is the opening's central tension, and it is felt.

**What I want in the full chapter:**

The sound. The full chapter must contain sound at a level the opening only promises. What did it actually sound like to stand in front of a sound system in Trench Town in 1966, the bass at a frequency you feel in your chest before you hear it in your ears? What did a rocksteady record sound like on the turntable in a small room? What was the specific sound of the guitar chop — the attack and the release — that distinguished reggae from every other music on Earth?

I have followed politicians into exile and dictators into their last hours, and the detail that stays is always the sensory detail. A reader who has never heard reggae should leave the full chapter with the music in their body. This is achievable. It requires specific sensory description of the music as physical experience.

The violence. The opening mentions the garrison communities coming. When the chapter reaches the 1970s — the Green Bay Massacre, the peace concert, the political shooting that sent Marley out of Jamaica for eighteen months — I want to feel the heat of that violence. Not to dwell in it. Not to explain it for a distance reader. But to make the chapter's reader understand: this was the air in which the music was made. The music was not a response to an abstract concept of oppression. It was a response to the specific sound of a gunshot in a Kingston yard.

**The question I'd push on:**

When Marley died in Miami in 1981 — thirty-six years old, from melanoma that started in a toenail he hurt playing football — what happened in Kingston? What happened in the yards? I want to know what that day felt like in Trench Town. Not the state funeral. The day before the state funeral, when it was still real and not yet ceremony.

**Overall:** The sensory foundation is solid. Build the full chapter to this standard and do not let the prose cool when the history gets complicated.
