# PANEL-1 Review — P5: Ryszard Kapuściński
## Mexico Era 4 — El Porfiriato y La Revolución

**Lens:** Sensory immersion — heat, smell, sound, texture

---

The chapter is already doing something Kapuściński would recognize: it knows that the ground is the argument. The iron box in the church floor, the fields of Anenecuilco, the transit and the document arriving from outside — these are physical presences, not abstractions. When Zapata's men take a town and return the land records to village authorities, I can see it. I can feel the weight of the documents changing hands.

What is not yet present in the opening: the heat of Morelos. This is sugar country — the tierra caliente, the hot lowlands where the cane grows. The hacienda of Hospital is not an abstraction; it is a physical place with a processing plant, a chimney, the smell of molasses and burning bagasse, the sound of machinery that the villages can hear from their fields. The sugarcane of Morelos is not background; it is the physical presence of the system that displaced the milpa. When the chapter opens into its middle sections, I want to feel the heat and the smell that Zapata's men were riding through, and the specific texture of sugar country — the cane rows taller than a man on horseback, the jaguey water tanks on the hacienda grounds, the caporal's house near the mill.

Zapata himself needs more body in the chapter. The opening gives him age and acts, but I want to know how he moves, what he looks like on a horse (he was a skilled horseman, known for it), the sound of his voice. He is already mythologized in 1920 — the corrido tradition has made him into an image. The chapter should give us the man before the myth calcified, in the specific sensory present of 1911.

The opening's closing line is the strongest thing in it: "They are still waiting." No authorial heat, no sentimentality, just the fact. The chapter should earn that line from the inside — by giving us, before we arrive at it, the specific heat and dust and cornfield smell of what "waiting" means in Morelos.

**The question I'd push on:** What does the disputed field look like in the moment when Zapata's men plant it? Describe the ground — what season, what crops, what the soil looks like when it has been disputed for thirty years between a hacienda's cane and a village's milpa. That moment should be physically present in the chapter.
