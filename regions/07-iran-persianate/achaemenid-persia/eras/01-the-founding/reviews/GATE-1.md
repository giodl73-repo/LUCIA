## Notes

**Exceptional:** This opening achieves something rare — the Cyrus Cylinder is made to do structural analytical work, not decorative citation. The translation excerpt functions as primary-source evidence, characterization of Persian imperial ideology, and voice-anchor simultaneously. This is the v1.3 "Primary Art as Structural Evidence" technique applied to an inscriptional source: the cylinder IS the argument.

**Voice identification:** The edict-inscriptional register is appropriate to Achaemenid culture, which communicated governance through inscription (the Behistun cliff, the Persepolis reliefs, the cylinder). The style guide notes this as a confirmed register. The voice carries analytical weight in the same rhythm as narrative — "The cylinder in the temple was not a proclamation of conquest. It was an application to govern." That sentence does the intellectual work without leaving the inscriptional mode.

**Engine visible:** The structural engine — decentralized legitimacy through honoring local sacred orders — is already visible in the opening. The mechanism (Persian king entering each sacred system as participant, not conqueror) and the terminal condition (what happens when this system encounters a party that doesn't recognize the premise?) are both suggested without being stated. This sets up the v1.6 "premise non-recognition" terminal condition (Alexander's conquest treating Persian sacred orders as irrelevant) without announcing it.

**Honesty v1.2 standard:** The "deception that was also true" formulation honors the complexity without verdict. The Nabonidus Chronicles are used as Babylonian evidence about Nabonidus's failures, not as outside confirmation of Persian legitimacy — the distinction the v1.2 amendment calls for. The priests' acceptance is grounded in their own institutional logic.
