# R1 — P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of The Kingdom's Rise (Opening)

**Overall:** The voice is right. This story belongs to Biainili. The Assyrians are the other, the pressure, the presence on the southern horizon. Sarduri is not measured against them. He measures himself, and then he discards the measure and speaks in his own tongue.

**What works:**
- The opening inhabits the Urartian perspective throughout. Sarduri borrows Assyrian script not because Assyria is superior but because there is no other script available. The distinction is crucial and the text handles it cleanly: "His choice was not submission. It was acquisition."
- The Assyrians are seen from the highland, not the other way around. They come north, they burn, they extract, they go home. The highland is the center of the story. Assyria is the recurring problem.
- The final paragraph gives the culture its own voice finding. "The script was borrowed. The language was their own." This is ownership. The culture does not need to apologize for borrowing a technology, any more than a writer needs to apologize for using an alphabet someone else invented.

**What doesn't:**
- I am watchful for one phrase: "a king having none equal to him, a shepherd to be wondered at." This is the Assyrian titulature, presented as Sarduri's borrowed formula. It works because the text explicitly frames it as borrowed. But in the chapter, when Urartian kings begin to develop their own inscriptional formulas, the text must let the Urartian formula be its own thing, not a variation on the Assyrian model. The danger is that the reader comes to see Biainili as a reflection of Assyria rather than as its own world.
- The people of the highland are absent. Sarduri inscribes. Masons cut. But the farmers, the herders, the people who make the kingdom something worth naming, they have no presence yet. The chapter must bring them in.

**The question I'd push on:**
When the Urartian language appears on stone, whose language is it? The king's? The court's? The highland peoples'? Was there a single Urartian language or a family of related dialects that the inscription standardized? The act of writing a language for the first time is also the act of choosing which version of that language becomes official. The chapter should be alert to this.
