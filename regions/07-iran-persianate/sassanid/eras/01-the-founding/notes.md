# Notes — Sassanid Era 1: The Founding (c. 224–272 CE)
*Production Bible*

---

## 1. Sources

**Primary (Tier 1)**
- Naqsh-e Rostam rock reliefs — physical primary source; Ardashir and Shapur I investiture scenes, Shapur's Roman victory relief (Ka'ba-ye Zartosht inscription nearby)
- Ka'ba-ye Zartosht (Cube of Zoroaster) inscription of Shapur I — trilingual (Middle Persian, Parthian, Greek); lists provinces, honors to fire temples, account of Roman wars
- Shapur I's *Res Gestae* on the Ka'ba-ye Zartosht — first-person account of Roman emperors defeated; directly comparable to Ardashir's cliff claims as genre
- Avestan liturgical texts — the Yasna, the Gathas (Zarathustra's own hymns); used for Zoroastrian cosmological framework
- Coins: Ardashir's drachms showing the fire altar on reverse, his royal portrait on obverse; early Sassanid numismatic evidence for the fire-legitimacy connection

**Academic (Tier 2)**
- Touraj Daryaee, *Sasanian Iran (224–651 CE): Portrait of a Late Antique Empire* (2008) — foundational modern scholarly treatment
- Zeev Rubin, "The Sassanid Monarchy" in *Cambridge Ancient History* vol. XII — political and religious structure
- Prods Oktor Skjærvø, scholarship on Avestan texts and early Zoroastrian tradition
- Richard Frye, *The Heritage of Persia* — integration of Sassanid into long Iranian cultural tradition
- Rika Gyselen, scholarship on Sassanid administrative seals and fire temple organization

**Scholarly secondary (Tier 3)**
- Josef Wiesehöfer, *Ancient Persia* — survey accessible and reliable
- Klaus Schippmann, *Grundzüge der Geschichte des sasanidischen Reiches* — German but used for structural detail

**Key caution:** Many sources for Ardashir's reign are either inscriptional (Sassanid propaganda), later Islamic-era Persian narratives (*Shahnameh* tradition), or Roman/Byzantine accounts. The chapter should handle this source asymmetry honestly: Ardashir left us his claim in stone; what we know of the Parthian-Sassanid transition is largely filtered through one side's commemorative record.

## 2. Key Figures

**Ardashir I (r. 224–242 CE)**
- Born in Istakhr, Fars; grandson of Sasan (fire temple custodian)
- Served as a local governor under Parthian suzerainty before beginning expansion c. 208 CE
- Defeated Ardawan V (Artabanus IV) at Hormozdgan, April 224 CE — traditional date for founding of empire
- Coronation scene at Naqsh-e Rostam: receives ring from Ahura Mazda; Artabanus crushed beneath horse's hooves
- Consolidated military control over the plateau 224–230 CE; began reorganization of Zoroastrian clergy
- First Roman war: invaded Mesopotamia, took Nisibis and Carrhae briefly (230 CE); Roman emperor Alexander Severus mounted counter-campaign but no decisive outcome
- Called *shāhanshāh* — king of kings — reviving the Achaemenid title

**Shapur I (r. 242–272 CE)**
- Son of Ardashir; co-ruler from c. 240 CE; sole ruler from 242
- Most celebrated military achievement: three Roman wars; capture of Gordian III (died 244 CE, disputed), negotiated settlement with Philip the Arab, and most dramatically — capture of Valerian in 260 CE at Battle of Edessa (only Roman emperor ever taken prisoner by a foreign power)
- Valerian's capture commemorated at Naqsh-e Rostam (Shapur's triumph scene), at Bishapur, at Darabgird — the multiplication of commemorative sites shows the scale of the achievement's significance within the dynasty
- Ka'ba-ye Zartosht inscription lists 67 provinces of the empire; 1,000 fires established for Shapur's soul at fire temples; material support for Zoroastrian clergy formalized in inscription
- Major building program: founded or rebuilt cities including Bishapur (using Roman prisoners as labor), Gundeshapur (which would later become a major intellectual center)
- Known as Shapur *dhul-aktaf* — "of the shoulders" — in Arabic tradition; possibly referring to a different Shapur, but the epithet signals his lasting memory
- Remarkable religious tolerance: inscription records grants to Zoroastrian, Buddhist, Brahmanical, and Christian communities — suggesting the Zoroastrian state had a more nuanced relationship to religious diversity than later orthodox accounts suggest

**Kartir (fl. c. 240–290 CE)**
- The major Zoroastrian high priest of the founding era; his inscriptions at Naqsh-e Rostam, Naqsh-e Rajab, and Ka'ba-ye Zartosht tell the story of the clergy's institutionalization from the priest's side
- Rose from "hrb priest" (teacher of religious law) to "mobadan mobad" — high priest of priests — under successive Sassanid kings
- His inscriptions claim he organized fire temples, suppressed heretical sects (including Buddhism, Christianity, Manichaeism, Judaism in some formulations), and established Zoroastrian orthodoxy
- The tension: Shapur I's inscription indicates tolerance of multiple faiths; Kartir's inscription indicates suppression of those same faiths. This tension is real and historically significant — two contemporary voices offering different accounts of the same state's religious policy.
- KEY FIGURE for the chapter: Kartir gives us the priestly perspective on institutionalization; the chapter should use him to show what formalization looked like from inside the clergy

**Mani (c. 216–274/277 CE)**
- Founder of Manichaeism; active during Shapur I's reign; received at the Sassanid court; accompanied Shapur on military campaigns
- His presence at the Sassanid court is historically documented through his own writings and Manichaean sources
- Executed after Shapur's death (under Bahram I, advised by Kartir) — a vivid illustration of the shift in religious policy
- NOT a side figure: Mani at the Sassanid court, and his subsequent fate, is one of the most revealing episodes about the real dynamics of Zoroastrian institutionalization. The court that tolerated Mani under Shapur and executed him after Shapur is the same dynasty, processing the same tension.

## 3. Structural Engine

**Engine:** Zoroastrian kingship as cosmic infrastructure — the sacred fire as the spine connecting Ahura Mazda's authority through the priesthood to the king to the empire.

**Mechanism:** The fire temple system works because all legitimate authority in Ērānshahr flows through the same channel: divine truth (asha) is embodied in the sacred fires, maintained by the magi, endorsed by the king who endows them, and expressed in the king's military victories over the forces of the Lie (druj). Military victory is not just political — it is cosmological proof. When Shapur captures Valerian, this is not merely a Roman defeat; it is evidence that Ahura Mazda endorses the Sassanid dynasty. The stone reliefs are not propaganda in the modern sense — they are liturgy. They tell the truth about how the world is ordered.

**Terminal condition (for Era 1 specifically):** The engine is at its strongest here. The founding era's terminal condition is the question the engine generates but cannot answer internally: what happens when the fire authority and the military authority diverge? Ardashir united them; Shapur's religious tolerance begins to create the gap; Kartir's career represents the clergy's attempt to formalize their authority independently of royal will. This tension — between royal tolerance and priestly orthodoxy — will define the Classical Sassanid period (Era 2). Era 1 ends with the tension visible but unresolved: Shapur dies, Mani is executed, Kartir's power grows.

**Non-chronological organizing principle (partial):** The chapter will use the rock reliefs as anchors — each major section returns to the physical record at Naqsh-e Rostam and Ka'ba-ye Zartosht, because those are the culture's own primary texts. The stone reliefs ARE the Sassanid historical method. To treat them as illustration rather than evidence would be to misunderstand the culture's own approach to truth.

## 4. Section Plan

**Section 1 (opening, ~800w) — The Cliff**
Opening from opening.md, integrated: the mason at Naqsh-e Rostam, Ardashir's investiture scene, the Achaemenid dialogue above. Revise per Panel-1 feedback: add one moment of felt Arsacid eclecticism (the Greek deity on the coin), add one body at the road below, use Zoroastrian idiom for the druj threat.

**Section 2 (~800w) — The Fire Temple**
Ardashir's origins. Istakhr and the fire of Adur Farnbag. What a fire temple means from inside the tradition: the specific practice, the wood, the ritual hours, the hierarchy of sacred fires (three grades: Adur Gushnasp for warriors, Adur Burzen-Mihr for farmers, Adur Farnbag for priests). The news of Hormozdgan arriving at Istakhr — what does the first day of Ērānshahr feel like at the fire? Kapuściński asked for this; give it.

**Section 3 (~700w) — Kartir**
The priest who builds the institution while the king builds the empire. Kartir's inscriptions — an unusual source: a priest commemorating his own career in the same genre as the kings he served. What the Zoroastrian clergy gained from the Sassanid arrangement: defined hierarchy, royal revenues, the power to define orthodoxy. What they gave: legitimation of the dynasty, the fire's certification of the king's divine authority. Tension: Shapur's tolerance versus Kartir's orthodoxy. Mani at court.

**Section 4 (~900w) — Shapur and Rome**
The three Roman wars. Not as military narrative — as the Sassanid understanding of what Roman defeat means. Valerian's capture at Edessa: how the Sassanid tradition processes this (the stone relief, the inscription's language). 70,000 Roman prisoners and the cities they build: Bishapur's Roman-style mosaic floors, the arched bridge over the Shapur River (still standing) built by Roman POWs. Roman engineering made to serve Zoroastrian imperial architecture. The paradox: the empire that is fighting Rome is also learning from Rome and building with Rome's bodies.

**Section 5 (~600w) — The Inscription**
Shapur's Ka'ba-ye Zartosht inscription as primary source. The trilingual nature: Persian, Parthian, Greek — because the empire has audiences in all three worlds. The list of provinces. The 1,000 fires endowed for Shapur's soul. The grants to Zoroastrian, Buddhist, Brahmanical, and Christian communities — the real picture of Sassanid religious policy, more complex than either Kartir's inscriptions or later Islamic accounts suggest. What does it mean to endow fires for your soul while also endowing other faiths' institutions?

**Section 6 — Landing (~300w)**
Shapur dies in 272 CE. Mani is executed shortly after. The fires burn. The cliff at Naqsh-e Rostam holds its carvings. The landing should use the stone itself — the permanence of the relief, the continuing function of the fires — to deliver the culture's own persistence mechanism. Not death as ending but stone as continuity.

## 6. Voice Register

**Primary register:** Edict-Inscriptional, as identified in GATE-1.

Characteristics: declarative, weighted, carved. Each sentence placed on the page as a mason places chisel-work. Modulates across modes:
- **War/kinetic** (Section 4, Roman wars): shorter sentences, forward movement, the weight of lance on Roman armor
- **Policy/enumerative** (Section 5, Ka'ba-ye Zartosht inscription): the voice counts, lists, specifies — Shapur's own method
- **Material/descriptive** (Section 2, fire temple): the weight of stone walls, the smell of sacred wood, the heat
- **Landing/elegiac** (Section 6): gravity returns, sentences lengthen slightly, stone and fire as the last images

Maintain inscriptional voice throughout. Zero em-dashes expected as emergent property of the register.

## 8. Source Asymmetry Note

The Sassanid record is almost entirely self-referential: Sassanid kings commemorating Sassanid victories in Sassanid media. Roman sources (Zosimus, the Historia Augusta) are hostile and unreliable in different ways. Later Islamic-era Persian sources (including the Shahnameh tradition) reflect a Sassanid legendary tradition that had passed through centuries of oral transmission.

The chapter should handle this openly — the source situation is itself evidence about the culture. A civilization that carves its history into vertical limestone faces rather than writing it on papyrus is making a statement about durability, about what truth looks like. The stone IS the archive. This is not an absence of record; it is a different kind of record, and the chapter should treat it as such (v1.5 Source Asymmetry amendment: "source gap as cultural honesty").
