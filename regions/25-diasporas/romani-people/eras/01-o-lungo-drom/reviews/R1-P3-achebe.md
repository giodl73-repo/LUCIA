## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Romani Era 1 Opening

**Overall:** This opening passes the Achebe Test. The story belongs to the people. The language is theirs, the road is theirs, the name Roma is theirs. The European names (Athinganoi, Tsigane, Zigeuner) are presented as impositions, and the people's own name arrives last and with quiet authority: "They called themselves Roma. The word means, simply, people." That is the right move, and it is made at the right moment.

**What works:**
- The culture owns its story from the first word. "Drom" is a Romani word, and the opening begins there — not with what Europeans called them, not with what scholars hypothesize about them, but with a word from their own mouth.
- "They were not the kind of people for whom chronicles were kept" — this is honesty without pity. It states the social fact without moralizing about it. The absence of records is not lamented; it is stated.
- The handling of the European names is exactly right: Athinganoi, Tsigane, Zigeuner, Zingaro appear in a single list, clearly marked as outsiders' names, "none of them the name the people used for themselves." Then the real name arrives. This structure enacts the history: the outside naming first, the inside truth last and lasting.
- The forbidden word "gypsy" does not appear. Good. It should never appear in this chapter.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening is told about the Roma, not by them. The voice is sympathetic, intelligent, respectful — but it is a narrator's voice, not the culture's own voice. The Romani oral tradition has its own way of telling the origin story: proverbs, songs, formulaic phrases. The opening does not yet sound like a Romani elder telling this story. It sounds like an exceptionally sensitive scholar telling it. For most LUCIA chapters, this would be sufficient. For a diaspora people whose entire history has been told by others, the bar must be higher.
- One phrase concerns me: "into a continent that did not want them." This is true, but it risks positioning the Roma as victims from the first paragraph. The Roma are survivors, builders, carriers of a complete culture. The persecution is real, but it should not be the first thing we learn about them after the departure. Let Europe's hostility arrive when the Roma arrive in Europe — not before.

**The question I'd push on:**
Whose voice is this, exactly? The discover voice — mythic-linguistic realism — is working well as a technique. But the Romani have their own oral traditions about the departure, their own way of framing the journey. Can the chapter find a way to let Romani speech patterns, proverbs, or story-forms shape the prose itself? Not as quotation, but as rhythm? The chapter's treatment of the people is impeccable. Its inhabitation of their voice has one more step to take.
