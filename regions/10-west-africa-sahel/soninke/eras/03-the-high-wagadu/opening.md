# The High Wagadu

The virgin walked north in the last dark before dawn, and the sand under her feet held the cold of the night.

The women of her family had dressed her before the stars faded. White cotton, the best cloth the compound held, wound tight against the harmattan air that still carried the Sahara's chill even as the dry season turned toward its end. The procession moved behind her: the elders, the diviners who read the sand and the kola nut, the drummers whose instruments had not yet spoken because the drums would not speak until the moment came. The well sat at the edge of the cultivated land where the millet fields ended and the scrub began, where the baobabs thinned and the acacias took over and the ground turned from red laterite to the pale grit of the desert's margin. The stone lip of the well had been worn smooth by generations of rope. Below, the darkness held water, and the water held Bida, and Bida held Wagadu in the world.

Every year, the Soninke gave Bida what the covenant demanded. The serpent lived in the well, or beneath it, or was the well itself, depending on which lineage carried the telling and what the telling needed to do. What the tellings shared: Wagadu existed because Bida sustained it. Gold surfaced in the streambeds of Bambuk and Bure because Bida willed it. The rains came to the Sahel, the narrow belt of country between the desert and the green lands to the south, because Bida received what was owed. In return, the most beautiful daughter of the Soninke walked to the well, and the family that gave her did not grieve as an outsider might expect them to grieve. They had given the world its continuation. The covenant was not an affliction. It was the highest form of participation in the order that kept all things in place.

The Soninke who built Wagadu understood their wealth as the product of this exchange. Gold for obligation. Rain for sacrifice. The covenant with Bida was their own explanation for something that needed explaining: why the Soninke, who sat in the dry country where the Sahara's edge met the savanna, who owned no goldfields, who grew millet and sorghum in soil that cracked and whitened four months of every year, had become the wealthiest people in the western Sudan.

The answer was the crossing. Wagadu sat where the world's goods changed hands.

To the north, the salt. At Taghaza and Idjil, men cut salt from deposits in the earth and loaded the blocks onto camels, wrapping them in reed matting against the cracking heat, and the caravans walked south for two months across gravel plains and sand seas where the midday air shimmered above the ground and the nights froze and the wells were three days apart. To the south, the gold. In the forested country of Bambuk and Bure, people dug shallow pits in the streambeds during the dry season, sifting red earth through calabash pans in water that ran the color of rust, and the gold dust collected in the creases of the wood. The gold went north. The salt went south. And in between, at the hinge of the exchange, the Soninke held the ground where salt and gold met, and the holding of that ground was what Bida's covenant sustained.

Al-Bakri, the Andalusian geographer, wrote from Cordoba in the year 460 of the Hijra (1067 CE) from the accounts of merchants who had been there. He described two towns, six miles apart. In one town: the king, sitting in a domed pavilion in a grove of trees, wearing a cap of gold wound in cotton, his officials in silk and brocade, ten horses tethered at the gate with gold-worked harness, guard dogs at his side in collars of gold and silver, and the drum, a drum hollowed from a single trunk, whose voice carried across the flat Sahel plain so that the people of both towns heard it and knew the king was sitting. In the other town, six miles of packed-earth road to the north: stone houses, twelve mosques with their imams and muezzins, and the call to prayer rising into the dry air and drifting south on the harmattan toward the royal grove. Muslim merchants. Berber traders. Scholars and scribes. The Arabic language overlapping with Soninke in the markets where the goods of two worlds were weighed and exchanged.

Two towns. Two orders. One state. The king's authority came from the covenant with Bida, from the Soninke understanding of sacred kingship, from the relationship between the ruler and the forces that held the land and its rain and its gold in place. The merchants' world ran on the networks of Islamic commerce that connected the Sahel to Sijilmasa, to Fez, to the broader web that reached from Cordoba to Baghdad. The Soninke structured these two orders to need each other. The king guaranteed the security of the routes. The merchants generated the wealth. The six miles of road between the royal grove and the merchant quarter was the distance that held distinction and dependence in balance, close enough for daily business, far enough that neither order absorbed the other.

The gold that moved through this crossing made the Soninke so central to the Saharan trade that the Arabic geographers took the royal title "Ghana," meaning war chief or king, and used it as the name of the state itself. But the Soninke did not call their country Ghana. They called it Wagadu. And the covenant held. And the virgin walked. And the gold moved north, and the salt moved south, and in the twin cities where the drum spoke and the muezzin called and the road between them was the whole structure of a world, the Soninke of Wagadu kept the crossing open.
