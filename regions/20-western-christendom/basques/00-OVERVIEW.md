# Euskaldunak — "The Speakers of Euskara"

We are the people who speak Euskara, and Euskara is the oldest language in Europe for which no ancestor can be found. When the Romans arrived, when the Celts came before them, when the Indo-European migrations remade the linguistic map of the continent, Euskara was already here. The Basques did not migrate to the Pyrenees. We are the Pyrenees. Our law — the *foru*, the ancient customs that the Castilian and Navarrese and Aragonese kings swore to uphold when they ruled Basque territory — was not written by any monarch or council. It grew from the land itself, from the customs of the valley communities, from the practices that made mountain life possible. Every lord who wanted Basque cooperation had to swear to the *foru* under the oak tree at Guernica, and this act of swearing was not ceremony. It was the price of governance.

The sea is our second country. Basque whalers were in the North Atlantic centuries before Columbus sailed — hunting the great whales off Iceland and eventually off Labrador, processing them at sea, returning with oil that lit the lamps of half of Europe. Basque fishermen knew the Newfoundland banks before any European king claimed them. Basque shipbuilders supplied the best vessels in Iberia; Basque navigators piloted the ships of Castile and Portugal. The maritime expertise that made the Iberian empires possible was, in significant measure, Basque expertise. We were the invisible infrastructure of the ocean age.

Key places:
- **Guernica (Gernika)** — the oak tree; the seat of the *Juntas Generales*; where the lords of Vizcaya swore to uphold Basque law
- **Pamplona (Iruñea)** — the capital of Navarre; the city where Basque and Navarrese political traditions intersected
- **Bilbao** — the commercial port; iron ore and wool; the maritime trade hub
- **Roncesvalles (Orreaga)** — the mountain pass; where Charlemagne's rearguard was destroyed; the place the *Chanson de Roland* transformed into legend

## What Defined Them

**Euskara — the language that survived everything.** Euskara is a language isolate — it has no known relatives anywhere in the world. Its survival through the Roman occupation, the Visigoth kingdom, the Frankish expansion, and medieval Castilian dominance is the central fact of Basque identity. The language is not simply a communication tool; it is the carrier of a legal vocabulary, an oral literary tradition, a way of naming the world that has no equivalent in any other European tongue. The Basque word for "knife" (*aizto*) means "stone tool" — a linguistic fossil from the period before metal. The language remembers things the history books have forgotten.

**The foru — law as covenant, not grant.** The Basque *forua* (fueros in Castilian) were not privileges granted by a king — they were customs the king swore to uphold. This distinction was fundamental. Castilian kings taking the lordship of Vizcaya, Álava, or Guipúzcoa were required to travel to the Basque lands and swear personally under the oak at Guernica or in the assembly of each province that they would honor existing Basque law. If they violated the oath, Basque political theory held that the covenant was broken. The *foru* protected local courts, exempted Basques from military service outside their territory, gave them control of their own customs revenues, and in general preserved a degree of self-governance that no other people under Castilian rule retained.

**The iron and the sea.** The Basque country sits over some of the richest iron ore deposits in Europe, and the combination of iron, charcoal from the Basque forests, and water power from the mountain streams produced a metalwork tradition — anchors, nails, cannon, tools — that supplied the entire Iberian maritime world. Bilbao grew as the export point for Castilian wool and Basque iron. The *Consulado de Bilbao*, founded 1511, was one of the earliest merchant guilds in Iberia. Basque shipbuilders built the ships; Basque ironworkers forged the anchors; Basque pilots navigated the routes. The Basque contribution to the Iberian ocean age was total and almost entirely invisible in the histories written by Castilians.

**The whaling tradition.** Basque whalers were hunting the great whales in the Bay of Biscay by the tenth century and had moved into the North Atlantic — Iceland, Newfoundland, Labrador — by the fifteenth. They were processing whales at sea (rendering the blubber into oil in try-works on the ships) before any other European maritime culture had developed the technique. The Basque word for "cod" — *bacallao* — appears in the languages of Atlantic fishing cultures from Newfoundland to Portugal, a linguistic trace of Basque commercial dominance of the North Atlantic fisheries before European colonization. The Basques knew those waters; they simply had no interest in claiming the land.

**The pelota and the bertsolari — culture as competition.** Basque culture organized competition into its deepest cultural forms. *Pelota* — the ball game played against a *frontón* wall, with bare hands or a curved wicker basket — is not simply recreation; it is a cultural expression of Basque athleticism, speed, and hand-eye precision that serves the same social function as the joust in chivalric culture. The *bertsolari* — the improvisational oral poet who competes in public verse duels — is a figure without parallel in European culture: a trained memorist who improvises rhymed verse on assigned topics in front of a crowd, with judges scoring the speed, rhyme, and content. The *bertsolaritza* tradition preserved Basque oral culture through centuries of pressure from written, Latin-educated, Castilian culture.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Ancient Time:** A Roman centurion attempts to levy taxes in the valley of the Nervión and is referred, politely, to the assembly of valley elders who will decide whether the levy is legitimate under their custom. The centurion has been dealing with Gauls, Iberians, and Celtiberians for years. He has never encountered a people who simply assume the right to negotiate with Rome on equal terms.

**2 — The Early Middle Age:** 778. Charlemagne's rearguard is crossing the Pass of Roncesvalles, returning from a failed campaign in Spain. The Basques of Gascony, who have been watching from the ridgeline, choose this moment. The pass is narrow. The armor of the Frankish knights is useless against boulders rolled from above. Roland dies. The *Chanson de Roland* will turn this local ambush into a heroic stand against the Saracens. The Basques who actually won do not appear in the poem.

**3 — The Kingdom of Navarre:** Sancho III the Great of Navarre controls more of the Iberian peninsula than any Christian king since the Visigoth era. His grandsons will divide it. But for one generation, the Basque political center holds an empire that stretches from the Atlantic to Castile. It will not last. The memory of it will.

**4 — The Castilian Integration:** The lord of Vizcaya, formally accepting Castilian overlordship, watches the king of Castile ride to Guernica to swear to the *foru* under the oak. The king is not happy about this requirement. The lord of Vizcaya is not apologizing. They understand each other perfectly.

**5 — The Age of the Sea:** A Basque pilot named Juan de la Cosa completes the first map of the Americas in 1500. He sailed with Columbus. He knows these coasts better than anyone alive. The map bears the Spanish crown's name. His own name is nearly invisible on it.
