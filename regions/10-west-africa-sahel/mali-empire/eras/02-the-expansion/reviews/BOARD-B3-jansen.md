# B-3 — Jansen — Board Review of "When the Empire Reached the Sea"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Mande political culture; the Kurukan Fuga as social charter; social categories (horon, nyamakalaw, jon); the logic of redistribution and obligation.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The Kurukan Fuga's function as a covenant between allied parties (not a royal decree) is correctly rendered. The chapter's consistent use of "covenant" rather than "charter" or "constitution" is appropriate — the Mande understanding is of a sacred agreement, not a legal document.
- The gbara's authority to depose a mansa is consistent with how the oral tradition describes the assembly's role. The chapter is right to note that the details of the deposition are sparse.
- The social categories are accurately described. The horon-nyamakalaw-jon framework is correctly placed within the Kurukan Fuga's terms. Sakura's status as jon is handled without apology or exoticization.
- The chapter's argument that the Manden was "a covenant, not a dynasty" is a strong reading that aligns with my own fieldwork on how Mande communities understand their political tradition. The covenant IS the political system. The mansa operates within it, not above it.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The Kurukan Fuga's self-correcting mechanism is the right structural reading. The covenant was designed to prevent tyranny because it was born from the defeat of a tyrant. The chapter makes this causal connection explicit.
- The relationship between the mansa and the Wangara goldfield communities is correctly described as "mutual obligation" rather than as direct administration. The Manden did not "own" the goldfields. It controlled the routes. This distinction is important and the chapter gets it right.
- The Dyula as carriers of Islam-by-example rather than Islam-by-conquest is accurate and important. The spread of Islam in the western Sudan was largely a commercial and social process, not a military one.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The handling of Sakura is the chapter's most important moment for Mande political philosophy. A jon becoming mansa is not an anomaly but evidence that the covenant's logic prioritized the Manden's survival over lineage claims. The chapter understands this.
- The covenant's response to Khalifa is framed as the system working, not as the system failing. This is the correct reading. Khalifa's deposition is institutional success, not institutional crisis.
- Abu Bakr's departure framed as "the covenant did not require the king to stay. It required the king to ensure that the kingdom could survive his absence" — this is a sophisticated reading of the covenant's terms.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter says "the jon, whose status was defined by service and whose social mobility was the most limited." This is broadly correct but could be more precise. Within the Kurukan Fuga's framework, the jon's status was complex: there were different categories of jon (jon born into the status vs. jon acquired through capture), and their roles, obligations, and possibilities varied. The chapter need not enumerate all categories, but acknowledging that "jon" encompasses multiple social positions would add accuracy.
- The phrase "the covenant's willingness to elevate competence over lineage" attributes agency to the covenant itself. In Mande political culture, the gbara (not the covenant in the abstract) makes the elevation. The covenant provides the framework; the assembly acts. This distinction matters for how Mande political authority actually operates.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS — Minor corrections on jon categories and gbara agency. The political philosophy is rendered with genuine depth.
