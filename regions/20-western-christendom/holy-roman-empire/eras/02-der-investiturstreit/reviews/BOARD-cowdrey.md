## Gregory VII — Accuracy of Representation

The chapter's Gregory is historically recognizable. The emphasis on his "brutal clarity" in correspondence, his absolute conviction, his willingness to act without diplomatic hedging — these are well-attested characteristics in his surviving letters (some 400 of which survive, edited in the Monumenta Germaniae Historica). The description of him as "severe with power and gentle with people" reflects the letters to Matilda and Hugh of Cluny with reasonable accuracy.

The key characterization — that Gregory believed in his own program, that the *Dictatus Papae* was not primarily a power grab but an expression of genuine theological conviction — is correct. My own research has consistently found that Gregory's letters reveal a man who experienced his actions as obligations, not ambitions. The chapter conveys this without romanticizing him.

**One correction to the figure section:** The chapter says Gregory "died believing he had failed." This is the traditional reading, derived from his last words and his geographical exile. It is not wrong, but it is incomplete. Gregory's letters from Salerno in his final months show him continuing to organize the Church, corresponding with allies, issuing directives — not the behavior of a man who believed the project was over. He believed this particular moment had gone against him. He did not believe the argument was lost. The distinction matters. Suggest revising: "He died in exile, having lost this particular round of the argument — though he did not believe, based on his final letters, that the argument itself was over."

## The Cluniac Reform — Accuracy

The chapter's rendering of the Cluniac reform movement is accurate. The charter from Duke William of Aquitaine in 910, Cluny's freedom from lay control, Hugh of Cluny's sixty-year abbacy — all correct.

The description of the Cluniacs as believing that "the quality of prayer offered to God depended on the freedom of those offering it" is an accurate summary of the reform theology, though it slightly undersells the Cluniac emphasis on liturgical elaboration. The Cluniacs were not only concerned with institutional freedom — they were concerned with the opus Dei, the divine office performed in the liturgically correct way, which required not only free monks but monks who had the resources and the stability to maintain an elaborate round of prayer. The institutional freedom was in service of the liturgical work. The chapter captures this in the Hugh figure section ("effective prayer requires institutional freedom") but could make the liturgical emphasis more explicit if space allows.

**No factual errors in the Cluniac material.**

## The *Dictatus Papae* — Accuracy

The chapter's rendering of three propositions from the *Dictatus Papae* is accurate. The choice of propositions — the universal title, the power to depose emperors, the power to release subjects from oaths — correctly identifies the most politically explosive elements.

The chapter describes the document as "a note to file — a statement of what Gregory believed to be true about the papacy's authority, placed in the record where it could be found." This captures the document's ambiguous status: it was entered in the register in 1075 but was not formally addressed to anyone and did not circulate as a papal decretal. Scholars still debate its precise function. The chapter's reading — a record of Gregory's working convictions — is defensible and does not overstate what the document was.

---

## Recommendation

One targeted revision recommended (Gregory's death: he did not believe the argument was lost, only that this round had gone against him). The Cluniac liturgical emphasis could be enriched but is not inaccurate. All other Gregory VII and Gregorian reform material is historically sound.

**Board status: APPROVED with one targeted revision**
