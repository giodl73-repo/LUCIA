## B-2 — Daniel K. Richter — Board Review of The Founding (Kaianere'kó:wa)

**Domain:** Mourning War complex, demographic/ecological context, structural conditions

**Factual assessment:**

The chapter's treatment of the Mourning War cycle is strong. The causal mechanism — grief produces raids, raids produce captives and new grief, the cycle tightens — is accurately described and matches the scholarly understanding of the Mourning War complex. The connection between agricultural neglect and intensifying raids is a reasonable inference from the archaeological record.

**Specific findings:**

1. **Mourning War nuance:** The chapter presents the Mourning War cycle as purely destructive ("the blood goes in a circle"). This is accurate as a description of the crisis that the Great Law addressed, but it should be noted that the Mourning War complex also served important social functions before it became unmanageable: captive adoption was a genuine mechanism for population maintenance and cultural integration, not merely an act of violence. The Great Law did not abolish the Mourning War entirely — it redirected it outward (against non-confederacy nations) and replaced the internal cycle with the condolence ceremony. The chapter's framing of the cycle as purely negative is acceptable for the founding narrative's internal logic, but the full chapter or the figures.md could note this complexity.

2. **Demographic context:** The chapter mentions palisaded towns and half-tended fields but does not address the demographic pressures that may have intensified the Mourning War cycle in the centuries before the founding. Population stress from epidemic disease (even pre-Columbian epidemics are debated), inter-nation competition for prime agricultural territory between the Finger Lakes, and the possibility of climate stress during the Little Ice Age could all have contributed to the crisis. The chapter does not need to resolve these questions, but a sentence acknowledging that the Mourning War cycle intensified for reasons that the archaeological record is still working to clarify would strengthen the honest-uncertainty approach.

3. **Captive adoption vs. condolence:** The chapter presents the condolence ceremony as a replacement for captive-taking. This is broadly correct for the internal relations among the five nations, but the mechanism is more precise: the condolence ceremony addressed the grief that drove the cycle, while the Requickening ceremony (the raising up of a new sachem to replace a fallen one) addressed the institutional vacuum that a death created. Both worked together. The chapter focuses on condolence; a brief mention of the Requickening would complete the picture.

**Ecological accuracy:**
The description of the northeastern woodlands landscape — hemlock, birch, sugar maple, the three sisters agricultural system — is accurate for the region. The connection between the longhouse and the forest (elm bark walls, birch bark canoes, hickory bows) is well drawn.

**Verdict:** APPROVE with notes. The chapter handles the Mourning War cycle and the ecological context with care. The findings above are enhancements, not corrections — the chapter does not make factual errors, but additional nuance would strengthen it.
