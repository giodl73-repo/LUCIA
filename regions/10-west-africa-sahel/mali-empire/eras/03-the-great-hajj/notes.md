# Production Bible — The Great Hajj

**Era:** Musa Keita's Reign, c. 1312-1337 CE
**Chapter target:** ~5,000 words
**Status:** Pre-draft

---

## 1. Chapter Arc

The opening establishes the departure from Niani — the jeli singing, the gold loaded, the caravan moving north. The full chapter must complete the circuit: departure, crossing, arrival, return, and consequence. But the arc is not the itinerary. The arc is the covenant consuming itself.

The story the chapter tells: The Manden Kurufaba built its legitimacy on spectacular redistribution. The Kurukan Fuga demanded that a mansa give — not moderately, not strategically, but completely. Musa's hajj is the system performing at full expression. The gold flows out of Niani because the covenant requires it. It crashes the Egyptian gold market because the covenant has no concept of enough. Musa borrows on the return journey because the covenant has emptied him. He brings back scholars and architects because the covenant also demands that what flows out must be replenished — not with gold, but with knowledge, with building, with the infrastructure that makes the next generation's wealth possible. And yet: the twelve-year gold depression means that the commodity on which the empire's trade position depends has lost its value precisely because the mansa did what the covenant demanded. The system that makes Mali great is the same system that destabilizes its material base. This is the self-consuming logic that Ibn Khaldun will demand and that the chapter must make visible.

All of this must be told from inside the Mande world. The jelilu are the measure. Cairo is a place Musa passed through; Majorca is a place that heard a rumor. The chapter's authority rests on the tradition that was singing at the gate when the caravan left, and that is still singing seven hundred years later. When Arab chroniclers appear, they appear as characters inside Musa's story — people he encountered, people who watched him pass — not as external validators whose written testimony makes the oral tradition real.

The chapter must end with weight in Mande terms. Not with the Catalan Atlas or the Egyptian gold market, but with what the hajj meant to the people who carried its memory: the mosques that rose in Timbuktu, the scholars who taught at Sankore, and the jelilu who are still singing.

## 2. Section Plan

### Section 1 — The Gate (Opening)
**Word target:** ~800 words (already drafted; revise per panel feedback)
**Anchor:** Bala Faseke Kouyate at the gate of Niani
**What it accomplishes:** Drops the reader into the departure morning. Establishes the jeli as first witness, the covenant as operating logic, the gold as mechanism not spectacle. Introduces Musa, Inari Kunate, the caravan's scale.

**Revisions needed from Panel-1:**
- Add one physical/sensory detail to the first sentence or two — the gate itself, the morning heat, dust already in the air — so the reader has a body before the voice begins (Kapuscinski)
- Remove "The sources agree on this" — let Musa's height be conveyed through presence, not historiographic aside (Achebe, Davis)
- Remove or reframe the Arab chroniclers' number disagreement — let the jelilu's testimony or the narrative voice carry the scale; save source discussion for later (Achebe, Davis)
- Remove the Majorca mapmakers entirely from the opening — the chapter's significance should not depend on European recognition (Achebe, Davis)
- Give Inari Kunate a moment — a physical detail, a gesture, a sensory particular. She cannot appear as a number on a manifest (Tuchman, Kapuscinski)
- Remove "the hajj is the fifth pillar" — trust the reader; this is textbook gloss inside a world where it is simply known (Achebe)
- Anchor the expository passage ("He was going because...") with one physical detail — what Musa is looking at, what the crowd is doing — to keep the ground under the reader (Kapuscinski)
- Change "five hundred men" to "five hundred jon" — name the social category the Kurukan Fuga established; evasion serves neither the Mande framework nor honesty (Achebe)

**Key source:** Sundiata Epic (oral tradition), al-Umari (for caravan details used sparingly)
**Panel risk:** Achebe will verify that every external reference has been removed or reframed. Kapuscinski will check that the first sentence has sensory ground.

### Section 2 — The Crossing
**Word target:** ~800 words
**Anchor:** The caravan in the Sahara — Inari Kunate as the perspective figure
**What it accomplishes:** The Sahara crossing is not scenery; it is the system made visible. The route from Niani to Walata to the desert is a chain of dependencies — water, fodder, political safe-passage, the infrastructure of alliances the Mali state spent generations building. This section puts the reader in the desert and makes them feel the logistics of moving sixty thousand people across sand. But it also introduces the first complication: the caravan is burning through resources. The gold is not just wealth to be distributed in Cairo — it is also the currency paying for water, for guides, for the political permissions that keep the caravan alive. The covenant's demand for generosity begins consuming material resources before the caravan reaches Egypt.

**Scene:** Inari Kunate's column on the march. She is the organizing intelligence — five hundred women, the food, the provisions. The desert through her eyes: the heat that cracks skin, the silence between dunes, the stars at night that the guides read, the weight of water carried on camelback. The scholars protecting their manuscripts from sand — wrapping them in oiled leather, checking them at each halt. A well that the advance party has found dry. The decision to push on or wait. Stakes: survival is not guaranteed, even for a mansa's caravan.

**Key sources:** Al-Umari (route details), Ibn Battuta (descriptions of Saharan crossing conditions from his own 1352 journey — used for physical detail, not as authority on Musa's specific journey), Levtzion (trade route infrastructure)
**Panel risk:** Tuchman will demand that something happens — a complication, a decision, a moment of stakes. The dry well provides this. Ibn Khaldun will check that the route is analyzed as system, not just described as landscape. Kapuscinski will demand that every sentence has temperature, weight, texture.

### Section 3 — Cairo
**Word target:** ~800 words
**Anchor:** Musa in Cairo — the gold spending and its consequences
**What it accomplishes:** This is the chapter's central scene and its most dangerous. The Cairo arrival is the moment most at risk of drifting to an outside perspective — the astonished Egyptians, the crashing gold market, the "richest man in the world" framing. The section must tell Cairo from Musa's perspective and the caravan's experience, not from Cairo's reaction.

**Scene:** Musa arrives in Cairo and does what the covenant demands: he gives. He gives to the poor, to the scholars, to the officials, to anyone who approaches. He gives because the Kurukan Fuga requires it and because generosity on this scale is what makes a mansa worthy of the jelilu's songs. The section should show the giving as deliberate, systematic, and rooted in Mande political logic — not as naive overspending.

But the section must also show the consequences. The gold market crashes. Musa does not know this in the moment — he is fulfilling the covenant, not monitoring exchange rates. The reader, however, begins to see the self-consuming logic: the covenant that makes Musa great is devaluing the commodity on which his empire's trade position depends. This is structural irony, not dramatic irony — the system is undermining itself by performing exactly as designed.

Al-Umari's informants appear here — not as external validators but as characters in the scene. A Cairo merchant watching gold lose its value. A court official receiving gifts he did not expect. These are people inside the story, witnessing the consequences of a political logic they do not understand. The incomprehension is theirs, not the reader's.

The section should also show Musa's encounter with the scholars and architects he will bring back — particularly Abu Ishaq al-Sahili, the Granadan architect. Musa is not simply performing piety; he is recruiting. The hajj is also a state investment in human capital.

**Key sources:** Al-Umari's Masalik al-Absar (the primary account of Musa in Cairo, based on interviews with witnesses), Ibn Khaldun's Kitab al-Ibar (the gold market disruption), Levtzion (economic analysis), Gomez (contextual framing)
**Panel risk:** Achebe will watch every sentence for drift to the outside perspective. The Cairo merchants must be characters inside Musa's story, not authorities on it. Ibn Khaldun will demand the self-consuming logic — the gold depression must be analyzed as structural consequence, not just anecdote. Davis will check that Musa's recruiting of scholars is framed as addition to an existing intellectual tradition, not correction of a deficit.

### Section 4 — The Return and the Building
**Word target:** ~800 words
**Anchor:** Abu Ishaq al-Sahili in Timbuktu — the architect building the Djinguereber Mosque
**What it accomplishes:** Musa returns. He has spent so much gold that he borrows on the return journey — at interest, the sources note — to fund the caravan home. This detail must be present: it is the covenant's self-consuming logic made concrete. The mansa who gave everything must now borrow to get home.

But the return is not defeat. It is transformation. Al-Sahili builds. The Djinguereber Mosque rises in mud-brick and limestone — a new architectural form that merges Andalusian and Sahelian building traditions. Timbuktu's character begins to change. Scholars who came with Musa stay. The private libraries grow. The Sankore mosque becomes a center of teaching where students from Morocco, Egypt, and villages along the Niger study jurisprudence, theology, mathematics, and astronomy side by side.

**Scene:** Al-Sahili at the building site. The physical detail of construction: mud-brick, the timber armatures (toron) that protrude from the walls, the lime plaster, the courtyard taking shape. The sound of building. The scholars unpacking their books. The smell of new plaster and ink. Musa watching the mosque rise — this is what the gold purchased. Not awe in Cairo, but a building in Timbuktu. The covenant demanded redistribution; what was redistributed comes back as knowledge and stone.

This section must treat Timbuktu's intellectual tradition as genuine scholarship — not as a surprising African achievement (Davis's demand). The scholars at Sankore are participants in the Islamic intellectual world, not recipients of it. They are writing commentaries, debating jurisprudence, teaching astronomy. This is what Timbuktu does.

**Key sources:** Al-Umari (on al-Sahili and Musa's return), Ibn Battuta (who visited Timbuktu in 1352-53 and described the scholarly culture — used carefully, as he arrived 15 years after Musa's death), Hunwick (Timbuktu intellectual history), Conrad (Mande oral tradition on the return)
**Panel risk:** Davis will verify that the intellectual tradition gets full depth — not shallow admiration but genuine engagement with what was studied and taught. Tuchman will check that the section builds rather than surveys — al-Sahili must be a person, not a fact. Kapuscinski will demand the sensory reality of construction and scholarship: mud, plaster, ink, the courtyard's acoustics.

### Section 5 — The Long Memory (Landing)
**Word target:** ~600 words
**Anchor:** The jelilu — Bala Faseke's tradition continuing
**What it accomplishes:** The chapter returns to its beginning. The jeli who sang at the gate is the tradition that will carry this story forward — not for the twelve years it takes the gold market to recover, but for seven hundred years and counting. The landing must close the circuit: the story began with the jeli and ends with the jeli. The outside world's records — al-Umari, Ibn Khaldun, the Catalan Atlas — are themselves becoming part of the song, absorbed into a tradition older and more persistent than any of them.

**Scene:** A specific, quiet moment. Not the grandeur of departure or the spectacle of Cairo but the persistence of memory. A jeli singing Musa's name in the generations after — perhaps Ibn Battuta's visit in 1352-53, fifteen years after Musa's death, when he found the jelilu still singing the names, the mosques still standing, the scholars still teaching. Or: the tradition as it exists now, the jelilu in Kangaba and Kela who still perform the Sundiata Epic with Musa's hajj woven into the genealogy. The landing should feel like the dust settling — slow, physical, final.

The last sentences must carry weight in Mande terms. What the hajj meant is not what Cairo thought or what Majorca drew. What the hajj meant is what the jelilu still sing: that a mansa left Niani with gold and came back with knowledge, that the covenant demanded everything and was given everything, and that the names are still being sung.

**Key sources:** Conrad and Jansen (contemporary jeli tradition), the Sundiata Epic (the living tradition), Ibn Battuta (for the 1352-53 visit as an epilogue voice)
**Panel risk:** Tuchman will check that the landing arrives at a specific moment rather than summarizing. Achebe will verify that the final authority rests with the Mande tradition, not with external validation. Kapuscinski will demand a closing image that is physical and felt — not rhetorical, not abstract.

## 3. Key Figures

### Mansa Musa (Musa Keita I)
**Role:** The mansa — ninth ruler of the Manden Kurufaba. The chapter's central figure, though not always its perspective character.
**In the narrative:** Present in all five sections. In the opening, he stands still while the jeli sings — tall, in white, the gold already loaded. In the crossing, he is the authority the caravan depends on. In Cairo, he gives. On the return, he watches al-Sahili build. In the landing, he is a name the jelilu carry.
**Human detail:** He came to power by accident — Abu Bakr II "went to sea and did not return," and Musa, his deputy, became mansa. He did not seek the throne. The sources describe him as tall, devout, and deliberate in speech. Al-Umari's informants said he was reluctant to meet the Sultan of Egypt because the protocol required him to kiss the ground, and a mansa does not prostrate before other kings. He found a compromise — he kissed the ground before God, not before the sultan. This is a man who navigates between systems of authority with care.

### Inari Kunate
**Role:** Musa's senior wife. Commander of her own column — five hundred women of her household.
**In the narrative:** Appears in the opening (revised to give her a moment) and becomes the perspective figure for the crossing section. She is the organizing intelligence of the caravan's domestic logistics — food, provisions, the survival infrastructure that makes the spectacle possible.
**Human detail:** The indigo of her wrapping cloth against the morning haze (already in the opening). She traveled with her own household, her own attendants, her own column — this is not a consort trailing behind the king but a political figure with her own retinue and authority. The sources give her name but little else; the chapter must be honest about what is inference and what is documented, while still giving her the presence the narrative demands.

### Bala Faseke Kouyate
**Role:** The jeli — praise-singer, genealogist, institutional memory of the Manden.
**In the narrative:** Opens the chapter and closes it. The framing voice. He sings the names at the gate; his tradition carries the names forward for seven hundred years. He is not a character in the conventional sense — he is the tradition embodied.
**Human detail:** His kora holds the low notes while his voice climbs. He is a Kouyate — the jeli clan whose connection to the Keita rulers goes back to Sundiata's time. Bala Faseke is the name the tradition gives to the jeli who accompanied the founding; the name recurs across generations of the Kouyate lineage. The chapter should let the name carry that weight without over-explaining it.

### Abu Ishaq al-Sahili (Abu Ishaq Ibrahim al-Sahili al-Tuwajjin)
**Role:** Andalusian-Granadan architect and poet whom Musa recruited during the hajj and brought back to Mali.
**In the narrative:** Appears in the Cairo section (the recruitment) and becomes the anchor figure for Section 4 (the return and building). He is the hajj's most tangible consequence — the man who built the Djinguereber Mosque in Timbuktu and introduced new architectural techniques to the Sahel.
**Human detail:** He was from Granada — al-Andalus, Islamic Spain — a world that was itself under pressure from the Reconquista. He was a poet as well as an architect. Al-Umari reports that Musa paid him 12,000 mithqals of gold. He built in a style that merged what he knew with what the Sahel demanded: mud-brick and limestone, the distinctive protruding toron timbers, flat roofs adapted to the climate. He lived out his life in Mali. He chose to stay.

### Al-Umari's Cairo Informants
**Role:** Witnesses to Musa's gold spending in Cairo — merchants, officials, members of the Egyptian court.
**In the narrative:** Appear in the Cairo section as characters inside the story, not as external authorities. They are the people Musa encountered — a merchant watching gold lose its value, a court official managing the protocol of a foreign king's visit.
**Important framing note:** Al-Umari himself never met Musa. He interviewed people in Cairo who had witnessed the visit, and wrote his account in the 1340s, nearly twenty years later. The informants are the witnesses; al-Umari is the compiler. In the chapter, the informants should appear as people in the scene. Al-Umari himself should not narrate.

### Ibn Battuta (possible epilogue voice)
**Role:** Moroccan traveler who visited Mali in 1352-53, fifteen years after Musa's death (Musa died c. 1337).
**In the narrative:** May appear in the landing section as an epilogue witness — someone who arrived after the events of the chapter and found the consequences still alive: the mosques standing, the scholars teaching, the jelilu singing. He is useful precisely because he is late — he shows the persistence of what Musa built.
**Human detail:** He was often querulous, opinionated, and culturally judgmental — his account of Mali includes complaints about Mande customs he found shocking. This makes him human and unreliable in specific ways. If he appears, his limitations should be visible. He is a witness, not an authority. The jelilu who were singing when he arrived had been singing for decades before he showed up.

## 4. Sources

### Tier 1 — Primary / Oral

**The Sundiata Epic (jelilu oral tradition)**
Multiple versions; Djibril Tamsir Niane's transcription (1960/1965) is the standard accessible text, but the tradition is living and plural. Jan Jansen's work on the Kela and Kangaba performances documents the contemporary continuation.
*Used in:* Sections 1 and 5. The jeli's genealogy, the rhythm of oral recitation, the tradition's persistence as the chapter's framing authority.

**Al-Umari, Masalik al-Absar fi Mamalik al-Amsar (c. 1342)**
The primary written account of Musa's hajj. Al-Umari interviewed witnesses in Cairo who had seen Musa's visit firsthand. Contains the most detailed descriptions of the caravan's scale, the gold spending, the meeting with the Egyptian sultan, and the recruitment of al-Sahili.
*Used in:* Sections 1, 3, and 4. Caravan details, the Cairo spending, al-Sahili's recruitment and payment, Musa's interaction with Egyptian protocol.

**Ibn Battuta, Rihla (c. 1355)**
First-person account of travel to Mali in 1352-53 — fifteen years after Musa's death. Describes the court, the scholarly culture, the mosques, and Mande customs. Valuable for physical detail of the Saharan crossing and of Malian cities, though his cultural judgments reflect his own Moroccan-Islamic perspective.
*Used in:* Section 2 (physical detail of Saharan crossing conditions), Section 5 (epilogue witness). Used carefully — he did not witness Musa's hajj.

**Ibn Khaldun, Kitab al-Ibar (late 14th century)**
History of the western Sudan including Mali's dynastic succession and the economic impact of Musa's gold spending. Contains the structural analysis of the gold market disruption.
*Used in:* Section 3. The twelve-year gold depression, the economic consequences of the hajj.

### Tier 2 — Academic

**Levtzion, Nehemia. *Ancient Ghana and Mali*. 1973. Methuen.**
The foundational modern scholarly work on the Mali Empire. Trade routes, political structure, economic analysis, chronology.
*Used in:* Sections 2, 3, and 4. Route infrastructure, economic context, political succession.

**Gomez, Michael A. *African Dominion: A New History of Empire in Early and Medieval West Africa*. 2018. Princeton University Press.**
The most important recent synthesis — reframes West African state-building on its own terms. Essential for avoiding the outside-in framing the panel will police.
*Used in:* All sections. Contextual framing, the Mande political worldview, the covenant logic.

**Hunwick, John O. *Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire*. 1999. Brill.**
Detailed history of Timbuktu's intellectual culture, the Sankore mosque, the manuscript traditions.
*Used in:* Section 4. The scholarly tradition, the libraries, the curriculum.

**Conrad, David C. *Empires of Medieval West Africa: Ghana, Mali, and Songhay*. 2005. Chelsea House.**
Accessible synthesis with strong treatment of oral tradition and Mande social structure.
*Used in:* Sections 1, 4, and 5. The jeli tradition, the Kurukan Fuga's terms, the oral tradition's contemporary continuation.

**Niane, Djibril Tamsir. *Sundiata: An Epic of Old Mali*. 1965. Longman.**
The standard written version of the Sundiata Epic. Essential reference for the genealogical tradition and the covenant's origins.
*Used in:* Section 1. The genealogy, the Keita lineage, the jeli's performance.

### Tier 3 — Scholarly Secondary

**Jansen, Jan. "The Sunjata Epic — The Ultimate Version." *Research in African Literatures* 32, no. 1 (2001).**
Critical analysis of the oral tradition's variation and the politics of transcription. Important for understanding why different jelilu tell different versions.
*Used in:* Section 5. The living tradition, the plurality of memory.

**Levtzion, Nehemia & Spaulding, Jay (eds.). *Medieval West Africa: Views from Arab Scholars and Merchants*. 2003. Markus Wiener.**
Translations of the key Arabic sources on West Africa, including al-Umari and Ibn Battuta extracts.
*Used in:* Sections 3 and 4. Direct access to the Arabic accounts in translation.

**Austen, Ralph A. *Trans-Saharan Africa in World History*. 2010. Oxford University Press.**
The trans-Saharan trade system, its logistics, its political dependencies.
*Used in:* Section 2. The crossing infrastructure, the chain of dependencies.

### ACQUISITION (Needed)

- **Studies on the Mansa Musa hajj's specific economic impact** — detailed analysis of the twelve-year gold depression in the Egyptian and Levantine markets. Needed for Section 3.
- **Recent archaeological work on Niani** — physical detail of the capital site for Section 1 revisions.
- **Timbuktu manuscript studies** — specific content of manuscripts from the Musa era and immediately after, for Section 4.
- **Scholarship on Inari Kunate** — any sources that name or describe Musa's senior wife beyond the bare mention. Needed for Sections 1 and 2. If the record is genuinely thin, the chapter must handle this honestly per Principle 13.
- **Al-Sahili's architectural legacy** — detailed studies of the Djinguereber Mosque's construction techniques and the fusion of Andalusian and Sahelian styles. Needed for Section 4.

## 5. Panel Risks

### Tuchman (P-1: Narrative Craft)
**What she praised:** The opening line, the genealogy's momentum, "Musa was going to Mecca" as structural pivot, "The gold was the point" as thesis-in-eight-words.
**What she will push on in the full chapter:**
- Complication. The opening is a beautiful procession; the chapter needs turns. Every section must have stakes, not just grandeur. The Sahara crossing must include a moment of genuine danger or decision. The Cairo arrival must have tension — the protocol confrontation with the Egyptian sultan, the dawning realization of economic consequences.
- Inari Kunate. She called the opening's treatment of her "negligence." The full chapter must give Inari Kunate a real presence — a scene, a decision, a human moment. If she is going to be named, she must be a person.
- The ending. She will watch for a landing that summarizes instead of arriving. The chapter must close on a specific moment, not on a paragraph of significance.
- Forward narrative momentum. She will check that each section pulls the reader into the next, not just adds more information. Build, do not survey.

### Ibn Khaldun (P-2: Historical Systems)
**What he praised:** The covenant logic, the causal connection between Kurukan Fuga and gold spending, the economic consequence stated in one sentence.
**What he will push on in the full chapter:**
- The self-consuming logic. This is his central demand. The covenant that requires spectacular redistribution is the same mechanism that destabilizes the empire's material base. The twelve-year gold depression must be analyzed as structural consequence — the system undermining itself by performing exactly as designed. This cannot be an aside; it must be a through-line.
- Origins of the covenant. Why did the Manden develop this specific legitimacy mechanism? What material conditions — the gold-salt exchange, the concentration of portable wealth, the need for allied clans to prevent any single lineage from monopolizing power — produced a system where legitimacy required giving everything away? The chapter must trace the mechanism, not just assert it.
- The route as system. The Sahara crossing must be analyzed as infrastructure, not described as scenery. The chain of dependencies — water, fodder, political permissions — is the system made visible.
- Why THIS mansa, at THIS moment. Musa's accession was contingent — Abu Bakr II disappeared. What political pressures made a hajj of this scale necessary for a mansa whose claim to power was accidental?

### Achebe (P-3: Cultural Voice)
**What he praised:** The jeli opening first, the covenant rendered as operating law, "The gold was the point" as inside logic.
**What he will push on in the full chapter:**
- Whose witnesses. This is his fundamental question. Every sentence that invokes Arab chroniclers or European mapmakers must be framed from inside the Mande world. Musa's world, not theirs. Al-Umari's informants can appear as characters in Cairo — people Musa encountered — but they cannot narrate. The Catalan Atlas must not appear as proof of Musa's significance.
- The jon. The opening evaded the social category of the gold-bearing men. The full chapter must name the jon within the Kurukan Fuga's framework — honestly, without modern apologetics and without evasion. The Mande world had its own understanding of these social categories, and the chapter must inhabit that understanding.
- Textbook glosses. Any sentence that pauses to explain something the culture would take for granted ("the hajj is the fifth pillar," "the dundun speaks in tonal language") must be removed. Trust the reader; trust the world.
- The intellectual tradition. When Timbuktu's scholarship appears, it must be presented as scholarship — not as a surprising African achievement, not as evidence that Africa too had learning. The scholars at Sankore were doing what scholars do. Let them do it without commentary.

### Davis (P-4: Anthropological Wonder)
**What he praised:** The jeli tradition enacted rather than explained, the Kurukan Fuga as genuine governance, the gold framed through Mande philosophy.
**What he will push on in the full chapter:**
- The intellectual tradition at full depth. Timbuktu's scholarly culture must be treated with the same depth and specificity that a chapter on medieval Paris or Baghdad would give their universities. What was studied? What was debated? What texts were produced? What was the relationship between the Sankore scholars and the broader Islamic intellectual world? This is not a paragraph; it is a section's worth of genuine engagement.
- Written vs. oral authority. The chapter must not accidentally reinstate the hierarchy that privileges written records over oral tradition. The jelilu's memory is a knowledge system — institutional, hereditary, authoritative. It must be treated as such throughout, not just in the opening and closing.
- Al-Sahili's work as fusion, not import. The architecture Musa brought back was not a correction of a deficit. It was an addition to an existing building tradition. The Sahelian mud-brick tradition was already sophisticated. Al-Sahili brought new techniques that merged with what existed. The chapter must frame this as encounter between traditions, not as modernization.
- The manuscript culture. The private libraries of Timbuktu held hundreds of thousands of manuscripts. These are products of a genuine West African intellectual tradition. The chapter should give the reader a sense of what was in them — not just that they existed.

### Kapuscinski (P-5: Sensory Immersion)
**What he praised:** "Dust and shea butter and woodsmoke," the harmattan haze, the sagging saddlebags, the drums building, the closing dust image.
**What he will push on in the full chapter:**
- Sensory ground in every section. No section can float. Each must have temperature, weight, texture, sound, smell. The risk is highest in expository passages — trade analysis, political structure, scholarly traditions. These passages must be anchored in physical detail.
- The Sahara crossing must be felt. Heat, sand, the weight of water, the silence between dunes, stars at night, cracked skin, the exhaustion of walking. This is not atmosphere; this is the body moving through a lethal environment.
- Cairo must be smelled. The souk, the crowd, the spices, the Nile. When Musa gives gold, the reader must see the physical act — hands, metal, weight changing hands.
- Timbuktu must be seen and touched. Mud-brick under hands. Wet plaster. Ink on manuscript pages. The courtyard of Sankore. The call to prayer from the minaret. The Niger seen from the mosque.
- Embodied characters. Every named person must have a body — a posture, a gesture, a physical relationship to the space they occupy. No name-and-title figures.

## 6. Sensory Palette

### Niani (Section 1)
- **Sight:** Morning light burning through harmattan haze. The mud-brick gate. Musa in white. Indigo of Inari Kunate's wrapping cloth. The column stretching beyond sight. Gold staffs catching light. Dust rising ankle-high.
- **Sound:** Bala Faseke's kora — low notes. His voice climbing. The dundun's tonal speech. The djembe setting the body moving. The crowd's response to the praise song.
- **Smell:** Dust. Shea butter. Woodsmoke. The Niger in the morning.
- **Touch:** Dry season air on skin. The weight of the gold staffs — six mithqals each. Sagging saddlebags.
- **Taste:** (Implied) Dry air, dust on the tongue.

### The Sahara Crossing (Section 2)
- **Sight:** Baobabs thinning. Grass shortening. Sand horizon. Stars at night — thick, close, the guides reading them. The caravan as a line across the dunes. Inari Kunate's column: color of fabric against sand.
- **Sound:** Silence between the dunes. Wind across sand. Camel footfalls — soft, rhythmic. The creak of loaded saddlebags. Voices carrying in dry air.
- **Smell:** Dry heat. Camel sweat. Leather. The absence of woodsmoke — the desert has no trees.
- **Touch:** Sand in everything — clothes, hair, between teeth. Cracked skin. The heat on the crown of the head. The weight of a waterskin — full, then lighter, then nearly empty. Cool of desert night after the heat of the day.
- **Taste:** Water rationed. Dust.

### Cairo (Section 3)
- **Sight:** The city's scale — minarets, the souk's narrow alleys, the Nile. Musa's caravan entering — the gold visible, the crowd gathering. Gold changing hands. The merchants' faces. The court's reception hall.
- **Sound:** The souk — vendors, languages overlapping, Arabic and Mande. The call to prayer. The murmur of a crowd watching something unprecedented.
- **Smell:** Spices in the souk. The Nile. Incense in the court. Dust of a different kind — limestone, not laterite.
- **Touch:** Gold coins and gold dust in the hand — the physical act of giving. The heat of Cairo, different from the Sahel — humid near the Nile. Cloth of Egyptian court dress vs. Musa's white pilgrim garment.

### Timbuktu (Section 4)
- **Sight:** The Djinguereber Mosque rising — mud-brick walls, the toron timber armatures protruding, the minaret taking shape. Al-Sahili directing construction. The Sankore courtyard — students sitting, manuscripts open. The Niger bend visible from the mosque.
- **Sound:** Construction — the thud of mud-brick being placed, voices of builders. The scratch of reed pen on paper. Students reciting. The call to prayer from the new minaret.
- **Smell:** Wet plaster. Fresh mud-brick drying in the sun. Ink — the particular smell of Arabic manuscript ink. The Niger breeze.
- **Touch:** Mud-brick under the hands — rough, warm from the sun. The weight of a manuscript. Plaster setting. The texture of locally made paper.

### The Long Memory (Section 5)
- **Sight:** A jeli performing — the kora, the posture, the audience. Or: the mosques still standing. The Sankore courtyard centuries later. The Niger, unchanged.
- **Sound:** The kora. The voice. The names being sung — the same names, Sundiata to Musa, carried forward.
- **Touch/Feel:** The continuity itself as something felt — the weight of seven hundred years of unbroken transmission. The dust settling. Stillness after movement.

## 7. Voice Notes

### The Griot Cadence in Prose
The opening established the voice: accumulative, rhythmic, confident. Repetition as emphasis, not redundancy. Short declarative sentences landing with weight after longer passages of building rhythm. The prose echoes the jeli's method — name after name, clause after clause, building toward a single plain statement that carries the force of everything before it.

**Examples from the opening that define the register:**
- "He sang Sundiata, the Lion, the hungering lion, who crawled and then walked and then ran and then conquered." (Accumulative rhythm, each clause advancing)
- "Musa was going to Mecca." (Short declarative after density — the exhale)
- "So the gold was not extravagance. The gold was the point." (Thesis as plain speech)
- "North through the savanna... North to Walata... North across sand..." (Anaphora as forward movement)

### Maintaining Voice Across 5,000 Words
The hardest part. The opening sustains the voice for 800 words because every sentence is scene. The full chapter must sustain it for 5,000 words that include exposition, analysis, and transition. The voice is most at risk in these passages:

**Trade route analysis (Section 2).** The temptation is to shift into textbook mode: "The trans-Saharan trade network connected..." Instead: stay in the caravan. The trade route is what Inari Kunate is walking through. Let the infrastructure reveal itself through the journey, not through explanation.

**Economic consequence (Section 3).** The gold market crash demands analysis. The temptation is to step outside and explain macroeconomics. Instead: show it through characters in the scene. A Cairo merchant whose gold is worth less today than yesterday. The exchange rates shifting in the souk. The system-level analysis must emerge from the scene, not replace it.

**Scholarly tradition (Section 4).** The description of Timbuktu's intellectual culture risks sounding like a museum placard: "Sankore was one of the great centers of Islamic learning..." Instead: stay with al-Sahili at the building site, stay with the scholars unpacking their books, stay with a student copying a text. Let the depth of the tradition reveal itself through specific acts of scholarship.

**The landing (Section 5).** The temptation is to turn philosophical or rhetorical — to make grand statements about the meaning of memory. Instead: stay with the jeli. The names are being sung. That is the meaning. The prose should be at its plainest and shortest in the final paragraph. When the weight is heaviest, the language is simplest.

### Voice Constraints
- No sentences that begin with "It is worth noting that..." or "What is remarkable is..." or "This illustrates..."
- No modern observer ("To a modern reader..." / "From our perspective today...")
- No comparative framing ("Unlike European kingdoms..." / "As sophisticated as any medieval...")
- No surprise at sophistication ("Remarkably..." / "Strikingly...")
- No textbook transitions ("We now turn to..." / "Another important aspect...")
- Em-dashes: no more than 8 in the full chapter
- The jeli's voice is the chapter's structural spine. When in doubt about register, ask: would a jeli tell it this way? Not in literal oral performance, but in the confidence, the rhythm, the assumption that the listener already belongs to this world.

## Open Questions

1. **Inari Kunate's historical record.** How much do the sources actually say about her? The opening names her; the OVERVIEW does not mention her. If the record is thin, Section 2 must acknowledge this honestly while still giving her narrative presence. Flag for ACQUISITION.

2. **The number problem.** Al-Umari's sources give wildly different estimates of the caravan's size. The chapter cannot use "the sources disagree" as its own voice (Achebe's critique). How to handle: let the narrative voice state a number with confidence, or describe the scale through physical detail (the column stretching beyond sight, the dust that did not settle) rather than through contested figures.

3. **Musa's borrowing on the return.** Al-Umari reports that Musa borrowed gold at interest in Cairo for the return journey. This is a crucial detail for the self-consuming logic (Ibn Khaldun's demand). But the sources are not entirely clear on the terms. Handle with honest specificity: state what is reported, note where the record thins.

4. **Al-Sahili's buildings.** The attribution of the Djinguereber Mosque to al-Sahili is traditional but not uncontested in recent scholarship. Some scholars argue the existing structure dates from later renovations. The chapter should use the traditional attribution while noting, briefly and honestly, that the specific buildings standing today may not be the original structures.

5. **Ibn Battuta's reliability.** His account of Mali is valuable but culturally biased — he complained about Mande customs that conflicted with his Moroccan-Islamic expectations. If he appears in Section 5, his limitations must be visible. He is a witness, not an authority.

6. **The twelve-year gold depression.** The specific figure of twelve years appears in multiple scholarly sources but traces back ultimately to Ibn Khaldun. The chapter should treat this as reported rather than independently verified, while still using it as the basis for the structural analysis.
