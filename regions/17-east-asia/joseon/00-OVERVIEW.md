# Joseon — The Confucian Kingdom

We are Joseon — the dynasty that made Confucianism into a total civilization, more rigorous in its Confucian application than China itself. Yi Seonggye founded us in 1392 by deposing the Goryeo dynasty, and within a generation the new dynasty had replaced Buddhism — which had dominated Korean state culture for five centuries — with Neo-Confucianism as the governing ideology. We built the Sungkyunkwan (National Confucian Academy), we established the four-stage examination system (gwageo), we produced the Annals of the Joseon Dynasty (Joseon Wangjo Sillok) — a continuous official record of every reign kept in four mountain fortress archives — and we created a Confucian ritual state in which every aspect of public life was governed by the principle that correct conduct (ye, ritual propriety) was the foundation of the social order.

We gave Korea the hangul alphabet. King Sejong the Great commissioned the Korean script in 1443, with the explicit goal of giving the people of Joseon a writing system suited to the Korean language. "A wise man can acquaint himself with the letters before the morning is over," the proclamation said. Hangul is a scientific achievement: twenty-four letters, systematically derived from the shapes the mouth makes when speaking, that can represent all the sounds of Korean and can be learned in hours. We also gave Korea the world's first rain gauge (1441), the first systematic weather records, the first astronomical instruments specifically designed for Korean latitudes. Sejong's court was one of the most scientifically and culturally productive in Korean history.

## What Defined Them

**Confucianism as total civilization.** The Joseon state was organized around Confucian principles in a way that transformed every aspect of social life. The ritual texts that governed mourning periods, ancestral rites, family hierarchies, and official conduct were more strictly observed in Joseon than in Ming or Qing China. The Annals of the Joseon Dynasty — 1,893 volumes covering five centuries — is the most comprehensive record of any pre-modern government in East Asia. The examination system created a civil culture in which classical literacy was the measure of worth.

**Hangul — the scientific alphabet.** The Korean alphabet created by King Sejong's Hall of Worthies in 1443 is unique in the history of writing systems: it was designed by scholars, has a documented origin, and is systematically phonetic in a way that most scripts are not (the shape of each letter represents the position of the mouth that produces the sound). Hangul was suppressed by the yangban aristocracy, who regarded it as a "vulgar script" and preferred the prestige of classical Chinese (hanmun), but it was used for women's writing, Buddhist texts, vernacular novels, and popular literature throughout the Joseon period and became the official Korean script only in the modern era.

**Yi Sunsin and the turtle ships.** The Japanese invasions of 1592–1598 (Imjin War) were among the most catastrophic events in Korean history: Japanese armies under Toyotomi Hideyoshi occupied Seoul within three weeks of landing and devastated the peninsula over seven years of war. The Joseon navy's survival was the work of one man: Admiral Yi Sunsin, whose ironclad (or iron-plated) turtle ships (geobukseon) destroyed Japanese fleets repeatedly, cutting off the supply lines that the land army required. Yi Sunsin was court-martialed twice for not following orders he believed were strategically wrong and was right both times. He died in the final battle of the war, at Noryang Strait, 1598.

**Factional politics and the literati purges.** The Joseon political culture was organized around factional disputes among the Confucian literati — the sarim (forest scholars) versus the hungu (meritorious subjects), and later the Southerners, Northerners, Westerners, and Southerners again — that resulted in repeated purges of scholars and officials. These were not merely political disputes; they were arguments about the correct interpretation of Confucian texts, about ritual precedence, about the proper conduct of the king. The intensity of these disputes reflects the degree to which Joseon intellectuals took their philosophical commitments seriously enough to die for them.

**Sirhak — practical learning.** In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, some Joseon scholars developed sirhak — practical learning — as a response to the perceived emptiness of the Neo-Confucian philosophical debates. Sirhak scholars like Yi Ik, Jeong Yakyong (Dasan), and Park Jiwon advocated empirical investigation, agricultural improvement, institutional reform, and openness to new knowledge including Western science. Dasan spent eighteen years in exile writing works on governance, agriculture, and classical scholarship that are among the most brilliant in the Korean literary tradition.

## The Chapters

**1 — Foundation:** Yi Seonggye accepts the throne in the name of a new dynasty. He calls it Joseon — the ancient name, recalling the original Korean state. He builds the new capital at Hanyang. The Buddhist monasteries that funded the Goryeo state are being moved outside the city walls. The Confucian transformation has begun.

**2 — Sejong:** He is studying the manuscript of the new script. The Hall of Worthies has worked for years on this. He asks: can a person learn this in a single morning? The scholars say: yes. He says: then we have what we need. He announces the hangul alphabet in 1443.

**3 — The Literati Purge:** The king reads the memorial accusing the reformist scholars of disrespect. The accusations are political, but the form is ritual: the scholars violated the propriety of court conduct. He orders the investigation. Scholars will die for their interpretation of a phrase in the classical texts.

**4 — Yi Sunsin:** The admiral is reading the intelligence about the Japanese fleet movements. He has been court-martialed and tortured. He has been reinstated. The turtle ships are in the harbor. He is planning the battle of Hansando. He will win.

**5 — The Manchu Surrender:** The king is in Namhansanseong fortress. The Manchu forces have surrounded it. The cold is killing his soldiers. He descends from the fortress and kneels before the Manchu khan three times. He kowtows nine times. He is the last tributary king to submit. The annals will record this day in detail.

**6 — Dasan in Exile:** Jeong Yakyong is in his eighteenth year of exile in Gangjin. He has written five hundred books. He is writing about rice farming, about the reform of government, about the meaning of Confucian governance in a changed world. He knows he will never return to office. He writes anyway.
