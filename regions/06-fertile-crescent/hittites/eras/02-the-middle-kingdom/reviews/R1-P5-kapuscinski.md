## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Review of Hittites Era 2 Opening

**Overall:** The opening puts me in the room in the first two sentences. Damp earth, lamp oil, the wind off the plateau. I am there. But the room fades as the analytical argument develops, and by the middle paragraphs I am reading about the edict's mechanism rather than feeling it. The opening needs to hold its physical ground throughout.

**What works:**
- "The scribe pressed the reed into wet clay and wrote what the king had spoken. The room smelled of damp earth and lamp oil." This is correct. I am in the room. I can smell it. The physical act of writing is present: the reed, the clay, the pressure. This is how history happens: in a specific room, with a specific smell, under a specific light.
- "Outside, the wind off the Anatolian plateau pushed against the walls of Hattusa." The landscape is a presence, not a backdrop. The plateau wind is the world pressing in on the kingdom. Sensory detail doing analytical work.
- The litany of murders in paragraph two has a physical weight despite being narrated without sensory detail. The compression itself gives the reader a bodily sensation: the pace is relentless, the breath quickens. This is a form of embodiment achieved through rhythm rather than description.
- "No plundered temples brought back through the Lion Gate to heap before the Storm God's temple." In one phrase, the physical reality of Hittite military culture is present: the gate, the god, the heaping of plunder. This is efficient sensory grounding.

**What doesn't:**
- The middle paragraphs (the theological argument, the analysis of the treaty form turned inward) lose the room. Where is Telipinu while the scribe writes? Is he standing? Sitting? Dictating from memory or from notes? Is the court assembly present, or is this a private act? The physical reality of the edict's creation has been established and then abandoned.
- "The harvests failed. The borders shrank. Enemies pressed in." What did a failed harvest look like on the Anatolian plateau? What did a shrinking border feel like to the garrison towns? Who were the enemies, and what did their raids look like? These sentences claim consequences but do not embody them. The chapter must ground them.
- The edict's distribution is mentioned but not felt. Copies sent to the provinces: who carried them? How far? What did a provincial official do when the tablet arrived? Was it read aloud? Displayed? The physical life of the document after its creation is part of the story.

**The question I'd push on:**
Clay. The entire argument rests on clay. The material itself should be present throughout: the weight of the tablet in a scribe's hands, the smell of it drying, the sound of the stylus, the fact that it endures (unlike papyrus, unlike parchment, unlike memory). The chapter is about whether a written document can stop violence. The physicality of the document itself, its resistance to time, its literal hardness, should be woven into the argument at every stage. The clay is not a metaphor. It is the argument made material.
