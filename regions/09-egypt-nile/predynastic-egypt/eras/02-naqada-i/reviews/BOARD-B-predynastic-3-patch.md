# BOARD — Diana Craig Patch

**Chapter:** Naqada I — The Amratian
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Material Culture Assessment

The chapter treats its objects well. This is a chapter about making, and the making is rendered with respect for the skill involved. The pottery section is the strongest: the coiling technique, the wall-thinning, the lime-slip painting, the firing process are all described at the level of craft knowledge. The potter is a person with hands, not an abstract cultural agent.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

1. **Pottery as craft tradition:** The description of the thinning process ("She worked from both sides, pressing with a smooth stone on the outside and her fingers on the inside") is exactly the kind of method-level rendering that takes the pottery seriously as an intellectual and physical achievement.

2. **The palette as personal object:** "The palette was one of the most personal objects a person owned. You ground your paint on it. You were buried with it." And later: "The dead carried their history of use into the west." These passages treat the palette with the weight it deserves. The concavity-as-record-of-use is a beautiful and accurate detail.

3. **The upraised-arms figure:** Treated with appropriate ambiguity: "a figure with upraised arms that might be a woman, might be a goddess, might be both." This is correct. The scholarly debate on this figure's identity is unresolved, and the chapter does not pretend otherwise.

4. **Copper smelting at method level:** The transformation from malachite to copper is described step by step, with the physical experience of the smelter present throughout. This is not "they had metallurgy." This is a person blowing on a fire until his face burns.

## What Could Be Stronger

1. **Ivory carving:** The chapter mentions ivory combs but does not engage with the ivory-carving tradition at the same depth as pottery or copper. Naqada I ivory combs and small figures are among the period's most distinctive objects. Even a sentence or two about the carved hippo-form combs or the small figurines would acknowledge this tradition.

2. **Figurines:** The Naqada I period produced distinctive clay and ivory figurines, including female figures that may relate to the upraised-arms motif on the pottery. The chapter does not mention them. This is a gap in the material-culture coverage.

## Verdict: PASS. The pottery and palette descriptions are excellent. The copper smelting is rendered with genuine skill. The ivory and figurine traditions could receive brief attention in a final revision.
