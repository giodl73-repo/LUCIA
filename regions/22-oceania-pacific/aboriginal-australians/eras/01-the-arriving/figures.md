# Figures — When the Ancestors Walked

## Maku, Songline custodian

Maku carried a section of a Tjukurpa Songline through the Western Desert. This means he held, in memory and in his body, the song-verses that mapped a stretch of country: the waterholes, the sacred sites, the law embedded at each place where the Ancestors had acted in the Dreaming. He had received this custodianship through his kinship line, as his father had before him, and the obligation that came with it was not optional. To hold a Songline is to maintain a piece of the world's operating system. If the song is not sung, if the country is not walked, the connection between the land and the law weakens.

Maku is a composite figure. He is drawn from multiple published accounts of Songline custodianship and walking practice as documented by researchers working with the authorization and collaboration of Aboriginal communities. His walking, his attention to tracks, his teaching of younger men by walking with them, his reading of the landscape as he moves through it, are all drawn from ethnographic and collaborative sources. He is not a single documented individual. The chapter acknowledges this honestly: the practice of walking Songlines has been described by many custodians and witnessed by many researchers, and Maku's walk is assembled from these accounts to render the practice faithfully without reproducing any specific custodian's sacred knowledge.

What makes Maku's knowledge a system rather than a skill is its integration. As he walks, he is simultaneously navigating (the song tells him where water and food are), performing law (the walking re-activates the Dreaming at each site), maintaining ecology (his knowledge of country informs the burning regime), and teaching (the younger men behind him are learning all of this by being present). He does not separate these functions. They are one practice.


## Ngali, fire-keeper

Ngali burned country with the precision of someone who had learned the land's response to fire across a lifetime of observation and practice. Her burning was not destruction. It was cultivation: the careful application of fire to specific patches of country in specific seasons to produce specific ecological outcomes. The fresh growth that followed a cool-season burn attracted game. The fire-dependent plants that germinated only after burning provided food. The mosaic of burned and unburned patches created the diversity of habitat that sustained the full range of species the community depended on.

Ngali is a composite figure. She is drawn from Bill Gammage's research in *The Biggest Estate on Earth* and from the work of Marcia Langton and other Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal scholars who have documented fire management practices. Women's burning is particularly underrepresented in the historical and ethnographic record, though Aboriginal women have always been active fire managers with specific knowledge of burning regimes for specific purposes. Ngali's practice represents the gendered depth of this knowledge system.

The fire knowledge Ngali carries is not separate from the Songline knowledge Maku carries. The burning regime for a stretch of country is part of the custodian's obligation to that country. When to burn, where to burn, and in what sequence are determined by ecological observation refined across generations, by the seasonal calendar encoded in the stars, and by the Dreaming law that specifies how country must be maintained. Fire is not a tool. It is a responsibility.


## Jirri, star reader

Jirri lay on his back on warm sand and learned the sky from his uncle. The astronomical knowledge being transmitted to him was not casual stargazing. It was a precision system that linked celestial events to terrestrial ones: the appearance of the Emu constellation with the emu breeding cycle, the disappearance and reappearance of the Pleiades with the timing of ceremonies and food availability, the heliacal rising of specific stars with the swarming of moths and the running of fish.

Jirri is a composite figure. He is drawn from the research of Duane Hamacher, Ray Norris, and Aboriginal astronomers and knowledge-holders who have collaborated in documenting Aboriginal astronomical systems at scholarly depth. The specific correspondences described in the chapter (Emu and emu eggs, Pleiades and seasonal ceremonies) are attested across multiple nations, though the specific stories and the specific timing vary by nation and by location.

What makes Jirri's learning significant is the medium of storage. The astronomical knowledge is encoded in Dreaming stories. The narrative of the Seven Sisters fleeing across the sky encodes the Pleiades' annual cycle. The Emu in the Sky encodes the breeding calendar. A child who learns the stories learns the astronomy, and the stories are carried in the same Songline network that carries the geographical, legal, and ecological knowledge. The sky and the ground are held in the same system.


## The Ancestors (Tjukurpa beings)

The Ancestors are not human figures. They are the beings of the Dreaming who walked the country when nothing existed but potential and sang it into form. The Honey Ant Ancestor, the Rain Ancestor, the Two Boys, the Seven Sisters, the Rainbow Serpent (by various names in various nations), the Wandjina of the Kimberley. They are not past-tense characters in a mythology. They are present-tense presences in the land.

Every waterhole, every ridge, every rock outcrop in a custodian's country holds the act of an Ancestor. The Ancestors are in the land the way the grain is in the wood. To remove the Ancestors from the land is not to remove a story from a place. It is to remove the meaning from the structure. The ceremonies that re-enact the Ancestors' acts are not commemorations. They are acts of maintenance. The world requires their repetition.

The chapter does not narrate the Ancestors' stories in full, because the stories are held by custodians under protocols that govern who may tell them, when, and to whom. What the chapter shows is the architectural role the Ancestors play in the knowledge system: they are the reason the Songlines exist, the reason the law exists, the reason the country must be walked and sung and burned and maintained. They are the foundation. Everything else is built on them.
