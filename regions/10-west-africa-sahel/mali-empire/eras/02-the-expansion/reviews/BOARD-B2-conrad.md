# B-2 — Conrad — Board Review of "When the Empire Reached the Sea"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Mande oral traditions; the Sundiata epic; the jeli's social role and methods of historical preservation.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The jeli's role as institutional carrier of the covenant is correctly rendered. The jelilu do not simply "remember" — they carry, which implies obligation, responsibility, and the sacred nature of the knowledge they maintain.
- The variations in Sundiata's death account ("some say the river took him, some say an arrow at a festival, some say he simply finished") are consistent with the oral tradition's multiplicity. The chapter correctly notes that "the variations do not trouble the tradition."
- The Kouyate and Diabate lineages are correctly placed (Kouyate as the premier jeli clan of the Keita rulers, Diabate as keepers of the septennial ceremony at Kangaba).
- The distinction between "he did not come back" and "he died" is an authentic feature of the oral tradition's epistemological precision. The jelilu do carry this distinction, and the chapter's treatment of it is the most sophisticated engagement with oral tradition's knowledge-keeping protocols I have seen in the project.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The jeli tradition is treated throughout as an authoritative knowledge system, not as colorful storytelling. This is correct.
- The oral and written sources are held in genuine parity. Al-Umari confirms what the jelilu carry, not the other way around. This is the right orientation.
- The genealogical listing in the landing ("Sundiata, the Lion, who crawled and then walked and then conquered. Wali, who honored the covenant...") echoes the actual structure of jeli performance — the succession as a chain of names with capsule characterizations. This feels authentic.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The treatment of Khalifa: the jelilu carry the act without explanation, and the chapter mirrors this precision. The silence around motive IS the tradition's judgment.
- Sakura's inclusion in the genealogy "with the same voice and the same cadence" is how the tradition handles him. He is not set apart. He is incorporated.
- The landing's use of the seven-year ceremony at Kangaba is specific and correct. This ceremony is documented and ongoing.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter uses "kora" in the opening when describing the jeli's instrument. The kora as we know it may postdate this period — it is typically associated with the Manding tradition from the 18th century or later in its current form. The balafon would be more period-appropriate for a jeli in the 13th-14th century. The kora appears in Era 3's opening as well (where I would raise the same concern), but for historical accuracy, the instrument should be the balafon or simply "the jeli's instrument."
- One small note: the chapter says "the clans in their places." The Mande social structure is organized by lineages (jamuw) as much as by clans. "The lineages in their places" would be more precise, though "clans" is not incorrect in colloquial scholarly usage.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS — The kora/balafon correction is the primary recommendation. The oral tradition is handled with exceptional care.
