# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel-1 Review: Opening, Philippe Auguste et la Grande Expansion

**Round:** 1
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (P-3)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The voice is right. This is the first thing to say, and it matters more than the rest. The opening does not explain France to an outsider. It does not pause to translate "rex Christianissimus" for a reader who might not know Latin. It does not step back to tell us what feudal obligation was in academic terms. It places the reader inside a world where these things are simply true, the way they were true to the people who lived in them.

The Château Gaillard passage is where the voice is most secure. "The story was told with relish at the French court." That phrase establishes exactly who is telling this story: the French court, not the modern historian. The pleasure in the telling — God not having been given the assignment — belongs to the culture being described. This is the v1.2 honesty technique functioning at its highest level: the humor is theirs.

I notice the opening navigates the Albigensian Crusade without touching it, which is prudent for an opening. But the chapter will need to address it without stepping outside the Capetian worldview to judge it. The Cathars of Languedoc did not share the Capetian framework, and their absorption into the French crown was accomplished through what they would have experienced as violent suppression. The chapter must hold both truths without resolving them from the outside. This is the hardest test the chapter will face: can it remain inside the Capetian worldview while also giving voice to those whom the Capetian expansion consumed?

**The question I'd push on:** The opening treats Philip's feudal-legal exploitation of John's failures as natural — as the obvious mechanism for expansion. This is correct; it WAS natural from within the Capetian frame. But the chapter will need to give at least some texture to what was lost in this expansion: the Norman barons who had held their lands for five generations, the Angevin institutional culture that Philip had to absorb or replace. The Capetian story is the story told by the winners. The chapter can tell it from inside without pretending the losers had no story. The technique here is not to switch voices but to let the winners' story include the weight of what was taken. "Philip received the keys to Rouen" is the French court's way of telling it. The chapter can hold that telling while letting the reader feel what receiving keys from a city means to the people who had kept them.

The opening earns the chapter. The voice is fully established and fully French.
