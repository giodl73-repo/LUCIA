# Figures — The Rise of Sargon

## Sargon (Sharru-kin, c. 2334-2279 BCE)

The name argues his case. Sharru-kin: the king is legitimate. A man who needs to name himself "legitimate" is a man whose legitimacy is in question, and the question is the most interesting thing about him. He appears in no royal genealogy of Kish or any Sumerian city. The birth legend gives him a priestess mother and an unknown father and a river journey in a basket of rushes. The legend was still being copied in Nineveh a thousand years after the Akkadian empire fell, because the story of a man who came from nothing and held everything was the story every subsequent dynasty needed: the proof that the gods could choose anyone, that power was not a bloodline but a bond.

What we know with confidence: he served as cupbearer to Ur-Zababa of Kish. He built a city called Akkade and used it as the capital of an empire that eventually encompassed the Sumerian city-states, the northern plain, and (by his claim) territories as far as the Cedar Forest and the Silver Mountain. He took the title "King of the Four Quarters." He ruled for approximately fifty-five years. His empire survived him but did not outlast his grandsons.

What we do not know: his actual origins. The birth legend is a literary text, attested in copies made centuries after his death. Whether a historical birth event underlies it is unknowable. We do not know what he looked like. The famous "Mask of Sargon" from Nineveh may represent him or may not; its identification is conventional, not established. We do not know where Akkade was. We do not know where he is buried.

**Honest limits:** Nearly everything we "know" about Sargon comes through later literary tradition and through copies of royal inscriptions made centuries after his reign. The historical Sargon is a reconstruction from fragments. The legendary Sargon is vivid, complete, and may bear little resemblance to the man who actually poured wine for Ur-Zababa. The chapter works with both, distinguishing them where possible and acknowledging the gap where distinction fails.

## Ishtar (Akkadian name for Sumerian Inanna)

Ishtar is not a figure in the biographical sense. She does not appear in the chapter as a character with dialogue or action. She appears as a force: the divine presence whose love legitimates Sargon's authority and whose withdrawal will eventually delegitimate his successors. In the Akkadian theological framework, Ishtar's love for the king is a specific, personal bond that functions as the foundation of political power. This is not a metaphor. The royal inscriptions present it as a fact: Ishtar loved Sargon, and therefore Sargon ruled.

The transition from Sumerian Inanna to Akkadian Ishtar is itself significant. The goddess who stole the me from Enki and brought them to Unug now grants her favor to a king who speaks a different language and worships her under a different name. The divine figure is larger than the linguistic community that named her. She crosses the language boundary in a way that the human political systems did not, and her crossing enabled the bilingual synthesis: the goddess herself was bilingual.

**Honest limits:** Ishtar's "appearance" to Sargon is a claim of the literary and inscriptional tradition, not an event that historical method can evaluate. The chapter treats the claim as the culture's own testimony about its political theology. It does not assert or deny the experience.

## Enheduanna (en-priestess of Nanna at Ur, fl. c. 2285-2250 BCE)

Sargon's daughter, appointed en-priestess of the moon god Nanna at Ur. The appointment was political: the conqueror's daughter installed in the conquered south's most prestigious religious office. But Enheduanna was a poet. She composed hymns in Sumerian, including "The Exaltation of Inanna" (nin-me-shar-ra), a text of extraordinary theological ambition that elevates Inanna/Ishtar above all other deities. Her name is the earliest author's name in history that can be attached to surviving literary works.

Enheduanna embodies the bilingual synthesis in a single person: Akkadian by birth, Sumerian by office and literary language, serving a Sumerian god in a Sumerian city while advancing an Akkadian theological agenda (the supremacy of Ishtar). Her presence in the chapter's bilingual synthesis section is grounded in the historical record. Her disc (a calcite disc bearing her image and name, found at Ur by Leonard Woolley) confirms her historicity. Her hymns survive in multiple copies from the Old Babylonian period and later.

**Honest limits:** Enheduanna's dates place her at the end of Sargon's reign or early in the reign of his successors. Her literary career may belong more properly to the era of Naram-Sin than to Sargon's own era. The chapter places her in the context of the bilingual synthesis that Sargon's conquest made possible, while acknowledging that her major works may postdate Sargon's death.

## Lugalzagesi (en of Umma, lugal of Unug, fl. c. 2340-2316 BCE)

The last of the old system. Lugalzagesi was a Sumerian city-ruler who achieved what many had attempted: the confederation of the major Sumerian cities under a single authority. His vase inscription from Nippur claims that Enlil granted him dominion from the Lower Sea to the Upper Sea. He was en of Umma by origin and took the title lugal of Unug, combining religious authority in one city with political authority in another.

His defeat by Sargon is the hinge event of the era. Sargon brought him in a neck stock to the gate of Enlil's temple at Nippur. The display was political theater with theological content: the defeated king presented before the god who grants kingship, demonstrating that Enlil's favor had passed from one man to another. After this, Lugalzagesi disappears from the record.

**Honest limits:** We know Lugalzagesi primarily through his own inscriptions and Sargon's inscriptions about his defeat. He is visible from two angles, both politically motivated. The confederator's own account of his achievement and the conqueror's account of his defeat. The historical figure between these accounts is largely inaccessible.

## Ur-Zababa (king of Kish, fl. c. 2340s BCE)

The king whose cupbearer replaced him. Ur-Zababa appears in the literary text "Sargon and Ur-Zababa" (known from Old Babylonian copies) as a king who dreamed of his own replacement and tried to prevent it. In the literary tradition, he is the foil: the man who held legitimate power and lost it to the man Ishtar chose. Whether the literary tradition preserves any historical memory of the actual relationship between Ur-Zababa and Sargon is uncertain.

**Honest limits:** Ur-Zababa is primarily a literary figure. His historicity is probable (he appears in the King List) but his character and actions are accessible only through texts composed centuries after the events they describe.

## The Governor (composite figure)

The chapter includes scenes depicting an Akkadian-appointed governor (ensi) administering a conquered Sumerian city. This figure is not a specific historical individual. He is a composite based on what the administrative and archaeological record tells us about the Akkadian governor system: seal impressions showing Akkadian-named officials in Sumerian cities, administrative texts showing the coexistence of Akkadian and Sumerian scribal practices, and the archaeological evidence of administrative continuity in conquered cities. The governor's experience of sitting in an inherited audience room, working with Sumerian scribes, and maintaining the temple system while reporting to an Akkadian king is reconstructed from the structural realities of the empire, not from any single individual's documented experience.

## The Scribe (composite figure)

The chapter references scribes of the bilingual tradition without naming specific individuals. The bilingual scribal system is one of the best-documented aspects of Akkadian-period culture, attested through thousands of administrative texts, school tablets, and literary fragments. Individual scribes from this period are known by name from colophons and seal impressions, but the chapter uses a generalized figure to represent the practice of bilingual education and the experience of the Sumerian scribal tradition under Akkadian political authority. The scribe's muscle memory, the physical act of pressing the same signs for different languages, is reconstructed from the material evidence of the tablets themselves.
