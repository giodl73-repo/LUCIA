# The Founding — Taizong and the Zhengguan Covenant

Wei Zheng had written the memorial in a single night, and in the morning he carried it to the Hanyuan Hall.

The hall stood on the southern edge of the Daming Palace, its roof of gray-green tile rising above the northern wall of Chang'an so that officials approaching from the outer city saw it long before they reached the steps. One hundred and eight steps, in three flights, each flight corresponding to a gate an official passed through as he entered the presence of the Son of Heaven. Wei Zheng climbed them slowly. He was not young. He had served the previous dynasty, and before that a rebel, and before that a Sui prince, and his face carried the creases of a man who had kept his convictions through three regime changes without ever learning to keep them quiet.

The memorial in his sleeve ran to two thousand characters. It was, in its essence, a list of the ways the emperor had lately fallen short of the standards the emperor himself had set in the first years of his reign. The hunts had grown too long. The palace construction had grown too costly. A particular court favorite had been permitted a latitude that the Grand Secretariat had not approved. None of these things were catastrophic. That was part of Wei Zheng's argument. The ruler who loses his dynasty does not lose it by a single great error. He loses it by the gradual erosion of the habits that made him worth obeying.

Li Shimin, who had taken the reign name Taizong, sat on the dais at the far end of the hall. He was in his early thirties. He had come to the throne seven years earlier by a path court historians would later be instructed to record truthfully: at the Xuanwu Gate he had killed his elder brother the crown prince and his younger brother the prince of Qi, and had compelled his father the founding emperor to abdicate. He had then set about building a government whose legitimacy could not rest on the succession that had brought him to it. It had to rest on something else. Taizong had decided it would rest on the quality of his governance, measured by his willingness to be corrected.

This was the covenant of the Zhengguan reign. A ruler who silenced remonstrance was a ruler who had already begun to fail. A ruler who welcomed remonstrance, who rewarded it, who set up offices whose explicit function was to tell him when he was wrong, was a ruler whose dynasty could last. Taizong had read the histories. He knew how the Sui had ended, in the space of thirty-seven years, from founding to collapse, because the second Sui emperor had preferred flattery. He had no intention of ending the same way.

Wei Zheng reached the top of the steps. The attendants announced him. He entered the hall and performed the three kneelings and the nine prostrations, and when he rose he drew the memorial from his sleeve and presented it with both hands.

The emperor read it.

The court historian, positioned behind a lattice screen to the left of the dais, wrote down what happened next without embellishment, because the historian's office had also been restructured in the Zhengguan reign so the emperor could not read what was written about him in his own lifetime. What the historian recorded was this: the emperor read the memorial through once. He read it a second time. He set it on the low table beside the dais. He was silent for the length of ten breaths. Then he said, aloud, that the minister was correct, that the hunts had been too long, that the construction had been too costly, that the favorite would be reassigned. The memorial would be entered in the archives and a copy circulated to the Grand Secretariat. If Wei Zheng had not written it, the emperor would have had to ask why.

Wei Zheng prostrated himself again and withdrew.

The hall emptied by stages. The sun moved across the tile floor. In the archive that afternoon, two clerks copied the memorial onto silk for the permanent record, and a third copied it onto paper for distribution to the Secretariat, and somewhere in the outer court an official who disliked Wei Zheng for reasons of faction received the paper copy and understood that the political weather of the reign had been confirmed once more. You could speak. The emperor would listen. The dynasty held together by this.

Outside, in the city being rebuilt below the palace, the markets opened at the second drum. The examination candidates for the year's keju were traveling from the provinces, aiming for a civil service Taizong had decided would be staffed by talent rather than birth. The Turkic tribes on the northwestern frontier were sending tribute. The grain was coming up the canal from the south. A dynasty was being built, day by day, on the principle that a ruler was only as strong as the counsel he permitted to reach him.

This is the story of that covenant, and of the long road by which the Tang would keep it, bend it, and eventually break it.
