## From: hypothesis

**CLAIM**: LUCIA's Innovation Engine, using a two-trigger rule (2-instance exact-match plus 3-instance thematic-cluster), produces bounded and calibratable trigger latency — median chapters-between-first-observation-and-promotion falls between 2 and 50 inclusive — when applied at a corpus scale of 115+ chapters.

**FALSIFICATION**: Measured median latency < 2 chapters (over-triggered on noise) OR > 50 chapters (under-triggered, pattern starvation) OR bimodal distribution with tail ratio > 5×.

**CONFIDENCE**: 70. The engine has already fired 30+ promotions across 7 rubric versions without obvious pathology. The cluster rule (v1.5) was added specifically to address a pattern-starvation bottleneck (46 innovations waiting for exact duplicates) — suggesting the original 2-instance rule was measurably too slow on cluster-shaped patterns.

**HYPOTHESIS CLASS**: Empirical.

### Prior evidence

1. **Supports**: Rubric evolved v1.0 → v1.7 across 115 chapters over 5 days of active production. That is a steady promotion rate without either stagnation or runaway amendment.
2. **Weakens**: Sample is one project, one rubric system, one AI-assisted production pipeline. Innovation logging timestamps are session-granular (log-date ≈ first-observation-date but not always identical).
3. **Unknown pre-pipeline**: Does the 3-cluster rule systematically shorten latency vs. 2-instance? Does the post-cluster-rule regime (v1.5+) differ measurably from pre-cluster-rule (v1.0–v1.4)?

### Falsification tests

- **TEST 1** — Latency distribution analysis. For each adopted amendment, compute chapters-between-first-observation-and-promotion. Falsifies if median < 2 or > 50, or bimodal with 5× tail ratio. Feasibility HIGH — INNOVATIONS.md has Source, Date, and Status (with version) fields for 98 adopted entries.
- **TEST 2** — Trigger-pathway stratification. Classify each promotion as 2-instance vs. 3-cluster. Compute latency per pathway. Falsifies if pathways show opposite calibration problems (one too lax, one too strict) that cannot collapse to a single calibrated rule.

### Null fallback

If distribution is bimodal with long tails, reframe from "the engine is calibrated" to "the engine requires dimension-specific calibration — voice registers, progression engines, and evidence techniques exhibit different latency regimes." Paper becomes a diagnosis, not a validation.

**Weaker alternative survives**: "The two-trigger rule produces a bounded latency distribution with identifiable pathway structure" (whether or not 2–50 is the right range).

## From: competitors

### Primary competitor: Inertia

**STATUS QUO**: Static quality rubrics defined once at project start and applied uniformly. Alternative: fully open-ended quality judgment (no rubric). The LUCIA-style evolving rubric is the middle path.

**SWITCHING COST**: MEDIUM. Adopting the engine requires: (1) infrastructure for logging innovations, (2) commitment to cluster-analysis at session boundaries, (3) explicit amendment documentation. Can be adopted incrementally.

**WORKAROUND QUALITY**: LOW. Static rubrics either calcify (becoming outdated as the project discovers new dimensions) or drift (silent amendment without version tracking). Both failure modes are common in AI-assisted creative pipelines.

**INERTIA VERDICT**: Genuine demand for change. Static rubrics do not handle emergent typology well. The Innovation Engine is specifically designed for the failure mode static rubrics exhibit.

### Named competitors

**COMPETITOR 1** — Static ML benchmarks (e.g., HELM, BIG-bench, creative-writing benchmarks).
  Approach: Define quality dimensions once, score models against fixed rubric.
  Feature overlap: LOW — these evaluate models, not evolve quality standards.
  Key strength: Reproducibility across runs and teams.
  Key weakness: Cannot detect dimensions the rubric was not designed to see.
  Threat level: LOW (orthogonal problem).

**COMPETITOR 2** — RLHF preference collection (OpenAI/Anthropic model training pipelines).
  Approach: Collect human preferences, train reward model, refine iteratively.
  Feature overlap: MEDIUM — both are discovery-driven, both update over time.
  Key strength: Scalable to billions of judgments.
  Key weakness: The typology is implicit in the reward model, never externalized or inspected.
  Threat level: MEDIUM — a reviewer may argue the LUCIA engine is a hand-crafted version of what RLHF does automatically.

**COMPETITOR 3** — Qualitative coding / grounded theory (social science methodology).
  Approach: Open coding → axial coding → selective coding; typology emerges through researcher-reflective comparison.
  Feature overlap: HIGH — the Innovation Engine's cluster rule is essentially axial coding at scale.
  Key strength: Mature theoretical foundation (Strauss & Corbin, Charmaz); established in qualitative research methodology.
  Key weakness: Human-labor-intensive, not designed for AI-production speed, no forward-only amendment policy.
  Threat level: MEDIUM — reviewer may ask for explicit positioning against grounded theory.

**COMPETITOR 4** — Active learning / online learning (machine learning).
  Approach: Model queries informative examples, updates classifier incrementally.
  Feature overlap: LOW — classifier evolution, not typology discovery.
  Threat level: LOW.

**COMPETITOR 5** — Constitutional AI / principle-based evaluation (Anthropic, 2022+).
  Approach: Define principles up-front; model self-critiques against principles.
  Feature overlap: MEDIUM — both use articulated quality criteria.
  Key strength: Well-theorized, published.
  Key weakness: Principles are fixed by authors; no discovery mechanism.
  Threat level: MEDIUM — a reviewer may ask how the Innovation Engine extends or differs from constitutional AI.

### Whitespace

**WHITESPACE**: An explicit, mechanism-level account of how a quality rubric can evolve through codified triggers (2-instance + 3-cluster) applied at AI-production scale, with measured trigger latency. No competitor offers this.

**DEFENSIBILITY**: Durable. The specific mechanism design is non-obvious (cluster rule as scale-response), and the empirical latency measurement requires a concrete production record.

**NOVELTY TYPE**: New mechanism + new result (measured latency distribution).

### Table stakes

1. Positioning against grounded theory (axial coding comparison).
2. Positioning against RLHF (typology externalization claim).
3. Positioning against constitutional AI (discovery vs. principle-fixed).
4. Empirical latency measurement on a real production record.
5. Falsification condition with pre-registered thresholds.

## From: causal

### Causal chain

**C-01**: 2-instance or 3-cluster trigger fires → rubric amendment issued.
  Evidence class: Observational (operational record in `scoring/INNOVATIONS.md` Status field).
  Strength: STRONG.

**C-02**: Rubric amendment issued → new quality bar applies forward-only to subsequent chapters.
  Evidence class: Observational (rubric version changelog applied consistently).
  Strength: STRONG (verified by reviewing that no retroactive re-scoring has occurred).

**C-03**: Forward-only bar applies → subsequent chapters are produced at a higher standard for that dimension.
  Evidence class: Observational (mean GATE-2 score trajectory across sessions).
  Strength: MODERATE (Session 2 mean ~82.5 > Session 1 mean ~79 — but model change also confounds).

**C-04**: Higher standard applied → more innovations logged in subsequent chapters.
  Evidence class: Observational.
  Strength: WEAK — confounded with corpus growth (more chapters produce more innovations mechanically).

### Skipped steps

**C-01 → C-02**: The trigger-to-amendment step requires a human (LUCY) to accept the promotion and draft the amendment text. This is a skipped step in the claim that the engine is "automatic."
  Missing link: Human-in-the-loop acceptance and drafting.
  Severity: ADVISORY — the paper should explicitly acknowledge this.

**C-03 → C-04**: The causal link from higher-standard to more-innovations is weak. It could run the other direction: more innovations logged produce more amendments, which produce the appearance of higher standards.
  Missing link: Directionality of the feedback loop.
  Severity: ADVISORY — reverse-causation test recommended in Discussion section.

### Confounders

**C-03** (forward-only → higher Session 2 score): Confounded by (a) model change (Opus → Sonnet as default), (b) reviewer panel refinement, (c) source-density growth (more sources available per chapter), (d) SCRIBE prompting refinement.
  Addressed in paper: PARTIALLY — model substrate is addressed in PR-C1, not here.

**C-04** (standard → innovation rate): Confounded by corpus growth (more chapters → more chances to log innovations).
  Addressed in paper: NOT YET — must address in Methods by normalizing by chapter count.

### Directionality

**C-03**: Could higher scores → more innovations rather than the reverse? YES, plausibly. Evidence for stated direction: temporal precedence (amendments issued before subsequent chapters' scoring, not after).
  Note: But the TRIGGER was the chapters themselves. So the direction is really: chapters produced with innovations → trigger fires → amendment → next chapters held to higher bar.

### Pre-write verdict

**PROCEED.**

**Rationale**:
- Data is available, parseable, and sufficient (n=98 adopted innovations).
- Hypothesis is falsifiable with pre-registered thresholds (median < 2 OR > 50 chapters).
- Preliminary computation provisionally supports the hypothesis (day-median = 1, chapter-median ≈ 15–30, max = 2 days).
- Competitive landscape is mapped: grounded theory (primary), RLHF + constitutional AI (secondary), distinct whitespace.
- Causal chain has 0 blocking gaps, 2 advisory gaps addressable in Methods.
- Coherence check: 0 blocking, 3 advisory.
- Pre-write score: ~19/25 (see score section below).

**Top 3 things to get right in the paper**:
1. **Latency measurement precision**: report both day-median (primary, robust) and chapter-median (derived with conversion uncertainty). Do not claim more precision than the timestamps support.
2. **Semi-automatic framing**: do not overclaim the engine is "automatic." The trigger fires mechanically; amendment text is human-drafted. This is a feature, not a limitation.
3. **Grounded theory positioning**: the Innovation Engine extends axial coding to AI-assisted production scale with forward-only amendment as the novel contribution. Position carefully; do not overclaim novelty against grounded theory.

**Risks flagged**:
- The max-latency = 2 days result is suspiciously tight. It may reflect session-cadence artifact rather than engine calibration. Write phase must investigate whether cross-session latency (innovations logged in Session N and promoted in Session N+1) shows a different regime.
- External generalization (claim that the engine works beyond LUCIA) is NOT within this paper's scope — that is PR-C3's job.
