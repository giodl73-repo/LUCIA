# Champa — The Sea-Facing Kingdom

We are the Cham people, and our kingdom faced the sea. The coast of central Vietnam — from the Hải Vân pass in the north to the Mekong Delta in the south — was Cham country for sixteen centuries, and the sea that washes it was never an obstacle but always a road. Our sailors knew the monsoon winds that connected our ports to the Malay Peninsula, to Java, to India; our merchants carried silk and ceramics between China and the Indian Ocean world; our kings built the great Shaiva tower-temples — the My Son sanctuary, the towers at Đà Nẵng, at Quy Nhơn, at Phan Rang — that marked the landscape with our sacred presence. We are not Indian; we are Cham, and the fact that we built in brick, worshipped Shiva, and inscribed our history in Sanskrit as well as Old Cham does not make us anything but ourselves.

We are also a kingdom that survived by knowing when to withdraw. The Dai Viet pushed south century by century, each generation taking another valley, another stretch of coast. We fought — sometimes brilliantly, sacking Hanoi in 1371 — and we retreated, and we fought again, and we retreated again. By 1832 the last formal Cham political entity had been absorbed. But the Cham people are not gone: the Cham communities of Vietnam and Cambodia maintain their language, their Shaiva and Bani (Muslim) religious traditions, their textile arts, and their memory of who they are. A civilization that outlasted its kingdom by two centuries and counting.

## What Defined Them

**The tower-temple tradition.** The Cham brick towers — kalan — are the defining architectural form of the civilization. Built from small kiln-fired bricks without mortar (the technique is still debated by scholars), they rise from their platforms with a taper that is distinctly Cham. The carved sandstone decorations — Shiva images, dancing apsaras, scenes from Hindu mythology — are not Indian copies; they are Cham interpretations that developed their own aesthetic vocabulary over eight centuries. The My Son sanctuary, with its concentration of towers in a sacred valley, is the spiritual heart of the tradition.

**The maritime trade network.** The Cham ports — at the mouths of the rivers that drain the Annamite Cordillera — were the collection and distribution points for the highland trade goods (forest products, aromatics, elephant ivory) that moved from the interior to the international maritime trade. The Cham position on the East-West trade route made them the middlemen through which Chinese silk and porcelain moved toward India and Arabia, and Indian Ocean goods moved toward China. This commercial position was the economic basis of the kingdom.

**Sanskrit and Old Cham bilingualism.** The Cham inscriptions — the largest pre-modern epigraphic corpus in Southeast Asia — were composed in both Sanskrit and Old Cham. The Sanskrit inscriptions followed the Indian model of royal panegyric: genealogy, divine connection, temple dedication, the king's cosmic glory. The Old Cham inscriptions recorded practical matters: land grants, temple maintenance obligations, administrative records. The two languages served different purposes for the same kingdom, and both are necessary to understand Cham civilization.

**The survival strategy — withdraw and return.** The Cham military strategy against the Vietnamese advance was not simply defensive: they combined periods of withdrawal with counter-attacks, including the spectacular 1371 sack of the Dai Viet capital Hanoi (then called Thang Long). The Cham ruler Che Bong Nga led three raids that nearly destroyed the Vietnamese state. The sack of the capital, while not leading to permanent conquest, demonstrated that Champa was not simply a state retreating before Vietnamese pressure; it was a maritime power capable of striking the interior decisively when conditions allowed.

**Islam and the Cham diaspora.** The Cham people who remained in Vietnam after the final loss of Panduranga in 1832 maintained their identity in two distinct religious forms: the Cham Balamons (Hindu-tradition) and the Cham Bani (Islamic tradition), both of which are distinct from mainstream Hinduism and Islam and represent the adaptation of these world religions to Cham cultural forms. The Cham diaspora communities in Cambodia are predominantly Muslim and maintain Cham language and identity with remarkable tenacity. The Cham experience is a case study in the persistence of cultural identity through political catastrophe.

## The Chapters

**1 — My Son:** The Cham king is overseeing the consecration of a new tower. The ritual fire is lit; the Brahmin priest recites the Sanskrit mantras; the Shiva linga is installed in the sanctum. In a thousand years, archaeologists will excavate this valley and find that hundreds of towers were built here over eight centuries.

**2 — The Monsoon Trade:** A Cham merchant in a port near Vijaya is watching the ship from Java unload spices. He is calculating the profit on the Chinese silk he is about to load. He speaks Cham, Malay, and enough Chinese to negotiate. He is the Champa economy.

**3 — Che Bong Nga Sacks Hanoi:** The Cham fleet is in the Red River delta. The Dai Viet king has fled the capital. The Cham warriors enter the city. For three times in this decade, the Vietnamese capital has fallen to the Cham. The Vietnamese remember this. The wars intensify.

**4 — The Fall of Vijaya:** Le Thanh Tong's army takes the Cham capital in 1471. The king is captured. Tens of thousands of Cham are taken as prisoners. The Cham state loses its political center. The southern territories continue, but the kingdom as a continental power is over.

**5 — Panduranga:** The last Cham territory is absorbed into Vietnam in 1832. The Cham communities survive. In the villages, the Cham language is still spoken, the tower temples still visited, the traditional dances still performed. A civilization outlives its kingdom.
