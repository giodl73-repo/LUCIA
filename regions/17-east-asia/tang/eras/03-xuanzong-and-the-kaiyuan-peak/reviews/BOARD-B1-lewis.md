## B-1 — Mark Edward Lewis — Board Review of The High Tang

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Tang dynasty political institutions, the equal-field and fubing systems, the jiedushi transition, Chang'an's urban structure, the An Lushan rebellion.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The equal-field allotment figures (80 mu personal-share, 20 mu permanent) are correct for the standard adult male allotment under the Tang system.
- The fubing militia system's description as farmer-soldiers equipped at their own expense is accurate for the early Tang, and the chapter correctly notes its replacement by professional frontier armies.
- The jiedushi commission details for An Lushan (Fanyang, Pinglu, Hedong) are correct. The troop figure of 180,000 is within the scholarly range (estimates vary from 150,000 to 200,000 depending on the source).
- The Tong Pass narrative (Geshu Han ordered to attack against his judgment) is consistent with the Zizhi Tongjian account.
- The Mawei incident details are accurate. The silk cord and the Buddhist chapel are from the Jiu Tang Shu account, though sources differ on specifics.
- The census figure of 36 million population decline is from the comparison of the 754 and 764 household registers. The chapter correctly notes scholarly debate about whether this represents actual deaths or census collapse. This is the appropriate handling.
- Minor issue: the chapter states Xuanzong came to power in "the first year of the Kaiyuan reign (713 CE)." Technically, Xuanzong became emperor in 712 but did not adopt the Kaiyuan reign name until 713. The coup against Empress Wei's faction was in 710; the removal of Princess Taiping was in 713. The chapter slightly conflates these events.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's treatment of the keju system as central to Tang political culture is appropriate. The 200,000 candidates figure is in the right order of magnitude for all examination tracks combined, though estimates vary.
- The ward-and-curfew city structure is accurately described. The Chunming Gate as the eastern entry from Luoyang is correct.
- The description of the Grand Canal as Sui infrastructure inherited by the Tang is historically sound.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The structural causation linking the equal-field system's breakdown to jiedushi independence to An Lushan's rebellion is the chapter's strongest analytical contribution. This is precisely how current scholarship understands the connection.
- The farmer paragraph in Section IV is a valuable addition that most popular accounts of the Tang golden age omit.
- The treatment of An Lushan as a product of the system rather than a foreign invader is consistent with Pulleyblank's analysis and the current scholarly consensus.

**What needs correction:**
- The Xuanzong/Kaiyuan chronology should be clarified: he became emperor in 712, adopted the Kaiyuan era name in 713. The coup against Empress Wei was 710, not 713.
- The chapter states the West Market "opened at midday." The Tang market regulations specified opening at noon (wu shi) and closing at sunset, which is correct. However, the chapter should note that this was the East and West Markets specifically; commerce occurred at other locations outside these regulated hours.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (minor chronological clarification needed; no structural or factual errors of substance)
