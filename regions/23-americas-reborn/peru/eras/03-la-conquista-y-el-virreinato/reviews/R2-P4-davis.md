# R2-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel-2 Targeted Review — Notes: La Conquista y El Virreinato

The notes' commitment to showing the taki at method level, and the quipu as counter-archive, is what I needed to see. I want to make sure the chapter's execution lives up to it.

**On the taki at method level:** Section 3 plans to show "a specific taki" traveling — who sang it, what it carried, how it moved. This is the right commitment. The specific harawi (lament-form taki) that mourned the conquest is documented in several colonial-era sources, including in Guaman Poma. The chapter should show the form: the call and response structure, the way the second voice carries the emotional weight while the first voice carries the narrative, the way a harawi could carry a cosmological claim ("the Inca will return, the world will turn") in language the Spanish priests could hear as funeral lamentation and therefore leave alone. The harawi survived because it was legible to the Spanish as grief and invisible to them as political theory. This is the knowledge-system operating at method level: the form itself is the protection mechanism.

**On the quipu as counter-archive:** The notes acknowledge that the quipu's narrative function remains undeciphered. This is honest. But the chapter can still show the quipu operating at method level in its deciphered functions — the census quipu, the tribute quipu — while acknowledging that the narrative quipus (if they exist) remain unread. The specific knowledge system: the color of the fiber, the type of knot, the position on the string, the direction of twist all encode different categories of meaning. A quipu reader (quipucamayoc) could read population data, tribute obligations, and historical records by running their fingers along the strings in specific light conditions. The Spanish destroyed most of them as idolatrous objects. Guaman Poma described the quipucamayocs being forced to stop practicing. The chapter should show one quipucamayoc who continued anyway — reading the record of the mita's violations in the knots, year by year.

**On the Andean botanical and agricultural knowledge systems:** The notes do not address this, but it is relevant. The vertical archipelago — the Andean ecological system of simultaneous cultivation across altitude zones — required a sophisticated understanding of microclimates, seed varieties, soil types, and labor coordination across communities at different elevations. This knowledge system was embedded in the very agricultural terraces (andenes) the Spanish allowed to fall into disrepair. When the andenes failed, the food security that underpinned the ayni system failed with them. The chapter does not need to develop this at length, but a single moment — a community reading the health of its andenes the way a doctor reads a patient's signs, knowing the reciprocal obligation to the soil — would show the Andean ecological knowledge system operating at the level it actually operated.
