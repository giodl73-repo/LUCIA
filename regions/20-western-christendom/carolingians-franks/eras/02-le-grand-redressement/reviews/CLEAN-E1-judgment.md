# CLEAN — E-1 Judgment Auditor
## Le Grand Redressement

**Reviewer:** E-1 — Judgment Auditor
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Mandate

Hunt outside-lens violations. Forbidden words (primitive, advanced, tribe).
Anachronistic morality. Romanticization or demonization. Anything that steps
outside the culture's own worldview.

---

## Findings

**Forbidden words check:** CLEAR. No instances of primitive, advanced, tribe,
Dark Ages, "discovered by," "the New World," or equivalent framings.

**Outside-judgment check:** CLEAN with one note.

The phrase "the lowercase letters you are reading now are the descendants of
the script that Alcuin corrected into existence" is a deliberate bridge to the
modern reader. It is not a judgment — it is an observation about material
continuity. It does not rank the Carolingian achievement as "sophisticated"
or "remarkable for its time." It simply notes a factual inheritance. This
passes the Achebe Test: it does not tell a Carolingian scholar what to think
of their own work; it tells a modern reader what is still present from that
work. Retained.

**Anachronistic morality check:** CLEAN.

The Verden section handles the massacre without modern verdict. The chapter
presents Alcuin's criticism (recorded in his letters: historically documented)
and Charles's decision (recorded in the annals: historically documented) without
adding an editorial judgment from above. "A decision that shocked some of his
own advisors" is an accurate description sourced from the Revised Annals'
discomfort with the event. The chapter does not call Charles brutal, genocidal,
or wrong; nor does it call Alcuin a good man for protesting. Both are rendered
inside the Carolingian frame.

**Romanticization check:** CLEAN.

The palace school is not presented as a golden age. The chapter notes what
it was (a gathering of capable minds for work) and what it was not (a
self-sufficient institution: "the system runs on Charles"). Alcuin's return
to Tours and his continued letters are not nostalgic — they are the
continuation of the reform program at a distance. The landing ("The empire
was another matter") explicitly refuses a triumphalist close.

**The Achebe Test (full reading):** Would a thoughtful Frankish scholar of
the ninth century, reading this account of Le Grand Redressement, feel their
story was told with dignity from inside? The answer is yes. The reform program
is taken seriously on its own theological terms. Charles is neither a saint
nor a monster. Alcuin's intellectual achievement is rendered at the level of
method, not as an anecdote of curiosity. The Saxon resistance is acknowledged
without centering it over the Frankish project. The coronation of 800 is
correctly placed at the chapter's edge rather than its center (it belongs
to Era 3).

**One note for the chapter:** The sentence "This was probably true, and
probably also a product of the fact that the educated men Charles preferred
had been selected for their ability to entertain" is a mild irony that the
narrator delivers from slightly above the scene. It is not a judgment, but
it is a wink at the reader. Consider whether the Capitulary voice admits this
kind of irony. I find it acceptable — it is the voice of a courtier who
knows the court — but flag it for E-2.

**Verdict: CLEAN. No forbidden words or framings found.**
