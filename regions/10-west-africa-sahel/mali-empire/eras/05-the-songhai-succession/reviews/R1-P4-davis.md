## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of "The Songhai Succession" Opening

**Overall:** The opening achieves something I did not think was possible in a decline chapter: it makes the persistence of the cultural system, not the loss of territory, the central subject. The Kurukan Fuga is treated as a living political philosophy, and its limitations are described from within its own framework rather than from the vantage of a more "successful" system.

**What works:**
- The opening treats the covenant as a genuine governance technology with specific operating parameters, not as a quaint tradition that happened to work for a while. "The covenant bound the tributary states not through garrisons or terror but through redistribution." This is political philosophy, not anthropological observation.
- The Songhai are given their own epistemic standing: "their own memory, their own river, their own reasons." This is not tokenism. It is the opening's acknowledgment that what is happening at Gao is a story of equal dignity that this chapter is not equipped to tell in full.
- The jelilu are treated as an institution, not as colorful performers. Their function in the opening is specific: they carry the covenant's terms, they maintain the genealogy, they do not edit the record. Their refusal to contract the genealogy when the empire contracts is the chapter's deepest argument about the relationship between cultural memory and political power.

**What doesn't:**
- The intellectual tradition is absent from the opening. Timbuktu's scholars, the manuscript libraries, the teaching at Sankore — none of these appear. For a chapter that covers the transition of Timbuktu from Mali's orbit to Songhai's, the scholarly tradition should register somewhere in the opening, even if only as a horizon.
- The Songhai's own knowledge systems — their understanding of the river, their military technology, their religious traditions — are mentioned only in passing. The full chapter must give these the same depth that Era 3 gave to Musa's intellectual patronage.

**The question I'd push on:**
The scholarly tradition's continuity across changes of political power is one of the most remarkable features of this period. Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti was born into a library that spanned Mali, Tuareg, Sonni, and Askia patronage. The full chapter must show that knowledge systems have their own persistence mechanisms, independent of the states that fund them.
