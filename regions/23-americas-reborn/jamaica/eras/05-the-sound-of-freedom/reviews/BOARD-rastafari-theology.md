# Board Review — Rastafari Studies / Caribbean Religion
**Chapter:** Jamaica Era 5 — The Sound of Freedom
**Reviewer:** Dr. Horace Campbell (Board — Political Science / Rasta and Resistance, Syracuse University)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

My book *Rasta and Resistance* covers the material in this chapter directly. I will be specific.

## Theological Accuracy

The Babylon/Zion framework is correctly rendered. The chapter understands that these are not metaphors in the Rastafari tradition — they are operative categories for understanding history and political economy. The connection to Marcus Garvey is correctly made, including the appropriate nuance that Garvey did not personally endorse Selassie as fulfillment of his prophecy. The chapter handles this cleanly: "Garvey himself... had not endorsed Selassie. He had complicated feelings about the Emperor. This did not matter to those who read his prophecy as fulfilled."

The founders of the Rastafari tradition — Leonard Howell, Joseph Hibbert, Archibald Dunkley, Robert Hinds — are correctly named in the notes and correctly contextualized in the chapter narrative. The Coral Gardens incident of 1963 is accurately described as a state-sponsored campaign of collective punishment against Rastafari communities.

The locks as Nazarite vow: theologically precise. The application of this to Marley's refusal to amputate: also theologically precise. This is how the tradition actually reasoned about the body.

The response to Selassie's death — "Jah does not die" — is accurate as a description of how the major Rastafari communities responded. The decentralized theology produced a distributed response that was, broadly, consistent: the divine aspect of Selassie is not contingent on the biological life of Tafari Makonnen.

## One Theological Precision

The chapter describes the herb's role as "sacrament-and-instrument." I would distinguish: in Rastafari, the herb is not primarily a sacrament in the Catholic sense (a ritual that effects what it signifies). It is more accurately described as a portal or a method — it opens the reasoning session, it enables discernment, it is the means by which Jah communicates to the reasoning community. The sacramental framing is close but slightly imports a Christian category. In the finished chapter, "the herb opened the eye" is the more accurate phrasing (and the chapter uses it elsewhere). The word "sacrament" appears once in the notes; I would flag it for revision in any future edition.

## The Garvey Chain

The chapter correctly identifies the intellectual lineage: Garvey provides the prophetic framework; Selassie provides the fulfillment as read in Kingston; Rastafari builds the living theology; reggae encodes it in musical form. This is the correct intellectual history of the movement.

## Recommendation

**Approved for FINAL.** One minor theological precision noted (sacrament vs. portal/method) — flag for future edition but does not require revision of the locked chapter. The cultural representation is accurate and respectful of the tradition's own terms.
