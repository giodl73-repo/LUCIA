# The House of Wisdom

Hunayn ibn Ishaq held the Greek to the lamp and read it again.

The text was Galen, the seventh book of the Anatomical Procedures, and the Greek was difficult not because Hunayn's Greek was poor but because Galen's Greek was dense, technical, layered with qualifications that the physician had added in old age when he no longer trusted simple statements. Hunayn had learned Greek in Alexandria and in the Greek-speaking cities of Bilad al-Sham, traveling for two years to find a complete manuscript of this text because the copy in Baghdad was corrupt, missing folios, the ink faded where water had touched it during the journey from a monastery in Upper Mesopotamia. He had found a better copy in Damascus. He had found an even better one in a merchant's collection in a town whose name the sources do not record. Now he sat in the Bayt al-Hikma, the House of Wisdom, in Madinat al-Salam, in the year 218 of the Hijra (833 CE), and he was not simply translating. He was arguing with Galen.

The lamp was olive oil. It did not smoke as much as sesame, but it smelled different, greener, and the smell mixed with the scent of the ink he mixed himself from gallnuts and gum arabic and iron salts, and with the particular dusty warmth of a Baghdad evening in late summer, when the Tigris ran low and the air above the river held the heat like a clay oven holds its fire. The reed pen was cut to a nib that suited his hand. He had been cutting reeds for thirty years. A translator's hand knows its instrument the way a surgeon's hand knows the knife, and Hunayn was both: physician and translator, the two skills feeding each other because the texts he translated were medical and the patients he treated were the evidence against which he tested what the texts claimed.

He did not translate word by word. No serious translator did. He read the Greek passage, understood its meaning, then expressed that meaning in Arabic, finding the words that carried the same weight in a language whose architecture was entirely different. Greek built its meaning through particles and case endings, each word inflected to signal its relationship to every other word in the sentence. Arabic built its meaning through roots, trilateral consonantal roots that branched into families of related words through patterns of vowels and prefixes and suffixes that a native speaker navigated the way a swimmer navigates currents. To move Galen's thought from one architecture to the other was not copying. It was reconstruction.

And sometimes it was correction.

Galen had written, in this passage, that a certain nerve ran from the brain to the eye along a path that Hunayn, who had dissected animals in the course of his medical practice, believed was wrong. The Greek said one thing. The body said another. Hunayn noted the discrepancy in the margin, in Arabic, in a hand smaller than his translation hand, and he continued, because the work of the Bayt al-Hikma was not to worship the Greeks. It was to take what the Greeks knew, and what the Indians knew, and what the Persians knew, and bring it into Arabic, and then to push beyond what any of them had known, because knowledge in Baghdad was not a museum. It was a living practice, and a living practice corrects its predecessors.

Outside the room, beyond the walls of the Bayt al-Hikma, Baghdad extended in every direction. The Round City that al-Mansur had built seventy years earlier was already too small; the population had spilled east across the Tigris into the Karkh quarter and south along the canals, and the city that had been designed as a perfect circle with the caliph's palace at its center was now a sprawl of markets and mosques and houses that followed the water rather than the geometry. The markets stayed open late in summer. Hunayn could hear, if he paused in his work, the faint sounds of the booksellers' quarter in the Karkh, where copyists sat in the fronts of shops and reproduced texts for anyone who could pay, and where scholars argued about manuscripts the way merchants argued about prices, because in Baghdad a book's accuracy was its value and a corrupt text was a cheat.

The caliph who had founded the Bayt al-Hikma was al-Mamun, the son of Harun al-Rashid, and al-Mamun had wanted something specific. He had wanted the knowledge of every tradition the caliphate had conquered to be available in Arabic. The project was not charity. It was strategy. A caliphate that governed Persians and Greeks and Indians and Copts and Berbers needed an intellectual language that could hold all of their knowledge, and al-Mamun had decided that Arabic would be that language. He paid translators by the weight of the books they produced, gold for gold, the manuscript placed on one side of the scale and the payment on the other, and the translators, being practical men, wrote on heavy paper. The story may be embellished. The investment was real: the caliphs and their courtiers spent fortunes on translation, and the return was an Arabic-language intellectual tradition that within two generations held more of the world's accumulated knowledge than any other language on earth.

Hunayn did not work alone. His son Ishaq ibn Hunayn translated beside him. His nephew Hubaysh ibn al-Hasan copied and checked. Around them, in other rooms, other translators worked on other texts. Thabit ibn Qurra, a Sabian from Harran, was translating Euclid and Archimedes and Apollonius, his Greek precise and his Arabic mathematical, creating the vocabulary that Arabic geometry would use for the next five centuries. In another part of the city, Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi was not translating at all. He was writing something new.

Al-Khwarizmi's Kitab al-mukhtasar fi hisab al-jabr wal-muqabala -- the Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing -- was not a translation. It took the Indian system of positional notation, the nine digits and the zero that had come to Baghdad from the scholars of the Sindh, and combined it with the geometric demonstrations of the Greek mathematical tradition, and produced from the synthesis a new discipline: al-jabr, the science of restoring and balancing, the systematic method for solving equations that no tradition before al-Khwarizmi had articulated as a unified field. The word "algebra" is Arabic. The word "algorithm" is al-Khwarizmi's name, Latinized. These are not borrowings. They are origins.

Hunayn turned back to his Galen. The lamp burned. The reed pen moved. Beyond the walls, Baghdad hummed with the particular sound of a city where knowledge was currency and the market for it never closed.
