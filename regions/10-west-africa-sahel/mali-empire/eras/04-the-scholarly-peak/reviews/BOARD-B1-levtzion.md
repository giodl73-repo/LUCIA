# B-1 -- Levtzion -- Board Review of "The Scholarly Peak"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Political and economic history of the medieval Western Sudan; trans-Saharan trade networks; the Arabic source tradition for West African history; the interaction between Islam and indigenous West African political systems.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The dating of Musa's death as "ten years, perhaps more" after the events described is acceptable. The scholarly consensus places his death c. 1332-1337. The chapter wisely avoids false precision.
- Ibn Battuta's visit in 753 AH / 1352-53 CE is correctly dated.
- The description of the Taghaza salt mines -- "buildings were made of rock salt and roofed with camel hides" -- is accurate and drawn from Ibn Battuta's own account.
- Mansa Sulayman as Musa's brother or half-brother: the sources are indeed not in perfect agreement. The Tarikh al-Sudan and Ibn Khaldun give slightly different accounts of the relationship. The chapter handles this correctly by noting the ambiguity.
- The Tuareg capture of Timbuktu in 1433 is accurately placed. The Songhai under Sunni Ali Ber taking the city from the Tuareg is accurate (1468).
- The Moroccan invasion of 1591 and Ahmad Baba's exile to Marrakesh are accurately placed.
- The characterization of Sunni Ali Ber's relationship with the scholarly class draws correctly from the Tarikh al-Sudan's perspective, which is explicitly hostile to him. The chapter properly attributes this characterization to the source.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The ijaza system is described accurately as a personal chain of transmission rather than an institutional certification.
- The description of the Mukhtasar of Khalil ibn Ishaq as the standard Maliki text is correct for this period.
- The waqf endowment system is correctly identified as a funding mechanism for scholarship.
- The scholarly families named -- Aqit, Baghayogho -- are documented in the Tarikh al-Sudan.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The integration of the book trade with the salt-gold trade is structurally sound and historically accurate. Manuscripts did travel on the same caravans.
- The treatment of Ibn Battuta's biases as characterization rather than fact is historically appropriate. His account of Mali is well-known for its cultural judgments.
- The Malian women's freedom as described by Ibn Battuta is accurately rendered -- he did record his disapproval of these specific practices.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter refers to the Risala of Ibn Abi Zayd and al-Mudawwana of Sahnun as part of the Aqit library. While these were standard Maliki texts, the specific claim that the Aqit library held them in this period is an inference. The texts were certainly available in Timbuktu; their presence in a specific family's library at a specific date is not documented. The chapter should either generalize ("texts that a Sankore library would typically hold") or flag the inference.
- Ibn Battuta calling the Niger "the Nile" -- while this is a common simplification, Ibn Battuta's text is more nuanced. He discusses the question of whether the river is connected to the Nile, reflecting a geographic debate, not simply an error. The chapter could be slightly more precise here.
- The number "hundreds of thousands of manuscripts" for Timbuktu's private libraries is frequently cited but refers to the total across the city's entire history, including much later periods. For the era covered by this chapter (c. 1337-1430), the collections would have been substantial but significantly smaller. The chapter's phrasing -- "by the time the tradition reached its fullest expression" -- acknowledges this is a later development, which is acceptable, but the temporal shift could be clearer.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS. The corrections above are minor and do not affect the chapter's fundamental accuracy or its structural argument. The treatment of the scholarly tradition is historically sound, and the integration of the intellectual, economic, and political dimensions is well-handled.
