# R1-P4 — Wade Davis — Review of "The High Wagadu" Opening

**Chapter:** Soninke / Era 3: The High Wagadu
**Round:** 1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## The Knowledge System

What I find most promising about this opening is what it does with the Bida covenant. It refuses to treat the serpent legend as a decorative preamble to the "real" history of trade and statecraft. Instead, it argues that the covenant IS the trade logic, expressed in the Soninke's own epistemological framework. "A theory of political economy expressed in the language of the sacred." This is exactly right, and it is the kind of intellectual move that separates genuine engagement with a knowledge system from patronizing acknowledgment of it.

The Soninke did not have one set of explanations for their prosperity (the sacred) and another (the economic). They had one explanation that operated in both registers simultaneously. The Bida covenant is not a myth that conceals the "real" economic forces underneath. It IS the economic analysis, in the culture's own terms. If the full chapter sustains this fusion, it will be doing something genuinely new in the manuscript.

## What Needs More Depth

**The ecology.** The Sahel is one of the most ecologically specific environments on the planet. The opening mentions "dry country between the desert and the savanna" and "soil that baked to clay four months of every year," but this is thin. The Sahel is a transition zone with its own logic: between the 200mm and 600mm isohyets, where the rain's arrival determines everything. The grasses change species as you walk north. The trees thin from shea and baobab and kapok to thorn and acacia and then to nothing. The wind shifts with the seasons: the harmattan blows dry dust south from the Sahara for months, and then the monsoon arrives from the southwest and the world transforms. The Soninke lived in this transition and their entire economic position was shaped by it. They sat where the Saharan economy met the Sudanic economy BECAUSE the ecology created a transition. The twin-city structure mirrors the ecological transition: dry and wet, desert and savanna, salt and gold.

I would like to see the ecology present not as background but as the foundation on which the intermediary position rests. The Soninke did not happen to sit at a crossroads. The ecology created the crossroads. The ecology created the divergence between the desert economy (salt, copper, dates) and the savanna economy (gold, kola, grain). Wagadu's power was ecological before it was political.

**The gold-mining knowledge.** The opening mentions alluvial gold "sifted with calabash pans in water that ran the color of rust." Good detail. But whose knowledge was this? The miners were not Soninke. They were Malinke, Diula, and other peoples whose relationship to the gold was mediated by their own sacred understandings. The gold was found because certain ritual specialists knew where to look, and the knowledge of where to find gold was itself a sacred knowledge system with its own protocols and protections. The Soninke's intermediary position was partly built on respecting the mystery of the gold's origin. They did not need to know where it came from. They only needed to know that it came.

## The Question I'd Push On

The "silent trade." Several Arabic sources describe a practice at the edges of Wagadu's territory where gold and salt were exchanged without the parties ever meeting face to face. Salt was laid out. The salt traders withdrew. The gold merchants came, left gold beside the salt, and withdrew. The salt traders returned. If the gold was sufficient, they took it and left the salt. If not, they withdrew again, and the process repeated until agreement was reached, or the gold merchants took their gold back and the deal was off. This practice, if it occurred as described (and the sources are multiple enough to suggest something real behind them), is one of the most remarkable economic institutions in human history. It is a market without language, without sight, without trust in the conventional sense. It is trust mediated entirely by ritual and custom. Does the chapter engage with this? It is exactly the kind of institution that reveals the depth of the Soninke knowledge system.
