# Rashtrakuta — The Deccan at the Center

We are the Rashtrakuta, the lords of the Deccan plateau, and we were the hinge on which the subcontinent turned for two centuries. From our capital at Manyakheta, we pressed north into the Gangetic plain and south toward the Tamil kingdoms simultaneously, fighting the Gurjara-Pratiharas for Kanauj, fighting the Eastern Chalukyas for the Vengi coast, fighting the Pallavas and later the Cholas for domination of the Deccan margins. Our power was active in every direction because the Deccan's position required it. To hold the center of the subcontinent, you must dominate its edges.

We are also the builders of Ellora. The Kailasa temple — the entire structure carved from a single basalt hill, a freestanding temple larger than the Parthenon and cut top-down from solid rock — was our architectural declaration. This was not assembled from cut blocks; it was subtracted from the mountain. An Arab traveler of our era, the merchant Sulaiman, wrote that the Rashtrakuta king was one of the four great kings of the world. We did not dispute his assessment. The Kailasa temple is the argument we made in stone.

Key places:
- **Manyakheta** — the capital; modern Malkhed in Karnataka; hub of the Deccan empire
- **Ellora** — the rock-cut temple complex; the Kailasa temple (Cave 16); a UNESCO World Heritage Site
- **Kanauj** — the symbolic capital of northern India; contested between Rashtrakuta, Pratihara, and Pala

## What Defined Them

**The Deccan's fulcrum politics.** The Rashtrakuta occupied the central position of the subcontinent and conducted a three-way struggle with the Gurjara-Pratiharas to the north and the Palas of Bengal to the northeast, while simultaneously managing the Tamil south. This was not accident; it was the political logic of the Deccan. The rulers who held the plateau held the balance, and every great Deccan power from the Satavahanas through the Mughals would work within this same geometry.

**The Kailasa temple at Ellora.** The construction of the Kailasa temple — a single freestanding temple cut from a single basalt hillside — required removing approximately 200,000 tonnes of rock, carved from the top down, over generations. The result is an architectural miracle: a temple that replicates Mount Kailash, residence of Shiva, at full scale. The political message was unambiguous: the king who built this could do anything.

**Pluralism as policy.** The Rashtrakuta rulers were primarily Shaiva Hindus, but they patronized Buddhist and Jain rock-cut caves at Ellora alongside the Hindu temples. Amoghavarsha I was personally devoted to Jainism. The Arab merchants of the Malabar coast traded freely in Rashtrakuta ports; Sulaiman observed that Muslims were permitted to build mosques and conduct religious practice freely. This was not enlightened tolerance in the modern sense; it was the pragmatic pluralism of a commercial empire that profited from every traveler's goodwill.

**The origin of Kannada literature.** The Rashtrakuta period saw the first flowering of written Kannada literature. The Kavirajamarga, attributed to Amoghavarsha I, is the earliest extant Kannada text on poetics. The Rashtrakuta court was a bilingual space where Sanskrit held cosmological authority and Kannada held the local — and under Amoghavarsha, the local began to be literary in its own right.

**Military reach as political argument.** The Rashtrakuta kings conducted successful campaigns in every direction, including a famous raid to the Gangetic plain that reached the Himalayas. This was not pure conquest — much of the territory was not permanently held. It was the demonstration of reach: the Deccan king who could strike Kanauj, occupy the Tamil coast, and send ambassadors to the Abbasid caliphate was not a regional power. He was a world power expressed through the military vocabulary his age understood.

## The Chapters

**1 — Dantidurga and Foundation:** A tributary chief of the Chalukya defeats his overlord. The hierarchy inverts. The Deccan has new masters, and they remember their beginning.

**2 — Dhruva and Northern Expansion:** The Rashtrakuta army crosses the Vindhyas. The northern kings watch from Kanauj. This southern force has done what no Deccan army had done before. Something has changed.

**3 — Govinda III:** The Arab merchant Sulaiman counts the world's great kings on his fingers: the Abbasid caliph, the Frankish emperor, the Chinese emperor, and the Rashtrakuta. He writes this down. He means it.

**4 — Amoghavarsha I:** The king is a poet and a Jain devotee. He writes the first Kannada text on poetics. He also cuts off his finger as an offering to Mahalakshmi when the realm is threatened by flood. Both acts are true to who he is.

**5 — Later Rashtrakuta:** The Kailasa temple still rises from the Ellora hillside. The dynasty that carved it is fading. The Chalukyas of Kalyani are rising to replace them. The Deccan never rests.
