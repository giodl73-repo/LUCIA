## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Meroe Era 3 Opening

**Overall:** The voice is right. This story belongs to Meroe. I read it and I do not find Rome's story being told with Meroitic decoration. I find Meroe's story being told, and Rome appears in it as Rome appeared to them: as a neighbor, a threat, a problem to be managed.

**What works:**
- The opening's most important achievement is its center of gravity. Rome does not arrive as the protagonist who encounters a "peripheral kingdom." Rome arrives as a prefect sent south, and the kandake handles him. The verb structures throughout maintain this: Amanirenas decided, Amanirenas earned, Amanirenas negotiated, Amanirenas knew. She is the subject. Rome is the object.
- The burial of the bronze head is presented through Meroitic logic, not through Western symbolism. "In Meroitic practice, to place something beneath a threshold was to master it" is the critical sentence. The author does not say "this was a symbolic gesture of defiance." The author says this was what Meroites did with power they had captured. The culture's own categories explain the act.
- The line "the lion god said nothing, because there was nothing that needed to be said" is the voice of a culture that does not need to explain itself. This is the Achebe test at its purest: the culture is so fully itself that no outside vantage point is available.

**What doesn't:**
- The sentence "This was the state that Rome encountered south of the first cataract" briefly positions the reader as someone who needs to be told what Meroe was. The preceding paragraph already showed it. This sentence steps slightly outside, as if the author is turning to a Western reader and saying, "See? They were significant." The significance was already established. Cut or rework.
- "Not a frontier people. Not a peripheral chiefdom. A kingdom with furnaces and scribes and an army that could take Aswan and negotiate with an emperor." These three sentences defend Meroe against a charge no one in the text has made. Who called them a frontier people? Who called them a peripheral chiefdom? This is the author pre-emptively arguing with an imagined skeptic, and that argument comes from outside the culture. Meroe does not need to defend itself. Let the evidence stand without the defense.

**The question I'd push on:**
The full chapter must sustain this voice for 5,000 words while incorporating treaty terms, military campaigns, trade analysis, and the source problem (Strabo, Pliny, Dio Cassius are all Roman). How will the chapter handle Roman sources without shifting the center of gravity? The opening does this well for 800 words. The test is whether it can do it for 5,000. Specifically: when Strabo describes Amanirenas, will the text let Strabo's description stand as Roman evidence about Rome's perceptions, or will it unconsciously adopt Strabo's framing?
