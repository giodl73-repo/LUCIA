# Figures — Philippe Auguste et la Grande Expansion
## Era 3, Capetian France — 1180–1226 CE

---

## Philip II Augustus (1165–1223)

He was not what a king was supposed to look like. Where Richard of England was tall and golden-haired and physically magnetic — the king as walking advertisement for himself — Philip was fair-haired and ruddy and possessed of a nervous stomach. Rigord of Saint-Denis, who gave him the name Augustus and who admired him without pretending he was a hero of romance, recorded that Philip had a weakness for wine that he had to discipline himself against, and that he suffered throughout his life from a constitutional anxiety that made him ill before battles and immediately afterward. He was afraid, and he went anyway, which is a different thing from being brave.

What Philip had instead of physical magnificence was the intelligence to understand what he actually was — not a knight-king but a lawyer-king, not a crusader but an administrator — and the patience to turn these qualities into instruments of power over a reign of forty-three years. He received a domain barely larger than the Île-de-France and returned a kingdom that was the largest in Europe. The Capetian trick was biology — unbroken male succession for ten generations — but Philip's personal contribution was the willingness to work the mechanism without glamour, case by case, document by document, bailli by bailli.

His treatment of Ingeborg is the wound in his record that contemporaries noted and historians have debated ever since. The repudiation the morning after the wedding was not explained then and has not been satisfactorily explained since. Philip was not a man who acted on impulse in other matters; in this one matter, across twenty years, he was rigidly, almost irrationally, unable to accept what the Church and his own theology demanded of him. The man who healed hundreds of strangers by touch would not touch his lawful wife.

He died in Mantes on July 14, 1223, of a fever. He was fifty-seven years old. He had reigned for forty-three years, more than any Capetian before him.

## Ingeborg of Denmark (1174–1236)

She was eighteen when Philip married her and eighteen when he repudiated her the following morning, and she spent the next thirty years refusing to accept that the repudiation was valid. This refusal was not passive. She wrote to Rome repeatedly. She maintained her title. She gave the papacy the political leverage that sustained the interdict of 1200. She was, in the specific terms that her world would have recognized, correct: the marriage was valid, the annulment was not, and the king who claimed to be the Most Christian King was in an indefensible position by the very theology that sustained his authority.

What Ingeborg experienced in the years of her confinement — variously described as a manor, a comfortable captivity, an honored exile — we know imperfectly. The French chronicles do not dwell on her. The papal correspondence mentions her in legal terms: the queen who is not being treated as a queen. She learned French. She remained in France. She did not go home.

Philip died in 1223. Ingeborg returned to Denmark, eventually, and died there in 1236. She is buried at Corbeil, in France, in the abbey she founded. The tomb inscription calls her queen of France.

She was. That is the full record.

## Guillaume de Breton (c. 1165–c. 1226)

Philip's court chaplain and official chronicler; the primary source for Bouvines. His *Philippide* is a Latin epic poem — twelve books modeled on Virgil — and his *Gesta Philippi Augusti* is a prose chronicle. Both are celebratory, and both are eyewitness accounts of events the author was present for. He was at Bouvines.

What this means for the historical record: Guillaume de Breton gives us the finest contemporary account of the battle we have, with tactical details, individual heroics, and the emotional temperature of a man who was terrified and is now exultant. He gives us the crowds in Paris, the singing through the night, the prisoners paraded through the streets. He also tells us what Philip said when his knights pulled him from beneath a pile of fallen men after a moment when the battle could have ended the other way, and whether Philip actually said it, the sentiment is genuine: that a king who runs is no longer a king.

The limitation: Guillaume de Breton did not write about Ingeborg, or wrote about her as briefly as the court's interest permitted. He did not write about the Béziers massacre in terms that dwelt on what it cost. He was Philip's chronicler, not France's — the story he tells is the story the court wanted told.

This is noted not as a criticism but as a fact of the source situation. What we know of Philip's reign, we know largely through men who served him and admired him. The south of France, the Denmark from which Ingeborg came, the English perspective on Château Gaillard — these survive in other sources, less complete, that press against the margins of the official record.

## Simon de Montfort, Earl of Leicester (c. 1160–1218)

The military leader of the Albigensian Crusade after Philip declined to lead it in person. A Norman lord of modest territorial holdings who became, through the crusade's mechanisms, the most powerful lord in Languedoc. He was killed at the siege of Toulouse in 1218 by a stone from a mangonel operated, the Occitan sources say, by women of the city.

He was a genuine crusader in the sense that he believed what he was doing was God's work. He was also a skilled military commander who understood that the crusade offered him the opportunity to acquire a principality. These two things were not in conflict in his understanding. He went to war against the Cathars of the south and he seized their vassals' lands by crusade law, and he held both as expressions of the same divine mandate.

The chapter does not judge him. The sources from both sides agree that he was effective, that he was brave, and that the war he conducted in Languedoc was ruthless. The massacre at Béziers happened under his overall command, though the initial assault was carried out by the camp followers who broke in before the organized force could stop them. Whether it could have been stopped: the sources differ. The dead do not.

## Pope Innocent III (1160–1216)

The most powerful pope of the medieval period; Philip's most formidable counterpart in the sacred sphere. Innocent III is the figure who confirms that Philip's sacred legitimacy was real — real enough to matter, real enough to be weaponized by both sides in the Ingeborg dispute.

Innocent launched the Fourth Crusade (which sacked Constantinople in 1204, to his horror — the crusaders he authorized attacked a Christian city and he had not authorized that). He launched the Albigensian Crusade in 1209. He convened the Fourth Lateran Council in 1215, the great reforming council of medieval Christendom. He placed France under interdict in 1200 over Ingeborg, lifted it when Philip nominally complied, and spent years in the complex negotiation of a papacy that needed the French crown as much as the French crown needed the papacy.

Philip and Innocent needed each other and competed with each other simultaneously. The Albigensian Crusade served Philip's territorial interests while being conducted under Innocent's authority. The Ingeborg interdict was Innocent's assertion of jurisdictional supremacy in matters of marriage — which the Church held exclusively — against a king who believed that his sacred anointing had given him a status that placed him above ordinary canonical requirements.

Neither won that argument. It was suspended by Philip's death. Innocent had died the year before, in 1216.

## Sources Note

The primary narrative source for this chapter is Guillaume de Breton's *Gesta Philippi Augusti* and *Philippide*, supplemented by John W. Baldwin's *The Government of Philip Augustus* (1986), the definitive modern scholarly treatment of Philip's reign. Marc Bloch's *The Royal Touch* (1973) is the essential source for the thaumaturgical kingship. Georges Duby's *Bouvines: The Making of Europe* (1990) provides the cultural and political analysis of the battle's significance. The Albigensian Crusade is documented in Guillaume de Tudèle's *Canso de la Crozada* (Occitan, early 13th century) and in the Latin sources of the northern French crusade.

Ingeborg's story is documented primarily through papal correspondence (in the Patrologia Latina) and through the work of modern scholars including Hallam's *Capetian France* and Jordan's *The French Monarchy and the Jews*, which contextualizes Philip's Jewish policy.

The figures in this chapter are all historically documented. Philip's physical description, his nervous stomach, his wine discipline, and his reaction at Bouvines are all recorded by Guillaume de Breton or Rigord of Saint-Denis. Ingeborg's timeline is documented through papal records. Simon de Montfort's death is recorded in the *Canso*. The figures.md draws on the historical record as transmitted; where gaps exist, they are acknowledged.
