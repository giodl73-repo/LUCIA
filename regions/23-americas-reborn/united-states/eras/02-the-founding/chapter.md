# The Founding — The Experiment Begins
## United States, Era 2 (1776–1800)

Philadelphia, September 1787. The State House has been locked and shuttered for four months. The windows are nailed shut against the summer heat and the ears of passersby. The room smells of candle wax and damp wool and the particular exhaustion of men who have been arguing in close quarters for weeks. Inside, the delegates work without public record. James Madison sits near the front, writing. He writes everything. He writes faster than he sleeps. At the end of each session he goes back to his lodgings and expands his shorthand notes into full sentences, and those sentences will eventually become the most complete account of what happened in that room, and for more than thirty years no one will see them, because Madison has promised to keep them secret until the last delegate dies. He is, at the moment, thirty-six years old and the last delegate is not going to die for a long time.

Outside the State House, enslaved people serve the delegates their meals.

This is not a detail added later for correction. It was true while it was happening. The men who were writing a government founded on the proposition that all legitimate authority derives from the consent of the governed were waited on by people whose consent had not been asked. The men who were designing the mechanisms of republican liberty were attended by people for whom the republic would not, for the moment, include liberty. This was visible to everyone in the room. Several of the delegates owned the people who served them. Several of the delegates were uncomfortable about this and said so, in the room, in the record Madison was keeping. Several of the delegates were comfortable about this and said so too.

The argument about what the republic would mean was happening at the same moment the republic was being invented. These are not sequential events. They are the same event.

---

## The Argument Before the Argument

The Declaration of Independence, signed eleven years earlier on July 4, 1776, had stated the propositions plainly: all men are created equal, endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable Rights, among them Life, Liberty, and the pursuit of Happiness. Governments derive their just powers from the consent of the governed. When a government becomes destructive of these ends, it is the right of the people to alter or abolish it. Thomas Jefferson wrote this in the second week of June 1776, working in rented rooms in Philadelphia, revising a draft he had completed in seventeen days. He was thirty-three years old and had brought 175 people to Virginia in chains who had no say in whether he went or stayed.

Jefferson's contradictions were not private. They were structural. Virginia's tobacco economy rested on enslaved labor, and Jefferson's ability to be a statesman rather than a farmer — to have the leisure for reading and writing and the salon conversations in which revolutions were planned — depended on the labor of people he owned. The Declaration's most famous sentence was written by a man whose own life demonstrated its limits. Jefferson knew this. He had included, in his original draft of the Declaration, a passage condemning the king for the slave trade — a condemnation that the Continental Congress removed, because South Carolina and Georgia would not sign a document that attacked the institution that organized their economy. The passage was cut. The principle survived. The tension between the principle and its excision was present from the founding document's founding moment.

The Articles of Confederation, which governed the United States from 1781, had turned the principle of limited government into a problem. The Congress could request money from the states; it could not compel payment. It could request soldiers; it could not draft them. It had no power to regulate commerce between states, which meant thirteen separate tariff regimes and constant economic friction. The currency was unstable. The war debt from the Revolution remained unpaid. When the Massachusetts government, in 1786, began seizing the farms of debt-burdened veterans who could not pay their taxes in the hard currency the courts required, a former Revolutionary War captain named Daniel Shays led an armed column to close the Springfield courthouse. The state militia eventually dispersed them. The episode traveled through the correspondence networks of the American leadership class like a fever. George Washington, reading the reports at Mount Vernon, wrote to James Madison: "There are combustibles in every State, which a spark might set fire to." Madison, already planning the Convention, wrote back that a stronger national government was the only remedy.

They were not wrong. But the Convention they gathered to held its own combustibles.

## The Machine

The Virginia Plan that Madison brought to Philadelphia was a blueprint for a genuinely national government: a bicameral legislature with representation proportional to population, an executive chosen by the legislature, a national judiciary, and the power to veto state laws. The small states rejected proportional representation immediately; they would be swallowed. The large states rejected equal representation by state; they would be outvoted. The Connecticut Compromise split the difference: proportional representation in the House, equal representation in the Senate. Neither side liked it. Both accepted it. The pattern of American constitutional politics was established in the first month.

The question of slavery took longer and went deeper. The northern states, several of which had already moved toward abolition, wanted to count enslaved people for purposes of taxation but not representation. The southern states wanted to count them for representation but not taxation. The result — three-fifths of all other Persons, for both representation and taxation — satisfied neither side's principle while satisfying both sides' immediate interest. It gave the southern states more House seats than their free population would have earned. It gave the northern states the principle, however attenuated, that those who could not vote should not generate the representation of those who oppressed them. It was a bargain, and both sides knew it was a bargain, and the document they signed contained it.

The Atlantic slave trade was protected until 1808. The Fugitive Slave Clause required the return of escaped enslaved people across state lines. The three-fifths ratio inflated southern representation for eighty years, giving the slaveholding states a structural advantage in the House of Representatives, the Electoral College, and consequently the presidency that only the Civil War resolved. These were not accidents. They were the price of the union.

What was built around the bargain was genuine. Alexander Hamilton, writing Federalist No. 78 in a single long night in New York during the ratification campaign, explained what judicial review meant: the courts could strike down laws that violated the Constitution. This was not in the document explicitly; Hamilton deduced it from the structure of the thing. No one had built this before at this scale. The idea that a written document could be the supreme law, that courts could measure any act of government against its text and find it wanting, was an invention. Hamilton understood that he was writing at speed about something that would still be argued about when everyone in the room was dead. He was right.

At the Virginia ratification convention, Patrick Henry argued that the Constitution would produce tyranny: the president would become a king, the Senate an aristocracy, and the people would lose the liberties they had fought a revolution to secure. Madison argued back, point by point, for days. Henry was eloquent and wrong about the specific mechanisms, but the question he was asking — what happens when the constitutional machine is operated by people who do not share the republic's purposes? — was the right question, and it did not have a clean answer. Madison's answer was Federalist No. 51: "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition." The system was designed to prevent the concentration of power by dividing it, not by trusting the people who wielded it to be virtuous. This was a realist position disguised as an idealist project, which is the most durable American political combination.

Virginia ratified. The Constitution went into effect. Washington was elected unanimously.

## The Other Philadelphia

Three blocks from the State House, in the same September of 1787, Richard Allen and Absalom Jones were meeting with other free Black Philadelphians to establish the Free African Society: a mutual aid organization, the first of its kind in America, organized and governed by Black people for Black people. Allen had purchased his own freedom at twenty years old, working as a laborer and woodcutter to raise the price. He was now twenty-seven. He had come back to Philadelphia because Philadelphia, with its Quaker tradition and its free Black population, was the most viable place in the new republic for a free Black man to build a life.

The Free African Society was incorporated on April 12, 1787 — four months before the Convention completed its work. It provided sickness benefits to its members, supported widows and orphans, offered arbitration for disputes within the community, and organized the social networks that made survival possible for people who were excluded from the republic's formal mechanisms. It was, in the precise sense, self-governance: a community organizing its own affairs on the principles of consent and mutual obligation that the Convention was simultaneously writing into the law for a different population.

In 1792, Allen and Jones led their congregants out of St. George's Methodist Episcopal Church after the congregation's deacons, during a Sunday prayer service, pulled them from their knees in the gallery and told them to move to a section designated for Black worshippers. Allen's account of the moment is direct: "We all went out of the church in a body, and they were no longer plagued with us in the church." From this exit, Allen built the Bethel African Methodist Episcopal Church, which became the founding congregation of the first independent Black denomination in American history. The denomination Allen established still exists. The document Madison wrote still governs.

Both were founded on the same principles. Both are still in operation. This is not a coincidence.

## Jefferson in Paris

While the Convention was meeting, Thomas Jefferson was in Paris as the American Minister to France, writing letters to Madison about the politics of Versailles, the economics of the Ancien Régime, the beauties of the Maison Carrée in Nîmes, and the need for a Bill of Rights in the proposed Constitution. His letters are careful, brilliant, and alive with the pleasure of a mind in motion. He was also, in Paris, the enslaver of a fifteen-year-old girl named Sally Hemings.

Sally Hemings was the daughter of John Wayles, who had been Thomas Jefferson's father-in-law, which made her the half-sister of Jefferson's late wife Martha. She had come to Paris to accompany Jefferson's younger daughter. In France, she was legally free; French law did not recognize slavery, and enslaved people who reached French soil could petition for their freedom. She was young enough to be Jefferson's daughter. She was old enough to understand her situation. The modern scholarship on what followed — the DNA evidence, the documentary record, the testimony of their descendants — is unambiguous: Jefferson and Hemings had a relationship that lasted decades and produced children.

In 1789, Sally Hemings chose to return to Virginia with Jefferson. The specific terms of that choice are not recorded in her words, because no record of her words survives. James Hemings, her brother, had also come to Paris and had also learned French and the arts of French cooking; Jefferson eventually freed him, after extracting a promise that Hemings would train a successor at Monticello. Madison Hemings, the son Sally and Thomas Jefferson conceived in France, recalled in an 1873 interview that his mother had negotiated her return on the basis of Jefferson's promise to free their children when they came of age. Jefferson kept that promise for four of them. He sold others.

The Declaration had said: all men are created equal. Sally Hemings returned to Virginia in 1789, into the condition the Declaration described as tyranny. She was not, in 1789, a citizen of the republic whose foundational text said she was endowed with unalienable rights. She was property, in law, of the man who had written that sentence. The gap between the sentence and the law was not Jefferson's private hypocrisy. It was the republic's structural condition.

## The Machine Operates

The first decade under the Constitution was the decade in which the theory was tested against the fact. Washington's unanimity in the electoral college could not last beyond him; the question was whether elections could actually transfer power, or whether the republic would find ways to perpetuate whichever faction held it first.

Hamilton, as the first Secretary of the Treasury, moved quickly to establish the republic on a commercial foundation. He assumed the states' war debts, creating a national debt and with it a national credit. He chartered the Bank of the United States, providing a mechanism for monetary stability and government finance. He proposed protective tariffs and subsidies for manufacturing, a program he called the "Report on Manufactures" (1791), arguing that national power required an industrial base and that industrial development required government encouragement. Jefferson, reading from Monticello and then as Secretary of State, regarded this program as the corruption of the republic: it would create a class of financiers dependent on government favor, it would drive farmers off their land and into factories, it would reproduce in America the social conditions of England that the Revolution had been fought to escape. He was not entirely wrong. Hamilton was not entirely wrong. The argument between them — about what the republic's economy should be for and who it should serve — is still being conducted.

The Alien and Sedition Acts of 1798, passed by the Federalist Congress and signed by John Adams, made it a crime to criticize the government in print. Matthew Lyon of Vermont was sentenced to four months in prison for writing that Adams had "an unbounded thirst for ridiculous pomp, foolish adulation, and selfish avarice." He ran for reelection from his jail cell and won. Jefferson and Madison, responding to the Acts, drafted the Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions, arguing that states could nullify federal laws they deemed unconstitutional. The argument about states' rights and federal power that would eventually contribute to the Civil War had its first formal statement in Jefferson's response to the Alien and Sedition Acts, written while Jefferson was serving as Vice President of the United States under Adams, who had signed them.

The republic was arguing with itself before it was four years old. This was not a failure of the machine. The machine was designed for argument. Whether the machine could survive arguments that touched the founding compromise was the question it had not yet answered.

## The Election of 1800

The election of 1800 was the first transfer of power in American history. That it was a transfer at all — that the party that had lost actually ceded control — was not inevitable and was understood not to be inevitable by the people involved.

Jefferson and Aaron Burr, both Republicans, received the same number of electoral votes: seventy-three each. The Constitution sent the decision to the House of Representatives, where Federalists held enough votes to block either man. The balloting continued for thirty-six rounds across six days. Hamilton, who despised Jefferson's politics but distrusted Burr's character, worked behind the scenes to break the deadlock in Jefferson's favor. He wrote to Federalist representatives explaining that Jefferson, whatever his faults, had "pretensions to character" that Burr lacked. Burr, Hamilton wrote, was dangerous. The argument was sufficient. On the thirty-sixth ballot, Jefferson was elected.

Hamilton and Jefferson had been political opponents for a decade. Hamilton had spent the previous years systematically opposing Jefferson's vision of the republic. He chose Jefferson over Burr anyway, because Burr represented a kind of factional opportunism that Hamilton believed would destroy the institution rather than merely govern it badly. This is the action of a man who believed in the machine more than in his own faction's victory.

Four years later, Burr shot Hamilton dead in a duel in Weehawken, New Jersey.

The peaceful transfer of power in March 1801 is one of the founding era's most important events, and it is easy to underestimate because it happened. It was not easy. It was not certain. The republic transferred power from one political party to another for the first time, without violence, because the people who had lost believed — barely, reluctantly — that the machine was worth preserving more than they were worth keeping in power. Jefferson's first inaugural was a statement of republican generosity toward his opponents: "We are all Republicans, we are all Federalists." Adams did not attend the inauguration. He left Washington before the ceremony began. It was the last time he and Jefferson spoke for many years; they reconciled in old age and died on the same day, July 4, 1826, fifty years after the Declaration.

## Banneker's Letter

In August 1791, Benjamin Banneker wrote to Thomas Jefferson. Banneker was a free Black man in Maryland, a surveyor and astronomer, a maker of almanacs that had been published to wide acclaim. He had recently been part of the surveying team that laid out the boundaries of the new capital city at Washington. He wrote to Jefferson, who was now Secretary of State, to point out a discrepancy.

"Sir," he wrote, "how pitiable is it to reflect, that although you were so fully convinced of the benevolence of the Father of Mankind, and of his equal and impartial distribution of these rights and privileges, which he hath conferred upon them, that you should at the same time counteract his mercies, in detaining by fraud and violence so numerous a part of my brethren, under groaning captivity and cruel oppression."

He enclosed a copy of his almanac.

Jefferson replied promptly. He said that no one wished more sincerely than he did to see a proof that the Black man was on intellectual par with other colors of men. He said that Banneker's almanac was remarkable. He forwarded it to the Marquis de Condorcet in Paris as evidence. He did not address the discrepancy Banneker had named. He freed no one.

Three blocks from where the Convention had met four years earlier, Richard Allen was building his church. The congregation that would become the African Methodist Episcopal Church had gathered, had organized, had acquired a building, had installed Allen as its bishop. The church was doing what the republic had said it was for: self-governance, voluntary association, the free exercise of religion. It was doing it for people the republic had not yet fully included in its own mechanisms.

Both men — Banneker with his letter, Allen with his church — were using the republic's own language to argue for what the republic's own language promised. The argument was not new. It had begun with the document. It had not ended.

## What Was Built

By 1800, the United States was a functioning republic in a world that did not have many of them. The Constitution had survived the first decade. The courts were operating. Elections had been held. Power had been transferred. The Bill of Rights, which Madison had written without fully believing in its necessity, was in force. The national bank existed. The credit of the republic was established. The army was small and the republic was not yet a great power by the standards of France or Britain, but it was stable in a way that had not been guaranteed.

What was also built, inseparably: the legal infrastructure of racial slavery, extended and protected. The Three-Fifths Compromise in force. The slave trade running for eight more years, protected by Article I. The Fugitive Slave Clause in force, requiring free states to return escaped people to bondage. The southern states' structural advantage in the House and the Electoral College, purchased at the Convention, operating. The founding paradox was not merely a stated contradiction; it was an institution, with its own mechanisms and its own legal protections, and those mechanisms would require a catastrophic war to dismantle.

Madison finished his notes. He kept them secret for fifty years. When they were published in 1840, four years after his death, readers could see for the first time the arguments that had been made in that sealed room, the compromises that had been reached, the language that had been chosen. They could see Madison's own words: that slavery was a "moral and political depravity." They could read the record of the Three-Fifths Compromise being drafted by men who understood what they were doing.

The windows had stayed sealed. But the record was the record. The argument was the argument. And the people who had cleared the dishes and gone home to whatever home meant for them — their children and grandchildren and great-grandchildren were still making the argument, with Banneker's pen, with Allen's church, with the republic's own language turned back on itself, insisting on the sentence that Jefferson had written and the Constitution had not yet honored.

The experiment had begun. The results were not yet in.
