# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Panel Review, Round 2 (Notes)

**Text reviewed:** notes.md — The Lueji-Chibinda Foundation
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

The notes address my primary concern from Round 1 directly: Chibinda Ilunga's Luba identity must be "central," and he must be recognized "as Luba first, as a person second." The chapter's plan for Section II correctly identifies that "he is not a local man elevated; he is a bearer of a different kind of authority, recognized and incorporated." This is the right frame.

What I want the chapter to avoid in execution: the word "outsider." It appears nowhere in the notes, but it can easily slip into prose when writing about someone who comes from another people. Chibinda Ilunga is not an outsider who was adopted. He is a bearer of bulopwe who was recognized by a holder of the lukanu. These are two different characterizations. One positions him relative to the Ruund (as other, as foreign, then as incorporated); the other positions him relative to the authority structure (as a specific kind of person whom a specific kind of authority recognized). The chapter must sustain the second framing throughout.

The notes are correct that the brothers' multiple departure reasons should be honored as structural evidence of how the tradition navigates sensitive genealogy. This is exactly right. The chapter should do this explicitly: not pick one reason and hedge it, but present the multiplicity as the tradition's own epistemological practice.

One thing the notes do not address: Lueji's fate after the transmission. The notes say "the chapter will acknowledge the uncertainty." But the tradition is not entirely silent here. Lueji's lineage remains significant; the Mwaant Yav's mother (the Rukonkesh) and the senior queen (the Swana Mulunda or equivalent) hold specific political roles in the Lunda state. If these roles descend from or connect to Lueji's authority, the chapter should say so. Her authority is not simply transferred to Chibinda Ilunga — it persists alongside his in transformed form.

**Recommendation:** End Section IV not with Lueji's disappearance from the story but with a brief indication of how her authority continues — what role her lineage holds, what ceremonies she may still perform. The tradition's memory of female political capacity is what makes this founding distinctive; it should not be confined to the founding moment.
