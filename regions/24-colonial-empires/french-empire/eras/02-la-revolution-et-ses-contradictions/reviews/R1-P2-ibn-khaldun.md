# Panel Review — R1 / P-2 / Ibn Khaldun
## French Empire Era 2: La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

The opening has identified the right engine. The productive contradiction between universalist principle and colonial practice is not merely an irony — it is a structural force, a mechanism that generates events. The Declaration of the Rights of Man is not background to the Haitian Revolution. It is the cause. This is the correct analytical frame, and the opening establishes it efficiently.

**The system logic is well rendered:**

Moreau de Saint-Méry's 128 categories are a social taxonomy — not merely prejudice but a governance instrument, the means by which 32,000 colonists administered a labor force sixteen times their size. The opening correctly identifies this as a system: it has an internal logic, it produces outputs (sugar, coffee, profit), and it requires its own reproduction (the legal apparatus, the classification, the enforcement). The chapter ahead must trace how this system destabilized itself — not through external shock but through its own internal contradictions. The enslaved and the gens de couleur libres were the system's most skilled readers of the system's stated principles.

**The asabiyya question:**

What was the binding force of French colonial identity in Saint-Domingue? The 32,000 white colonists (grands blancs and petit blancs) were internally divided on virtually everything — economics, politics, their relationship to the metropole — except the maintenance of racial hierarchy. That shared interest was the asabiyya of the colonial class. The chapter should trace how the Revolution in Paris split that asabiyya: the grands blancs wanted local autonomy, the petit blancs wanted racial solidarity, the gens de couleur libres wanted legal equality within the existing racial order. Three competing groups, each with a legitimate claim within the Declaration's own language, and none of them able to hold the center.

**The cycles:**

I am interested in the speed of the cycle here. The revolutionary cycle in France accelerated from 1789 to 1799 — monarchy, Constituent Assembly, Legislative Assembly, National Convention, Thermidor, Directory, Consulate, Empire. In Saint-Domingue, the cycle ran in parallel but at a different tempo: slave insurrection (1791), abolition by the Convention (1794), Toussaint's quasi-independence (1798–1801), Leclerc's expedition (1801–1803), independence (1804). The chapter should trace these parallel cycles as related phenomena — what the metropole did determined what Saint-Domingue could do, and what Saint-Domingue did forced the metropole's hand.

**The question I'd push on:**

Napoleon's restoration of slavery in 1802 is the chapter's structural hinge — the moment the French imperial self-understanding chose the economy over the Declaration. How did the men around Napoleon understand this choice? Was it presented to them as a choice at all, or as a correction, a restoration of proper order? The chapter should render the decision from inside the Napoleonic administrative mind, not from the outside as betrayal.
