# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Songhai Empire / Era 2 — The River War
**Round:** 1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

The voice is right. That is the first thing to say, because if the voice is wrong, nothing else matters.

**What works:**

The opening tells the Songhai story from inside. Sunni Ali is not explained to a foreign audience. He is a river king in a world where rivers are power, and the reader either enters that world or does not. There is no hand-holding, no "to understand the significance of," no moment where the author steps outside to provide context for a reader assumed to be unfamiliar. This is correct.

The scholars' relationship with power is rendered without judgment. They sent word to Sunni Ali, asking for deliverance. They received something other than what they asked for. The opening does not take sides. It does not make the scholars wise and Sunni Ali crude, or Sunni Ali decisive and the scholars naive. It presents the encounter and lets the tension stand.

The Tuareg are present as actors in their own right: Akil ag Malwal held Timbuktu, governed it, made a tactical decision to withdraw. He is not a villain or a victim. He is a man who understood the situation and acted accordingly.

**What I would push on:**

"The scholars of Sankore and Djinguereber had sent word south to Sunni Ali at Gao, asking for deliverance." The word "deliverance" carries a connotation I want to examine. It suggests the scholars were passive, waiting for rescue. The Timbuktu scholarly community was not passive. They were political actors who chose to ally with Sunni Ali over the Tuareg for specific reasons: commerce, security, the possibility of patronage. The word should be adjusted to reflect their agency.

The Songhai cosmological world is thin so far. The river is rendered as strategic and military. But the Niger was also sacred to the Songhai. The Songhai relationship with the river had dimensions that went beyond the tactical. What role did the Niger play in their cosmology, their sense of identity? A river king is a good frame, but if the river is only a highway, we have tactics without culture. The chapter must eventually show that the river meant more than transportation.

**The question I'd push on:** Who are the Songhai to themselves? The opening tells me what Sunni Ali does. It does not yet tell me how the Songhai understood their own relationship to the river, to their ancestors, to the forces that governed their world. The culture must speak, not just the strategy.
