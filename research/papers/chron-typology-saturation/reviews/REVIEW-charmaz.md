# Review — Kathy Charmaz (constructivist grounded theory)

**Paper**: PR-A3 — Typology Saturation: Convergence Evidence in a Self-Evolving Quality Rubric
**Reviewer lens**: Theoretical saturation in grounded theory; what "saturation" means as an epistemological claim
**Score**: 2/4 (Major revision)

---

## Substantive review

I appreciate the authors' care in engaging the grounded-theory saturation literature rather than either ignoring it or collapsing it into the ecological model. §2.1 correctly distinguishes Glaser's and Strauss-Corbin's operational criterion from my own pragmatic framing in *Constructing Grounded Theory*, and §1 explicitly flags the normative claim — that one can only say one has "characterized" the domain if new discoveries have stopped. This is the right framing and I would not want the paper to abandon it.

My concern is that the paper then proceeds as if quantifying saturation and retaining its GT meaning are compatible operations, and I do not think they are — not without more argumentative work than §2.1 currently supplies. Theoretical saturation in constructivist GT is not the point at which *no new codes appear in the data*; it is the point at which new codes no longer materially alter the *analytic framework* the researcher is building. Those are different claims. In GT, two researchers can look at the same interview corpus, apply the same coding frame, and disagree about saturation because their theoretical interests differ — one might be saturated for a claim about identity and not yet saturated for a claim about institutional change. The paper's operationalization (count new typological entries, fit a rate curve) treats saturation as a property of the corpus, not of the analytic claim. This is closer to Glaser's data-driven posture than to the constructivist one I advocated.

The authors should either (a) claim they are operationalizing Glaser's (not Charmaz's) saturation and distance themselves from the constructivist framing, which would simplify the argument; or (b) explain what analytic claim the rubric is being saturated *for*. The Terminal Condition finding makes this question unavoidable: the typology is "not saturated" for discovering new terminal-condition types, but it may well be saturated for the claim the rubric is actually making ("LUCIA chapters have culturally-specific closure mechanisms"). Which saturation does the paper care about?

The second concern is related. The 80% falsification threshold reads, to a qualitative-methods reader, like an arbitrary number presented with quantitative confidence it does not earn. Why 80% and not 50% or 90%? Guest, Bunce, and Johnson's 12/60 ratio (which the authors cite) was derived empirically from their data, not pre-registered — and their paper is careful to note that the specific ratio is dataset-dependent. Pre-registering a threshold is better epistemic practice than fitting one post-hoc, but the threshold still needs a rationale. The paper would benefit from a paragraph in §4.6 explaining why 80% was chosen (was it based on Guest's implicit threshold? on ecological convention? on a desired Type-I error rate?). Without this, the threshold feels like methodological theater.

Third — and more constructively — the scale-dependent saturation finding is *exactly* the kind of result the GT literature has needed for a decade. Guest et al. (2006) found 12/60 in themes; my own work has suggested that saturation timing depends on conceptual depth; the authors' Voice-vs-Engine-vs-Terminal stratification gives this intuition a crisp structure. I would like to see §6.3 engage GT more, not less. The transportable-design-principle framing to "other AI domains" is fine but narrower than the finding deserves. Does the Voice/Engine/Terminal stratification mean that *any* GT study of cultural production should stratify by observational scale? That would be a genuine contribution to the qualitative methods literature and not only to the AI-rubric methods literature.

## Major concerns

1. **Constructivist vs. classical GT saturation is conflated.** §2.1 cites Charmaz alongside Glaser/Strauss-Corbin as if we all operationalize saturation the same way. We do not. Disambiguate which tradition the paper is extending, and revise §1 accordingly.
2. **The 80% threshold lacks rationale.** A pre-registered threshold is better than a post-hoc one but still needs defense. Add a paragraph explaining the threshold's source — empirical precedent, desired error rate, or convention.
3. **Saturation-of-corpus vs. saturation-of-analytic-claim is undertheorized.** The Terminal Condition finding forces the question and the paper should answer it. What analytic claim is the rubric being saturated *for*?

## Minor concerns

1. §2.1's treatment of my 2006 book is accurate but brief; the constructivist saturation criterion gets one clause. Worth a paragraph given the paper's positioning.
2. §6.3's design-principle framing undersells the potential GT contribution. The stratification-by-scale principle is applicable beyond AI rubrics.
3. "Pragmatic decision" (§2.1, attributed to me) is fair but the paper could note that pragmatism is context-sensitive, which further complicates the "bounded typology" claim.

**Verdict**: Major revision. The paper engages GT respectfully but insufficiently; the scale-dependent finding has more to say to my tradition than the current §6.3 acknowledges.
