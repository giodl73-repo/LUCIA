# Production Notes — The Lion's Time (Era 1)

## Structural Engine

The structural engine for this chapter is the **covenant logic of qualification through suffering**. In the Manden worldview, authority is not inherited automatically — it must be earned through trial. Sundiata's crawling years, exile, and return are not obstacles to his kingship; they ARE the process by which a legitimate king is made. Sumanguru's power, by contrast, comes from secret forge-knowledge held alone. The conflict at Kirina is not simply military — it is a contest between two theories of authority: covenant (public, earned through suffering, ratified by assembly) vs. sorcery (private, held through secret knowledge, imposed by force).

The Kurukan Fuga — the Great Covenant proclaimed after victory — is the structural resolution. It is not a reward for winning. It is the proof that the covenant theory of authority has prevailed. The chapter's arc: **unqualified boy → suffering → qualification → contest of legitimacies → victory → covenant**.

## Arc Design

1. **The Crawling** (~600w) — Sundiata's years in the compound. Sensory grounding in Niani. The mockery of Sassouma Berete. The iron staff. Focus: what the Manden requires of a king before it will accept him.

2. **The Exile** (~800w) — Sogolon's decision to leave. The road between courts. What Sundiata sees and learns. One vivid scene at the court of Mema. Focus: the education of exile — what a future king learns when he has nothing.

3. **Sumanguru's Sosso** (~700w) — The smith-king's power. The forge, the bala (xylophone), the occupation of the Manden. Not villainized — explained in terms of his own logic. Focus: the alternative theory of authority (sorcery/craft vs. covenant).

4. **The Return and Kirina** (~1200w) — The gathering of allies. The march. The battle itself — with the jeli's riddling logic (the spur of the white rooster, Sumanguru's weakness). The moment of decision. Focus: the battle as a contest settled not by numbers but by knowledge — the right knowledge, held by the right person.

5. **The Kurukan Fuga** (~1200w) — The assembly after victory. The covenant is spoken aloud. The social categories named. The obligations declared. Not a legal document — a covenant between free peoples, witnessed by the jelilu who will carry it. Focus: what the Manden built from the ashes of Sosso, and why it lasted.

6. **Landing** (~400w) — The jelilu carry the Kurukan Fuga forward. The covenant endures because it is spoken, not written — and what is spoken can be renewed. Close with the culture's own persistence mechanism: the jeli's voice.

## Key Figures

- **Sundiata Keita** — The Lion of Mali. Born unable to walk. Exiled. Returns. Defeats Sumanguru at Kirina. Proclaims the Kurukan Fuga. Historical figure whose life is carried by the jeli tradition.
- **Sogolon Kedju** — Sundiata's mother. From the land of Do. Makes the decision to go into exile. Present as an agent, not just a mother-figure.
- **Sumanguru Kante** — King of Sosso. Smith-king. His power comes from the forge and from the spirits. Not a villain — a king whose theory of authority is fundamentally different from the covenant model.
- **Sassouma Berete** — First wife of Maghan Kon Fatta. Wants the succession for her son Dankaran Touman. Her mockery of Sundiata is structurally necessary — it is part of the process of qualification.
- **Bala Faseke Kouyate** — The jeli. Captured by Sumanguru (who took him for his skill). Returns to Sundiata. The bala (xylophone) he plays becomes the Sosso Bala, still held by the Kouyate family.
- **Fakoli Koroma** — Sumanguru's nephew who defects to Sundiata's alliance. His defection is one of the triggers for Kirina.

## Sources and Their Limits

**Oral sources (primary):**
- D.T. Niane, *Sundiata: An Epic of Old Mali* (1965, from Djeli Mamoudou Kouyate)
- Fa-Digi Sisoko's version (recorded by John William Johnson)
- Wa Kamissoko's version (recorded by Youssouf Tata Cisse)
- Versions vary significantly — on chronology, on specific events, on the role of different clans. These variations are the tradition, not flaws in it.

**Written sources:**
- Ibn Khaldun, *Kitab al-Ibar* — written c. 1375, drawing on informants; gives a political genealogy
- Al-Umari, *Masalik al-absar* — written c. 1337-38; describes the Mali court from Cairene informants
- No contemporary written account of Kirina or the Kurukan Fuga exists

**Archaeological evidence:**
- Niani excavations (Filipowiak) — limited results; debate continues about whether this is the right site
- Material culture of the upper Niger region confirms the technology and trade networks described in oral sources

**Key scholarly works:**
- Levtzion, *Ancient Ghana and Mali* (1973) — political/economic reconstruction
- Conrad, *Sunjata: A West African Epic* (2004) — the oral tradition analyzed
- Jansen, work on the Kurukan Fuga and Mande political culture
- Austen (ed.), *In Search of Sunjata* (1999) — multiple scholarly perspectives

**Honest uncertainty:** The chronology of Sundiata's life is approximate. The Battle of Kirina is traditionally dated to 1235 but this is not fixed with certainty. The Kurukan Fuga is known entirely from oral tradition. No written contemporary source describes Sundiata's reign. This must be acknowledged — not as weakness but as the nature of the record.

## Voice Notes

This is the most oral-tradition era in the project. The Voice Spectrum guidance for oral-tradition cultures: "Cadence of the spoken word. Repetition as structure. Proverb-weight sentences. Phrasing that sounds right when read aloud."

The jeli's voice should be audible throughout — not as quotation, but as a shaping presence. The prose should carry the rhythmic patterns of the epic tradition: paratactic construction, repetition with variation, proverb-like formulations.

Em-dash budget: 8 maximum in the full chapter. Use sparingly.

## Forbidden-Word Watch

No: primitive, advanced, civilization (as compliment), discovered by, the New World, tribe, Dark Ages. Also: "constitution" for Kurukan Fuga (use "covenant"). "Mythical" for Sundiata (use "carried by the jelilu"). "Evil" for Sumanguru. "Griots" only if the Mande term "jelilu" has been established first.
