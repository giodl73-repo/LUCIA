## B-2 — W. G. Lambert — Board Review of Hammurabi's Babylon (Era 1)

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Editor of the critical edition of the Enuma Elish; specialist in Babylonian theology, creation myths, and the divine assembly.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The summary of the Enuma Elish is accurate in its essential narrative: Tiamat as chaos-monster, Marduk's offer to fight in exchange for supremacy, the splitting of Tiamat's body, the creation of humanity from Kingu's blood, the fifty names. Correct.
- The statement that Marduk "had been a minor deity" and rose with Babylon's political fortunes is broadly accurate but slightly overstated. Marduk appears in earlier texts, including the god-lists, and was not negligible even before the First Dynasty. His elevation to head of the pantheon is tied to Babylon's rise, but he was not entirely obscure beforehand. A minor nuance.
- The Akitu description is correct in its ritual structure. The humiliation of the king, the restoration, the renewal of mandate, the recitation of the creation epic.
- The statement about "blank years" when Assyrian kings did not perform the Akitu is accurate and reflects the Babylonian chronicle tradition.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter presents the Enuma Elish as performed theology, not as myth, and the Akitu as cosmic mechanism, not as ceremony. This is the correct approach. The Babylonians did not distinguish between ritual and reality in the way the chapter's presentation reflects.
- The relationship between theological and political claims (Marduk's rise = Babylon's rise) is handled without reducing either to the other. Correct.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The Enuma Elish is integrated into the political narrative rather than presented as a separate "religion" section. This reflects how the Babylonians experienced it: theology and politics were the same field.
- The Akitu as "the mechanism by which the cosmos renewed itself" is precisely the right framing.

**What needs correction:**
- As noted above, the dating of the Enuma Elish's composition is uncertain. My own work suggests that while the epic's theological claims served the First Dynasty's political interests, the text as we have it may have been composed or substantially revised later (Kassite period or later). The chapter should acknowledge this uncertainty. A phrase such as "composed or consolidated during this period" would suffice.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (one minor dating qualification)
