# R1-P4 — Wade Davis

**Opening reviewed:** Brazil, Era 2 — A Colônia — Açúcar, Ouro, e Escravos
**Round:** Panel-1

---

What strikes me immediately is the epistemological claim at the center of this opening: the axé could not be confiscated because it was not a thing. It was a quality of sacred power that moved through persons, through practice, through sound and rhythm and the body's learned responses. This is a sophisticated claim about the nature of a knowledge system, and it is made without condescension or explanation. The chapter does not say "the enslaved people maintained their cultural traditions" — which is the museum-plaque formulation, the outside view. It says the axé arrived intact. From inside. As a fact about the world.

I spend considerable time in my own work on the difference between knowledge systems that live in objects and texts, and those that live in persons and practices. The latter are both more resilient and more fragile than they appear. More resilient: they cannot be destroyed by burning books or demolishing temples. More fragile: they are exactly one generation from silence, always. If the people who carry the knowledge die without transmission, the knowledge dies with them. The chapter's hook — "the answer was already in the drum" — gestures toward this. The chapter needs to make it explicit: Palmares was not just a political community. It was a transmission mechanism. Its survival mattered not only to the people who lived there but to the knowledge system they were keeping alive.

**What I want to watch for:** I am alert to the ways in which African religious traditions in the diaspora are romanticized — presented as perfectly preserved, unchanging, recovered whole. The Candomblé of Bahia is not the Candomblé of Lagos. It is something new, something that happened in Brazil, under specific conditions of suppression and synthesis. The opening is careful not to claim perfect continuity; it says the axé arrived "intact," which is a defensible claim about the core transmission, not about the forms. The chapter will need to honor the transformation as well as the continuity.

The capoeira detail is the best single moment in the opening. "Which looked, to a Portuguese observer, like dancing — which was the point." This is knowledge systems as epistemological camouflage. The colonizer's misreading was structural to the practice's survival. I want the chapter to explore this further: what other forms of knowledge looked, from outside, like something else? What did the Portuguese think Candomblé was? What did they actually see when they looked at a terreiro?
