## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — PANEL-2 Review of Notes

**Overall:** The sensory plan is now visible. Section 2 (The Forge) and Section 4 (The Mountain Pass) are designated as primary sensory sections. Good. But I want more.

**What works:**
- "The forge's heat IS the economic argument" — this is sensory-analytical fusion stated as a planning principle. If the chapter delivers on this, the forge section will be exceptional.
- The section plan recognizes that every section needs a sensory anchor. The awareness is there.

**What I would push on:**
- Section 3 (The Persian Road) is planned as a linguistic and structural section. But Persia is one of the most sensory landscapes on earth: the plateau's dry heat, the caravanserai's shade, the smell of copper and charcoal in the metalworking quarters of Persian cities. The linguistic evidence should be embedded in the physical experience of being in Persia, not separated from it. When the travelers acquire the Persian word for "marketplace" (a word I would want to know), they acquire it because they are standing in a Persian marketplace. Let the word arrive with the smell.
- Section 5 (The Arrival) in the Balkans: the landscape changes again. The Balkan forests, the first European rain after the dry plateau. The travelers encountering stone churches, the sound of Greek liturgy, the visual shock of Byzantine mosaics for people who have been walking through predominantly Islamic lands. The sensory shift marks the cultural shift.
- The camp. Where is the camp? These people made camp every night for three centuries. The nightly routine of the traveling group — the fires, the horses tethered, the children asleep in the wagon, the watchfulness of a people who are always guests and never hosts — this is the physical reality of the lungo drom. At least one section should end with the making or breaking of camp.
