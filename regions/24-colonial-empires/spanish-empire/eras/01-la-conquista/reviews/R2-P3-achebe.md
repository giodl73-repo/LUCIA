## On the Inside Voice Through Violence

The notes correctly identify the danger: the chapter must stay inside the Spanish theological framework even when describing the violence. Las Casas is the answer to this problem — he is inside the framework, he uses the framework's own moral vocabulary, and he describes violence as a believer describing the violation of his faith's own standards.

The chapter should use a specific passage from the *Brevísima relación*. Las Casas is not abstract. His account is full of specific detail — specific places, specific acts, specific numbers. The chapter should not reproduce his account wholesale, but should find one scene — perhaps his account of the punishment of the Taíno cacique Hatuey, burned at the stake, offered baptism at the last moment, asking whether Christians go to heaven, being told yes, and saying he would rather go to hell — that can be quoted or paraphrased in a way that places the reader inside Las Casas's register: the horror of a believer watching his faith's name being used for this.

The Hatuey scene is important because it presents the indigenous response entirely from within the Spanish account — and yet the indigenous logic is clear: Hatuey has correctly understood what Christianity means in practice, on this island, and drawn a rational conclusion. Las Casas understands this. He records it. His theology requires him to find it damning.

## On Guaman Poma de Ayala

The notes identify him correctly. The chapter should use him as the closing figure, not the Valladolid Debate. He is the better landing: a man who wrote 1,200 pages, in Spanish, to the King of Spain, arguing for his people's rights using the King's own legal and theological framework — and whose letter was filed, lost, and found three hundred years later in Copenhagen.

The notes' open question about the landing should be resolved in favor of Guaman Poma. The archive image is correct, but the Guaman Poma image is more powerful: the letter arrived. It was received. It was filed. It was lost. It was found three centuries later by a Danish scholar in a library. The empire documented everything — including the argument that it was doing wrong. The argument was there all along. No one answered it.

This is the landing. Not summary. Not the archive in the abstract. The specific letter, in Copenhagen, not answered, and the man who wrote it, who died before it arrived.

## The Sentence to Watch For

If the chapter writes "they were innocent" without attributing the word to Las Casas's theological vocabulary, that is the line that must be cut. Las Casas's use of *inocentes* is a legal-theological term: they had not committed the offense that would justify war against them. It is not a modern moral evaluation. The chapter must keep that distinction.

## The Question I'm No Longer Pushing On

The Moctezuma section: the notes plan this correctly. Davis's concern about rendering both worldviews in the same scene is valid. The chapter's task is not to give equal weight — that would produce a false symmetry. It is to render the Mexica worldview as coherent on its own terms, without placing it in comparison or opposition to the Spanish worldview. They are not competing; they are simply different, and the meeting of those differences is what the scene contains. The notes' formulation "the meeting of those two incomprehensibilities" is correct. Keep it.
