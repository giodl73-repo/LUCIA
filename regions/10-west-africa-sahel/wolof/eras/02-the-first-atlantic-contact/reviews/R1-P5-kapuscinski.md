# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Wolof / Era 2: The First Atlantic Contact
**Round:** 1 (Opening review)

## What Works

The opening puts me on the beach. I can feel it. The horses stumbling on sand, legs stiff from weeks at sea, nostrils wide, flanks dark with sweat. This is a body arriving in a place, and the reader's body arrives with it. The Portuguese ship is not an abstraction; it is a vessel that has made animals sick with its motion, and the evidence of the crossing is in the horses' unsteady legs.

"They had seen horses before" is a good grounding line. It tells me the Wolof are not impressed. The horses are familiar objects being assessed, not marvels. The lack of wonder is itself a sensory detail, a temperature of the encounter.

The material goods are named with specificity: salt, dried fish, cotton cloth, gold dust, kola nuts, horses, iron, cloth. These are not generic "trade goods." They have weight and texture. The reader can feel the economy because the economy is made of things.

## What Needs Work

**The beach disappears.** After the first paragraph, I lose the physical setting entirely. Where does the negotiation happen? On the beach? In a compound? Under a shade tree? What is the temperature? What does the river mouth look like, smell like? The Senegal River delta is a specific environment: tidal flats, mangrove, the smell of brackish water, the heat reflecting off the sand, the birds. I should hear those birds. The opening gives me the beach in paragraph one and then moves to a conceptual space where trade axes and political geometries exist without physical bodies or physical ground.

**The Burba Jolof is disembodied.** He receives a message and "considered this." Where? In what kind of space? What does the Jolof court look like? What does the architecture of power look like in the inland Sahel? The Mali chapter puts me in Niani, at the gate, with the mud-brick and the harmattan haze. I need the same physical specificity for the Burba Jolof's seat. Give me the compound. Give me the shade. Give me the heat of the interior, which is different from the heat of the coast.

**The ambassadors-to-Lisbon passage has no body.** "Wolof ambassadors had traveled to Lisbon, had been received at the Portuguese court." This is reported, not felt. What did Lisbon feel like to a Wolof noble? The stone buildings, the cold (if it was winter), the strange food, the smell of a European city, the sound of church bells. Or if the opening cannot sustain a scene here, give me one physical detail: the ambassadors' robes in a Portuguese stone hall, the temperature difference, the sound of a language no gewel had ever learned. One detail. The moment is too important to exist only as a summary.

**Smell is absent.** The opening has sight and touch but no smell. The Senegal River delta smells of salt water and mud and drying fish. A Portuguese ship smells of tar and bilge. Horses smell of sweat and manure. The Sahel smells of dry grass and wood smoke. Give me one of these in the first three paragraphs.

**The closing paragraph is too abstract.** "The axis had shifted. The ocean was open." These are ideas, not sensations. The closing needs to return to the physical: the horses being led up from the beach, the sand under their hooves, the sound of the surf behind them, the inland road ahead. Let the abstraction live in the image, not in the statement.

## The Question I'd Push On

Where are the fishermen? The Wolof coast was a fishing coast. When the Portuguese ships appeared on the horizon, the first people to see them would have been the men in pirogues, the fishing boats that worked the coastal waters. What did a Portuguese caravel look like to a man in a dugout canoe? Give me that moment. Not the political calculation, not the buur's assessment, but the fisherman in his boat, looking up, seeing something he had never seen before. That is where this story begins, physically, before it becomes a negotiation. And it would give the chapter a witness who is not an elite.
