# Production Bible — Nafarroako Erresuma
## The Kingdom of Navarre (c. 824–1234 CE)

---

## 1. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The *foru* as covenant-not-grant — the legal mechanism by which any ruler of the Basque country is required to swear, on Basque ground, to honor pre-existing Basque law as the condition of governance. Not a privilege conferred by a sovereign but a condition imposed on sovereignty.

**Mechanism:** The king must travel to Guernica (or the relevant provincial assembly). He stands in Basque space, not his own. He swears before the gathered community. The oath is binding because the community's prior claim is prior to the king's arrival. If he violates the oath, *foruaren haustea* — the breaking of the *foru* — releases the community from any obligation of loyalty. The mechanism makes Basque political legitimacy depend on the continuous performance of consent, not on conquest or inheritance alone.

**Why it works:** The ecological base of the *foru* is the valley community's need to manage shared resources — pasture, forest, iron ore deposits, water rights, fishing access — in conditions where no single authority can impose rational allocation. The *foru* is the solution to a collective-action problem specific to mountain pastoralist communities with mixed-economy livelihoods (herding, iron, fishing, some agriculture). It predates any kingdom. The kingdom is the latest political form built on top of the *foru*, not the source of it.

**Terminal condition:** The *foru* engine's vulnerability is that it requires the king to care enough about Basque cooperation to make the journey and swear the oath. As Navarre is absorbed into French dynastic networks after 1234, the kings are increasingly remote — French-speaking, Paris-centered, physically absent from the Basque lands. The mechanism requires a present king who values Basque territory. When the kingdom's political center moves north of the Pyrenees, the covenant becomes harder to enforce without military capacity the Basques do not have. This sets up Era 4 (Castilian Integration) where the *foru* survives by finding new oath-takers in the Castilian crown rather than through the Navarrese dynasty. The terminal condition of this era is specifically: **legitimacy gap** — the mechanism requires a king who will come to Guernica, and the French-succession Navarrese kings do not consistently come.

**Voice register:** Mountain-laconic. Short declaratives with the weight of stone. The voice of people who know a thing by living in it, not by reading it. Sentences placed like footholds on a ridge. Zero em-dashes (voice discipline requires it). Closest analogy: Saga-Spare, but warmer and more declarative; less interested in recording absence and more interested in the persistence of presence. A new register — call it **Foral-Mountain** until further calibration.

## 2. Sources for This Era

**Primary:**
- *Fuero General de Navarra* (13th century) — the major Navarrese-Basque law code; will be used to anchor the *foru* mechanism in documented practice rather than assertion
- Frankish Annals (Annales Regni Francorum) — the Frankish account of Roncesvalles; used for what the Franks saw, not for what the Basques did
- Arab chronicles (al-Bakri, Ibn Hayyan) — Umayyad accounts of Navarre as a border polity; useful for external perception of the kingdom's status
- Sancho III's charters (c. 1000–1035) — administrative documents showing the extent of his territory and the nature of his relationship to surrounding kingdoms

**Secondary:**
- Collins, Roger. *The Basques* (1986) — foundational; primary scholarly account
- Kurlansky, Mark. *The Basque History of the World* (1999) — accessible secondary, good on the *foru*'s social logic
- Trask, R.L. *The History of Basque* (1997) — linguistic evidence for language isolate status and archaic vocabulary

**Source asymmetry note (v1.5 technique):** The Basque side of Roncesvalles has no contemporary written record. The Frankish side has the Annales. The Arab side has al-Bakri and Ibn Hayyan. The Basque oral tradition of the event survives in fragmentary form, mediated through later Navarrese chronicles. The chapter will use the **twin-source narration** technique: Frankish sources for the material event (the ambush, its tactical logic), Basque legal tradition for the interpretive framework (why the Basques acted, what it meant to them). The gap between these two source traditions is itself evidence: the Basques had no need to write down what everyone who lived there already knew.

## 3. Key Figures

**Iñigo Arista (c. 824–851 CE)**
First king of Pamplona, or rather: first person recognized by external powers as king of the region that would become Navarre. His name is itself a hybrid — Iñigo (Basque) Arista (possibly Latin or Iberian). He held Iruñea against both Frankish pressure from the north and Umayyad pressure from the south by playing them against each other. He was not, by the records, a great warrior or a great administrator. He was a great survivor. The chapter should render him as a pragmatist of the mountain kind: the man who holds what he holds by understanding where the pass is narrow.

- Primary source: limited; appears in Frankish annals as a regional lord who caused problems; in Arab sources as a client who sometimes paid tribute and sometimes did not
- Honest limits: we do not know what Iñigo called himself; "king" is a later attribution; "Lord of Pamplona" is closer to what he likely claimed

**Sancho III the Great (c. 992–1035 CE)**
The peak of Navarrese power. His territory at his death stretched from the Atlantic to the borders of Castile and Aragon; he styled himself "Emperor of Spain" — a claim that was audacious and brief. His reign is the moment when the Kingdom of Navarre was the largest Christian state in Iberia, and it lasted approximately one generation. His will divided the kingdom among his heirs, producing the fragmentation that would ultimately make Navarre a dependency of France and then of the Spanish crown.

- The chapter's structural engine provides the context for why Sancho's "empire" could not consolidate: the *foru* mechanism, which works at the valley and provincial level, resists the centralization that a larger state requires. You cannot have a strong central monarchy and a covenant-with-each-valley simultaneously.
- Primary source: his charters are extensive and shed light on his administrative style
- Figures note: the fragmentation at his death is not presented as a personal failure but as the logical consequence of a political tradition built on distributed covenant rather than hereditary accumulation

**García Ramírez "the Restorer" (1134–1150 CE)**
After Navarre's absorption into Aragon under Alfonso I "the Battler," García Ramírez reestablished an independent Kingdom of Navarre. He is the figure who demonstrates the *foru*'s persistence through dynastic disruption: the kingdom can be extinguished and re-lit because the covenant mechanism doesn't depend on the dynasty. The kingdom is not the *foru*; the *foru* is the condition that makes any kingdom possible.

- Figures note: García Ramírez is not a widely documented figure in popular history; the chapter should treat him honestly as someone who held together a fragile restoration rather than a triumphant reconstitution

**The unnamed bertsolar at the assembly**
Not a historical individual but a composite figure drawn from the *bertsolaritza* tradition: the improvisational oral poet who competed at public gatherings and in doing so preserved the community's memory, legal precedents, and cultural identity. The chapter should use this figure to embody the knowledge-transmission system — showing how the *foru* was remembered and transmitted in a community that did not rely on written text for its primary legal culture.

- Figures note: the bertsolar tradition is documented from the medieval period onward; individual names from the ninth through thirteenth centuries are not preserved; the composite figure is ethno-historically valid and will be disclosed as such in figures.md

## 4. Narrative Arc — Section Plan

**Section 1 (Opening integrated): The Pass, c. 778 CE**
Opens with Orreaga as the opening.md establishes. Integrates the sensory fixes from Kapuściński (sound of the approaching rearguard, smell of August at altitude) and the Tuchman fix (more specific presence of the Basque watcher). Closes with the poem's erasure as the founding condition.

**Section 2: Before the Kingdom, c. 600–824 CE**
The period between the Visigoths and Iñigo Arista. The Basques resisted Visigothic integration; the Wascones of Gascony were a persistent problem for Merovingian Franks. The *foru* tradition as it existed before the kingdom had a name. What the valley assemblies did, how decisions were made, how the oath-system functioned at the sub-kingdom level.

Key technique: show the *foru* operating at method level — a specific assembly, a specific dispute, the process by which the community reached a decision. Not described but enacted on the page.

**Section 3: Iñigo Arista and the Kingdom's First Form, c. 824–900 CE**
The pragmatics of founding a kingdom that can sit between two greater powers without being absorbed. The Basque-Umayyad relationship: paradoxical, often cooperative at the practical level despite nominal hostility. Iñigo playing Frankish pressure against Umayyad pressure. The kingdom as a buffer state that acquired its own legitimacy through persistence.

Key technique: show Iñigo through what he does (hold Iruñea, negotiate, survive) rather than what sources say he was. Honest limits on his interiority.

**Section 4: Sancho III and the Peak, c. 1000–1035 CE**
The moment of maximum Navarrese territory. Sancho's administrative achievements: his coordination of the Camino de Santiago pilgrimage route through Navarrese territory, which made Navarre rich through pilgrim traffic and gave the kingdom a role in the trans-European cultural network of Western Christendom. The Camino as the Navarrese version of a trade route — not commercial goods but prestige and political connection.

Key technique: the Camino de Santiago is a sensory opportunity — the pilgrimage road, the hospices, the sound and smell of thousands of travelers passing through, the mixed languages, the coins from a dozen kingdoms. Basque geography as an asset (the road through Navarre is the most accessible Pyrenean crossing from France).

The fragmentation: Sancho's death and the division among his heirs as the structural consequence of the *foru* mechanism — you cannot consolidate what is held by covenant.

**Section 5: The Kingdom's Slow Passing, c. 1076–1234 CE**
After Sancho III, Navarre passed through Aragonese dominance, restoration under García Ramírez, and eventual French dynastic absorption through the marriage of Sancho VII's heiress to Count Theobald of Champagne in 1234. The kingdom did not fall by conquest. It married away.

The *bertsolaritza* tradition as counterpoint: while the kingdom's political center moved toward France, the cultural transmission system — oral poetry, the assembly tradition, Euskara — held. The chapter shows both the political arc (the kingdom passing from Basque hands) and the cultural persistence (what the kingdom was a vessel for: still intact).

**Landing:**
The chapter closes not with 1234 but with the *foru*. When Theobald of Champagne rode to Iruñea to claim the inheritance, he swore to the *foru*. He swore in a language he did not speak, before a people who had been there before his name existed, under a covenant older than the kingdom he had just married into. The oath was the price. He paid it. The mountain does not care who crosses it, but those who want to stay must swear.

## 5. Panel Pressure Points (Anticipated)

**Tuchman's concern:** The human figure in the opening. Fixed: the opening revision will give the Basque watcher on the ridge a name and a specific action. Candidate: draw from the oral tradition's "man of the ridge" figure; alternatively, use the figure of Sancho VII's representative at the 1234 succession as a frame and flash back.

**Ibn Khaldun's concern:** Why did the kingdom become necessary? The chapter will address this in Section 2 — the transition from informal valley-assembly resistance to a named kingdom with a recognized ruler is driven by the external pressure of two simultaneously demanding powers (Carolingian Franks and Umayyad Córdoba), both of which needed to deal with a single counterpart. The kingdom is the Basques' answer to the administrative demand of their neighbors: "Who do we negotiate with?" The Basques invented a king who could answer that question without surrendering the *foru*.

**Achebe's concern:** Whose telling? The chapter uses the source-asymmetry-as-technique approach: the Frankish record tells us what happened at Orreaga; the *foru* tradition tells us what it meant. The chapter will be explicit about this without apologizing for it.

**Davis's concern:** Show the *foru* at method level. Section 2 will do this: one specific assembly, one specific disputed matter (inheritance of a valley right, perhaps), the process by which the community reached a decision without a king. The *bertsolaritza* figure in figures.md will also address this — showing how the knowledge system worked at the level of practice.

**Kapuściński's concern:** Sound and smell at Orreaga; physical image of the *foru* oath. Both integrated into the opening revision. Section 4 (the Camino) is the chapter's richest sensory opportunity.

## 6. Open Questions

- **What specific legal dispute is documented in the Fuero General de Navarra that could illustrate the assembly process in action?** To be researched for Section 2. Collins (1986) likely has material.
- **Was the oath at Guernica performed in Euskara, Latin, or both?** The language of the oath is itself a political statement. This matters for the voice section.
- **What did Sancho III's relationship to the Camino de Santiago look like administratively?** He is credited with rerouting the Camino through Navarrese territory; the specifics of this administrative act would strengthen Section 4.
- **Is there surviving Basque oral material specifically about Orreaga / Roncesvalles?** Even fragmentary. Collins would know. The Lelo song and Altabizkar Kantua are later (19th century) but claim older roots — need to handle this carefully so as not to use romantic-era forgeries as medieval evidence.
