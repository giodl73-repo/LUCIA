# The Network's Height
## Kuikuro | Era 2: c. 1200–1500 CE

---

The road wants clearing again.

Iauatá can see it even from the edge of the plaza: the fringe of new shoots along the western margin, the way the young cecropia is already reaching its pale arms over the cut edge, feeling for the light that the road's opened corridor provides. Two weeks since the last crew worked it. The forest moves fast in the rains. If you stop cutting, the road stops being a road within a season. It becomes a memory of a road, and then it becomes forest, and then no one knows it was ever there.

He calls to the younger men. They come with their stone blades and their fire-hardened wooden tools, still eating from the morning's manioc cakes. The road crew is not ceremonial. There is no song for clearing roads, no ritual preparation. You wake up, you eat, you cut. The road does not care about ceremony. The road cares only about being a road.

They walk the corridor west. It runs straight. This is the thing about the road between the two towns, the thing that a stranger would notice first, if a stranger ever came this way, which they do not. The road runs straight through forest that does not want to be straight. The forest is always trying to be curved, growing in arcs toward the light, following the rivers' bends, tangling around its own fallen weight. The road cuts against the forest's nature. Someone chose this line. Someone maintained this line, generation after generation, and that choosing, that maintenance, is the fact of the road: not the soil beneath it but the decision to keep it open.

Iauatá is not thinking about any of this. He is thinking about the Quarup.

The ceremony is three moons away. Kuhikugu is hosting. Kuhikugu, the largest of the cluster towns, the one whose plaza can hold all the visiting groups at once, whose chief's house faces east across a space large enough for the wrestling matches and the mourning songs and the two days of dancing that accompany the egitsü. That is the Kuikuro name for the ceremony the whole network calls the Quarup, after the sacred hardwood log that stands at its center. The chief who died last dry season, Iauatá's own mother's brother's father-in-law, a lineage connection complex enough that Iauatá has spent time thinking through exactly how he should mourn, will be honored there. The egitsü for him will close the period of grief. The network will affirm itself.

This is why the road matters. Not in the way that Iauatá is thinking about it. Roads are simply infrastructure, they require maintenance, you clear them as you clear the fish weirs after the rains. The road matters in what it represents: a commitment. Every community in the network that maintains its road is announcing, in the medium of cleared earth, that it intends to arrive. The Mehinako arrive from the east along their road. The Kamayurá come from their forest to the south. The Kalapalo, the Aweti, the Wauja, each community's road is the physical expression of its intention to remain part of what they are all part of together.

The road crew reaches the worst section, where a tall Inga tree has dropped a branch across the corridor. The branch is half-rotted but still heavy, and the understory has grown up around it in the weeks since it fell, threading vines through the bark and seeding in the damp shadow underneath. They spend an hour on this section. The work is sweaty and specific: hacking the vine stems, rolling the branch to one side, cutting back the new growth to re-expose the straight line, the intentional line, the line that says someone lives at both ends of it and values the connection enough to keep it open.

When they finish, Iauatá stands at the cleared section and looks west toward Matuwa, the neighboring town. On a clear day, from the canopy's height, you would see the smoke of their morning fires. Here on the road, you can only see the corridor running forward through the green wall of the forest, the light at the end of it pale and inviting. The road looks like it leads somewhere important. It does. It leads to people who will come to the egitsü. It leads to the wrestling matches at which Kuhikugu's young men will prove themselves, at which the visitors' young men will prove themselves, at which the whole point of being a people with other peoples will be proven again, in public, through the body, through song, through the careful movement of food and gifts between the anetü of one town and the anetü of another.

It leads to the network, which is not a word any of the people of the upper Xingu use, because they do not need to name what simply is.

Three moons. The road needs to be clear when the visitors come. Iauatá picks up his blade and moves to the next section.

## The Garden Beneath the Garden

The manioc comes out of soil that is not like the soil anywhere else.

Tüke knows this the way she knows the difference between the cry of the blue-and-yellow macaw and the scarlet macaw before she sees either one: not as a fact she learned but as a thing her hands understood through practice. The earth in the old garden is dark. Dark in a way that the pale lateritic soil at the garden's edge is not, in a way that the forest floor with its thin layer of leaf rot is not. This dark earth holds water differently. It crumbles between the fingers in a certain way. When she pulls a manioc tuber from it, the root is large, full, dense. The same plant in the pale soil at the edge grows thin.

Her mother's mother gardened here. Before her, her mother's mother's mother. The dark soil exists because of them, because of generations of women who added to this earth what the earth needed: the charcoal from slow-burning fires, the smoldering organic waste that produces black carbon rather than white ash; the bones of the animals eaten at feasts; the fish waste from the weirs. Not all at once. Steadily, season after season, worked in with wooden paddles while the garden was being prepared for planting. The soil does not know it is being built. It simply becomes what it is through what it receives.

Tüke can read the soil the way the hunters read the river. The dark earth, itá in Kuikuro, has specific properties that she has learned to attend: how wet it stays after rain, what plants volunteer in it versus what must be planted, how far the roots of the manioc push down before they find the pale soil below. At the garden's margins, where the dark earth thins, she works extra charcoal in before planting. She watches where the growth comes up strong and where it comes up weak, and she amends accordingly. This is not guesswork. It is centuries of accumulated observation held in the hands of women who have never written anything down, who have maintained the most productive soil in the upper Xingu basin for as long as anyone's grandmother's grandmother can remember.

The dark earth is the foundation of everything. The Quarup feast that will be held at Kuhikugu in three moons requires large quantities of manioc, processed into cakes, into the fermented drink called caxiri, into the thick paste that feeds the visitors who travel the roads from their own towns. That manioc comes from this soil. This soil exists because someone enriched it. The enrichment goes back further than any ceremony: before the Quarup was the Quarup, before Kuhikugu was Kuhikugu, before the roads ran straight between the towns, the dark earth was being built in the gardens of the upper Xingu. The ceremony rests on the soil. The soil was here first.

Tüke reaches the end of the dark section and the blade of her wooden planting stick hits the pale subsoil with a different resistance. She notes where the boundary is. Next season, she will work the charcoal further out. The garden expands one season's work at a time. Her granddaughter will plant in soil that she is building now without knowing it, because the charcoal and the bone and the care go into the earth and they stay.

## Arrival

They begin coming three days before the egitsü.

The Kalapalo arrive first, which is fitting. Kuhikugu and the Kalapalo towns have maintained their roads in both directions for longer than any living person can remember. Their canoes come up the tributary in the morning light, the men paddling, the women sitting with the gifts bundled in woven mats and the children watching the unfamiliar riverbanks. At the Kuhikugu landing, people are waiting. There is no formal greeting yet, that comes at the plaza, but there is the particular sound of a community receiving kin: recognition, the quick establishment of who has arrived, the questions that begin before the visitors have fully stepped ashore.

Then the Mehinako, coming along their eastern road, their feet knowing the path. Then the Kamayurá, then the Aweti. Then smaller groups, families that have married across community lines and who belong to both sides of the gathering, moving between their obligations with the ease of those who are always in transit between two homes.

Kuhikugu does not normally hold all of these people at once. The town's resident population, several hundred, becomes something different when the visitors swell it. The plaza fills. Cooking fires are lit around the plaza's edge, each community maintaining its own fire, its own circle of sleeping mats, its own pattern of evening conversation. The sound of the upper Xingu network in assembly is the sound of at least four language groups talking at once, with the trading language that most adults know running underneath as a current that carries information between fires.

The anetü of Kuhikugu has been preparing for weeks. His obligation is the feast: the large quantities of food and the caxiri that must be available for the duration of the ceremony. The Kuikuro phrase for the chief's responsibility here is approximately: he who feeds the guests is he who holds the people together. Not a metaphor. A description. The guests come because the feast will be there. The feast is there because the anetü organized the gardens, the fishing, the hunting, the processing of manioc into the forms that can be served in quantity. If the feast fails, if the caxiri runs out before the ceremony is complete, if the food is inadequate, the failure belongs to the anetü, and the failure is public, witnessed by every community in the network. The anetü who cannot give, cannot lead.

The visiting anetü arrive with their own gifts: feathers from the harpy eagle and the blue-and-yellow macaw, woven cotton goods, pottery. These are not payment. They are the other half of the exchange that begins when the host anetü feeds the guests and continues through the ceremony until the visiting anetü present their gifts at the formal moment when the two parties acknowledge each other in front of the assembled network. The exchange is specific. The anetü of the Kalapalo gives specific things to the anetü of Kuhikugu, and the anetü of Kuhikugu receives them and knows what he owes in return: not at this ceremony, but at the next one, when another community will host and Kuhikugu will be the guest. The obligations travel with the community from Quarup to Quarup, and the anetü who keeps track of them is the anetü who understands the network's full extent.

## The Hüge Prepares

Kairü has known the songs for the dead chief since before the death.

Every chief of the network's major towns has songs that belong to them: songs that were composed, or emerged, or were recognized as appropriate at some earlier moment in the lineage's history. These songs are the hüge's responsibility. The hüge, the song-leader, does not own the songs. He carries them. He is the vessel the songs travel through on their way from the moment of composition to the moment of performance, and from performance through the generations of hüge who will come after him and carry them further.

The egitsü for the dead chief requires the correct songs. Not similar songs. Not songs that capture the spirit of what the chief was. The specific songs, performed in the correct order, with the correct intervals, the correct tonal variations that signal the transitions between the ceremony's phases. If the songs are performed incorrectly, the egitsü does not close the grief. The mourning period extends. The dead chief's spirit remains in proximity to the living community rather than completing its journey. The community cannot fully return to its normal obligations until the egitsü is properly closed.

Kairü has been rehearsing for three months, since the moment the anetü confirmed that Kuhikugu would host. He sings the songs in the morning before the community is awake, alone at the eastern edge of the plaza, facing the direction from which the dead chief's spirit approaches the ceremony. He sings them through, from beginning to end, noting the places where his voice stumbles on the tonal shift, where the interval between the second and third section feels slightly wrong. He adjusts. He sings them again. A hüge who is uncertain at the moment of performance will hesitate, and hesitation is visible. The assembled network has heard these songs before, at other egitsü for other chiefs, and they know when something is wrong.

There is a second song that has not been performed in living memory. The dead chief had a particular relationship with one of the forest beings, and the hüge's tradition preserves the song composed for this relationship, a song that belongs to this specific lineage rather than to the general ceremonial repertoire. Kairü is not certain of the third interval. He sits with the uncertainty for a day. Then he goes to his own teacher, who lives in the neighboring town and who is old enough to have heard this song performed at the previous generation's egitsü. They sit together in the afternoon with the forest behind them and they go through the third interval until Kairü can hear it correctly. His teacher does not tell him what the interval should be. He sings the interval, Kairü listens, Kairü sings it back, and they adjust until both agree it is right.

This is what the hüge knows: not written, not diagrammed, not reducible to instruction. It lives in his ear and his voice. It will live in his student's ear and voice after him. The songs are as old as the lineage they belong to, and they travel forward through time in exactly this way, corrected at each generation by the accumulated consensus of people who remember what they should sound like.

## The Ceremony's Work

The egitsü begins at nightfall on the second day of the gathering.

The quarup log stands in the center of the plaza, cut from hardwood, decorated with the geometric patterns that are his ceremonial identity, wrapped in the feathers that mark his status. The log is him. Not a representation of him. Him, present in the form the ceremony requires, for these three days during which the living and the dead are in proximity. The communities have arranged themselves around it. The fires burn at the plaza's edges. The smell of the roasting fish and the manioc paste and the caxiri warming in the clay vessels is part of the ceremony's atmosphere, not separate from it: the smell of abundance at night is itself an announcement that the host has fulfilled his obligation.

Kairü begins the mourning songs.

The first song establishes the dead chief's name: not his daily name, but his ceremonial name, the one that belongs to the lineage and that he carried in ceremony. The name is sung three times. On the third repetition, the assembled community joins, not all at once, but in the way that water finds its level, the voices arriving at the song from their different distances until the song is being held by everyone at once. The women who knew the chief well are weeping. Not quietly. The egitsü does not ask for quiet grief. It asks for grief expressed, held up, witnessed by the community, acknowledged. The weeping and the song are simultaneous. This is the ceremony's first work: to name the dead, to cry for them in public, so that the grief does not remain trapped in individual bodies but moves through the community and is shared.

After the mourning songs, the wrestling.

The transitions in the egitsü are not abrupt. The mourning moves toward silence, and in the silence the first pair of wrestlers enter the plaza. Young men from Kuhikugu, one of them the dead chief's nephew, who will wrestle the first match of the day in his uncle's name. Wrestling is not combat. It is not war. The men clasp each other in the specific holds that the upper Xingu style requires and they push against each other, reading each other's weight, finding the leverage point, until one is on the ground. The match is short. The community watches in the same silence they gave to the mourning songs, and then there is a sound from the crowd that is not quite a cheer: closer to a collective exhalation, a release of something that had been held.

The wrestling matches continue through the morning. Every community that has sent wrestlers has its young men test themselves against the others. The ranking that emerges, who beat whom, by what margin, with what technique, is information that the network carries away from the ceremony. Status among young men tracks closely with the status of the communities they represent. The wrestling is not purely ceremonial. It is a medium for communicating information about the network's relative capacities, information that is difficult to communicate any other way. The community whose young men win consistently is announcing something about itself.

By midday, the formal exchange between the anetü begins. The host anetü receives the visiting leaders at the eastern edge of the plaza. They come in the order of their proximity to Kuhikugu: nearest communities first, most distant last. Each visiting anetü presents what he has brought, the feathers, the cotton goods, the pottery. The host anetü receives each gift and sets it to one side, not inspecting it, not commenting on it. Inspection comes later. At this moment, the form matters more than the content. The visiting anetü has given. The host has received. The obligation has moved.

What the network would not survive is an anetü who arrives without gifts. This happens, occasionally, a community that has had a bad season, that lost fishing gear in floods, that could not prepare the cotton goods in time. When it happens, the anetü arrives anyway, because not arriving is worse than arriving without full gifts. The host receives him as he receives the others, but the difference is visible, and the network registers it. The obligation from this moment is larger. The community that could not give must give more at the next opportunity. The exchange system runs on trust that obligations will be honored over time. It is tolerant of individual seasons of hardship. It is not tolerant of communities that consistently take without giving. A community that breaks the exchange cycle without cause eventually finds that its road is not worth clearing.

## What the Road Carries Home

The quarup log is taken from the plaza and brought to the water. The dead chief's spirit has been named, mourned, honored, released. The mourning period is over. The community can return to its normal obligations. The visiting communities take their roads home with the gifts the host anetü has arranged: not the gifts they brought, but the host's reciprocation, carried in the same woven mats, adding to the obligations that will be settled at the next ceremony.

At Kuhikugu, the plaza empties over the course of the day. By evening, the resident community is alone again. The fires at the visiting circles are cold. The sound of the four languages has resolved back into Kuikuro. The anetü walks the plaza at dusk and registers what the ceremony cost: the manioc flour, the smoked fish, the caxiri, the labor of the women who processed it all. He registers what it produced: the renewed obligations, the wrestling results that he will need to remember, the specific gift from the Kalapalo anetü that carries a particular meaning he is still reading. He has run a ceremony that has redrawn the network's social map. All the lines are now confirmed, the broken ones acknowledged, the strong ones celebrated.

At the landing, the last of the Mehinako canoes has pushed off into the tributary. The paddlers are already talking about something else: the trip back, the fish in the lower channel, the work that has accumulated while they were traveling. The ceremony recedes into the past with the same speed with which the daily work recedes. You hold the ceremony in mind as long as you need it. Then you put it down and pick up the day.

In the upper Xingu, in the twelve moons between one egitsü and the next, seven roads need maintaining. Seven communities need to grow their manioc, process their cotton, smoke their fish, prepare what they will bring when it is their turn to arrive. The network lives in the interval between ceremonies, in the specific work of the specific roads that connect the specific towns that have decided, generation after generation, that the connection is worth the work.

Something is coming that the network was not built to survive. Three thousand kilometers to the east, on islands the upper Xingu peoples do not know exist, a different people has arrived on ships, carrying in their blood a suite of diseases for which the forest peoples have no immunity. The diseases move faster than the people who carry them. They move along trade routes and river systems and roads. They arrive in communities before the news of them does. In twenty years, or thirty, or forty — the timing is not precise — the first wave will reach the upper Xingu and find a population whose generations of managed landscape and ceremonial maintenance have made them dense enough for epidemic transmission to work with terrible efficiency.

No one at Kuhikugu knows this. The anetü does not know it. Iauatá does not know it. Tüke does not know it. Kairü does not know it.

The road is clear. The garden is dark and rich. The songs are correct. The network is whole.

## Landing

The Inga branch is gone from the corridor.

Iauatá's crew took it out in the weeks before the egitsü, rolled it to the side of the road where it will slowly become the forest floor, its wood becoming the soil that becomes the next generation of trees that will eventually need to be cut back from the corridor's edge. The road that runs between the two towns is cleaner now than it was. The pale light at the end of the cleared section is a little brighter. Someone walks it almost every day: a trader carrying cotton thread, a family visiting kin, a young man traveling to see the wrestling style of another community's best men before he faces them at the next Quarup.

The forest presses. The road presses back.

The road does not care about ceremony. The road cares only about being a road.

What is remarkable, though the people who built and maintained it would not use that word because they were simply doing what needed to be done, is how long it held.
