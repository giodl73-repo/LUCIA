The notes name three physical spaces that the chapter will inhabit. Good. But the notes are vague about the Venetian Arsenal and the Florentine banking bench. Let me be specific about what I need.

**The Arsenal:**

The Venetian Arsenal in the late 13th and early 14th centuries was the largest industrial complex in Europe. At its peak it employed thousands of workers — *arsenalotti* — who were organized by trade: caulkers, carpenters, riggers, oarmakers, sailmakers. The chapter needs to put the reader inside ONE shed, watching ONE stage of construction, following ONE worker through ONE day. Not a description of the Arsenal as an institution — a body in a shed.

What does it smell like? Pitch, burning in the great cauldrons for caulking the hulls. Pine resin. Salt from the timber soaking in the canal that runs through the Arsenal's center. What does it sound like? The rhythmic mallets of the caulkers, who worked in crews that sang to synchronize their blows. What does the finished galley look like at the stage when it emerges from the shed and the rigging crew takes over? The chapter should be able to answer these questions from the sensory record, not from institutional description.

**The Florentine banking bench:**

The *banchieri* — the money changers from whom "bank" derives — sat at wooden benches in the Mercato Nuovo and the Mercato Vecchio. They had actual tables with actual cloth covers (the *banco* was a table). Coin was sorted, weighed, and valued in public. The exchange rates were posted. The Bardi and Peruzzi had offices off the main trading floor — more private, but physically connected to this street-level activity.

The chapter needs at least one moment where the reader is at the bench: the coins on the cloth, the sorted piles, the clerk checking a bill of exchange against the firm's records, the handshake that completed a transaction. This is where the financial revolution is physically located — not in abstract accounting but in human bodies around a wooden table.

**The Genoese colony:**

Caffa, on the Crimean coast, is the furthest physical extension of the Italian commercial world. If the chapter can put the reader inside a Genoese *fondaco* in Caffa — the smell, the languages, the goods piled in the warehouse — even briefly, it will give the reader a physical sense of how far this network extended. This is worth 150–200 words. It is the edge of the world the chapter is describing, and the reader should feel that edge.
