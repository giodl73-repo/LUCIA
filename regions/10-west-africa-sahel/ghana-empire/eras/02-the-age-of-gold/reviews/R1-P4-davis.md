# Panel Review — R1-P4 — Wade Davis

**Chapter:** Ghana Empire — Era 2: The Age of Gold (opening.md)
**Voice:** Wade Davis — Anthropological wonder, hidden condescension, worldview depth
**Stage:** PANEL-1

---

The sacred grove passage in the opening is handled with appropriate restraint: "the sacred grove held its darkness behind the palace wall." The darkness is not mysterious in a romantic sense; it is simply the shade of trees that no one cuts. This is the right approach. The grove does not need to be explained or wondered at. It simply is. The merchant cannot enter, which means the reader cannot enter, which is exactly correct.

The two gestures of recognition — dust-pouring and hand-clapping — are a small but significant act of equivalence. The writer does not explain either one. Both are just things people do when the Ghana sits in judgment, and both mean the order holds. This is anthropological honesty: not reducing one gesture to a translation of the other, but allowing both to occupy the same space and carry the same weight.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter will need to give the sacred knowledge system the same depth it gives the commercial knowledge system. The tax structure, the toll rates, the nugget-vs-dust distinction — these are rendered with specificity and insider logic. The rites of the sacred grove, the Ghana's covenant with the land and the forces beneath it, the priests' knowledge — these must receive equivalent treatment. The sacred system is not decoration for the commercial one. They are the same system, operating in two registers simultaneously. The chapter must show both registers with equal analytical care.

In particular: what does the Ghana's covenant actually mean to the Soninke in terms of how they understand where gold comes from? This is not a superstition to be noted. It is an epistemological framework to be inhabited.
