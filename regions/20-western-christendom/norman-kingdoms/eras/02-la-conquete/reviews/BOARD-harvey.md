## Overall Assessment

My area is the Domesday survey and its context, and I can say that the Domesday section of this chapter is the most accurate and culturally honest narrative treatment I have seen of what the survey actually was and what it actually did. The standard treatment presents the Domesday Book as a remarkable achievement of Norman administration. This chapter asks the harder question: remarkable for whom, and at whose cost?

## Domesday Methodology — Accuracy

**The questions are correctly rendered.** The six standard questions (who held TRE, who holds now, how many villagers, smallholders, slaves, how many hides, what is it worth now, what was it worth in King Edward's time) are accurately represented in simplified form. The chapter does not enumerate all of them but captures the structure: past versus present, the TRE comparison as the survey's organizational spine.

**The organization is correct.** By county, then by tenant-in-chief, then by subtenants. The Winchester manuscript (Domesday proper, as opposed to the Exeter satellite) is organized exactly this way. The chapter describes this accurately.

**The Æthelric scenario is historically sound.** The chapter correctly identifies that English smallholders appeared before commissioners and testified through interpreters. The detail that Æthelric held "of the sheriff" is consistent with a documented Domesday tenure category for English holders whose tenures were insecure under the new regime. The figures.md disclosure of the composite technique is appropriate and honest.

**One nuance to add (not a correction, a refinement):**
The chapter says "The village knew what it held. The document said what it held." This is beautifully stated, but it could acknowledge one additional fact: the document also said what the village USED to hold, and that comparison was often more devastating than the current holding. Many Domesday entries record holdings that had been larger TRE and were smaller now, with the gap visible in the text. The survey's TRE column is itself an indictment, written into the document by the same commissioners who recorded the dispossession. This is optional additional content that the author may or may not wish to include; it is not a correction.

## The Salisbury Oath

The chapter correctly notes the Salisbury Oath and its unusual character (direct oath to the king from all significant landholders, bypassing the normal feudal chain). The date is correct: 1086, following the Domesday survey. The interpretation is accurate: William was securing direct personal loyalty from every significant landowner in England.

## English Landholding After the Conquest

The chapter's treatment of English landholders under Norman rule is consistent with the scholarly record. The category of tenants holding "at the will of the sheriff" or "at the king's will" is documented. The loss of English land is understated in the chapter (which is appropriate for space), but not misrepresented. By 1086, English landholders above the village level had largely been replaced by Normans except in a few specific cases and in the church.
