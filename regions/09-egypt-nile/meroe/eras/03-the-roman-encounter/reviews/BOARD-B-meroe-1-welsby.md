## BOARD — B-meroe-1 — Derek Welsby — Review of Meroe Era 3

**Overall:** The chapter's use of archaeological evidence is strong and generally accurate. The material culture of Meroe is well represented. A few points of precision.

**Accuracy confirmed:**
- The bronze head's discovery by Garstang in 1910, its burial beneath the temple threshold, and its current location in the British Museum are all correct.
- The description of the iron smelting complex (eastern edge of city, slag heaps visible from a distance, charcoal from acacia woodlands) is consistent with the archaeological record.
- Naga and Musawwarat es-Sufra are accurately described. The three-headed, four-armed Apedemak at Naga is correct. The debate over Musawwarat's function is honestly represented.
- Qasr Ibrim's description (cliff fortress, sandstone, chokepoint) is accurate. The site's role as a frontier garrison with mixed Meroitic-Roman material culture is correct.
- The description of Meroitic pyramids (smaller, steeper than Egyptian) is accurate.

**Corrections needed:**
1. The chapter states the furnaces were "clustered on the eastern edge of the city, upwind." The prevailing wind direction in the Meroe region varies seasonally. The furnace location is correct (eastern/southeastern), but the wind explanation is a simplification. Consider: "clustered on the eastern edge of the city, away from the royal quarter." **Severity: Minor.**

2. The chapter describes the elephants at Musawwarat as "African forest elephants, smaller than the Indian elephants the Ptolemies had tried and failed to use in battle." The Ptolemaic use of African elephants (likely the African forest elephant, Loxodonta cyclotis) in battle is documented, but the characterization as "tried and failed" oversimplifies. The Ptolemies used African elephants at the Battle of Raphia (217 BCE) against Seleucid Indian elephants, and the African elephants were reportedly intimidated. But this is a single engagement. The Ptolemies continued to maintain elephant stations. Consider: "African elephants, smaller than the Indian elephants that Seleucid armies used." **Severity: Minor.**

**Cultural authenticity assessment:** The chapter treats Meroe as a state of genuine depth, not as a satellite of Egypt or a curiosity for Roman ethnographers. The archaeological evidence is used to establish Meroitic sovereignty, not to illustrate a Roman narrative. This is the correct framing. The material culture (iron, script, temples, pyramids) is presented as evidence of what Meroe was, not as evidence that Meroe was "surprisingly" sophisticated. Approved.

**Verdict:** 2 minor corrections. Chapter approved.
