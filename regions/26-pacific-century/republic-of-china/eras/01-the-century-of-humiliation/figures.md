# Key Figures — The Century of Humiliation

## Lin Zexu (1785–1850)

The scholar-official who understood his task completely and executed it with integrity. Lin rose through the examination system the way the system intended—studying the Classics, demonstrating moral virtue, showing that he understood governance through the application of moral principle rather than force. By his sixties, he had governed provinces and managed crises. When the Qing court appointed him Commissioner of Canton to solve the opium problem, he brought the same method that had worked in every provincial crisis before: study the problem, create protocols, issue proclamations, negotiate, enforce the law.

What Lin could not have known was that the protocols of the Qing did not apply to the British. His assumption—that the rules he made for his own territory would be enforced in his own territory—was reasonable and false. He burned 1,200 tons of opium and started a war he did not intend to fight.

After the war, he was exiled to a minor provincial post five thousand li away from the coast. He never returned to positions of real power. The court had removed the man who had exposed the system's limits, trying to forget the problem he had tried to solve. Lin spent the last sixteen years of his life in administrative work that did not matter, in a place where no one cared about the great things he had tried to do.

His grave is in Fujian province. For most of the twentieth century, Chinese schoolchildren did not learn his name; the century of humiliation was treated as shameful, not as something with heroes. Late in the twentieth century, his reputation was revived. He is now seen as a patriot who stood up to the British, even though he lost. The irony is that he understood the situation perfectly well—he just lacked the power to change it.

## Cixi (Tzu Hsi), The Empress Dowager (c. 1835–1908)

A woman who entered the imperial household as a concubine and, through intelligence and political acumen, became the de facto ruler of China for nearly fifty years. Cixi bore the Xianfeng Emperor a son, the Tongzhi Emperor, and when the Xianfeng Emperor died in 1861, she became regent for her young son. When the Tongzhi Emperor died without an heir, she placed her nephew, the Guangxu Emperor, on the throne and continued her regency.

Cixi understood power. She had seen the Summer Palace burn. She knew that the Qing could not defeat the foreign powers militarily in the 1860s, and she made the decision to negotiate rather than fight. She supported the self-strengthening movement, understanding that military capacity was necessary. But she also understood that creating an independent military bureaucracy would threaten her control. So she navigated between them: yes to modernization, but not so much as to diminish imperial authority.

Her reversal of the Hundred Days Reform in 1898 is often portrayed as conservative obstruction, but it was something more coherent than that. She understood that the reforms, if implemented, would create a system in which the throne had less direct control over the selection of officials. She chose imperial survival over systemic transformation. This choice was politically intelligent and dynastically catastrophic. By preventing the system from adapting on its own terms, she ensured that the adaptation would come through the system's dissolution.

In her final years, she attempted reforms again—abolishing the examination system, creating new ministries, promising constitutional government. But these changes, coming after she had prevented the more coherent reform effort, were too late and too partial. She died in 1908, a few hours before the Guangxu Emperor died. Within three years, the dynasty had collapsed.

Cixi was not wise and not foolish. She was a political operator who understood the constraints she faced and made choices within them. The choices were intelligent given what she knew. They were catastrophic given what she could not know—that the system could not be preserved by limiting its transformation.

## The Guangxu Emperor (1871–1908)

Placed on the throne by Cixi when he was a child, he became the figurehead for hopes of fundamental reform in his early twenties. The Guangxu Emperor had been reading the works of Kang Youwei and other reformers. He believed that the system could be saved through comprehensive transformation—if the examination system was modernized, if new institutions were created, if the government structure was rebuilt, the Qing could survive in the modern world.

In 1898, with the support of reformers, he issued a series of edicts attempting to implement these changes. It lasted one hundred days. Cixi saw the reforms as a threat to her power and reversed them. The Guangxu Emperor was placed under house arrest and confined to the imperial compound for the rest of his life.

He never wielded real power again. He is less a person in the historical record than a crystallization of the reform question: can the system change without destroying itself? His life answered: not under Cixi's control. The system required either her departure or the emergence of a different kind of power—the kind that would not respect the throne at all.

## Foreign Figures (Britain, France, Russia, Japan)

The foreign powers did not appear in Beijing as individuals, but as representatives of states with interests. The British and French merchants wanted access to markets. The British government wanted trade. The Russian government wanted territory. The Japanese government wanted to prove it was a modern power equal to Europe. Each had their own logic; each pursued their own interests. They did not all want the same thing, but they shared an interest in imposing unequal treaties on the Qing that would guarantee their advantage.

What is important to understand is that they were rational actors pursuing rational interests. They were not evil; they were not trying to destroy Chinese civilization out of malice. They were trying to advance their own interests, and they had the military power to make those interests override the Qing's objections. This is the actual structure of imperialism—not malice, but asymmetry of power combined with rational pursuit of advantage.

From the Qing perspective, the foreign powers were incomprehensible because they did not operate according to the logic of the tributary system or the rules of the examination bureaucracy. They simply decided what they wanted and took it. The Qing's repeated attempts to negotiate, to create protocols, to establish rules for engagement simply did not register. The foreigners had already decided what the rules were.

## The Boxers (c. 1898–1900)

A rural movement that emerged from the north China plain, mixing martial arts training, popular religion, and a deep rejection of foreign presence. The Boxers were young men, often from poor families, who practiced boxing styles and saw themselves as defenders of traditional Chinese culture against foreign invasion.

The Boxers understood the world differently than the court understood it. The court was trying to figure out how to govern a nation while respecting the foreign powers' presence and power. The Boxers were saying: no, we will not accept this. We will fight. The movement had its own logic, its own understanding of what China was and should be.

By 1900, the Boxer movement had grown into something the Qing court could not ignore. Cixi, having failed with the self-strengthening military and having suppressed the reform effort, saw in the Boxers a last hope: perhaps this popular movement could do what the court's armies could not. She gave the Boxers implicit imperial backing.

It was a catastrophic miscalculation. The Boxers were defeated with ease by the international force that relieved the siege of the foreign legations in Beijing. The defeat of the Boxers demonstrated that neither the court's modern military nor a popular movement could resist the foreign powers' organized force.

From the foreign perspective, the Boxers were primitives trying to resist modernity with martial arts and superstition. From the Boxers' perspective, they were defending their homes and their culture against invasion. Both perspectives were coherent and both were inadequate to the actual situation, which was one of pure asymmetry of power.

## The Generation of Reformers (Kang Youwei, Liang Qichao, and others)

The young intellectuals and officials who, in the 1890s, began to argue that the Qing system could be saved through comprehensive reform. They had read foreign philosophy, studied foreign political systems, and concluded that the Qing could adopt the forms of modern governance while maintaining Chinese culture and imperial authority.

Kang Youwei proposed a radical reinterpretation of Confucianism in which the Master's teachings were compatible with constitutional government and scientific knowledge. The Guangxu Emperor listened to these reformers. For one hundred days, they had the ear of the throne.

The reforms failed, but the reformers' ideas did not disappear. They would reemerge in the twentieth century, modified and adapted, as Chinese intellectuals continued to grapple with the question the century of humiliation had posed: what must China become in order to survive?
