# Goryeo — The Buddhism-State

We are Goryeo — the state that gave Korea its name to the world, that produced the Tripitaka Koreana, that survived three Mongol invasions through endurance and negotiation, and that created celadon porcelain of such beauty that even the Chinese Song dynasty connoisseurs praised it as the finest in the world. We called ourselves Goryeo — a name derived from Goguryeo, deliberately recalling the northern kingdom's grandeur — and we unified the peninsula after the Later Three Kingdoms period (918 CE) by absorbing the last Silla court and conquering Hubaekje. Our founder Wang Geon was a Buddhist general whose vision of the state was inseparable from his vision of the dharma.

We hold the dharma as the skeleton of our state. The Goryeo kings believed that Buddhism protected the nation — the Tripitaka, the complete Buddhist canon carved on wooden blocks, was commissioned twice: first in the eleventh century (destroyed by the Mongols), then again in the thirteenth century in response to the Mongol invasions, as the most important act of national preservation possible. The Haeinsa Tripitaka blocks, carved between 1236 and 1251 while the Mongols occupied the mainland, still survive: 81,258 blocks, over 52 million characters, the most complete Buddhist canon in the world. We carved the dharma while the enemy burned our country.

## What Defined Them

**Goryeo Buddhism and state protection.** The Goryeo state organized itself around the belief that the Buddhist dharma protected the nation. The great national prayer monasteries (hoguksa) were built at the capital and throughout the country; the examination system had a separate track for Buddhist clergy; the royal family took Buddhist vows and sponsored translations and ceremonies. This was not merely piety; it was governance theory: a well-functioning Buddhist institutional network was the strongest possible protection for the state.

**The Tripitaka Koreana.** The Haeinsa Tripitaka — 81,258 woodblocks, carved between 1236 and 1251 while the Mongols were occupying the mainland of Korea — is one of the most extraordinary achievements in Korean history. The first Tripitaka (commissioned 1011) was destroyed by the Mongols; the second was commissioned as a national act of dharmic protection. The precision of the carving (scholars estimate there are almost no errors across 52 million characters) was itself a form of prayer: correctness as sacred act. The blocks have survived intact for eight hundred years at Haeinsa temple.

**Goryeo celadon.** Goryeo ceramic art produced celadon porcelain — the jade-green glazed stoneware — of a quality that Song Chinese connoisseurs praised as superior to their own. The signature technique of inlaid celadon (sanggam), in which designs are inlaid in white or black slip before glazing, was a Korean innovation. The imagery — cranes, clouds, willows, fish, water plants — expressed a Buddhist and poetic sensibility that was distinctly Korean in its refinement. Goryeo celadon is among the most valuable ceramics in the world today.

**Mongol invasions and endurance.** Three major Mongol invasions (1231, 1232–1259, and continued pressure afterward) devastated the Korean mainland. The Goryeo court retreated to Gangwha Island, separated from the mainland by a shallow strait, and endured. The Mongols could not easily operate against islands; the Goryeo resistance, though it could not expel the invaders from the mainland, preserved the court's independence. The eventual submission to the Mongol Yuan was negotiated, not unconditional: Goryeo maintained its own king, laws, and customs as a Mongol tributary state, and Goryeo princes intermarried with Mongol royalty — a status that gave the later Goryeo court unusual access to Yuan China.

**The aristocracy and its conflicts.** Goryeo was governed by a small hereditary aristocracy — the gwanbum (official families) who held land and office across generations. The military coup of 1170, in which the military officer Jeong Jungbu and others killed the civilian officials who had humiliated them at a royal banquet, began a century of military dominance that paralleled the Japanese shogunate in some ways. Under the Choe military regime, the king was a figurehead and the real power lay with the military strongmen — until the Mongol invasions created a crisis that the military could not resolve through force.

## The Chapters

**1 — Wang Geon:** The general who will be the Goryeo founder accepts the abdication of the last Later Three Kingdoms rival. He has spent his career fighting; he will spend his reign building. The Ten Injunctions he leaves to his successors: how to govern, how to honor the dharma, how to maintain the capital. A founder's wisdom, for those who will listen.

**2 — Goryeo Buddhism:** A monk completes the commentary he has been writing for a decade. He rolls it and places it in the monastery archive. Scholars from the Song dynasty will come to copy it. The great Buddhist thinker Uicheon is producing a synthesis that draws on traditions from across Asia. Goryeo is the center of international Buddhist scholarship.

**3 — The Celadon Kiln:** The master ceramicist is mixing the glaze. He has spent twenty years achieving the right shade of jade green. The inlay design has been prepared: cranes ascending through clouds. This piece will be presented to the king.

**4 — Mongol Invasion:** The Mongol cavalry is burning the towns on the mainland. The court is on Gangwha Island. The Tripitaka commission has received its instructions: carve the complete Buddhist canon, every character correct. The carving will save Korea.

**5 — Yi Seonggye Turns:** The general is at the Wihwa Island ford in the Yalu River. He has been ordered to invade the Ming. He considers. He turns his army around. He marches back to Kaesong. The Goryeo dynasty ends when his army enters the capital. He will name the new dynasty Joseon.
