# PANEL-2 Review — P3: Chinua Achebe (Targeted)
## Mexico Era 4 — El Porfiriato y La Revolución

**Focus:** Cultural voice, indigenous specificity, ownership of the story

---

The notes are substantially stronger on indigenous specificity than the opening alone suggested. Naming Anenecuilco as a Nahua village, connecting the milpa to Nahua agricultural knowledge, and anchoring the close in the village council are all right decisions. The corrido as the surviving form of Zapata's memory — distinct from the muralist-nationalist absorption — is the correct instinct for the landing.

One refinement on the Achebe concern: the murals of Rivera, Orozco, and Siqueiros are referenced in sources (Anita Brenner). The chapter should not ignore them, but it should be clear about what they did and did not do. The murals gave Zapata a heroic national image while simultaneously incorporating him into a state narrative that served the PRI. The corrido tradition kept a different Zapata: more specific, more local, more insistently about the land rather than the symbol. The chapter should hold this distinction without editorializing — show the two forms of memory, let the reader feel the difference.

On the soldadera question: the notes are right to be cautious about inventing biography. The better approach is what you name as "composite figure honestly disclosed" (Figures v1.1 amendment). Use the corrido "Adelita" as the literary form and give one actual soldadera from the Zapatista campaign a named presence if the record supports it. María de la O (a Zapatista soldadera from Morelos who survived and gave testimony later) is documented. Her presence in the chapter would give the soldadera tradition a body without fabrication.

The Yucatán dimension: one paragraph in Section 2 is correct. What the chapter should make clear is that the Maya workers of the henequen plantations are not an aside — they are proof that the Porfiriato's dispossession was not limited to the Nahua villages of Morelos. The continental scale of the same system — and the continental scale of the revolution that resulted — is part of the structural argument.

Final note: the chapter's landing should not give Zapata the last word. It should give Anenecuilco the last word. The village council still exists. The claim is still alive. Let the village speak, not through the corrido alone (which is Zapata's myth), but through the community that made him their instrument.
