## Re-Explanation Check

Principle: if the scene showed it, the prose should not then explain what it showed.

**Review of high-risk moments:**

1. **Opening through shabono section:** The opening shows Aramo's work as maintenance. The old woman's look-and-look-back is the most efficient image of this. The revised opening (removing the "outsider" paragraph) now ends: "It was all one system, and Aramo was attending to the part of it that no one else in the shabono could reach." — This sentence explains what the old woman's glance already showed. It is borderline re-explanation. However, it is the first occurrence of the chapter's engine-statement and it is brief; it functions as a pivot rather than an explanation. Acceptable as written.

2. **Feast section:** "The feast was not a transaction. It was a thickening of the thread." — This summarizes what the preceding paragraph showed. However, the metaphor ("thickening of the thread") does new work — it gives the reader a concrete image they did not have before. The distinction between "transaction" and "thread-thickening" is substantive, not merely restating. Acceptable.

3. **Reahu section:** "This was not a celebration of a system. It was the operation of the system." — Not present in the chapter. No re-explanation at this point. Good.

4. **Landing:** "The knowledge was alive because people were alive who held it. While they held it, and passed it on, and held it again, the world it described held together." — This is the chapter's only explicit thesis statement, delivered once, in the landing, after the full chapter has earned it. It is not re-explanation; it is the distillation of what has been shown, placed at the moment when the reader can hear it with full weight. Correct.
