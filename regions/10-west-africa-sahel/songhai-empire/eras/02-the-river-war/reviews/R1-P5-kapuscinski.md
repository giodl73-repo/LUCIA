# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Songhai Empire / Era 2 — The River War
**Round:** 1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

I can hear the water. That is a good beginning.

**What works:**

The paddle-rhythm reaching the city before the canoes do. This is a sensory detail that earns its place because it establishes the medium: sound travels on water differently than on land. The grey light before dawn. The dust of the Tuareg retreat thinning and vanishing. These are specific, grounded, and they put me on the bank of the Niger at a particular hour.

"Horses could not stand on water." I feel this. The frustration of a cavalry force watching a fleet they cannot intercept. The helplessness has a physical quality: the horse at the water's edge, the canoes out of reach. The tactical point is made through a bodily reality.

The final image of Sunni Ali looking west, toward Djenne, toward the war that is not finished, is spatial. I know where I am on the map. I know where the story is going. Geography has become narrative.

**What I would push on:**

The heat is missing. This is the Sahel. The dry season. The temperature at dawn on the Niger in March or April is already twenty-five degrees, and by midday the air shimmers. The opening has light and sound but not temperature. I need to feel the air thicken as the sun rises. I need the sweat on the paddlers' arms. I need the contrast between the cool of the river and the heat of the bank.

The smell is missing. A river this size has a smell: the wet clay of the bank, the vegetation of the floodplain, the particular scent of stagnant water in the channels of the inland delta versus the clean current of the main channel. Timbuktu itself has a smell: dust, shea butter, woodsmoke, animal dung, the dry-season Saharan grit that gets into everything. The opening mentions woodsmoke once. It is not enough.

The canoes are not yet physical objects. I know they hold thirty men. I do not know what they look like. The wood, the shape, the width, the way they sit in the water when loaded with armed men. A war canoe loaded to capacity sits lower and moves differently than an empty one. The paddlers' technique with a loaded boat is different from their technique with a light one. These details matter because they put me in the canoe.

**The question I'd push on:** Can I feel the river beneath the canoe? The opening puts me on the bank watching the fleet arrive. The chapter should eventually put me IN the fleet, on the water, feeling the current and the weight of the boat and the rhythm of the paddle. The river is the protagonist. Let me feel what the protagonist feels like.
