## Cultural Authenticity — Religious Traditions

The chapter treats Lucumí, the Abakuá brotherhoods, and Palo Monte as distinct traditions, which is correct. These three Afro-Cuban religious-cultural systems are related by their African origins but are distinct in their specific origins, their ritual practices, and their social organization. The chapter does not conflate them, which is the most important thing.

The Ogún characterization is accurate: Ogún is indeed the orixá of iron, war, and labor, associated with the machete, the forge, and the warrior. The connection made between the machete (cane knife become weapon) and Ogún's domain is historically and religiously appropriate. The Afro-Cuban soldiers in the Liberation Army would have understood this connection as real and functional, not as metaphor.

One small clarification: the chapter refers to Lucumí as "the Cuban form of Yoruba religion." More precisely, Lucumí is the Cuban form of the Yoruba religious system known in Nigeria as Ifá/Orisha worship. The word "Lucumí" itself comes from a Yoruba greeting ("Oluku mi," my friend) and was used by Cubans to describe both the Yoruba-descended enslaved people and their religious practices. This distinction matters because it roots the Cuban tradition specifically in the Yoruba diaspora rather than in "Yoruba religion" as a generic category. A slight revision: "the Cuban form of Yoruba religious practice" would be more precise, or simply "Lucumí, the Yoruba-descended religious tradition of Cuba."

## The Abakuá

The Abakuá brotherhoods mentioned in the chapter deserve a brief note: they originated not from Yoruba but from the Efik and other peoples of the Cross River region of present-day Nigeria/Cameroon. Their social organization (mutual aid brotherhoods, initiation, oath-bound solidarity) was significant in the independence context because these networks provided community solidarity and resistance capacity that was specifically relevant in Oriente. The chapter does not explain the Abakuá's origins or structure, but it does not mischaracterize them either; mentioning them alongside Lucumí and Palo Monte is appropriate without further elaboration.

## Protection Before Battle

The chapter states: "The protection invoked before battle, the prayers, the small objects carried on the body, was not separate from the military preparation. It was part of it." This is exactly right. The small objects referred to are the elekes (bead necklaces) and other ritual objects associated with specific orixás. Soldiers would carry protective objects consecrated to Ogún or to Obatalá (associated with peace and protection). The curanderos mentioned in the following paragraph would have been versed in both botanical knowledge and ritual practice — these are not separated in the Lucumí tradition. Well-rendered.

## Verdict

The chapter's treatment of Afro-Cuban religious traditions is accurate, respectful, and appropriately integrated into the military and social history of the era. The one suggested refinement: "Lucumí, the Yoruba-descended religious tradition of Cuba" rather than "the Cuban form of Yoruba religion." No other corrections required.

**Recommended for FINAL with minor language refinement.**
