# Naqada I — The Amratian

## The Workshop

The copper was cold when he pulled it from the mold, but it would not stay cold for long.

The man working in Nubt held the awl and turned it against the morning light that came through the reed screen of his workshop. The air smelled of charcoal and the sharp, faintly sweet residue of smelted ore. He had pulled the copper from malachite himself, feeding the green stone into a crucible no larger than a drinking bowl, blowing through a reed tube until his face burned and the charcoal went white and the metal wept out of the stone. The awl lay in his palm, slender, the color of a new wound. It was good. The point would pierce leather or score wet clay as cleanly as a thorn.

He did not know that three days' walk downriver, at a settlement where the wadi met the floodplain, another man was shaping the same object from the same ore.

The copper was the same because the source was the same. The wadis of the Eastern Desert held malachite in seams through the red sandstone, and the people of the upper valley had been walking those wadis for generations, bringing back the green stone to grind into pigment for the palettes they painted their eyes with. Somewhere in those generations, someone noticed that the green stone, when it sat too long in a hot fire, did something the flint and the bone and the clay did not do. It changed. It ran like water and cooled like stone but was neither. It was copper, and it could be shaped, and it held an edge, and it gleamed.

The people of Nubt did not have a word for revolution. They had copper. They had the fine-grained clay of the riverbank, which they shaped into vessels with walls so thin that a thumb behind them showed pink through the fired surface, and they painted those vessels with white lime: hippos in the current, crocodiles watching from the shallows, a figure with upraised arms that might be a woman, might be a goddess, might be both. They had ochre, the red earth, which they ground on slate palettes and mixed with fat and applied to the skin of the living and the skin of the dead. They had flint, which they knapped into blades of a precision that would not be surpassed in the valley for another thousand years, the ripple-flaked surfaces catching light like water. They had ivory from the hippos that lived in the river shallows, which they carved into combs and small figures and pins for holding cloth. They had bone, which they shaped into needles and awls and the piercing tools that preceded copper in the same functions copper now performed. And they had the flood.

## The Green Stone

The man who walked into the Eastern Desert for the malachite left the valley before dawn, when the air still held the night's coolness and the stars over the western ridge were beginning to fade. By midmorning the heat would come off the sandstone in waves that rippled the air, and the silence of the wadi would be the silence of a world scraped clean of everything soft. He carried water in a skin, food wrapped in reed matting, and a leather pack for the stone he would bring back. The route was not marked. He had learned it by walking it with someone who had walked it before, and that person had learned it the same way, and the knowledge lived in the sequence of landmarks: this outcrop, that bend, the dry watercourse that pointed toward the seam.

The malachite lay in veins through the red sandstone, green against red, the color of copper before it knew it was copper. He worked the stone loose with a hammerstone, choosing the pieces with the densest green, the ones that felt heaviest for their size. The ore-rich stone had a particular weight that the hand learned to recognize. He would carry it back across the desert in the pack, thirty or forty pounds of it, and by the time he reached the valley the blisters on his shoulders would have broken and healed and broken again.

For most of the time anyone could remember, the green stone had been for pigment. You ground it on a slate palette until it became a fine powder, the color of young river reeds, and you mixed it with fat and painted it around your eyes. The palettes themselves were shaped from siltstone, flat ovals or rhomboids, polished smooth on the grinding surface. Some were simple. Some were carved at the edges with shapes of animals, hippos or birds, worked with a patience that the grinding surface would eventually erase. The palette was one of the most personal objects a person owned. You ground your paint on it. You were buried with it.

The palette was a quiet object, but it connected the living to everything they needed to be. The malachite painted around the eyes was not decoration the way a later age might understand the word. The green pigment protected against the glare of the sun on water and sand. It marked the face as a face that had been prepared, attended to, made ready for whoever would see it, living or dead. The palette was ground by use, its surface developing a shallow concavity over months and years, and the wear itself was a record: this much grinding, this many mornings, this many preparations. When a palette was placed in a grave, the concavity went with it. The dead carried their history of use into the west.

But the malachite did something else in fire. The smelter at Nubt had learned this the way all smelters in the valley had learned it: from someone who had tried it, or seen it tried, or stumbled onto the fact that the green stone in a hot enough fire became something that was not stone at all. The process was specific. The ore had to be crushed to the size of lentils. It had to be layered with charcoal in a crucible, a small vessel of fire-hardened clay. The charcoal had to burn hot enough to turn white, which meant blowing through a tube, a reed or a hollow bone, leaning over the crucible and feeding the fire with breath until the muscles of the face ached and the eyes watered from the heat. When the charcoal was white and the crucible glowed, the malachite began to change. The green darkened. The stone softened. And then, if the heat held and the air was right, the copper separated, running to the bottom of the crucible in a bright, trembling pool no larger than a fingertip.

It was not much. A single smelting produced a few grams of metal, enough for an awl, a pin, a small fishhook. Copper at this stage was not replacing flint. Flint was still the material that did the serious work: the long blades for cutting, the scrapers for hides, the bifacial knives that the best knappers shaped into objects of startling precision, each flake removed with a control that the copper-worker could only admire. Copper was something else. It was new. It gleamed where flint did not. It could be hammered into shapes that stone could not take. It was rare, and the rarity made it worth carrying across the desert, and the carrying made the network, and the network connected communities that had no other reason to know each other existed.

## The Clay

The potter worked at the river's edge in the weeks after the flood receded, when the banks were still dark with new silt and the clay was at its best. She dug the fine-grained alluvial clay from the exposed bank and carried it to her working ground, a flat area above the floodline where she could spread it and let it dry to the right consistency. The clay had to be cleaned: she picked out the pebbles and root fragments by hand, then kneaded it with water until it was smooth and even, the texture of thick paste that held its shape when she pressed her thumb into it.

She built the vessels by coiling. A flat disc of clay for the base, then ropes of clay rolled between the palms and laid in spirals, one on top of the next, each coil pressed and smoothed into the one below until the wall rose. The skill was in the thinning. She worked from both sides, pressing with a smooth stone on the outside and her fingers on the inside, reducing the wall to a thickness that other communities in the valley could not match. At Nubt, the potters made vessels whose walls were barely thicker than the width of a fingernail. When she held the finished vessel up to the light, her thumb behind the wall showed pink through the fired clay. This was not accident. This was generations of potters teaching their hands to other hands, the pressure points, the speed of the stone, the moment when the clay was thin enough and further thinning would collapse the wall.

The painting came after forming, before firing. She mixed lime with water to make a white slip, thin enough to flow from a brush of bundled reeds, thick enough to hold a line. The patterns were the patterns her community knew: the wavy lines that might be water, the cross-hatching that covered the vessel's shoulder, the hippos drawn with their mouths open and their bodies half-submerged, the crocodiles with their tails trailing, the figure with upraised arms that appeared on certain vessels and appeared again on the rock faces along the desert edge and appeared again painted on the walls of structures that may have been shrines. The figure's meaning was not separate from its making. She painted it because it was painted. It belonged on the vessel the way the vessel belonged in the world.

The firing was the moment of risk. An open pit, charcoal and dung for fuel, the vessels stacked and covered and left to the heat. A full firing took hours. The potter watched the smoke and the color of the flames, reading the fire the way the smelter read the crucible: by signs that the hand could not feel and the eye learned to see. The red-brown color of the finished pottery came from the iron in the clay, oxidized by the fire's air. The black tops that distinguished some vessels came from inverting them in the cooling ashes, where the reduced atmosphere turned the rim dark. These were not random effects. They were controlled, repeated, consistent across hundreds of vessels found in graves and settlement debris along the upper valley. The white cross-lined pottery of Nubt traveled. It appeared at sites thirty and fifty and eighty kilometers away. It was traded, or it was carried by potters who moved, or it was imitated by communities who saw the patterns and learned them. The pottery was the first thing that made Naqada visible: a tradition so distinctive that it named an entire period.

## The Flood Calendar

The flood was everything. He had watched it come, as he watched it every year: the river rising past its banks in summer, spreading across the low ground until the fields vanished and the valley smelled of wet silt, dark and heavy. The water stood on the land for weeks. The grain was under it. The paths were under it. The world contracted to the settlement and the desert edge, and in that contraction the other work expanded.

The change was felt in the body. During the planting months, the copper-worker had been a farmer like everyone else, bending over the grain, pulling weeds, carrying water in clay jars to the plots the flood had not quite reached. His hands were the same hands that worked metal, but in the dry months they worked soil. When the flood came, the daily rhythm shifted. He woke to the smell of standing water and walked not to the fields but to the workshop. The charcoal was where he had left it. The crucible was where he had left it. The reed tube hung on the wall. He sat down, and the muscles in his back that had spent months bent over grain now bent over the fire, and the work of making resumed.

This was the rhythm the valley imposed. When the fields were drowned, the flood gave back in labor what it took in land. The copper-worker smelted. The potter coiled and fired. The flint-knapper sat at the edge of the settlement where the chips would not cut anyone's feet and worked the stone into blades, the sound of hammer on core a steady tapping that carried across the still water. The women ground grain from the previous harvest on sandstone querns, the rhythmic scraping audible from every part of the settlement, the sound of stored food becoming the food that would carry them to the next planting. Every craft had its season, and the season was the flood, and the flood came because the rains came in highlands that no one at Nubt had ever seen, and they accepted this the way they accepted the sun: it happened, and the world was organized around the fact that it happened.

The flood did something to communities that the dry months did not. It concentrated people and it concentrated their work. A settlement that spent the planting and harvest months dispersed across its fields came together during the inundation, and in the coming together the specialization became visible. The man who worked copper was identifiably the man who worked copper. The woman who made the thin-walled painted vessels was identifiably the potter. The knapper who could turn a nodule of flint into a blade twenty centimeters long with edges that cut cleaner than the copper ever would was identifiably a master of something the rest of the settlement could not do. Skill differentiated people during the flood months in a way that fieldwork, where everyone did roughly the same thing, did not. And the products of that differentiation, the copper awl and the painted pot and the finely knapped blade, were the objects that traveled when the flood receded and the paths reopened and the desert routes became walkable again.

The flood pulsed, and the network pulsed with it. During the inundation: production. After the recession: movement. The desert routes were only walkable in the cooler months when the heat did not kill. The river was most navigable for cargo when the water was high enough to float a loaded reed boat but not so high that the current was dangerous. The exchange had its own calendar, layered on top of the agricultural calendar, and both of them were layered on top of the river's cycle. The people of Nubt did not separate these rhythms. They lived inside them the way a swimmer lives inside the current.

The flood was not background. It was the mechanism. It created the surplus labor that enabled specialization. The specialization created products worth trading. The trading created the network. And the network, growing each year as the flood pulsed and the goods moved, was building something that no one at Nubt was trying to build.

## The Dead

The burial-maker cut the grave into the desert gravel above the floodplain, working in the afternoon heat with a stone adze and her hands. The pit was oval, just large enough for the body and the goods. The desert edge was where the dead belonged: high enough that the flood would not reach them, dry enough that the sand would preserve what it held. The living occupied the floodplain, between the river and the desert. The dead occupied the margin between the cultivated land and the red emptiness beyond it.

The body was brought wrapped in matting or animal skin. She could feel the weight of it, the heaviness of a person who was no longer holding himself up. It was placed on its left side, knees drawn up, hands near the face, facing west. The sun went down in the west. What lay beyond the western horizon was where the dead continued. This was not a belief the burial-maker would have separated from the rest of what she knew about the world. The sun crossed the sky and went somewhere. The dead left the body and went somewhere. The two somethings were the same somewhere. West. She rubbed red ochre between her palms until her hands were stained the color of sunset, and she painted the body with it, the pigment catching in the skin's creases, settling into the lines of the face. The ochre was for the journey. It had always been for the journey.

She placed the grave goods with the care of someone writing a letter. A pottery vessel with white-painted hippos at the head of the body: the dead would need to carry water, and the vessel's images connected it to the river world the dead were leaving. A slate palette, already worn from years of grinding, with traces of malachite still green in the surface: the dead would need to paint their face for whatever they would meet. A flint blade, long and thin and beautifully pressure-flaked: the dead would need to cut. In some graves, not this one, there was a copper pin or awl, and the presence of copper marked a difference that was becoming harder to ignore.

Some graves held more than others. This was true all along the desert edge above the floodplain, at the cemeteries that stretched from Nubt to settlements upstream and down. Most graves held pottery and a palette and perhaps a comb carved from ivory. Some graves held these things and also copper, obsidian, imported shells, objects that had traveled distances that most of the dead had never traveled in life. The difference was not dramatic. No grave at this stage held the wealth that later centuries would pour into the ground. But the difference was consistent. It was not random who received the copper and the obsidian. It was the same families, the same positions within the settlement, the same access to the networks that brought materials from the desert and the coast and the southern highlands.

The dead were the archive. The living left ambiguous traces: postholes that could be any structure, hearths that could be any fire. But the graves were deliberate. Every object was placed. Every position was chosen. And the graves, read together across the cemeteries of the upper valley, told a story that the living were not yet telling about themselves.

The network was creating winners. Access to distant materials was not evenly distributed. The settlements closest to the desert routes, the ones that sat at the junctions where the wadis met the valley, were accumulating more of these materials than their neighbors. Nubt, at the mouth of the Wadi Hammamat, the route to the Eastern Desert and the Red Sea beyond it, was one of these. Hierakonpolis, where the desert routes from the south converged on the floodplain, was another. The network's geography was already sorting the valley's communities into centers and margins, and the graves were recording the result.

The inequality was not yet sharp. This was not a world of kings and subjects, not yet a world where one grave held gold and the next held nothing. It was a world where some families had a copper awl and a string of Red Sea shells alongside the standard pottery and palette, and other families had only the pottery and the palette, and the difference was consistent enough, generation after generation, that it was not accidental. Something was being passed down. Access, perhaps. Position within the settlement. Proximity to the person who controlled the route into the Eastern Desert or the relationship with the community upstream that had obsidian. The mechanism was invisible, but the result was legible in the sand.

## The Network

The network had no name and no center and no one who managed it. It existed in the simple fact that materials moved. Copper left the Eastern Desert in someone's pack and arrived at Nubt as an awl. Obsidian left the southern highlands and arrived in a grave at a settlement near Hierakonpolis, black and cold in the hand, sharper than anything the valley produced from its own stone. Shells from the Red Sea coast, pierced and strung as ornaments, appeared in graves a hundred miles from the nearest salt water. Fine flint from outcrops that occurred in only a few locations along the valley turned up at settlements that had no local source.

Each of these movements was a relationship. The copper-worker at Nubt had something the settlement downriver wanted. What did he receive in return? The record does not say. Perhaps grain. Perhaps pottery from a tradition he admired. Perhaps nothing tangible at all, only the obligation of a return, a gift that created a debt that created a connection. The exchange was not commerce. There was no marketplace, no currency, no price. It was something older and more binding: the movement of valued objects between people who, by the act of exchange, became people who knew each other.

The network ran on the valley's geography. The Nile was the road. In the dry months, when the river was low and the banks were passable, goods moved upstream and downstream by foot and by boat. The reed boats of the upper valley, bundles of papyrus reeds lashed together and bent upward at the prow, were light enough for two men to carry overland and sturdy enough to float a load of pottery or stone. The boats appeared painted on the pottery itself, curved at both ends, sometimes with a figure standing in the hull, sometimes with what may have been a sail or a standard at the mast. The river was already not just a water source but a highway, and the people who used it knew its currents and its shoals the way the desert-walker knew the wadis.

The desert routes ran east to the wadis where the ores and stones were found, and south along the river to the communities that later scholarship would call the A-Group Nubians. They were contemporaries. They were neighbors. Their settlements lay south of the first cataract, in the narrow valley where the river cut through granite, and their graves held objects from the upper valley: Naqada pottery, Egyptian flint, palettes of the same siltstone. In return, or in parallel, materials from the south moved north: certain stones, certain resins, objects that the archaeological record identifies but the people who carried them would have called by names the record does not preserve. The relationship was not colonial. It was not one-directional. It was the southern arm of the same web, and it ran on the same principle: you made something, someone wanted it, the object moved, and a relationship followed.

The network was not linear. It was a web, and each node was a settlement where someone made something that someone else wanted.

By the end of Naqada I, the web had a pattern. The settlements at the junctions, the places where the desert routes met the river, were growing. Hierakonpolis, where the routes from the south and the eastern wadis converged, was becoming the largest settlement in the upper valley. Its precinct covered a larger area than any other community's. Its graves held a concentration of imported goods that smaller settlements could not match. Nubt, at the mouth of the Wadi Hammamat where the route to the Eastern Desert and ultimately the Red Sea began, was becoming a center of metalwork and exchange. The settlements between the junctions were smaller, less materially rich, more dependent on the goods that flowed through the network they did not control. The differentiation was not political. No one ruled. No one commanded. But the geography was sorting people, and the sorting was visible in the graves, and the graves were the only testimony anyone was leaving.

## What the Coals Knew

The man in the workshop finished the awl and set it beside the crucible. Outside, the sun was climbing and the river was low. The flood would come again. When it did, the fields would vanish under water, and in the weeks of waiting he would make more tools, and some of them would travel, and some of them would end up in the ground with someone who had not yet died.

The network was growing, and the growth was changing the valley. The communities that sat at the right junctions were accumulating more, and the communities that did not were accumulating less, and the difference, repeated across generations, was building something that none of them had intended. No one at Nubt was trying to create a state. No one at Hierakonpolis was trying to become a capital. The copper-worker wanted to make awls. The potter wanted to make vessels. The desert-walker wanted to bring back ore. The burial-maker wanted to send the dead westward with the proper provisions. Each of them was doing the thing that was theirs to do, and the aggregate of their doing, the sum of their making and carrying and burying, was assembling the conditions from which, in the centuries to come, the first kings of the valley would emerge.

But that was later. For now, the awl lay on the ledge, and the crucible still held the faint green residue of the last smelting, a skin of slag no thicker than a leaf. The charcoal needed to be fed. There was ore to be worked. The reed screen of the workshop filtered the light the same way it had filtered it that morning, and the morning before, and the morning before that, and the morning when the copper-worker's own teacher had sat in this same spot and shown him how to crush the ore and layer it with fuel and blow until his face burned.

The network had no name. The hierarchy had no crown. The valley held communities of people who made things with their hands and buried those things with their dead and traded what they could spare with neighbors they had come to know through the simple, repeated act of exchange. The flood came and went and came again, and each time it came the pattern deepened: more specialization, more exchange, more material moving further from where it began. The graves recorded the accumulation. The settlements at the junctions grew. The desert routes were walked by men who knew them and taught them to other men who would walk them in their turn.

He picked up the next piece of ore and blew on the coals. The coals brightened. The heat rose. Somewhere downriver, another man did the same.
