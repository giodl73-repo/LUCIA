# AmaNdebele — "The People Who Disappeared Over the Horizon"

Mzilikazi kaMashobane did not retreat. He left. In the early 1820s, Mzilikazi was a commander in Shaka's Zulu army — one of the ablest, given an independent command and sent to raid the Sotho peoples of the interior. He raided successfully and did not send the cattle back. This was not an oversight. It was a declaration. Mzilikazi was breaking from the Zulu state and building his own.

What followed was one of the most remarkable political migrations in southern African history. The Ndebele — the people Mzilikazi was building, drawing together Zulu core fighters, captured Sotho and Tswana peoples, and anyone else willing to join a state that rewarded loyalty regardless of origin — moved north and west across the Highveld, fighting off Zulu punitive expeditions, absorbing and defeating Sotho and Tswana chieftaincies, living for years in the Transvaal as a raiding state that no one could pin down. When the Boer voortrekkers pushed north in the 1830s, Mzilikazi's Ndebele fought them too — and lost badly enough at the Battle of Marikana (1837) that Mzilikazi made the calculation he had made before: move on rather than fight a sustained war he could not win.

The second migration took the Ndebele north of the Limpopo River onto the Zimbabwe plateau. Here the Changamire Rozvi state was in decline, and the Ndebele established themselves in the southwestern corner of the plateau around the Matobo Hills — Matabeleland — transforming the regional political order and building a state that would endure until the British South Africa Company's conquest in 1893. The Zimbabwe plateau became the Ndebele homeland.

## What Defined Them

**The Zulu foundation rebuilt.** Mzilikazi took the Zulu military and political system — the ibutho age-regiment structure, the royal barracks, the discipline of unified command — and rebuilt it in a new context, for a different purpose. The Zulu system had been designed for a king who needed to control an existing dense population in a defined territory. Mzilikazi used it to create a mobile conquest state, then a settler state, incorporating peoples of radically different origins under a single political identity. The ibutho system became not just a military organization but a mechanism for ethnogenesis: men from Zulu, Sotho, Tswana, and Shona backgrounds entered the regiments and emerged as Ndebele. The army made the nation.

**The three tiers: abezansi, abenhla, amahole.** The Ndebele state organized its people in a hierarchy that reflected the history of conquest and incorporation. The abezansi ("those from below" — the original Nguni core from Zululand) held the highest social status. The abenhla ("those from above" — Sotho and Tswana peoples incorporated during the Transvaal years) held middle status, organized into their own regiments. The amahole ("the servants" — Shona and other peoples incorporated after the arrival in Matabeleland) were the subordinate tier, required to provide tribute and labor but not admitted to full Ndebele status. This hierarchy was not a caste: men could rise through military distinction, and the boundaries softened over generations. But it was real, and it shaped the political economy of Matabeleland for the half-century of the kingdom's existence.

**Lobengula and the concession crisis.** Mzilikazi died in 1868. His son Lobengula kaMzilikazi came to power after a succession struggle and ruled the Ndebele kingdom during the most dangerous decades of the colonial advance. By the 1880s, European concession-seekers were arriving in Bulawayo in a steady stream — missionaries, hunters, prospectors, emissaries of mining companies — each wanting to extract permissions that could be turned into legal claims. Lobengula was not naive: he understood that the concessions were dangerous, that the Europeans were not all saying the same thing, and that signing anything could be used against him. He tried to play the concession-seekers against each other, to maintain ambiguity, to delay. He failed. The Rudd Concession of 1888, which Lobengula signed believing he was granting a limited mining right for ten men, was translated into a claim to sovereignty over all of Matabeleland and used by Cecil Rhodes's British South Africa Company to obtain a royal charter to colonize the region.

**The first and second Matabele wars.** The BSA Company's Pioneer Column occupied Mashonaland in 1890 without Ndebele permission, establishing Fort Salisbury — an act of appropriation that Lobengula protested but could not militarily counter. In 1893, a raid against Shona subjects of the Ndebele gave the Company the pretext it needed: the BSA Company's forces, equipped with Maxim guns, invaded Matabeleland. The Ndebele army — brave, disciplined, organized along the traditional regimental lines — could not survive Maxim gun fire. Bulawayo fell. Lobengula fled north and died, in disputed circumstances, in early 1894. In 1896, the Ndebele rose again in the First Chimurenga — a coordinated rebellion that drew on the spirit medium tradition (the Matobo Hills were sacred sites) and nearly expelled the Company. The rebellion was suppressed. The famous "indaba in the Matobo Hills" — Rhodes's personal negotiation with the Ndebele indunas in 1896 — ended the armed resistance but left the fundamental dispossession in place.

**The Matobo Hills and the sacred landscape.** The granite kopjes and caves of the Matobo Hills were not just dramatic landscape; they were the sacred geography of the Ndebele state. The hills contained rock paintings of the San peoples who had lived there before the Ndebele arrived — an ancestral layer the Ndebele recognized and incorporated into their own sacred geography. Mzilikazi was buried in the Matobo Hills, establishing the site as a royal burial ground. When the 1896 rebellion's leadership gathered to negotiate with Rhodes, they met in the hills — a choice that was political and spiritual simultaneously. Cecil Rhodes, who was buried in the hills on his own instruction when he died in 1902, understood that he was placing himself in the sacred center of the people his company had destroyed.

**The linguistic and cultural persistence.** The Ndebele — isiNdebele — is today spoken by approximately two million people in Zimbabwe (where it is an official language) and by a separate Ndebele community in South Africa (whose ancestors were Mzilikazi's peoples left behind in the Transvaal). The Zimbabwean Ndebele identity carries both the martial tradition of Mzilikazi's state and the complex legacy of the Matabeleland massacres of 1983–84 (Gukurahundi), when the ZANU-PF government's Fifth Brigade killed thousands of Ndebele civilians in what Ndebele survivors and international investigators have called a genocide. The history of the AmaNdebele is thus not finished: it runs from Mzilikazi's departure from Shaka through the colonial conquest through independence through Gukurahundi into the present, an unbroken argument about land, sovereignty, and who belongs.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Departure:** Mzilikazi is counting cattle. The cattle that should have gone to Shaka are still here. He knew when he kept them what he was deciding. The Zulu impi will come. He has time to prepare. He is not afraid of Shaka. He is afraid of nothing. This, too, is what Shaka taught him.

**2 — The Highveld Years:** The Ndebele state is moving. It has moved before and will move again. The regiments carry what is needed; the cattle are driven; the new peoples incorporated along the way become Ndebele within a generation. Mzilikazi is building not a place but a form — a way of organizing men that can pick up and carry itself across any landscape.

**3 — The Crossing:** The Limpopo River is behind them. The Zimbabwe plateau rises ahead. The Rozvi state is weakening. There is room here for a kingdom. Mzilikazi surveys the Matobo Hills. He knows immediately that the dead are present here. He will be buried here.

**4 — Lobengula's Reading:** The treaty is in front of him. The translator is explaining what it says. Lobengula is listening carefully. He is also watching the translator's face. Something is not right. He will sign, believing one thing. The paper says another. By the time he understands the difference, it will be too late.

**5 — The Indaba:** The Matobo Hills, 1896. The indunas are meeting with Rhodes. The rebellion has failed — the Maxim guns could not be answered with spears. But the indunas have something Rhodes needs: peace, legitimacy, the end of a war the Company cannot afford. They know it. He knows it. The negotiation is between two parties who understand exactly what they are trading.
