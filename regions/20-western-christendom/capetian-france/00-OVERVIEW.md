# La France des Capétiens — "The Kingdom God Gave the Franks"

France did not exist when Hugh Capet was elected king in 987. He ruled a domain barely larger than the Île-de-France — a strip of territory from Paris to Orléans that his own vassals could have overrun in an afternoon if they had organized themselves. The dukes of Normandy, the counts of Flanders, the counts of Champagne, the dukes of Burgundy, and the dukes of Aquitaine all held territories larger than the king's. Yet the Capetians, reigning in unbroken male succession for three and a half centuries, turned this unpromising start into the largest and most powerful kingdom in Europe — through patient territorial accumulation, through the theology of sacred royal anointing, through the alliance with the Church that made the French crown the most legitimate in Christendom, and through the building of Paris into the intellectual capital of the medieval world.

The secret of Capetian success is biology — they produced male heirs for ten generations — and the theological elaboration of royal sacrality that surrounded those heirs. The king of France was anointed with the Holy Ampulla, a vial of sacred oil said to have been brought from heaven by a dove for the baptism of Clovis. He could heal scrofula — the king's evil — by touch. He was *rex Christianissimus*, the Most Christian King, whose kingdom was the chosen instrument of divine providence. This was not idle ceremony. It gave the Capetian kings a legitimacy that their greatest rivals — the Holy Roman Emperors, the Angevin kings of England — could not quite match, because the French theory of sacred kingship was more comprehensively worked out and more deeply institutionalized than any other in Europe.

## What Defined Them

**The theology of sacred monarchy.** The Capetian kings developed the most elaborate theory of sacred royal authority in medieval Europe. The coronation ordo at Reims — the ritual of anointing, the regalia, the liturgical prayers — made the king into a quasi-priestly figure, consecrated to a divine purpose. The ability to heal scrofula by touch (*thaumaturgical kingship*) was both a practical demonstration of sacred power and a political instrument: the kings who touched the sick thousands of times in a single ceremony were performing a sustained claim to divine favor that their subjects found convincing. This sacred aura gave the Capetians a political legitimacy that made challenges to royal authority feel like sacrilege.

**The slow accumulation of territory.** The Capetian expansion was mostly achieved through inheritance, marriage, and the exploitation of legal feudal claims rather than through direct conquest. Philip II took Normandy and Anjou from John in a war, but he took them by exploiting John's feudal obligations as a vassal of the French crown. Louis VIII added Languedoc through the Albigensian Crusade. Philip III inherited Champagne. Louis IX's brothers ruled Anjou and Provence. The Capetians were patient: they did not need to conquer if they could inherit, and they seemed to inherit everything eventually. The kingdom was assembled like a puzzle, piece by piece, over three centuries.

**Paris as the center of European learning.** The University of Paris — which grew from the cathedral school of Notre-Dame in the twelfth century into the greatest intellectual institution in Europe — was the Capetians' most important soft-power instrument. Scholars came from every country in Christendom to study theology, philosophy, and medicine in Paris. The Capetian kings funded it, protected it, and occasionally tried to control it; the university resisted control while basking in royal favor. Aquinas, Albertus Magnus, Bonaventure — the greatest theologians of the thirteenth century all passed through Paris. The Gothic cathedral, developed at Saint-Denis under Abbot Suger, spread from France to every corner of Europe. French intellectual and aesthetic culture was the culture of medieval Christendom.

**The Parlement de Paris as royal justice.** The *Parlement de Paris* — the supreme royal court that evolved in the thirteenth century from the king's council sitting in judicial session — is the institutional expression of the Capetian claim to provide justice for the entire kingdom. It heard appeals from every royal court in France, overruled baronial courts, and gradually accumulated a body of royal jurisprudence that competed with and eventually superseded local custom. The Parlement was also a political institution: it registered (and could remonstrate against) royal legislation, and over time it became a conservative check on royal power — a check that would eventually contribute to the political crises of the ancien régime. But under the Capetians it was primarily an instrument of royal expansion.

**The crusade as Capetian enterprise.** Louis IX's two crusades — 1248–1254 and 1270 — were personal expressions of genuine religious conviction, and they were also the greatest demonstrations of French royal prestige since Charlemagne. Louis went to crusade as the representative of Christendom, funded by French royal revenues, accompanied by the French aristocracy. His capture in Egypt and his ransoming — and his refusal to abandon his army to secure his own release — created a moral reputation that his canonization in 1297 officially recognized. The French crown was not merely the most powerful in Europe; it was, in the person of Saint Louis, the most holy.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Beginnings:** Hugh Capet rides out of Paris with his knights to enforce his nominal authority over a baron forty kilometers away. The baron is not impressed. Hugh wins the confrontation, barely, and returns to a domain that is more embarrassment than kingdom. He has one strategic advantage: God seems to keep giving him sons.

**2 — The Awakening:** Louis VI spends his reign on horseback, moving from castle to castle, breaking the brigand-lords who have made the roads of the Île-de-France impassable. He is not elegant. He is fat and effective. When he dies in 1137, the royal domain is, for the first time, actually royal.

**3 — Philip Augustus and the Great Expansion:** Philip Augustus receives the news from Bouvines: Otto IV's army has broken. England, the empire, and Flanders aligned against France and lost. Philip rides back to Paris in a triumph that the city will remember for a century. He has, in one afternoon, made France the dominant power in Europe.

**4 — Saint Louis and the Peak:** Louis IX, returning from a failed crusade that cost him four years of captivity, refuses to abandon his remaining soldiers in Egypt even though his own ransom has been paid. The barons are furious. Louis explains that a king who abandons his men does not deserve to be king. No one has a response to this.

**5 — The Capetian Crisis:** Philip IV's lawyers present the dossier against Pope Boniface VIII — who has, they argue, committed heresy, sodomy, and sorcery. The accusations are almost certainly false. The purpose is to humiliate the papacy into submission. Philip wins the confrontation and loses something he cannot name: the sacred compact between the French crown and the Church that has been the Capetian source of power for three centuries.
