# R2-P4 — Wade Davis (PANEL-2)
## Elam Era 1 | Notes Review

The notes identify the scribal knowledge system at method level as a requirement. I want to be specific about what this must look like in practice.

The proto-Elamite scribal training left traces in the tablets themselves. Scholars studying the corpus (particularly Englund and Dahl) have identified tablets with repeated signs, corrections, and what appear to be practice sequences — the material signature of learners. The chapter should use this: describe the practice tablet, the sign pressed incorrectly and pressed again beside it, the archive that holds both the master's work and the student's attempt. This is the knowledge system at its most vulnerable and most human moment — the moment of transmission.

The dual-script situation of this era is genuinely extraordinary and the chapter should not underplay it. Elam produced *three* distinct writing systems across its history: proto-Elamite (unread), Linear Elamite (partially deciphered now, per Desset 2022), and cuneiform Elamite (fully read). This era encompasses the first two. A civilization that generated multiple distinct writing systems — not borrowings from Mesopotamia but its own original inventions — is demonstrating an intellectual relationship with the problem of writing that is remarkable not because it is "sophisticated" but because it is genuinely different from Mesopotamian solutions. Proto-Elamite solved the accounting problem one way. Linear Elamite, emerging when Puzur-Inshushinak wanted royal inscriptions, solved the prestige-text problem a different way. These are two different intellectual approaches to two different communicative needs. Show this distinction.

**The knowledge system I'm most concerned about:**

The Elamite theological knowledge system — the relationship between Inshushinak, the dead, the grain, and the administrative apparatus — is not yet rendered at method level in the notes. The grain is "the god's grain" but we do not yet see the religious knowledge system operating. What did the temple priests know that others did not? What was the specialized knowledge of the Inshushinak cult? If the redistribution economy is theology made administrative, then the theologians were also administrators — and the chapter should show us one of them doing the theological-administrative work simultaneously.

**A specific request:**

The Lament for the Destruction of Ur is cited as a source. It is also one of the most beautiful texts of the ancient world. The chapter should quote it, or at least let its voice be heard — not as external scholarly evidence but as the voice of the system Elam had destroyed. The contrast between the Sumerian voice (heard, literary, lamenting) and the Elamite voice (silent, administrative, unread) is the source asymmetry made into art. Do not miss this.
