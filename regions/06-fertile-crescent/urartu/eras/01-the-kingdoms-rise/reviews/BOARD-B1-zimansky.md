# B-1 — Paul Zimansky — Board Review of The Kingdom's Rise

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Urartian state structure, political ecology, the fortress-network as an organizational system. Author of *Ecology and Empire: The Structure of the Urartian State* (1985).

**Historical accuracy:**
- The fortress-canal cycle as the chapter's structural engine is consistent with the ecological-structural model. The chapter correctly identifies that the highland ecology (defensible terrain, mineral wealth, water that requires channeling) shaped the kingdom's organizational form.
- The consolidation around Tushpa in the mid-ninth century is accurately placed. The dating of Sarduri I to approximately 860-840 BCE is within the accepted range, though the exact dates remain uncertain.
- The canal attribution to Menua is correct. The "Shamiram Su" canal is well-documented in Menua's inscriptions. The chapter's description of the canal's engineering (gradient management, trapezoidal cross-section, stone lining) is consistent with archaeological survey data.
- The deportation and resettlement system under Argishti I is accurately described. The 6,600 figure from the Erebuni foundation inscription is correctly cited.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's presentation of Urartu as a highland organizational achievement rather than an Assyrian imitation is sound. The parallel between Haldi theology and Ashur theology is handled well: acknowledged as structurally parallel without reducing Biainili to a copy.
- The deportees are acknowledged honestly. This is important. The Urartian inscriptions are royal propaganda, and the chapter does not uncritically adopt the kings' perspective on their own campaigns.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The fortress-network as an organizational principle, not just a military one. The storeroom-surplus-garrison logic is the actual mechanism of the Urartian state.
- The highland ecology as a structural advantage, not just a geographic fact. The chapter understands why the Assyrians could not hold the highland permanently.
- The inscriptional shift from Akkadian to Urartian as a political event, not just a linguistic one.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter states that the Tushpa foundation blocks were "fitted without mortar." This is broadly correct for the cyclopean lower courses but should be qualified: later construction phases at Tushpa and other sites used mud-brick superstructures above the stone foundations. The chapter's implication that the entire fortress was dry-stone masonry should be softened.
- The "50 kilometers" figure for the canal is at the low end of estimates. Belli's surveys suggest 50-70 km depending on how the route is measured. The chapter uses "over fifty kilometers," which is acceptable but could be more precise: "approximately fifty to seventy kilometers" would better reflect the uncertainty.
- The chapter places Shalmaneser III's campaign against Sarduri at "859 or 856 BCE." The Assyrian Eponym List dates this to 856 BCE. The "859" alternative is less well-supported. Consider dropping it.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS. Three minor corrections needed, all factual precision. No structural problems.
