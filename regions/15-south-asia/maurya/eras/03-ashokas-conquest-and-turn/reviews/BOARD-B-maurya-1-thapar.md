## B-maurya-1 — Romila Thapar — Board Review of Ashoka's Conquest and Turn

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Ashokan political history, edict interpretation, the relationship between Buddhist textual tradition and epigraphic evidence. Standard authority on Maurya chronology and the nature of dhamma as a political concept.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The edict citations are generally accurate. RE 13 (Kalinga), RE 1 (animal sacrifice), RE 2 (medical facilities), RE 5 (dhamma-mahamatras), RE 9 (ceremonies), RE 12 (tolerance of sects), PE 7 (summary), MRE 1 (upasaka declaration), SRE 1-2 (Kalinga address) are all correctly identified and their content accurately represented.
- The Kalinga casualty numbers (150,000 carried away, 100,000 slain) are as given in RE 13. Correct.
- The distinction between the edicts' own account and the later Buddhist textual tradition (Ashokavadana, Mahavamsa) is clearly maintained. The chapter presents the hagiographic tradition honestly (ninety-nine brothers, Chandashoka/Dhammashoka) without endorsing it, and notes that the edicts themselves say nothing about fratricide. This is the correct scholarly position.
- MRE 1's evidence that Ashoka was already a Buddhist layman before Kalinga is correctly cited and correctly used to complicate the sudden-conversion narrative. This is an important point that many popular accounts miss.
- The Arthashastra is accurately attributed ("attributed to his grandfather's minister Kautilya") with appropriate hedging about the text's composite nature and uncertain dating. The vijigishu and mandala concepts are correctly deployed.
- The approximate dating (260 BCE for Kalinga, "eighth of his reign") is defensible, though I would note that the chronology remains debated and the "eighth year" is Thapar's own reconstruction, not universally accepted.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- Dhamma is consistently presented as the edicts define it: an ethical code of conduct, not a sectarian religion. The chapter does not conflate dhamma with Buddhism. RE 12's tolerance passage is correctly cited as evidence of dhamma's non-sectarian character. This is essential and the chapter gets it right.
- The chakravartin concept is accurately deployed as a Maurya self-understanding of sovereignty.
- The paternalist character of the Separate Kalinga Rock Edicts ("all men are my children") is correctly identified and allowed to speak without editorial commentary. The chapter lets the paternalism be visible, which is honest.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The three-explanations structure (religious, structural, personal) for the turn is well-handled. The chapter does not choose. This is appropriate, because the evidence does not compel a choice.
- The treatment of dhamma as policy rather than private practice is the correct reading.
- The honest handling of what the record cannot tell us (Ashoka's interiority, the Kalingans' response) builds trust.
- The observation that the dhamma-mahamatras had no Arthashastra precedent is accurate and important.

**What needs correction:**
- RE 1's description of the royal kitchen: "now only two peacocks and one deer were killed" is a close but imprecise paraphrase. The edict says "formerly in the kitchen of the Beloved of the Gods many hundreds of thousands of animals were killed daily for the sake of curry. But now when this edict on dhamma is written, only three animals are killed, two peacocks and one deer, and the deer not invariably." The distinction matters: "three animals" not "two peacocks and one deer." Minor but should be precise.
- The chapter states Ashoka ruled "approximately thirty-seven years." The conventional regnal span is c. 268-232 BCE, which is thirty-six years. The approximation is acceptable but could be tightened.
- The Sarnath capital as "four lions": the capital actually features four Asiatic lions. The chapter says "four lions" which is adequate but "four Asiatic lions" is more precise.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

Conditions: Correct the RE 1 kitchen paraphrase to match the edict's specificity ("three animals" not "two peacocks and one deer... and even that would cease"). The "that would cease" claim needs checking; RE 1 says "and the deer not invariably," not that the killing would end entirely. Minor factual tightening only. The chapter's core interpretation is sound.
