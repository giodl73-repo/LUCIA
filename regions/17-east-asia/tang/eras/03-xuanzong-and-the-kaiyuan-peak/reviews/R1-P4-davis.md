## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of The High Tang (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening treats the Tang as a genuine intellectual and artistic civilization — not "the Chinese equivalent" of anything Western, not a comparison point, but a world complete in itself. The poetry is introduced as a mode of political life, not as cultural decoration. This is the right orientation.

**What works:**
- The keju examination is presented as an intellectual achievement of the first order — a system that required mastery of regulated verse, the classics, and original composition. Two hundred thousand candidates, fewer than thirty passing. This is not a civil service exam in the modern bureaucratic sense; it is a civilization that organized itself around literary mastery, and the opening treats that with the seriousness it deserves.
- The cosmopolitanism is not presented as tolerance (a modern Western category) but as absorption — "all wisdom could be absorbed without threat." This is a different intellectual framework, closer to the Tang's own self-understanding, and the distinction matters enormously.
- The Sogdian cooks, the kaiyuan tongbao, the Silla merchants — each is presented with specificity that honors the knowledge system behind it. The spices "had no Chinese names, because the spices came from Persia and the cooks came from Samarkand and the recipes belonged to a world that stretched from Chang'an to Baghdad." The world is named as itself, not translated into Western geography.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening is, necessarily, focused on the elite and the urban. Du Fu was an educated man from a literate family; the West Market was the cosmopolitan heart of the capital. The full chapter will need to acknowledge that the Kaiyuan peak was experienced differently by the farmer in Henan or the conscript on the frontier. The Tang's intellectual achievement was real, but it rested on a base of agricultural labor and military service that the opening does not yet acknowledge.
- Li Bai is mentioned — "somewhere in the empire writing poems that made men weep into their wine" — but this is a reputation, not a presence. The full chapter should give Li Bai the same embodied specificity that Du Fu receives in the opening.

**The question I'd push on:**
The Tang poetic tradition is one of humanity's great intellectual achievements — a system in which political commentary, personal grief, landscape observation, and philosophical inquiry were all conducted through the same medium. The full chapter must treat Tang poetry not as cultural output but as a way of knowing. Will the chapter quote the poems themselves? Will the reader encounter Li Bai's and Du Fu's actual words, or only descriptions of their effect?
