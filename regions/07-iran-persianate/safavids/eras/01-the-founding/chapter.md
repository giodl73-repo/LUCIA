# The Founding — Shah Ismail I
## Safavids, Era 1 (c. 907–930 AH / 1501–1524 CE)

---

The imam was summoned from Bahrain on a ship he did not know would carry him to a country that did not yet know what it was.

His name was Ibrahim al-Qatifi, and he had lived his whole life among the Shia communities of the Gulf littoral — communities old enough to remember when Ali's right to the caliphate was not a theological dispute but a living political wound. He had spent his years teaching the fiqh of the Twelver tradition, the jurisprudence of a faith whose imam was hidden, awaiting the end of the age. He had not expected to be wanted. He had not expected that in the year 907 of the Hijra, a fourteen-year-old boy would ride into Tabriz at the head of an army whose helmets bore twelve folds in red wool — one for each imam — and declare that this ancient Persian plateau, Muslim for nine centuries in every mosque from the Zagros to Khorasan, was no longer Sunni. Was, had always been, would henceforth be, Shia.

Ibrahim sailed north. He did not know the language of the court he was traveling to. He did not know that the country that had summoned him had no Shia scholars to spare for its mosques, its courts, its Friday sermons, its new and urgent institutional life. He did not know what he would have to build.

---

## I. The Mountain

The plateau that Ismail ibn Haydar would inherit had been, for a generation, a space without a center.

The Aq Quyunlu confederation — the White Sheep Turkmen, who had ruled the Iranian plateau, Azerbaijan, and much of Anatolia from their capital at Tabriz since the middle of the fifteenth century — had broken apart after the death of their greatest sultan, Uzun Hasan, in 882 AH. His sons did not inherit his capacity for holding diverse armies and tribal constituencies together, and the confederation fragmented in a succession of civil wars across the 880s and 890s AH. By the time Ismail emerged from hiding, the Aq Quyunlu controlled Tabriz in name but not in fact. The plateau was available to be claimed by whoever arrived with sufficient force and sufficient conviction.

The force was the Qizilbash.

They had been building for half a century. The Safaviyya Sufi order — founded in Ardabil by Safi al-Din Ardabili in the early 700s AH as a Sunni devotional brotherhood, transformed over three generations into a militant Shia-inflected movement as its leaders drove it toward political and military power — had, under Ismail's grandfather Junayd and his father Haydar, completed that transformation. Haydar had given them the twelve-fold red headgear that made them recognizable across a battlefield: twelve folds of red wool for the twelve imams of Twelver Shia Islam, a declaration in cloth — sewn into the very shape of the helmet — that this army's loyalty was not to any temporal king but to the line of Ali and to the divine authority it carried. The order that Safi al-Din had founded had been Sunni. The army that wore his name into Tabriz was not. When Haydar was killed by the Aq Quyunlu in 893 AH, his sons were taken prisoner. Two of them were killed. Ismail, seven years old, was sent into exile in Gilan.

The forests above the Caspian smell of wet cedar and river fog. They are dense enough in the foothills that the open plateau feels like another world. Ismail grew up there, in the care of the Caspian chieftains who sheltered him, surrounded by the Qizilbash who had followed his father and who now transferred their devotion to the child. They prayed with him in the forest clearings, their red headgear visible against the green. They kept his lineage alive through the years when the Aq Quyunlu would have preferred to forget it. They waited, as people wait for something they believe is inevitable, with the patience of the certain.

He was twelve when he emerged from Gilan with a small following. He was thirteen when he won his first battles in the Pontic region, gathering Qizilbash fighters as he moved. He was fourteen when he took Tabriz.

What carried him was not only numbers. Sixty thousand Qizilbash riders followed him into Tabriz, but they did not follow him as soldiers follow a capable commander. They followed him as disciples follow a murshid — as the devoted follow the one in whom God's will has taken form. Some of them believed he was the hidden imam returned. Some believed he was the *mazhar* — the divine light made present in a human body, God's own luminescence visible through a vessel of flesh and bone. His own poems — written in Azerbaijani Turkish under the pen name Khatai, the sinner — speak in the voice of one who has fully inhabited this theology:

*I am the living Khidr, and Jesus, son of Mary.
I am the Alexander of my time.
Do not say I am just a human —
the soul of God has taken form in me.*

It is the declaration of a man who has grown up inside a theological system that made room for exactly this claim, surrounded by people who believed it and treated him accordingly. The murshid is the axis of the Sufi world. When the murshid is also of the line of Ali, when he is also the imam's representative in the hidden imam's absence, the claim is not delusion — it is the system operating as designed.

## II. The Declaration

Tabriz in the autumn of 907 AH smelled of dye and horses.

The city was one of the great trading centers of the Islamic world — a node on the Silk Road's western branch, where caravans from Anatolia met caravans from Central Asia, where merchants who dealt in silk, wool, copper, and dyes kept their counting houses and warehouses in the bazaars that lined the approach to the congregational mosque. Sixty thousand Qizilbash cavalry arriving was not an abstract political event. It was a physical transformation of the city: the drumbeats of tribal armies, the smell of horses in tens of thousands, the red headgear visible on every tower and rooftop. The city had changed rulers before. It had not changed theological identities.

Ismail gave the order. The Friday sermon — the khutba, recited weekly in every mosque across the Islamic world, the public declaration of loyalty to the caliph and the faith — would be preached in the Shia manner. The names of the twelve imams would be invoked. The cursing of the first three caliphs — Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, those whom Shia Muslims held to have usurped Ali's rightful place — would be pronounced aloud, in the congregational mosque, in front of a congregation that had been Sunni for as long as anyone could remember.

The chief religious scholar of Tabriz came to him. It is reported that the scholar said: this cannot be done. The people will not accept it. There will be blood.

Ismail answered: if a single person objects, I will draw my sword.

The Friday congregation gathered in the mosque's courtyard. The preacher ascended the minbar. In Arabic, in the formal diction of the khutba, the names were spoken: Ali ibn Abi Talib, al-Hasan, al-Husayn, Ali ibn al-Husayn — the sequence of the twelve imams, each name a statement about where authority resided, each name a negation of the caliphal tradition that Sunni Muslims upheld. The congregation listened. Some of them had never heard these words spoken in this space. The words fell into the courtyard like stones into still water.

No one objected.

The sword was not drawn. The sermon was complete.

What had just happened was not only a change of ritual. It was a declaration that this territory, this people, this state would be organized around a different account of the sacred order. The Shia tradition carries a specific theology of justice and suffering: the imams were the rightful leaders of the community, they were denied that leadership, twelve of them were killed or marginalized, and their followers carry that historical wound as the engine of their devotion. To be Shia is to be in mourning for what should have been and was not. Ismail had just made that mourning the official theology of the Iranian plateau.

## III. The Scholars

The problem was immediate.

The Safavid state had declared itself Shia. It now needed the institutional infrastructure of a Shia state: judges who could administer Shia jurisprudence, clerics who could preach the Shia manner, teachers who could instruct in the Shia theological tradition, administrators who could manage the religious endowments (awqaf) through which mosques, schools, and charitable institutions were funded. Iran did not have these people. Nine centuries of Sunni political dominance had left the Shia communities of the plateau small, scattered, and largely unequipped for institutional governance. The scholars who could do the work were elsewhere.

The summons went out to the Shia communities of the Arab world: to the scholars of Jabal Amil in what is now Lebanon, where a Shia community had maintained itself since the early Islamic centuries; to the scholars of Bahrain and the Qatif region, who had preserved the Twelver tradition through centuries of political marginalization; to the shrine cities of Iraq, where the tombs of Ali and Husayn drew Shia pilgrims and sustained communities of scholars. Come. The state has need of you. The plateau is Shia now. What it lacks is the means to be so.

Ibrahim al-Qatifi was one of those who came.

He arrived in a city still smelling of horses and dye, carrying in his baggage the Arabic texts that were the tools of his trade: the works of the great Shia jurists — Shaykh Mufid, Shaykh Tusi, Allama Hilli — who had developed Twelver jurisprudence over the centuries when no Shia state existed to implement it. He arrived to find a court where Qizilbash warriors, red-helmeted and absolutely loyal to a king some of them believed was God, sat alongside newly appointed Shia judges trying to figure out how to handle inheritance cases under Shia law in a country whose property records were organized under Sunni legal frameworks. The practical challenges were immediate and granular: which form of the testimony did Shia practice require in a property dispute? What was the Shia ruling on the validity of contracts made under the previous Sunni government? How did Shia inheritance law divide an estate that had been registered under Sunni categories?

But beneath the practical challenges was a theological question that could not be avoided.

Twelver Shia Islam had been built around the fact of the imam's absence. The Twelfth Imam had entered occultation in 260 AH. In his absence, his community waited. The scholars taught, the judges adjudicated, the devotion continued — but always with the understanding that full legitimate governance belonged to the imam alone, and that in his absence, the faithful operated in a condition of imperfect, temporary authority. This had worked when Shia communities were minorities under Sunni governments: the question of who should rightfully govern was held in the discipline of patient waiting, because someone else governed and the imam was absent. Now a Shia king had declared a Shia state. The question was no longer theoretical. Who governed in the imam's absence? On what authority?

Ali al-Karaki, a Lebanese scholar who became the most important legal mind of the early Safavid period, worked out the answer in a series of treatises that his opponents called presumptuous and his supporters called necessary. His doctrine of *niyabat al-'amma* — the general deputyship of the qualified jurists for the hidden imam — constructed the argument as follows: the hidden imam, before his full occultation, had appointed specific deputies to act in his name. When the line of specific deputies ended (in 329 AH), the imam did not withdraw his authority entirely — he extended it, in general form, to all qualified Shia jurists. A jurist who meets the conditions of learning, piety, and judgment is the imam's deputy in the full sense: authorized to judge, to collect the khums tax, to administer the religious endowments, and to conduct the affairs of government under the imam's mandate.

Al-Karaki was in his study in Tabriz or Qazvin — the smell of ink, the weight of books stacked on shelves around him, the sound of students reciting texts in the adjacent room — working through the chain of reasoning with the precision of a man who knew that every step had to hold, because the state's legitimacy depended on it. If the jurist is the imam's general deputy, and if the king enforces the jurist's judgments with military power, then the king's authority is legitimate not by charismatic claim but by institutional delegation. The sword enforces the law. The law derives from the jurist. The jurist derives from the imam. The chain is complete.

The Qizilbash, who believed their king was God, and the scholars, who believed their senior jurist was the imam's legal delegate, had arrived at a working arrangement. It was not a comfortable one. The tension between charismatic and legal authority would shape Safavid history for two centuries. But in the founding era, neither side had the luxury of pursuing the tension to its conclusion. The plateau needed staffing. The scholars staffed it.

## IV. The Sacred Map

In the great audience hall of the Safavid court, the floor was the world.

The carpet beneath the courtiers' feet — a garden carpet, perhaps twenty feet by forty, woven in wool and silk by masters working from a designer's cartoon — showed the divine order made visible from above. Four water channels divided the field into quadrants, each quadrant filled with the flowers of paradise: iris, pomegranate, lotus, the cypress of immortality. At the center, the fountain or medallion from which the channels flowed. Animals grazed among the blossoms: deer, pheasants, lions at rest. The borders contained this world within a frame the way a garden wall contains a garden.

*Pardis* — the word from which "paradise" comes, borrowed by Persian from the Avestan *pairi.daeza*, the walled garden of a king — was not a metaphor for the Safavid court. It was a claim. The garden that Ahura Mazda had promised the faithful, the divine order that would prevail when the hidden imam returned, was being made present in wool and silk on the floor of the throne room. The sovereign sitting at the room's center was not merely a king. He was the axis of this garden, the point from which the four channels of divine authority flowed.

Outside the window, the royal garden made the same claim in water and cypress and rose. The *chaharbagh* — the four-garden, the formal Persian garden laid out with precisely the same quadripartite structure as the carpet — repeated the same theological image in a different medium. A courtier in the audience hall was simultaneously in two *pardis* at once, the carpet below his feet and the garden through the window, and behind both images was the third paradise that Twelver theology promised: the restored order of the imam, the end of the age of occultation, the divine garden made fully real at last.

The Safavid aesthetic was not decoration. It was argument. The naqqash — the master designer who produced the cartoon from which the weavers worked — was not primarily a craftsman. He was a theologian working in wool. The geometric patterns that filled the borders of the great court carpets, the interlace of arabesque and vegetal form, expressed the same mathematical order that Islamic scholars saw in the structure of creation: the unity of God manifesting in the infinite variety of forms. Every knot in a court carpet was tied by weavers who worked from above, following the cartoon laid across the loom, producing from their position on the weaving bench a form they could not see whole — the same position, the Safavid cosmology suggested, that human beings occupied in relation to the divine plan they were weaving through their lives.

The Ardabil carpet — woven in 946 AH for the shrine of Safi al-Din, the order's founder, by master weavers using twenty-five million individual knots — carries at one end a lamp inscription from Hafiz: *I have no refuge in the world other than thy threshold. There is no shelter for my head other than this door.* The carpet itself is the threshold. The shrine is the door. The devotee who steps onto it steps into the sacred geography of the order that made the Safavid state possible.

## V. Chaldiran

Sultan Selim I was not a patient man.

He had removed his father from the Ottoman throne in a coup in 918 AH, and he had a clear idea of what needed to be done about the Safavid problem. Before marching east, he dealt with the problem's internal dimension: the Alevi and Shia communities of Anatolia, who had been a Safavid recruitment base since before the founding. His administrators compiled lists. In the spring of 920 AH, Ottoman forces moved through eastern Anatolia and killed, by the estimates that survive, somewhere between twenty and forty thousand people. The Qizilbash who had not already crossed to Ismail's side would not be crossing now.

Selim's army marched east in high summer: Janissaries with muskets, artillery hauled over Anatolian roads, cavalry in numbers that could match anything Ismail could field. He sent messages to Ismail in the manner of a man inviting a response. Ismail's responses were cool. He offered the Ottoman sultan prayer beads, perhaps as a suggestion about appropriate activities for a man of his age. Selim was forty-six. Ismail was thirty-two.

Chaldiran, in August of 920 AH: a broad plain in northwestern Iran, west of Tabriz, where the two armies met in the first week of the month Rajab.

The Qizilbash cavalry came as they always came: in the name of the murshid, in the name of Ali, in the certainty that the divine will was expressed through the man they followed. They had charged armies before and the armies had broken. The Ottoman army did not break. The Ottoman artillery — cannons dug into field redoubts, protected by chains between the wagons — held the center while the Janissaries' musket fire did the systematic work that cavalry charges cannot survive. The Qizilbash rode into the cannon smoke and the musket volleys and died by the thousand. Their military virtue — the absolute willingness to advance, grounded in the theology of the murshid's divine protection — was precisely what made them vulnerable to weapons that did not respect devotion.

Ismail was wounded. His favorite wife, Tajlu Khanum, was captured. He withdrew.

The Ottomans entered Tabriz and held it for eight days before the supply lines failed and they withdrew. Selim could not keep the city, and he knew it. He took what he could — craftsmen, scholars, treasures — and marched back to Anatolia. The Safavid state survived.

What did not survive was unchanged.

Inside the Qizilbash world, Chaldiran was processed through the theological categories available. The imam's cause is not defeated by worldly reversal — this is a teaching as old as Karbala, where Husayn ibn Ali and seventy-two companions faced the Umayyad army and were killed. Chaldiran was not Karbala, but the theological grammar was the same: the just cause endures its test. The murshid has been tried. The faithful do not abandon him when God tests him.

But Ismail knew something his followers' theology could absorb more easily than he could. He had believed, in some register of himself, that the divine protection was real. That the charge would work. That the cannon smoke would part. It had not. He was thirty-two years old and he understood, on that plain, that he was a man.

He did not say this. He continued to reign. The Qizilbash continued to follow him. The clerical infrastructure continued to build. The poets of the court continued to praise him as the murshid, as the shadow of the imam, as the divine light in human form. But those who were close to him in the years after Chaldiran noted that he drank more heavily, hunted with greater frequency, appeared less often in the full ceremony of court. He wrote poems in Turkish, still under the name Khatai — the sinner — but the voice had changed:

*My heart is like the bird that has fallen from the nest.
The world has turned. The beloved has gone far.
I, Khatai, am dust at the foot of the saints.
Weep for me, or do not. Either way.*

## VI. The Endurance

Ismail died in 930 AH / 1524 CE. He was thirty-six years old.

He left a state that had not existed twenty-three years before. The Iranian plateau was Shia. Not completely, not without resistance, not without the compromises and negotiations that any religious transformation of a large population requires. But the Friday sermon was preached in the Shia manner in every congregational mosque on the plateau. The clerical infrastructure that Ibrahim al-Qatifi and Ali al-Karaki and the other scholars from Bahrain and Lebanon had come to build was taking form: Shia judges in the major cities, the endowment system reorganized under Shia legal frameworks, the first students entering theological schools that had not existed before 907 AH.

The Qizilbash still wore their red helmets. They still followed the murshid's successors with the same devotion. The theology had absorbed Chaldiran and continued. The dynasty would continue for two more centuries. Shah Abbas I would rebuild Isfahan into one of the great cities of the Islamic world. The Silk Road silk trade would be reorganized. The Persian miniature would reach its greatest height.

But what Ismail had founded in the autumn of 907 AH — the declaration spoken in the mosque, the sword kept in its sheath while the Shia khutba fell into the silence of the congregation — outlasted all of it. The theological identity he imposed, and that the scholars he summoned built the institutional frame around, and that the Qizilbash defended with their cavalry and their certainty, became the identity of a country. Not only of the Safavid dynasty. Of the country itself.

Ibrahim al-Qatifi, who had sailed north not knowing what he was sailing toward, spent his years in that country writing law. His *al-Siraj al-Wahhaj* — The Blazing Lamp — worked through the cases that a Shia judge encountered in a newly Shia state: property disputes, inheritance conflicts, the question of ritual validity, the application of Shia law to commercial relationships formed under the previous Sunni order. His lamp was a lamp for practical problems, lit by a tradition that had been waiting, in the Gulf's small communities, for exactly this moment: a state that needed what it knew.

The carpet on the throne room floor still showed the garden from above. The cypress stood at the quadrant's boundary. The channels ran toward the center. Outside the window, the roses were blooming.

The sermon had been preached. The country knew what it was.
