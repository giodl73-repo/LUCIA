# Figures — The High Tang

## Du Fu (杜甫), 712–770 CE

Du Fu came from a family that valued the Confucian tradition of public service. His grandfather, Du Shenyan, had been a minor court poet. His father held a modest official post. The family expected Du Fu to pass the jinshi examination and enter the bureaucracy, and Du Fu expected it of himself. He failed. He failed repeatedly, across more than a decade of attempts, and the failure did not make him bitter so much as it made him attentive. A man who has been rejected by the system he believes in does not stop believing. He watches more carefully.

His early poems are confident, expansive, the work of a young man who expects the world to open for him. His later poems are something else. After the An Lushan rebellion, after his capture in occupied Chang'an, after years of exile and poverty and the deaths of one of his children to starvation, Du Fu wrote with a directness that the Tang poetic tradition had not previously achieved. He wrote about soldiers and their families. He wrote about refugees. He wrote about the tax collector coming to a household where only a daughter-in-law remained because every man had been conscripted. He wrote about his own cold, his own hunger, his own white hair.

Later generations called him "the poet-sage," shisheng, and the title is not merely honorary. Du Fu's poems are treated in the Chinese tradition as a moral record — evidence of what it means to look at the world honestly and refuse to stop looking, even when what you see is unbearable. He never received the official career he sought. He received something the bureaucracy could not grant: thirteen centuries of readers who recognize in his lines the sound of a conscience that would not be quiet.

## Li Bai (李白), 701–762 CE

Li Bai's origins are uncertain and he preferred them that way. He may have been born in Central Asia, in the Broken Leaf garrison (Suyab, in modern Kyrgyzstan), where his family had been exiled or had emigrated. He may have had Turkic or Sogdian ancestry. He never sat for the jinshi examination — some scholars believe his family's merchant background made him ineligible under Tang sumptuary laws, but Li Bai himself seemed indifferent to the question. He presented himself as a swordsman, a wanderer, a Daoist aspirant, and a poet whose talent required no institutional validation.

He was not wrong. When Xuanzong summoned him to the Hanlin Academy around 742, it was not because Li Bai had proven himself through the examination system but because his poems had reached the court and the emperor wanted to see the man who wrote them. The appointment lasted approximately two years. Li Bai drank. He offended courtiers. He wrote poems of astonishing beauty on command and poems of blistering contempt when he felt constrained. The court dismissed him, and Li Bai took dismissal as a gift.

His voice is the voice of freedom within the Tang world — not freedom from the Tang, but freedom within it, the freedom of the Daoist sage who sees through the structures that bind ordinary men and laughs. His poems about the moon, about wine, about mountains and rivers and the passage of time, carry an ease that is almost impossible to imitate because the ease is real. He was not performing spontaneity. He was spontaneous. When the rebellion came, Li Bai briefly joined the retinue of Prince Yong, the emperor's sixteenth son, who was attempting to establish his own power base in the south. The prince failed, and Li Bai was arrested and exiled. His final years were diminished, the freedom that had defined him now indistinguishable from purposelessness. The Tang remembered the young Li Bai, the one who reached for the moon. That was the one worth remembering.

## Emperor Xuanzong (唐玄宗, Li Longji), 685–762 CE

Xuanzong came to power through violence and ruled, for thirty years, with genuine competence. He was the third son of Emperor Ruizong, and he seized the throne from the Empress Wei's faction in a palace coup in 710, then maneuvered his aunt, the formidable Princess Taiping, out of power in 713. The early Kaiyuan years were the peak of Tang governance: competent ministers, fiscal reform, military expansion, the Silk Road open and profitable.

He was also a man of extraordinary artistic cultivation. He played the jie gu drum. He composed music. He established the Pear Garden conservatory, and the term "children of the Pear Garden" (liyuan zidi) is still used in Chinese to refer to performers of traditional opera. His patronage of the arts was not separate from his governance; in the Tang system, aesthetic refinement and political authority were expressions of the same legitimacy.

His love for Yang Guifei changed him, or rather it coincided with a change that had already begun: the exhaustion of a ruler who had been making decisions for three decades. The historians blame Yang Guifei for the emperor's withdrawal. The record is more ambiguous. Xuanzong had been delegating authority to Chancellor Li Linfu since the early 730s, well before Yang Guifei entered his life. What she provided was not distraction but consolation — the company of a person who offered beauty and affection in a court that offered only calculation. Whether that consolation cost the empire is a question the Tang debated for centuries and never settled.

After Mawei, after the rebellion, Xuanzong abdicated in favor of his son Suzong and lived his final years as a retired emperor in the Xingqing Palace, attended by a diminished household, listening to music in rooms that had been built for a court that no longer existed. He died in 762, the same year as Li Bai.

## Yang Guifei (楊貴妃, Yang Yuhuan), 719–756 CE

The sources tell us she was beautiful. They tell us she danced. They tell us the emperor loved her. They tell us her family rose to power on her proximity to the throne. They tell us soldiers strangled her at Mawei because someone had to be blamed for the rebellion and she was there and she could not fight back.

What the sources do not tell us is what she thought.

Yang Yuhuan was the daughter of a minor official in Sichuan. She was originally married to Xuanzong's son Li Mao, the Prince of Shou. The emperor saw her, desired her, and arranged for her to enter a Daoist convent — a formality that dissolved her existing marriage — before taking her into his own household. She was twenty-six. He was fifty-six. The power differential requires no commentary.

Within the court, she was a figure of extraordinary cultural influence. The Nishang Yuyi dance was not merely a performance; it was a collaboration between her artistry and the emperor's composition, and it became the defining artistic expression of the Tianbao court. She was a musician herself — the pipa was her instrument. She had a quick wit, a capacity for managing the complex emotional dynamics of a court that revolved around her, and the kind of presence that the sources, written by men who were simultaneously fascinated and threatened, describe as "overwhelming."

Her cousin Yang Guozhong's rise to the chancellorship was the political consequence of her position, and the resentment it generated was the kindling that An Lushan's rebellion ignited. At Mawei, the soldiers' demand was simple: the Yang family caused this; the Yang family must pay. Xuanzong wept. He consented. Bai Juyi, writing fifty years later, gave the moment its literary form: "In the dust before the army, she died, and the flower-adorned hairpin fell to the ground, and no one picked it up." The hairpin, the ground, the absence of anyone willing to pick it up — the detail is precise and terrible because it reduces a political execution to an object no one will touch.

## An Lushan (安祿山), 703–757 CE

An Lushan grew up speaking the languages of the frontier: Sogdian from his father's people, Turkic from his mother's, and Chinese learned as the language of the empire he intended to serve and then to seize. He was a market trader before he was a soldier, working the horse markets where the steppe met the settled world, and the commercial instinct never left him.

His military career was rapid. Multilingual, shrewd with non-Chinese soldiers, he rose through the northeastern frontier armies until by the mid-740s he held three jiedushi commissions simultaneously — a concentration of military power the Tang system was not designed to permit. He played the fool at court: the whirling dance, the enormous belly, the clowning for the emperor and the "adopted son" relationship with Yang Guifei were performances calculated to make the court see a buffoon rather than a threat.

When he marched in 755, the speed of his advance revealed how thoroughly the Tang had delegated military power to its frontier commanders. He declared himself emperor of the Yan dynasty in Luoyang and held power for two years before his own son, An Qingxu, murdered him in 757 — the rebellion consuming its own creator the way the Tang system had consumed the conditions that made the rebellion possible.
