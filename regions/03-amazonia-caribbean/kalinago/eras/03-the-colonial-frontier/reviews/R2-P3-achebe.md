# PANEL-2 Review — P-3 Chinua Achebe
## Kalinago, Era 3: The Colonial Frontier — Notes Review
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The production bible handles the source problem correctly. The decision to use European accounts as evidence about the Europeans — not about the Kalinago — is the right technique for this chapter. And the Breton dictionary as structural pivot is the best available solution to the inside-voice problem: the Kalinago language, preserved in a colonial document, speaks from inside regardless of who held the pen.

**The composite figure question:**

I want to be direct about what the composite figures require. The notes say these figures will be "disclosed honestly in figures.md." The honesty of disclosure is necessary but not sufficient. The figures must also be constructed correctly — meaning: from what we know the Kalinago actually knew, actually did, actually thought. They cannot be constructed from what we imagine people in resistance situations feel. The composite war captain's knowledge must be drawn from the archaeological and linguistic record: what navigation terms appear in the Breton dictionary, what route evidence appears in the archaeological record, what tactical decisions appear in the European accounts (read against their grain). If the chapter makes the composite war captain worry about things the Kalinago record does not support, it has crossed from historical composite into historical fiction.

**The explaining-voice risk:**

The notes include this: "Raymond Breton lived among the Kalinago on Dominica from 1642 to 1654 — the very end of the era this chapter covers. His dictionary preserves Kalinago vocabulary: words for kinship, warfare, sea navigation, plant use, cosmology. The words are the evidence. What a culture names, it attends to." This is excellent as notes. In the chapter itself, "the words are the evidence" cannot appear — that is the author's observation. In the chapter, the words must simply be presented, and the reader must arrive at that conclusion. Show the Kalinago words. Trust the reader to understand what it means that these words exist.

**The cannibal frame:**

The notes correctly note that the word does not appear in the opening. It must not appear in the chapter either. But — and this is the harder requirement — the chapter must address the semantic theft without naming it as such from outside. The Kalinago knew they were being renamed. The Breton dictionary may have a Kalinago word for the outsiders' name they were given. If so, that word, and the Kalinago attitude embedded in it, is the way to address the renaming from inside. If not, the chapter should find a Kalinago character who knows that the strangers are calling them something wrong, and who makes that knowledge present through action or speech, not through editorial note.

**The question I'd push on:** Open Question 2 (African-Kalinago contact in Era 3) should be answered directly. The first African enslaved people reached the Caribbean by 1505. Escaped communities (cimarrones) were present on several islands within decades. The Kalinago encountered them. What happened at those encounters? The Kalinago raid-integration economy had a category for people from outside the island-world. Were Africans integrated through the same mechanism? What does the record say, and what does it not say? The chapter should go to the edge of what is known and stop there, with honest uncertainty, rather than leaving the question open.

---

*The voice risk is managed well at the notes level. The chapter must maintain the same discipline: every moment of analysis must be carried by the story, not stated above it.*
