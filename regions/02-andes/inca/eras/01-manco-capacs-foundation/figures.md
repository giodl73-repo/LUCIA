# Figures — Manco Cápac's Foundation (Era 1)

## Manco Cápac (mythological)

First Sapa Inca. Son of Inti the sun. Emerged from Lake Titicaca with Mama Ocllo carrying a golden staff. Founded Qusqu where the staff sank into the earth.

**What we know:** Manco Cápac is attested in all major colonial chronicles (Cieza de Leon, Betanzos, Cobo, Garcilaso) and in the quipucamayoc genealogies reported to those chroniclers. The origin myth is consistent across sources in its core structure: emergence from the lake, the golden staff, the founding of Cusco. There is a variant tradition (reported by Betanzos and others) placing the emergence at Pacaritambo, the "place of origin," rather than Titicaca, which suggests the myth was adapted as the Inca's political reach extended southward to incorporate the Titicaca basin into their legitimating geography.

**What we cannot know:** Whether a historical individual corresponds to the mythic figure. The genealogy of eight Sapa Incas before Pachacuti is carried in the chronicles but cannot be independently verified through archaeology or non-Inca sources. The chapter treats Manco Cápac as what the Inca intended: a mythic founder whose story authorizes the state that tells it. No interiority is attributed, no historical personality is invented.

**Sources:** Cieza de Leon, *Cronica del Peru* (1553); Betanzos, *Suma y Narracion de los Incas* (1557); Cobo, *Historia del Nuevo Mundo* (1653); Garcilaso de la Vega, *Comentarios Reales* (1609); Julien, *Reading Inca History* (2000).

## Mama Ocllo (mythological)

Co-founder. Sister-wife of Manco Cápac. Bearer of the weaving arts in traditions reported by Cobo and others.

**What we know:** Mama Ocllo appears in the origin myth as Manco Cápac's partner, carrying complementary knowledge (weaving, domestic arts) to his political and military role. This pairing reflects the Andean concept of complementarity between male and female roles, which was structural, not ornamental. The textile arts attributed to her were the foundation of the Andean economy's most important product.

**What we cannot know:** Same limits as Manco Cápac. A mythic figure whose historicity cannot be established.

**Sources:** Cobo (1653); Silverblatt, *Moon, Sun, and Witches* (1987); Murra, *Economic Organization of the Inka State* (1980).

## The Quipucamayoc (composite figure)

A reader of quipus in the pre-imperial period, presented in the chapter as a composite drawn from multiple colonial descriptions and modern scholarly analysis.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual quipucamayoc from the pre-1400 period can be identified by name. The scene in Section IV is assembled from: Garcilaso's descriptions of quipucamayoc practice; Urton's analysis of quipu physical structure and encoding methods; Salomon's work on the Tupicocha quipu keepers who maintained a living tradition into the colonial period; and the physical evidence of the approximately 900 surviving quipus. The chapter explicitly identifies this as a composite moment. The details of the quipucamayoc's practice (sitting posture, finger movement along cords, color identification) are consistent with the colonial sources and the physical evidence but are combined into a single scene for narrative purposes.

**Sources:** Garcilaso de la Vega (1609); Urton, *Signs of the Inka Khipu* (2003); Salomon, *The Cord Keepers* (2004); Ascher & Ascher, *Mathematics of the Incas* (1981).

## The Weaver (composite figure, female)

A woman weaving at a backstrap loom, presented as representative of the Andean textile tradition.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual weaver from the pre-imperial period is named in any source. The scene in Section V is assembled from: archaeological evidence of backstrap loom technology in the Andes (continuous tradition from at least 3000 BCE); colonial descriptions of weaving practice; Silverblatt's analysis of women's labor in the Inca economy; and the surviving textiles themselves, which demonstrate the technique, materials, and complexity described. The weaver is not a specific individual but a representation of a practice attested across millennia.

**Sources:** Silverblatt (1987); Murra (1980); Phipps, Hecht & Esteras, *The Colonial Andes: Tapestries and Silverwork* (2004); Frame, *Andean Art at Dumbarton Oaks* (1986).

## The Ayllu Kuraka (composite figure)

A headman of a Cusco valley ayllu, mediating reciprocal obligations.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual kuraka from the pre-imperial Cusco valley period can be identified. The description of the kuraka's role is drawn from colonial administrative records that describe the kuraka system as it functioned in the early colonial period (Spalding 1984) and from the inference that the system predated the empire since the Inca governed through it rather than replacing it. Covey's archaeological work on the pre-imperial Cusco valley provides the material context.

**Sources:** Spalding, *Huarochiri: An Andean Society Under Inca and Spanish Rule* (1984); Covey, *How the Incas Built Their Heartland* (2006); Bauer, *The Development of the Inca State* (1992).

## A Note on Sources

The pre-imperial Inca period presents the sharpest version of a challenge that runs through this entire book: our knowledge of the Inca comes overwhelmingly through Spanish colonial filters. The quipucamayoc tradition, which carried the Inca's own historical memory, was disrupted and largely destroyed within decades of the conquest. What survives in the colonial chronicles is what the Spanish asked about, in the categories the Spanish understood, recorded by men whose purposes ranged from genuine ethnographic curiosity (Cieza de Leon) to legal advocacy (Garcilaso) to theological justification of the conquest (many others).

The chapter uses these sources while acknowledging the filter. Composite figures are explicitly identified. The distinction between myth and archaeology is maintained without ranking one above the other. The quipucamayoc tradition is treated as a knowledge system that was destroyed, not as an absence that the colonial record fills.
