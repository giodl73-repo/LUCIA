# Kerma — The Kingdom at the Third Cataract

The mound was already old when they opened it again to receive the king.

Four generations of hands had shaped it, carrying the dark Nile mud in baskets up the growing slope until it rose above the plain like a hill that the river had not made. The women who packed the mud worked in the cool hours before dawn, when the cattle were still and the air carried the smell of dung fires and wet earth, and they pressed each layer smooth because smoothness was what the dead required. The mound stood south of the city of Kerma, between the river and the eastern desert, and from the far bank it could be seen for the distance a herdsman walks in half a morning. It was meant to be seen. A king who cannot be found after death has ruled for nothing.

They brought the cattle first. Two hundred head, the finest of the royal herds, the long-horned breed that grazed the savanna between the Third Cataract and the gold country to the south. The herdsmen who led them knew each animal by the shape of its horns and the pattern of its hide and the sound of its particular lowing at dusk, and they led them into the corridor that ran along the southern face of the mound, and there the cattle were killed, and their bodies were laid in rows with their horns facing east, toward the place where the sun returned each morning. Cattle were not possessions in the land the Egyptians called Kush. They were the measure of the world. A man's herd was his voice in council, his daughter's marriage price, his claim upon the respect of other men, and his passage into whatever came after the body was still. A king's herd, at the hour of his burial, became the proof that his life had weight.

Then the people came. Not the herdsmen, not the women who had packed the mud. The others. Three hundred of them, perhaps more. They walked into the corridor and they lay down beside the cattle and they did not come out. The excavators who opened these graves four thousand years later found them arranged in careful rows, their bodies flexed, their hands near their faces, some still wearing the leather garments and the shell ornaments they had put on that morning. There is no evidence of violence on the bones. There is no evidence of restraint. The people who went into the mound went willingly, or at least they went in the manner that their world required, and the distinction between those two things belongs to them and not to us.

The great deffufa stood above it all. Mud-brick, massive, the color of the floodplain baked hard by the sun, rising from the city's center until a man standing at its base had to lean back to see the top. The people of Kerma built in mud, in the material the river left behind each year, and they built large, and they built on their own terms. The deffufa was not a pyramid. It was not an imitation of a pyramid. It shared nothing with the structures rising at that same moment six hundred miles to the north except the ambition to make power visible. The Nile ran past both, carrying the same silt, feeding the same black soil, but what each people built from that soil was their own.

Kerma was old before Egypt took notice. By the time the scribes of the Middle Kingdom pharaohs began writing about the troublesome southerners, the troublesome southerners had been burying their kings in mounds visible from the river for five hundred years. They had been trading gold and ebony and incense northward, and receiving copper and faience and oil in return, and growing wealthy on the exchange. They had been organizing labor on a scale that required administration, surplus, and command. They had been doing all of this in a place where the Nile narrows between cataracts and the desert presses close and the arable land is a thin ribbon and everything depends on the river and the rain and the grass and the cattle that eat the grass.

The river ran north. The gold went with it. The cattle stayed.
