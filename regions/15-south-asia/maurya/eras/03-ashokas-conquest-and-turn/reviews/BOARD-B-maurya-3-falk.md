## B-maurya-3 — Harry Falk — Board Review of Ashoka's Conquest and Turn

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Ashokan epigraphy, Indian palaeography, site archaeology. Author of Asokan Sites and Artefacts (2006), the comprehensive survey of all known Ashokan inscription sites.

**Historical accuracy:**
- **Dhauli:** Described as "a low laterite hill." The Dhauli hill is indeed laterite. The description of the Daya River flowing through rice fields toward the coast is geographically accurate. The carved elephant above the inscription is correctly noted as "the first known example of Indian stone sculpture in the round." The letters described as "cut deep enough to be legible from a distance on a clear day" is consistent with the Dhauli inscriptions' legibility.
- **Girnar:** Described as "a single enormous rock beside a sacred lake at the base of a mountain" with the stone identified as "granite, dark, harder to carve." The Girnar rock is actually a large boulder near Junagadh, and the identification as granite is approximately correct (the geological classification is more complex, involving a range of igneous rock types, but "granite" is an acceptable simplification for narrative purposes). The observation that the letters are "sharper, narrower, adapted to the resistance of the stone" is accurate: the Girnar inscription shows a different carving technique from the softer-stone sites.
- **Kandahar:** The bilingual Greek-Aramaic edict is correctly identified. The translation of dhamma as eusebeia is noted in the scholarly literature.
- **Shahbazgarhi and Mansehra:** Correctly identified as Kharosthi script sites in the northwest.
- **Sarnath pillar:** The four-lion capital, the lotus base, the frieze with elephant, horse, bull, and lion separated by wheels, the height of over twelve meters, the Chunar sandstone quarry origin, and the approximate weight of twenty-five tons are all within the range of scholarly estimates. The polish that led early observers to believe the pillars were metal is a well-documented observation.
- **Lumbini:** The pillar and its inscription recording Ashoka's visit and the tax reduction are correctly described. "Twenty years after his consecration" is consistent with the inscription.
- **Scripts:** Brahmi and Kharosthi are correctly attributed to their respective geographic zones. The Greek and Aramaic at Kandahar are correctly placed on the empire's western frontier.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's treatment of the edicts as material objects in landscapes is exceptionally strong. The variation in stone type, carving technique, and site placement across the empire is accurately represented. The observation that "the mason adjusted" (at Girnar) captures an important epigraphic reality: the edicts were not mechanically reproduced. Each site required adaptation to local materials and conditions.
- The emphasis on the edicts as communicative objects (meant to be read aloud, placed at crossroads and travel routes) is consistent with the site selection patterns documented in the archaeological survey.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The materiality of inscription is given appropriate weight. The chapter understands that the stone type, the carving depth, the site placement, and the script choice are all part of the edicts' meaning, not incidental to it.
- The Chunar quarry identification and the transport logistics (hundreds of miles) are accurate.
- The Dhauli elephant is correctly identified and its significance as early Indian stone sculpture is accurately noted.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter says "early British observers believed them to be metal." This is attributed to various colonial-era visitors. The more precise claim is that the Maurya polish on the pillars was so fine that visitors commented on its metallic appearance. The chapter's phrasing is acceptable as narrative shorthand.
- The chapter does not specify whether the Dhauli inscription is a Major Rock Edict or a Separate Kalinga Rock Edict. In fact, Dhauli contains both: a version of Rock Edicts 1-10 (with RE 11-13 replaced by the Separate Kalinga Rock Edicts). This distinction is important epigraphically but may be too granular for narrative purposes.

**Verdict:** APPROVED

The chapter's physical descriptions are accurate. The material culture of the Ashokan edicts is well-represented. No corrections required, though the Dhauli edict categorization could be noted in the production notes for precision.
