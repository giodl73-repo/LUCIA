# When the Ancestors Walked

## The Song

The old man sang the country into being as he walked.

Not loudly. Not for performance. The song came out of him nasal and rhythmic, pitched low against the cold air, each phrase rising and falling on a breath, and the land answered it. The sound carried across the flat ground the way sound carries in the desert before sunrise: clean, undamped, as though the air itself were thinner. Each verse carried a place: the ridge where the sandstone broke into shelves and the water pooled after rain, the stand of desert oaks whose bark smelled of camphor in the heat and where the honey ants built their galleries deep beneath the roots, the clay pan that held water for three months after the wet season and then dried to a surface so hard it rang under your feet. Maku knew these places the way he knew his own hands. He had walked this stretch of Tjukurpa since his father first brought him here as a boy, singing him through the country, teaching him what the song held.

Maku's feet found the stone shelf where the sandstone surfaced, and the song changed on the hard ground, his voice thinning against rock the way water thins when it runs over stone. The song was not about the country. The song was the country. In the Tjukurpa, which is not a time but the ground of all time, the Ancestor beings had walked this same ground when nothing existed but potential. They had sung it into form. Where the Honey Ant Ancestor stopped and dug, the honey ant galleries appeared. Where the Rain Ancestor wept, the waterholes filled. Where the Two Boys ran from the snake, the ridgeline cracked into the gully that carried floodwater east toward the salt lake. Every feature of the land held an act, and the song recorded that act, and to sing the song while walking the country was to re-enter the original making. Not to remember it. To participate in it. The Tjukurpa was not the past. It was the ground beneath the present, holding everything up.

Maku walked east with two younger men. The sun was not yet high and the desert morning was cold enough that the spinifex was silver with dew, each clump catching the light in a way that made the red ground look jewelled. By midday the heat would flatten everything, bleach the colour out of the sky, drive the lizards under rock, and the walking would become a different thing, measured and slow, mouth closed to keep moisture in. But now, in the early hours, they moved easily, bare feet reading the sand. Maku pointed with his lips, the way you point in this country, and the younger men saw: the track of a perentie, a large one, the tail drag cutting a line between the footprints. Old track. Two days, maybe. The perentie was heading for the same water they were heading for, because everything in this landscape was heading for water, and the knowledge of where water lived and when it died was the knowledge that held all other knowledge together.

The song shifted as they crossed into new country. The melody changed, because this stretch belonged to a different Ancestor and the song followed the Ancestor, not the walker. But the rhythm held, because rhythm is how you walk and how you breathe and how the country itself moves, in seasons, in cycles of rain and dry, in the slow turning of the stars that told you when the rain would come and when the food plants would fruit and when the ceremonies must be performed to keep the whole system alive.

Maku was not the only person carrying this song. Hundreds of kilometers to the west, other custodians held the earlier sections. Hundreds of kilometers to the east, others held what came after. The Songline crossed language boundaries: the words changed as the country changed, because the people of each stretch sang in their own tongue, but the melody stayed the same, a thread of sound running across the whole of the country from water to water, from sacred site to sacred site, connecting nations that spoke different languages and followed different kinship laws into a single web of shared obligation. If you knew the song, you could walk the country. If you could walk the country, you could live. The song was not decoration. It was infrastructure.


## The Burning

Ngali waited for the wind to settle.

She stood at the edge of the spinifex plain with a slow-burning branch in her hand, the bark still smoking where the fire had eaten into the wood, and she watched the grass. The spinifex was dry. It had been dry for weeks, the green leached out of it by the cold season, and the clumps stood stiff and pale across the red sand, each one a dome of resinous needles that would burn fast and hot once the fire touched it. But fast and hot was not what Ngali wanted. She wanted slow and low. She wanted the fire to creep through the understory, clearing the dead growth, opening the ground to light, and stopping at the edges she had chosen.

The wind dropped. Ngali touched the branch to the base of a spinifex clump and stepped back. The fire caught with a soft rush, the resin in the needles flaring yellow, and the smoke went up straight in the still air. She watched it. The smoke told her the fire's direction and speed the way a track in sand tells a walker what has passed. She moved along the edge of the burn, touching fire to the next clump and the next, spacing the ignitions so that the fire fronts would meet in the middle and die there, having consumed what needed consuming and left what needed to remain.

This was not random burning. This was the reading and rewriting of country.

Behind Ngali, the ground she had burned three seasons ago was thick with new growth: the bright green of fresh spinifex shoots, the first leaves of solanum bushes that fruited only after fire, the tiny flowers of native tobacco plants emerging from ash-enriched soil. Wallabies had already found it. Their tracks crossed the regenerated ground in every direction, converging on the new growth the way the tracks of everything alive in this landscape converged on food and water. The wallabies drew the hunters. The hunters maintained the burning. The burning maintained the wallabies. Each act sustained the next in a cycle that had been turning for longer than anyone could count, and Ngali's grandmother had taught her this the way Maku's father had taught him the song: by walking the country with her, burning beside her, showing her what to read in the smoke and the wind and the regrowth.

The precision was in the sequencing. You did not burn everything at once. You burned this patch in the cool season and that patch in the warm season and the patch between them not at all this year, because the mulga trees on it needed three years to seed and the seed needed undisturbed ground to germinate. The result, across the span of a single custodian's lifetime of burning, was a mosaic: patches of country at every stage of regrowth, from freshly burned ground to mature scrub, each stage offering different food to different animals and different plants, the whole landscape shaped into a patchwork of abundance that looked, to a person who did not know what they were seeing, like untouched wilderness.

When the first Europeans walked into this country, they saw open woodland, grassy plains, park-like landscapes that reminded them of the estates of English gentlemen. They wrote in their journals about the beauty of the land. They did not ask who had made it beautiful. The answer was Ngali and every woman and man who had burned this country before her, generation after generation, reading the wind and the grass and the season and the regrowth with an attention so sustained and so precise that much of the visible landscape was their work. The burning varied by country: desert nations burned spinifex, woodland nations burned grass, coastal nations used fire to open pathways to the shore. Some places, deep rainforest and high alpine country, were less intensively managed, though even these felt the influence of burning on their margins. But the principle held across the land. They had not farmed in the way the newcomers understood farming. They had done something deeper. They had shaped a country.

The knowledge encoded in Ngali's burning was not hers alone. It was held in the same Songline network that held the waterholes and the law. The Dreaming stories for a stretch of country specified how that country should be maintained, and burning was part of the maintenance, as much a ceremonial obligation as a practical one. To stop burning was to stop caring for country, and to stop caring for country was to let the Ancestors' work come undone. Fire was not a tool separate from the Dreaming. Fire was part of the Dreaming's instructions for how the world must be kept alive.

Ngali moved along the fire line. The smoke thinned. The burnt ground behind her was black and still warm, smelling of ash and the faint sweetness of burned resin, and already, in the cracks where the heat had opened the soil, the seeds of the fire-dependent plants were beginning to respond.


## The Stars

At night, after the fires were down to coals and the cold pressed in from every horizon, Jirri lay on his back on warm sand and looked up.

The sky in this country, away from any light that was not starlight, is not a dome. It is a depth. The stars do not decorate it. They fill it, layer behind layer, until the eye gives up trying to separate the near from the far and the sky becomes a single field of white fire. The Milky Way cuts across it in a band so bright it casts faint shadows on the ground, and within that band the dark spaces between the stars form shapes as definite as the bright ones.

Jirri's uncle sat beside him and pointed. Not at the bright stars. At the darkness.

The dark shape that ran along the Milky Way was an emu. The head was the dark nebula beside the Southern Cross. The neck stretched northeast. The body widened into the dark clouds of Scorpius and Sagittarius. Jirri had known the Emu in the Sky since he was small, the way he had known the shapes of the animals on the ground, but tonight his uncle was teaching him something else: when to read it.

When the Emu's legs appeared above the eastern horizon at dusk, the real emus were laying their eggs. The timing was precise. The Emu constellation's position in the sky tracked the breeding cycle of the birds on the ground, and for the people who needed to know when the eggs would be ready for gathering, the sky was a calendar written in darkness rather than light. The Pleiades, the cluster that other nations in other countries called the Seven Sisters, the people of this country also called the Seven Sisters, because the Dreaming story of the sisters fleeing across the sky was carried by Songlines that crossed the entire land. When the Pleiades disappeared from the evening sky in late autumn, certain ceremonies had to be performed. When they reappeared at dawn in winter, the cold season had reached its depth and the time for particular foods had arrived.

This was not casual observation. Jirri's uncle had learned the positions and movements of hundreds of stars and dark-sky features, their rising and setting times through the seasons, and the correspondences between celestial events and terrestrial ones. He knew which star's heliacal rising marked the season when the bogong moths swarmed into the mountain caves in the southeast. He knew which alignment of stars in the western sky marked the running of the fish in the rivers of the north. He knew that certain stars changed brightness over long cycles and that the changes carried meaning that the old people had tracked across generations.

The knowledge was not stored in tables or charts. It was stored in stories. The Seven Sisters fled across the sky because Jukurra-Jukurra, the man of the morning star, was pursuing them. The story encoded the constellation's movement: the Sisters set in the west because they are fleeing, and the morning star follows them, and when the Sisters vanish below the horizon they are hiding, and when they reappear they are running again. A child who learned the story learned the astronomy. The story was the notation.

Jirri lay on the warm sand and listened to his uncle name the darkness. Above them the Emu stretched across the sky, patient and enormous, and on the ground somewhere to the east the real emus sat on their nests in the cold, and the timing of one was the timing of the other, and the knowledge of both was held in the same song.


## The Web of Kin

When a stranger walked into another nation's country, the first question was not "Where do you come from?" The first question was "What is your skin?"

The skin name located you. It placed you in a web of relationships that determined everything: who you could speak to, who you must avoid, who you could marry, who could teach you, who you were obligated to feed, and whose ceremonies you could attend. The system varied from nation to nation in its specifics, but the architecture was shared across the whole of the land. The Arrernte organized through eight named subsections that intersected with patrilineal and matrilineal moiety divisions. The Warlpiri used a similar eight-subsection system. The Yolngu of the north used a moiety system that divided everything in the world into two halves, Dhuwa and Yirritja, and every person, every animal, every plant, every Ancestor, every stretch of country belonged to one half or the other. Marriage was always across the moiety line. Ceremony required both halves.

What the skin system achieved was the distribution of knowledge across the entire community in a pattern that made loss nearly impossible.

No single person held all the Songlines. No single person knew all the ceremonies. No single person could access all the sacred sites. The knowledge was divided by skin group, by gender, by age, by initiation level, so that the complete picture existed only in the community as a whole. A ceremony required specific skin groups to perform specific roles. A Songline's custodianship passed through specific kinship lines. The sacred objects associated with particular Dreaming tracks were held by the people whose skin gave them the right and the obligation to hold them.

This was not secrecy for its own sake. It was a way of holding knowledge that ensured no single death could erase it. Every piece of the Dreaming had multiple custodians, connected to each other by kinship obligations that required teaching: you held the song, and you were obligated to pass it to the person whose skin position made them the next holder, and they were obligated to receive it and hold it and pass it in turn. The loss of any one custodian was survivable because the obligation to teach had created redundancy. The loss of an entire community was catastrophic, because the knowledge existed only in the web of relationships between people and country.

The kinship system also governed the economics of daily life. Sharing was not optional. The obligations of kin determined who received meat from a hunt, who was fed during lean seasons, who provided labor for ceremony preparations. A person who accumulated resources without sharing them violated kinship law as surely as a person who married within the wrong skin group. The community held together not through centralized authority but through the reciprocal obligations that kinship created. There were no chiefs. There were no kings. There were elders whose authority came from knowledge, and the knowledge itself was held in trust for the community by the kinship system.

A stranger arriving in country could declare their skin name and know, immediately, where they stood in the web. The encounter had a physical grammar. Two men facing each other at the edge of camp, the visitor speaking his skin name across the fire, and the camp's elders recognizing the name, placing it: if you are Tjungurrayi, then I am your classificatory uncle, and that woman there is your classificatory mother, and you must not look at her directly, and this man here is your brother, and he will feed you. The visitor's posture would shift. The camp's behavior toward him would shift. Within minutes, a person from a nation a thousand kilometers away would be woven into the local web as though he had always been there, because the web was designed to absorb strangers. The system was portable. It worked across language boundaries, across ecological zones, across the vast distances that separated nations whose country occupied different corners of the land. It was the social technology that made the Songline network possible, because a person walking a Songline through another nation's country needed to know how to behave there, and the skin system told them.


## The Deep Country

Sixty-five thousand years. That is the minimum, by the reckoning of those who measure in years.

At Madjedbebe, a rock shelter in the sandstone country of what is now called Arnhem Land, the ground holds the evidence: stone tools, ground-edge axes, ochre crayons worn to nubs from use, all of it sealed in layers of sand and ash that the published dating places at sixty-five thousand years before the present. Some scholars argue the true date is closer to fifty thousand. The debate is unresolved. The axes are the oldest ground-edge tools found anywhere on earth. The ochre is the oldest evidence of pigment processing. The people who made these things had crossed water to get here. The sea was lower then, but not low enough to walk the whole way. They came by raft or canoe, navigating open ocean, which means they carried knowledge of currents and wind and the reading of sky and sea before they ever touched this shore.

What they found when they arrived was a country unlike anything their ancestors in the islands to the north had known. Megafauna moved through the forests: diprotodon the size of rhinoceroses, giant short-faced kangaroos that stood three meters tall, marsupial lions with retractable claws. The landscape itself was different, wetter in the north, drier in the interior, and the process of learning it, of reading it closely enough to live from it, began immediately and continued across the millennia that followed. The megafauna disappeared. The reasons are debated, and the debate is honest: climate change, human hunting, fire, some combination of these, or forces that no one has yet identified. What is certain is that the people stayed and the knowledge deepened, and the country and the people shaped each other across a span of time so long that the distinction between natural landscape and human-made landscape ceased to have meaning.

The Tjukurpa does not count years. The Ancestors walked when they walked, and they are still walking, and the gap between sixty-five thousand years ago and this morning is a gap that belongs to the measuring system, not to the Dreaming. To ask an elder "When did the Ancestors walk?" is to misunderstand the question's own terms. The Dreaming is not a period. It is a dimension: the layer beneath the visible world, continuous and present, accessible through ceremony and song and the walking of country. The Arrernte call it Altyerrenge. The Yolngu call it Wangarr. The Pitjantjatjara call it Tjukurpa. Different words in different languages for the same underlying reality, the way different Songlines in different languages carry the same melody.

The stone tools at Madjedbebe were made by people whose names no one knows, whose languages no one can reconstruct, whose specific Dreaming tracks have been walked and re-walked and modified and carried forward by their descendants across three thousand generations. What persisted was not the specific content of the knowledge but the architecture of the system that held it: the Songlines, the kinship web, the ceremony cycle, the burning of country, the reading of sky and sand and track. The architecture itself may be older than any specific story it carries. It may be the oldest surviving way of holding knowledge in the human record.

Not because it makes these people anything by some external standard. But because it reveals what knowledge can do when it is carried in landscape and body and song rather than in text.


## The Painted Walls

In the Kimberley, in the sandstone shelters above the rivers that run to the northwest coast, the paintings watch from the walls.

The oldest of them are the Gwion Gwion figures. Thin, elegant, full of movement: people dancing, people running, people wearing elaborate headdresses that trail behind them like streamers. The figures carry boomerangs and spears and objects that scholars have not yet identified. Their bodies are drawn with a precision that suggests the painter knew anatomy from the inside, the way a dancer knows anatomy. The pigment has been dated, tentatively, to at least seventeen thousand years ago, though the figures themselves may be older. They were painted in a style that has no surviving parallel, and the people who painted them left no other record that anyone has found.

Later, on the same walls and on walls nearby, the Wandjina appeared. Large, round faces without mouths, eyes wide and haloed, bodies that seem to float against the rock. The Wandjina are Ancestor beings, rain-bringers, holders of law. Their images are not old in the archaeological sense. They are repainted. Every generation, the custodians of the Wandjina sites return to the rock shelters and repaint the figures, refreshing the image and, in the act of repainting, refreshing the Ancestor's presence in the country. A Wandjina painting on a Kimberley wall is not a historical document. It is a living ceremony in pigment. The oldest Wandjina may be thousands of years old in origin, but the paint on the wall may be from last season, because the act of maintenance IS the act of connection.

In Arnhem Land, a different tradition. X-ray paintings: fish and kangaroos rendered with their internal organs visible, the bones and the digestive system drawn inside the body outline, a way of seeing that is simultaneously anatomical, spiritual, and practical. The X-ray style records what the hunter knows about the animal's body, what the ceremony requires the painter to show, and what the Dreaming reveals about the animal's inner nature. It is knowledge in three registers, painted in ochre and clay on sandstone and on sheets of bark stripped from stringybark eucalyptus. The bark paintings are the living continuation of the rock-wall tradition: the same knowledge, the same visual language, carried forward into a portable medium that moves with the people who hold it. The tradition has been practiced for thousands of years and continues today.

At Lake Mungo, in the dry lake country of the southeast, the evidence reaches a different kind of depth. Mungo Lady, found in the eroded dunes of the ancient lakeshore, was cremated roughly forty thousand years ago. Her bones were burned, then crushed, then placed in a shallow grave. Mungo Man, found nearby, was laid out full-length, his body dusted with ochre. These are the oldest known cremation and the oldest known ceremonial burial in the archaeological record. Forty thousand years ago, the people of this lake country were treating their dead with a formality and a care that tells you something about how they understood death, even if you cannot know the specific words they used or the specific stories they told. The ochre on Mungo Man's body is the same pigment that was being ground at Madjedbebe twenty-five thousand years earlier. The practice connects across time like a Songline connects across space.

What the paintings and the burials share is this: they are the visible trace of a knowledge system that is mostly invisible. For every painting on a rock wall, there were a thousand songs. For every burial, there were a thousand ceremonies. For every ground-edge axe, there were a thousand conversations about how to read the country, when to burn, where the water would be next season, which stars to watch, which kin to call. The rock art, the burials, the tools are the dorsal fin. Beneath them, the whale.


## What Country Holds

The fire was out now and the desert cold was the cold that comes only in dry country far from the sea, the kind of cold that makes the stars sharper and the silence louder. Maku and the younger men had reached the waterhole the song had told them about, and it was where the song said it would be, holding water from the last rain in a sandstone basin sheltered by overhanging rock. They drank. They slept near the water with their backs to the rock and their feet toward the coals of a small fire, and the country around them was quiet in the way that country is quiet when nothing is wrong.

In the morning they would walk east again, into the next section of the Songline, into country that one of the younger men would one day hold as custodian, and the song would continue to unfold ahead of them, verse by verse, site by site, carrying the law and the water and the names of the Ancestors who had made this ground and were still in it.

The Arrernte have a word, kwertengerle, for the person who is the secondary custodian of a stretch of Dreaming track. The primary custodian holds the authority; the kwertengerle holds the responsibility of ensuring the knowledge is maintained. The roles are assigned by kinship. Together they are the failsafe: two lines of custodianship for every stretch of country, running through different skin groups, so that the knowledge survives even if one line is interrupted. This is what sixty-five thousand years of refinement produces. Not complexity for its own sake. Redundancy in the service of persistence.

The knowledge is in the country. It is in the song that maps the country. It is in the kinship system that determines who may sing which section of the song. It is in the ceremony that re-enacts the Ancestors' original making. It is in the burning that maintains the country in the form the Ancestors established. It is in the stars that tell the people when to burn and when to gather and when to perform the ceremonies. Each of these systems holds the others in place. Remove one and the others weaken. Remove several and the whole begins to fail.

This is not a description of fragility. It is a description of design. A system that has persisted for sixty-five thousand years is not fragile. It is resilient in a way that no written archive, no digital database, no carved-in-stone law code has yet matched. It is resilient because it is distributed: not held in one place or one medium or one person's memory but spread across an entire landscape, an entire community, an entire sky of stars and an entire ground of tracks and an entire generation of singers who will teach the next generation the same song in the same country at the same sites, because the Tjukurpa requires it.

The country does not forget. The people do not let it.

Maku woke before dawn. The cold had not yet broken. He lay still and listened to the silence, and the silence held everything: the waterhole filling slowly from a seep in the rock, the faint rustle of a spinifex mouse in the grass beyond the fire, the breathing of the two younger men asleep beside him, and, beneath all of it, the country itself, patient, ancient, alive in the way the Dreaming says the land is alive, waiting for the song to begin again.

He stood. He faced east. He began to sing.
