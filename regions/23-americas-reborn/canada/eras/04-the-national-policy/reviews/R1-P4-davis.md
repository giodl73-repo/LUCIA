# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Review: Opening — Canada Era 4, The National Policy and Its Discontents

The opening has grasped something anthropologically essential and I want to name it precisely: the braids are not a symbol of culture. They are a technology of transmission.

The grandmother's song is not decorative. It is a mnemonic system. The song is a teaching about how water moves through the land, which means the song contains hydrological knowledge, geographical knowledge, and the relational framework within which that knowledge is held. When the song is sung while braiding, the braiding becomes a form of inscription — the body learns the song through the rhythm of the work. Cutting the braids is not an act of aesthetics. It is an act against a specific knowledge-transmission technology. The author has intuited this even if they have not theorized it.

The Secwepemctsin language itself is a knowledge system. The word for water moving through land is not a translation of an English concept; it is a concept that the English vocabulary cannot contain without remainder. The opening honors this by naming the language rather than translating it, by acknowledging that the word "did not appear in any federal ledger." The ledger has a different ontology. The chapter should make this ontological gap legible — not as an abstraction, but through specific moments of what the language could carry and what the administrative system could not.

**My concern:** The full chapter risks treating the residential school primarily as a human rights violation, which it is, but which is not the only or even the deepest frame for understanding what was destroyed. What was destroyed was a knowledge system — a set of ways of knowing the land, the seasons, the animals, the relationships between them — that the land itself had been teaching for thousands of years. The chapter should carry this register alongside the human rights register. Not because the human rights frame is wrong, but because the epistemological frame is where the deepest loss sits, and the Indigenous peoples of Canada have consistently articulated the loss in these terms.

**A specific lack:** The Secwépemc people's relationship to the Thompson River watershed, to the salmon, to the land management practices that had sustained them for millennia, is entirely absent from the opening. This is understandable for a scene set in a school — the child is inside, away from the land. But the chapter must bring the land in. The knowledge being destroyed in the school was land knowledge. The reader needs to feel what the land contained before understanding what the school took.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening moves between two cultural registers — the Secwépemc interior and the Canadian administrative narrative. But there is a third knowledge system present and mostly invisible: the specific religious cosmology of the Oblates of Mary Immaculate, who ran the Kamloops school. Their worldview, their theory of souls and civilization and what it meant to save a "pagan" child, is not neutral bureaucracy. It is a specific set of beliefs about the structure of reality that produced specific practices. Understanding the school requires understanding the Oblates from inside their own logic — which the chapter must do without endorsing it.
