## Overall Assessment

The chapter succeeds in doing what almost all Yanomami literature fails to do: it represents the Yanomami from within their own framework without either romanticizing or sensationalizing. The "fierce people" shadow is entirely absent. The chapter has not overcorrected either — it does not produce a beatified Yanomami world at peace with nature and each other. It produces a working world with internal tensions, maintenance requirements, and the ordinary friction of communities in negotiation with each other and with the spirit landscape.

The feast section's treatment of the host community's anxiety about demonstrating adequate generosity — the guests receiving, assessing, filing the assessment — is exactly correct. This is not a ceremonial exchange between harmonious communities. It is diplomacy between entities with interests. The chapter gets the stakes right.

## Specific Findings

**Endorsements:**

1. The structural engine — shamanic maintenance = diplomatic maintenance — is a legitimate and sophisticated characterization of how Yanomami political life operates. The inter-community shamanic cooperation requirement (different shapori hold different territorial knowledge) is well-documented and correctly used as the chapter's engine. This is ethnographically accurate.

2. The composite-figure acknowledgment in figures.md is correctly handled. The chapter does not pretend to name pre-contact individuals. The disclaimer is honest and appropriately placed.

3. The source-asymmetry situation — heavy reliance on post-contact sources, primarily Kopenawa, as the interpretive frame for a pre-contact era — is named in notes.md and handled appropriately. The chapter does not pretend the sources are primary.

4. The inter-community alliance system (feasting, marriage exchange, shamanic knowledge sharing) is characterized accurately. The dynamic of obligation accumulation ("the thread thickens") is consistent with my own understanding of how the system functions.

**Corrections required:**

1. **"Headman" characterization:** The chapter mentions "the headman" in the landing section — "The headman moved through the sections, listening more than speaking." The Yanomami headman (nochile or pata in different dialects) is not a conventional authority figure. He has influence but not command — he cannot compel compliance. The chapter's characterization ("listening more than speaking, keeping the temperature of the community's internal relationships in his awareness") is actually correct for this, but the term "headman" may carry more authority than the role actually holds. Consider: "the community's most respected elder," or simply use the word without the English-authority connotations. The characterization is right; the label may mislead.

2. **Regional specificity:** The chapter is set in the Parima highlands, which is primarily Venezuelan Yanomami territory. Some practices (particularly the specific yakoana preparation protocols and certain hekura associations) vary between Venezuelan and Brazilian communities. The chapter appropriately does not over-specify, but the figures.md should note this regional variation rather than presenting a unified Yanomami practice.

3. **The tepui hekura's accessibility:** The chapter states Aramo had "met only two other shapori who could manage them." The tepui hekura's difficulty is real, but "manage" may undersell the collaborative rather than individual nature of tepui shamanism. In documented accounts, it is less that individual shapori can or cannot call tepui hekura and more that tepui shamanism typically requires experienced shapori working together, often at multi-community gatherings, not individual calling. Minor revision: consider "the shapori who called the tepui hekura without a partner, without a gathering — Aramo had met only two."

## Source Assessment

The chapter's interpretive reliance on Kopenawa is correct and the most defensible choice for inside-voice authority. The Virola theiodora identification is the appropriate species for Venezuelan highland communities. The diagnostic grammar is consistent with documented practice. No source misuse identified.

**Chagnon assessment:** The chapter contains zero residual echoes of the "fierce people" frame. The Yanomami in this chapter are not violent, not warlike, not unusually conflict-prone — because the chapter covers an era before intensive contact, and because the era's logic does not center conflict. This is correct. Chagnon's distortions belong to Era 3 as a characterization of the anthropological encounter, not to Era 1's self-representation.

## Corrections Summary

1. **Required:** Figures.md — note regional variation between Venezuelan and Brazilian communities in practice specifics.
2. **Recommended:** Tepui hekura passage — "manage" → language that reflects collaborative rather than individual calling.
3. **Optional:** "Headman" — consider alternative framing to avoid over-authority connotations.

The chapter endorses at ethnographic accuracy level with minor recommended revisions.
