# Figures — Pachacuti's Transformation (Era 2)

## Pachacuti (Cusi Yupanqui), r. c. 1438–1471 CE

Ninth Sapa Inca. Defeated the Chanka confederacy when his father Viracocha Inca fled Qusqu. Took the name Pachacuti ("earth-shaker" / "he who overturns time"). Rebuilt Qusqu, constructed the Coricancha, formalized the ceque system, began the qhapaq ñan road system, built Machu Pikchu, and transformed the Inca valley polity into Tawantinsuyu.

**What we know:** Pachacuti is the most consequential figure in Inca history and is attested across all major colonial chronicles (Betanzos, Cieza de Leon, Sarmiento de Gamboa, Cobo, Garcilaso). The core narrative is consistent: Chanka attack, father's retreat, the young prince's defense, victory, assumption of power, systematic rebuilding and expansion. The archaeological evidence supports the chronology: the major construction at Cusco, Sacsayhuaman, Machu Picchu, and the road system dates to this period.

**What we cannot know:** Pachacuti's inner life. The chronicles give us his decisions and their outcomes but not the deliberation behind them. The purunaucas (stone warriors) are part of all accounts and represent the Inca's own framework for understanding divine sanction, not a falsifiable historical claim. The chapter presents the battle through the Inca lens, as it should be presented.

**Sources:** Betanzos, *Suma y Narracion de los Incas* (1557); Cieza de Leon (1553); Sarmiento de Gamboa, *Historia de los Incas* (1572); Bauer, *The Development of the Inca State* (1992); Covey, *How the Incas Built Their Heartland* (2006).

## Viracocha Inca, r. c. 1410–c. 1438 CE

Eighth Sapa Inca. Father of Pachacuti. Retreated from Qusqu during the Chanka attack. The old order Pachacuti replaced.

**What we know:** Viracocha Inca is attested in the chronicles as a ruler who expanded Inca influence modestly before the Chanka crisis. His retreat is consistent across sources. His naming (after the creator deity Viracocha) is itself significant — it suggests either divine pretension or a retrospective framing by later chroniclers.

**What we cannot know:** Whether his retreat was cowardice, tactical calculation, or a narrative device constructed after the fact to magnify Pachacuti's achievement. The sources are all post-Pachacuti and serve a Pachacuti-legitimating narrative.

**Sources:** Betanzos (1557); Cieza de Leon (1553); Covey (2006).

## The Chasqui (composite figure)

A relay runner on the qhapaq ñan, carrying messages or quipus between tampus.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual chasqui from Pachacuti's era can be identified. The description is assembled from: Guaman Poma de Ayala's illustrations (c. 1615), which show chasqui with distinctive headgear, conch-shell trumpets, and carrying bags; colonial descriptions of the relay system's speed and organization; and the archaeological evidence of tambo spacing along the road. The physical experience described (running at altitude, the burn of thin air, the handoff at speed) is consistent with these sources and with modern experience of altitude running.

**Sources:** Guaman Poma, *El Primer Nueva Coronica y Buen Gobierno* (c. 1615); Hyslop, *The Inka Road System* (1984); Cieza de Leon (1553).

## The Mit'a Laborers (composite)

Workers building the roads, terraces, and storehouses under the state labor tax system.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual mit'a laborer is named in any source from this period. The description of the labor experience (organized by ayllu, recorded by quipucamayoc, accompanied by chicha feasting) is drawn from colonial administrative records and from the archaeological evidence at construction sites. The chapter portrays the system as both reciprocal and coercive, per the production notes.

**Sources:** Murra, *Economic Organization of the Inka State* (1980); D'Altroy, *The Incas* (2002); Morris & Thompson, *Huanuco Pampa* (1985).

## A Note on the Chanka War

The historicity of the Chanka war has been debated. Some scholars (notably Bauer) accept the core narrative while questioning specific details. Others note that the Chanka-battle-as-origin-of-empire is a common Andean narrative pattern (the transformative crisis that legitimates the new order). The chapter treats the battle as historical while acknowledging that the accounts are shaped by the political purpose they serve — legitimating Pachacuti's assumption of power. The purunaucas (stone warriors) are presented as the Inca's own explanatory framework, not as a historical claim.
