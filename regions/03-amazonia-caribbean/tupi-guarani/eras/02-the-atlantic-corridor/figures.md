# Figures — Tupí-Guaraní Era 2: The Atlantic Corridor

---

## On the Figures

The Atlantic Corridor era (c. 1000–1400 CE) belongs almost entirely to the oral tradition. No Tupí-Guaraní writing system existed; the memories of these migrations were carried in song, in the karai's transmissions, and in the practice of Yvy Maraey that outlasted any individual. The first written accounts of the Tupinambá tradition come from French travelers in the 1550s — Jean de Léry and André Thevet — and from Jesuit records of Guaraní communities in the seventeenth century. What we know of the era is reconstructed from the tradition's continuity: the same dream, the same karai authority, the same direction east, appearing in documents written three and five centuries after the period they describe.

The figures presented here are composite — drawn from the structural pattern of karai migration documented across multiple sources, given names from Tupí-Guaraní and Guaraní vocabulary, rendered as individuals because the composite figure is not a fiction but an honest representation of what the tradition preserved. This approach follows the convention established for archaeological and oral-tradition cultures in LUCIA: when the record cannot support a named individual, a figure drawn from the pattern of what the record does support, honestly disclosed, is more valuable than an absence.

## Arapoty — The Karai

**Name meaning:** Arapoty — "flower of the sky" in Guaraní-related vocabulary; a name appropriate to someone who receives knowledge from the dreaming body, the body that travels in the sky while the waking body sleeps.

**What the record supports:** The pattern of karai authority in Tupí-Guaraní tradition is extensively documented from contact-era sources and is broadly consistent across the Guaraní (Hélène Clastres, 1995), Tupinambá (Métraux, 1948), and related communities. The karai was always a specific person — not a hereditary office, not a position that could be held without the gift of dream-reception — who received the ñe'ë word in a form that the community recognized as authoritative. The authority was demonstrated in the community's response: not coerced but persuaded. A karai who could not put the community in motion was a karai who had not received the word, or had received it in a form the community could not recognize. A karai who could was one of the most consequential figures in the pre-contact Americas.

**What we cannot know:** Arapoty's specific thoughts during the departure. His relationship to doubt — whether a karai experienced doubt about the direction, or whether the word in its authentic form was experienced as beyond doubt. Hélène Clastres suggests the latter: the dream was not a message to be evaluated but a knowledge to be transmitted. But we cannot reach the interior of any specific karai's experience from this distance.

**What the figure carries:** The chapter gives Arapoty the dream, the public speech, the daily ceremony, the negotiation with the other karai on the road, and the final image on the coastal bluff. These moments are all attested as elements of karai practice in the historical record. The specific combination is the chapter's construction; the elements are historical.

## Guyrá — The Going One

**Name meaning:** Guyrá — "bird" in Guaraní and related languages; in Tupí-Guaraní cosmology, birds are often associated with the ñe'ë, the divine word, because they are the creatures of movement and because their songs are the closest thing in the natural world to the sacred song.

**What the record supports:** Contact-era accounts of Tupí-Guaraní migrations consistently document that not everyone who migrated did so because of the karai's call. Florestan Fernandes (1949) documents the centrality of kinship obligation in Tupinambá social structure — the specific weight of the obligations between sisters, between sisters' husbands and their wives' families. The women's networks of obligation were frequently the actual mechanism of migration: the karai called, but the bonds between women decided who followed and who remained. This is not speculation. It is the structural logic of a kinship-based society in which the women's labor (manioc processing, ceramic making, the raising of children) was the foundation of the community's daily function, and the women's movement was therefore the community's movement.

**What we cannot know:** Guyrá's specific name. Her specific relationship with her sister. The content of the conversations she had with her sister before the departure. But the pattern is there: in a kinship society, you do not go because of a dream you did not have. You go because the people you are obligated to are going, and the obligation is stronger than the comfort of the familiar grinding stone.

**What the figure carries:** The chapter gives Guyrá the moment of leaving the grinding stone behind — the specific object that focuses the abstract cost of departure into a tangible loss — and the smell of the ocean on the thirty-sixth day, experienced from the back of the group. These moments give the chapter a perspective outside the karai's sacred certainty: Guyrá is going for love, not for the dream, and she arrives at the ocean not with the karai's foreknowledge but with the full physical surprise of a person encountering something their world had not prepared them for.

## Tatupeba — The Chief

**Name meaning:** Tatupeba — "armadillo" in Tupí-related languages; the armadillo appears in Tupí-Guaraní oral tradition as a figure of protective endurance. The chief's name as armadillo is speculative but appropriate.

**What the record supports:** The Tupinambá and Guaraní chief's authority was expressed entirely through generosity. Pierre Clastres (1974) and Florestan Fernandes (1949) both document this: the chief who could not feast was not a chief. The feast was not a ceremony secondary to the chief's political function — it was the political function. By feasting, the chief demonstrated that the community's resources were sufficient and that he had the capacity to mobilize them for the community's benefit. By giving away more than he could easily spare, he demonstrated that his capacity was larger than the occasion — which meant the community could trust him in the occasions that exceeded the ordinary.

**What we cannot know:** How Tatupeba experienced the departure feast. Whether the giving-away-of-everything was experienced as loss or as fulfillment. The sources suggest the latter: a chief who gave less than everything at a departure feast would have been diminished by his restraint, not protected by it. The calculation was not about what he could afford to give but about what the occasion required. Tatupeba gave everything because the occasion required everything. This is the logic the chapter tries to render.

**What the figure carries:** Tatupeba appears only in the departure section. He organizes the feast, gives everything, and disappears into the community in motion. He is not the story's center — the karai is the story's center — but he is the mechanism that makes the departure possible, and his absence from the narrative after the departure is itself meaningful: on the road, the chief's authority is partially absorbed by the karai, because the feast cannot be held and the karai's ceremony can. The political structure reorganizes itself around what is available.

## Potirava — The Coastal Man

**Name meaning:** Potirava — "strong arrow" in Tupí-related vocabulary; a common honorific in warrior-centered Tupinambá society.

**What the record supports:** Contact-era accounts consistently document that Tupinambá coastal communities maintained watchers at the forest edge who monitored arriving groups from the interior. Jean de Léry (1556–1558) describes the protocol of welcome extended to arriving communities, which was formal and elaborate, involving specific exchanges of goods and ceremony before the arriving group was fully incorporated into the coastal community's social network. Potirava is the representative of a practice that is well-documented even if his specific name is not.

The coastal Tupinambá's knowledge of Yvy Maraey is documented in multiple sources. Métraux (1927) analyzes the tradition's geographic spread: the eastern orientation is consistent from inland Amazon communities to the Atlantic coast, where the tradition shifts to locate the Land across the ocean. This is not a contradiction of the original claim but an adaptation: the direction remains true, and the fact that the coast has been reached means only that the Land is further east than the coast. The tradition sustains itself precisely because it cannot be disproved — it is always ahead of wherever the community has arrived.

**What we cannot know:** The specific content of Potirava's conversations with Arapoty. The chapter renders these as what the sources support: the acknowledgment that the Land is east, the honest admission that people disagree about whether it is across the water or south along the coast, the practical hospitality of the shared meal. The rest is imagined plausibly from the documented protocol.

**What the figure carries:** Potirava is the chapter's most important structural figure because he embodies the tradition's adaptability. He has heard every karai who has come out of the forest for forty years say the same thing — east — and the ocean is east, and the land is still not there, and he holds this without crisis because the tradition does not require arrival to be valid. Yvy Maraey is not a failed prophecy. It is an orientation. Potirava knows this, and the chapter tries to show him knowing it without explaining it.

## The Staying Elder

He has no name in the chapter because he chose not to be named to those who were leaving. This is a narrative choice, but it is grounded in the tradition's own logic: among the Tupí-Guaraní, naming was an act of relationship-making, and a person who was remaining while others went might deliberately not give their name to the departing, because a name shared with someone who goes is a connection that will pull you after them.

**What the record supports:** Hélène Clastres documents that the karai tradition in its fullest form included a teaching about the relationship between migration and the body's aging. The young and middle-aged migrated; the very old and the very ill sometimes did not. But the tradition also included a teaching about the ñe'ë's relationship to the dying body: as the body weakened, the ñe'ë prepared for its departure from the body, and a person close to death was understood to be close to the state that migration sought — the dissolution of the body's suffering into the sacred whole. The staying elder is, in this reading, not failing to reach Yvy Maraey. He is reaching it by a different route.

**What the figure carries:** One silent moment. He watches the departure from the clearing. He does not speak. The chapter does not give us his thoughts, because we do not know his thoughts. It gives us the silence, and the not-speaking, and the chapter trusts the reader to hear what the silence contains.
