### Romanticization check
The chapter avoids the pastoral-people-in-harmony-with-nature romanticization that most often afflicts this type of chapter. The cattle breed is assessed ecologically (complementary to goats, requiring more water). The palabrero's work involves calculation and strategic framing, not noble mediation. The terminal condition (holding under load, ecological degradation, inequality between eirruku) explicitly prevents an elegiac reading.

The one passage I examined with care: "The meanwhile was most of life." This line appears in the opening, in the closing, and is the chapter's organizing image. It could read as romanticization of patient pastoral waiting. In context, it does not: it is the chapter's structural claim, namely that the Wayúu's resistance to colonial administration was not dramatic confrontation but continuous, organized, productive persistence. The "meanwhile" is active, not passive. No revision needed.

### Anachronistic morality check
The chapter presents no verdicts on Wayúu practices or Spanish colonial administration. Narváez y la Torre is treated as "not a stupid man," which is not a compliment but an accurate description of someone operating within his system's limitations. No modern moral evaluation is placed on the colonial encounter. The legal asymmetry is described structurally (Spanish patrilineal vs. Wayúu matrilineal), not as Spanish evil vs. Wayúu virtue.

### One finding requiring revision

**Flagged passage:** "The animals that the Spanish had brought to anchor a colonial economy in the lowlands had become, on the Alta Guajira, the currency of a matrilineal world that the colonial economy could not enter."

This sentence positions the colonial economy as the outside referent and the Wayúu world as defined by its relationship to that economy. The sentence structure implies the colonial economy is the primary frame. Recommend: rewrite to center the Wayúu's own economic world without the comparative framing.

**Suggested revision:** "On the Alta Guajira, the cattle were Wayúu cattle, moving through a matrilineal world whose economic logic the colonial ports at Riohacha could trade with but never organize."
