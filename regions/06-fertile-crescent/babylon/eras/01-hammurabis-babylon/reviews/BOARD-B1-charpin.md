## B-1 — Dominique Charpin — Board Review of Hammurabi's Babylon (Era 1)

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Old Babylonian political history; author of the definitive modern biography of Hammurabi; specialist in the Mari archive and royal correspondence.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The year-names are correctly used and accurately described. The year-name "Hammurabi-Is-the-Abundance-of-the-People" is attested (Horsnell 1999). The sequence of conquests (Elam, Larsa, Eshnunna, Mari) matches the year-name sequence.
- The Mari diplomatic quotation ("There is no king who is powerful by himself") is correctly attributed and accurately reflects the political situation described in the archive. This passage comes from a report by a Mariote diplomat and is widely cited.
- The portrayal of Hammurabi's administrative letters is accurate. The chapter's description of his correspondence with Sin-iddinam and other officials reflects the tone and content of the AbB corpus.
- The dating of Sumu-abum (c. 1894 BCE) follows the Middle Chronology, which is the standard used in the field. Acceptable.
- The description of Mursili I's raid (c. 1595 BCE) is accurate. The Hittites did sack Babylon and withdraw; the Kassite succession is correctly noted.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The portrayal of the code as public theology rather than modern legislation is correct and aligns with the scholarly consensus that the code is primarily a royal monument (though one with legal significance).
- The Akitu festival description is accurate in its essentials: the ritual humiliation, the renewal of mandate, the recitation of the Enuma Elish.
- The naditu women at Sippar are accurately described. The gagum is archaeologically attested, and the legal records are extensive.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The structural argument (law as public theology, the self-binding loop) is historically sound and well-executed.
- The use of primary sources (year-names, the code, the Mari letters, Hammurabi's administrative correspondence) is excellent.
- The Ammi-saduqa misharum decree is a good inclusion; it demonstrates the dynasty's legal tradition continuing even in decline.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter states the stele stood "in the Esagila precinct." This is a reasonable inference from the prologue's language but should be noted as inference rather than established fact. The stele was found at Susa, and its original placement is uncertain. **The notes.md acknowledges this uncertainty, but the chapter text presents it as fact.** Minor: consider adding a qualifying phrase.
- The Enuma Elish's dating is debated. The chapter implies it was composed during the First Dynasty period (as political theology supporting Marduk's rise). Some scholars (Lambert included, partially) argue for a later composition date (Kassite or even post-Kassite). The chapter should note this uncertainty or frame the epic as "composed or codified" during this period.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (two minor corrections recommended; neither affects the chapter's structural integrity)
