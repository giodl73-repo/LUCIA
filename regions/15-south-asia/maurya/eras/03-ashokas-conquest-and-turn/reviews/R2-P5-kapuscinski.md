# R2-P5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Panel-2 Review (Targeted)

**Focus:** Sensory plan for War and Stone sections; embodied inscription

## On the War Section

The notes allocate 900 words to the war. The Arthashastra describes the Maurya army: the four-fold force (chaturanga) of elephants, chariots, cavalry, and infantry. I want to feel the army on the move. The road from Pataliputra to Kalinga runs southeast through the Gangetic plain, across the Chotanagpur plateau, and down to the coastal lowlands. The terrain changes. The humidity rises. The army of a subcontinental empire on the march is not an abstraction. It is dust, elephant smell, the creak of cart axles, the sound of thousands of feet on packed earth.

The battle itself may be unrecoverable in detail. The notes acknowledge this. Good. But the aftermath is not. The Daya River valley at Dhauli has a specific character. The hill is laterite. The river is sluggish. The rice fields stretch to the coast. Ashoka's edict is carved into a rock face that faces the river. The carving includes a sculpted elephant above the text. These are specific, verifiable, and they ground the moment.

**What I need from the war section:**
- The army moving through landscape (heat, dust, river crossings)
- The aftermath as landscape (the Daya valley after battle; bodies are gone now, but the land remembers the scale through the edict's numbers)
- The Kalingan land as a physical place, not just a strategic target

## On the Stone Section

The notes plan to make this "densely material." I support this. The pillar at Sarnath is polished Chunar sandstone, so highly polished that early British investigators believed it was metal. The four-lion capital weighs approximately twenty-five tons. It was quarried at Chunar (near Varanasi), transported to Sarnath, and raised on a pillar that stands over twelve meters high. How? The logistics of moving a twenty-five-ton polished stone capital across the Gangetic plain in the third century BCE is a feat that should be felt.

The rock edicts are carved into existing rock faces: granite, sandstone, gneiss, depending on the site. At Girnar in Gujarat, the edict is carved on a massive boulder beside a lake. At Shahbazgarhi in the northwest, the rock is schist. Each site has its own stone, its own landscape, its own light. The edicts are not uniform. They are adapted to the geology and the community at each location.

**What I need from the stone section:**
- The weight of the Sarnath capital (twenty-five tons of polished sandstone)
- The variation in stone across the empire (granite in one place, sandstone in another)
- The physical labor of carving: the mason's posture, the angle of work, the sound of copper or iron on stone
- The act of reading aloud at a crossroads: the officer's voice, the gathered crowd, the provincial language

## On the Landing

The notes plan a brief mention of Prinsep's decipherment. I think this works if it is purely sensory: a man in Calcutta in 1837 holding a rubbing of the Brahmi script, staring at the marks, and then suddenly reading what a mason carved twenty-three centuries before. The connection between the mason's hand and Prinsep's eye. Do not explain the significance. Let the moment carry itself.
