# Banū Umayya — The Arab Empire

We were the Arab empire. When Muawiya ibn Abi Sufyan moved the caliphal capital from Medina to Damascus in 661 CE and founded the Umayyad dynasty, he was making a decision about what the caliphate would be: not a continuation of the Prophet's Medinan community, but a state organized around Arab privilege, Arab administrative language, and Arab military power — a state governed through the *dīwān* system that channeled the revenues of an expanding empire into the stipends of the Arab Muslim fighting class. Our coins had Arabic inscriptions; our chancery wrote in Arabic; our garrison cities were organized as Arab settlements separate from the conquered populations. We were the caliphate that made Arab the synonym for Muslim in the political imagination of the world we governed.

We called ourselves *Banū Umayya* — the house of Umayya, the Qurayshi clan whose members had been among the Prophet's most determined opponents before becoming his most effective administrators. Muawiya had served as the governor of Syria before he challenged Ali; his organization of Syria as a stable, prosperous province was the demonstration that he could govern. The Umayyad caliphate built on this foundation: Damascus as the center, Syria as the core province, the former Byzantine administrative infrastructure repurposed for Islamic governance. We expanded to Spain in the west and to Central Asia and Sindh in the east, creating the largest empire that had existed to that point in terms of territory. And then, in 750 CE, we were overthrown by the Abbasid revolution — a coalition of discontented non-Arab Muslims and partisans of the Prophet's family — who built something more cosmopolitan from what we had assembled.

## What Defined Them

**Arab privilege and the *mawlā* system.** The Umayyad caliphate organized its society around Arab privilege: the Arab Muslims who had conquered the empire received stipends, served as the fighting class, and governed the provinces. Non-Arab converts to Islam — the *mawālī* — were required to attach themselves as clients to Arab tribes, received lower stipends, and were largely excluded from the military and administrative elite. This system reflected the political reality of a conquest empire in which the Arab fighters were the source of military power. But it also created a growing class of educated, economically productive non-Arab Muslims — Persians, Arameans, Berbers, Copts — who were fully committed to Islam but excluded from the political order that claimed to represent it. The Abbasid revolution was, in large part, a *mawlā* revolt.

**The Arab language as the empire's medium.** The Umayyad decision to make Arabic the language of administration — replacing the Greek and Persian and Coptic administrative documents that had governed the conquered territories — was one of the most consequential acts in the history of language. Arabic bureaucrats replaced bilingual Roman-era administrators; Arabic spread from a liturgical and military language into the daily administrative medium of an empire stretching from Spain to India. The Arabic-speaking populations of Syria, Iraq, and Egypt began to think in Arabic; the populations of Persia and the eastern provinces learned Arabic for administration while maintaining their own languages. The Arabic language's current geographic extent across the Middle East and North Africa is, in large part, the Umayyad administration's legacy.

**The Umayyad Mosque — the statement in stone.** The construction of the Umayyad Mosque at Damascus — the first great congregational mosque of the Islamic world — was a declaration in architectural form: the caliphate had arrived, it would govern from this city, and it would transform the landscape of the city to reflect its power. The mosque was built on the site of the Byzantine Cathedral of John the Baptist (whose shrine the mosque incorporated), using Byzantine craftspeople and materials. The result was a building that declared itself heirs to both the Byzantine architectural tradition and the new Islamic dispensation — as Umayyad governance itself claimed to be the heir of the Byzantine administrative tradition transformed by Islamic purpose.

**The limits of Arab empire.** The Umayyad caliphate's structural problem was that the Arab privilege system could not survive the empire's success. As more and more non-Arabs converted to Islam, the logic of a religious empire organized around Arab ethnic identity became increasingly difficult to maintain. The *mawlā* were fully Muslim — they performed the prayers, paid the *zakat*, made the *hajj* — and they increasingly resented the stipend system that treated them as second-class believers. The Abbasid propaganda, which appealed to the equality of all Muslims before God and promised to restore the Prophet's communal spirit against Umayyad tyranny, found its most receptive audience among the *mawlā* of Khorasan — the Persian and Aramean converts who would be the military backbone of the Abbasid revolution.

## The Chapters

**1 — Muawiya and the Founding:** Muawiya rides into Damascus as caliph and immediately begins the work that will define the Umayyad caliphate: organizing Syria's governance on the Byzantine model it inherited, building the first Islamic navy from Syrian and Levantine shipwrights, and establishing the principle that the caliphate is a state, not a community. The debate about what the caliphate should be has been settled, for now, by the most effective governor in the Islamic world.

**2 — The Great Expansion:** The general Tariq ibn Ziyad crosses the strait between Africa and Spain at the rock that will take his name — Jabal Tariq, Gibraltar — and within three years the Visigothic kingdom of Spain has collapsed. Simultaneously, Qutayba ibn Muslim is pushing into Transoxiana and Muhammad ibn Qasim is organizing Sindh. The empire is at its maximum extent. No subsequent Islamic state will govern this much territory as a single political unit.

**3 — The Internal Tensions:** The Abbasid propagandists are moving through the villages of Khorasan, speaking to the *mawlā* communities and the Arab settlers who feel the Umayyad center has neglected them. The message is calibrated: the Prophet's family has been wronged, the community has been corrupted by the Umayyad dynasty's Arab privilege, a restoration is coming. In 750 CE the Abbasid army defeats the last Umayyad caliph at the Zab River. The dynasty ends, its members hunted down systematically — except for one prince who escapes to Spain and founds the emirate that will become the Umayyad Caliphate of Cordoba.
