# Vijayanagara — The City of Victory

We are Vijayanagara — the City of Victory — and we stood between the Deccan Sultanates and the remaining Hindu kingdoms of the south as the last great empire of the Hindu tradition in the subcontinent. We were founded in 1336 by Harihara and Bukka, who had served in the Delhi Sultanate and returned to found a kingdom on the Tungabhadra River that would last three centuries. We called our capital Vijayanagara — and the city justified the name: at its height it held perhaps half a million inhabitants, astonishing visitors from Portugal to Persia who described it as the greatest city they had ever seen, a city of markets and temples and royal processions and elephant stables, a city where merchants from every corner of the Indian Ocean world traded in a single bazaar.

We understood ourselves as the protectors of the Hindu tradition against the northern Sultanates. The Vijayanagara kings took the title of "Hindu Suratrana" — the Hindu Sultan — both claiming legitimacy from the Sultanate form of governance and asserting themselves as its Hindu counterpart. We patronized Sanskrit and Kannada and Telugu and Tamil literature simultaneously. Our Krishnadevaraya was not only a conqueror but a Telugu poet of the first rank. The empire we built was not a fortress; it was a civilization.

## What Defined Them

**The last great Hindu empire.** Vijayanagara self-consciously positioned itself as the protector of the Hindu tradition against the Deccan Sultanates. This identity shaped its architectural program — massive temple complexes, the Hazara Rama temple's extraordinary carved narrative walls, the endowment of pilgrimage centers across the south — its literary patronage, and its royal ideology. Whether or not this self-understanding was historically accurate, it shaped how the empire acted and how it was remembered.

**Krishnadevaraya — king and poet.** Vijayanagara's greatest ruler was also one of the Telugu language's greatest poets. His Amuktamalyada — "She Who Wears the Garland She Has Made" — is a literary masterpiece as well as a devotional text. He was also a military genius who extended the empire to its greatest extent, defeating the Sultans of Bijapur and Golconda. That the same person composed exquisite devotional poetry and commanded successful military campaigns was not paradoxical; it was the ideal of the Vijayanagara court expressed in one life.

**The Nayaka system.** Vijayanagara governed its vast territory through nayakas — military governors who held revenue rights in exchange for military service. This was not feudalism in the European sense; it was a more fluid, negotiated arrangement in which nayakas could inherit their positions but were also directly appointed by and responsible to the center. The nayaka system allowed Vijayanagara to govern a large territory with limited central bureaucracy, but it also created the conditions for fragmentation when central authority weakened.

**The city that astonished the world.** Portuguese, Persian, and Arabic visitors to Vijayanagara in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries were unanimous: this was among the greatest cities in the world. Domingo Paes described the city's markets in detail — spices, textiles, horses, jewels — and the king's processions with hundreds of elephants. Abdur Razzaq, the Persian ambassador, compared it favorably to the greatest Islamic courts he had visited. The city's physical scale — covering an area larger than Manhattan — was matched by its commercial complexity.

**The Battle of Talikota — and what followed.** In 1565, a coalition of five Deccan Sultanates defeated the Vijayanagara army at Talikota and then systematically destroyed the capital. The destruction of Vijayanagara was not total — the empire survived in diminished form for another century, and the Nayaka kingdoms continued for longer — but the city itself was never rebuilt. What the coalition destroyed was the symbol of Hindu royal power; the tradition scattered into the south.

## The Chapters

**1 — Foundation:** Harihara and Bukka, former officers of the Delhi Sultanate, plant their new capital on the Tungabhadra. The sage Vidyaranya blesses the site. They are starting something they cannot yet see the end of.

**2 — Saluva Dynasty:** A general takes power from a weakened dynasty. He does not abolish the old dynasty; he claims to rule in its name. This is the Vijayanagara political tradition: continuity of name, transfer of actual power.

**3 — Krishnadevaraya:** The king composes the forty-third verse of the Amuktamalyada. Outside the palace, the army is preparing the Raichur campaign. He will ride out tomorrow. Tonight he is a poet.

**4 — Talikota and Aftermath:** The five Sultans' coalition crosses the river. The battle lasts one day. Vijayanagara's army breaks. The city is emptied and burned over several months. A Portuguese merchant surveys what remains and writes: "the best-provided city in the world is now a ruin."
