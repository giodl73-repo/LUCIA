# Panel Review — R1 / P-3 Chinua Achebe

**Chapter:** Luba Empire / Era 1 — The Upemba Shore
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (post GATE-1)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

I read this as I read everything: asking whose voice is telling the story.

The voice here is correct. It is not a European voice looking at an African lakeshore and finding it interesting. It is not an anthropologist's voice cataloguing a subsistence economy. It is a voice that stands inside the morning, inside the smell of charcoal and fish, inside the specific weight of knowledge passed in the hands. The opening woman is not a specimen. She is a person who knows things — the weight of a net, the knot pattern that holds, the knowledge of when to cast and where. She knows her world. The prose knows it with her.

I will say plainly what could have gone wrong here and did not. A lesser chapter would have opened with the burials. "Archaeologists discovered..." — and immediately the Upemba shore would have become someone else's discovery, the people of Lake Kisale a population whose sophistication had to be vouched for by a Western excavation. This chapter does not do that. The burials appear as evidence after the person, after the morning, and they appear in terms of what they tell us rather than as the discovery itself. This is correct.

What I want from the full chapter: the woman's voice — the knowledge-in-the-hands voice — must be sustained. She must not disappear once the chapter moves into its larger arc. Too many chapters about archaeological cultures open with an embodied figure and then retreat into summary as the writing becomes harder. The chapter's voice is established in this woman. Keep her, or keep her register.

One note of concern: the passage "The archaeologists who opened those graves a millennium and a half later found iron hoes and copper anklets..." This is the only moment where the chapter steps slightly toward the outside world — toward those who came later to interpret. It is not a violation, but it needs to be handled carefully in the full chapter. The archaeologists are a tool here, not a protagonist, and that is correct. But the chapter should be careful not to let the archaeological frame become the primary frame. The woman's frame is the primary frame. The archaeology is evidence she cannot see but we can.

**The question I'd push on:** When the chapter covers the grave goods in its full length, whose voice describes them? If the answer is the woman's voice — what she would have recognized, what she would have known about the person in that grave, what the community knew — then the chapter is honest. If the answer is the archaeologist's voice looking backward, the chapter has stepped outside.

---

**Voice assessment:**
- The register is correct and consistent in this opening
- The woman is a person, not an illustration
- The knowledge-in-the-hands framing is exactly right for a non-literate culture
- The archaeological passage is handled without condescension
- The full chapter must sustain this voice for 5,000 words — that is the real test
