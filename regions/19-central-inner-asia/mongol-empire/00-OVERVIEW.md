# Mongol Empire — Yeke Mongol Ulus

We are the Yeke Mongol Ulus — the Great Mongol Nation — and we built the largest contiguous land empire in history. From the Mongolian steppe where Chinggis Khan unified the tribes in 1206, our armies and administrators reached the Pacific coast of China, the Adriatic Sea, the Persian Gulf, and the jungles of Southeast Asia within seventy years. This is not a boast; it is a fact that requires explanation rather than mere repetition. The explanation begins with what we were before: a fragmented collection of pastoral clans on the Mongolian steppe, perpetually raiding each other, buying and selling captives, forming alliances that collapsed with each season. Chinggis Khan ended this. He reorganized the steppe clans into decimal units of military organization that cut across kinship lines, created a law code (the Yasa) that governed the empire, established the yam relay system that made communication possible across continental distances, and declared that all peoples under Heaven belonged to him.

We were not simply destroyers. The Pax Mongolica — the Mongol Peace that stretched from China to Eastern Europe across the 13th and 14th centuries — enabled a level of Eurasian connectivity that had never existed before and would not exist again until the modern era. A Venetian merchant (Marco Polo) traveled from Venice to the court of Kublai Khan. A Moroccan traveler (Ibn Battuta) circuited the entire Islamic world including the Mongol successor states. The Black Death traveled the same routes. Ideas, technologies, and peoples moved in ways that the fragmented political geography of pre-Mongol Eurasia had made impossible. We paid for this connectivity with a violence that was genuine and deliberate — cities destroyed, populations killed, irrigation systems collapsed — but the connectivity was real, and its consequences shaped every civilization from East Asia to Western Europe.

## What Defined Them

**The decimal military organization.** Chinggis Khan's fundamental military and political innovation was the organization of the steppe population into units of 10 (arban), 100 (jaghun), 1,000 (mingghan), and 10,000 (tümen), commanded by leaders appointed for merit rather than birth. This cut through the existing clan structure — separating relatives into different units, mixing formerly hostile clans under new commanders — and created a loyalty to the new Mongol order rather than to the old kinship networks. The decimal system was simultaneously a military organization, an administrative structure, and a social engineering project. It worked.

**The Yasa — the great law.** The Yasa (or Jasaq) was the Mongol law code, promulgated by Chinggis Khan at the great quriltai of 1206 and expanded over subsequent decades. Its contents are known only indirectly — no complete text survives — but it governed military conduct, commercial transactions, treatment of envoys, religious practice, and criminal punishment. The Yasa's most important principle for the empire's functioning was the protection of merchants and the death penalty for those who harmed them. Mongol commercial policy was consistent: merchants and their goods were to be protected, and trade was to be encouraged. The Silk Road worked under Mongol rule because the Yasa enforced the safety of travel.

**The yam relay system.** The Mongol postal relay system — horses and riders stationed at intervals across the empire — allowed messages to travel from Karakorum to the Il-Khanate in two weeks, and urgent dispatches faster. This communications speed was itself a form of military power: the khan could coordinate campaigns across thousands of kilometers in real time (by the standards of the era). The yam also moved goods, transported ambassadors, and served as the physical infrastructure of imperial administration. No previous steppe empire had institutionalized this system at this scale.

**The deliberate use of terror.** The Mongol military doctrine included the systematic use of psychological warfare: cities that surrendered without resistance received protection and good treatment; cities that resisted were destroyed and their populations killed as an example to the next city. This was not random cruelty but calculated policy, documented in Mongol administrative texts as well as in the accounts of the defeated. The destruction of Merv, Nishapur, and Baghdad was deliberate messaging. The cities that submitted without fighting — and many did — survived intact. The policy was rational from the perspective of minimizing military casualties; its human cost was catastrophic.

**Tolerance of religions.** The Mongol court under Chinggis and his immediate successors practiced a systematic religious pluralism: Nestorian Christians, Buddhists, Muslims, Shamanists, and Taoists were all present at court and all received patronage. The Mongol khans' wives included Christians (Nestorian), the khans themselves variously favored Buddhism, and the Il-Khanate eventually converted to Islam. This pluralism was not philosophically principled indifference; it was practical politics in an empire that included populations of every major religion, and it was enforced by the Yasa's protection of religious institutions.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Quriltai of 1206:** All the Mongol clans have assembled on the Onon River. Temüjin — who has spent twenty years unifying the steppe by warfare, diplomacy, and betrayal — is proclaimed Chinggis Khan: Universal Ruler. The shaman Teb-Tenggeri announces that Tengri has decreed that all peoples of the world belong to Chinggis. This is the founding declaration of the empire. It is also a political program.

**2 — Nishapur:** The Mongol army has arrived at the walls of Nishapur. The city resisted. The Mongol general's son-in-law was killed in the assault. The orders are clear: no one survives. The sources say that when the killing was finished, the skulls were piled in pyramids, men separate from women separate from children. The pyramid says: this is what resistance costs. The next city on the road surrenders.

**3 — Karakorum:** William of Rubruck, the Flemish friar sent by King Louis IX of France, arrives at the Mongol capital. He describes it: smaller than Saint-Denis near Paris, but containing a permanent Muslim quarter, a permanent Chinese quarter, Nestorian churches, a Buddhist temple, and the silver tree fountain that a French craftsman built for the Khan, with four channels delivering koumiss, wine, mead, and rice wine. This is the empire: violent, cosmopolitan, genuinely strange.

**4 — The 1258 Sack of Baghdad:** The Abbasid Caliph has refused to submit. Hulegu's army enters the city. The Caliph is executed. The library of the House of Wisdom is thrown into the Tigris. The caliphate — the symbolic center of Sunni Islam for five centuries — is destroyed. The world does not end; Islam survives; but the classical Islamic world is over. The center will shift, eventually, to Cairo and Istanbul.

**5 — The Collapse:** The Black Death moves along the Mongol trade routes in the 1340s. In China, Zhu Yuanzhang leads the Red Turban revolt against the Yuan dynasty. In 1368, the Yuan emperor flees to Mongolia. The Ming dynasty begins. In the west, the Il-Khanate had already collapsed; the Golden Horde is fragmenting under internal pressure. The great empire is over. The successor states — Timurid, Golden Horde remnants, Yuan Mongolia — will carry the Mongol inheritance forward in different forms.
