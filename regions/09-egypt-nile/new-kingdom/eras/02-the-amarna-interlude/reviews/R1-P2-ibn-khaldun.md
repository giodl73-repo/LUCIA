## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of The Amarna Interlude (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening identifies the correct structural question: what happens when the mechanism of legitimacy is turned against its own foundations? This is the Amarna problem stated in its proper form, and the stonecutter makes it concrete.

**What works:**
- The logic chain (flood-ma'at-ritual-names-walls) is not merely atmosphere. It is a causal model. The opening correctly renders the Egyptian theological system as a system with parts, dependencies, and therefore vulnerability. Remove one part, and the chain is broken. This is the systemic understanding that the chapter will need to sustain.
- The paradox of the king's authority is precisely stated: "the king could not command disorder. What the king commanded was, by definition, what the gods required." This is the hinge on which the entire Amarna crisis turns, and the opening locates it without resolving it. Good. Resolution belongs to the chapter, not the opening.
- The chisel as the material object through which power flows is correct. Power is not abstract in this culture. It moves through material instruments: the chisel, the ritual, the stone, the name. The opening understands this.

**What doesn't:**
- The economic dimension is absent. Akhenaten's attack on Amun was also an attack on the Amun priesthood's wealth. Karnak's temples controlled estates, grain, labor. The stonecutter may not have understood the economics, but the opening gives no hint that there are economic stakes beneath the theological ones. A reader who has only this opening will think the Amarna crisis was purely religious.
- The opening treats Akhenaten's revolution as sudden: decree, destruction, new city. But the historical evidence suggests a phased process: early years of co-worship, gradual elevation of the Aten, eventual proscription of Amun. The opening's compression serves narrative economy but risks misrepresenting the timeline.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening's structural engine is the chisel-as-instrument: creation reversed into destruction. But the deeper engine of the Amarna period is the feedback loop between theological monopoly and political isolation. Akhenaten's concentration of religious authority also concentrated political vulnerability. When the sole mediator dies, the system has no fallback. Is the chisel-engine sufficient to carry a chapter about systemic collapse, or does the chapter need a second engine (the isolation loop) to carry the political dimension?
