# PANEL-1 Review — P-1 Barbara Tuchman

**Chapter:** Kongo Kingdom / Era 2: The Portuguese Encounter
**Round:** R1
**Reviewer:** Barbara Tuchman (P-1 — Narrative Craft)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## First Pass (Reader's Experience)

I read this straight through without stopping. That is the first thing worth saying. The opening sentence — "The letter is written in Latin" — is a bet, and it wins. Present tense. Five words. The reader is already inside the scene before they understand why.

What works: The structure is unusual. Instead of opening with Afonso at his desk, quill in hand, the chapter gives us the letter first, then the man, then the man's logic, then the world the letter was moving through. This is actually stronger than a conventional scene-opening, because it allows the reader to understand the stakes before they meet the protagonist. By the time we arrive at the ships at Mpinda and the letter's journey to Lisbon, the reader has been given everything they need to feel the weight of the ending — the king waiting for an adequate reply that will not come.

The narrative arc is clean: here is the letter, here is the man who wrote it, here is why he wrote it the way he wrote it, here is what he was up against, here is what happened. Five beats. Each one earns the next.

## Second Pass (Craft Evaluation)

**Where the arc builds:** The pivot point is the cosmogram passage — "The crucifix looked, to a Kongo eye, like the cosmogram." This is where the reader's understanding shifts. What had seemed like adoption of a foreign faith becomes something more complex: recognition, selective appropriation, strategic theology. The chapter doesn't tell the reader this; it shows Afonso seeing the cross and recognizing something in it. From that moment, the reader understands that Afonso is not a convert who has been conquered culturally. He is a diplomat who has found a shared vocabulary.

**The tension:** The chapter establishes a clean dramatic irony: Afonso believes the covenant is real, the reader suspects it isn't, and the chapter makes us feel the gap between belief and reality. "He is asking Dom João to enforce the covenant" — the reader already knows this won't work, but the chapter makes us feel what Afonso felt in writing the letter. That is good historical tragedy.

**Where it could go deeper:** The letter's quoted Latin is the strongest moment in the piece. I want more of it. Give me another line of Afonso's own words — somewhere in the 800 words, one more direct quotation from his letters would do what no paraphrase can. The reader should feel Afonso's actual voice, in his own syntax, at least twice.

**The ending:** "The ships left Mpinda with their cargo. The letter went to Lisbon. And in Mbanza Kongo, the Manikongo waited." This landing is earned. The three short sentences, each carrying an equal weight, create a rhythm of departure and waiting that resonates past the page. I would leave this alone.

**One structural caution:** The paragraph beginning "He was not trying to become European" is doing important work — it prevents the reader from misreading Afonso's project as assimilation — but it reads slightly more like authorial explanation than narrative. Consider whether the same point can be shown through Afonso's actions (the cathedral built to African commission, the bishop he created) rather than stated. The chapter is close to trusting the reader; it needs to go all the way.

## The Question I'd Push On

**"Does the chapter know what the letter's failure looks like in human terms?"**

The opening establishes the argument beautifully. What it does not yet show is what it felt like, inside Mbanza Kongo, when the reply arrived and it was inadequate. The chapter has given us the letter's sending; the chapter (full version) must give us the letter's reception. Who read the reply? Who told Afonso? How did the Manikongo absorb the information that the covenant he had staked his reign on was not being honored? The opening earns this question. The chapter must answer it.
