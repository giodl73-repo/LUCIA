# Figures — Der Investiturstreit (1024–1122 CE)

---

## Heinrich IV (1050–1106)

He was six years old when his father died and he became king of the Germans. Six years old, which meant that for a decade, the empire was governed by regents — his mother Agnes of Poitiers, then by the Archbishop of Cologne Anno II. The chronicler Lampert of Hersfeld records that Anno effectively kidnapped the young king in 1062 by rowing up alongside his boat, pretending to show him a fine vessel, and persuading him to step aboard, at which point the oarsmen pulled away before anyone could stop them; modern scholars treat Lampert's account with caution, but the political fact is undisputed: Heinrich was removed from his mother's custody and placed under the princes' control. He was eleven. He was a hostage in a political struggle between his mother and the princes, and the princes won.

This is the background against which the Investiture Controversy should be understood. Heinrich was not a man who grew up with secure power and chose to fight the papacy. He was a man who spent his entire reign — from his first independent actions in the 1060s to his death in 1106 — fighting to recover and hold the authority that his father had possessed and that his regents had partially dispersed. The fight with Gregory was, from his perspective, not an assault on the Church but a defense of the empire he had inherited. The bishops were his: not personally, but institutionally. His father had appointed bishops. His grandfather had appointed bishops. The chain of appointment stretched back to the Ottonians. To concede that the pope alone could install bishops was not a theological surrender — it was an administrative catastrophe.

He had genuine gifts. He was a shrewd military commander who suppressed the Saxon revolt, defeated the anti-king Rudolf at the Unstrut, and captured Rome after three years of siege. He understood that Canossa was a tactical retreat and executed it without visible hesitation: the man who stood in snow for three days was performing humiliation, but he was performing it as a king, using the Church's own rules as a shield. When he died in Liège in 1106 — deposed by his own son, refused consecrated burial by the bishops who had not forgiven the antipope schism — he was buried in unconsecrated ground beside the Meuse. His son eventually allowed the body to be moved to Speyer cathedral, to rest beside his father.

What we cannot know about Heinrich is what he believed about the argument beneath the fight. Did he think Gregory was wrong about the theology of investiture? Or did he think Gregory was right and simply intolerable? The letters he wrote suggest a man who was not interested in the theological question but in the practical one: who ran the empire. The theology was Gregory's domain. The empire was his.

## Gregory VII (Hildebrand of Sovana, c. 1020–1085)

He was born to a family of modest means in the Tuscan hill town of Sovana, and he rose in the papal curia not through family connection but through ability, endurance, and the sustained clarity of his convictions. By the time he was elected pope in 1073, he had served under five predecessors, had negotiated with five emperors, had been to Germany and back on papal business, and had spent thirty years watching what happened when the Church's freedom was limited by secular control. He had also spent thirty years being certain he knew what to do about it.

The *Dictatus Papae* was not an innovation — most of its twenty-seven propositions had precedents in earlier canon law or papal claims. What was new was the clarity with which Gregory stated them and the firmness with which he acted on them. He was not a man who hedged. When Heinrich's bishops declared him deposed at Worms, Gregory did not seek negotiation. He excommunicated the king and released his subjects from their oaths, within weeks, without visible deliberation. He believed the action was required. He took it.

Canossa cost him politically. The princes who had been prepared to elect a new king lost their grounds when Heinrich was absolved. The civil war that followed was partly of Gregory's making — by restoring Heinrich, he had left the anti-imperial party with no legitimate candidate, which forced them into the position of electing Rudolf without clear papal sanction, which split the Gregorian party. Gregory eventually recognized Rudolf, which gave Heinrich the grounds to install Clement III as antipope, which gave Heinrich the grounds to besiege Rome, which drove Gregory into exile. The logic of his own actions pursued him south.

In Salerno he was the guest of the Norman prince Robert Guiscard, whose armies had technically "liberated" Rome from the imperial siege — by sacking the city so thoroughly that the Romans turned on Gregory and forced him to flee. He died in a room in Salerno, in the palace of a Norman warlord, attended by the cardinals who had followed him south. His final letters from Salerno continued to issue directives — he did not experience his exile as the end of the argument, only as the loss of this particular round. He died with the conviction that his successors would continue.

His body was moved to the Salerno cathedral, where it remains. In 1606, Pope Paul V canonized him. The man who had excommunicated a king and died in exile was made a saint five centuries later. The program he had fought for — the Church's freedom from lay investiture — had been vindicated by then for nearly five hundred years.

He was, by all accounts, a difficult man to like. He did not soften his positions for diplomacy's sake. He did not leave people room to interpret his silence as consent. He wrote letters in Latin of a brutal clarity that left no ambiguity about what he meant and no face-saving for those who disagreed. But he was also capable, apparently, of genuine pastoral feeling — the letters to his friends, to the Countess Matilda, to Hugh of Cluny, carry a warmth that the official correspondence does not. He was severe with power and gentle with people. The combination was, in the end, his greatness and his political isolation.

## Hugh of Cluny (1024–1109)

He was abbot of Cluny for sixty years, which is a duration that requires explanation. He became abbot in 1049, when he was twenty-five years old. He was still abbot in 1109, when he died at eighty-five. In those sixty years, he received at Cluny three emperors, five popes, and the Kings of France, England, and Germany. He turned a great monastery into the center of a network of three hundred affiliated houses, each organized on Cluny's principle of freedom from lay control. He was, for much of the second half of the eleventh century, the most influential man in Western Christendom who held no secular office.

He had been Heinrich IV's godfather at baptism, which is why Heinrich sent to him, at Canossa, as an intermediary. He was Gregory VII's most trusted counselor, which is why Gregory received his intercession seriously. He stood between the two men in the Canossa courtyard — not physically, but relationally — as the only person both of them trusted. He interceded for Heinrich. He persuaded Gregory to receive the penitent. He did not do this because he thought Heinrich was right. He did it because canon law required it and because he believed that the argument between pope and emperor, however necessary, should not produce a permanent break in the body of the Church.

What Hugh believed, and what the entire Cluniac reform movement believed, was that the quality of the Church's prayer — the liturgy offered to God in monasteries and cathedral chapters and parish churches across the Latin West — depended on the freedom of the institution from secular contamination. A monastery whose abbot was appointed by a local lord prayed, in a sense, for the lord's interests. A diocese whose bishop was appointed by the emperor administered the sacraments under a shadow of secular obligation. The reform was not primarily about politics. It was about whether God was being properly served. Hugh of Cluny was, at bottom, a liturgist — a man who believed that the great work of human life was the proper and free offering of prayer to God, and that everything else, including the argument between Gregory and Heinrich, was in service of or in hindrance to that.

He died at eighty-five, having outlived both Gregory and Heinrich, having seen the controversy that his monastery's ideas had helped ignite finally produce a settlement at Worms. He is remembered in Cluny's calendar as a saint. He was the figure in this era who most consistently held both dimensions of the argument — the institutional and the liturgical — in view simultaneously, and who spent sixty years trying to keep both visible.

## Ariald of Milan (d. 1066)

He was not a bishop. He was not a prince. He was a deacon — a junior member of the clergy — who preached in the streets of Milan in the 1050s and 1060s about the sins of the cathedral clergy. He named names. He accused the Archbishop of Milan of simony. He led demonstrations that sometimes turned violent. He was beaten by the cathedral clergy, run out of the city, restored by popular pressure, run out again. He built a following among the ordinary people of Milan — the artisans, the minor merchants, the laypeople who attended mass in the cathedral and had begun to ask, guided by the reform preaching, whether the sacraments they were receiving were spiritually valid when the bishop who administered them had purchased his office.

The movement around Ariald was called the Pataria — a word whose origin is disputed but which was certainly a term of abuse, applied by the cathedral clergy to the reformers. It stuck, and the reform party wore it. The Patarenes were not a simple movement; they had leadership figures, they had connections to Rome, they had supporters among the Milanese aristocracy who had their own reasons to oppose the existing episcopal establishment. Their program had three targets: simony (the purchase of offices), nicolaitism (married or concubinary clergy), and lay investiture. The nicolaitism target was the most viscerally immediate for ordinary people, because a priest with a wife and children was visible at every mass — the reform argument said that such a priest was spiritually compromised, that the sacraments administered at his altar were tainted. This was not an abstract concern. It touched the bread and wine that made salvation possible. The Patarenes had followers because this argument made sense to people who had nowhere else to bring their anxiety about their souls.

This was not a question without consequences. It touched every baptism, every confirmation, every absolution administered in Milan for decades. It was the investiture argument experienced not as constitutional theory but as daily anxiety about the state of one's soul. Ariald preached that it mattered. The cathedral clergy said it didn't — that the sacraments' validity derived from the office and the ordination, not from the moral character of the officiant. The argument between these positions — known in technical theology as the debate between Donatism and anti-Donatism — had been decided in the fourth century by Augustine, who said the sacraments were valid regardless of the minister's character. But Augustine's answer did not satisfy the people of Milan, who wanted bishops they could trust.

Ariald was murdered in June 1066, on an island in Maggiore, by agents of the Archbishop of Milan. His body was found mutilated. Gregory VII's predecessor Alexander II condemned the murder and began the process of his beatification; Gregory VII continued it. He was canonized. His feast day is June 28.

He is the figure in this era who shows that the investiture controversy was not only a constitutional argument between popes and emperors but a religious argument between laypeople and their clergy — a popular demand that the institution they relied on for their spiritual lives should be free from the commerce that compromised everything else they knew.
