# notes.md — Production Bible
## La Reconquesta Aragonesa (1137–1238 CE)

---

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** Pactisme as covenant-machine — the system by which Aragonese power was legitimate only when compacted, and expanded only by extending compact.

**Mechanism:** Every territorial acquisition required not military victory alone but constitutional absorption. Zaragoza (1118) gained its own fueros. Majorca (1229) gained its own Corts. Valencia (1238) gained its own Furs and its own parliament. The Crown of Aragon was not a centralizing empire but a federation of compacts — each new piece absorbed on its own terms, the king bound by each separate set of laws. The machine expanded through multiplying covenant obligations, not through imposing uniformity.

**Terminal condition (for this era):** The engine generates the machine that will sustain the crown for two more centuries, but it plants the specific vulnerability that later eras will stress: the Crown of Aragon cannot impose uniformity without violating its own constitutional logic. When the Trastámara dynasty needs to centralize, and when union with Castile creates a kingdom that does not operate by compact logic, the machine's multiple constituencies will pull against each other. The pactisme system works while the king needs each constituency separately; it becomes a liability when the king needs them as one. That terminal condition is planted here — in the very act of granting Valencia its own separate laws.

## Key Figures

**James I of Aragon (Jaume I, "el Conqueridor," 1208–1276)**
Ruled 1213–1276. Born at Montpellier. Father killed at the Battle of Muret (1213) when James was five; he was briefly held hostage by Simon de Montfort and then by the Templars, who raised him. His regency was a period of faction war among the Aragonese nobility. He took personal power around 1225. His two great conquests were Majorca (1229) and Valencia (1238). He dictated the *Llibre dels Fets* in Catalan — the first major prose autobiography by a European monarch. He was also responsible for the *Furs de València*, the law code that governed the new kingdom. He had multiple children by multiple women; his succession arrangements caused immediate conflict after his death. By Aragonese standards, a king who genuinely operated within the compact system — not because he loved it but because he understood it.

**Zayyan ibn Mardanish (c. 1190–c. 1269)**
Last Almohad governor of Valencia. From a family with deep roots in the Taifa period — his grandfather was the famous Wolf King, Muhammad ibn Mardanish, who had governed Valencia and Murcia and fought the Almohads. Zayyan inherited a weakened position: the Almohad empire was fragmenting after Las Navas de Tolosa (1212), there would be no reinforcement from North Africa, and the Aragonese had been systematically taking his northern strongholds for a decade. He chose negotiation over siege to the last. He negotiated the capitulació that allowed the Muslim population to remain, then sailed to North Africa with those who chose to leave. His subsequent history: he tried and failed to retake Valencia from exile, made alliances with the Hafsids of Tunis, and died in North Africa still holding the title of king of Valencia in his own mind. He is the figure the chapter must give full dignity: a ruler who lost what could not be held and chose the terms that protected as many as he could.

**Abu Zayd (c. 1190–c. 1270)**
The Muslim governor of Valencia's northern territories who converted to Christianity in the 1220s and became a key Aragonese ally, providing intelligence about Valencia's defenses and the location of water sources. His conversion was political — he was in a losing position and allied with the winning side — but it placed him in the strange position of being an Aragonese noble of Muslim origin helping a Christian king take a Muslim city. His lands near Valencia were granted back to him as a Christian vassal. His figure complicates the binary of the conquest: the front line was not solely Christian vs. Muslim. It was also about which local power structures survived by adapting.

**The *Justicia* of Aragon (institutional figure)**
The great judicial officer who held the constitutional authority to restrain the king. The *Justicia* was not elected — he was appointed by the king but served for life and could only be removed by the *Corts*. His jurisdiction included the *manifestación* — the right to protect any person, even one accused of a crime, from royal violence until the courts had heard the case. The *Justicia* is the institutional embodiment of pactisme: the living mechanism by which the compact was enforced. In this era, the office is establishing its precedents. The chapter should give it one specific moment — a case, a confrontation, a moment when the *Justicia* held.

**The Acequia Amin (archetypal figure, composite from Burns's scholarship)**
The Muslim water judge who managed the distribution rights of a section of Valencia's acequia irrigation system. Not a named individual the sources preserve for this specific period, but the institution was real and documented. The amin operated within the Islamic *falaha* agricultural tradition — a body of agronomic and jurisprudential knowledge about water rights, planting schedules, soil preparation, and dispute resolution that had developed across centuries of Arab presence in Valencia. After 1238, the mudéjar communities continued to operate the acequia system under the *Tribunal de las Aguas*, which is still operational in Valencia today — one of the oldest continuously functioning courts in Europe. The amin is the knowledge system embodied. The chapter should find him in the weeks after the gates open, still doing his work, because there is no one else who can.

## Sources

**Primary:**
- James I, *Llibre dels Fets* — autobiography; primary source for the Valencia campaign; must be used directly for the siege, the negotiation, the entry, the weeping at the mosque.
- *Furs de València* (1261) — the law code James gave Valencia; demonstrates the constitutional absorption model in action.
- *Usatges of Barcelona* — the Catalan legal code; background on the compact tradition.

**Secondary:**
- Robert I. Burns, *Muslims, Christians and Jews in the Crusader Kingdom of Valencia* (1984) — essential for the mudéjar community, acequia system, and the knowledge transfer question. Burns shows that the Tribunal de las Aguas has roots directly traceable to the Islamic acequia amin institution.
- Thomas N. Bisson, *The Medieval Crown of Aragon* (1986) — structural analysis of the Corts and pactisme system; best source on constitutional absorption model.
- Donald J. Kagay, *The Usatges of Barcelona* (1994) — the compact's legal foundation.
- J.N. Hillgarth, *The Spanish Kingdoms 1250–1516* (1976) — broad context.

**Source situation:** This era is unusually well-sourced by LUCIA standards. The *Llibre dels Fets* gives us the king's own voice. Burns gives us the mudéjar side. Bisson gives us the constitutional machinery. The chapter can operate at high evidence density throughout.

## Narrative Arc and Section Plan

**Section 1 — The Wall (opening integrated)**
James at the walls. The calculation. The compact formula as inheritance. The negotiation with Zayyan's emissaries — rendered, not merely reported. The capitulació. The gates opening.

**Section 2 — What the City Held**
Entry into Valencia. The first morning inside. The acequia channels carrying water. The acequia amin continuing his work because someone has to. The silk merchants assessing what the change means. The smell and texture of the city. Zayyan's departure — the boats, the shore, what he took and what he left. The mudéjar decision: stay or go. Mostly stay. Why. The orchards are their orchards. The channels are their channels. The law courts James has promised will be their courts.

**Section 3 — The Machine of Compact**
How the Aragonese absorption model worked. The *Furs de València*: James chose to give Valencia its own laws rather than impose Aragonese law, because he already had Aragonese law and Catalan law and couldn't reconcile them — so a third law was cheaper than a war with his own barons. The *Justicia* of Aragon and a specific confrontation — a case where the king was restrained. The Corts demanding supply before they supplied. The compact operating as mechanism, not as principle: what it looked like when the *Corts* refused the king.

**Section 4 — The Island Logic**
Majorca before Valencia: the 1229 conquest. Note: the Majorcan arrangement was harder than Valencia — a significant portion of the Muslim population was enslaved or expelled. This contrast must be in the chapter: James made a different calculation at Valencia than at Majorca, and the difference was deliberate. Valencia's agricultural complexity demanded a different compact. The Catalan commercial intelligence network operating behind the military advance. Barcelona merchants knowing where Valencia's silk was going before the king knew the city would fall. The commercial empire and the covenant empire feeding each other. The island chain as strategic vision: Majorca, then Menorca, then the Valencian coast, eventually Sicily.

**Section 5 — The Fragility Inside the Engine**
What the compact system built, and what it could not solve. Valencia kept its own laws, but the Aragonese nobles who received Valencian land grants wanted Aragonese law — which gave them more power over their tenants. The tension between the king's compact with the mudéjars and the nobles' demand to impose Aragonese feudal terms on their new Valencian holdings. James had to hold both. He was not entirely successful. The *Furs de València* were a political compromise that satisfied no one completely. The mudéjar communities survived — but under a system of protections that could be retracted by a future king who did not share James's calculation.

**Landing**
The acequia amin in the late autumn of 1238, after the last of Zayyan's soldiers have gone. He is checking the main channel above the city — the one that feeds the orchard districts. The water is running. The distribution schedule is unchanged. He will record it in the same register he has used for fifteen years, under the same categories, by the same rules. A Catalan notary has arrived to witness the recording. The amin does not speak Catalan. The notary does not speak Arabic. They manage. The record is made. The channel carries the water south. The oranges will ripen in December, as they do every year.

## Panel Pressure Points Anticipated

**From Tuchman:** Must dramatize the negotiation scene (emissaries, terms, Zayyan's decision-making). The chapter cannot merely report what the *Llibre dels Fets* reports — it must render the moment.

**From Ibn Khaldun:** Must operate pactisme at mechanism level, not assertion level. The *Justicia* confrontation scene must show the institutional logic in motion. Must plant the terminal condition: the covenant system's internal fragility.

**From Achebe:** Must give Zayyan full dignity — his reading of the situation, his calculation, his choice. Must give the mudéjar community at least one particular figure (the acequia amin). The chapter must be readable from inside the Valencian Muslim perspective, not only from inside James's.

**From Davis:** Must render the acequia knowledge system at method level — not just that it existed, but how it worked, what expertise it required, why it could not be quickly replicated. The Tribunal de las Aguas reference is key: a living institution whose roots the chapter can trace to this moment.

**From Kapuściński:** Must render the physical entry into the city. The negotiation room. The acequia channel. The mudéjar farmer in the moment of transition. The body of Zayyan on the shore, departing. The body of the amin continuing to work.

## Voice Register

**Aragonese Compact-Legal.** Not exactly legal-public (Roman), not exactly inscriptional (Maya). The Aragonese voice is contractual, deliberate, and accustomed to negotiation. Sentences close like clauses in an agreement — definitive, weighted, but acknowledging the counterpart. The rhythm is not staccato (steppe) or rolling (maritime) but steady, measured, attentive to consequence. The Aragonese were people who wrote things down and then argued about what they meant. The prose should carry that quality: precise without being cold, measured without being distant. The *Llibre dels Fets* in Catalan has a quality of frank, first-person directness — not boasting, but stating. That register should inform the chapter's voice.

**Em-dash target: 0.** The Aragonese compact-legal voice finds its pauses in the period and the semicolon. Each sentence closes like a clause.

## Open Questions

1. The exact date of Zayyan's departure from Valencia — some sources say September 28, others October — does not need to be resolved. The *Llibre dels Fets* gives the timeline with enough specificity.
2. What happened to the large mosque after the surrender? James converted it to a cathedral (La Seu). This is noted in the *Llibre dels Fets*. Worth including briefly — but without making it the chapter's moral centerpiece, as that would distort the balance toward religious framing rather than political/covenant framing.
3. The Majorca conquest of 1229: this precedes the Valencia campaign and must be handled in Section 4 as establishing the island strategy. Burns has material on the Majorcan Muslim community's parallel fate.
4. The acequia amin as composite figure: disclose in figures.md that this is an archetypal figure drawn from Burns's scholarship on the mudéjar acequia institutions, not a single named individual the sources preserve for this specific period.
