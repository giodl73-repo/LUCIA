# Notes — The Kingdom's Rise

## Production Bible

### Sources

**Primary (Tier 1):**
- Sarduri I inscription at Van citadel (in Akkadian): royal titulature, foundation text. UKN I, 1 (Melikishvili corpus).
- Ishpuini and Menua joint inscriptions (in Urartian): earliest Urartian-language texts; campaigns, dedications to Haldi, building projects.
- Menua's inscriptions: canal construction at Van (the "Menua canal" / Shamiram Su); fortress building; campaigns against Diauehi and other highland neighbors.
- Argishti I inscriptions: Erebuni foundation text (782 BCE); campaign lists; deportation counts.
- Assyrian royal annals: Shalmaneser III campaign accounts against Sarduri I; Ashurnasirpal II and the Nairi lands; descriptions of highland geography and peoples.

**Academic (Tier 2):**
- Mirjo Salvini, Geschichte und Kultur der Urartäer (1995): standard Urartian epigraphy and history.
- Paul Zimansky, Ecology and Empire: The Structure of the Urartian State (1985): ecological/structural analysis of Urartian state formation. The fortress-network model.
- Boris Piotrovsky, The Ancient Civilization of Urartu (1969): archaeological overview, excavation reports from Karmir-Blur and Erebuni.
- Alison Summers, "The Urartian Empire" in A Companion to the Ancient Near East (2005): recent synthesis.
- Charles Burney and David Marshall Lang, The Peoples of the Hills (1971): highland archaeology, material culture.

**Scholarly Secondary (Tier 3):**
- Walter Wartke, Urartu: Das Reich am Ararat (1993): museum catalogue with extensive material culture documentation.
- Oktay Belli, The Irrigation Systems of Urartu (2001): canal engineering, the Van canal, highland hydraulics.
- Simo Parpola, "The Neo-Assyrian Empire" in The Dynamics of Ancient Empires (2009): Assyrian perspective on Urartu as rival.

### Key Figures

**Sarduri I, son of Lutipri (c. 860-840 BCE):**
Founder of the dynasty. Built the citadel walls at Tushpa on the Van rock. Inscribed his titulature in Akkadian cuneiform. The inscription is the earliest datable Urartian royal text. His father Lutipri is named only in this inscription. The record cannot support interiority. He exists as a builder and an inscriber: the king who made the walls and then made the words. Handle as an inscriptional presence, not a psychological portrait.

**Ishpuini, son of Sarduri (c. 830-810 BCE):**
Transitioned the inscriptions from Akkadian to bilingual Akkadian-Urartian to fully Urartian. Campaigned in the regions around Lake Urmia. Joint inscriptions with his son Menua suggest co-regency. Established Haldi as the supreme deity in the royal inscriptional formula. The shift in language and the establishment of Haldi are the two foundational acts of his reign.

**Menua, son of Ishpuini (c. 810-786 BCE):**
The canal builder. The "Menua canal" (also called Shamiram Su, and later attributed to Semiramis in local legend) ran approximately 50-70 km from its source to the Van plain. One of the most significant hydraulic engineering projects in the ancient Near East. Menua also expanded the fortress network and campaigned extensively. His inscriptions are the most numerous of the early Urartian kings. The canal IS the structural argument made physical.

**Argishti I, son of Menua (c. 786-764 BCE):**
Founded Erebuni (782 BCE), the fortress-city near modern Yerevan. The Erebuni foundation text records the deportation and resettlement of 6,600 prisoners from campaigns in the Hatti lands and the Tsupani lands. Represents the kingdom at its organizational peak within this era: fortress-building, population management, agricultural expansion into the Ararat plain.

**The Masons / Canal Builders:**
No names survive. The material record shows extraordinary skill in megalithic construction (cyclopean walls at Tushpa, precisely fitted basalt blocks) and hydraulic engineering (the Van canal's gradient management over 50+ km of terrain). Present as embodied craftsmen. The archaeological record gives us the stone, the technique, the tool marks. It does not give us names.

### Structural Engine

**Engine:** The inscription-as-state-making cycle. A highland kingdom that had no writing borrows the script of the dominant power, uses it to declare sovereignty, then develops its own inscriptional language, and through that language creates a theology (Haldi), an administrative grammar, and a territorial claim. The act of inscribing becomes the act of governing. The fortress and the canal are the material forms of what the inscription claims.

**Mechanism:** Each generation of kings inscribes more extensively, in an increasingly Urartian voice. Sarduri inscribes in Akkadian. Ishpuini inscribes bilingually. Menua inscribes in Urartian and builds the canal. Argishti inscribes in Urartian and builds Erebuni. The inscription accumulates: more territory claimed, more buildings dedicated, more campaigns recorded. The kingdom expands as the inscriptional record expands. The stone face fills.

**Terminal condition:** The inscriptional logic reaches its limit when the territory claimed exceeds the fortress network's capacity to hold it. The next era (Era 2, the Expansion Age) pushes the kingdom to its maximum extent, and Era 3 brings the Assyrian reckoning. But within Era 1, the terminal condition is the completion of the founding project: when the kingdom has its own language on stone, its own god on the citadel, its own canal feeding the plain, and its own fortress network spanning the highland, the founding is done. What follows is the test.

### Narrative Arc

**Movement:**
1. **The Inscription** (opening integrated) — Sarduri at the Van rock. The borrowed script. The basalt. The founding act in someone else's language.
2. **The Highland** — The physical world of the Armenian plateau. Volcanic rock, altitude, the lake, the mineral wealth. Why this landscape resists invasion and rewards fortification. The ecological foundation of Urartian power.
3. **The Fortress** — Tushpa's walls. Cyclopean construction. The fortress-network as organizational principle. The highland chieftains coalescing around defensible nodes. Why fortress-building precedes state-building in this environment.
4. **The God** — Ishpuini's establishment of Haldi as the national deity. The inscriptional formula: "by the greatness of Haldi." The temple on the citadel. A highland god for a highland people, a war god for a kingdom that exists in confrontation with Assyria.
5. **The Canal** — Menua's canal. The engineering. The gradient across 50+ km. The transformation of the Van plain from scrub to agriculture. Water as the material foundation that the inscriptions claim in words.
6. **The Landing** — Argishti at Erebuni. The fortress on the Ararat plain. The kingdom complete in its founding form: inscription, fortress, god, canal. The stone holds. What comes next is the weight it will have to bear.

### Open Questions

1. **Sarduri I dating:** c. 860-840 BCE is approximate. Zimansky and Salvini differ slightly. Use "around the middle of the ninth century" and note uncertainty.
2. **Canal attribution:** The Van canal is attributed to Menua by inscription but may have had earlier phases. Menua's inscription claims it; treat the claim as the evidence, note the possibility of earlier work.
3. **Haldi's origins:** Pre-Ishpuini worship of Haldi is possible but undocumented in inscriptions. Present Ishpuini's inscriptions as the earliest datable evidence of Haldi as supreme deity, not necessarily the origin.
4. **Population of the highland:** Archaeological estimates vary widely. Avoid specific numbers. Describe through the material record: storerooms, agricultural terraces, deportation counts from inscriptions.
