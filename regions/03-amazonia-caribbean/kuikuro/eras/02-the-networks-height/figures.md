# Figures — The Network's Height
## Kuikuro | Era 2: c. 1200–1500 CE

---

## On the Record

The era covered by this chapter (c. 1200–1500 CE) is known primarily through two source streams: the archaeological record produced by Michael Heckenberger's Upper Xingu research (excavations at Kuhikugu and satellite remote sensing of the broader town network), and the ethnographic record of the living Kuikuro and neighboring upper Xingu peoples, who are the documented descendants of the pre-contact population. Individual named figures do not survive in the record for this period. The figures presented here follow the v1.4 rubric protocol for archaeological cultures: embodied presence without requiring a name. Three figures are composite, drawn from the social roles documented in both the archaeological and ethnographic record; one figure (the Kalapalo anetü) is a structural figure, defined by the exchange relationship the chapter documents.

---

## Iauatá — The Road Keeper

Iauatá is a composite figure, constructed from the social role of the adult Kuikuro man responsible for community infrastructure maintenance — the category of work that in the upper Xingu covers road-clearing, fish weir maintenance, and the preparation of the community's physical plant for the Quarup ceremony. He is not an exceptional figure. He is a working figure, which is the point.

The road-clearing scene in the opening is grounded in Heckenberger's documentation of the upper Xingu road system: roads running in straight lines between towns, maintained at widths that permitted communal use, oriented to connect specific pairs of communities in the network. These roads required continuous maintenance in a tropical forest environment where the growing season is year-round and the forest's pressure on any cleared space is constant. The labor of maintaining them was community labor — not specialist labor, not chiefly prerogative, but the ordinary work of people who understood that the road's existence depended on their effort.

Iauatá's interiority (thinking about the egitsü while doing maintenance work) reflects what the ethnographic record suggests about the relationship between the Quarup and the daily work of the upper Xingu community: the ceremony is the horizon that gives the interval its meaning. The preparation for the Quarup — the manioc processing, the road maintenance, the gift preparation — is not separate from the ceremony. It is the ceremony's body, the physical accumulation that makes the event possible.

His mourning obligation (the complex lineage connection to the deceased chief) reflects the actual structure of kinship in upper Xingu society, where marriage patterns create overlapping obligations between communities and between individuals within communities. The complexity of his grief — how to mourn, at what intensity, for how long — is a real feature of the social landscape of a network maintained through kinship as much as through ceremony.

## Tüke — The Terra Preta Gardener

Tüke is a composite figure drawn from the evidence for the women's role in manioc horticulture in the upper Xingu and from the archaeological record of terra preta production at Kuhikugu and its associated sites.

The name means approximately "the one who tends" in the Kuikuro lexical tradition, but Tüke is a character, not a translation. She represents the class of upper Xingu woman who maintained the dark earth gardens — a role that was simultaneously domestic (feeding the family), economic (contributing to the community's feast surplus), and ecological (continuing the centuries-long practice of soil enrichment that sustained the upper Xingu's unusual population density).

The terra preta knowledge she embodies is documented in the archaeological record. Heckenberger's excavations at Kuhikugu found terra preta deposits up to 2 meters deep at the site's oldest residential areas — evidence of centuries of deliberate soil enrichment. The chemical signature of these soils (elevated charcoal, elevated phosphorus from bone and organic waste, elevated pH) has been analyzed in detail. The technique is now understood well enough that researchers are attempting to reproduce it — but reproduction requires understanding what the people who created these soils knew. That knowledge was held by the gardeners, and it was held in practice, not in writing.

Tüke's sensory knowledge of the soil — reading its color, its moisture retention, its feel between the fingers — represents the epistemological fact that the terra preta knowledge system was embodied and empirical. The gardeners were not following a theory. They were applying centuries of accumulated observation about what specific additions to specific soils produced what specific results. This is agricultural science practiced at a sophistication level that its own practitioners would not have recognized as science, because it was simply gardening.

The chapter makes her the chapter's second major embodied presence after Iauatá, because the road and the garden are the two material foundations of the network — the road connecting communities and the garden feeding the ceremonies that maintain them. The network collapses when either one is no longer maintained.

## Kairü — The Hüge

Kairü is a composite figure drawn from the documented role of the hüge (song-leader) in upper Xingu ceremonial life. The role is documented ethnographically across the upper Xingu peoples with enough consistency to constitute strong evidence for its pre-contact form.

The hüge is a specialist. Not a shaman, not a political leader, not a healer — a person whose specific responsibility is the correct performance of ceremonial music. In the upper Xingu context, this means knowing the specific songs associated with specific lineages and their deceased chiefs, knowing the correct sequence and interval and tonal variation for the mourning portions of the egitsü, and maintaining this knowledge through a teacher-student transmission that runs across generations.

The scene of Kairü rehearsing the uncertain interval with his teacher is the chapter's knowledge-at-method-level moment for the oral-ceremonial tradition. The detail about the third interval — the specific tonal uncertainty, the decision to go to the teacher, the process of singing and correcting until both agree — is drawn from ethnographic descriptions of the training process for upper Xingu ceremonial specialists. This is how oral knowledge is maintained: not by writing it down, not by formal instruction, but by singing it until the ear can distinguish correct from incorrect, and then having the correction witnessed by someone who learned the same distinction from someone who learned it before them.

The consequence the chapter assigns to incorrect performance — the egitsü that does not close the grief, the mourning period that extends — is consistent with the ethnographic record's description of the ceremony's function. The egitsü is not a performance. It is an efficacious act. Its correct performance has specific consequences for the community. Its incorrect performance has different consequences. The hüge who is uncertain at the moment of performance is carrying a real responsibility, and the chapter tries to make that responsibility felt.

## The Anetü of Kuhikugu — The Feast-Giver

This figure has no name because the record does not preserve individual anetü names for the pre-contact upper Xingu. He is characterized entirely through function: the obligations of the hereditary chiefly role as documented in both the archaeological record (the scale of feast deposits at Kuhikugu) and the ethnographic record (the description of chiefly obligations in Gregor, Basso, and Franchetto).

The chapter's characterization of the anetü draws on several specific features of the upper Xingu chiefly role. First, the obligation of hospitality: the anetü is responsible for providing food and caxiri for the duration of any ceremony his community hosts. The feast deposits at Kuhikugu are archaeological evidence that this obligation was fulfilled at scale — the quantities of fish bone, manioc processing residue, and ceramic vessels associated with the Kuhikugu plaza area are consistent with large-scale ceremonial feasting. Second, the gift exchange structure: the formal exchange between the anetü of visiting and hosting communities is documented in the ethnographic record as the ceremony's political center — the moment at which inter-community obligations are renewed and new obligations are created. Third, the continuous legitimacy requirement: the anetü's authority depends on his capacity to give. An anetü who cannot fulfill the feast obligation has failed publicly, in front of the network. This structural feature of upper Xingu leadership is consistent across multiple ethnographic accounts and is one of the features most clearly visible in the archaeological record's evidence of feast scale.

The chapter also shows the anetü managing a structural tension in the network: the community that arrives without adequate gifts. This is not a scenario from the historical record (we have no documentary evidence of specific failure moments in the pre-contact exchange system). It is a structural inference — the system's exchange logic implies that individual communities will have bad seasons, and the network requires a mechanism for handling this. The anetü's quiet registration of the shortfall, and the doubled obligation it creates, is the chapter's rendering of what that mechanism might have been.

## A Note on the Network's Scale

Heckenberger's remote sensing work, published in *Science* in 2008, documented approximately twenty major settlements in the upper Xingu connected by a road system of at least 250 kilometers of maintained corridors. The largest settlements (including the Kuhikugu cluster) covered areas comparable to small European medieval towns and housed estimated populations in the hundreds to low thousands. The total network population in the pre-contact period may have been in the tens of thousands across the connected towns.

These numbers are significant not because they are "impressive" by some external standard but because they are the foundation for understanding what the Quarup ceremony was maintaining. A ceremonial system designed to hold together a network of tens of thousands of people across a 50,000 square kilometer landscape — without a unified political authority, without a standing army, without a writing system — is solving a genuine organizational problem. The Quarup is the solution. The road is its physical infrastructure. The terra preta is the food system that makes the ceremony's scale possible. The hüge is the carrier of the ceremony's knowledge.

The network lived in the interval between ceremonies, in the specific work of the specific roads that connected the specific towns that had decided, generation after generation, that the connection was worth the work.
