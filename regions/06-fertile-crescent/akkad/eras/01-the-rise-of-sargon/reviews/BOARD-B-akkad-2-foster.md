## B-akkad-2 — Benjamin Foster — Board Review of The Rise of Sargon

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Comprehensive political, economic, and military history of the Sargonic period; integration of inscriptional, administrative, and archaeological evidence.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The political narrative (cupbearer at Kish, rise to power, defeat of Lugalzagesi, conquest of Sumerian cities) is consistent with the scholarly reconstruction. The sequence is correct.
- The administrative apparatus (appointed governors, standardized weights, trade networks) is accurately described in its essentials. The tamkarum system and the road network are appropriately presented.
- The fifty-five-year reign is from the Sumerian King List and is appropriately qualified ("if the King List is to be believed").
- The revolts during Sargon's reign are attested in the inscriptions and are correctly represented as a recurring feature of the empire.
- The description of Sargon's sons (Rimush and Manishtushu) being killed in palace conspiracies is based on later tradition (the "Cruciform Monument" and related texts). The historical reliability of these accounts is debated. The chapter's treatment is acceptable as narrative but could note the source is later tradition.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The distinction between Lugalzagesi's confederation and Sargon's centralized administration is valid and well-articulated.
- The standing army as a Sargonic innovation is a defensible position, though the evidence is indirect. The "5,400 soldiers" passage is from the later literary tradition and is correctly attributed.
- The canal management section is a strong addition. The empire's dependency on Sumerian infrastructure for agricultural management is a key point that many accounts underplay.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The structural argument about non-transferable legitimation is the chapter's strongest contribution. The connection between Ishtar's personal bond and the succession crisis is logically coherent and historically productive.
- The trade network section accurately represents the geographic scope of Sargonic commercial claims (Cedar Forest, Silver Mountain, Magan, Meluhha, Dilmun).
- The handling of Akkade's unknown location avoids both romanticization and false precision.

**What needs correction:**
- "Shar kishati" is glossed as "Lord of the Universe." The title is more accurately "King of the Universe" or "King of Totality." The Akkadian "sharru" is consistently "king" in the inscriptions.
- The claim about the "maritime pidgin of the Dilmun sailors" is speculative. We have no evidence for a specific Dilmun pidgin. The multilingual character of the Akkade marketplace is a reasonable inference, but the specific detail should be softened or removed.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (two minor corrections noted above)
