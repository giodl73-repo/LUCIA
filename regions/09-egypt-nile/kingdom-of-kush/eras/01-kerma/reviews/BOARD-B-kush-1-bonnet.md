## Archaeological Accuracy

**Tumulus description: ACCURATE.** The description of the royal tumuli, the corridor arrangement of cattle and retainers, the east-facing horn orientation, and the retainer positioning (flexed, hands near faces, personal ornaments intact) is consistent with the excavation reports. The tumulus dimensions (K III at 90m diameter) are correct. The retainer numbers (300+ in the largest tumuli) are consistent with our findings.

**Deffufa description: ACCURATE with one note.** The Western Deffufa dimensions (approximately 50m x 25m base, surviving height over 18m) are correct. The description of its ceremonial function is appropriately cautious. The chapter correctly notes that the specific nature of the rituals performed there is unknown. One note: the chapter states "It was not, as far as the evidence shows, a residence." This is correct for the Western Deffufa, but the Eastern Deffufa (not discussed in this chapter) may have had different functions. This is acceptable for a chapter focused on the Kerma period's political culture.

**Pottery description: ACCURATE.** The description of the black-topped redware firing technique (position-dependent reduction/oxidation) is correct. The claim that it was made without a potter's wheel is correct. The claim about the quality relative to Egyptian wheel-made pottery is defensible and well-supported in the literature.

**Chronological framework: ACCURATE.** The three-phase division (Ancient, Middle, Classic Kerma) is standard. The date range (c. 2500-1550 BCE) is within the accepted range. The claim that the Classic Kerma period was c. 1750-1550 BCE is consistent with current dating.

## Concerns

1. **Retainer burial method.** The chapter states: "The evidence is consistent with the ingestion of a substance, but the evidence is not conclusive." This is appropriately cautious. The archaeological evidence does not conclusively establish the method of death. Some scholars have proposed poison; others have proposed other methods. The chapter is right to leave this open.

2. **The naming problem.** The chapter acknowledges that we do not know what these people called themselves. This is correct. "Kush" is the Egyptian designation. "Kerma" is the modern archaeological designation. No self-designation from the Kerma period has been recovered.

3. **Trade goods interpretation.** The chapter's claim that "The Egyptian objects in a Kerma tomb are not evidence of Egyptian influence. They are evidence of Kerma wealth" is a strong statement. I would accept it as defensible. The presence of Egyptian imports alongside local production in Kerma tombs is consistent with trade acquisition rather than cultural submission.

## Recommendation

**APPROVE.** The chapter represents the archaeological record accurately. The independence of Kerma's political culture is well-established. The cautious handling of the retainer burials is appropriate. The pottery description is technically accurate. No factual corrections required.
