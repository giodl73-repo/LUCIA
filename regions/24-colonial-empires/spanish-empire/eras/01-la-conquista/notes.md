# Notes — La Conquista: La Espada y la Cruz
## Production Bible

**Era:** Era 1 — La Conquista — La Espada y la Cruz (1492–1550 CE)
**People:** Spanish Empire
**Region:** 24 — Colonial Empires & Systems

---

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** The theological-legal requirement of justification.

The Spanish Empire could not act without first writing down that it was permitted to act. This was not cynicism — it was the inheritance of seven centuries of Reconquista theology, in which holy war required divine sanction, papal authority, and formal legal proceeding. Applied to the Americas, this inheritance produced the Requerimiento: a document that was simultaneously a legal instrument, a theological argument, and a performance of legitimacy covering conquest.

**Mechanism:** The requirement of justification generates both the act and its critique simultaneously. The same framework that produced the Requerimiento produced Las Casas. The same Crown that conducted the conquest convened the Valladolid Debate to argue about whether the conquest was just. The form demands the debate; the debate cannot resolve the form's contradictions; the form proceeds anyway.

**Terminal condition:** Premise non-recognition (v1.6 type 4). The Mexica and Quechua peoples did not fail to comprehend the Requerimiento because they were unsophisticated. They could not comprehend it because it was not addressed to them as they were — it was addressed to a legal fiction of what the Spanish theology required them to be. The system does not fail because it is weak or wrong within its own terms. It fails because the people on whom it operates do not recognize its premises as binding. The Requerimiento is read to no one because the people for whom it is intended have gone into the forest. They have made their verdict. The document cannot hear it.

- Inga Clendinnen, *Aztecs* (1991) — inside the Mexica worldview

**On the Voice Spectrum:** Legal-Theological register (confirmed: Babylon, Hammurabi's Babylon). The Spanish modulation is distinct: it holds the theological and the juridical in the same utterance AND holds the sincere believer AND the devout critic within the same framework. The Babylon Legal-Theological is a single voice (king-priest-god). The Spanish Legal-Theological is a structure in tension (Crown, jurist, friar, soldier). The pause mechanism is the period-as-verdict; the colon for theological enumeration; zero em-dashes.

## Key Figures

1. **Juan López de Palacios Rubios** (c. 1450–1524) — the jurist who drafted the Requerimiento. Trained at Salamanca and Bologna. Member of the Council of Castile. Sincere believer. When Montesinos raised the question of just war, Palacios Rubios was the man the Crown called on to provide the answer. His answer was the Requerimiento. Las Casas reported that when he heard how the document was being used, Palacios Rubios laughed. (A different laughter than Las Casas's.)

2. **Bartolomé de las Casas** (1484–1566) — the Dominican friar who spent fifty years arguing from inside the Catholic framework that the conquest was a sin. Born in Seville. Came to the Caribbean in 1502. Was an encomendero — he held the labor rights to indigenous people — until the Montesinos sermon of 1511 changed his life. Joined the Dominicans in 1522. His *Brevísima relación* (1542) was addressed to the Crown Prince of Spain. His figure at the Valladolid Debate (1550–51) arguing against Sepúlveda is the chapter's climactic figure: a man arguing, before the King's council, that what the King's agents were doing was a mortal sin, using the King's own theology to make the case.

3. **Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda** (c. 1490–1573) — the humanist scholar who argued the other side at Valladolid. He invoked Aristotle's doctrine of natural slavery: some people, by nature, were suited to be governed by others. The indigenous peoples of the Americas, he argued, were such people. He was a sincere humanist; he had read Aristotle carefully; his argument was coherent within the tradition he was drawing from. This is the point: both men at Valladolid were sincere, learned, and operating within recognized intellectual traditions. The debate was real. Its indecision was real.

4. **Hernán Cortés** (1485–1547) — the soldier. His letters to the Crown — the *Cartas de Relación* — are written in the same theological-legal register as the Requerimiento. He is explaining, constantly, the justice of what he is doing, the benefits brought to the indigenous peoples by Christianity and Spanish civilization, the gifts given to the Crown. He is simultaneously describing the destruction of one of the greatest cities in the world. The gap between the description and the justification is the gap the chapter inhabits.

5. **Moctezuma II** (c. 1466–1520) — the Mexica *tlatoani*, "speaker." Not a passive victim. A sophisticated political actor operating from a coherent cosmological framework in which the arrival of strangers from the east in the year One Reed had specific meaning. He received Cortés in Tenochtitlan; he was taken captive; he died in disputed circumstances (the Spanish said the Mexica killed him; the Mexica said the Spanish did). His worldview operated on premises incomprehensible to Cortés. Cortés's worldview operated on premises incomprehensible to Moctezuma. The meeting of those two incomprehensibilities is what Davis pushed on — the chapter should hold both.

6. **Guaman Poma de Ayala** (c. 1535–after 1616) — the indigenous Andean nobleman who wrote a 1,200-page illustrated chronicle in Spanish for the King of Spain, arguing that the indigenous peoples had rights under Spanish law and that the Spanish administrators were systematically violating those rights. He is the Achebe test's answer: a man who learned the colonizer's language and framework and used it to argue for his people's dignity, from inside, without abandoning his own identity.

## Narrative Arc

### Section 1 — The Form (opening, integrated): The Requerimiento, the empty village, the legal fiction. Establishes the structural engine. (~800 words)

### Section 2 — The Reconquista Logic: Why the theological-legal requirement was necessary. The 1492 year: Columbus arrives in the Caribbean on the same year the Reconquista ends at Granada; Ferdinand and Isabella sign the expulsion of the Jews. Three events in one year. The Spanish identity is formed by holy war, and holy war requires justification. Established quickly (~500 words), through the figure of Isabella at Granada.

### Section 3 — Tenochtitlan, November 1519: Cortés and his soldiers descend from the mountain pass and see the city. Bernal Díaz's account: "we soldiers asked if it was a dream." 200,000 people. The great market at Tlatelolco. The causeways over the lake. The Mexica emperor receiving the Spanish in his city because his own cosmological framework required careful management of their arrival. Both worldviews present. The encounter before the violence. (~700 words)

### Section 4 — The Montesinos Sermon and the Legal Response: Fourth Sunday of Advent, 1511. The Dominican's challenge. The colonists' outrage. The Crown's response: Palacios Rubios called to provide the theological-legal justification. The drafting of the Requerimiento. Its logic explained from inside: not cynicism, but the Reconquista's habit of justification, applied to a new theater. (~600 words)

### Section 5 — Valladolid, 1550–51: The debate. Las Casas and Sepúlveda before the Council of the Indies. Both sincere. Both learned. The question on the table: do the indigenous peoples of the Americas have rational souls? If yes, what follows for the legality of the conquest? If no, what follows for the humanity of the people? The council cannot verdict. The debate produces no resolution. The conquest continues. This section carries the chapter's maximum internal tension. (~700 words)

### Section 6 — The New Laws and Their Failure: 1542. Las Casas finally persuades the Crown. The New Laws of the Indies: the encomienda system to be phased out, indigenous people to be protected, the viceroy to enforce. Blasco Núñez Vela sent to Peru to implement. The encomenderos revolt. The viceroy is killed. The laws are suspended. The gap between Madrid and Peru, between the law and the practice, is made literal: eight weeks by ship, and everything changes in the crossing. (~500 words)

### Section 7 — Landing: What the documents say. The Archivo General de Indias in Seville holds 80 million pages of records from the Spanish colonial empire. Every transaction, every trial, every Requerimiento, every testimony of every indigenous witness who appeared before a Spanish court. The empire documented itself. It generated both the atrocity and the record of the atrocity and the argument against the atrocity and the failed reform of the atrocity and the record of the failure, all in the same archive. The landing should find the persistence mechanism: the documents themselves. The Requerimiento is still in the archive. Las Casas's testimony is still in the archive. The silence of the people who could not speak Spanish and could not appear before the courts is also, by its absence, in the archive. (~400 words)

## Panel Pressure Points (from PANEL-1)

**Tuchman:** Cortés and Pizarro must be present as specific figures, not surveys. The opening's legal-theological weight must be matched with scene-level specificity in the chapter. The transition from the Requirement to the practice must be earned through scenes, not assertion.

**Ibn Khaldun:** The Valladolid Debate must hold the no-verdict as the historical fact of maximum importance. The Crown's structural incapacity to adjudicate its own legitimacy crisis is the chapter's explanatory core. The Reconquista as the prior history of the theological-legal requirement must be present.

**Achebe:** Las Casas's theological register, not modern human rights language. Guaman Poma de Ayala as the intersectional figure. The word "innocents" from Las Casas's own vocabulary, not the text's. The chapter must stay inside the Spanish theological framework even through the violence — and find Las Casas's way of describing the violence from inside.

**Davis:** The encounter of worldviews at Tenochtitlan. Moctezuma as a sophisticated political actor with a coherent cosmological framework. The destruction of knowledge systems as one of the chapter's dimensions. The Mexica astronomical knowledge, the agricultural knowledge of the milpa.

**Kapuściński:** More sensory specificity in the Caribbean / Mexican landscape. The Bernal Díaz account of Tenochtitlan as the sensory anchor for Section 3. The heat, the smell, the specific physicality of the encounter.

## Open Questions

1. Can the chapter find a moment of Guaman Poma's voice — a sentence or phrase from the *Nueva Corónica* — that can be quoted in the text to place him there as a presence rather than a reference?

2. The Valladolid Debate's no-verdict: how to render this without turning it into an abstraction? One approach: describe the specific scene — the council chamber, the two men present, the jury of theologians taking notes — and end the section with the empty verdict, the silence where the resolution should be.

3. The landing's archive image: is it too quiet for a chapter this charged? Alternatives: end on Las Casas's last testimony (he was still writing at age 82, still arguing); end on the Requerimiento in the empty forest; end on Guaman Poma's letter to the King, which arrived in Spain and was filed and lost for three centuries, found in Copenhagen in 1908.
