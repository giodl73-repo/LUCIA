# Figures — The Songhai Succession

## The Mansalu of the Contraction

The historical record thins here, and honesty requires saying so. The mansalu who ruled the Manden Kurufaba in the mid-15th century are names in genealogies without the vivid descriptions that al-Umari's informants gave to Musa. The jelilu carried their names, and the Tarikh chronicles recorded some of them, but the details that would make them dimensional as narrative figures do not survive in forms we can verify. Mansa Musa II, Mansa Maghan III, others whose ordinal numbers scholars dispute: they held the throne and received the genealogy and faced the contraction. What they thought about the tributaries who stopped paying, what strategies they attempted, what private calculations they made as the court grew smaller around them, the record does not say.

This silence is not a failure of the jeli tradition. The jelilu carried the names faithfully. But the written sources that supplement the oral record for this period are sparse, and the oral tradition's own conventions preserve genealogy and covenant terms more reliably than they preserve the inner lives of individual rulers. The chapter places the mansalu of this period in the scene because they were there. It does not invent interiority the record cannot support. What it can say is that they sat on the raised platform and received the genealogy and heard the full list of names from Sundiata forward, and that the list did not shorten because the empire did, and that this mattered because the genealogy was the covenant made audible.

## Sonni Ali Ber (r. c. 1464-1492)

The Tarikh al-Sudan, written by the Timbuktu scholar al-Sadi roughly two centuries after Sonni Ali's death, depicts him as a man of immense energy and military power. His war canoes moved on the Niger with a speed that gave him strategic reach no other ruler in the region could match. He took Timbuktu from the Tuareg in 1468. He besieged Djenne for years and took it around 1473. He built the Songhai into a territorial power that controlled the Niger bend and the inland delta, the commercial heartland that had sustained Mali's wealth.

The scholars of Timbuktu, who composed the chronicle that preserves his memory, feared and disliked him. Al-Sadi's account describes Sonni Ali as harsh toward the learned, as someone who kept traditional Songhai religious practices alongside his Islam in ways the scholarly class considered impure or dangerous. This judgment is the scholars' own. They were men of Islamic learning whose authority rested on their relationship with rulers who respected that learning, and Sonni Ali was not such a ruler. His power came from the river, from his soldiers, from the Songhai people's own traditions, and he did not defer to Timbuktu's scholars the way the Askia dynasty later would.

What the scholarly sources do not provide is the Songhai oral tradition's own account of Sonni Ali. The jeli tradition of the Mande did not carry Songhai genealogies, and the Songhai's own oral historians, the jesere, left accounts that were not recorded in writing in the way the Tarikh chronicles were. We have the scholars' Sonni Ali, a figure seen from the vantage of the men he did not favor. The Sonni Ali that his own people would have sung is largely lost to the written record. The chapter presents him from inside the logic of his actions: a ruler building power on the river, using the tools the river gave him, expanding when the opportunity the Manden's contraction created presented itself. The scholars' judgment is noted; it is not endorsed as the final word.

## Askia Muhammad (Muhammad Ture, r. 1493-1528)

He came to power through violence. Sonni Ali's son, Sonni Baru, inherited the throne, and Muhammad Ture, a general in Sonni Ali's army, overthrew him at the battle of Anfao in 1493. The coup was justified, in the Tarikh chronicles, as the triumph of Islamic orthodoxy over the syncretic practices of the Sonni dynasty. Whether Muhammad Ture's motivations were religious, political, or both is a question the sources do not resolve cleanly.

What he built after the coup was an administered state. Provinces, governors, courts of law, systematized taxation. His hajj to Mecca in 1496-97 served the double purpose of piety and legitimation. He sought and received the title of Caliph of the Sudan from an Abbasid authority in Cairo, placing himself within the framework of Islamic political authority in a way that the Mande mansalu, whose legitimacy rested on the Kurukan Fuga, had never sought or needed.

He was a patron of scholars. The Sankore mosque and the scholarly families of Timbuktu flourished under his rule in ways they had not under Sonni Ali. The manuscripts multiplied. The correspondence with Fez and Cairo and the Hejaz intensified. The scholarly tradition that had survived the changes of patron now entered another productive phase.

But the pattern repeated. Askia Muhammad went blind. His sons, unwilling to wait, deposed him in 1528. He lived on, powerless, while his children fought over the state he had built. He died around 1538. The dynasty he founded continued, each succession more contested than the last, until the Moroccan invasion of 1591 ended the Songhai Empire by a mechanism no internal dynamic had prepared it for: muskets against swords, fired by mercenaries crossing the desert from the north.

## Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti (1556-1627)

He was born into the scholarly tradition the way a Kouyate jeli was born into the oral tradition: the work was in the family's blood and in the family's library. His family, the Aqit, had been prominent scholars in Timbuktu for generations. The manuscripts in their house had been copied and collected under Mali's patronage, under Tuareg control, under Sonni Ali, and under the Askia dynasty. Ahmad Baba grew up handling pages that carried the intellectual life of centuries.

He became Timbuktu's most prolific scholar, writing works on Islamic jurisprudence, biographical dictionaries, and legal opinions. His scholarship was recognized in Fez and Cairo and Mecca. When the Moroccan army arrived in 1591 and dismantled the Songhai state, Ahmad Baba was among the scholars arrested and deported to Marrakesh. He spent years in exile, teaching and writing, maintaining the practice of scholarship in conditions that were not of his choosing. He returned to Timbuktu eventually and died there in 1627, his life spanning the full arc of the Songhai Empire's rise and destruction.

His significance for this chapter is what he represents: the persistence of intellectual tradition across the changes of political power. The manuscripts did not belong to Mali or to Songhai or to Morocco. They belonged to the families who carried them and the scholars who read them and the students who copied them. Ahmad Baba is the tradition made visible in a single life.

## The Jelilu of Kangaba

They are not a single person. They are an institution that has persisted for eight centuries and is still persisting. The Kouyate, the Diabate, and their fellow jeli lineages carry the genealogy of the Keita dynasty, the terms of the Kurukan Fuga, the history of the Manden in all its expansion and contraction. The seven-year ceremony at Kangaba, maintained by the Diabate lineage, renews the covenant through the physical act of rebuilding the roof of the sacred house and the oral act of reciting the terms within it.

In this chapter, the jelilu frame the story. They sing the full genealogy at the opening, in a court that has contracted around them, and they continue singing at the close, in a world that has contracted further still. The covenant they carry is not dependent on the empire's size. It was a social agreement before it was an imperial one, and it persists in the Mande heartland because the people who live by it persist. The jelilu do not mourn the empire. They do not comment on the contraction. They sing the names, all of them, and the completeness of the genealogy is itself the argument: the Manden did not end. The Manden changed shape. The song remained whole.
