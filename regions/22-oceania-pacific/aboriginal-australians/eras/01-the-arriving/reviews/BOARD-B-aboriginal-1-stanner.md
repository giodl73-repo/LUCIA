# B-aboriginal-1 — W.E.H. Stanner — Board Review of "When the Ancestors Walked"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** The Dreaming as ontology; the relationship between Country, identity, and law; the philosophical framework that underlies Aboriginal knowledge systems.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The Tjukurpa is correctly presented as an ever-present dimension of reality, not a past-tense mythology. "The Tjukurpa was not the past. It was the ground beneath the present, holding everything up." This is accurate to the scholarly consensus and to what Aboriginal people themselves describe.
- The multiple names for the Dreaming (Altyerrenge, Wangarr, Tjukurpa) are correctly attributed to their respective nations (Arrernte, Yolngu, Pitjantjatjara).
- The description of ceremony as maintenance of reality ("refreshing the Ancestor's presence in the country") is accurate. The Wandjina repainting passage correctly describes the act of repainting as ceremony, not art restoration.
- The "everywhen" concept is present in spirit ("The Dreaming is not a period. It is a dimension") though the term itself is not used, which is appropriate since it is my coinage, not an Aboriginal term.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter does not use the word "believe" in relation to the Dreaming. This is correct. One does not "believe in" the Dreaming any more than one believes in gravity.
- Country is presented as having agency and being alive. "The country itself, patient, ancient, alive in the way the Dreaming says the land is alive." This is consistent with how Aboriginal people describe their relationship to Country.
- The sacred-knowledge boundary is respected. The chapter does not reproduce ceremony content. The figures.md explicitly acknowledges that custodians hold restricted material.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The Songline as simultaneously navigational, legal, ecological, and cosmological. This is the correct rendering.
- The deep-time passage's resolution through the Dreaming's own temporal logic. "The Tjukurpa does not count years." This is philosophically accurate and avoids imposing a Western temporal framework.
- The integration argument: Country as the point where all knowledge systems converge. This is the most accurate single statement the chapter could make about Aboriginal epistemology.

**What needs correction:**
- The phrase "the time before time, in the Tjukurpa" slightly risks positioning the Tjukurpa as a temporal period despite the later correction. Consider: "in the Tjukurpa, which is not a time but the ground of all time" or simply removing "the time before time."
- The Madjedbebe dating (65,000 years) should note that this dating, while published in Nature, has been questioned by some archaeologists who argue for a lower date (c. 50,000 years). The chapter should acknowledge this uncertainty. The current phrasing "That is the minimum" implies the dating is settled, when it is debated.
- Minor: the chapter says the Arrernte had "eight skin groups." The Arrernte subsection system is more complex than eight discrete groups; it involves eight named categories that interact with patrilineal and matrilineal moiety divisions. The simplified statement is not wrong but could be more precise.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (apply the three corrections noted above)
