# The Late Republic and the Crisis

## I. The Gracchi

Tiberius Sempronius Gracchus stood on the Rostra in the summer of 133 BCE and asked the citizens of Roma a question.

The wild beasts of Italia have their dens, he said. The men who fight and die for Roma have nothing. They fight for the wealth of others. They are called the masters of the world, and they do not own a clod of earth.

The speech is in Plutarch, who wrote it two centuries later. The words are Plutarch's construction. But the problem Gracchus named was real and structural. The small farmers who had been the Republic's citizen-soldiers were being swallowed. The great estates, the latifundia, worked by the enslaved captives of the eastern wars, were absorbing the ager publicus, the public land that had been the citizen's patrimony. The wealthy held land beyond the legal limit, and the men who should have enforced the limit were the men who benefited from violating it. The Senate that governed the res publica was the class that was taking the public land.

Gracchus proposed a land commission to enforce the existing law. The Licinian-Sextian rogation of 367 BCE had limited individual holdings of public land to five hundred iugera. The law had been ignored for generations. Gracchus asked that it be enforced and the surplus redistributed to citizens who had lost their farms. The proposal did not create new law. It demanded the enforcement of old law. In the Republic's own terms, it was conservative.

The Senate's response was to deploy the tribunate against the tribune. Marcus Octavius, a fellow tribune, vetoed the bill. Gracchus, in the act that broke the first precedent, had the assembly remove Octavius from office. The act was without precedent because the tribune's person was sacrosanct and his term fixed. What Gracchus did was logically defensible: a tribune who vetoed the people's will was not serving the people's interest, and the people who had elected him could remove him. But the logic destroyed the veto's absolute character, and the veto's absolute character was what made the tribunate a constitutional check rather than a political tool.

The land commission was established. Gracchus stood for re-election, which was unusual but not clearly illegal. The opposition hardened. On election day, Scipio Nasica, the pontifex maximus, led a group of senators and their clients to the Capitoline where Gracchus was addressing the crowd. They armed themselves with clubs and broken chair legs from the benches. They killed Gracchus and three hundred of his supporters. The bodies were thrown into the Tiber without the funeral rites that every Roman citizen was owed.

The Senate declared that it had acted to defend the res publica. The formula that would be used to justify the killing, the senatus consultum ultimum, the Senate's ultimate decree, would be formally articulated later, but the principle was born on that day: the Senate could declare an emergency and authorize lethal force against citizens without trial. The principle was extra-constitutional. No law authorized it. No precedent supported it. But the res publica had no provision for what happened when the Senate and a tribune were in irreconcilable conflict, and the Senate filled the gap with violence and called the violence legal.

Gaius Gracchus, Tiberius's younger brother, took up the cause ten years later and pushed further. He extended the grain subsidy, established colonies for landless citizens, reformed the tax collection in the provinces, and challenged the Senate's monopoly on the juries that tried provincial governors for corruption. He was a more systematic reformer than his brother, and the Senate's response was more systematic in turn. In 121 BCE, the senatus consultum ultimum was formally invoked. Gaius died in the violence that followed, either by his own hand or by a loyal follower's. Three thousand of his supporters were killed. The tribunate, which had been designed to protect the plebs from patrician abuse, had become the weapon that both sides fought to control.

## II. The General's Army

The Republic's army changed, and the change was decisive.

Gaius Marius was a novus homo, a new man, the first in his family to hold the consulship, an outsider from the Italian town of Arpinum who had made his career in the military rather than the Senate. In 107 BCE, facing a difficult war in North Africa against the Numidian king Jugurtha, Marius did what no commander before him had done: he recruited soldiers without regard to the property qualification. The landless, the proletarii who had nothing to declare at the census, were admitted to the legions.

The reform solved an immediate problem. The census-based army was running out of qualified men, because the qualified men were the small farmers who were disappearing. The landless were available, willing, and numerous. But the reform created a new problem that the Republic would not survive. The landless soldier owned nothing. He had no farm to return to after the campaign. His future depended entirely on the general who recruited him and who would, at the end of service, be expected to provide for him, usually through a land grant. The soldier's loyalty shifted from the res publica, which had given him nothing, to the general, who had given him everything: food, equipment, identity, and the promise of a future. Marius's army was not the Republic's army. It was Marius's army.

Marius defeated Jugurtha. He defeated the Cimbri and Teutones, Germanic peoples who had invaded Gaul and threatened Italia, in two battles (Aquae Sextiae in 102, Vercellae in 101) that the Romani treated as deliverance from existential crisis. He was elected consul six times in succession, a record without precedent that would have been impossible under the old norms. The mos maiorum said a man should wait ten years between consulships. Marius held the consulship as if it were a permanent office, and the city accepted it because the city was afraid of the Cimbri, and Marius was the man who stopped them.

The Marian army reform was the hinge. Before Marius, the army belonged to the census. After Marius, the army belonged to the general. The men who marched under their commander's eagle owed their future to his political success in Roma, and the commander who could deliver land grants to his veterans was the commander whose veterans would follow him anywhere. Including into Roma itself.

## III. Sulla's Republic

Lucius Cornelius Sulla marched his legions into Roma in 88 BCE. He was the first Roman general to do so. He would not be the last.

The immediate cause was the tribunate again. The tribune Sulpicius Rufus had proposed transferring an eastern military command from Sulla to Marius. The command was lucrative: the war against King Mithridates of Pontus promised the plunder of the richest region in the Mediterranean. Sulla was consul, the command was legally his, and the tribune's bill would strip it from him. Sulla did what no Roman had done. He turned to his soldiers, told them that the tribune's act would transfer the plunder to another general's army, and asked them to follow him. They followed. Six legions marched on Roma. Marius fled. Sulla imposed his will by force and then left for the east.

When he returned in 82 BCE, after defeating Mithridates and accumulating the wealth and the army to make himself unchallengeable, he did something the tradition could not categorize: he became dictator. The dictatorship was a Republican institution, a temporary grant of supreme power for a specific emergency, limited to six months. Sulla took the title without a time limit and used it to restructure the Republic.

The proscriptions came first. Lists of names were posted in the Forum. Any citizen whose name appeared was declared an outlaw. His property was confiscated. His children were barred from public office. Anyone could kill a proscribed man and claim a bounty. The lists grew. Sulla's enemies, his rivals, men whose property was coveted by his allies, were added. The proscriptions killed perhaps nine thousand people, including forty senators and sixteen hundred equestrians. The wealth of the dead was auctioned and bought by Sulla's supporters, including a young man named Marcus Licinius Crassus, who would become the wealthiest man in Roma by purchasing proscribed property at a fraction of its value.

Then Sulla reformed the Republic. He doubled the Senate's size. He stripped the tribunes of their legislative power. He strengthened the cursus honorum, requiring fixed age and experience requirements for each magistracy. He increased the number of courts and the Senate's control over them. He did everything in his power to restore the Senate's supremacy and to prevent another man from doing what he himself had done.

Then he resigned. He laid down the dictatorship, became a private citizen, and retired to his estate in Campania, where he died in 78 BCE. The Romani did not know what to make of a man who had seized absolute power and given it back. Sulla's epitaph, which he wrote himself, said that no friend ever did him a kindness and no enemy ever did him a wrong without being fully repaid. The epitaph was not about the Republic. It was about Sulla.

The Sullan constitution lasted less than a decade. The tribunes' powers were restored in 70 BCE. The extraordinary commands that Sulla had tried to prevent were granted to Pompey within a year. The men who dismantled Sulla's system were not revolutionaries. They were practical men who needed tools that the Sullan constitution had locked away. But the lesson Sulla had taught could not be unlearned: the army could take Roma, and the man who held the army held the power, and the constitution was paper if the swords said otherwise.

## IV. The Last Men

Three men divided the world.

Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus had made himself the most powerful Roman by the conventional means of the late Republic: military victories abroad, political alliances at home, a personal army of veterans loyal to his name. He had cleared the Mediterranean of pirates in a single campaign season, conquered the east from Pontus to Syria, reorganized the Roman provinces in Asia with an authority the Senate had not formally granted, and returned to Roma in 62 BCE with more wealth, more clients, and more prestige than any Roman since Sulla.

Marcus Licinius Crassus was the wealthiest man in Roma, his fortune built on proscription-era purchases, fire-brigade extortion, and the systematic exploitation of every opportunity the late Republic offered to a man with capital and no scruples. His wealth bought influence. His influence bought clients. His clients bought elections. He wanted military glory to match his financial power, and the wanting would kill him: in 53 BCE he led an army into Parthia, was defeated at Carrhae in the Mesopotamian desert, and his head was delivered to the Parthian king, who was watching a performance of Euripides when the trophy arrived.

Gaius Julius Caesar was the youngest, the most indebted, the most ambitious, and the most dangerous. He was a patrician of the gens Julia, a family that claimed descent from Venus through Aeneas, and the claim mattered because it placed him in the oldest layer of the Roman tradition while giving him access to the popular politics his family connections enabled. He had served in the east, held the aedileship and praetorship, borrowed enormously to fund the public games that built his popularity, and allied himself with Pompey and Crassus in the arrangement the later historians called the First Triumvirate: an informal pact in which three men agreed to use their combined resources to control the Republic's machinery.

The arrangement gave Caesar what he needed: the consulship in 59 BCE and a proconsular command in Gaul. The command was meant to last five years. It lasted nine, and in those nine years Caesar conquered Gaul from the Pyrenees to the Rhine, crossed the Channel to Britannia, crossed the Rhine into Germania, and created an army of ten legions whose soldiers worshipped him and whose officers owed their careers to his patronage. The wealth of Gaul flowed to Roma in his dispatches and in the slave caravans that followed the legions. The war killed, by Caesar's own accounting, a million Gauls. The number was propaganda. The scale was real.

By 50 BCE, Crassus was dead and the triumvirate was a rivalry. The Senate, or the faction within it that feared Caesar more than it feared Pompey, demanded that Caesar disband his army and return to Roma as a private citizen. Caesar knew what awaited a private citizen who had made the enemies he had made: prosecution, exile, the destruction of everything he had built. On January 10, 49 BCE, he stood at the Rubicon, the small river that marked the boundary between his province and Italia, the boundary across which no general could legally lead his army.

He crossed. The civil war that followed lasted four years and ranged from Iberia to Greece to Egypt to North Africa. Pompey fled to Greece, lost at Pharsalus in 48, fled to Egypt, and was murdered on the beach by agents of the Egyptian king who calculated that Caesar would prefer a dead rival to a living one. Caesar pursued the war to its end, defeated the last Pompeian armies in Africa and Iberia, and returned to Roma as dictator. He was appointed dictator perpetuo in 44 BCE: dictator in perpetuity. The title abolished the time limit that was the dictatorship's constitutional justification.

## V. The Ides

On March 15, 44 BCE, Caesar entered the Senate meeting in the Theatre of Pompey and was surrounded by men he knew.

Twenty-three stab wounds. The assassins called themselves Liberatores, the Liberators, and the name claimed that what they were doing was what Brutus had done five centuries earlier: freeing the res publica from a king. Marcus Junius Brutus, a descendant (he said) of the first Brutus, was among them. Gaius Cassius Longinus had organized them. They killed Caesar at the foot of Pompey's statue, which later writers found meaningful, and they walked into the Forum with their bloody hands raised, expecting the citizens to cheer.

The citizens did not cheer. The Forum was silent. The Liberators had killed the tyrant, and the people they claimed to have freed stood in the silence and waited, and the waiting was the Republic's verdict on itself. The machinery the Liberators had tried to restore was already gone. The Senate that should have resumed its authority was too frightened and too divided to act. The legions in the provinces answered to their commanders, not to the res publica. The veterans Caesar had settled on Italian land were loyal to Caesar's memory, not to the Senate's principles.

What followed was the final civil war. Marcus Antonius, Caesar's lieutenant, seized the initiative. Octavian, Caesar's adopted heir, entered the contest at eighteen years old with nothing but the name and the inheritance. Cicero, the Republic's most eloquent voice, tried to use Octavian against Antonius and miscalculated. The Second Triumvirate, a formal pact between Octavian, Antonius, and Lepidus, produced proscriptions worse than Sulla's. Cicero's name was on the list. The soldiers found him in his litter, fleeing. He bared his neck. They cut off his head and his hands, and Antonius's wife Fulvia, who had personal reasons for hatred, was said to have driven her hairpin through the tongue that had spoken against her husband.

Cicero had written, in the Republic's last years, that the res publica was a commonwealth that belonged to the people and was held together by agreement on law and by community of interest. The definition was the Republic's epitaph. The agreement was gone. The community of interest was gone. What remained was power, and power belonged to the men who commanded the armies.

## VI. The Settlement

At Actium, in 31 BCE, Octavian's fleet defeated Antonius and Cleopatra, and the last obstacle to one-man rule was gone.

Octavian returned to Roma and did not make Sulla's mistake or Caesar's. He did not declare himself dictator. He did not abolish the Senate or the magistracies or the tribunate. He stood in the Senate in January of 27 BCE and offered to return his extraordinary powers to the res publica. The Senate, which understood the offer as the Romani had always understood the public gesture, asked him to keep the powers. They gave him the title Augustus, a word with religious overtones that placed him above the ordinary without calling him king. They gave him the tribunician power permanently, which made his person sacrosanct and gave him the veto. They gave him proconsular imperium over the provinces where the armies were stationed, which gave him command of every legion. They gave him these things in the form of grants from the res publica, renewable and (in theory) revocable, because the fiction that the Republic continued was necessary for everyone who needed the fiction.

The Republic was over. The machinery was still there. The consuls still held office for a year. The Senate still met and debated and issued its consulta. The tribunes still stood in the Forum. The elections still happened. The augures still watched the sky. The geese on the Capitoline were still fed. Every form of the res publica continued, and every form was now a shell around a different reality: one man held the power, and the man's name was Augustus, and the settlement he made in 27 BCE was the foundation of what the Romani would call the principate, the rule of the princeps, the first citizen, the first among equals who was in practice the last word.

The oath the first Brutus had sworn, that Roma would never again have a king, was technically unbroken. Augustus was not a king. He was something the mos maiorum had no category for, and the absence of a category was itself a form of power. The man who cannot be named cannot be opposed with the tools the tradition provides for opposing named threats. The Republic had built its machinery to prevent kingship, and the machinery had been defeated not by a king but by a man who held all the powers of a king without the title, and who held them with the Senate's permission, granted in a ceremony that looked, to anyone who wanted it to look, like the res publica functioning as it always had.

The Forum still stood between the hills. The Tiber still moved below. The roads ran outward in every direction, and on those roads the legions still marched, and the legions now belonged to the princeps, who was not a king. The Senate still deliberated. The consuls still served their year. The tribune still said veto, though what the tribune forbade and what the princeps permitted were now, in practice, the same thing. The public thing had not been destroyed. It had been absorbed into a single man, and the single man maintained its forms, and the forms would hold for another five centuries in the east, and the holding would look, from inside, enough like the res publica to let the Romani tell themselves that the oath still held.
