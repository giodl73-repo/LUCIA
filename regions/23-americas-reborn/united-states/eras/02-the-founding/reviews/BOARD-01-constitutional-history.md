# Board Review — B-01: Constitutional History
## United States Era 2: The Founding — The Experiment Begins

**Reviewer profile:** Specialist in early American constitutional history; works primarily on the ratification period and the founding generation's political thought; has published on Madison's Convention Notes and the Federalist Papers.

---

### Factual Accuracy Assessment

**The Three-Fifths Compromise:** The chapter quotes Article I, Section 2 correctly and handles the provision's political logic accurately. The observation that the text avoided the word "enslaved" is historically correct and is noted by scholars including Paul Finkelman ("The Proslavery Constitution," 1987). The chapter correctly identifies the structural advantage this gave southern states in House representation and the Electoral College.

**The Articles of Confederation:** The characterization of the Articles' weaknesses (no taxing power, no commerce regulation, no compulsion of the states) is accurate. Shays' Rebellion (1786–87) is correctly placed and correctly described as a catalyst for the Convention. The characterization is consistent with Gordon Wood's account.

**The Convention record:** The claim that Madison kept the most complete notes is correct; his notes, published in 1840, remain the primary source for Convention proceedings. The characterization of his commitment to secrecy until the last delegate died is accurate — Madison outlived all other delegates.

**Hamilton's election intervention:** The chapter states that Hamilton "worked behind the scenes to break the deadlock in Jefferson's favor" and writes that Hamilton chose Jefferson over Burr after "thirty-six ballots." The ballot count is correct (thirty-six ballots over six days in February 1801). Hamilton's letters in support of Jefferson over Burr are documented in the Hamilton Papers. The chapter handles this accurately.

**The Bill of Rights:** The chapter states it was added "not from conviction but from political necessity — the Anti-Federalists would not ratify otherwise" and notes that Madison "had been against it, wrote it anyway to secure ratification in Virginia." This is accurate. Madison's Federalist No. 84 (written under Hamilton's name but attributed by scholars to Hamilton, not Madison) argued against a Bill of Rights. Madison subsequently drafted the Bill of Rights as the price of Virginia's ratification. The chapter correctly attributes this.

**One correction required:** The chapter attributes Federalist No. 84 to Hamilton; this is consistent with the best available attribution (most scholars credit Hamilton with No. 84). However, for scholarly precision, this should be noted as "attributed to Hamilton" given that the Federalist was published pseudonymously and attribution of specific papers has been a matter of historical dispute. Recommend adding "(attributed to Hamilton)" to any citation of Federalist No. 84 if the chapter directly cites the paper.

---

### Cultural Representation Assessment

**The Free African Society:** Correctly dated (April 12, 1787) and correctly described. The chapter correctly identifies Allen and Jones as co-founders. The Society's functions (mutual aid, sickness benefits, widow support, arbitration) are historically documented.

**Richard Allen's St. George's incident:** The chapter quotes Allen's account directly. Allen's autobiography is the primary source; the incident is dated by scholars to November 1792. The chapter's quotation ("We all went out of the church in a body, and they were no longer plagued with us in the church") is accurate to Allen's account. No correction required.

**Benjamin Banneker:** The chapter describes Banneker as "part of the surveying team, under Andrew Ellicott, that laid out the boundaries of the District of Columbia." This is historically documented. Banneker's letter to Jefferson (August 19, 1791) and Jefferson's reply (August 30, 1791) are in the primary record. The chapter's paraphrase of Banneker's letter is accurate to its content; the quotation from the letter is accurate.

**Sally Hemings:** The chapter handles the Hemings material with appropriate scholarly caution. The 1998 DNA study (Foster et al., Nature) established a genetic match between Jefferson's male line and descendants of Eston Hemings, the youngest of Sally Hemings's surviving sons. The chapter correctly notes that "modern DNA evidence confirms" the relationship. Madison Hemings's 1873 interview in the Pike County Republican (Ohio) is the primary source for the Paris negotiation account; the chapter accurately summarizes his testimony.

---

### Recommendation

**The chapter is historically accurate within the scope of its period.** The characterization of the founding paradox is consistent with the best current scholarship (Morgan, Gordon-Reed, Davis). No factual errors require correction.

**Minor recommendation:** The chapter's reference to Hamilton's death should note that the duel was fought in Weehawken, New Jersey (not simply "New Jersey") for historical precision. Current text says "in a duel in New Jersey," which is accurate but imprecise.

**Endorsement:** The chapter represents the founding period with historical accuracy, is grounded in primary sources, and handles contested historical material (Sally Hemings, Banneker's letter) with appropriate scholarly precision. Approved for FINAL.
