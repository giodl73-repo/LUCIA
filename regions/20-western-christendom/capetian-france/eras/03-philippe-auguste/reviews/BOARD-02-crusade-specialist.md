# BOARD Review — Domain Expert 2
## Albigensian Crusade and Cathar Studies
### Philippe Auguste et la Grande Expansion, Era 3

**Reviewer profile:** Specialist in the Albigensian Crusade, Catharism, and Languedocian society in the 12th-13th centuries. Expertise in Guillaume de Tudèle's *Canso*, Cathar theology, and the military and legal dimensions of the crusade.

---

## Factual Accuracy

**Cathar theology:** The chapter characterizes the Cathars as those who "believed that the material world was the creation of an evil god and that salvation required the rejection of matter altogether." This is correct for the dualist position and is the standard characterization. The use of *bonshommes* and *parfaits* as the Cathars' own terms for themselves is accurate and appropriate.

**Béziers date:** July 22, 1209. Correct.

**Arnold Amalric's statement:** The chapter correctly notes the uncertainty: "Whether he said precisely these words, the chroniclers differ." The statement appears in Caesarius of Heisterbach's *Dialogus Miraculorum* (c. 1223), written more than a decade after the event; Arnold Amalric's own letter to Innocent III does not contain it. The chapter's handling of the uncertainty is exemplary.

**Guillaume de Tudèle:** The characterization of him as "an Occitan cleric who wrote the first part of the *Canso de la Crozada* and who was not hostile to the crusade's purposes" is accurate. The *Canso* changes authorship and tone at approximately verse 2770; the second part, by an anonymous continuator, is markedly more hostile to the crusade and the northerners.

**Figures for Béziers:** The chapter presents Guillaume de Tudèle's figure of "seven thousand in the Church of the Madeleine alone" and the legate's figure of "nearly twenty thousand." Both are documented in the sources cited. Modern scholarship generally considers both figures inflated; estimates of the actual dead range from several thousand to the low tens of thousands. The chapter handles this by presenting both source figures without endorsing either, which is the correct scholarly approach.

**Raymond-Roger Trencavel:** The chapter correctly states he "died in de Montfort's custody in November." The death is documented in the sources; the cause is disputed (disease in prison vs. foul play). The chapter does not specify cause, which is appropriate given the uncertainty.

---

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter's treatment of the Albigensian Crusade is one of the most delicate passages in the chapter, and it handles it correctly. The section maintains the Capetian frame (Philip watching from Paris, the mechanism working without his direct hand) while letting the physical reality of Béziers carry what cannot be stated within that frame. The sentence "The southern landscape around Béziers was dry and pale in the July heat, the limestone hills bleached and still. The city burned for three days. The smoke was visible from the next town on the road" achieves exactly what the best historical writing about atrocity achieves: it does not editorialize, but the reader understands.

The inclusion of Guillaume de Tudèle as a southern counter-voice, rendered briefly, is correct and sufficient. A full treatment of the Cathar perspective belongs to a different chapter (possibly the Toulouse or Languedoc chapter in a later era); this chapter correctly limits itself to what can be held simultaneously with the Capetian frame.

---

## Corrections Required

**Minor correction:** The chapter describes the crusaders as "bearing the cross of a papal crusade." More precisely, participants took the crusading vow and wore the cross (hence "crusaders"), but the theological mechanism was the promise of the same indulgences as a Crusade to the Holy Land. The phrase is not incorrect but could be more precise: "bearing the cross, promised the same indulgences as those who went to Jerusalem."

**Minor note:** Philip's role is accurately characterized as authorization without direct participation. The chapter might note briefly that Philip did send his son Louis (who led the 1215-1219 phase of the crusade) and that the crown's legal acquisition of Languedoc was formalized by the Treaty of Paris of 1229, under Louis IX, rather than under Philip directly. This is the era's next chapter, but the chapter's framing ("the French crown would eventually receive" the territories) is accurate and appropriately nonspecific.

---

## Recommendation

APPROVE with minor suggestions. The Béziers section is the chapter's hardest historical challenge and it meets it with both scholarly accuracy and literary integrity. The use of dual sources (Guillaume de Tudèle and the legate's letter) for the casualty figures is exemplary. The theological characterization of Catharism is accurate.
