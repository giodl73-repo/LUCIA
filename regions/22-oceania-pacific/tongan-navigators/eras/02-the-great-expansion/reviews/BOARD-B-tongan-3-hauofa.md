## B-tongan-3 — Epeli Hau'ofa — Board Review of The Great Expansion

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Tongan-Fijian intellectual perspective on Pacific identity, the ocean-as-world framing, and the politics of how Pacific peoples are represented.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The use of my essay "Our Sea of Islands" in the landing section is appropriate. I wrote that essay because the language of "small islands in a far sea" was diminishing our people, and this chapter uses my argument the way I intended it: as a correction, from the inside, of an outsider's error.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter passes the basic test. The ocean is the road, not the obstacle. The Tongans are the protagonists. The knowledge system is real.
- The fine mat exchange is treated with the weight it deserves. The 'ie tonga as biography, as material record of relationships, as the highest-value item in the network — this is correct and important.
- The Tu'i Tonga Fefine's sacred status is correctly described. Her position in the system is not an addendum. It is central.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The structural premise: that the Pacific is a connected world, that the ocean is full, that the navigators read a structured landscape. This is not a metaphor. It is what is. The chapter understands this.
- The women's section does not patronize. It does not celebrate women's work as a footnote to men's adventure. It positions women's production as structurally necessary to the network. This is correct.
- The landing does not sentimentalize. It says the knowledge persists and says how and says why, and it does not mourn what was lost because it focuses on what continues.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter should mention that Tongans today still practice forms of traditional navigation, particularly among fishing communities. The knowledge did not survive only in academic documentation or in cultural memory. It survives in practice. The landing section's "for as long as the songs were sung" should be grounded in the present tense: the songs ARE being sung.
- My name in the landing section is fine. But the paragraph that introduces me should not call me a "scholar." I was a Tongan and Fijian who happened to work in a university. The distinction matters. The chapter's inside voice should position me as a voice from within the culture, not as an academic authority applied from outside.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (2 corrections above)
