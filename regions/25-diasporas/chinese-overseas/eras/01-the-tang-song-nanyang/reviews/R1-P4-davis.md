## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of The Tang-Song Nanyang (Opening)

**Overall:** The knowledge system is present and shown at method level. Lin reads water the way a navigator reads waves, the way a tracker reads ground. But the opening treats the Nanyang as a trade route rather than as a world of its own, and this concerns me.

**What works:**
- The water-color reading in the first paragraph is precisely what I look for. Brown water means silt, silt means river, river means monsoon, monsoon means timing. This is a chain of ecological knowledge that does the analytical work: the reader understands the navigation system through the sensation of reading water, not through a description of the navigation system. This is sensory-analytical fusion at its best.
- The cargo paragraphs treat goods as embodied knowledge. Camphor that sublimates, sandalwood that loses scent when wet, the weight of cargo as the balance of the ship. Each commodity carries its own physical logic, and Lin's knowledge of these logics is what makes him a trader. The goods are not inventory; they are the medium through which the knowledge system operates.
- "He carried the route the way he carried his dialect, in his mouth and in his hands." This is the correct formulation. The route is not external to Lin; it is part of his body. The dialect analogy is exact: both are inherited, both are corrected by experience, both are the medium of his relationship to the world.

**What doesn't:**
- The Nanyang's own ecological and cultural complexity is flattened. The strait between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra is one of the most biologically and culturally diverse zones on Earth. The mangrove forests, the reef systems, the river mouths where freshwater and saltwater mix, the tropical forest that produces the camphor and sandalwood, the agricultural systems of the coastal peoples. None of this is present except as a source of trade goods. The camphor comes "from the forests of Borneo," but the forest is not felt. The pepper comes "from the Sumatran highlands," but the highlands are not seen. The Nanyang is treated as a warehouse, not as an ecology.
- The local knowledge systems are absent. The Malay and Javanese peoples had their own navigational traditions, their own trade networks, their own systems of ecological management. The orang laut (sea people) who controlled the strait passages had a maritime knowledge at least as deep as Lin's. Their absence from the opening is a form of condescension, even if unintentional. If this is a story about commerce as civilization-building, whose civilization?

**The question I'd push on:**
The Nanyang is not a space between China and China's customers. It is a world. The chapter must give that world its depth, even though the anchor figure is Chinese. What does the strait look like to someone who lives there, not to someone passing through?
