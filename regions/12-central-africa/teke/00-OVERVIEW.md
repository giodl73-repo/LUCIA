# Bateke — "The Plateau Brokers"

The Bateke sit on the savanna plateau between the Congo River forest and the Kongo coast, and they have been sitting there, holding the middle position, for as long as there has been a trade between the interior and the coast. The Teke plateau — the Plateaux Teke — is a high grassland that gives way in every direction to forest or river valley or coastal lowland. The Teke people, organized in dispersed chieftaincies rather than a centralized kingdom, were the commercial intermediaries who set the terms for the movement of goods between the forest peoples to the east and north and the coastal states to the southwest. When the Kongo kingdom wanted ivory and copper from the interior, the Teke controlled the routes. When the forest peoples wanted iron and coastal goods, they went through the Teke. The broker is always in a position of power, and the Teke maintained that position across centuries of changing commercial landscapes.

The Teke concept of sovereignty was organized around the onkani — the spirit of the land, embodied in the Makoko, the Teke paramount chief, who was understood as the land's representative rather than its owner. The Makoko did not command armies; he held the land's spiritual authority and could deny or grant access to it. This gave him leverage over traders and political competitors without requiring him to maintain the kind of centralized military force that a conventional state would need. When Stanley arrived in 1880 on behalf of King Leopold II of Belgium and signed a treaty with the Makoko, he was treating the Makoko as a sovereign who could cede territory. The Makoko may have understood the encounter differently — as a commercial arrangement with a foreign trader, not a cession of sovereignty. The treaty became one of the legal instruments of the Congo Free State.

## What Defined Them

**The broker's power.** The Teke's fundamental strategic position — sitting between the forest interior and the coastal commercial powers — gave them a structural advantage that did not depend on military dominance or agricultural surplus. They set the prices, managed the relationships, and maintained the neutrality that made the trade routes function. Merchants from the forest would not trade directly with the coast if the Teke could prevent it, and the Teke could prevent it by controlling access to the plateau roads and the Pool. This gave the Makoko leverage over both the Kongo kingdom and the forest peoples without requiring him to defeat either in battle.

**The Makoko as land-spirit holder.** The Teke political theology organized authority around the relationship between the ruler and the land's spiritual essence — the onkani. The Makoko was not simply a political chief; he was the person in whom the land's power resided for the purpose of human governance. He could grant access to the land's resources and curse those who violated its terms. This spiritual authority was the source of the Makoko's leverage in commercial negotiations: traders needed not just the physical route but the ritual permission to use it safely. When Stanley presented the treaty as a straightforward land cession, he was ignoring (or exploiting) the different frameworks through which the Makoko understood what he was signing.

**The Pool as commercial center.** Malebo Pool — the wide, lake-like expansion of the Congo River where the rapids end and navigation begins — was the greatest commercial junction in Central Africa. Goods from a thousand kilometers up the river system arrived at the Pool by canoe and were transferred to land transport for the journey to the coast. The Teke markets at the Pool were where the prices were set for the interior trade. When the Belgian and French colonial forces built their stations at the Pool — Leopoldville and Brazzaville facing each other across the water — they were occupying the exact commercial junction that the Teke had controlled for centuries.

**The honest limits.** The Teke are less well documented than the Kongo or Luba because they produced no sustained court-based literary or artistic tradition of the kind that colonial-era scholars found easy to record. Much of what we know comes from the accounts of travelers, missionaries, and colonial administrators — perspectives that systematically underestimated the sophistication of the broker role. Teke internal political and religious traditions are documented primarily through twentieth-century ethnographic work. The pre-colonial history is reconstructed from these sources and from the Teke's own oral traditions, which are maintained but have received less sustained scholarly attention than some neighboring peoples.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Plateau Route:** A trader from the forest interior has arrived at the Teke market with a load of copper cross-shaped ingots. The Teke broker is examining them. He knows the current price on both ends of the route. He knows what he will take for his service. The deal is being made.

**2 — The Kongo Factor:** A Kongo provincial governor has sent agents to the Teke market. They want ivory. The Teke broker has ivory. He also has ten other buyers. The price reflects this.

**3 — The Makoko and Stanley:** A man named Henry Morton Stanley has arrived at the Makoko's court with gifts and a document. The Makoko receives him. The gifts are substantial. The document is being explained through an interpreter. What the Makoko understands and what Stanley writes in his dispatch may not be the same thing. Both documents will be used.

**4 — The Pool Divided:** Brazzaville has been built on the north bank. Leopoldville on the south. The Teke broker looks at the two colonial stations from the plateau. The trade still moves. The routes are still his — for now.
