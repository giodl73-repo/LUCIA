# Panel-1 Review — Wade Davis
## Hausa Era 3: The Manufacturing Peak (Rewrite v2)

**Voice:** Anthropological wonder — hidden condescension, worldview depth

The brass weight is this opening's most important object. Ibrahim holds it and says his father told him it was "the beginning of trust." A brass weight in a Hausa merchant's hand is not simply a measuring instrument. It is a claim about what commerce is: the weight of things, made consistent, held in common, so that distance does not break the agreement. The scholars in their counting houses near the eastern gate issue legal opinions that do the same thing in text. The guild dyers in Kano's Marina quarter maintain their recipes for the same reason. Every institution in this opening is a technology of trust — a way of extending the weight of things across distance and time.

This is the chapter's real engine, underneath the economic engine: trust as the manufacturing city's deepest product. The cloth is what crosses the Sahara. Trust is what makes the cloth cross the Sahara. And trust is maintained by an interlocking set of institutions: guilds, scholars, sarkis, qadis — and the Bori, which does something for the city's social fabric that none of the above can do.

**On the Bori as knowledge system:** Ibrahim filing the drums under "not his business" is correct for an opening. But the full chapter must treat the Bori with the same epistemic depth as the Maliki legal tradition. The iskoki are not beliefs; they are entities. The possession is not performance; it is arrival. The senior Bori women who know which drums call which spirits are technicians in the same sense that the guild dyers are technicians: they have embodied expertise, years of training, a discipline transmitted through practice. The chapter must show this at the level of method — not describe what the Bori does, but show it doing what it does, from inside the practitioner's understanding.

**On the craft guilds' spiritual dimension:** The smiths, the dyers, the weavers had their own relationships to the spirit world. This is documented. The chapter should show at least one instance of this integration: a dyer or a smith making an offering, not as superstition, not as decoration, but as professional practice — the thing you do before beginning important work, the way that Ibrahim's father gave him the brass weight as a principle, not just a tool.

**The question I would push on:** At what point does the chapter show that the Hausa city's knowledge systems — guild craft, Maliki jurisprudence, Bori practice — are all doing the same fundamental work of managing uncertainty? The three systems are not in conflict. They are parallel answers to the question: how do you know what to trust?
