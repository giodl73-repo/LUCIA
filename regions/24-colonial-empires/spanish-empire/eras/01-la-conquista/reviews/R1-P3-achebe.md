## On Ownership

This opening does something I did not expect: it writes the Spanish Empire from inside the Spanish Empire's own moral framework, and it does so without flinching, without stepping outside to reassure the reader that of course we know better now.

The opening does not begin with the victims. It begins with the perpetrators — the notary, the captain, the document — and it presents them as they understood themselves: as agents of a legal and theological process. Palacios Rubios believed what he wrote. This sentence is the most important sentence in the opening. It is the sentence that makes everything else possible, because if he was a hypocrite the story is simple, and simple stories about colonialism are almost always someone else's verdict being applied to a culture they have already decided to condemn.

The complexity here is that Palacios Rubios was not a hypocrite. He was sincere. And Las Casas was sincere. And Sepúlveda was sincere. They all operated within the same framework — Catholic moral theology — and arrived at different conclusions, and argued those conclusions before the King of Spain, and the system both produced the atrocity and generated the argument against it simultaneously. This is the truth of the Spanish conquest that is most often missed: it was not a conspiracy of cynics. It was the work of sincere believers in a framework that contained, within itself, the seeds of its own moral crisis.

## What Passes the Test

**The empty village is the right indigenous response.** The people have gone into the forest when the ships appeared. They have done this because they know what the ships bring. This is not rendered with pity or explanation. It is simply true. The people understood the situation correctly and responded rationally. The opening does not need to explain this — it trusts the reader to understand that the empty village is the indigenous people's verdict on the Spanish arrival.

**Las Casas's laughter.** "He could not help it, he wrote later. He laughed because it was so impossible to take seriously, and then he stopped laughing, because it was very serious indeed." This is perfect. Las Casas is inside the framework — he is a Dominican priest, not a modern human rights lawyer — and his laughter is the inside-framework response, not an outside-framework verdict. He does not laugh because the Requerimiento is morally wrong (though he believes it is). He laughs because it is impossible to take seriously as a legal document, as a performance. And then he stops laughing because the performance is very serious indeed, because people are dying because of it.

**No pity for the conquered.** The opening does not offer the indigenous people pity. It offers them presence — the empty village, the people in the forest, the rationality of having gone there. This is the right treatment. Pity is a form of distance. The empty village is a form of presence.

## What to Watch

**Guaman Poma de Ayala.** Felipe Guaman Poma de Ayala wrote an illustrated chronicle of the conquest from inside the indigenous-Spanish encounter — in Spanish, for the King of Spain, by a man who was both a product of the colonial world and a witness to its violence. He is in the sources list. The chapter should find a way to use him that does not make him simply a victim's voice, because he was not simply a victim. He was a man who had learned the colonizer's language and was using it to argue, from inside the colonizer's own framework, for the rights of his people. He is the Las Casas parallel from the other side of the encounter — and the chapter that holds both figures simultaneously will be doing something important.

**The word "innocents."** If the chapter uses it, it needs to come from Las Casas's own vocabulary — he used *inocentes* throughout the *Brevísima relación* — not from the chapter's own moral position. Las Casas calling them innocent is a theological claim, not a modern human rights claim, and the distinction matters.

## The Question I'd Push On

**Does the chapter stay inside the Spanish theological framework even when describing the violence?** The opening succeeds because it inhabits the framework without endorsing it. The chapter will face its hardest test in the sections on the destruction of Mexico and Peru — specific, documented, horrific violence. The temptation will be to step outside the framework to register moral response. Las Casas did not step outside the framework. He used the framework's own terms to indict what was being done. The chapter must find that register: not modern horror at historical atrocity, but the horror of a believer watching his own civilization violate its own stated values.
