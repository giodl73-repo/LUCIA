# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Round 1 — Opening Review

**Chapter:** Argentina Era 3 — La Organización Nacional y la Generación del 80
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The voice is right. That is not a small thing. The opening inhabits the conventillo without explaining it to a reader who might not know what a conventillo is. It assumes the reader can stand in that courtyard without a guide. The smells and sounds and social frictions arrive without apology. The text does not say "to understand why this matters, consider..." It shows a pump being fought over and trusts the reader to understand why that matters.

The Afro-Argentine women in the opening sentence — I want to say something about them carefully. They appear: "three Afro-Argentine women who have lived in the Barrio Sur since before any of the newcomers arrived and will be here when most of them have moved on." This is the most honest sentence in the opening. It places the African-descended community as the indigenous inhabitants of this neighborhood — prior to the Italian, prior to the Galician, prior to the Pole. Their presence names the deeper substrate: the African root of the tango's rhythm, the fact that Buenos Aires had a black population that the immigration narrative tends to erase. But the sentence appears and then the text moves on, and the African women become a parenthesis rather than a thread.

This is a choice that the chapter will have to reckon with. Argentina's official national story — the one the Generación del 80 built and that the country still half-believes — is European, white, and immigrant. The Afro-Argentine community that predated the European immigration, that contributed the African rhythmic base of what became the tango, was systematically marginalized by that story. The opening acknowledges them and then drops them, which is exactly what Argentina did. If this is intentional — if the opening is enacting the erasure — then the chapter needs to name this as mechanism, not accident. If it is not intentional, the chapter should either follow the thread or remove it from the opening.

I am not asking the chapter to make this its primary story. But having introduced these women, the text owes them a reckoning. The voice that inhabits the conventillo cannot then perform the conventillo's own erasure without noticing what it is doing.

The final paragraph concerns me slightly: "This is the story of La Organización Nacional." This is the author stepping outside to name the story. The tango-as-compression image is excellent. But the naming of "this is the story" returns the narration to an outside position for one moment, and in that moment the voice is no longer the conventillo — it is a writer looking at the conventillo. Worth reconsidering at the FIXES stage.

**The question I'd push on:** The Afro-Argentine women. What does the chapter do with them? Their presence in the opening is not neutral. The author should decide.
