# Očhéthi Šakówiŋ — "The Seven Council Fires"

They called the full political community the Očhéthi Šakówiŋ — the Seven Council Fires, the seven divisions of what English speakers have called the Sioux nation. The Lakota are the westernmost of the three great divisions (Lakota, Dakota, Nakota) of the Očhéthi Šakówiŋ, and in the nineteenth century it was the Lakota who became the preeminent power of the northern Great Plains — the people of the Black Hills, the bison, and the Sun Dance, the people whose resistance to American expansion produced Red Cloud, Crazy Horse, and Sitting Bull, and whose defeat at Wounded Knee in 1890 marked one of the most consequential moments in the history of American settlement of the continent.

Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ — "all my relations" — is the Lakota phrase that opens every ceremony and underlies every significant act. It is a statement of cosmological fact: the person speaking it acknowledges their kinship with all living beings, with the earth, the sky, the water, the stones. This is not poetic hyperbole. It is the operative principle of a worldview in which the human community is not set apart from or above the natural world but is embedded within it as one of the many kinds of persons — human persons, animal persons, spirit persons, place persons — whose relationships with one another constitute the world. To live well was to live in right relationship with all these relations. The ceremonies, the governance, the warfare, the healing — all of these were the practices of maintaining that relationship.

Key places:
- **Paha Sapa (Black Hills)** — "hills that are black" — the sacred center of the Lakota world; the place where Wakan Tanka's presence was most concentrated; the site of the 1868 Fort Laramie Treaty guarantee and the 1874 gold rush that violated it
- **Greasy Grass (Little Bighorn)** — the site of the 1876 battle where Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho forces defeated Custer's Seventh Cavalry; the high point of Lakota military resistance
- **Wounded Knee** — the site of the 1890 massacre; 250-300 Lakota killed by the Seventh Cavalry; the end of armed resistance on the northern Plains
- **Wind Cave** — in the Black Hills; in Lakota tradition, the site from which the first Lakota people emerged into this world

## What Defined Them

**Wakan Tanka — the Great Mystery.** The Lakota understanding of the sacred was organized around Wakan Tanka — the "Great Mystery" or "Great Spirit," a term that encompasses all sacred power, all the forces that human beings cannot fully understand. Wakan Tanka was not a personal deity in the Abrahamic sense; it was the totality of the sacred, present in all things and most concentrated in certain places (the Black Hills, certain mountains, certain water sources) and certain beings (the white buffalo, the eagle, the thunder beings). The human task was not to worship Wakan Tanka from a distance but to be in right relationship with it — which required the ceremonies, the personal vision quests, the Sun Dance, and the daily practices of Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ that maintained the person's alignment with the sacred order.

**The Sun Dance as the world's renewal.** The Sun Dance — wiwáŋyaŋk wačhípi, "dance looking at the sun" — was the Lakota's most important communal ceremony: a multi-day gathering at which men who had pledged to dance were pierced through the chest or back with skewers attached to the central pole by thongs, and danced pulling against the thongs until the skewers tore free. This was not torture imposed from outside; it was a voluntary offering of the body's pain to Wakan Tanka on behalf of the community's needs — prayers for the health of the people, for the abundance of the bison, for the protection of the children. The Sun Dance gathered the scattered bands into a single community for the summer, renewed the political and social bonds between them, and enacted the Lakota's relationship with the sacred order in the most concrete possible way: the body's suffering in service of the community's life.

**The bison as the world's gift.** The bison was not simply a food source. It was the manifestation of Wakan Tanka's generosity — the great herds that covered the Plains were understood as the sacred's gift to the people, given on the condition that the people honored the bison's sacrifice with proper ceremony and used everything the bison gave. The hunters addressed the bison they killed with prayers of gratitude; the women who processed the carcasses used every part — the hide, the organs, the bones, the fat — with the precision required by the obligation of reciprocity. A people who wasted the bison's gift would find the herds withdrawing. This was not superstition; it was a governance framework for managing a resource that could be depleted by profligacy.

**The vision quest and personal relationship with the sacred.** Every Lakota man was expected to seek a vision — to go alone into the landscape for days, without food or water, and wait for the sacred to speak. The vision that came would give the person a specific relationship with a specific spirit helper, a specific sacred identity, and specific obligations. The vision quest was the mechanism through which the individual's place in the cosmic order was revealed — not imposed by community or tradition but disclosed directly by the sacred to the individual willing to suffer the exposure of the quest. This personal relationship with the sacred was the foundation of Lakota spiritual life and explained why Lakota authority was never simply inherited: the leader who had sought his vision and received his power from the sacred had an authority that the leader who had not could not claim.

**The Great Sioux Nation and the treaty that mattered.** The Fort Laramie Treaty of 1868 guaranteed the Lakota the Black Hills "for as long as the grass shall grow." The treaty was violated when gold was discovered in the Black Hills in 1874 and the American government failed to prevent the rush of miners that followed — and then refused to purchase the hills at a price the Lakota would accept, and then demanded that the Lakota accept whatever was offered. The Great Sioux War that followed was not simply a military conflict. It was a legal and moral argument that the Lakota have never stopped making: the treaty is still in force, the Black Hills are still Lakota land, and the Supreme Court's 1980 ruling that the United States owes the Lakota just compensation has been refused by the Lakota because accepting money would mean accepting the legitimacy of the land's taking.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Eastern Woodlands:** A Lakota family in Minnesota is harvesting wild rice from a canoe, pulling the stalks over the boat's edge and knocking the grain free. They have heard stories of the open grassland to the west and the animals there that are bigger than anything in the forest. They are not yet the Plains people. The transformation is two generations away.

**2 — The Horse and the Plains:** The first horses the Lakota acquire change everything within a decade. The range over which the bands can move, the size of the bison they can hunt, the speed at which they can respond to threats — all of these are transformed. The bands are moving west, following the bison, finding the land that will become Paha Sapa. The world is opening.

**3 — The Height of the Horse Nation:** At the Sun Dance gathering, perhaps five thousand Lakota have converged on the summer camp from their winter ranges. The Sun Dance pole is being raised. The men who have pledged are preparing. The community that is scattered across six hundred thousand square kilometers for most of the year is, for this moment, one body. The Sun Dance holds the nation together.

**4 — The Wars for the Plains:** On June 25, 1876, at the Greasy Grass, Crazy Horse leads his warriors into the valley where Custer's force has split. The battle lasts less than an hour. The Seventh Cavalry is destroyed. Sitting Bull, who had refused to go to the reservation, has led his vision-ceremony the night before — a vision of soldiers falling into the Lakota camp. He saw what was coming. The victory is real. The war is not won.

**5 — The Long Survival:** The Black Hills claim is still open. The Supreme Court ruled in 1980 that the taking was illegal. The Lakota refused the money. The hills are still the hills. The grass is still growing.
