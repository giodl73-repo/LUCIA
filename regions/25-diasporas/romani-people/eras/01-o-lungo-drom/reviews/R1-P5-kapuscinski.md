## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Review of Romani Era 1 Opening

**Overall:** I cannot feel the road. This is the opening's one serious weakness, and it is mine to name. The intellectual architecture is extraordinary — language as witness, vocabulary as map, borrowings as footprints. But the opening puts me in a library, not on a road. I should be on the road. These people walked from India to Greece. I should feel the dust, the heat, the weight of what they carried, the sound of the wagon wheels, the cold of the Armenian passes.

**What works:**
- "The dust of those countries had settled into the hems of their clothing" — this is the one moment where I feel a body on the road. It is good. There need to be five more like it.
- The Romani words themselves have a sensory quality when listed: dej, dad, phen, grast, pani, yag. The sounds are specific. They sit differently in the mouth than English words. This is almost sensory — the reader's mouth shapes different vowels. But it is not quite physical environment.
- The structural move of layering loanwords like geological strata is almost tactile. Persian, Armenian, Greek: each layer is a place. I can almost feel the journey's duration through the accumulation. But "almost" is the problem.

**What doesn't:**
- Northwestern India in the eleventh century was a specific place with specific heat, specific dust, specific light. The Punjab plains in autumn have a color. The trade routes through Persia have a temperature. The Armenian highlands in winter would have killed people who were not prepared. None of this is present. The opening tells me they walked through Persia. It does not put me in Persia.
- There are no bodies in this opening. No hands. No feet on a road. No child on a hip. No smith with soot on his arms. No musician carrying an instrument. The Roma are a people of extraordinary physical presence — their crafts, their music, their way of making camp, their relationship with horses. The opening exists entirely in the world of words and ideas. For a people who live in their hands and their voices, this is a gap.
- The weather is absent. The landscape is absent. The road is the central metaphor, but it has no stones, no mud, no gradient, no shade or lack of shade. A road you cannot feel underfoot is an abstraction, not a road.

**The question I'd push on:**
Put me on the road. One paragraph. The heat of the Punjab, the sound of the wagons, the smell of the forge, the feeling of an Armenian mountain pass in December with children in the cart. The linguistics are doing brilliant intellectual work. Now the body needs to arrive. Can the chapter do both — language AND landscape — simultaneously? If the words are footprints, let me feel the ground they were pressed into.
