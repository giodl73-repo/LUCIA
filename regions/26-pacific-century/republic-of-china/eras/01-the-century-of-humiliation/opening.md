# The Opium Wars: The World Discovers It Has Different Rules

In the summer of 1839, Commissioner Lin Zexu stood on the shore near Canton and watched the foreign poison burn. 

The opium lay in heaps — 1,200 tons of it, seized from the warehouses where British merchants had been selling it to Chinese addicts for decades. Lin had ordered it poured into channels that led to the sea, mixed with saltwater and lime, and set alight. The smoke rose in pillars that could be seen from miles away. Scholars who were there wrote that it looked like the earth itself had caught fire.

Lin was sixty-three years old. He had spent a lifetime in the service of the throne, rising through the examinations the way a scholar was meant to — by understanding the Classics, by demonstrating virtue, by showing that he knew how a proper official should conduct himself. He had been a provincial governor, a military strategist, a man trusted to fix what the provinces could not fix on their own. When the Qing court looked at the problem of the foreign merchants dumping opium into China and making Chinese people addicts, they sent Lin to Canton to solve it.

His solution was elegant and complete. He did what any careful administrator would do: he studied the problem. The merchants were organized. He organized against them. They had protocols; he created protocols to match. They brought their goods in junks and brigs; he surveyed the harbors and recorded what came and went. He wrote memoranda. He issued proclamations. He explained the consequences of trading in the poison — death for the smugglers, punishment for those who used it. He even negotiated with the foreign merchants directly, meeting with them in the trading houses, discussing the rules of commerce as they understood it: you may bring tea, silk, porcelain. You may not bring opium. This is the law of the Middle Kingdom.

The British merchants ignored him.

So Lin asked the court for permission to seize and destroy what had been brought into China illegally. The court granted it. And in the summer of 1839, he destroyed it — all of it, systematically, methodically, the way one removes a disease from the body.

He did not know he had just started a war.

The British government, when word reached London, did not see an administrator enforcing the law of his own country. They saw an act of piracy against their merchants. They saw merchants denied access to a market. They saw a nation that would not obey the rules that Europe had decided should govern trade in Asia. The East India Company had invested heavily in the opium trade. The British treasury had come to depend on the revenues that opium brought from China. The response from London was immediate and surgical: send the navy.

The ships that arrived in Chinese waters in 1840 were not trading vessels. They were warships — steam-powered, iron-hulled, armed with cannon that could fire farther and more accurately than anything the Qing navy possessed. The Chinese junks, built for river navigation, caught fire when the cannon rounds hit them. The Chinese soldiers, armed with bows and early muskets, could not reach the foreign warships before their crews were dead.

The war lasted two years. It was never really a war, in the sense that requires two sides to be fighting. It was more like a demonstration of a principle: we have the power to enforce what we have decided, and you do not. The Treaty of Nanjing, signed in 1842, was called a treaty, but from the Chinese perspective it was a wound opening: Hong Kong ceded to Britain forever. Five ports opened to foreign traders — Canton, Amoy, Foochow, Ningpo, Shanghai — where foreigners could live under their own laws, answerable not to Chinese courts but to British judges. Most-favored-nation status granted, meaning any rights given to any power would automatically extend to Britain. Indemnities of 21 million silver dollars paid to the British government for the cost of the war — the war the British had started.

Lin was not blamed for the defeat. He had fought the war as best he could with the tools he had. But he was removed from his position and exiled to a minor province, where he spent the rest of his life in administrative work that mattered to no one. The court tried to forget the whole episode.

But something had changed in the calculus of the world. The dynasty that had regarded itself as the center of everything — the civilizing force around which all other peoples organized their tribute and their behavior — had discovered that it could not enforce its own laws within its own borders. A foreign power had simply decided that the rules the Qing had made no longer applied, and had brought enough cannon to make the decision stick.

The century of humiliation had begun.
