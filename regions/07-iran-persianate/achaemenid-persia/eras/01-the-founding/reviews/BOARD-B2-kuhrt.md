# Board Review — B-2
**Reviewer:** Amélie Kuhrt (Ancient Near Eastern historian; *The Persian Empire: A Corpus of Sources*, 2007)
**Chapter:** Achaemenid Persia, Era 1 — The Founding
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Domain Assessment

My work has been primarily concerned with recovering the Achaemenid empire from the distortion of Greek sources and making available, in translation, the Near Eastern textual record that allows us to read the empire from inside its own administrative and sacred traditions. This chapter has done exactly what I would want a narrative treatment to do: it has used the Babylonian records as its primary framework for understanding the Cyrus conquest, and it has treated Herodotus as a problematic secondary source rather than as the authoritative narrative voice.

## Factual Review

**The Nabonidus Chronicles:** Accurately characterized. The chronicles' hostile tone toward Nabonidus is real and reflects the Babylonian priestly establishment's genuine institutional grievances. The chapter correctly notes that the Persian conquest was, in significant part, a political transition within Babylonian institutional politics.

**The Cyrus Cylinder:** The translation used is accurate. The cylinder's claim that Marduk chose Cyrus has a long interpretive history; the chapter correctly presents this as both politically motivated and, within the world's own logic, theologically meaningful. The chapter does not overclaim by calling this "propaganda" (a modern category that distorts the ancient framework) or underclaim by calling it sincere religious feeling disconnected from political calculation.

**Cult-statue returns:** The chapter mentions the return of statues to Sippar and the Shamash temple. This is correct; the Sippar case is documented in the cylinder's text. The "thirty-six thousand" Judeans is the figure from Ezra; as Briant's review notes, the exact numbers vary across sources. The chapter should use approximate phrasing.

**Cambyses in Egypt:** The Saqqara stelae evidence for the Apis bull's natural death and proper burial under Cambyses is the current standard. My corpus includes the relevant texts. The chapter's treatment is accurate except for not explicitly stating that the Egyptian evidence contradicts not only the stabbing but also the entire sacrilegious-madman framework of Herodotus. The chapter implies this but should state it more directly.

**The Akitu sequence:** The description is accurate in structure. I note one potential issue: the chapter says "On the second day, the high priest of Esagila rose before dawn." The Akitu liturgical texts we have are fragmentary and partially reconstructed; the exact day-by-day sequence is known in broad outline but not in complete detail for every day. The chapter should either add a brief note ("in the sequence we can reconstruct from the surviving liturgical texts") or make the description slightly less day-specific.

**Persepolis Fortification Tablets:** Accurately characterized. The tablets were found in 1933–34 excavations; the chapter says "discovered beneath Persepolis in the 1930s" which is accurate. The tablets are in Elamite cuneiform, as stated.

## Cultural Representation

The chapter makes a claim I find particularly well-judged: that the Achaemenid system required not just tolerance of other sacred orders but active participation in them. This is stronger than the "religious tolerance" framework that has dominated popular treatments of Cyrus. The difference matters: tolerance is passive (not interfering); the Achaemenid approach was active (the king must show up, must perform, must be endorsed by the local sacred authority). The chapter captures this distinction well.

The Zoroastrian background of the Persian kings is appropriately handled: present, referenced to Ahura Mazda, but not overdetermined in a way that makes the Achaemenid system read as a Zoroastrian missionary project. The tension between Zoroastrian rejection of the *daiva* and the accommodation of other peoples' sacred orders is noted and deferred to Era 2, which is correct.

## Corrections Required

**P1 — Factual:** Apply Briant's correction on the Apis bull natural death. State explicitly that the Egyptian records indicate natural causes, not violence.

**P2 — Precision:** "The Akitu sequence was prescribed" description should include acknowledgment of partial record: "in the sequence attested in the surviving liturgical texts."

**P2 — Numbers:** "Thirty-six thousand people of the Judean exile" should be softened to "tens of thousands" or "the exiles, whose numbers the sources give variously."

## Endorsement

With corrections applied, the chapter accurately represents the founding era and handles the complex source situation with appropriate scholarly honesty. The balance between the Persian self-presentation and the Babylonian record is well-maintained. Endorsed.
