# BOARD — B-norse-1 — Neil Price — Board Review of Vikingalond

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Viking-age archaeology, social structure, belief systems, mortuary practices. Author of the most current comprehensive treatment of Viking-age culture.

---

## Historical Accuracy

**Correct:**
- The ship-building description is accurate: riven strakes, spruce-root lashing for flexibility, clinker construction, bog-ore iron. Consistent with the Skuldelev, Oseberg, and Gokstad ship evidence.
- The social categories (bonde, jarl, thrall, karl) are used correctly and in their proper relationship.
- Kaupang as a Vestfold-region trading site is archaeologically correct (Skre excavations, 1998-2003).
- The Oseberg burial description (woman, ship, grave goods) is accurate.
- The thing assembly description is broadly correct for the period.
- The blot sacrifice passage is consistent with what we can reconstruct from later textual evidence and archaeological traces.
- The Lindisfarne date (793) and the subsequent raid chronology (Jarrow 794, Iona 795) are correct per the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle and insular annals.
- The Dorestad raids (834-837) are correct per the Frankish Royal Annals.
- The Dublin longphort (841) is correct.

**Needs attention:**
- The "sixty feet" keel length is at the upper end for early Viking-age vessels. The Oseberg ship (c. 820) had a keel of about 20 meters (65 feet), which fits, but this was an exceptionally large vessel. Typical raiding vessels of the 790s may have been somewhat smaller. Suggest: "sixty feet" could be kept as within the possible range, but the notes should acknowledge this is a large vessel.
- The Gokstad burial is dated c. 900, slightly later than the chapter's 793-850 window. The chapter uses it as illustration, not as a claim about the era, so this is acceptable but worth noting.

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter renders Norse culture from inside with impressive consistency. The gods are real forces, not "beliefs." The thing assembly is governance, not folklore. The ship is life and death, not illustration. The thrall passage is handled with exactly the right register: factual, inside, no modern moral commentary.

The composite bonde is a legitimate choice given the absence of named individuals from the Lindisfarne expedition. The figure is consistent with what we know of Vestfold landholders from the archaeological record.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The core argument that early Viking-age raiders were farmers, not professional warriors, reflects the scholarly consensus.
- The integration of western raiding and eastern trading as parts of a single network is correct and important.
- The self-reinforcing logic of raiding (success -> followers -> organization -> more raids) is a valid causal mechanism.
- The treatment of the thrall trade as an integral part of the Norse economy, not a moral aside, is historically appropriate.
- The navigation section treats Norse wayfinding as genuine body-knowledge, which reflects the current understanding of Norse navigational practice.

## What Needs Correction

1. **Minor:** The chapter states the Norse "had no compass, no charts, no instruments." This is mostly correct for the early Viking Age, but the possible existence of the sunstone (solarsteinn, a calcite crystal for locating the sun in overcast conditions) is debated. Scholars disagree on whether it was used in practice. A passing acknowledgment would be appropriate but not essential.

2. **Minor:** The phrase "the silver mines of the Abbasid caliphate" is slightly misleading. The silver was primarily mined in Central Asia (especially the Samanid domains, Panjshir valley in Afghanistan, and mines in Transoxiana). The caliphate controlled the minting, but the mines were at the empire's eastern periphery. Suggest: "the silver of the Abbasid lands" or "the Abbasid silver economy."

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (2 minor corrections suggested)
