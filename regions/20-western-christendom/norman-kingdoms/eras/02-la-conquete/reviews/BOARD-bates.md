# Board Review — B-1 (David Bates)
## La Conquête — Norman Kingdoms, Era 2

**Reviewer:** B-1 David Bates (Norman governance and Domesday studies)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

## Overall Assessment

This is a historically serious chapter that handles the primary sources with care and structures the narrative around a genuine historical argument rather than a chronological survey. The castle-as-governance-engine framework is not merely metaphor; it reflects how Norman scholars understand the period. My corrections are specific and limited.

## Factual Accuracy

**Correction 1 — Required**
Location: Body at Caen section
Text: "The September heat had done what September heat does."

The chapter was revised from "August" to "September" — William died 9 September 1087. However, the burial at Caen and the coffin incident would have occurred in the days following, still in September. The text is now correct. No change needed. (Previous draft had "August heat" — this was already fixed.)

**Correction 2 — Required**
Location: Harrying section
Text: "Orderic put the dead at 100,000."

Orderic does not give a specific figure of 100,000. He describes widespread death and devastation but his account is qualitative rather than quantitative. The figure 100,000 has appeared in secondary literature as an estimate derived from reading Orderic, but it should not be attributed to Orderic directly.

Fix: "Modern estimates, drawn from reading Orderic's account alongside the Domesday waste entries, have put the dead in the tens of thousands. Orderic himself does not count. He says only that the roads were strewn with the starved, that the survivors could not bury the dead."

This correction improves accuracy and, incidentally, strengthens the passage: Orderic's refusal to count is more devastating than any number.

**Correction 3 — Minor**
Location: Castle Grammar section
Text: "The White Tower at London was under construction within the year, its Caen stone arriving by ship up the Thames because the local English limestone was not, in Norman estimation, adequate."

The Tower of London's stone donjon was not begun until approximately 1078, not "within the year" of 1066. The initial fortification (1066-67) was probably a simpler timber work on the eastern edge of the old Roman city wall. The permanent stone tower came later.

Fix: Change to "The permanent White Tower at London, its Caen stone arriving by ship up the Thames, would take more than a decade to complete. The first fortification went up within the year: timber on earthwork, fast and functional, the stone to follow when the kingdom was secure enough to build for centuries."

This is more accurate and actually strengthens the passage: the evolution from timber to stone is itself part of the Norman governance story.

**Note on Æthelric of Writtle**
The composite figure of Æthelric is a legitimate technique for this period, correctly disclosed. The figures.md treatment of him is exemplary. No factual concern; the circumstances described are consistent with what the Domesday Essex folios record about English smallholders.

## Cultural Representation

The treatment of the English experience — particularly through the Domesday testimony scene and the Bayeux Tapestry embroiderers — is handled well. The Tapestry passage is evocative and historically defensible; the Canterbury embroidery tradition is established, and the figure of Ælfgyva is indeed unexplained in the Tapestry, as the chapter notes.

The Orderic Vitalis presentation is accurate. His doubled identity (Anglo-Norman parentage, English birth, Norman monastery) is correctly described. My main scholarly contribution here: Orderic was sent to Saint-Évroul at approximately age 10, around 1085, by his father Odelerius of Orléans, who was a Norman-born priest in Shropshire. The chapter says the father was "Norman"; Orderic himself gives his father's origin as Orléans. This is not a significant error for the chapter's purposes, but noting it for accuracy.

## Source Use

The use of William of Poitiers, Orderic Vitalis, and the Domesday Book is sound. The chapter correctly characterizes the deathbed speech as reconstruction rather than transcript. The reference to Douglas's biography is appropriate for secondary support.

One note on the deathbed speech: the specific quote "massacred by slow famine" does appear in Orderic's account (Book VII), though it is part of a longer speech. The attribution is correct.

## Endorsement

Subject to the above corrections: the chapter accurately represents the Norman Conquest of England, handles the primary sources appropriately, and achieves a level of cultural depth that most narrative histories of this period do not reach. The Domesday survey as knowledge-system is a genuine contribution to how a general reader can understand what the survey was actually doing. The chapter is endorsed.
