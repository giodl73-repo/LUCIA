## The Body in the Room

I have covered wars, revolutions, the fall of emperors, and the end of colonial systems. The one thing I have learned is that history happens to bodies in rooms. The body sweats or shivers. The room smells of candle fat or horse or ink or fear. If I cannot smell the room, I am not there. And if I am not there, the reader is not there.

This opening is close. It is very close to putting me in the room. But it is not quite there.

## What Works

The chancery detail is correct. The clerk who copies a hundred writs — I see him. I see the monotony of his work and the slight pause at the phrase that is slightly different. This is how large events work: they pass through the hands of people doing small work, and the people doing small work notice something without knowing what they have noticed. The opening earns this moment.

"At the far end of the hall where the acoustics were worse and the ceremony less gilded." — I am suddenly in that hall. I can hear the difference in the air — the low ceiling, the crowd of wool merchants who don't carry swords, the murmur that doesn't carry to the king's dais. One sentence and I am in the room. This is the opening's best physical moment.

## What Is Missing

The hall. I want the hall. November 1295 — which means cold. Westminster Hall, if it is Westminster Hall, is large and drafty. The knights have ridden from their counties; some of them are still wearing road dust. The bishops are warm in their vestments. The burgesses are uncomfortable in a space designed for a ceremony they don't belong to.

Give me: the smell of the hall. The light — candles, torches, or grey November through high windows? The sound before the proceedings begin — what does a room of magnates, clergy, and commoners sound like when they are waiting? This is not decorative. This is where the social structure of the institution lives, in the physical experience of being present in it.

The statutes paragraph has no body at all. "The Statute of Westminster in 1275, the Statute of Mortmain in 1279, the Statute of Winchester in 1285" — these are names on a page. What did the Statute of Winchester require in practice? Someone had to ride out and implement it. Give me one moment of one statute in action — a judge arriving at a county, sitting at a table, opening a case — and the list becomes history rather than reference.

## De Montfort's Body

The opening mentions that de Montfort's body was quartered and his head sent to enemies. This is correct. But it is stated in the abstract. De Montfort at Evesham — the battle in August 1265, the reversal of fortune, the young Edward who had escaped capture and turned the tide — this is a story with bodies in it. The opening does not need to tell it in full. But a single physical detail — the battle's weather, the meadow, the weight of what was lost — would make the "precedent" that follows carry more weight. Right now the precedent floats. It needs to be anchored in something that smelled of blood and rain.

## The Question I'd Push On

When the knights rode home from the Parliament of 1295, what did they carry? Not metaphorically — physically. Did they carry the king's writs? The county's petitions, denied or granted? A record of what had been agreed? What was the material form in which Parliament's decisions traveled from Westminster to the shires? This is not a trivial question for a chapter about how a parliamentary precedent propagates. The answer is: a piece of parchment, sealed, carried by a messenger. The chapter should show us that parchment. It is the institution made physical.

---

**Overall:** Ninety percent of the way there. The institutional argument is elegant. The sensory world needs ten percent more body — November cold, the hall's acoustics (mentioned but not felt), de Montfort with blood on the ground, and the parchment that rides home after the Parliament is over.
