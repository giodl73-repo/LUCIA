## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of The Fragmentation (Opening)

**Overall:** This opening does something difficult: it makes administration dramatic. The grain-counting is the story, and the story works because the stakes are real and specific.

**What works:**
- The hook is immediate and concrete. "Khety son of Tefibi counted the grain." One sentence, and we know who, what, and why it matters. The information unfolds from that action rather than preceding it.
- The escalation from grain to geopolitics is handled through accumulation, not through authorial explanation. We learn the political situation because Khety is calculating within it, not because the author pauses to brief us.
- The biographical inscription passage ("I fed my people. I held my nome.") is the strongest moment. It gives us the nomarch's voice directly, and the repetition of "I" is the entire political revolution in miniature: these men are speaking for themselves.
- The final line earns its weight. It closes the opening without closing the question.

**What doesn't:**
- The middle section (paragraph 3, the genealogy of record-keeping back to the Memphis days) slows the forward momentum. The connection between Khety's record-keeping and the Old Kingdom's is important, but it arrives as backstory rather than as pressure. Could it emerge through a specific detail rather than through a summary passage?
- The flood passage (paragraph 5) is accurate but reads slightly more like information delivery than scene. "Fourteen cubits, or twelve" is good specificity, but the paragraph shifts from Khety's perspective to a more generalized narrator. Can the flood data come through Khety's own experience or observation?

**The question I'd push on:**
Where is the sound? Where is the heat? Khety is in a granary in Upper Egypt. I want to feel the dry air, smell the stored emmer, hear whatever is outside those walls. The opening is strong on political and administrative texture but thin on the physical world Khety inhabits. One good sensory paragraph would anchor the abstraction.
