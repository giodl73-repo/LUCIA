# R1-P4 — Wade Davis — Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Wolof / Era 2: The First Atlantic Contact
**Round:** 1 (Opening review)

## What Works

The opening succeeds at the fundamental level: it presents the Wolof as a people with a functioning political system, commercial expertise, and diplomatic sophistication, and it does so without ever making that sophistication the point. The Wolof do not need to be remarkable. They are simply doing what a trading polity does when a new partner arrives. The absence of surprise is itself the achievement.

The treatment of the horse trade as an extension of existing networks is exactly right. The Wolof were already acquiring horses through the Saharan trade. The Portuguese offered a new supply route, not a new concept. This framing denies the narrative of European disruption-as-awakening that infects so much West African historiography.

The gewel as institutional memory, mentioned in passing, correctly positions the oral tradition as a knowledge system with political authority, not as folklore or cultural color.

## What Needs Work

**The Wolof knowledge system is gestured at but never entered.** The opening tells me the Wolof had "been managing the southern terminus of a trade network for generations" and that they knew "exactly what a horse was worth." But HOW did they know? What was the Wolof system for assessing value? How did a buur calculate the worth of a horse against what he gave in exchange? The Wolof commercial tradition was a genuine knowledge system, and the opening should give the reader at least a glimpse of its logic. Not a textbook explanation, but a moment of the calculation being made: the nobleman looking at the horse's teeth, its legs, its breed, and weighing that against the cost. Let me see the expertise in action.

**The confederacy's political system deserves more than a label.** "The Burba Jolof, the paramount chief of the six-state confederacy" tells me the structure but not the logic. The Jolof Confederacy was not simply a hierarchy with a paramount chief at the top. It was a negotiated political arrangement in which each buur retained significant autonomy and the Burba Jolof's authority was maintained through a web of obligations, marriages, and ceremonial relationships. This is a sophisticated political technology, and the opening should let the reader feel its complexity rather than reducing it to "paramount chief."

**The jon deserve more than a legal category.** "War captives whose status under Wolof law was defined and bounded" is honest and correct, but it is also abstract. Who were these people? Where had they been captured? What did "defined and bounded" mean in practice? The opening does not need to dwell on this, but the jon are human beings being traded for horses, and the text should give them at least as much physical presence as it gives the horses. The horses have stiff legs and wide nostrils. The jon are a legal category. This imbalance needs correction.

## The Question I'd Push On

The opening frames the Wolof-Portuguese encounter as a negotiation between equals. This is historiographically correct for the first decades. But the framing risks obscuring the power differential that was already embedded in the encounter from the Portuguese side. The Portuguese arrived with a papal bull (Dum Diversas, 1452; Romanus Pontifex, 1455) that explicitly authorized the enslavement of non-Christians and the seizure of their territories. The Wolof did not know this. They negotiated as equals with a partner who regarded them, on paper, as potential subjects. The chapter must eventually address this asymmetry of understanding. The opening should at least plant the seed, not by stepping outside the Wolof worldview, but by letting the Wolof's confidence carry a weight the reader can feel.
