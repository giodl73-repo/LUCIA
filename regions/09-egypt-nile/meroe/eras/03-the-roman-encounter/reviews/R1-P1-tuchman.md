## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of Meroe Era 3 Opening

**Overall:** This is one of the strongest openings I have seen in the project. The bronze head is a masterstroke of narrative organization: a single physical object that carries the entire political, military, and theological argument of the era.

**What works:**
- The opening line ("They brought the head back to Meroe in the fourth year of the kandake's war") places us in medias res with perfect economy. No throat-clearing, no context-setting. A head, a war, a destination. The reader is moving before they know where.
- The structural decision to anchor everything on the bronze head gives the opening a narrative spine that most historical openings lack. The head travels, the head is buried, the head is found, the head is still there. This is not chronological narration; it is narrative architecture.
- The closing paragraph is extraordinary. The temporal shift to Garstang in 1910, then the snap back to "in the year the head was buried," creates a resonance that earns the reader's attention without tricks. The lion god's silence is the right last note.
- The treaty paragraph manages to deliver diplomatic detail (Maharraqa, the Dodekaschoinos, tribute cancellation) without losing narrative momentum. The line "which reading the kandake preferred" is a perfect authorial touch: dry, pointed, and entirely from inside.

**What doesn't:**
- The paragraph beginning "What made the kandake's position possible" is the one place where the opening drifts toward survey mode. Iron furnaces, trade routes, the script, Apedemak, temple sites. It is all necessary context, but it arrives as a list rather than as narrative. The reader pauses here. The current slows. This paragraph needs either to be compressed or to find a narrative vehicle for its information.
- The sentence beginning "Not a frontier people. Not a peripheral chiefdom." is slightly overwritten. The preceding paragraph already showed us what Meroe was; these negative definitions risk explaining what the scene already demonstrated.

**The question I'd push on:**
The bronze head is brilliant as a hook, but it must also serve as a structural engine for the full chapter. Does the head's journey (from Rome's forum in Aswan to the temple threshold in Meroe to Garstang's excavation) have enough weight to organize 5,000 words? Or is it an opening device that will be left behind when the chapter moves into the treaty and its aftermath? If the head stays, this could be exceptional. If it is abandoned after the opening, it becomes a trick.
