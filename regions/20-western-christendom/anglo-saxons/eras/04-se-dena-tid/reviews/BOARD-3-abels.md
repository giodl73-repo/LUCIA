# BOARD-3 — Viking Age and Military History
**Reviewer Profile:** Specialist in Viking-Age England, the Danish settlement, Alfred's military strategy, and the formation of the Danelaw. Expertise in the *micel hæþen here*, the *Burghal Hidage*, and the social history of the Danish settlement. Works in the tradition of Richard Abels and Dawn Hadley.
**Chapter:** Se Dena Tíd — The Danish Time (Anglo-Saxons, Era 4)

---

## Assessment

The military and settlement history is sound. A few points of note.

**On the Great Heathen Army:**

The chapter correctly identifies the *micel hæþen here* as distinct from earlier raids — "an army with the intention of staying." This distinction is exactly right and reflects current scholarly consensus (Hadley, Downham). The chapter does not name Ivar the Boneless alongside Halfdan — Ivar is mentioned, and both are correctly identified as leaders of the Great Heathen Army. The chapter's restraint in not over-characterizing Ivar (a figure about whom much is speculated and little is certain) is appropriate.

**On the Somerset campaign (878):**

The identification of the refuge as "the island of Ætheling" is accurate — the Chronicle names it Athelney (*Æthelinga ig*, the island of the princes). The chapter's rendering as "Ætheling" is a reasonable approximation for the narrative register. The Chapter's account of guerrilla raiding from Athelney is consistent with the Chronicle and with modern reconstructions of Alfred's strategy (Abels, *Alfred the Great*, 1998).

**On the Danelaw treaty:**

The treaty boundary (Watling Street) is correctly stated. The chapter's treatment of the Danelaw as a "governance technology" — recognized territory rather than indefinite occupation — is historically sophisticated and reflects the best current scholarship (Wormald on law, Keynes on administration). The detail that Guthrum "kept the peace" for eleven years is accurate and noteworthy — the chapter is right to mark it as significant.

**On the *Burghal Hidage*:**

One minor precision: the formula is "4 men per pole of wall" (a pole being approximately 5 meters). The chapter's phrasing ("four men per pole of wall") is correct. The modern archaeological verification is also accurate — Alfred's burhs have been surveyed and the formula checks out.

**On Guthrum's motivation:**

The chapter's treatment of Guthrum's baptism as a pragmatic governance transaction is defensible. Some historians have argued for a more sincere conversion (the coins he minted as Æthelstan bear a Christian monogram). The chapter does not overstate the cynicism, saying only that "the nature of what the ritual meant to him in his own tradition" is unknown. This is honest uncertainty correctly deployed. No revision needed.

**On the Viking presence in the chapter:**

One gap: the chapter focuses almost entirely on the Danish side of the Danish presence (the Great Heathen Army, Guthrum, the Danelaw). The Norse settlements of northern England — the Jorvik kingdom, the Scandinavian element in Northumbria — are present by implication but not directly engaged. This is justifiable given the chapter's focus on Alfred and Wessex, but the chapter might benefit from a sentence noting that the Danelaw was not homogeneous: the eastern settlements of East Anglia (Danish) and the northern settlements of Jorvik (Norse) had different characters. This is a P3 suggestion — the chapter works without it.

**Overall:** Historically accurate. No required corrections. One P3 suggestion noted.
