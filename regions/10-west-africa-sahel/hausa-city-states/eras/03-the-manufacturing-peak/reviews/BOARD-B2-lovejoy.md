# Board Review — B-hausa-2: Paul E. Lovejoy
## Hausa Era 3: The Manufacturing Peak (v2)

**Expertise:** Trans-Saharan trade system, Hausa commercial economy, kola trade, slave trade, production-to-export chain.

---

**Historical accuracy:**

The trade network description is accurate. Kano as production center, Katsina as Saharan exchange hub, the northbound caravans carrying finished goods — this matches the documented commercial geography of the Manufacturing Peak period.

The "Moroccan leather" claim — that Hausa leather reached North Africa and Europe under a different name — is correctly handled. The chapter frames it as what "the North African merchants called by the name of the place it passed through on its way to them, not the place it was made." This is accurate and is framed without overclaiming about European market documentation (which is thin).

The kola trade's role is present (Section III, the goods flowing north) and correctly placed as part of the broader commercial network.

**On the slave trade:** The chapter handles this correctly and directly. The v1 chapter was honest but somewhat formulaic in its treatment. The v2 chapter is more precise: "the movement of people from the forest zone into the urban economy and onward onto the trade routes" captures the range of outcomes (urban labor, porterage, further trade northward) without flattening to a single description. The Maliki legal tradition's categories are correctly noted as the framework within which the practice operated. The gap between legal categories and economic reality is noted without editorial insertion.

The Amina section's framing of the campaigns as supply-chain logic — "What flowed north through the routes her walls held was the fuel of the manufacturing economy" — is analytically correct. The campaigns secured routes; the routes moved goods including people; the people entered the urban labor economy. This is the documented relationship.

**What the chapter gets right:**

The Songhai collapse as commercial hinge is well-handled. The redirection of Saharan trade from Gao/Timbuktu to Katsina is documented. The chapter's description of this as transforming Kano from "productive" to "dominant" is analytically sound.

Ibrahim al-Katsinawi's understanding of Amina's campaigns — "He had no particular feeling about this. The walls meant the route was more secure" — is exactly how a Katsina cloth merchant would have processed the news of Zazzau's military activity. The inside-voice economics is correct.

**Concern:**

The chapter does not explicitly mention the kola nut's role as a stimulant consumed during the Saharan crossing. This is a minor omission — the kola trade is mentioned in passing but without its specific function in the trans-Saharan economy. The v1 chapter had a fuller account. Given the chapter's word constraints, this is acceptable, but I would note that the kola trade's role in sustaining the Saharan trade (as a functional trade good that Tuareg merchants needed for the crossing) was economically significant beyond its social use at gatherings.

**Verdict:** APPROVED
The v2 chapter accurately represents the commercial economy and the slave trade's relationship to the manufacturing system. The Ibrahim figure is an excellent device for inside-view economics.
