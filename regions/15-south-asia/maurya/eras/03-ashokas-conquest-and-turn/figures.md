# Figures — Ashoka's Conquest and Turn

## Ashoka Devanampiya Piyadassi

Beloved of the Gods, of Gracious Mien. Third emperor of the Maurya dynasty, reigning from approximately 268 to 232 BCE. The name Ashoka means "without sorrow," and whether it was a birth name or a regnal one is uncertain. The edicts never use it. They use the title: Devanampiya Piyadassi. It was this title, not the name, that Prinsep first deciphered from the Brahmi inscriptions in 1837, and for years scholars debated which king it designated before the Minor Rock Edicts at Maski and Gujarra yielded the name Ashoka alongside the title.

What the edicts reveal is a man who governed through inscription. No other ruler of the ancient world left a comparable record of his own moral reasoning. The edicts do not simply announce policies; they explain why. They narrate the king's own change of heart, describe his regrets, and instruct his officers in the principles he wishes to see practiced. The voice is distinctive: measured, repetitive in the way that legal language is repetitive, paternalist without apology, and occasionally personal in a way that breaks through the formulaic structure. "This is a matter of profound sorrow and regret to the Beloved of the Gods." The formula is royal. The sorrow, if it is genuine, is human.

No image of Ashoka survives from his own time. The Buddhist artistic tradition depicted him in later centuries, but these are representations of a legend, not a portrait. The edicts are the portrait. They are the face he chose to present, carved in stone, in the languages his subjects spoke.

What the record cannot tell us is what Ashoka believed. The edicts are public documents. They present a king who practices dhamma and wishes his people to practice it. Whether the man behind the edicts experienced the remorse he described, or whether the edicts were a calculated instrument of governance, the stone does not say. Both things may be true simultaneously. The edicts' power is that they do not require the reader to choose.

## Kautilya

Also called Chanakya. Attributed author of the Arthashastra, minister to Chandragupta Maurya, founder of the strategic tradition that Ashoka both inherited and departed from. Whether Kautilya was a single historical person or a tradition of political thought crystallized under one name is debated. The Arthashastra itself is a composite text, likely compiled and revised over several centuries, but its core reflects a political philosophy that was operational during the Maurya period.

The Arthashastra's vision of the state is unsentimental. The king exists to maintain order. Order is maintained through the saptanga, the seven-limbed theory of state: king, ministers, territory, fortified capital, treasury, army, and allies. The vijigishu, the king who desires to conquer, operates within the mandala system, a concentric model of interstate relations where the neighbor is the natural enemy and the neighbor's neighbor is the natural friend. The logic is geometric. It does not accommodate remorse.

Ashoka's edicts do not mention the Arthashastra or Kautilya by name. The relationship is structural: Ashoka governed an empire that Arthashastra principles had built, using an administrative apparatus that Arthashastra logic had designed, and he repurposed that apparatus for a different end. The dhamma-mahamatras operated within the same bureaucratic structure as the Arthashastra's revenue officers and spies. The infrastructure was the same. The purpose had changed. Kautilya gave Ashoka his state. Ashoka gave the state a different reason to exist.

## The Dhamma-Mahamatras

Not a person but an institution, and the most significant institutional innovation of Ashoka's reign. Created by Rock Edict 5, the dhamma-mahamatras were officers appointed to promote the practice of dhamma across the empire. They worked among all sects: Buddhist, Brahmanical, Jain, Ajivika. They supervised charitable activities. They visited prisons and investigated cases of unjust imprisonment. They ensured that men with families were not punished excessively and that prisoners were given time to put their affairs in order. They traveled to the border territories and worked among the Yonas, Kambojas, and Gandharans.

No previous Indian state had created officers for moral governance. The Arthashastra's bureaucracy included officers for revenue, justice, commerce, agriculture, mines, and espionage. It did not include officers for virtue. The dhamma-mahamatras were Ashoka's addition to the inherited structure, and they represent the institutional form of his personal transformation: the conversion of a state apparatus from the maintenance of power to the promotion of conduct. Whether this conversion was sustainable beyond a single reign is answered by the record: the dhamma-mahamatras do not appear in any text after the Maurya period. The institution died with the dynasty. The edicts that created it survived.

## Megasthenes

Greek ambassador to the court of Chandragupta Maurya, resident in Pataliputra around 300 BCE. His account, the Indica, survives only in fragments quoted by later Greek and Roman authors. Megasthenes described a city of enormous extent, defended by a wooden palisade with 570 towers and a moat fed by the Ganga's tributary streams. He described a palace that rivaled Persepolis, with gilded pillars, gardens of cultivated trees, and pools stocked with fish. He described an administration of remarkable reach: officers who counted the population, monitored trade, maintained roads, and measured the rivers.

Megasthenes saw Pataliputra through Greek eyes. His descriptions carry the assumptions of a Hellenistic observer: he compared what he saw to the Persian court, to the cities of Alexander's empire, to the standards of Mediterranean urban life. His surprise is visible. The Indica is the account of a man encountering a civilization he had not expected to find operating at the scale he found it operating. The biases are Megasthenes', not the Maurya's. The city he described existed on its own terms. He measured it against his own and found it comparable, which tells us about his categories, not about Pataliputra's purpose.

## The Masons

They have no names. The edicts were carved by skilled stone workers who traveled across the empire or were recruited at each edict site. The variation in letter forms from site to site indicates that different hands carved different edicts, adapting the standard Brahmi or Kharosthi characters to the local stone. At Dhauli, the letters are wide and deeply cut into soft laterite. At Girnar, the same text is carved in narrower, shallower strokes suited to hard granite. At Mansehra, the Kharosthi letters follow the contour of the rock face. The masons were literate, or worked from templates provided by literate officials. They were artisans serving a king's will, and the durability of their work is a function of their skill: a chisel mark cut at the right angle, to the right depth, in the right stone, lasts. The masons' work has outlasted the empire, the dynasty, the script, the language, and the religion that Ashoka practiced. The stone holds because the hand that cut it knew what it was doing.
