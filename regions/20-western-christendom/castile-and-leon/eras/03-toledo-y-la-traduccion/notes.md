## 1. Sources

### Primary
- Gerard of Cremona's student biography (*Vita Gerardi*, c. 1187) — lists 87 translations, notes his reason for coming to Toledo, confirms his 40-year residence
- Rodrigo Jiménez de Rada, *Historia de rebus Hispanie* (c. 1243) — Archbishop of Toledo, first-hand knowledge of the translation milieu; written after the era but drawing on living memory
- Las Navas de Tolosa letters (1212) — Pope Innocent III's call for crusade, Alfonso VIII's victory dispatch, Ibn Abi Zar's account of the battle
- *Cantar de mio Cid* — composed within this era; evidence of the Castilian cultural register and the value placed on honor and effective excellence
- Alfonso VII's imperial title documentation — *Imperator totius Hispaniae* invoked 1135; reveals the political framework within which Toledo's prestige was cultivated

### Secondary
- Menocal, *The Ornament of the World* — convivencia in Andalusia; essential for the cultural context, though written primarily from the Muslim-ruled south
- Glick, *Islamic and Christian Spain in the Early Middle Ages* — structural analysis of institutions across the religious divide
- O'Callaghan, *Reconquest and Crusade in Medieval Spain* — the military framework within which translation occurs
- Linehan, *Spain, 1157–1300* — covers the political fragmentation after Alfonso VII's death and its effect on Castilian institutions
- Burnett, Charles, "The Translating Activity in Medieval Spain" (in *The Legacy of Muslim Spain*, ed. Jayyusi) — specialist source on the translation school's methods and participants

### Source asymmetry note
The Arabic-language sources for this era are extensive but largely external to Castile — Ibn Tumart (Almohad founder), Ibn Abi Zar (Moroccan chronicler), al-Maqqari (later historian of Andalusia). Castilian Christian sources are institutional (chronicles, charters, fueros). The Jewish scholars who did the most translation work — men like Abubacer, Abraham ibn Daud, Johannes Hispanus — appear primarily as names in Latin dedicatory prefaces, rarely in their own voices.

**Technique:** Source asymmetry as narrative technique (v1.5 rubric). The gap in Jewish scholarly voices is itself a historical fact to be honored. Abraham ibn Daud's *Sefer ha-Qabbalah* (Book of Tradition, 1161) — a Hebrew chronicle of Toledo's Jewish community — should be treated as primary evidence from inside the Jewish scholarly world, not merely background. Ibn Daud presents the Jewish translators as part of a continuous intellectual tradition, not as intermediaries serving Christian patrons.

## 2. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The translation cycle as self-sustaining knowledge circuit.

**Mechanism:** Toledo's three-community intellectual infrastructure — Arabic manuscript libraries, Jewish scholars with trilingual competence, Latin-reading Christian clerics — created a translation cycle that fed itself. Each translation produced demand for the next: Euclid's *Elements* required astronomical tables to be useful; the astronomical tables required Ptolemy's *Almagest* for their framework; the *Almagest* required Aristotle's *Physics* for its metaphysical underpinning. The chain pulled more scholars to Toledo, which created more translations, which created the university curriculum that institutionalized demand for Toledo's output. The university of Paris adopted the translated Aristotle; the university curriculum demanded more translations; Toledo produced them.

**Terminal condition: Legitimacy gap (v1.5 type).** The translation cycle was structurally dependent on Toledo's three-community stability. The Almohad invasions (1145–1212) created the military crisis that resolved at Las Navas de Tolosa but also changed Toledo's position: as the frontier moved south and Castile expanded dramatically after 1212, the city's function as the singular point of Arabic-Latin contact was diluted. The fuero system allowed Christian settlers to flood the newly conquered territories; the Jewish and Mudéjar communities that had sustained the translation infrastructure became politically exposed as the Reconquista ideology intensified. The translation cycle continued after 1212 — it would peak under Alfonso X "el Sabio" in the second half of the thirteenth century — but its original three-community form was already transforming. The engine's terminal condition is not military defeat but social displacement: the very success of the Reconquista that the translation era served ultimately undermined the conditions that made the translation era possible.

**Note on engine type:** This is not quite "legitimacy gap" (Songhai type) or "deliberate self-consumption" (Tibet type). Closest to "premise non-recognition" — the military-expansion ideology of the Reconquista and the multicultural infrastructure of Toledo operated on incompatible premises, and as Castile's expansion succeeded, the military ideology displaced the tolerationist pragmatism. However, the displacement was slow and incomplete: Alfonso X's *Siete Partidas* (1265) still drew heavily on Jewish and Arabic legal learning. The engine transformed rather than collapsed. This may be a new terminal condition type: **gradual premise erosion** — the engine's conditions are slowly replaced by the conditions of its own success.

## 3. Key Figures

### Gerard of Cremona (c. 1114–1187)
The anchor figure — the lens through which the opening enters Toledo. Italian; came to Toledo c. 1145 specifically for the *Almagest*. Stayed 40 years. Translated 87 texts across mathematics, astronomy, medicine, philosophy, and logic. His student biography is the primary source. Note Achebe's concern: Gerard is an outsider. He must function as a lens into Toledo's world, not as the subject of a reception history. His presence in the chapter should pull us toward Toledo's three-community infrastructure, not away from it.

### Abubacer (Ioannes Avendeuth / Abraham ibn Daud / multiple identifications)
**Source problem:** The identity of "Abubacer" — Gerard's primary Arabic interlocutor — is disputed. The name in Latin sources is likely a Latinization of Abu Bakr, a common element in Arabic names. Some scholars identify the interlocutor with Abraham ibn Daud, the philosopher and historian of Toledo's Jewish community. Others identify multiple interlocutors conflated under one name. The opening uses "Abubacer" as the historical record uses it, but the notes should acknowledge the uncertainty. The figures.md will need to handle this carefully.

**Technique:** Honest limits as technique (v1.1 rubric). The gap in Abubacer's identity becomes part of the chapter's argument: these scholars worked as infrastructure, named in dedicatory prefaces when useful to the patron, otherwise anonymous. Their anonymity is historical evidence about how the translation economy worked — and who it served.

### Dominicus Gundissalinus (fl. 1150–1190)
Archdeacon of Segovia, resident in Toledo. Wrote original philosophical works in addition to translating. His *De divisione philosophiae* (c. 1150) is the first Latin synthesis of Arabic Aristotelian philosophy — he was not merely transcribing but thinking with what he translated. This is important: Gundissalinus is not just a scribe. He is a philosopher who uses translation as a mode of original thought.

### Alfonso VI (r. 1065–1109)
Took Toledo in 1085. Styled himself *rex trium religionum* — king of the three religions. Confirmed the existing Mozarabic community's rights. This is not documented tolerance as policy but pragmatic governance: a city of 25,000 could not be repopulated from the Christian north, so the existing population had to be retained. The "policy" was necessity rationalized as legitimacy.

### Alfonso VII (r. 1126–1157)
Called himself *Imperator totius Hispaniae*. Under his reign, the translation school reaches its first major output (Gerard arrives 1145). The imperial title required a cultural capital worthy of it. Alfonso VII understood this — or his court did.

### Alfonso VIII (r. 1158–1214)
The king who faces the Almohad threat in full: the catastrophic defeat at Alarcos (1195), then the recovery and victory at Las Navas de Tolosa (1212). The translation era takes place under the military shadow of his reign's first half. He is the figure who must fund both the frontier war and the cultural infrastructure simultaneously.

### Ibn Tumart (c. 1080–1130) and the Almohad movement
The structural antagonist — not a villain but the force that gives the era its existential pressure. Ibn Tumart's religious reform movement in the Maghreb produced the Almohad dynasty, which retook much of Andalusia from the more tolerant Almoravids and drove north into Castile. The Almohads are doctrinally opposed to the convivencia that Toledo embodied — their theology did not accommodate the same pragmatic coexistence. This gives the translation era its compressed urgency: the city that produces Aristotle for Europe is simultaneously the cultural flagship of a kingdom that may not survive the century.

## 4. Section Plan

**Section 1 — The Arrival (Opening integrated)**
Gerard arrives in Toledo, 1145. The smell of two cities. The three-language translation chain. The forty-year answer. This is the chapter's door: a specific person entering a specific world for a specific purpose and finding something larger.

**Section 2 — The City on the Rock**
Toledo's physical geography and its significance. The rock above the Tagus — why it was defensible, why it was desirable, why Alfonso VI took it specifically (not merely as a milestone but as a capital worthy of imperial pretension). The three communities within the walls: the Christian chapter with its cathedral-in-progress, the Jewish aljama in its quarter, the Mudéjar craftsmen in theirs. The Zocodover market as the daily point of overlap — here we enter the market that the opening named but did not enter. Responding to Kapuściński's panel note.

**Section 3 — What the Libraries Held**
The Arabic manuscript collections Toledo inherited from the taifa kingdoms — what was in them, how it got there, what it represented as an intellectual tradition in its own right. Responding to Davis's panel note: the Arabic-language tradition is not a relay station. Avicenna's *Canon of Medicine*, al-Kindi's optics, al-Farabi's political philosophy — these are original achievements built on Greek foundations but extending well beyond them. The transmission to Toledo is the second movement of a translation cycle that began in Baghdad. The chapter must show what the Arabic-language tradition was before showing what Latin Europe received from it.

**Section 4 — The Workshop**
The translation school at work — who participated, how it was organized, who funded it. Gundissalinus as philosopher-translator, not just transcriber. Abraham ibn Daud's *Sefer ha-Qabbalah* as evidence of the Jewish scholarly tradition within which the translators understood themselves. The anonymous figures who did not receive dedicatory prefaces. The question of credit and ownership: whose knowledge was this?

**Section 5 — The Shadow from the South**
The Almohad threat as structural context. The defeat at Alarcos (1195) — a disaster that for a decade made Castile's survival genuinely uncertain. The call for crusade. The concentration of the peninsula's Christian kingdoms against a common enemy. How do you fund a translation school when you are rebuilding an army? What happens to convivencia under military pressure? The answer is: imperfectly, unevenly, but not completely. The Mudéjar craftsmen still built. The Jewish scholars still translated. The market still functioned.

**Section 6 — Las Navas de Tolosa and What It Changed**
July 16, 1212. The battle that broke Almohad power on the peninsula — permanently, as it turned out, though no one knew that in 1212. The combined armies of Castile, Aragon, Navarre, and Portugal crossing the Sierra Morena through a pass shown to them, according to one account, by a shepherd whose identity is disputed. The aftermath: the path to Córdoba is now open. But the conditions that made Toledo what it was are already beginning to shift. Victory is the beginning of the engine's transformation.

**Landing**
Not a summary. Toledo, 1212, after Las Navas. The translation workshops are still running. Gerard died twenty-five years ago; his translations are now in university curricula from Paris to Bologna. The city that gave Europe Aristotle is also the city from which the armies just left to win the battle that will eventually end the world that made the translation possible. The manuscripts stay. The city continues. What the city was — three-community, three-language, built on necessity rationalized as policy — begins, slowly, to change into something else.

**Achebe concern (addressed in section plan):** Sections 3 and 4 develop the Jewish scholarly tradition in its own terms (Abraham ibn Daud's *Sefer ha-Qabbalah*), the Arabic intellectual tradition as original achievement, and the anonymous translators who did not receive Latin credit. Gerard is the lens into Toledo but not the subject of a reception history. The chapter must balance Gerard's narrative function with authentic representation of Toledo's permanent communities.

**Ibn Khaldun concern (addressed in section plan):** Section 5 integrates the Almohad structural pressure. The translation cycle is shown as operating within — not separate from — the existential military context. Section 6 shows the terminal condition beginning.

## 6. Voice Register

**Proposed register: Scholarly-precise with diplomatic cadence.**

This is a chapter about translation — about the movement of ideas across linguistic and cultural boundaries, mediated by specific people with specific competencies. The prose register should carry that precision: careful about words, attentive to the specific term that carries the right meaning, aware that the same concept can mean different things in different languages.

Not academic prose — no passive constructions, no hedged generalizations. But precise. The voice of someone who has been in the room where three men stopped to argue about what a word meant — not lexically, but in terms of what Ptolemy was actually describing.

Distinct from: Legal-public (Roman), Philosophical-Devotional (Tibetan), Argumentative/Text-centered (Jewish Talmud). Closest to: a new register that might be called **Translation-Diplomatic** — the prose of a culture whose primary intellectual activity was moving ideas across linguistic borders, which requires both precision about language and diplomacy about ownership.

Em-dash discipline: Target 0 in the chapter. The scholarly-precise register finds its pauses through periods and semicolons, not em-dashes.

## 7. Open Questions

1. **Abubacer's identity:** The figures.md must address the scholarly dispute. Is Abubacer a single person or a composite? The opening uses the name as the Latin sources use it; the chapter should acknowledge the uncertainty without making it the story.

2. **The fuero dimension:** The OVERVIEW notes the fuero as a key Castilian institution. This era (1085–1212) is when many frontier fueros were granted. The chapter focuses on Toledo's urban intellectual life; it may be thin on the rural frontier-settlement story. Consider whether one section can acknowledge the fuero system without narrating it fully (that is Era 2's story).

3. **Women in the translation school:** The available record is almost entirely male. Are there documented women in Toledo's intellectual life in this period? If not, acknowledge the lacuna in figures.md without inventing.

4. **The terminal condition type:** The chapter may be logging a new terminal-condition type — gradual premise erosion — for the rubric's innovation log. Flag for GATE-2 scorer.

5. **Alfonso X's continuation:** The chapter covers 1085–1212. Alfonso X's great translation project (c. 1252–1284) is Era 4 or Era 5 territory. The landing should gesture toward it without narrating it — it is the engine's continuation in a different mode.
