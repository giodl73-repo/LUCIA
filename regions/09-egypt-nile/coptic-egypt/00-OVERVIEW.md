# The Church of St. Mark — "Al-Kanisa al-Qibtiyya"

The Coptic people are the Egyptians who did not Arabize. When the Arab armies entered Egypt in 641 CE, they found a Christian country with a living theological tradition, a monastic movement that had shaped the entire Christian world, and a language — Coptic, the last form of the ancient Egyptian tongue — that was still in daily use. The Arab conquest changed the political order, and over the following centuries changed the language of administration and eventually of daily life. But the Coptic Church and the Coptic community maintained their identity, their liturgy, their monasteries, and their language — used today in the liturgy of the Coptic Orthodox Church — with a tenacity that represents one of the most remarkable continuities in human history. The Coptic language is the ancient Egyptian language, written in a Greek-derived alphabet, carrying within its theological vocabulary concepts that the pharaonic civilization developed four thousand years before Christianity existed.

The Copts are not a remnant. They are not a survival against the odds. They are a community that chose its identity with deliberate care across fourteen centuries of minority existence — under Arab caliphates, under Fatimid and Ayyubid and Mamluk and Ottoman rule, into the modern Egyptian republic. The monastic tradition that Pachomius founded in the Thebaid in the fourth century is still alive: Wadi el-Natrun, St. Antony's monastery near the Red Sea, St. Catherine's in Sinai. The church that Mark founded, according to tradition, in Alexandria in the first century still has its patriarch in Cairo. When Coptic Christians celebrate Easter with the liturgy of the resurrection, they are participating in a continuous act that has been performed in Egypt without interruption since the Roman period — and they are performing it in the language of pharaonic Egypt's last form.

- **Sohag / White Monastery** — the complex of Shenouda of Atripe; the great fifth-century monastic reformer

## What Defined Them

**The Coptic language as living pharaonic inheritance.** Coptic is ancient Egyptian in its last form, written in a Greek-derived alphabet with six additional letters from demotic Egyptian. Its theological vocabulary carries pharaonic concepts: the Coptic word for "soul" (psyche in Greek is rendered in Coptic with a word that connects to the ancient Egyptian ka and ba traditions). The liturgical language of the Coptic church is thus, at a deep level, the language that pharaonic priests used to describe the soul's journey through death — transformed, translated, carried forward into a Christian theology that nevertheless maintains structural resonances with its ancient Egyptian past. This is not accidental preservation; it is the consequence of Christianity developing in a culture with four millennia of theological depth.

**The desert as sacred space.** The Egyptian Christian understanding of the desert was shaped by an ancient Egyptian tradition in which the desert — the deshret, the red land — was the realm of the divine, the uncanny, and the transformative. When Anthony went into the desert in the late third century, he was not simply imitating Elijah in the wilderness; he was participating in an Egyptian theological geography that understood the desert as the place where the ordinary world's rules did not apply and where encounter with the divine was possible. The explosion of desert monasticism from Egypt into the entire Christian world — the monastery system that eventually produced Benedictine Europe — was an Egyptian theological export carrying an Egyptian spatial theology.

**The Miaphysite position.** At the Council of Chalcedon in 451 CE, the Coptic church refused to accept the formula that Christ had two natures, divine and human, "without confusion or change." The Coptic position — Miaphysitism, the unity of Christ's nature — was condemned as heresy by the Byzantine imperial church. The Coptic church maintained its position. This was not theological stubbornness; it was the affirmation of a theological tradition developed in Alexandria by Cyril and Athanasius, and the refusal to accept an imperial council's authority to override it. The split between the Coptic Orthodox Church and the Byzantine/Roman churches that followed Chalcedon has never been fully healed, and the Coptic church has maintained its theological independence for fifteen centuries.

**Continuity under pressure.** The Coptic community has survived fourteen centuries of minority existence without assimilating. This is not a passive survival — it required the active maintenance of institutions: the patriarchate, the monasteries, the church schools, the liturgical tradition, the arts of icon painting and church architecture. The periodic violence against Coptic communities — under some medieval rulers, and in modern Egypt as well — did not destroy the community because the institutions were resilient and the identity was deeply held. The Copts do not understand themselves as the remnant of a lost civilization; they understand themselves as the living continuation of Egypt's deepest spiritual inheritance.

**The global diaspora and the ancient root.** The twentieth and twenty-first centuries have produced a Coptic diaspora in the United States, Australia, Canada, and Europe that numbers in the millions. Coptic churches in New Jersey and in Sydney perform the same liturgy — in Coptic, with Arabic and English translations — as the church in Cairo. The diaspora has not abandoned the tradition; in some ways it has strengthened and documented it. But the root remains in the Nile valley, in the desert monasteries, in the church that Mark founded in Alexandria — or so the tradition holds, and the tradition is what the Coptic people have chosen to carry.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Apostolic Age:** A letter from Rome arrives in Alexandria addressing a community of Christians. The letter knows they are there. Someone in Alexandria writes back.

**2 — Anthony in the Thebaid:** An old man has been in the desert for twenty years. Visitors come to him with questions. He turns most of them away. The ones he answers carry his words back to their communities, and the communities found monasteries.

**3 — After Chalcedon:** The Coptic patriarch in Alexandria refuses to sign the Chalcedonian formula. The Byzantine emperor sends a new patriarch, backed by soldiers. The Coptic community knows who their patriarch is. It is not the man the soldiers put in place.

**4 — Under Arab Rule:** A Coptic scribe in a government office writes in Arabic all day. At home, he reads the Psalms in Coptic. His son will read them in Arabic. His grandson will read them in Coptic again, because the church has decided not to let the language die.

**5 — The Living Church:** A Coptic deacon in a church in New Jersey chants the liturgy in a language that descends from the tongue of the builders of the pyramids. The congregation follows in English. Both things are true at once. Both things have always been true at once.
