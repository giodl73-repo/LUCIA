### Random-Paragraph Test

Three paragraphs selected at random:

**Paragraph 1** (Kindattu section): "He was not the only force that brought Ur III down. The Amorites pressed from the west at the same time. The empire was already straining — overstretched, drought-stressed, its administrative demands exceeding what its revenues could sustain. Kindattu did not break a healthy empire. He broke a weakening one, at the moment when the break was possible."

**Test:** Could a reader, encountering this paragraph in isolation, identify the culture from the rhythm alone?

**Result:** The short, declarative, weight-bearing sentences are consistent with the sedimentary/administrative register. The analysis is embedded in the prose's rhythm rather than standing outside it. "He broke a weakening one, at the moment when the break was possible" — this sounds like the chapter. Pass.

**Paragraph 2** (Sukkalmah section): "This was not an arrangement that happened by accident. The dual-kingdom's structural logic — neither pole complete without the other, both poles requiring mediation — produced the institutional form that could manage the mediation. The sukkalmah stood between Anshan and Susa not as their master but as their guarantor. His authority derived from his capacity to maintain the exchange. When the exchange functioned, the sukkalmah's legitimacy was self-evident. When it failed, the legitimacy failed with it."

**Test:** Same.

**Result:** The sedimentary voice is slightly under pressure here from the analytical content — "neither pole complete without the other, both poles requiring mediation" is abstract in a way that the more sensory sections are not. But it recovers: "when the exchange functioned, the sukkalmah's legitimacy was self-evident" carries weight rather than explaining it. Monitor but not a violation.

**Paragraph 3** (God section): "Napirisha was the second great god — the great god of the highland, whose name means 'great god' in the Elamite language itself. His domain was the upper country, the Fars plateau, the stone and altitude of Anshan."

**Test:** Same.

**Result:** "The stone and altitude of Anshan" is right. The enumeration (upper country, Fars plateau, stone and altitude) builds like an account entry. Pass.

### Note 1 — Analytical Register Pressure in Sukkalmah Section

The sukkalmah section carries the highest analytical load of the chapter. The structural argument (tripartite succession as the institutionalized form of the dual-kingdom logic) requires abstract explanation. The voice holds, but it is under more pressure here than elsewhere. This is acceptable — the sedimentary register can carry analysis — but the balance between abstract argument and sensory grounding tips slightly toward the abstract. The note for the board and any subsequent revision: if a future draft of this section can find one more physical scene (a specific administrative moment, a tablet bearing the tripartite titles, a physical object that embodies the mediating function), the analytical sections will be better supported.

### Note 2 — Sentence in Landing Slightly More Literary

"He pressed the stylus. The grain was counted. The god's portion was set aside. It was enough." — this is correct and deliberate. But "It was enough" edges toward a slightly more literary-meditative register than the sedimentary voice's usual weight-bearing declarativeness. The previous three sentences are pure sedimentary. "It was enough" is the landing. This is a judgment call and I support it — the landing earns its slight elevation. But it is a departure and should be conscious.
