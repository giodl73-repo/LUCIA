## Assessment of Mexica Representation

The chapter treats the Mexica with care that is not universal in popular accounts of the conquest. Several specific assessments:

**Moctezuma as political actor:** The chapter correctly refuses the passivity narrative. The statement "he was a sophisticated political actor who had governed the most powerful state in Mesoamerica for seventeen years" is historically defensible. The recent revisionary scholarship (Restall, Townsend, Schroeder) strongly supports this characterization. The chapter presents his reception of Cortés as the management of a situation within a coherent cosmological framework, not as credulity or surrender. This is the correct scholarly position.

**The Quetzalcóatl framing:** The chapter handles this well. The chapter notes the year One Reed and the god's prophesied return as "operative cosmological categories" without asserting that Moctezuma believed Cortés was Quetzalcóatl. The Quetzalcóatl-Cortés identification is now regarded by most Nahuatl scholars as a post-conquest interpretive overlay, not a pre-conquest Mexica belief. The chapter does not commit to it. Correct.

**Mexica cosmology:** The one paragraph on Mexica cosmology — the sun, the debt of sacrifice, the maintenance of the cosmic order — is accurate in its main claims. The framing "not as cruelty, but as maintenance" is well-chosen and avoids the trap of requiring the reader to find the practice monstrous before the chapter can proceed.

**Tenochtitlan description:** The market at Tlatelolco, the 60,000 figure for an ordinary day, the causeways — all consistent with Díaz and with archaeological evidence. The 200,000 population figure is the standard estimate (actual figure debated between 150,000 and 250,000 by scholars; 200,000 is the conventional middle).

## Concern: Omission of Tlaxcala

The chapter mentions "Cortés and his Tlaxcalan allies" only in the figures section (Moctezuma entry), and only in passing. The Tlaxcalan role in the conquest is now recognized as structurally essential — the fall of Tenochtitlan was in significant part a Nahua civil war, with Tlaxcaltec warriors constituting the majority of the force that besieged the city. The chapter does not address this at all.

This is partly a scope question: the chapter's focus is the Spanish theological-legal framework, not the military history of the conquest. The Tlaxcalan alliance does not directly bear on the Requerimiento's logic. However, its absence means the chapter presents the conquest as a Spanish-Mexica confrontation rather than a multi-party political realignment within Mesoamerica. This is not false, but it is incomplete in a way that a reader unfamiliar with the scholarship might not notice.

**Recommendation:** Add one sentence in the Tenochtitlan section or the figures' Moctezuma entry acknowledging that Cortés's force included tens of thousands of Tlaxcaltec warriors who had their own reasons to oppose Mexica dominance. This would not disrupt the chapter's argument and would prevent the chapter from inadvertently reproducing the "Spanish defeat of the Aztecs" simplification.

## Guaman Poma Assessment

Guaman Poma de Ayala is an Andean figure, not Mesoamerican, but my expertise extends to colonial-period indigenous documentation. The chapter's account is accurate. The use of Guaman Poma as the chapter's persistence mechanism — an indigenous figure who learned the colonizer's framework to argue from within it — is historically and analytically sound. The detail that his letter was not answered is correct; there is no evidence of any royal response.

**No factual errors identified beyond the Tlaxcala omission noted above.**

## Endorsement

The chapter represents the Mexica with appropriate complexity and avoids the most common distortions (passive victims, credulous ruler, simple military defeat). The Tlaxcala omission is a recommendation, not a required correction. The chapter may proceed as is; the addition would strengthen it.

**Endorsed. Tlaxcala addition recommended but not required.**
