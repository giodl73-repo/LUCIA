# Dzimba dza Mabwe — "Houses of Stone"

Great Zimbabwe was not mysterious. The people who built it knew what they were building and why: the stone enclosures were palaces, cattle kraals, and the visual expression of a political authority that controlled access to the gold-bearing reefs of the Zimbabwe plateau and the trade routes running east to the Indian Ocean coast. The Shona-speaking people who quarried and laid the massive granite blocks of the Great Enclosure and the Hill Complex were doing what skilled stone-masons do everywhere: creating durable expressions of power and prestige in the most permanent material available. The dry-stone walling technique they developed — no mortar, stones selected and shaped to interlock in stable courses — represents a mastery of granite construction unmatched anywhere in pre-colonial Africa.

The "mystery" of Great Zimbabwe was a colonial invention. When European explorers encountered the ruins in the 1860s–1890s, they were unable to accept that the stone buildings had been built by the ancestors of the people living nearby, and they invented alternative explanations: Phoenicians, Arabs, the Queen of Sheba, King Solomon. These theories required dismissing the oral traditions of the Shona people — who knew perfectly well what Great Zimbabwe was — and the archaeological evidence, which supported the local origin completely. The story of the "mystery" of Great Zimbabwe is a story about how European racism could look at an extraordinary African architectural achievement and conclude that it must have been built by someone else. The ruins are not mysterious. They are evidence.

## What Defined Them

**Stone as political grammar.** The dry-stone walling tradition of Great Zimbabwe — and the later tradition of stone-walled enclosures (dzimba dza mabwe) across the plateau — developed a distinctive architectural vocabulary for expressing political authority. The enclosure wall proclaimed: here is a chief's space, bounded by stone, requiring effort to enter. The height of the wall expressed the level of authority: the Hill Complex, the highest and most defensible, was the senior ruler's seat; the Great Enclosure in the valley, with its 250-meter outer wall and its conical tower, was a secondary enclosure of great prestige. Stone did not simply protect — it performed hierarchy. Every stone enclosure on the Zimbabwe plateau was a statement in the same political language.

**Gold as the engine of the world.** The gold-bearing geology of the Zimbabwe plateau — alluvial gold in river beds and reef gold in quartz veins — was the resource that made Great Zimbabwe possible. Shona miners extracted the gold; Shona traders managed its movement to the coast; Swahili merchants at Sofala bought it and shipped it north to Kilwa and into the Indian Ocean trade. In exchange, Indian cloth, Chinese porcelain, glass beads from Egypt, and Persian ceramics came back along the same route. The Chinese porcelain found in the ruins of Great Zimbabwe got there because someone carried it inland from a ship that had sailed from a Chinese port to an Indian port to a Swahili port to Sofala to Great Zimbabwe. This is not remarkable because it is improbable; it is remarkable because it shows how completely the Zimbabwe plateau was integrated into a global trade system a full century before Europeans arrived on the African coast.

**The cattle kraal and the political economy.** The largest stone enclosure at Great Zimbabwe is probably a cattle kraal — the evidence for cattle bones in large quantities suggests that the enclosures served partly as cattle pens. In the Shona political economy, as in most of the southern African plateau zone, cattle were the primary form of wealth and political obligation. The ruler's cattle kraal was the center of the redistribution economy: tribute came in as cattle, labor was organized around cattle, and the ruler's power was expressed through his ability to accumulate and distribute cattle as well as through his control of the gold trade. Stone walls around cattle kraals were not simply practical; they were the display of wealth made durable.

**The Zimbabwe Birds.** Eight carved soapstone birds — figures of birds perched on stone columns, now known as the "Zimbabwe Birds" — were found at Great Zimbabwe in the late nineteenth century and removed to various locations including a private collection in South Africa. They have since been repatriated to Zimbabwe, where one appears on the national flag. The birds are almost certainly ritual objects related to the Shona tradition of ancestor veneration — the eagle or fish-eagle (chapungu) associated with the spirit mediums who communicate with royal ancestors. They are not mysterious; they are the spiritual technology of a state that understood the relationship between the living and the dead as the foundation of legitimate political authority.

**The colonial denial and its refutation.** The theory that Great Zimbabwe was built by Phoenicians, Arabs, or the Queen of Sheba was maintained as official Rhodesian government policy until the 1970s, when the archaeologist Peter Garlake published work that definitively established the local African origin of the site. The Smith government in Rhodesia censored archaeological reports that contradicted the non-African origin theory and dismissed archaeologists who insisted on the evidence. That the evidence for local African construction was available from the beginning — from the first archaeological work at the site in 1905 by David Randall-MacIver — and was suppressed for seventy years because it was politically inconvenient, is a documented history of academic fraud in the service of racism.

## The Chapters

**1 — The First Wall:** The stone has been placed without mortar. The builder knows that the weight of the upper courses will lock the lower ones. He has done this before, on smaller walls. This one will be eleven meters high and 250 meters around. He has calculated the labor and the time.

**2 — The Gold Route:** A Shona trader arrives at Sofala with a load of gold. A Swahili merchant is waiting with a bolts of Indian cotton cloth and a crate of Chinese porcelain. Both men know the exchange rate. Both men have done this before. The porcelain is going inland. The gold is going to sea.

**3 — The Great Enclosure:** The conical tower is complete. It is not a granary; it has no door. It is a statement — a form that asserts permanence and prestige in the most visible possible way. The ruler who commissioned it knows exactly what it says.

**4 — The Colonial Denial:** A Rhodesian official is reading the archaeologist's report. The report says the walls were built by Shona people, their ancestors, the same people living in the area today. The official puts the report in a drawer. He writes an official finding: the origin is unknown. It is not unknown. He knows it is not unknown.

**5 — The Birds Come Home:** The carved soapstone bird is returned to Zimbabwe from South Africa in 1981. It was removed in 1890. It is on the national flag. A curator places it in the museum. The ancestor looks out from the stone, across the ruins, toward the plateau where the gold used to be.
