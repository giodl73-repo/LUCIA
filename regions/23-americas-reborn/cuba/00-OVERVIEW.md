# Cuba — La Revolución Permanente

Cuba has organized itself around two revolutions and cannot let either one go. The first, against Spain (the Ten Years' War of 1868–1878, and then the final independence war of 1895–1898), produced the poet-martyr José Martí, whose vision of a free Cuba and a free Latin America is still the moral vocabulary of the Cuban state. The second, against Batista (1953–1959), produced Fidel Castro and Che Guevara, whose faces are the most globally distributed political images of the twentieth century. Between these two revolutions lies a period of formal independence (1902–1959) that Cubans experienced largely as informal occupation — the Platt Amendment gave the United States the legal right to intervene in Cuban affairs until 1934, and the economic and political dominance of American corporations and the American government continued for decades after that. When the second revolution came, it was in part a revolution against the first revolution's betrayal: the independence that had not produced sovereignty.

Cuba is an island of 11 million people that has reshaped the global conversation about colonialism, capitalism, imperialism, and the possibility of an alternative. The question of whether the Cuban Revolution succeeded depends entirely on what you think it was trying to do. By some measures — literacy, infant mortality, life expectancy, educational attainment — Cuba compares favorably with countries ten times its GDP. By others — political freedom, freedom of the press, the right to leave — it ranks among the most repressive states in the hemisphere. Both sets of data are real. The Cuban Revolution has always been a set of competing claims about what freedom means and who gets to decide.

- **Havana (La Habana)** — the colonial jewel; the revolutionary capital; the city that time preserved and poverty preserved differently
- **Sierra Maestra** — the mountains of eastern Cuba where Castro's guerrilla force built its base (1956–1958)

## What Defined Them

**Martí's vision and its contradictions.** José Martí — poet, journalist, revolutionary organizer, and martyr — is the founding conscience of the Cuban nation, invoked by every Cuban political tendency including those that would have horrified him. His vision was of a free Cuba in a free Latin America, organized not around racial categories but around a shared humanity — "Cuban is more than white, more than mulato, more than Black." His political project was liberal and democratic in the nineteenth-century sense: independence, sovereignty, dignity. Castro claimed him as a precursor; so do Cuban dissidents. The competition for Martí's legacy is the competition for the Cuban soul.

**The sugar and the African roots.** Cuba's economy was built on sugar and enslaved labor — more than 800,000 Africans were imported between 1790 and 1860, the majority after the slave trade was formally (if not practically) prohibited. The African presence in Cuba — Yoruba, Fon, Bantu, and others — produced Santería (Lucumí), the Cuban equivalent of Haitian Vodou: a West African religious system maintaining the orixás under Catholic names, still practiced widely in Cuba today. It also produced the musical traditions — son, rumba, guaguancó, mambo, salsa — that are the most globally influential popular music Cuba has generated. The African roots of Cuban culture are not a subaltern story; they are the story.

**The US as permanent presence.** No country in the Western hemisphere has had its history shaped by the United States more directly than Cuba. The Platt Amendment (1901–1934) gave the US the legal right to intervene in Cuban affairs. The Bay of Pigs invasion (1961) was planned and funded by the CIA. The US economic embargo (in place since 1962) is, in the Cuban government's account, the primary cause of the island's economic difficulties — and while this claim is self-serving, the embargo's effects are real. The US naval base at Guantánamo sits on land Cuba has never agreed was legitimately leased. The Miami exile community is a political force in both countries. Cuba and the United States cannot be understood independently of each other.

**La Revolución as permanent present.** The Cuban Revolution did not end. It institutionalized itself, declared itself the permanent condition of Cuban society, and organized the state around its own perpetuation. This has been both the revolution's strength (extraordinary durability; the survival of the state in the face of fifty years of US hostility) and its deepest problem (the inability to self-correct, to allow internal criticism, to evolve). The generation that made the revolution governed for sixty years. The society they governed changed — became younger, more globally connected, less willing to accept political restrictions in exchange for social guarantees — in ways the revolution's institutions have been unable to accommodate.

## The Chapters

**1 — La Perla:** Havana in 1850 is the jewel of the Spanish empire. The sugar economy has made it wealthy, the enslaved labor has made it cruel, and the Cuban creoles — born here, educated here, proud of their island — are beginning to ask why they should be governed from Madrid.

**2 — Martí:** José Martí dies on May 19, 1895, in the first cavalry charge of the Cuban War of Independence, at Dos Ríos. He has spent most of his adult life in exile, writing and organizing. He is 42 years old. He knew, his friends say, that he would die early in the war — that he had to be present to be credible, and that being present meant dying. He is the revolution that always precedes the revolution.

**3 — July 26, 1953:** One hundred and sixty young people attack the Moncada barracks in Santiago. The attack fails completely. More than half the attackers are killed — many of them after capture. Fidel Castro is captured and tried. His defense at trial becomes his political manifesto: "History will absolve me." He is sentenced to fifteen years. He serves two.

**4 — January 1, 1959:** Batista flees Cuba at midnight. The guerrillas are still in the mountains; they will not reach Havana for several days. In the streets, ordinary Cubans tear apart the parking meters that the American mafia companies installed — symbols of the corruption of the old order. The revolution has won. The question of what it is for is just beginning.

**5 — El Período Especial:** The Soviet Union has collapsed. Cuba's oil supply disappears overnight. The economy contracts by 35% in three years. Cubans lose an average of 20 pounds. They ride bicycles. They plant urban gardens. The state that promised to protect its people finds the promise hardest to keep precisely when it is most needed.

**6 — July 11, 2021:** Thousands of Cubans take to the streets in the largest protests since the revolution. The slogan is "Patria y Vida" — homeland and life — a play on the regime's "Patria o Muerte" (homeland or death). The government calls it a US-orchestrated provocation. The protesters call it sixty years of accumulated frustration. Both are probably true.
