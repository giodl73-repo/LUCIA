## Sensory Plan Assessment

Section IV (Akkade) has the best sensory plan: cedar planks, copper ingots, lapis lazuli, marketplace sounds. This should be the most physical section of the chapter.

Section II (the governor) needs a sensory plan. What does it feel like to sit in another king's audience room? The chair is too large or too small. The reed mats are Sumerian-woven. The incense is the same incense the previous king burned for the city's god, and the god is still there, in the same temple, and the god did not choose you. The governor's physical discomfort in an inherited space is the sensory expression of the structural engine: authority granted by a chain of personal bonds, seated in a room that belongs to someone else's god.

Section III (the bilingual synthesis) could ground the intellectual content in physical practice. A scribe's hand moving differently when writing Sumerian versus Akkadian. The wedge shapes are the same but the combinations differ. The clay tablet smells the same. The reed is the same reed. But the words are different, and the hand must learn a second set of muscle memories. This is the bilingual synthesis as a physical practice, not an intellectual concept.

## Missing Sensory Anchors

The march south (Section I revision) needs: heat, dust, the flat horizon, irrigation canals cutting the landscape, date palms along the riverbanks, the brown water of the rivers in the campaigning season.

The landing (Section VI) needs: the aging body of a man who has been campaigning for decades. Sargon at the end of fifty-five years of rule. The weight of the name on a body that is no longer young.

## Recommendation

The sensory-analytical fusion principle (v1.4) should operate in every section. The cedar in the marketplace IS the evidence of imperial reach. The governor's discomfort IS the structural engine. The scribe's muscle memory IS the bilingual synthesis. Do not separate the sensory from the analytical. They are the same thing.
