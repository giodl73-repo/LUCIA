# Ashoka's Conquest and Turn

## I. The Edict

This is what Devanampiya Piyadassi, Beloved of the Gods, says concerning Kalinga.

One hundred and fifty thousand persons were carried away captive. One hundred thousand were slain. Many times that number died. The Beloved of the Gods felt remorse, for the conquest of a country that had not been conquered before involves the slaughter, death, and carrying away captive of the people. That is a matter of profound sorrow and regret to the Beloved of the Gods.

He had these words cut into the rock face at Dhauli, on a low laterite hill where the air smelled of wet earth and the Daya River ran brown through rice fields toward the coast. The stone was grainy, pale, warm under the hand in the afternoon heat, the kind that held a chisel mark cleanly if the mason's hand was steady. The masons worked in the local script, Brahmi in some places, Kharosthi in others, because the king wanted the words understood by the people who would stand before this rock and read them aloud to those who could not read. Not Sanskrit. Not the court language. The language of the province, the language of the conquered.

The tradition of rajashasana, the royal decree carved in stone, was older than the Maurya. Kings had cut their victories into rock faces and pillar surfaces for generations, boasting of enemies trampled and cities taken, listing the tribute they had extracted and the lands they had seized. The grammar of Indian royal inscription was a grammar of power: I came, I conquered, I am great. Ashoka carved his grief.

The year was approximately the eighth of his reign, perhaps 260 BCE if the reckoning holds. He had been king of the Maurya dominion for less than a decade. His grandfather Chandragupta had assembled the empire from the wreckage of the Nanda dynasty and the retreat of Alexander's successors, seizing Pataliputra and then pressing outward until the Maurya frontier ran from Gandhara in the northwest to the edge of Tamil country in the south. His father Bindusara had pushed the borders further. Ashoka inherited an empire that stretched from the Hindu Kush to the Bay of Bengal, administered from a capital the Greek ambassador Megasthenes had described in careful detail: wooden palisades, moats stocked with fish, a palace whose pillars were gilded and whose gardens held peacocks and tame parrots. Pataliputra sat on the Ganga, and the Ganga was the spine of the kingdom.

Kalinga lay on the eastern coast, below the mouth of the Mahanadi. It had not submitted to the Maurya. It was the gap in the map, the territory that Chandragupta had not taken and Bindusara had not reached, and Ashoka marched on it with the full weight of the imperial army. The Arthashastra, the treatise on statecraft attributed to his grandfather's minister Kautilya, laid out the logic of such campaigns with a physician's detachment: the vijigishu, the king who desires to conquer, must assess the enemy's treasury, army, terrain, and alliances, and strike when the balance favors him. The balance favored Ashoka. Kalinga fell.

The cost was what he carved into the rock. One hundred and fifty thousand carried away. One hundred thousand killed. And then the line that no treatise on statecraft would have included: many times that number died. The number that exceeds the count. The dead who were not soldiers, who were not carried away, who simply died because a war had come to the place where they lived. Ashoka counted them. Or rather, Ashoka acknowledged that they exceeded counting, and he carved that acknowledgment into stone, and the stone stood on the hill above the river, and the river ran to the sea, and the words remained.

## II. The War

The Maurya army moved in four divisions, the chaturanga that the Arthashastra described as the body of the king's power: elephants, chariots, cavalry, infantry. The elephants went first on open ground, enormous shapes in the dust, their mahouts seated behind the ears with iron-tipped goads. Behind them the chariots, heavy-axled, drawn by horses bred in the northwestern provinces where the grass was thick enough to raise them. The cavalry on the flanks. The infantry behind, carrying spears, bows, swords, and the long bamboo shields that the monsoon climate made more practical than metal. The column stretched for miles. The supply train, the camp followers, the carts of rice and dried pulse, the smiths, the physicians, the scribes who recorded rations and losses alike. When the army moved, the road was the army, and nothing else passed in either direction.

The route from Pataliputra to Kalinga ran southeast, out of the humid river plain where the Ganga's heat pressed on the skin like wet cloth, across the broken country of the Chotanagpur plateau where the forest grew thick with sal and teak and the rivers were shallow enough to ford in the dry season, and down through the passes to the coastal lowlands where the monsoon air carried the smell of salt and paddy and the light changed, becoming whiter, sharper, the light of a coast. The army's passage through the plateau country would have taken weeks. The elephants moved slowly on inclines. The chariots required roads or cleared ground. The baggage train stretched behind the fighting column like a second army, slower and louder, the creak of wooden wheels and the lowing of draft cattle audible for a distance on still mornings.

Kalinga's people farmed rice. They fished the rivers and the coast. They wove cloth. They traded along the Bay of Bengal with the peoples of Southeast Asia, their ships carrying goods that the Arthashastra's mandala theory would have classified as a strategic asset in hostile hands. In the mandala system, the kingdom beyond the border was the ari, the natural enemy. The kingdom beyond the ari was the mitra, the natural friend. Kalinga was the ari. The mandala required its subjugation.

What the battle looked like, the sources do not say. The edict gives numbers, not narrative. The Arthashastra gives tactical doctrine: the elephant line breaks the enemy's formation; the cavalry encircles; the infantry holds. The elephants wore leather armor and carried archers in wooden howdahs, and the sound of a war elephant charging is a sound the Arthashastra does not describe but that anyone who has stood near an elephant in motion can imagine: the ground shaking, the trumpet blast that carries over every other noise on the field, the panicked scattering of men and horses in the animal's path.

Dhauli's rock edict stands on a hill that looks down on the Daya River, and the river's floodplain is the kind of flat, open ground where a four-fold army would deploy. Whether the battle was fought there, or on another field along the river, or in stages across a campaign of weeks, no surviving text records. The Kalingans had their own army. They had resisted the Nanda dynasty before the Maurya, and they had kept their independence through the reigns of Chandragupta and Bindusara. They were not a small people surprised by a large army. They fought. The edict's numbers are the measure of their resistance: a hundred thousand killed, a hundred and fifty thousand deported. These are the numbers of a war, not a surrender.

What survives is the number. The dead, the deported, the uncounted remainder. And the landscape that held them: the Daya flowing past the hill, the rice fields stretching to the coast, the laterite rock face that would, within a few years, hold the king's own account of what his victory had cost.

The Separate Kalinga Rock Edict, carved at Dhauli and at Jaugada further south, addressed the conquered directly. "Beloved of the Gods commands thus: all men are my children. As for my own children I desire that they may be provided with all the welfare and happiness of this world and of the next, so do I desire for all men." The paternalism is exact. They were not his subjects. They were his children. The word carries both affection and ownership, and the edict does not distinguish between the two, because in the Maurya understanding of sovereignty, the distinction did not exist. The chakravartin's subjects were his dependents the way children were a father's dependents: cared for, provided for, and not consulted.

The edict continued with a direct instruction to the provincial officers of Kalinga: they were to govern justly, to avoid unnecessary imprisonment and torture, to treat all classes with fairness. The Beloved of the Gods wanted the Kalingans to know that he was their father and that they should trust him as they would trust a father, and that he desired their welfare. The words were carved into the rock above the river, and the Kalingans who climbed the hill to read them or who stood at the base while an officer read them aloud were hearing their conqueror explain that his conquest was for their benefit.

Whether they believed this, the stone does not record.

## III. The Turn

Rock Edict 13, the Kalinga edict, is the longest and most personal of Ashoka's major rock inscriptions. It describes the conquest. It gives the numbers. And then it turns.

"Beloved of the Gods felt remorse on account of the conquest of Kalinga. For the conquest of a country that had not been conquered before involves the slaughter, death, and carrying away captive of the people. That is a matter of profound sorrow and regret to the Beloved of the Gods."

The edict then explains what the sorrow produced. The Beloved of the Gods now practices dhamma, promotes dhamma, and teaches dhamma. He considers the moral instruction of all peoples to be the highest duty of a king. If he had not conquered Kalinga, he would not have felt remorse. If he had not felt remorse, he would not have turned to dhamma. The edict contains its own causal chain, and the chain is public. The king is explaining, on stone, to anyone who reads, why he changed.

The edict does something else. It counts the cost beyond the battlefield. Among those who were not killed or carried away, the edict says, the suffering extends to their relatives: the Brahmins and shramanas who lost their pupils, the householders who lost their friends, the neighbors who lost their neighbors. The violence done to those who were themselves "well-behaved" and "devoted" and "obedient to their teachers." The edict is counting the concentric rings of damage that spread outward from the dead, the way a stone dropped in water sends rings to the shore. The Arthashastra could calculate the cost of a campaign in treasury units. The edict calculates the cost in grief.

The Buddhist textual tradition told a sharper story. The Ashokavadana and the Sri Lankan chronicles described a man who had come to power through violence, who had killed ninety-nine of his brothers to secure the throne, who was known as Chandashoka, Ashoka the Fierce, before his transformation into Dhammashoka, Ashoka the Righteous. These are later texts, composed centuries after Ashoka's death, and their dramatization of the turn serves the narrative needs of Buddhist hagiography. The edicts themselves say nothing about fratricide. They say nothing about fierceness. They are a king's public account of his own moral transformation, and they are composed with the care of a man who knows his words will be read by millions across the subcontinent.

What happened inside Ashoka between the battle and the edict, no source can say. Minor Rock Edict 1, found at sites across the empire, states that the king had been an upasaka, a Buddhist layman, for more than two years but had not been zealous, and then for more than a year he had drawn closer to the sangha and become more zealous. The timeline does not match a sudden conversion on the battlefield. Ashoka was already a Buddhist before Kalinga. What Kalinga produced was not faith but urgency: the transformation of a private affiliation into a public policy.

Three explanations coexist, and the record does not compel a choice among them.

The first is religious: Ashoka saw the dead, felt the weight of what his kingship had cost, and turned to the Buddha's teaching on suffering with a convert's intensity. This is the explanation the Buddhist tradition preferred.

The second is structural: the Maurya empire in its third generation governed peoples of a dozen languages and many religions. The Arthashastra's coercive model worked for a compact state built on conquest. At subcontinental scale, coercion alone was insufficient. Dhamma offered a universal ethic that transcended sectarian boundaries, a moral ligature for a diverse empire that the Arthashastra's spies and punishments could not provide.

The third is personal: Ashoka's path to the throne, whatever its details, placed him in a dynasty whose founding violence he had inherited and extended. Kalinga was not his first encounter with death. It was the encounter he chose to make public.

The edicts do not choose among these explanations. They present a king who governs differently now.

What is certain is the sequence. After Kalinga, Ashoka did not conquer again. The Maurya army remained intact. The provinces remained under Maurya administration. The revenue system continued to function. The spies continued to operate. But no new campaign was launched. For nearly thirty years after Kalinga, the largest military power in the subcontinent did not use its military power for expansion. The army stood. The king carved stone.

Rock Edict 13 addresses this directly. It notes that even now, when the Beloved of the Gods has conquered Kalinga, he still feels remorse. And if anyone were to do him wrong, he would bear what can be borne. He tells the forest peoples on the borders of his empire that they should not be afraid of him. He wants them to know that he desires their welfare, not their submission. The language is paternalist, as always. But the content is unprecedented: a king at the height of his power, commanding the largest army on the subcontinent, telling the peoples he has not yet conquered that he does not intend to conquer them. The edict addresses the Yonas, the Kambojas, the Nabhakas and Nabhapanktis, the Bhojas and Pitinikas, the Andhras and Paulindas. It names them. It speaks to them. It promises them that the sword has been put away.

## IV. The Policy

Dhamma was not a religion. Ashoka was careful about this, and the edicts are explicit. Rock Edict 12 declares that the king honors all sects and religions and that the essential thing is not the form of worship but the substance of conduct: self-control, purity of mind, gratitude, and faithfulness. Rock Edict 9 states that the ceremonies people perform for marriage and childbirth and illness are largely empty. The true ceremony is the practice of dhamma: proper treatment of slaves and servants, respect for teachers, restraint toward living beings, generosity to friends and relatives and to Brahmins and shramanas.

The content of dhamma, as the edicts define it, was specific. Non-violence toward living beings. Obedience to parents. Respect for teachers and elders. Proper treatment of Brahmins and shramanas. Generosity to friends, relatives, and the poor. Restraint in speech. Moderation in expenditure. These were not abstractions. Rock Edict 1 banned animal sacrifice in the capital and restricted the killing of animals for food. Where the royal kitchen had once slaughtered hundreds of thousands of animals daily for curry, the edict declared, now only three animals were killed: two peacocks and one deer, and the deer not invariably. The specificity is the point. This is not a philosophical treatise. It is a king counting carcasses and reducing the count.

Rock Edict 2 established medical facilities for humans and animals alike, listed the medicinal herbs that had been imported and planted where they did not grow naturally, and ordered the digging of wells and the planting of banyan trees along the roads for shade. The roads of the Maurya empire already ran from Pataliputra to the frontier provinces, built and maintained under the Arthashastra's administrative system. Ashoka did not build new roads. He planted trees beside the existing ones, dug wells at regular intervals, and established rest houses so that travelers could sleep under a roof. The infrastructure of conquest became the infrastructure of care. The road that had carried the army to Kalinga now carried shade trees and water stations.

Rock Edict 5 created the dhamma-mahamatras.

The dhamma-mahamatras were the institutional innovation. The Arthashastra had established a network of officers for revenue, for justice, for military administration, for espionage. Ashoka added a new class with no precedent: officers whose duty was the propagation of dhamma. They worked among all sects. They supervised charitable activities. They visited prisoners, investigated whether men were held unjustly, and arranged for the release of those who had dependents or had been falsely imprisoned. They ensured that dhamma was practiced not only in the capital but in the border territories and among the Yonas (Greeks), Kambojas, and Gandharans of the northwest. The edicts name these peoples. The officers went to them. Dhamma was not a proclamation from the center. It was a system with officers, duties, and a reach that matched the empire's own.

The edict network was the system's infrastructure. Ashoka carved his edicts on rock faces at the edges of his empire and on polished sandstone pillars in the heartland. The rock edicts at Shahbazgarhi and Mansehra in the northwest were carved in Kharosthi, the script of Gandhara. The rock edicts at Girnar in Gujarat were carved in Brahmi on a massive granite boulder beside a lake, where travelers and pilgrims would pass. At Kandahar, on the empire's western frontier, an edict was carved in Greek and Aramaic, the languages of the Hellenistic and Iranian populations who lived there. The king spoke to each community in its own script and its own tongue. This was not tolerance as a passive virtue. It was communication as a deliberate strategy. Every edict site was chosen for visibility: a hill above a road, a rock beside a river crossing, a pillar at a crossroads.

Pillar Edict 7, the last and most comprehensive, reviews the entire reign. The Beloved of the Gods has worked for the welfare of the whole world. He has planted medicinal herbs for humans and animals. He has dug wells. He has planted trees. He has appointed dhamma-mahamatras. And all of this, the edict says, has been done so that people may practice dhamma. The tone is not boastful. It is enumerative, the voice of an accountant who has been tracking his king's moral expenditures the way the Arthashastra's officers tracked revenue. The dhamma had its own ledger. The ledger was stone.

The pillars were the most ambitious. Quarried from the Chunar sandstone deposits near the Ganga, polished to a sheen so fine that early British observers believed them to be metal, transported across distances of hundreds of miles, and raised on sites from Lumbini (the Buddha's birthplace) to Rampurva in northern Bihar. The Sarnath pillar bore a capital of four lions seated back to back on a lotus, above a frieze of an elephant, a horse, a bull, and a lion separated by wheels. The capital weighed some twenty-five tons. It was carried from the quarry to Sarnath, raised on a shaft that stood over twelve meters high, and polished until the sandstone caught the light. The four lions faced the four directions. The wheel between them was the dhammachakka, the wheel of dharma. The capital is now the emblem of the Republic of India. The wheel appears on the Indian flag. The stone that Ashoka raised is still doing its work.

## V. The Stone

The edicts are objects in landscapes.

At Dhauli, a carved elephant emerges from the rock above the inscription, its body round, its trunk curled, the first known example of Indian stone sculpture in the round. The elephant faces the road. Below it, the edict text runs in horizontal lines across the rock face, the letters cut deep enough to be legible from a distance on a clear day. The hill is low, the river below it wide and slow, and the rice paddies stretch to the horizon in every direction. A farmer walking the levees of the Daya River could look up at the hill and see the elephant and know that the king's words were there, even if he could not read them, because someone would be sent to read them to him.

At Girnar in Gujarat, the edict is carved on a single enormous rock beside a sacred lake at the base of a mountain. The pilgrims who climbed the mountain to worship at the Jain and Hindu shrines passed the rock on their way up. The stone is granite, dark, harder to carve than the sandstone of the Gangetic plain. The letters at Girnar are cut with a different hand than the letters at Dhauli: sharper, narrower, adapted to the resistance of the stone. The edict text is the same. The rock is different. The mason adjusted.

At Kandahar, the fragment of a bilingual edict in Greek and Aramaic survives on a broken slab. The Greek translates Ashoka's concepts into Hellenistic philosophical vocabulary: eusebeia for dhamma, piety for right conduct. The translator was working at the edge of two intellectual worlds, finding equivalences where none were exact. The Greek communities of Gandhara and Arachosia could read the edict in the language of Alexander's successors and understand, approximately, what the Maurya king was asking of them. The approximation itself was part of the policy. Dhamma did not require agreement on terminology. It required agreement on conduct.

At Lumbini, a pillar marked the birthplace of the Buddha. Ashoka had visited the site personally, the edict records, twenty years after his consecration. He had walked to a village in the Tarai foothills where the sal trees grew and the Himalayan foothills were visible to the north, and he had stood at the place where Siddhartha Gautama had been born, and he had reduced the village's tax burden to one-eighth and exempted it from the bali, the customary tribute. A king who controlled the largest state in the subcontinent used his power to lighten a village's burden because of what had happened there two centuries before. The pillar recorded the act. The act was the policy. The policy was the man.

The edicts were not silent objects. They were meant to be read aloud. Rock Edict 4 instructs that the dhamma-mahamatras shall cause the edicts to be read aloud at regular intervals. The voice of the dhamma-mahamatra at a provincial crossroads, reading Prakrit from a polished pillar to a crowd of farmers and merchants and travelers who had stopped to listen, was the sound of the state speaking to its people in a voice they could understand. The words were not law, exactly. They were instruction, exhortation, the voice of a father who has learned something and wishes to share it. The gathered crowd heard the king's own phrasing, repeated from memory or read from the stone, and each person carried some fragment of it away, into the fields, along the road, into the next village.

Across the empire, the edicts accumulated. Rock Edict 4 noted that for centuries the killing of living beings, ill-treatment of relatives, and disrespect to Brahmins and shramanas had increased, but now, through the Beloved of the Gods' practice of dhamma, the sound of war drums had been replaced by the sound of dhamma. This is the edict's own phrase: dhammaghosa replacing bherighosa. The sound of the dharma replacing the sound of the war drum. It is a claim about the physical world. Where you once heard the drum, now you hear the teaching. The stone carries the sound.

## VI. What the Stone Holds

Ashoka ruled for approximately thirty-seven years. He died around 232 BCE. Within fifty years of his death, the Maurya empire had fragmented. Pushyamitra Shunga, a general, killed the last Maurya king and established his own dynasty. The provinces pulled away. The roads fell into disrepair. The dhamma-mahamatras disappeared from the record. The great administrative system that the Arthashastra had described and that Ashoka had repurposed for moral governance dissolved into the regional kingdoms that would divide the subcontinent for the next millennium and a half.

The pillars stood.

The edicts stood. Buried under silt in some places, exposed to wind in others, their Brahmi letters forgotten as the script fell out of use and the languages shifted and the centuries piled over them like the alluvial mud of the Gangetic plain. For two thousand years no one alive could read what Ashoka had written. The pillars were objects: tall, polished, beautiful, mysterious. Local traditions attached legends to them. Sultan Firoz Shah Tughlaq moved two Ashoka pillars to Delhi in the fourteenth century, raising them as curiosities in his new buildings, unable to read a word of what they said.

In 1837, James Prinsep, an assay master at the Calcutta Mint, stared at copies of the inscriptions that had been gathered from pillars and rock faces across the subcontinent. He had been working on the script for years. He knew it was not Sanskrit. He did not know what language it was. He worked letter by letter, cross-referencing the characters against every known Indian script, until the Brahmi alphabet opened and the words came through, and he read the phrase Devanampiya Piyadassi and did not yet know it meant Ashoka, but he could read the stone for the first time since the last mason had set down his chisel.

The empire was gone. The officers were gone. The roads were overgrown. The policy of dhamma had not survived the dynasty that proclaimed it. But the stone was doing now what it had done then: holding the words of a king who had wanted them held. The Arthashastra had built a state designed to perpetuate itself through institutions, through revenue, through the rational application of power. That state lasted three generations. The edicts, which asked for nothing except to be read, lasted twenty-three centuries. The vulnerability carved into stone proved more durable than the power that had carved it.

And on the stone, in the language of the province, in letters cut deep enough to last, a king spoke to his people about what he had done and what he wished he had not done and what he intended to do differently, and the stone held the words the way stone holds everything: without opinion, without forgiveness, without forgetting.

The edicts stand in the open air across the subcontinent, from the dry schist of the northwest to the wet laterite of the eastern coast. The rain falls on them. The sun heats them. Farmers pass them on the way to their fields. The words are legible again. They say what they have always said: I did this, and I grieve, and I will govern differently now. The stone does not say whether the grief was enough. It holds the words. It has always held the words. It will hold them after everything else that Ashoka built is gone.
