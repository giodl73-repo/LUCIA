# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of "The High Wagadu" Opening

**Chapter:** Soninke / Era 3: The High Wagadu
**Round:** 1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## First Read — Where My Attention Went

The opening line caught me: "The virgin walked north, toward the well at the edge of the desert, because the covenant required her." I wanted to follow that woman. I wanted to see what happened at the well. Good. That is what an opening line should do.

But then the opening pivots away from her. She never comes back. She is a mechanism, not a person, and the reader who was following her finds themselves instead following an argument about political economy. The argument is excellent. The twin-city structure, the intermediary logic, the covenant-as-contract framing — all of it is intellectually satisfying and genuinely illuminating. But the person who drew me in has vanished.

## What Works

The Al-Bakri passage is superbly handled. The ten horses with gold-plated harness, the guard dogs in gold and silver collars, the drum hollowed from a single trunk — these are the kinds of details that make a reader lean forward. I can see the court. I can hear the drum. The decision to let Al-Bakri's informants provide the physical reality while the Soninke understanding provides the interpretive framework is exactly right.

The closing paragraph has genuine rhythm: "The covenant held. The virgin walked. The gold moved." This is prose that knows its own music. The landing of the opening is strong.

The structural insight — that the six miles between the two towns is itself the architecture of the state — is the kind of observation that earns the reader's trust. It is an idea that rewards attention.

## What Needs Work

**The virgin.** You opened with her. You owe her a story. Not necessarily a name (the record may not provide one), but a fate. What happened at the well? What did the procession look like? What did the people do as she walked past? You created a hook and then abandoned it. Either commit to the scene or find a different opening.

**The analytical middle.** Paragraphs 3-5 (from "This was not myth told to children" through "the Soninke of Wagadu, who controlled neither the salt nor the gold") are intellectually strong but narratively static. The reader is receiving an explanation. For five paragraphs, no one does anything. The information is essential, but it needs to be embedded in action or scene, not presented as analysis.

**Pacing.** The opening moves from ritual (the virgin) to analysis (the covenant) to scene (Al-Bakri's court) to analysis (twin-city logic) to summary (the landing). The rhythm is: scene-analysis-scene-analysis. I would prefer: scene-scene-analysis-scene, with the analysis earning its place by being shorter and surrounded by vivid moments.

## The Question I'd Push On

Can you find a single moment — a specific day, a specific transaction, a specific person at the crossing point — that embodies the intermediary logic in action? The Mali chapter opened with Sundiata in the dust. This chapter opens with a virgin at a well, which is promising, but then it retreats to explanation. The intermediary economics need a human face. A merchant. A king making a decision. A caravan arriving. Something that lets the reader discover the system through a person, rather than having the system explained and then illustrated.
