# B-3 — Phyllis Martin

## Domain Assessment

**Kongo-Teke Commercial Relationship:** The chapter correctly positions the Kongo-Teke relationship as asymmetric in military power but balanced in commercial leverage. The Manikongo's governors wanting interior goods and having to pay Teke prices is consistent with what the Kongo correspondence record shows — the Kongo kingdom was chronically dependent on interior trade goods that it could not produce or control on its own territory. The chapter's account of how the Teke maintained this position — through the Makoko's spiritual authority and the commercial infrastructure of route knowledge and porter networks — is consistent with the Loango coast parallel, where the interior broker position was maintained through similar mechanisms.

**Coastal Demand and Goods Record:** The goods listed in the chapter — copper ingots, ivory, iron tools, cloth, shell beads — are all consistent with the attested trade goods of the period. Katanga copper cross-shaped ingots (handa) are documented in Central African trade from the sixteenth century. The cloth and shell beads as Kongo payment currency are documented in the Kongo commercial record. The transition to guns as an Atlantic trade good in the seventeenth century is correctly placed in the chapter's chronology (the chapter mentions "guns" in passing in the late Atlantic period).

**Information Flows:** The chapter's account of how Pool market prices propagated back to the plateau — "the coastal rates were something the Pool market reported whenever anyone returned from the Pool" — is consistent with how the information system worked in the Loango coast parallel. Market intelligence moved with the traders; it was embedded in personal relationships and trip reports rather than in formal price lists. The chapter has this right.

**One observation on framing:** The chapter emphasizes the Teke at the expense of the northern parallel — the Loango coast system, which was itself a significant broker in the Atlantic trade running north of the Kongo River mouth. The Teke were not the only Central African brokers managing an interior-to-coast trade; the Loango coastal merchants had their own deep networks into the basin north of the Pool. This is not an error in the chapter, which is focused on the Teke, but it is worth noting in the figures that the Teke operated within a competitive landscape of multiple Central African broker systems, not as a unique or isolated case.
