# Figures — Plenitudo Potestatis

---

## Innocent III (1160/61–1216)

Born Lotario di Conti dei Segni, in the Segni family's castle in Lazio. His father was a count; his mother was a Scotti, connected to the Roman administrative class. He studied theology at the University of Paris under Peter of Poitiers, a noted theologian. He then studied law at Bologna. When he left Bologna, he carried both disciplines simultaneously: the theologian's understanding of why the Church's authority mattered and the lawyer's understanding of how it worked.

He was made a cardinal-deacon by his uncle, Pope Clement III, at twenty-nine. He wrote, while still a cardinal, a treatise called De Miseria Humanae Conditionis — On the Misery of the Human Condition. It is a meditation on the wretchedness of the body, the vanity of earthly goods, the corruption of everything perishable. He planned to follow it with a second work, On the Dignity of Human Nature. He never finished it. The papacy intervened.

He was elected pope on the first ballot, within a day of his predecessor's death, at thirty-seven. This was unusual enough to require noting: the cardinals who elected him knew they were choosing not just a candidate but a program. He brought to the chair of Peter the full apparatus of a Paris-trained theologian and a Bologna-trained lawyer, combined with a practical intelligence that recognized exactly what the papacy's authority required to function.

His correspondence — more than five thousand letters survive — is the record of a man managing an institution at the scale of a continent. He wrote to kings, to bishops, to abbots, to the leaders of crusades. He wrote to Saladin's successors in Egypt about the status of Christian pilgrims. He wrote to the Greek emperor of Byzantium about the prospects for reunion between the Latin and Eastern churches. He wrote to the leaders of the Fourth Crusade after they had sacked Constantinople, telling them what they had done. He wrote to Francis of Assisi, confirming the rule of a group of poor friars who wanted to live without property.

He did not survive his greatest achievement by more than a year. He died in Perugia in July 1216, on a road he had traveled many times before. The July heat in Umbria is specific and merciless. Whether he knew he was dying is not recorded. The chronicles note his death and proceed to the election of his successor.

## Gratian (fl. c. 1140)

Almost nothing is known about Gratian as a person. He was probably a monk, possibly a Camaldolese, working in Bologna in the first half of the twelfth century. His name appears in later manuscripts but not in contemporary documents. What is certain is that he produced, around 1140, the text called the Decretum.

The Decretum organized approximately 3,800 canonical texts — councils, papal letters, patristic writings — into a coherent legal code by a method that became the foundation of both canon law and, eventually, secular legal education across Europe. The method was argument-by-distinction: when two authorities seemed to conflict, Gratian identified the question each was actually answering, showed that they were answering different questions, and derived the rule that governed each type of case.

He was not performing sophistry. The canons did genuinely conflict, because they had been issued in different centuries, in different contexts, by different authorities with different concerns. Gratian's contribution was to treat this multiplicity as a system rather than a contradiction. The system had rules. The rules could be learned. A student who mastered the Decretum could reason about any novel case by applying the established distinctions to the new facts.

He probably did not work alone. The Decretum as it survived in the twelfth century shows signs of multiple hands, additions, revisions. What Gratian produced was more likely a teaching tradition than a finished text — the living intellectual process of a law school, captured on parchment. The text that circulated under his name was authoritative enough that subsequent popes issued decretals to fill its gaps rather than replace it.

He did not know what he had made. No one in the Camaldolese monastery, if that is where he sat with his sources, was thinking about the machine that would run on this architecture two generations later.

## Boniface VIII (c. 1230–1303)

Born Benedetto Caetani, in the town of Anagni, in Lazio. He studied law, served as a papal notary, traveled as a legate to France and England. He was a man of his institution: formed by the Curia, expert in its procedures, acutely aware of its political landscape. When he became pope in 1294 — after the brief and extraordinary pontificate of Celestine V, a hermit whom Dante placed in Hell for his "great refusal" — he knew what he was inheriting.

He was combative in ways that made his position more difficult than it needed to be. He feuded with the Colonna cardinals, issuing a crusade against them in central Italy. He feuded with Philip IV of France in terms that gave Philip the legal and political material he needed to construct a case against him. He chose the most extreme available formulation when a more temperate one might have made the same point with less vulnerability. The Unam Sanctam did not say anything that had not been said before. It said it with a finality that made retreat impossible.

He was not wrong about the doctrine. He was wrong about the political situation in which he was asserting it. The papacy's legitimacy authority had always depended on secular powers finding it more convenient to accommodate papal claims than to refuse them. Philip IV had calculated that refusal, backed by sufficient political strength, was more convenient. Boniface had no instrument for a calculation of that kind.

He died in Rome on October 11, 1303, thirty-five days after the soldiers left Anagni. The chronicles say his mind failed toward the end — that he raged and wept and could not be comforted. Whether this is accurate or legend designed to make a moral point about the limits of pride is not recoverable. What is recoverable: he had stated the fullness of power in its most complete form, and he had been held in a room for two days by soldiers, and then he had gone home and died.

The Unam Sanctam was never revoked.

## A Parish Priest in Northern France, c. 1216

He has no name in the sources. He was probably literate — the Fourth Lateran canons required clergy to be educated — though the quality of his Latin was likely serviceable rather than elegant. He had probably served his parish for years before 1215. He knew his parishioners' faces, their quarrels, their harvests, their deaths.

After 1215, he knew something else. Every adult in his parish — everyone who had reached the age of reason — was required by canon law to confess their sins to him at least once a year and receive communion at Easter. He was required to keep those confessions absolutely secret. He was required to impose penance appropriate to the sin. He was prohibited from revealing what he had heard even if his own life depended on it — a rule the Lateran canon stated explicitly, with the penalty of permanent deposition from office and confinement in a strict monastery for any priest who violated it.

The annual confession requirement created something that had not existed before in the same form: a formal, legally mandated, regularized pastoral relationship between every Christian soul and their priest. The priest was now the point at which the entire edifice of canonical theology — sin, penance, absolution, the saving function of the sacraments — made contact with an individual human life. Every year. Without exception.

He heard what people had done. He kept it secret. He gave them something to do in return. He did this until he died, and when he died, his successor did it too. The canon did not require him to be good at it, or wise, or kind. It required him to do it.

This is the fullness of power reaching its actual extent.
