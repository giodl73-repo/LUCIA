# The Amarna Interlude

The stonecutter's name is not recorded. His chisel is.

It was a copper blade, half a forearm in length, re-hammered so many times the edge had thinned to a wafer where it met the stone. He had used it on the columns of Ipet-Isut for eleven years, cutting the signs that the master draftsmen painted in red ink on the dressed limestone: the reed, the quail chick, the seated god, the water line. He was not a scribe. He could not read the signs he carved. But he knew their shapes the way a weaver knows thread, by feel, by repetition, by the particular resistance each curve offered to the copper edge.

Now he had been ordered to destroy them.

The decree had come from Per-Aa, from the king who had changed his own name. The king who had been Amenhotep, Amun-is-Satisfied, was now Akhenaten, Effective-for-the-Aten. The god whose satisfaction had named him was to be removed from every wall, every column, every lintel in the Two Lands. The name of Amun. The seated figure. The ram's horns, the tall double plumes. Wherever the god appeared in stone, the stonecutters were to hack the signs away, leaving blank scars on walls that had stood for generations.

The order was not symbolic. A name written in stone was not a record. It was a presence. The hieroglyphs on the temple walls were not descriptions of the gods. They were the gods, held in their material form, fed by the daily ritual in the sanctuary. To cut the name of Amun from the stone was to cut Amun from the world. Not to deny that Amun had existed. To make Amun cease to exist. The chisel was a tool of annihilation.

The stonecutter stood in the hypostyle hall where the morning light fell in angled shafts between columns taller than any tree in Egypt, and the stone was already warm under his feet, and the air smelled of incense and dust. He held his chisel above signs he knew by shape but not by sound. He had carved signs like these. He had never destroyed them. The work of a stonecutter was to make permanent. The stone held the names, and the names held the gods, and the gods held the order of the world. The flood came because ma'at held. Ma'at held because the rituals were performed. The rituals were performed because the names were on the walls. He knew this the way he knew that copper cuts limestone and granite breaks copper, because the world was built on certainties that did not require explanation.

But the pharaoh's order was the pharaoh's order. Per-Aa was the living Horus. His word was ma'at made audible. If the king said Amun's name must go, then the removal of Amun's name was itself the maintenance of order, because the king could not command disorder. What the king commanded was, by definition, what the gods required.

Unless the king was wrong. A stonecutter did not think that thought. A stonecutter placed the chisel's edge against the stone and struck.

The sound carried through the hypostyle hall. Copper on stone sounds the same whether you are carving a name or removing one. Only the direction of the work has changed. The chips that fell were the same pale limestone dust that fell when the signs were first cut, and the dust settled on the floor of the temple of the god who was, if the king was right, already ceasing to be.

Akhenaten had not come to Ipet-Isut to watch. He was two hundred miles north, in the city he was building on clean ground where no god had ever been worshipped, where the only name on the walls would be the Aten, the visible sun disk whose rays ended in hands that held the ankh and extended it only to the king and his queen. The city was called Akhetaten, the Horizon of the Aten. It rose from the desert between the cliffs and the river, built in five years, a royal capital that would last less than twenty.

The stonecutter did not know that. He knew the chisel, the stone, the order. He struck, and a piece of Amun fell to the floor.

What fell with it was the certainty that the cosmic order had one shape and one direction. For a thousand years the temples had held the names of many gods, and the many gods had sustained the many functions of the world: Amun in his sanctuary, Ra in his solar barque, Osiris in the underworld, Hathor with her mirror, Thoth with his stylus, each one a load-bearing pillar in the architecture of ma'at. Akhenaten looked at that architecture and saw not balance but clutter. One god. One sun. The rest was noise.

The stonecutter's chisel did not require his agreement. It required only his hand, and his hand obeyed the king, because the king was the one certainty that remained when all the other certainties were being chiseled from the walls.

He struck. The stone gave. The dust fell. And the Two Lands held their breath.
