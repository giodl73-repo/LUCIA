# Figures — The First Atlantic Contact

## The Burba Jolof

The earliest Portuguese accounts do not name the specific Burba Jolof who received word of their arrival in the 1440s. The gewel tradition preserves the office and its lineage without always fixing individual names to specific decades in the way that a written chronicle would. What the tradition preserves is the function: the Burba Jolof was the paramount chief of the six-state Jolof Confederacy, the figure whose authority was sustained by the mediation of trade, the adjudication of disputes between the buur, and the ceremonial weight of the founding agreements.

He governed from the inland heartland, in a receiving compound built of mud-brick and thatch where the baobabs gave shade in the dry season and the rains turned the laterite to red mud. The gewel who served his court sang the genealogy that connected his rule to the founding of the confederacy, and this genealogy was his legitimacy, renewed in each performance.

When the Portuguese ships arrived at the river mouth, the Burba Jolof responded as a sovereign managing a new variable in an existing system. He sent men to assess. He sent ambassadors to negotiate. He attempted to maintain his paramount authority over a confederacy whose coastal states were acquiring independent power. That he failed was not a failure of intelligence but a failure of structure: his authority rested on mediation, and mediation loses its value when the parties find direct channels. The Burba Jolof was the last figure of a political form that the Atlantic made obsolete, and the gewel carry his title with the dignity owed to a man who held what could be held for as long as it could be held.

## Bemoim (Bumi Jeleen)

He was a prince of the Jolof lineage who lost a succession struggle and went looking for leverage. The Portuguese sources call him Bemoim or Bemoi. Boulegue identifies him with the Wolof name Bumi Jeleen. He was a political man in a political crisis, and his solution was political: seek an alliance with the new power on the coast, use their soldiers to reclaim what he had lost.

He sailed to Lisbon in 1488. He accepted baptism with King Joao II as his godfather. He wore Portuguese clothing and learned Portuguese protocols and spent months in a stone city where the cold seeped through walls he had never seen the like of. He did all of this because he needed an army, and the Portuguese sold armies the way the Saharan merchants sold horses: with conditions attached.

Joao II gave him twenty caravels and soldiers. Bemoim sailed back toward the Senegal. Before he reached the Jolof heartland, the Portuguese commander Pero Vaz da Cunha killed him. The Portuguese chronicle says Pero Vaz suspected treachery. Whether this was true, whether it was pretext, whether Pero Vaz simply decided that a living Wolof prince was less useful than a dead one, the chronicle does not resolve. What it records is that the fleet withdrew and the military intervention never happened.

The gewel remembered Bemoim as a man who borrowed power from a source he could not control. He was not a fool. He was a calculator who ran out of variables. The power he sought was real; the people who offered it had their own calculations, and when those calculations changed, Bemoim was expendable. His story is the chapter's clearest warning: the Atlantic trade offered tools, but the people who made the tools retained the right to take them back.

## The Buur of Cayor

Like the Burba Jolof, the specific buur of Cayor who presided over the early decades of Portuguese trade is not named with certainty in the earliest sources. He enters the narrative as a position: the ruler of the coastal state best situated to benefit from the Atlantic trade's new axis.

His compound sat on the lowland plain between the coast and the sandy hills of the interior, close enough to the Atlantic that the surf was a constant sound. His ceddo warriors rode horses that arrived by ship. His traders dealt directly with the Portuguese factors at the coastal posts. His gewel sang the names with a new emphasis: not the Burba Jolof's paramount authority but the buur of Cayor's independent strength.

When Cayor broke from the confederacy around 1549, it was not an act of rebellion so much as a formalization of what had already become true. The buur of Cayor did not need the Burba Jolof's mediation. He had the coast, the Portuguese, the horses, and the cavalry they sustained. The new Cayor that emerged was the strongest of the independent Wolof states, and the buur who built it understood, in the way a man who has bet on the right horse understands, that the future of Wolof power lay with the ocean.

His successors, the Damel of Cayor, would rule an Atlantic-facing state for the next three centuries, until Lat Dior rode out to fight the French railway in 1886. The lineage that began with a buur on a beach counting horses would end with a warrior on a battlefield counting rifles. The gewel would carry both.

## The Gewel

The gewel is not a figure in the way that a king or a prince is a figure. The gewel is a function, a lineage, a tradition. The Wolof praise-singer and genealogist served noble families as the keeper of political memory: who ruled, who fought, who married, who broke the agreements and who kept them. The gewel's knowledge was his authority, and his authority was real. A noble without a gewel had no history, and a man without a history could not rule.

The gewel tradition is distinct from the Mande jeli tradition, though they share a common origin in the broader West African griot complex. The gewel served Wolof political culture specifically: the songs named the Wolof lineages, the Wolof battles, the Wolof agreements. The instruments were different: the gewel's xalam, a plucked string instrument, is not the Mande kora. The performance context was different. The political role was the same: the living archive that no fire could burn and no army could seize. The chapter names "Njaay Sall" as a gewel of the Burba Jolof's court. The Sall lineage is associated with gewel functions in the Wolof tradition, but the specific name is not firmly attested for this period. The tradition carries lineage names forward across generations, and the name here represents the function as much as the individual.

In this chapter, the gewel is the framing authority. The Portuguese chronicles provide dates and details. The gewel provides the meaning. When the two accounts diverge, the chapter follows the gewel, not because the gewel is always accurate in the historian's sense but because the gewel carries the story as the Wolof understand it, and this chapter tells the Wolof story.

## The Ceddo

The ceddo warriors were the military aristocracy of the Wolof states, the men who rode the horses and carried the lances and defined their identity through service to the buur. They drank millet beer when the serigu preached abstinence. They maintained the old rites when the serigu offered Islam. They organized their lives around the compound, the stable, and the raiding season, and their culture was the culture of men who lived on horseback and measured their worth in what they could take and hold.

The ceddo identity was already old by the time the Portuguese arrived, but the Atlantic trade intensified it. More horses meant more warriors. More warriors meant more raids. More raids meant more captives to trade for more horses. The ceddo were the human engine of the horse-captive cycle, and their status rose as the cycle accelerated.

They were not mindless. A ceddo warrior who rode into a raid understood the economics of his own existence: the horse beneath him had been purchased with someone's freedom, and his ability to ride into the next raid depended on his producing the same currency. This was not a moral crisis for the ceddo. It was the way power worked, and the ceddo understood power in their bones, the way a horseman understands a horse: through the body, through repetition, through the daily practice of an authority that was never permanent and always needed to be renewed.

The conflict between the ceddo's martial aristocratic identity and the spread of Islam would become the defining tension of later Wolof history. In this era, the tension was nascent. The ceddo drank. The serigu prayed. The buur navigated between them with the same diplomatic flexibility the Wolof brought to every negotiation. For now, the horses kept coming, and the ceddo kept riding, and the gewel kept singing their names.
