# La Reconquesta Aragonesa — The Aragonese Reconquest
## opening.md — Stage 1

---

The king was thirty years old and had been at war for most of them.

James stood at the edge of the Aragonese lines outside Valencia in the spring of 1238 and looked at what he had spent nine years approaching. The city rose from the Valencian plain in white stone and flat-roofed towers, crossed by the acequia channels that watered the orchards of orange and mulberry and rice stretching south toward the sea. The walls were long. The gates were closed. Inside, Zayyan ibn Mardanish — the last Almohad governor of Valencia — held a city of fifty thousand souls and knew there would be no relief from the south.

James had first taken the field in this campaign at thirteen, after his regency council had handed him a war he had not chosen. The choosing had been done for him, by a regency council and by circumstance and by the wars that preceded his birth. But now, at the walls, the choosing was his. He had driven Zayyan back from Burriana, back from Peñíscola, back from every position along the road south, and here was the reckoning.

What he knew, standing at the edge of his own lines, was this: he needed Zayyan's people to stay.

Not as prisoners. Not driven out. He needed the farmers in the orchards and the silk merchants in the souks and the engineers who maintained the ancient Roman and Arab acequia system to remain and work the land, because a Valencia emptied of its people was a Valencia worth nothing — a city of walls and dry irrigation channels and dead orange groves. The Aragonese did not have enough people to replace fifty thousand Moors. His kingdom's economy ran on Moorish agricultural knowledge and Catalan commercial intelligence, and the deal he was about to offer was not charity. It was calculation.

The *Llibre dels Fets* — the Book of Deeds he would dictate in Catalan decades later, the king's own account of his own reign — records the negotiations with the measured pride of a man who considered himself a good bargainer. Zayyan sent emissaries. James received them. The terms that emerged were what his advisors called *capitulació*: the governor and his household and those who chose to leave would go free, with safe conduct to the sea. Those who chose to remain on their land under Aragonese rule would remain as *mudéjars* — Muslims living under Christian law, protected in their mosques and their faith and their customary courts, paying their rents, farming their orchards.

It was, by the logic of the Aragonese crown, an arrangement that worked. The king's obligations ran not only upward to God and to the demands of *creuada*, crusade — they ran sideways, through a web of compacts and covenants that the Aragonese had been building since before James was born. The *Corts* of Aragon and Catalonia had established the terms under which kings could demand service and taxation: with consent, within the laws, under the watching eye of the *Justicia* of Aragon, the great judicial officer who held the extraordinary power of restraining the king himself when the king exceeded the compact. James had lived his whole reign inside this web. It shaped him.

His grandfather had called the arrangement — half mockingly, half as article of faith — the old Aragonese truth: *We who are as good as you swear to you who are no better than us to accept you as our king and sovereign lord, provided you observe all our liberties and laws — and if not, not.*

The *and if not, not* was not empty. James knew it was not empty because his own regency had nearly ended in a faction war that the *Corts* had had to restrain. The covenant ran in both directions. You could not be king of Aragon without understanding this.

So when he looked at Valencia's walls and thought about what kind of king he wanted to be on the other side of them, he thought not in the language of conquest — not in the clean grammar of *we have won and they have lost* — but in the language of arrangement. Of compact. What did Valencia need to be worth keeping? What did the people inside those walls need to believe about the man outside them?

September came. Zayyan opened the gates. James rode in.

The *Llibre dels Fets* says he wept when he saw the city — that he dismounted at the great mosque and gave thanks, and that the tears came before he could stop them, not from piety alone but from the long weight of nine years in the field, nine years of fourteen-thousand-man armies encamped in the Valencian heat, of negotiating with his own nobles for supplies, of watching men die of fever and crossbow quarrel alike. He was thirty years old. He had been king since he was five.

The city that opened before him smelled of orange blossom and animal smoke and the particular dust of flat-roofed stone streets in late summer heat. It was the richest single prize the Aragonese crown had ever possessed, and it had come to him not purely by the sword but by the logic of compact: that even a conquered people must find in their conqueror something worth trusting, or the conquest would cost more to hold than it had cost to take.

The Valencian kingdom James established that autumn would keep its own laws — the *Furs de València* — its own courts, its own parliament, its own *morería* districts where the Moorish communities would work the acequia systems for three more centuries under Aragonese rule. It was not equality. But it was arrangement. The word the Aragonese used for it was *pactisme*: the government of covenant, the king under law.

James I rode through the gates of Valencia in the high autumn of 1238, and what followed was not the end of the story. It was the shape of it.
