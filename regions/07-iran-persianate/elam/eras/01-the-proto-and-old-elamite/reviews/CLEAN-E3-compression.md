### Finding 1 — The "What It Means" Pair in Opening

**Location:** Opening section, final paragraphs before the closing image.

**Passage:**
> What it meant to them to press these marks: this the tablets do not say, or say in a language we have not yet learned to hear.
>
> What it means that they pressed them at all: that on the Iranian plateau...

**Assessment:** The "What the tablets hold... / What it meant to them... / What it means that they pressed them at all..." triple structure in the opening is an intentional rhetorical device — accumulation, not redundancy. Each "what" does different work: the tablets hold physical evidence; the meaning to the scribes is unknowable; the meaning of the act itself is the claim the chapter makes. This is not re-explanation. It is escalation. Keep.

### Finding 2 — Minor Redundancy Between Section 1 and Section 2

**Location:** Opening section closes with the archive and the tablet waiting. Section 2 ("The God Whose Grain It Was") opens with "The grain was not the scribe's grain, not the king's grain, not the palace's grain. The grain was Inshushinak's grain."

**Assessment:** The transition is clean and the repetition of "grain" is intentional — the opening ends with grain counted and the second section begins with whose grain it was, which is the correct deepening. Not a redundancy. Keep.

### Finding 3 — Slight Over-Explanation in Kassite Paragraph

**Location:** Sukkalmah section, final paragraphs.

**Offending passage:** "This was, paradoxically, a more formidable challenge to the Elamite system than military pressure would have been. A unified, commercially stable Babylonia could access highland goods through routes that bypassed the Elamite redistribution network."

**Assessment:** "This was, paradoxically, a more formidable challenge..." is explaining the reader's expected reaction ("you'd think military pressure would be worse, but actually..."). The chapter can trust the reader to understand that commercial re-routing is a different kind of threat than military assault. The paradox can be implied rather than announced. 

**Recommended fix:** Cut "This was, paradoxically, a more formidable challenge to the Elamite system than military pressure would have been." The paragraph that follows already shows WHY it was formidable without announcing it.

**Revised paragraph opening:** "A unified, commercially stable Babylonia could access highland goods through routes that bypassed the Elamite redistribution network. The engine that had driven Elamite power — control of the exchange between the plain and the plateau — found a competitor that did not need to be defeated militarily because it was being routed around."
