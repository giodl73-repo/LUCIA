## Domain Assessment

The chapter engages with the Atlantic corridor as a structural phenomenon — multiple communities over centuries, not a single migration — and this is correct. The period described (c. 1000–1400 CE) is the era during which the coastal Tupinambá world was being established, and the chapter correctly renders this as a process of gradual settlement and continued southward movement rather than a single arrival event. This aligns with the historical record and with my own analysis of the migration pattern.

**On the social organization:**
The chief's feasting function is accurately rendered. The chief's authority expressed through generosity, the feast as the primary mechanism of social cohesion, the principle that giving away more than you can spare is the demonstration of capacity rather than recklessness — these are all correct. The manioc chicha is correctly identified as manioc-based rather than maize-based (this is a common error that the chapter avoids).

**On the contact-era sources:**
The chapter correctly identifies Jean de Léry as an observer of coastal Tupinambá culture in the 1550s and uses Potirava's account of the ocean-finding tradition in a way consistent with what Léry documents. I would flag one detail: Léry records that the Tupinambá community he visited (near present-day Rio de Janeiro, 1557) had the ocean-finding tradition pointing east and that the community maintained this orientation despite being on the coast for what community members said were "many generations." This is consistent with the chapter's treatment of Potirava knowing the tradition without having resolved the geographic question.

**One correction required — manioc processing accuracy:**
The chapter describes the manioc processing as "scraping, pressing, the flat cakes already forming on the clay griddles." Manioc processing for eating also requires grating and washing to remove the hydrocyanic acid from bitter manioc varieties (which were the primary varieties grown by Tupinambá communities). The scraping and pressing steps described are from later in the process — the grinding and water extraction should precede the griddle work. This is a technical detail but it matters for accuracy: a reader who knows manioc processing would notice the gap. Recommend adding the grating step before "scraping, pressing."

**On the migration timeline (corroborating Clastres's finding):**
My records of karai migration accounts in the Jesuit sources confirm that preparation times for community migrations were measured in weeks to months, not days. The chapter's "three families began preparing that week" is plausible for the initial decision phase, but the community departure follows too quickly. I recommend the same correction as Clastres: expand the preparation period to "the following weeks" or similar.

**On the corridor exchange network:**
The chapter describes the exchange of manioc seeds suited to coastal forest soils and the right to use a fishing weir. I cannot confirm the specific exchange of manioc varieties from inland to coast-adapted strains in this period from any contact-era source; this detail may be authorial inference. It is plausible — manioc variety exchange along movement corridors is documented in other contexts — but should either be noted as inferential or softened slightly to "seeds of cultivated plants suited to the soils ahead." I flag it for the notes.md rather than as a required correction.

## Summary Judgment

Two required corrections: (1) manioc processing sequence (add grating step); (2) preparation timeline (expand to weeks). One flag on the manioc-variety exchange as potentially inferential. Structurally and historically sound. The rendering of the karai migration tradition is the most careful narrative treatment I have encountered outside specialist scholarship.

**Required corrections:** Two (manioc processing sequence; preparation timeline).
**Flag for notes:** Manioc-variety exchange as inferential.
