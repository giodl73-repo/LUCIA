## B-1 — Dierk Lange — Board Review of "The Bornu Empire"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Translator and analyst of Ahmad ibn Fartuwa's chronicle of Mai Idris Alooma's reign. Author of *A Sudanic Chronicle: The Borno Expeditions of Idris Alauma (1564-1576)* (1987). Specialist in the Kanem-Bornu state, the Diwan, and the Arabic source tradition for the Lake Chad region.

**Historical accuracy:**

- Alooma's reign is dated "c. 1564-1596." This is within the range of scholarly estimates, though the exact dates remain debated. The Diwan does not give absolute dates; the chronology depends on calculations from the king list's relative positions and cross-referencing with external sources. Acceptable as an approximation.
- Ahmad ibn Fartuwa is correctly identified as imam and chronicler. The chronicle covers military expeditions during a period that Lange's translation frames as roughly twelve years of campaigning. The chapter's description of the chronicle as covering "the first twelve years of the mai's reign" is a reasonable reading of the text's scope.
- The musket count of sixty is a narrative detail not drawn from the chronicle itself. Ahmad's chronicle mentions the use of musketeers (described as "gunmen" or "bearers of firearms" in the text) but does not provide a specific count of weapons in the initial shipment. The number sixty is plausible but should be understood as a narrative construction, not a documented figure. **Recommend noting this or adjusting the framing.**
- The Ottoman correspondence is historically attested. Multiple scholars have documented the diplomatic relationship between the Bornu court and the Ottoman Porte, including the exchange of letters and the provision of military assistance. The chapter's framing of this as statecraft between equals is consistent with the evidence.
- The musketeers from Tripoli are correctly described. Ahmad's chronicle documents the presence of musketeers in Alooma's army and their role in campaigns. The chronicle does describe the integration of firearms with cavalry, and the tactical detail in Section III (rotating fire, earthen embankments) is consistent with what the chronicle suggests about the evolution of Bornu military practice, though the specific tactical details are somewhat more granular than what Ahmad's text provides.
- Mai Ali Gaji and the founding of Ngazargamu "around 1470" is consistent with the scholarly consensus derived from the Diwan and archaeological evidence. The date is approximate.
- The Maliki legal reforms are attested in Ahmad's chronicle and in the broader scholarly literature on Islam in Bornu. The chapter's description of Alooma formalizing the application of Maliki jurisprudence is consistent with what the sources document.
- The hostel in Medina is documented in the sources. Alooma's pilgrimage and his construction of a hostel for Bornu pilgrims are reported in the chronicle and in later sources.
- The end of the Saifawa dynasty "in 1846" is the conventional date for the last Saifawa mai's death. Acceptable.
- Muhammad al-Kanemi's role in defending Bornu against the Fulani jihad is correctly described. The chapter's framing of al-Kanemi as holding real power while the mai retained ceremonial authority is historically accurate.

**Cultural authenticity:**

- The sacred kingship is well handled. The curtain, the prohibition on seeing the mai eat, the intermediary system through the waziri and kaigama are all attested in the sources and in the anthropological literature on the Saifawa court. The chapter treats these not as exotic customs but as governance mechanisms, which is the correct approach.
- The treatment of Islam as the culture's own framework, rather than as an import, is historically appropriate for the sixteenth century. By Alooma's time, Islam had been part of the Bornu court for over five centuries.
- The tributary incorporation system ("layer, do not replace") is consistent with what the sources describe about Saifawa governance.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The structural argument about the tension between sacred immobility and strategic action is analytically strong and historically grounded.
- The portrayal of Ahmad ibn Fartuwa as both chronicler and jurist, and the use of his chronicle as a narrative device, is effective and historically defensible.
- The post-Alooma decline in Section V is handled as gradual systemic change rather than catastrophe, which matches the historical evidence.
- The Bulala are given their own perspective in the figures section, acknowledging the limits of the court chronicle's viewpoint.

**What needs correction:**

- **The sixty-musket count.** Ahmad's chronicle does not specify sixty muskets in an initial shipment. The chapter should either frame this as a representative detail or note that the specific number is the author's estimate. As written, it reads as a documented fact.
- **The lakeshore legal dispute in Section IV.** This is a constructed representative scene, not a documented case from Ahmad's chronicle. This is acceptable as narrative technique but should not be mistaken for a specific historical incident. The reader may assume this is a real case Ahmad adjudicated. Consider a signal, however subtle, that this is illustrative.
- **Population estimate for Ngazargamu.** The chapter says "perhaps a hundred thousand people." Population estimates for pre-colonial African cities are notoriously difficult. Connah's archaeological work suggests a substantial settlement, but "a hundred thousand" is at the upper end of estimates and should be flagged as uncertain. "Tens of thousands" would be more defensible.
- **The phrase "the most detailed portrait of any Saifawa ruler"** for Ahmad's chronicle is accurate but should be understood as "the most detailed surviving written portrait." Oral traditions may preserve detailed portraits that are not accessible in the same way.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

The chapter is historically sound in its major claims and analytically strong in its central argument. The sixty-musket count should be adjusted or qualified. The population estimate for Ngazargamu should be softened. The constructed legal scene, while effective, should be handled with awareness that the reader may read it as documented. With these adjustments, the chapter is defensible.
