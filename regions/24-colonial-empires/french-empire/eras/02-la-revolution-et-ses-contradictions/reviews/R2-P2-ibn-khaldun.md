# Panel Review — R2 / P-2 / Ibn Khaldun (PANEL-2, targeted)
## French Empire Era 2: La Révolution et Ses Contradictions — Notes Review
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

The production bible has correctly identified the structural engine and the terminal condition. The parallel cycle structure (French revolutionary cycle / Saint-Domingue cycle) is the right analytical frame. My focused comment is on the interaction between the two cycles, which the notes describe but do not yet fully develop.

**The key interaction:**

The Convention's abolition of slavery in February 1794 was not a humanitarian act in isolation — it was a military decision. France was at war with Britain and Spain, both of which were attempting to occupy Saint-Domingue and absorb its slave labor into their own imperial systems. The abolition brought Toussaint, who had been fighting for Spain, into alignment with France. This is the engine operating: the Declaration's universalism, when applied in an emergency, produced the alliance that saved Saint-Domingue for France — temporarily. The chapter should trace this calculation explicitly from inside the French military-political mind: not "France did the right thing" but "France did the expedient thing, which happened to coincide with the right thing."

**The asabiyya fracture:**

The white colonial class (the grands blancs) had the highest asabiyya of any group in Saint-Domingue — the tightest internal solidarity. They also had the most to lose from the Declaration. They eventually allied with Britain, inviting British occupation rather than accept abolition. This is the colonial asabiyya's self-destructive moment: in choosing racial hierarchy over national loyalty, the grands blancs demonstrated that their solidarity was narrower than France's imperial interest. Napoleon's 1802 restoration of slavery was, in part, an attempt to reconstitute that colonial asabiyya — to bring the white colonists back within the imperial frame. It failed because by 1802, the enslaved had developed a more powerful asabiyya of their own.

**The indemnity:**

The 150 million franc indemnity that Haiti paid France beginning in 1825 is the system's most revealing postscript. The asabiyya of the former slaveholders was strong enough to extract a payment from the country they had formerly enslaved. Haiti borrowed money from French banks (specifically, the Banque de France's designated creditors) to pay former slaveholders for the loss of their "property." This debt — which Haiti did not finish paying until 1947 — is the colonial ledger finding a way to continue collecting after the colony had ceased to exist. The chapter's landing should trace this arithmetic.

**One structural concern:**

The Louisiana Purchase (April 1803) needs more weight in the chapter's arc. It is not merely the consequence of the Saint-Domingue defeat — it is the French Empire accepting that its Atlantic architecture was finished. Napoleon sold not just Louisiana but the entire vision of a French Western Hemisphere empire. Jefferson understood this. The 15 million dollar price was the market value of an empire that no longer existed. The chapter should render the transaction from inside the French administrative mind: what it meant to sell not just land but a strategic position.
