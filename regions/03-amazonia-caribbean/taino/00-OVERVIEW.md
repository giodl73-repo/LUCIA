# Taino — "The Good People"

Taino means "good" or "noble" — it was the word the people of the Greater Antilles used when they needed to distinguish themselves from those they considered enemies, and it was the first word the people of Hispaniola offered to Christopher Columbus when he arrived in December 1492. The admiral recorded it as a name; it stuck. The Taino themselves organized their identity around their island communities (yucayeques), their chiefs (caciques), their kinship networks, and the zemís — the sacred objects and ancestral presences that inhabited every significant place and person in their world. They were not a single unified people but a network of related communities sharing a language, a ceremonial complex, a material culture, and an understanding of how the cosmos worked that had developed over centuries of island life.

Their world was organized around reciprocity — between the living and the dead, between the human community and the zemí-inhabited forces of nature, between the island communities themselves through the ball court ceremony (batey) and the areíto (the ceremonial gathering at which history, genealogy, and sacred knowledge were transmitted through song and dance). The cacique's authority was not military command; it was the responsibility to maintain these reciprocities — to ensure that the zemís were properly honored, that the areíto was performed, that the community's relationship with the forces that governed the islands was in good order. When Columbus arrived and began the process of dismantling the reciprocity structures — enslaving the population, seizing the zemís, interrupting the ceremonies — he was not simply destroying a political system. He was dissolving the structure that made life meaningful.

Key places:
- **Ayiti (Hispaniola)** — "mountainous land" — the Taino name for the island that Columbus called Española; the Taino political heartland; five major cacicazgos at contact
- **Borikén (Puerto Rico)** — "land of the noble lord"; the easternmost major Taino territory; a frontier zone facing Kalinago territory in the Lesser Antilles
- **Cuba** — the western Taino world; less politically consolidated than Hispaniola; the first Taino community Columbus encountered
- **Caonao, Maguana, Marién, Higüey, Xaragua** — the five cacicazgos of Hispaniola at contact; each led by a paramount cacique

## What Defined Them

**The zemí and the inhabited world.** The zemí was not a simple idol or a charm. It was a presence: an ancestor, a spirit force, or a deity inhabiting an object — a carved wooden figure, a stone, a cotton figure, a natural formation of unusual shape. Zemís governed rain, crops, the outcome of births, the behavior of storms. Caciques kept zemís that embodied the authority of their lineage. The areíto was in part a ceremony of zemí activation — the song and dance that kept the zemís present and responsive. To seize the zemís, as the Spanish did, was not trophy-taking. It was the dismantling of the community's nervous system.

**The cacique as the reciprocity manager.** The cacique's authority was expressed most visibly in the areíto — the ceremonial gathering at which the community assembled, sang the genealogical and historical narratives that transmitted collective memory, ate and drank the communal food and chicha, and maintained the relationships with the zemís that kept the world functioning. The cacique organized the areíto, provided the food, mediated between the community and the supernatural presences. His power was not coercive; it was generated by his demonstrated ability to keep the reciprocity flowing. A cacique who could not feed his community at the areíto was a cacique losing his authority.

**The batey and the body's sacred function.** The ball court (batey) was the center of Taino ceremonial life in the physical landscape — a flat, rectangular court surrounded by stone-lined edges that served as the space for the rubber-ball game played without using hands or feet (the ball struck only with hips, knees, and shoulders). The game was not sport in the modern sense; it was a ceremonial act that maintained cosmic balance, similar to the Mesoamerican ballgame tradition from which it was ultimately derived. The batey was also a gathering place for community ceremonies beyond the game itself — a public space where the community's collective relationship with the sacred was enacted.

**Manioc and the island economy.** The Taino agricultural system was centered on manioc (yuca) — specifically the bitter manioc variety that required processing to remove the toxic prussic acid, then pressed, dried, and baked into the cassava bread (casabi) that was the Taino staple. This processing technology was not trivial; it required specialized graters, presses, and heating implements, and the knowledge of how to transform a toxic root into edible bread was itself a form of cultural capital. The manioc garden (conuco), organized in earthen mounds that retained moisture and improved drainage, was the agricultural foundation of the Taino world, and its management was women's work — the women's knowledge domain that sustained the community.

**The areíto as living library.** The Taino had no writing. Their historical memory, cosmological knowledge, genealogical records, and ceremonial protocols were all transmitted through the areíto — the communal song-and-dance ceremony that could last for days. The lead singer (the arieto) guided the community through the performance of narratives that were memorized precisely and transmitted whole across generations. This was not informal tradition; it was a library maintained in the body and the voice, requiring practice, memory, and the ceremonial context of the gathering to function. When the Spanish disrupted the areíto — forbidding the ceremonies, scattering the communities, killing the caciques — they were destroying a library. The books were people, and the people were dying.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Island Settling:** A canoe from the Orinoco delta arrives at what will become the southern coast of Hispaniola. The passengers have been island-hopping for generations, following the Lesser Antilles chain northward. They are looking for the place that has enough land, enough water, enough fish. They have found it. The island will be called Ayiti.

**2 — The Areíto World:** At a batey court in the interior of Hispaniola, the areíto has been going for two days. The lead singer is moving through the genealogy of the cacique's lineage, linking it back to the ancestor who first emerged from the cave at Cacibajagua. Every person in the gathering knows these lines. They are singing the world into existence again.

**3 — The Classic Taino:** Anacaona, the cacica of Xaragua, is the most respected leader on the island. She composes areítos that other communities perform. Her zemís are famous for their power. She does not know that she will be executed by the Spanish in 1503 — that the world she presides over has eight years left. She is writing poems for a world that will outlast her only in the memory of those who flee.

**4 — Contact and Transformation:** Columbus has been received. The gifts have been exchanged. The admiral has written in his journal that the Taino would make good servants. On Hispaniola, the first reports of the strangers are moving through the cacicazgos. Guacanagarí is trying to read what these new people want. The zemís give no guidance that he can interpret. He will offer them friendship. It will not be enough.
