# The National Policy and Its Discontents
## Canada, 1867–1945

---

### I. The School

**Kamloops Indian Residential School, British Columbia, 1920**

Her name was Qwenqwent. She was seven years old, and she had traveled three days on roads that smelled of pine dust and horse sweat to reach a building larger than anything she had seen: red brick, three stories, a cross above the roof that caught the September light. The mountains she knew rose behind it. They were the same mountains. Everything else was wrong.

The nun came with scissors.

Qwenqwent had not understood, until this moment, that they would cut her braids. Her grandmother had braided her hair that morning, standing behind her in the early dark, singing quietly in Secwepemctsin, the song that was also a teaching about how water moves through the land. The braids were done. The singing was done. Her grandmother pressed her palms against Qwenqwent's shoulders once, and then Qwenqwent's father carried her luggage to the wagon, and no one cried because that would have made it worse.

The scissors were cold. The cut was quick. The braids fell to the floor and lay there like something that had once been living.

A priest came with a ledger. He wrote a number in it. He said a name she did not recognize. He said it twice, louder the second time, as if she were far away. The nun pointed to Qwenqwent and said the name again. She understood, then, what the number meant. What the name meant. What was being asked of her.

The Kamloops Indian Residential School had been run by the Oblates of Mary Immaculate since 1890. The Oblates were a Catholic missionary order whose founding charism was the evangelization of the poor and the marginalized, and in Canada their understanding of that charism had taken a specific form: to save the souls of Indigenous children, you had first to empty them of what they had been given. Salvation required a vessel that was not already occupied. The child who arrived speaking Secwepemctsin and carrying the grandmother's songs was, in the Oblate theology of the time, not a vessel waiting to be filled but a vessel that needed to be emptied before it could receive what the missionaries meant to give. The braids were not symbolic of culture; in the Oblate understanding, they were of it, and of it meant not of God.

The hallway she walked down smelled of boiled oats and floor wax. The dormitory held forty iron beds, each with a gray blanket, each with a number at the headboard that corresponded to a number in the ledger. The windows were high and let in gray light in the morning and none at night. The food was the same every day: porridge in the morning, boiled vegetables at noon, bread at night. It was not the food she had grown up eating. The dried salmon, the bitter root, the camas bulbs, the foods whose preparation was itself a teaching about the watershed and the seasons and what the land gave at what time and what it asked in return. The food here had no teaching in it. It was fuel. The body was being retrained for different purposes.

She was not allowed to speak Secwepemctsin. She was not allowed to practice her ceremonies. She was not allowed to go home for ten months. She slept in the dormitory with the forty other girls, and at night, in the dark, some of them whispered their own languages under their breath, and the words grew smaller as the weeks went on, and some of the girls stopped whispering, and the dark in the dormitory was the particular dark of rooms where you learn not to make sounds that might bring someone to the door.

The Kamloops school was one of 139 such schools operating across Canada. Over the century between the 1880s and 1996, approximately 150,000 First Nations, Métis, and Inuit children passed through them. The schools were funded by the federal government under the authority of the Indian Act and were administered by the Roman Catholic, Anglican, Methodist, United, and Presbyterian churches. They were, in their official documents, institutions of care and civilization. The practice was different from the documents.

In 2021, ground-penetrating radar will scan the grounds of the Kamloops school and find 215 anomalies consistent with unmarked graves. The Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc Nation will say: we knew they were there. Their grandmothers and grandfathers had said so. What changed in 2021 was that the instruments confirmed what the oral tradition had carried for generations. The oral tradition had not waited for the instruments to make it true.

### II. The Machine's Design

The Indian Act of 1876 is not a law in the ordinary sense. It is an administrative architecture. It defined who was an "Indian," legally and for purposes of state management, and in doing so it created a category of person who existed differently before the law than every other Canadian. People in this category did not own their reserve land; the Crown held it in trust. They could not vote in federal elections until 1960. They could not hold their ceremonies without risking arrest under the 1885 amendment that prohibited potlatch and sun dance. They could not leave their reserves without a pass signed by the Indian agent, a government representative appointed by Ottawa and accountable to Ottawa, whose decisions were not subject to appeal.

Duncan Campbell Scott became deputy superintendent of Indian affairs in 1913 and held the position until 1932. He wrote poetry. His verse about the Canadian landscape is anthologized in the Confederation poets collections, the literary generation that built an idea of Canada from its rivers and rocks and distances. He also wrote policy. In 1920, before a Parliamentary committee, he stated his objective without ambiguity: he wanted to "continue until there is not a single Indian in Canada that has not been absorbed into the body politic and there is no Indian question, and no Indian Department." He was not unusual in his views. He was the most direct.

The residential school was the instrument of Scott's theory, and the theory was specific: culture is transmitted through children. Separate the children from their communities during the years when transmission happens, and the culture will not pass. The government and the churches would fill the space where Secwepemctsin, Cree, Anishinaabemowin, Haida, and a hundred other languages had been. What they called civilization would replace what they called paganism. The replacement would take a generation, or two, or three, but if the system ran long enough and intensively enough, the transmission would break and there would be no need to continue the effort because there would be nothing left to suppress.

The per-capita funding model made the theory operational and made the outcome worse. Churches received federal payment for each enrolled child. More children meant more money. The incentive was to maximize enrollment regardless of whether the schools could safely care for the children they enrolled. Inspector reports throughout the system's operation documented overcrowding, inadequate food, disease spread by poor ventilation, and abuse. The reports were filed. The funding continued. The inspectors noted the conditions; the system continued on the logic of its own design.

The system produced not assimilation but alienation: people who had lost the knowledge of their own communities without gaining full membership in the one they had been trained for. This outcome was documented and the system continued anyway, because the outcome was not the goal. The goal was the elimination of the prior culture, and the elimination was proceeding. That survivors were alienated rather than assimilated was, in the administrative calculus, an acceptable intermediate condition.

What the government called "the Indian problem" generated more legislation, more amendments, more institutions in a self-reinforcing cycle. The reserve system confined First Nations people to small parcels of land. Confined to reserves, First Nations people did not enter the settler economy on terms the settler economy recognized. The residential school was the administrative solution to the problem created by the reserve: remove the children, train them for domestic service and farm labor, and return them to a world that had been designed to have no place for what they were trained to do. Each solution produced new problems that required new solutions.

### III. The Land That Was Settled

What the government called settlement was a process. The land had people on it.

Before the numbered treaties, before the CPR surveyed its route across the prairies, the land between the Red River and the Rocky Mountains held the Blackfoot Confederacy, the Niitsitapi, "the real people," and the Cree, the Assiniboine, the Ojibwe, the Nakoda, the Saulteaux. They had been there for millennia. Their economy was organized around the buffalo, which numbered in the tens of millions and sustained not just food but clothing, shelter, tools, and ceremony. Their nations had governance structures, trade networks, and diplomatic relationships that had been operating since long before any European had seen the Great Lakes.

Treaty 7 was signed at Blackfoot Crossing on the Bow River in September 1877. Crowfoot of the Siksika, Sitting on an Eagle Tail of the Kainai, Old Sun of the Siksika: they spoke. Crowfoot said, in the translation recorded by the government's own interpreters: "The advice given me and my people has proved to be very good. I will sign the treaty."

Crowfoot understood the treaty as a covenant. Not a transfer of ownership: that concept did not exist in Niitsitapi political thought in the form it existed in British common law. The land was not his to sell because it was not property in the sense the law intended; it was relationship. The Niitsitapi were a people organized around mobility, around the buffalo runs and the seasonal movements that the land itself prescribed. You did not own the land; you were in relationship with it, responsible to it, accountable to its patterns. A river does not belong to the person standing on its bank. The treaty was an agreement about sharing, about coexistence, about the obligations each party undertook toward the other. As long as the sun shines and the river flows, the promises hold.

The government understood it as a transfer of title.

The Secwépemc, on the other side of the Rockies, had organized their knowledge of the Thompson River watershed over thousands of years into a system that could track the salmon runs, the timing of the bitter root harvest, the locations of medicinal plants by season and elevation, the management of the fur-bearing animals in ways that sustained the populations. This knowledge was held in language, in ceremony, in the annual round of practices that moved people through the landscape in ways that maintained the relationships on which everything else depended. When the church and the government looked at this system, they did not see a system. They saw a population that had not yet been organized into productive, taxable, Christian labor. The residential school was the mechanism of reorganization.

These were not the same understanding, and the difference was not a miscommunication. The numbered treaties were legal instruments designed within British legal theory, which held that land was property and that property could be alienated. The Plains nations negotiated in good faith within their own legal frameworks. The implementation proceeded within the British one. The gap between the two frameworks is where the reserve system, the pass system, and the residential school all live.

The CPR's construction through the prairies in the early 1880s ran through this gap. The Plains buffalo herds were already collapsing under commercial hunting pressure before the railway arrived. What James Daschuk's archival research has documented is what happened simultaneously: Indian Affairs withheld food from starving Cree and Assiniboine bands to coerce them onto reserves and away from the railway construction zone. The internal government correspondence is explicit about the purpose. People died. The documentation exists in federal archives.

Seventeen thousand Chinese laborers built the mountain sections of the CPR. They received half the wages of white workers. They were assigned the most dangerous work: the tunnels, the explosives, the sheer-faced cuts through the Rogers Pass. Between six hundred and a thousand died, though the records are incomplete because the records of Chinese laborers were kept incompletely by the railway companies. They received no land grants, no pension provisions, no citizenship. In the same year the CPR was completed, the government imposed the Chinese Head Tax of fifty dollars. The National Policy's prosperity required conditions that it did not acknowledge requiring.

### IV. 1885

The year the CPR was completed, the Métis of the Northwest Territories rose for the second time under Louis Riel.

Riel had led the first resistance, the Red River Resistance of 1869 to 1870, when the Dominion purchased Rupert's Land from the Hudson's Bay Company without consulting the Métis who lived there. He had negotiated Manitoba's entry into Confederation, the first and only time an indigenous-led negotiation produced a lasting constitutional result: Manitoba entered as a bilingual province with Métis land rights guaranteed. Then the implementation of those guarantees failed. The surveyors came anyway. The Métis moved west along the South Saskatchewan River and spent the next fifteen years petitioning the government about their land claims. The petitions went unanswered.

They brought Riel back from Montana in 1884. The Northwest Rebellion of 1885 lasted three months. The CPR, completed in November of that year, carried government troops west from Ontario in eleven days. The railway that was supposed to hold the country together proved its value by holding it together militarily against the people whose land it crossed. The rebellion was suppressed. Riel was captured, tried for treason, and hanged on November 16, 1885, in Regina.

He spoke at his trial. He had been offered an insanity defense that might have saved him; he rejected it. He had not been insane. He had been the president of a provisional government representing a people with legal rights the Dominion had not recognized. His defense counsel wanted to argue that the visions, the prophecies, the sense of divine mission that had characterized his years in exile were evidence of a mind that could not be held fully responsible. Riel refused this. He said: "I know that through the grace of God I am the founder of Manitoba. I know that though I have no open road for my influence, I have big influence, concentrated as a big amount of vapour in an engine." He asked the court to take him seriously as a political actor, as the leader of a nation. The court sentenced him to death. He was hanged on November 16, 1885. His last words, reportedly, were a prayer.

In Quebec, the execution was a catastrophe. Wilfrid Laurier said: "Had I been born on the banks of the Saskatchewan, I would myself have shouldered a musket." Henri Bourassa, seventeen years old when Riel was hanged, would spend the next four decades arguing that the execution was evidence that French Canadians could not trust the English Protestant majority to honor the obligations of Confederation. He was not wrong about the lesson. The question was what followed from it.

In 1885: the CPR completed, its costs distributed among those who could not refuse to bear them. Riel hanged, and the English-French fault line cracked open. The Chinese Head Tax imposed. The residential school enrollment growing under new policy directives from Ottawa. These are not separate stories. They are the expression, in a single year, of a single project.

### V. Two Wars, Two Conscriptions

Canada entered the First World War in August 1914 automatically, as a British dominion. There was no Parliamentary vote. Canada was at war because Britain was at war.

Over the course of the war, 620,000 Canadians enlisted. They fought at Ypres, the Somme, Passchendaele. At Vimy Ridge in April 1917, four Canadian divisions attacked together for the first time and captured a ridge the French and British had failed to take for two years. The battle became a founding myth of Canadian nationhood: the moment when Canada proved itself distinct from Britain, capable and lethal and self-reliant. The story Canada told about Vimy was not wrong. It was partial.

In the same year, the government introduced conscription under the Military Service Act. Quebec erupted. Bourassa and the nationalist press argued that French Canadians had no obligation to die for a British imperial war in a European quarrel that had nothing to do with them. The Conservatives who had supported Riel's execution in 1885 were now demanding French Canadian blood for the Empire. Bourassa said: you ask us to love this country; give us reason to. The conscription crisis sent a fracture through the country that would not fully close.

Approximately 4,000 First Nations men enlisted voluntarily during the First World War, at a time when they were not citizens of the country they enlisted to fight for. They were exempt from the draft but went anyway, for reasons that varied by community and by individual: family warrior tradition, economic necessity, treaty obligations read as including defense of the Crown, a belief that service would produce recognition. Some had brothers in residential schools while they were in the trenches. When the war ended, First Nations veterans were denied the soldier settlement land grants that white veterans received under the Soldier Settlement Act of 1919. The mechanism of denial was administrative, not personal: Indian Act land was held in trust by the Crown and could not be used as collateral for the loans the Act provided. The Indian Act excluded them through its own internal logic, without anyone needing to state a policy of exclusion.

The Second World War brought a second conscription crisis. The government held a plebiscite in 1942 asking Canadians to release it from its promise not to conscript for overseas service. Quebec voted seventy percent against. The rest of Canada voted seventy percent for. The country had voted against itself. Conscription was imposed in 1944 and produced a smaller crisis than 1917 only because fewer men were actually sent before the war ended.

Between the two world wars, the prairies that the National Policy had settled were struck by drought. The Dust Bowl years of the 1930s turned the wheat economy into catastrophe: the topsoil blew away, the prices collapsed, the banks foreclosed, and the settlers who had been brought to the prairies under the promise of agricultural prosperity found themselves in boxcars heading back east or west. The suffering was real. The irony was that the Indigenous communities of the plains, confined to reserves and excluded from the wheat economy's prosperity, found themselves less exposed to its collapse. They had never been permitted to join the system. They were therefore not ruined by the system's failure in the same way.

Between 1939 and 1945, residential school enrollment continued. The system did not pause for the war or for the Depression or for the drought. The administrative machine ran alongside the national emergencies and independently of them. The two stories, the Canada being built and the conditions being suppressed to build it, occupied the same years without acknowledging each other in the official record.

### VI. What the Language Carried

The Secwepemctsin language has a word that is not a translation of any English word. It names the relationship between water, its movement through a specific landscape, the seasonal patterns of that movement, the fish who depend on those patterns, the people whose food and ceremony are organized around the fish, and the obligations that arise from all of these relationships simultaneously. The word is not a noun and not a verb in the way English distinguishes them. It is a process, a relationship, a set of responsibilities held in a single sound.

The word did not appear in any federal ledger. The Indian Act had no category for it. The residential school curriculum did not include it and could not have included it, because the curriculum was built from a theory of knowledge that could not recognize what the word was holding.

The residential school system was, in its own terms, a partial success and a partial failure. It interrupted the transmission of cultural knowledge for multiple generations across hundreds of communities. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission documented this in testimony after testimony: survivors who returned home unable to speak to their grandparents, who watched ceremonies performed in forms they could no longer read, who found themselves between two worlds without full footing in either. The system had done what it was designed to do.

It had not done everything it was designed to do.

In the dormitories at night, children whispered their languages. In the fields during work periods, in the moments between supervised activities, in the small intervals that no institution can fully close, children found ways to carry what they had been brought with. Some memorized stories and songs and the names of places in their languages and said nothing in the hearing of the supervisors and kept it anyway. Some maintained ceremonies in secret, in forms that changed under the pressure of concealment but continued. The Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples documented in its 1996 five-volume report, twenty-five years before the flags went to half-mast, that what the system had not destroyed it had driven underground. Survival was not passive. It was a decision made daily under conditions designed to make it impossible.

The RCAP report was tabled in Parliament. The government of the day issued no formal apology and implemented almost none of its 440 recommendations. The report was real; its reception demonstrated how much of the story had not changed.

Nellie McClung, who won women the right to vote in federal elections in 1918 and won them personhood under the law in 1929, did not campaign against the residential school system. She shared the assimilationist assumptions of her class and era. In her writing, she spoke of "helping the Indian" in ways that assumed the goal was incorporation into European-Canadian civilization, the goal that Scott had stated more bluntly from his administrative desk. McClung's progressive tradition and Scott's administrative tradition were not opposites. They were adjacent expressions of the same premise: that European-Canadian civilization was the destination, and that the question was only how quickly and by what means other peoples would arrive there.

The progressive reform tradition that expanded democratic rights for women and workers in this period and the Indian Act that suppressed First Nations political and cultural existence were not opposites. They were simultaneous products of the same civilization's self-understanding. The Famous Five who petitioned the Privy Council and the inspectors who filed reports on abusive residential schools existed in the same decade, within the same legal and political culture, and mostly without seeing any contradiction between what each was doing.

### VII. Landing

In June 2021, the Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc Nation announced that ground-penetrating radar scans of the former Kamloops school grounds had identified 215 anomalies consistent with unmarked graves. The announcement spread across the country within hours. The federal government ordered flags to half-mast. Politicians who had been slow to act on the TRC's 94 calls to action since 2015 spoke of shock and grief.

The Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc were not shocked. Chief Rosanne Casimir said: "We had a knowing in our community, we had always had a knowing." The oral tradition had carried this knowledge for generations. It had not waited for the radar to make it true.

This is the distinction that runs beneath the chapter's entire structure. The oral tradition, the grandmother's song in the early dark, the words whispered under dormitory blankets, the ceremonies maintained in secret for decades, was not an inferior form of knowing that required instrumental confirmation to become real. It was knowledge. The instruments confirmed it. The instruments did not produce it.

The National Policy built a country. The country it built required, as a structural condition, the suppression of the peoples who had organized life on this land for millennia. The suppression was not incidental to the project; it was the project's premise. The reserve system created the space the settlers moved into. The residential school prevented the prior culture from regenerating in the next generation. The pass system kept First Nations people on reserves while the settlers moved freely through the land that the reserves had been carved from. The Indian Act maintained all of this as a coherent legal architecture that ran continuously, regardless of which party held Ottawa, regardless of what was happening in the trenches in Belgium or on the prairies during the drought years of the 1930s. It was administrative in its character, bureaucratic in its instrument, and it ran alongside the railway, the wheat economy, the conscription debates, and the women's suffrage movement, simultaneously and without acknowledgment in any of those stories.

In 1931, the Statute of Westminster gave Canada formal legislative independence from Britain: no longer a dominion bound by imperial legislation, but a sovereign state in the British Commonwealth. The country that had fought two wars as Britain's ally had become, in law, its equal. The residential school system continued into its sixth decade.

The discontents of this era are multiple and do not agree with one another. Riel's discontent and Bourassa's discontent and McClung's discontent do not map onto each other, and none of them maps onto Qwenqwent's. The railway that was Bourassa's symbol of English Protestant domination was also the instrument of Cree dispossession. The women's suffrage movement that McClung led was a genuine expansion of rights that excluded the Indigenous women whom the Indian Act's status categories would continue to constrain for another half-century. The conscription crisis that broke English-French unity was also the era when Indigenous men volunteered to fight in a war the country did not give them citizenship to fight and then denied them the land grants it gave to white veterans.

These discontents are not resolved by the era's end. They continue. The mosaic that Canada would come to define itself by in the years after 1945 was built on this foundation, and the foundation's character determines the weight it can hold.

Qwenqwent kept the word for water moving through the land. She gave it to her daughter in the early dark, over the braid. Her daughter gave it to hers. The Thompson River still runs through the watershed that the language names. The salmon still return on the schedule that the traditional ecological knowledge tracked long before Western fisheries management began to track it. The mountains, the same mountains Qwenqwent saw from the school window in September 1920, never recognized the names Confederation placed on them.

The school had known what it was taking. It had not known what it could not take.

The mountains were still there. They did not recognize the name.
