# Era 1: The Proto-Elamite and Old Elamite Periods
## c. 3200–1500 BCE

---

The scribe pressed the stylus into the clay while it was still wet. That was the first requirement: wet enough to hold the mark, dry enough not to smear it. The tablet in his left hand was palm-sized, the clay pale and river-damp, pulled from the channel that fed the temple granary. He had been doing this since before his beard came in. His teacher had done it before that. His teacher's teacher had done it before that, back and back through generations in which the marks had multiplied and the system had deepened and what had begun as tokens pressed in soft clay had become something that no single person had designed, something that had grown the way cities grow — one necessity at a time, one sign at a time, until the structure was so large that no one could see all of it at once.

He made the marks. The grain was counted. The god's portion set aside.

Susa in the thirty-first century before the common era was not yet the city it would become — not yet the layered thing of brick and plaster and memory that would outlast a dozen empires — but it was already old. The mound that the city stood on was ancient before the scribe's grandfather was born. People had lived here, on this particular rise above the Khuzestan plain, for so long that the accumulation of their living had raised the ground itself: packed clay floors, collapsed walls, the ash of ten thousand cooking fires pressed down into the earth until the earth was no longer just earth but human time made solid. You built on what came before. That was not a philosophy. It was how construction worked.

The plain stretched west and south, flat and rich, river-watered, given over to barley as far as the eye could follow. To the east and north, visible on clear mornings when the air had not yet thickened with heat, the Zagros lifted. The mountains were not distant scenery. They were the other half of the world. Down from them came the highland peoples with their herds and their own ways of reckoning the world; down from them came the passes that led to the plateau, to Anshan, to the kingdom's other pole. Up into them went the caravans carrying the surplus grain the plain produced, the copper and lapis that came in along the old routes from Elam's eastern connections, the administrative tablets that kept track of what had gone where and how much. The scribe at his clay was part of this flow. His work was the record of it.

He did not think of himself as inventing anything. The signs he pressed into clay had come from his teachers, and his teachers from theirs, and back further than anyone could verify. But signs had proliferated. New goods required new signs. New transactions created new conventions. The counting system had acquired symbols for large quantities because the granary needed them; the granary needed them because the surplus was growing; the surplus was growing because the canal system had extended the reach of irrigation into lands that had been dry before. Necessity accumulated. The scribe recorded the necessity. In doing so he made the necessity legible to people who had not been present, to administrators in other buildings, to future accountants who would open the archive and read what he had pressed into clay.

No one has yet read these tablets.

The proto-Elamite script — the system the scribe was using, in which he had been trained, in which Susa's scribal tradition was keeping the records of a complex redistributive economy operating across hundreds of kilometers of plateau and plain — has not been deciphered. It ran for six hundred years, from approximately 3200 to 2600 BCE, and left behind more than five thousand tablets from dozens of sites stretching from Susa in the west to the edges of what is now Afghanistan in the east. It is the widest geographically distributed writing system of its era. And the words in it are still silent.

What the tablets hold, even in silence: columns of signs pressed in consistent order, commodities and their quantities arranged in the logic of a trained hand. One sign for grain counted by the unit; a different numerical base for grain measured by volume. Two systems for two kinds of knowing, running simultaneously on the same tablet. Signs that repeat across hundreds of kilometers, pressed by hands that had never met but had trained in the same conventions — which means the conventions were taught, which means there were teachers, which means there were places where teaching happened, which means a class of people whose function was the maintenance of written knowledge had emerged here on the plain and on the plateau before most of the world had decided that marks could outlast memory.

What it meant to them to press these marks: this the tablets do not say, or say in a language we have not yet learned to hear.

What it means that they pressed them at all: that on the Iranian plateau, in the foothills of the Zagros and on the alluvial plain below, a civilization had organized itself around the written word before most of the world knew writing was possible. That the lowland city and the highland kingdom were already, in 3100 BCE, connected by a bureaucratic system sophisticated enough to require a uniform script across vast distances. That the Mesopotamian world to the west, where Sumerian scribes were also pressing clay in this same era, was not the only place on earth where human beings had discovered that a mark could outlast a memory.

The scribe's tablet dried. The grain count was fixed in it.

Somewhere in the archive, in the dark and dry of a storeroom built to hold just this, it waited.

It is still waiting.
