## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of Hammurabi's Babylon Opening

**Overall:** This opening earns its reader on the strength of its central conceit: that the stele is not a relic but a public document, and that the act of publishing law is itself the story. The structure is excellent.

**What works:**
- The opening image (Shamash handing the rod and ring to Hammurabi) is specific, visual, and anchors the reader immediately in a real object that survives.
- "Then he published the law." The one-sentence paragraph after the conquest sequence is a masterful pacing decision. Twenty-nine years of patience compressed to a clause, four years of conquest in a single sentence, and then the weight lands on the publication, not the victory. This is the structural argument: the law, not the war, is the point.
- The closing return to the merchant in the courtyard creates a frame. The stele is introduced as monument; it closes as something a person reads. The shift from the cosmic to the particular is the opening's best move.
- The direct quotation of the law cases works. Letting the reader hear the actual language of the code does what description alone cannot.

**What doesn't:**
- The middle section (Hammurabi's genealogy and the five-king political situation) is necessary but feels like the opening pausing for context. The transition from the stele's theological claim to "Hammurabi was the sixth king" shifts from immersion to exposition. Can the genealogy be delivered through action rather than summary?
- The merchant at the end is vivid but arrives late. If the opening spent more time with him, his presence would anchor the closing more powerfully. As it stands, he appears in the last two paragraphs and carries the whole emotional weight.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening tells us the stele stood in the Esagila. Can we feel the Esagila? The courtyard's architecture, the scale of the temple complex, the sound of the city beyond the walls? The stele is tactile (warm under the hand), but the space around it remains abstract. A single sentence of architectural grounding would set the stele in a living place rather than a vacuum.
