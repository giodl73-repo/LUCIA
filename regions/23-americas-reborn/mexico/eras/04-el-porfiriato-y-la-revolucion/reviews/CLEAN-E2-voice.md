### Voice-Random-Paragraph Test

Three paragraphs selected at random for isolation test (does the voice identify the culture from rhythm alone?):

**Paragraph 1 (Section I, Ley de Baldíos):**
> "The surveyor arrived. He set up his transit in the cornfield of Anenecuilco, measured the land, drew his maps, wrote his report. The hacienda of Hospital received the transfer documents. The village received notice that the land it had farmed since 1607 — since before the Díaz dictatorship, before the Reform War, before independence, since the Spanish colonial system had at least recorded the indigenous communities' rights in its own legal forms — now belonged to someone else."

Voice test: Pass. Corrido-narrative rhythm is identifiable — declarative sequence, named figure and place, period as cadence-stop, the sardonic "now belonged to someone else" as refrain-weight. Could only be Mexico.

**Paragraph 2 (Section IV, Aguascalientes Convention):**
> "The Convention of Aguascalientes, in October 1914, was the attempt to unite the victors. The Constitutionalists, the Villistas, and the Zapatistas sent delegates. The Zapatista delegation arrived with the Plan de Ayala and refused to negotiate it: the land provisions were not a bargaining position. The Convention accepted the Plan de Ayala in principle. Carranza rejected the Convention and withdrew his delegates. The revolution split into two."

Voice test: Pass. Tight declarative sequence, short sentences for political clarity, no editorial weight. The colon in "refused to negotiate it: the land provisions were not a bargaining position" is the corrido's way of delivering a refrain-fact.

**Paragraph 3 (Section VI, Chinameca):**
> "Colonel Jesús Guajardo was a Constitutionalist officer in Morelos. In late March 1919, he contacted Zapata's camp with a message: he was disgusted with Carranza's betrayal of the revolution, he wanted to bring his men and his arms over to the Zapatistas, he was ready to defect with four hundred soldiers and a quantity of ammunition."

Voice test: Pass. The corrido's accumulative facticity — everything named, the four hundred soldiers, the quantity of ammunition, the staging of the betrayal — delivers the dread of what comes next without announcing it.

### Full Chapter Voice Audit

**Textbook voice (encyclopaedic summary):** No instances found. Every analytical passage is in narrative voice.

**Modern commentary:** One near-miss. In Section II: "He was not an evil man in the manner of people who want evil." This approaches the register of the moral philosopher. It passes the test because it refuses to condemn rather than offering modern ethical judgment, and the corrido-narrative voice is capable of irony. No correction needed.

**Register drift:** One instance in Section IV (Decena Trágica section), the paragraph beginning "Huerta ruled for seventeen months." This paragraph summarizes his rule without a scene or sensory anchor. It reads: "He was a drunk and a murderer and he made no pretense of constitutional legitimacy." This is corrido-narrative at its most compressed; the information-density is appropriate to the corrido's reportage function and does not slip into academic mode. Marginal pass.

**One correction recommended:** Section V (Querétaro), the paragraph beginning "Carranza's draft was moderate." The phrase "municipal freedoms" is unexplained — a reader unfamiliar with Mexican constitutional history may not know what this refers to. Consider one phrase of clarification: "municipal freedoms — the right of local governments to govern without appointee interference." Minor adjustment; does not require revision of the chapter.
