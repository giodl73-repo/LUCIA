# Mexica — "Children of the Sun's Navel"

They called themselves the Mexica — "people of Mexictli," a name connected to their patron deity Huitzilopochtli and to their origin in the mythological homeland of Aztlan (hence the colonial shorthand "Aztec," a name the Mexica themselves rarely used). Their founding story was a migration: the long wandering south from Aztlan under Huitzilopochtli's guidance, ending only when an eagle was seen perched on a cactus growing from a rock in the middle of Lake Texcoco, holding a serpent in its beak — the sign the god had promised. There they built Tenochtitlan on an island in the lake, a city that would grow to house perhaps two hundred thousand people in two centuries and that the Spanish, arriving in 1519, described as more magnificent than any city in Europe.

The Mexica understood themselves as the people the Fifth Sun had been made for and the people responsible for keeping it moving. The current age — Nahui Ollin, "Four Movement" — was fated to end in earthquake, and the only force that could postpone its end was the sustenance of the sun through human blood. The xochiyaoyotl — the "flower war," a formalized conflict conducted primarily to produce living captives rather than territory — was not a euphemism for ordinary warfare. It was a specific institution designed to ensure that the supply of sacrificial captives was maintained without the annihilation of the neighboring powers needed to produce them. The Tlaxcalans and Huexotzincans who fought the flower wars against the Mexica understood the logic. They were not victims of incomprehensible savagery; they were participants in a shared cosmological economy, even as they chafed against its terms.

Key places:
- **Tenochtitlan** — the island capital; the Templo Mayor (Huey Teocalli) at its center; the causeways connecting it to the mainland; the great market at Tlatelolco
- **Tlatelolco** — twin city on the same island; the largest market in Mesoamerica; housed the merchant guild (pochteca)
- **Templo Mayor (Huey Teocalli)** — the dual pyramid dedicated to Huitzilopochtli (the sun/war deity) and Tlaloc (the rain deity); rebuilt seven times, each new pyramid encasing the previous
- **Aztlan** — the mythological homeland; its location was symbolic rather than geographic in the Mexica tradition

## What Defined Them

**Huitzilopochtli and the solar covenant.** The Mexica deity most uniquely theirs was Huitzilopochtli — "Hummingbird of the South," the solar-war deity born each dawn at the Templo Mayor's summit and who required blood to complete his journey across the sky. This was not imported from older Mesoamerican traditions; it was specifically Mexica. The claim that human sacrifice was cosmologically necessary — that the sun required the hearts and blood of warriors to maintain its movement — was the theological proposition that organized the Mexica state. Every military campaign, every tributary relationship, every flower war produced the captives whose sacrifice kept the Fifth Sun moving. The Mexica were not exceptional in their use of sacrifice in Mesoamerica; they were exceptional in the scale and the institutional elaboration they brought to it.

**The chinampas and the city that floated.** Tenochtitlan was built on an island in a lake, and its food supply required solving the problem of feeding two hundred thousand people with no adjacent agricultural land. The solution was the chinampa — the "floating garden," a narrow rectangle of built-up soil anchored to the lake bottom by willow roots, extending the island's productive area across the shallow southern end of Lake Texcoco. The chinampa system was not an emergency improvisation; it was a sophisticated water-management technology that produced multiple harvests per year, sustained a dense urban population, and turned the lake from an obstacle into an asset. Tenochtitlan was not in spite of the water. It was because of it.

**The pochteca and the merchant-spy network.** The Mexica long-distance merchant class, the pochteca, occupied a peculiar position in the social order: nominally subordinate to the warrior nobility, they were in practice the intelligence and commercial network of the empire. Pochteca traveled to distant markets beyond the Triple Alliance's tribute zone, carrying goods and bringing back information about political conditions, local resources, and potential resistance. They traveled armed, traveled in organized groups, and were entitled to wage war if attacked. They reported to the Cihuacoatl (the second-in-command) and ultimately to the tlatoani (the supreme speaker). The commercial network and the military-intelligence network were the same network.

**The Templo Mayor as the cosmos made stone.** The dual pyramid at Tenochtitlan's center was rebuilt seven times, each new version encasing the previous, so that the final structure was seven pyramids in one. Its two summits — Huitzilopochtli's on the south, Tlaloc's on the north — expressed the fundamental duality of the Mexica cosmos: the solar-war force that killed and the rain force that gave life, maintained in permanent coexistence at the city's axis. The offerings buried within the pyramid's construction fill at each stage — thousands of objects, including Olmec and Teotihuacan artifacts taken from ancient sites — declared that Tenochtitlan was the heir of all that had come before. The Templo Mayor was a recapitulation of Mesoamerican history in stone and sacrifice.

**The altepetl as the world's basic unit.** The Mexica political world was organized around the altepetl — the city-state, whose name combined "water" and "mountain," the two essential Mesoamerican markers of a bounded, inhabitable place. Every altepetl had its patron deity, its calpolli (neighborhood-clan units), its tlatoani (ruling speaker), and its ritual cycle. The Triple Alliance did not replace altepetl governance; it extracted tribute from it. The conquered altepetl kept its internal structure, its deity cult, its calpolli organization. The Mexica innovation was the tribute apparatus that sat on top of this base — comprehensive, systematic, and enforced by the memory of what happened to altepetl that resisted.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Wandering:** The Mexica are not yet a people to be reckoned with. They are a group of pilgrims following a hummingbird voice through other people's territory, periodically driven off, periodically enslaved. The island in the lake is not a destination anyone else wanted. That is why it was available.

**2 — The Subject Years:** Tenochtitlan is growing. The Mexica are fighting Tepanec wars, building Tepanec causeways, earning a reputation as the most effective soldiers in the Valley — for someone else. The city is accumulating the military expertise and the political resentments that will explode in 1428.

**3 — The Triple Alliance:** The Tepanec War ends with Azcapotzalco burned and the Triple Alliance in its place. Itzcoatl and his adviser Tlacaelel make a decision: the old histories of the Mexica migration will be rewritten to claim Toltec descent. A people who were recently servants will now be the heirs of civilization's founding lineage.

**4 — The Fifth Sun's Servants:** Ahuizotl dedicates the newly rebuilt Templo Mayor. The sacrifice that accompanies the dedication is remembered as the largest in Mexica history. The sun will keep moving. The city at the lake's center has made itself the axis the cosmos requires.

**5 — The World That Was:** Moctezuma II receives the news of strangers on the coast. He sends gifts. The gifts are understood by the recipients as submission. What Moctezuma understood himself to be doing is more complicated, and the Mexica accounts of these negotiations do not support the later Spanish claim that he believed Cortés was Quetzalcóatl returning. He was a sophisticated ruler managing an unprecedented situation — and the situation was unprecedented in ways that would not become clear until it was too late.
