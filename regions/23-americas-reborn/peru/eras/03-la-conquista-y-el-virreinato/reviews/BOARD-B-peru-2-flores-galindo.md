# BOARD Review — B-peru-2 — Alberto Flores Galindo
## La Conquista y El Virreinato — Peru Era 3

**Role:** Andean cosmological framework, utopian tradition, Taki Onqoy continuity
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Overall Assessment

The chapter does what I spent my career arguing was possible: it treats the Andean cosmovision not as background context for a political event but as the event's structural logic. The pachakuti concept is not explained as a belief system; it operates as a physics. The Taki Onqoy is connected to the uprising not as a precursor but as the same eruption at an earlier moment. This is correct. This is what the Andean long durée actually looks like.

The harawi is the chapter's most important contribution to how this period should be written. The call-response structure as protection mechanism — the song's legibility as grief to the Spanish, its legibility as political theology to the Andean ear — is the chapter's deepest innovation. It names something that my own work gestured at without formalizing: the Andean oral tradition survived not only by going underground but by being formally legible to the oppressor as something harmless. The form itself was the disguise.

---

## Specific Observations

**On pachakuti:** The chapter uses pachakuti correctly — not as "revolution" or "uprising" but as a cosmological turning, a return, a going-around. The phrase "the world was not in political crisis. The world had structurally failed" (Section II, the Cajamarca passage) is the precise formulation I would use. The chapter understands the difference.

**On the Inkarri:** The chapter does not explicitly invoke the Inkarri myth — the belief that the Inca's severed head is buried and growing, waiting to rejoin its body. This is a choice. The Inkarri is the deepest expression of what the chapter is tracing, and its absence is notable. However, the chapter's closing argument — "what is given does not disappear, that the cord cut in the plaza is still attached at both ends" — is the Inkarri in structural terms, without naming it. I find this acceptable. The chapter earns the structure without needing the label.

**On the Taki Onqoy:** The chapter places the Taki Onqoy in Section III and treats it as the same theological physics as the later uprising, two centuries earlier. The sentence "Toledo had moved against both simultaneously, understanding that the cosmological resistance and the political resistance were the same thing. He was correct. They were the same thing" is exactly right.

**On the oral tradition's political function:** The chapter handles this through the harawi analysis. "You cannot arrest a song form" is the key sentence. This is historically and anthropologically accurate: the harawi tradition continued through the colonial period, and its survival is documented in linguistic records and subsequent rebellions.

**One recommendation:** The chapter mentions "the Pleiades star cluster" in the taki onqoy section, correctly noting that onqoy means both sickness and the Pleiades. The agricultural significance of the Pleiades in Andean cosmology — the Pleiades' rising and disappearing as markers of the planting cycle — is briefly noted. The chapter could add one more sentence here: in some Andean communities, the Pleiades' clarity in June predicted the harvest's quality. A clear cluster meant plenty; a dim or dispersed cluster meant scarcity. The word onqoy — sickness — was the word for when the Pleiades appeared dim or wrong. The Taki Onqoy was the world appearing wrong. Not a required addition, but it deepens the cosmological precision.

---

## Verdict

The chapter correctly handles the Andean cosmological tradition. The Inkarri omission is acceptable given how the landing earns the structure. The Pleiades precision could be deepened but is not required for accuracy. Cleared for FINAL.
