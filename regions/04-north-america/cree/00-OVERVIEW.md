# Cree — Néhiyawak, "The People Who Speak the Same Language"

They call themselves Néhiyawak in Plains Cree and related forms in the other Cree dialects — "the exact people" or "the people who speak the same language" — a name that points to linguistic community as the primary marker of identity across a vast geographic range. The Cree occupy one of the largest territories of any indigenous people in North America: from Labrador in the east to the Rocky Mountains in the west, from the boreal forest's southern edge to the treeline of the subarctic. This is not a single environment but several: the Maritime Cree of Quebec's James Bay coast, the Woodland Cree of the boreal forest interior, the Swampy Cree of the Hudson Bay lowlands, the Plains Cree who moved onto the Great Plains after acquiring horses in the eighteenth century. The dialects differ significantly; the ecological adaptations are distinct; the political traditions vary by region. What holds them together is the language family and the shared traditions of reciprocal relationship with the animal persons of the northern world.

The canoe was not the Cree's technology; it was their civilization. The birchbark canoe allowed the Cree to navigate the network of rivers, lakes, and portages that constituted the boreal forest's road system — a road system invisible to those without the knowledge to use it, perfectly suited to those with it. The knowledge of which portage to take between which lakes, where the rivers ran shallow in late summer, where the beaver built their lodges and the moose gathered at the shore — this was the accumulated expertise of generations, passed from grandparent to child in the canoe itself, learned through the practice of traveling. The canoe knowledge was also spiritual knowledge: the relationships with the waterways, the animals, and the spirits of the northern landscape that made successful travel possible were maintained through ceremony, prayer, and the correct handling of the animal persons who gave themselves to the hunter.

Key places:
- **James Bay (Wînipekw)** — the southern arm of Hudson Bay; the homeland of the James Bay Cree; the site of the James Bay hydro project (1971-present) and the consequent Cree resistance and legal battles
- **Hudson Bay lowlands** — the vast flat wetland between the Shield and Hudson Bay; Swampy Cree territory; the world's largest undrained peatland
- **Saskatchewan River system** — the major river artery of the western boreal; Plains Cree canoe route from the interior to the Bay
- **Cumberland House** — the first inland Hudson's Bay Company post (1774); a center of the fur trade and of Cree-European commercial interaction

## What Defined Them

**Reciprocity with animal persons.** The Cree understanding of the natural world was not of a resource to be harvested but of a community of persons — animal persons, plant persons, water persons, spirit persons — with whom the Cree community was in ongoing reciprocal relationship. The beaver who allowed itself to be caught by the hunter was not simply prey; it was a person making a gift, and the correct response was gratitude, ceremonial acknowledgment, and the careful use of the animal's body so that nothing was wasted. A hunter who was careless with the animal persons' gifts would find that the animals no longer offered themselves. This was not superstition; it was a governance framework for sustainable use of the boreal ecosystem's resources.

**The dream as guidance.** Cree spiritual life centered on the dream as the primary vehicle through which the spirits communicated with humans. The shaking tent ceremony — in which a specialist (the conjuror) entered a small structure that shook violently as spirits arrived — was the most dramatic form of spirit communication, but the dream was the everyday medium. Hunters dreamed of where the animals would be; healers received their knowledge through dreams; the course of journeys was modified based on the guidance that came in the night. The dream was not simply a personal mental event. It was information from the spirit world, to be read with the same care given to reading the tracks in the snow.

**The canoe and snowshoe as technologies of a world.** Two technologies made the Cree's vast territory navigable: the birchbark canoe in summer and the snowshoe in winter. Together, they transformed a landscape that would otherwise be impassable — the boreal forest's tangle of rivers, portages, muskeg, and deep snow — into a connected world. The knowledge of how to make and use both technologies was itself a form of wealth, and the people who possessed it had access to a territory that outsiders could not easily penetrate or follow. When the fur trade required the Cree to travel further and faster to supply the trading posts, the canoe and snowshoe proved adequate for the expanded demands — and when snowmobiles replaced them in the twentieth century, the knowledge of the old technologies became cultural capital rather than practical necessity.

**The fur trade and the maintained tradition.** The Cree's extensive participation in the Hudson's Bay Company's fur trade (the Company's motto, pro pelle cutem — "skin for skin" — captured the exchange with a Latin cynicism the Cree might have recognized as accurate) transformed the Cree economy without destroying the ceremonial and spiritual traditions that organized the community's relationship with the natural world. The Cree became expert commercial hunters, processing beaver and other furs for the trade while maintaining the reciprocity ceremonies that governed the relationship with the animal persons. The tension between the commercial logic (which required maximizing harvest) and the ceremonial logic (which required maintaining the relationship with the animals) was real, and it did eventually produce overtrapping and ecosystem disruption — but the tradition's durability through two centuries of fur trade is itself significant.

**Big Bear and Poundmaker — resistance and consequence.** When the Canadian government imposed Treaties 4 and 6 on the western Cree in the 1870s, two leaders — Big Bear (Mistahimaskwa) and Poundmaker (Pîhtokahanapiwiyin) — negotiated and ultimately resisted in ways that cost them their freedom. Big Bear refused to sign Treaty 6 until 1882, holding out for better terms that were not forthcoming. Both were imprisoned after the 1885 Riel Rebellion, in which some of their followers participated. Both died within a year of their release. What they had tried to do — maintain the Cree's freedom to move, to hunt, and to maintain their relationship with the land — was the specific thing the treaty system was designed to end. Their names are still spoken as the Cree's most honored resisters.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Boreal Foundation:** A Cree family is making the fall move from the lake where they spent the summer to the winter hunting territory where the moose are. The canoe is loaded. The grandmother is teaching the grandchildren the names of the portage marks — the specific trees and rock formations that indicate where the carry crosses between lakes. This knowledge is the map. The map is in the people.

**2 — The Fur Trade Era:** The Hudson's Bay Company post at the bay has been open for a decade. A Cree trapper arrives with a load of beaver pelts. The factor who receives him speaks no Cree; the trapper speaks no English. The trade language they are developing between them is Cree-inflected and European-vocabulary'd, and it is becoming the medium of the entire trade. The trapper goes home with metal tools and cloth. The beaver ceremony will be performed tonight.

**3 — The Plains Expansion:** A Plains Cree band is following the bison on the Saskatchewan prairie. They have been on the Plains for two generations. The bison economy is more productive than the forest economy was; the winters are harder, but the summers are extraordinary. The alliance with the Assiniboine has made both peoples stronger. The horse has changed everything, exactly as it changed things for every people who acquired it on the Plains.

**4 — The Treaties and the Reservation:** Big Bear is looking at the treaty document. The terms are unacceptable — they would confine his band to a reserve and require them to farm. His people have never farmed. They follow the bison. He refuses to sign. He will hold out for six years before starvation forces his hand. By then, the bison are gone.

**5 — The James Bay Fight and After:** In 1971, the Quebec government announces a hydroelectric project that will flood the traditional hunting territory of the James Bay Cree. The Cree file suit. The Quebec Superior Court rules in their favor. The project is suspended for nine months. Eventually, the James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement is negotiated — the first modern land claim agreement in Canada. The Cree have not won everything they wanted. They have established that they must be consulted. That is the beginning.
