# The Age of Gold

The weight was what he noticed first. Not the smell of the city, not the sound of the drums carrying south across the flat land, but the weight leaving him. Three months on the road and the salt had become his body: the ache of it, the constant rocking forward in the saddle to compensate for the load shifting on the camels behind him, the way he had learned to sleep sitting up so the rocking did not stop. Now the camels were offloaded. The slabs lay on the stone floor of the merchant quarter's warehouse, chalky-white and massive, each one the labor of men who lived and died cutting salt from the earth at Taghaza, and the merchant from Sijilmasa sat in the shade of a mud-brick wall and felt his spine unknot, vertebra by vertebra, from the neck down.

He was thirsty. He drank water that tasted of the clay jar it had been stored in, and he was grateful for it, and around him Kumbi Saleh went about its business.

This was the city al-Bakri would describe forty years later, in the year 460 of the Hijra, from a house in Cordoba where he read the reports of merchants who had made this crossing. He never crossed the sand himself. He wrote from reports, merchant to merchant, generation to generation, so that what he recorded was not the city as any one man had seen it but the city as the caravans collectively remembered it: the stone town to the west, twelve mosques, their minarets pale against the late-afternoon sky; the royal city one mile east, where no mosque stood and no minaret rose, but where the drums began before dawn and the sacred grove held its darkness behind the palace wall. Two cities, one mile apart, each incomplete without the other. The merchant quarter needed Islamic law and standardized weights and courts that a man from Sijilmasa could trust to judge a contract dispute fairly. The royal city needed the merchants, because gold from the Wangara fields to the south and salt from Taghaza to the north produced wealth only when they met in one place, and that place was the Ghana's capital, and the Ghana taxed every load that entered and every load that left.

One dinar on a load of salt coming in. Two dinars on a load going out. Gold nuggets above a certain weight: the Ghana's, kept in the royal treasury as a hedge against flood. Gold dust: the traders', because dust could flow and nuggets would pool. This was not arbitrary. It was a system maintained for generations, each rule the residue of a decision someone had made about how to keep the caravans coming and the balance holding.

The merchant from Sijilmasa understood these rules the way a man understands his own bones. They were not external. They were the conditions of his life, and his father's life before him, and his grandfather's before that. The route from Sijilmasa south to Kumbi Saleh was older than any man alive could say. It was the weight of salt going south and the weight of gold coming north, and in between it was the weight of forty days of desert, and the weight of the crossing was the price of everything else.

He paid his toll at the customs house. A clerk weighed the salt and scratched the figure into a tablet. Three other caravans had arrived this week. The price of salt was lower than last season.

That evening, from his place in the merchant quarter, he heard the drums begin in the royal city to the east. Low and resonant, struck with padded sticks that made the sound travel in the chest rather than the ears. Audience day tomorrow. The Ghana would sit beneath his golden umbrella and the court would arrange itself around him: horses in their gold-embroidered blankets, pages with golden shields, the dogs on their gold and silver leads, and the people would come and fall to their knees and pour dust over their heads, and the ones who were Muslim would clap their hands instead, and both gestures meant the same thing: that the order held. That the Ghana still stood between the Soninke and the forces that made the gold come out of the ground and the rains come down from the sky. That the covenant was intact.

He had seen the court, once, from a distance. He had not been permitted inside. He had heard the drums and watched the dust rise and glimpsed, far off, a figure under a canopy of gold, and he had thought: this is the other city. This is the city that makes my city possible.

The negotiation began the next morning, in the cool hours before the heat returned. The buyer was Soninke, a man who knew salt prices the way the merchant knew desert routes, and the talking would go on until midday or later, the two of them moving through prices with the patience of men who understood that patience was also a skill. The merchant's back ached. He had slept on the mat in the merchant quarter and woken already calculating.

Around him, in both cities, the arrangement that had made the western Sudan rich continued to do what it had done for generations. Salt came south. Gold went north. Between them, at the point where they met, the Ghana held the balance.

He held it because it held him. And because it held, everything else held too.
