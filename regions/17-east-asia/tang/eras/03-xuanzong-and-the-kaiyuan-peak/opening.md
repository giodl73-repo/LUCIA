# The High Tang

Du Fu arrived in Chang'an in the autumn of the twenty-third year of the Kaiyuan reign (735 CE), and the city was larger than anything he had prepared himself for.

He came from the east, through the Chunming Gate, where the road from Luoyang entered the capital along the central avenue that ran straight as a bowstring from the eastern wall to the western. The avenue was wide enough for twelve carriages abreast. On either side, the walls of the wards rose — rammed earth faced with plaster, each ward a city within the city, gated and closed at the drumbeat of curfew. He could not see over them. He could not see the end of the street. Chang'an held perhaps a million people within its walls, and another million in the suburbs and satellite towns that clung to it, and all of them were invisible behind those walls, living in the gridded order that the Sui had laid down and the Tang had filled with the noise and mess of a civilization that believed itself the center of everything under heaven.

Du Fu was twenty-three. He had failed the jinshi examination that spring — the examination that, if passed, would open the door to an official career, a salary paid in grain and silk, a life of service to the Son of Heaven. Two hundred thousand candidates sat for the keju system's various examinations each year. Fewer than thirty passed the jinshi. Du Fu had written his poems, composed his essays on the classics, demonstrated his mastery of the regulated verse forms that the examiners prized, and he had not been among the thirty. He would try again. He would try again for the next ten years, and fail each time, and the failure would shape everything he wrote.

But that autumn he was not yet the poet of suffering. He was young and he was in Chang'an and the city was astonishing.

The West Market opened at midday, when the drum in the market tower sounded and the gates swung wide. Du Fu would have smelled it before he saw it — sesame oil frying in the Hu restaurants where Sogdian cooks made flatbreads stuffed with lamb and spices that had no Chinese names, because the spices came from Persia and the cooks came from Samarkand and the recipes belonged to a world that stretched from Chang'an to Baghdad along roads the Tang kept open. In the market, a Persian could sell glass. A Silla Korean could sell ginseng. An Arab could sell incense. A Uyghur could sell horses. They traded in Tang copper cash, the kaiyuan tongbao — "currency of the inaugurated era" — stamped with the same four characters in every city from Guangzhou to the Tarim Basin, because the Tang mint was the common language of a commercial world that reached further than any army.

There were Nestorian Christians in Chang'an. There were Zoroastrians tending their sacred fires. There were Manicheans and Jews and followers of every Buddhist school from Theravada to Tantric, and the Tang court did not merely tolerate them — it patronized them, debated with them, gave them land for their temples and monasteries, because Xuanzong's Chang'an operated on the principle that all wisdom could be absorbed without threat. The Daqin monastery — the Nestorian church — stood within the city walls, its liturgy conducted in Syriac while the monks wore Tang robes. The Zoroastrian temple burned its eternal flame three wards from the imperial palace. These were not curiosities. They were the architecture of a confidence so immense that it could welcome any god without fearing for its own.

Du Fu walked through this. He wrote about it, later — not the market specifically, not the temples, but the feeling of a city where everything converged and the convergence was itself the meaning. In a poem written years afterward, looking back on Chang'an from the ruin of civil war, he remembered the capital as a place where "the nation, though shattered, its mountains and rivers remain." The mountains and rivers remained. The city he walked through in the autumn of Kaiyuan 23 did not.

He did not know that yet. No one did. Xuanzong sat in the Daming Palace on the city's northern edge, and the court around him was brilliant — Yang Guifei danced the Rainbow Skirt and Feathered Garment to music composed by the emperor himself, and the pipa players competed for imperial favor, and Li Bai was somewhere in the empire writing poems that made men weep into their wine. The Tang had been building toward this moment for a century: the Kaiyuan reign, the "Inaugurated Era," when the empire was rich, the borders were secure, the Silk Road was open, and poetry was the currency of political life.

Du Fu could not have known, standing in the West Market in the smell of sesame and lamb and foreign spice, that he was standing at the top of something. That the man on the northeastern frontier — An Lushan, half-Sogdian, half-Turkic, already gathering power — would, in twenty years, break it all open. That the city would burn. That the emperor would flee. That the poetry Du Fu had not yet written would become the most important record of what it meant to live through the destruction of a world that had believed itself permanent.

All of that was coming. But in the autumn of Kaiyuan 23, the drum sounded, the market gates opened, and a young man who had failed his examination walked into the crowd and disappeared among a million people who did not yet know they were living in the last years of something.
