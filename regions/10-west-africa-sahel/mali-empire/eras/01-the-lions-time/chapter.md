# The Lion's Time

## I. The Crawling

The boy could not walk.

The jelilu who carry this story begin here, at the lowest point, because the lowest point is where the story earns its meaning. Sogolon Kedju had come to Niani from the land of Do, a woman whose back was bent, whose appearance the court mocked, and whom the king Maghan Kon Fatta married on the word of a hunter-diviner who said she would bear a son who would shake the world. That son was born, and his name was Sundiata, and he could not walk.

Not for a year. Not for two. Not for seven. The jelilu vary on the number because the jelilu are not a single text. They are a living argument, carried by families whose telling reflects the politics of the moment and the truth of the lineage. Fa-Digi Sisoko's version says seven years. Djeli Mamoudou Kouyate, speaking to Djibril Tamsir Niane in the 1950s, says the boy crawled until the day he chose to stand. What the versions share: the son of the king dragged himself through the red laterite dust of the compound at Niani, through the smoke of the cooking fires and the scatter of groundnut shells, while the women of the court stepped around him and his mother wept and the king's first wife, Sassouma Berete, laughed where the laughter would carry.

That? she said. That is the lion who will rule the Manden?

The laughter was the point. In the Manden's understanding, a ruler who has not suffered cannot be trusted with power. The one who has been low knows the weight of the ground. The one who has been mocked knows what cruelty costs. Sundiata's years in the dust are not a misfortune the story must overcome. They are the qualification the story requires.

The compound at Niani sat in the river country of the upper Niger, where the rains came heavy between June and October and the dry season cracked the earth into patterns like old pottery. Shea trees grew in the fields beyond the walls. Women pressed the shea nuts for butter that smelled of smoke and green wood, and the smell hung in the air of the compound alongside the dust and the woodfires and the faint wet breath of the river when the wind came from the east. Chickens moved through the compound. Children ran, and Sundiata watched them run. This was the heartland of the Manden, the country the Mande people had named for themselves, and it was here that the boy who could not walk would either stand or be forgotten.

The day he stood, the iron staff bent under his hands.

This is in every version. The blacksmith brought a rod of iron, and the boy gripped the rod and pulled himself upright, and the rod bent like a drawn bow under the force. Then he walked. Then, in some tellings, he ran. Then, in others, the ground trembled and the women who had mocked his mother fell silent. In Niani the baobab trees still grow where he was said to have pulled them from the earth and carried them to Sogolon's door, roots trailing and dirt falling from them, because she had asked for baobab leaves and he would not bring her leaves alone when he could bring the whole tree.

The jelilu do not tell this to make Sundiata a wonder. They tell it because the Manden needs to know what kind of strength was being forged in the dust of that compound. A boy who bends iron with his grip is a boy whose suffering has been transmuted into something the Manden can use. The iron bent. The boy stood. The story could begin.

## II. The Exile Road

But standing was not enough to save him.

Sassouma Berete wanted the succession for her own son, Dankaran Touman, and Sogolon knew what that meant. In the politics of a court with multiple wives and multiple heirs, the one with the weakest patron must leave or die. Sogolon chose to leave. She gathered Sundiata and her other children and walked out through the gate of Niani and onto the road, and the road would be their home for years.

The road ran through the Manden in every direction, through savanna country where the grass grew tall in the wet season and burnt gold in the dry, where the baobab trees stood like inverted roots against the sky and the smoke of village fires marked the distance between one compound and the next. Sogolon carried what she could. Her children walked. The exile road in the Manden was not simply banishment. It was a social category with its own logic. The exile becomes a guest, and the host who takes in the exile assumes obligations under the jatigui system that governed hospitality across the Mande world. A host who harms a guest has violated something deeper than manners. The exile, in turn, owes the host a debt that may take a generation to repay. So Sogolon and her children moved from court to court inside this web of obligation, and at each court the web held them, and at each court Sundiata watched and learned.

They came to Wagadou, where the old empire's memory still lingered in the stone ruins and the trade roads that ran north toward the desert. They came to Tabon. At each court Sogolon presented herself and her children, and at each court the jatigui took them in, and at each court the hospitality held for a season or a year before the road called again. They came at last to Mema, in the Sahel country toward the north, where the land flattened and dried and the grass grew thin and yellow in the dry months and the trees gave way to thorn scrub and the horizons opened until the sky was the largest thing in the world. The king of Mema took them in. Here Sundiata grew from a boy into a young man, and here he became a hunter.

The bush country around Mema held antelope and buffalo and the cats that followed the herds, and the young exile learned to track in the Sahelian grass, to read the bent stalks and the scattered dung and the faint prints left in the sandy soil at the edges of waterholes. He learned to move silently through thorn scrub in the heat of midday, when the air shimmered above the ground and the only sound was the whirr of grasshoppers and the call of the francolin from somewhere in the dry brush. He learned the patience of the stalk and the quickness of the kill.

The jelilu say he became a hunter of renown, and hunting in the Manden was not sport. It was a knowledge system, a practice that connected a man to the wild spirits of the bush, to the nyama, the dangerous energy that flows through all living things and concentrates in blood and iron and the boundary spaces between the human world and the wild. The hunter who walks into the bush walks between worlds. The hunter who returns brings knowledge back. Sundiata's years of hunting in the Mema bush were his second education, following his first, which had been the dust of the compound and the sound of laughter that was meant to break him.

He learned something else in those exile years. At every court that sheltered him, he watched how a king held power. He saw courts where the jeli sang the genealogy and the people listened, because the genealogy placed them, gave them their position in the web of clans and obligations that made the Mande world cohere. He saw courts where the king gave generously, where food and cloth and metal moved outward from the king's hand to the hands of those who served him, and the generosity bound the recipients in ties that were stronger than fear because they were ties of mutual need. He saw, too, the courts where the king did not give, where the king hoarded and the court thinned and the people's loyalty thinned with it.

And he heard, from the travelers and traders who moved along the Niger and the overland routes, what Sumanguru Kante was doing to the Manden he had left behind.

## III. The Smith-King

Sumanguru Kante, king of Sosso, held the Manden by the throat.

He had come out of the wreckage of the old Wagadu, the state the Arabic sources called Ghana, whose power had fractured in the generations after the Almoravid disruptions of the eleventh century. The Sosso were a Mande-speaking people, and Sumanguru's family, the Kante, were smiths. In the Manden world, smiths occupied a position of extraordinary power and ambiguity. They belonged to the nyamakalaw, the category of specialists whose skill gave them access to nyama, the volatile spiritual energy that ordinary people could not safely handle. Smiths worked iron, and iron came from the earth, and the earth held the dead, and the dead held knowledge that the living needed and feared. The sound of the hammer on the anvil carried across the compound at dusk, and that sound meant power, and the power was not to be touched by those who did not understand it.

Sumanguru's power was rooted in this. The jelilu describe him as a man whose skin could turn to iron, who kept a room of fetishes, of owls and snakes and human skins, whose accumulated power made him invulnerable. He played the bala, the wooden xylophone, and the sound of his playing held a force that no jeli could match. This is not metaphor translated for an outside audience. This is the Manden's own language for describing a king whose authority came from a source the covenant did not sanction.

And the difference between the sources of authority mattered more than any other difference.

A mansa who ruled through the covenant held power because the clans had agreed to give it to him, and the agreement was public, spoken aloud before the jelilu who would remember it. A king who ruled through sorcery held power because he possessed knowledge that others could not access or contest. The first kind of power required generosity, because a mansa who does not give is not a mansa. The second kind required only fear. The first was accountable, because the jelilu who witnessed the agreement could recite its terms to anyone who asked. The second was opaque, because the sorcerer's knowledge was held in secret rooms where no jeli could enter.

Sumanguru's Sosso controlled the territory that had made the old Wagadu wealthy: the routes connecting the goldfields of Bambuk and Bure to the Saharan crossings where Saharan salt came south and Wangara gold went north. Gold was the blood of the trade. Salt was the bone. The merchants who moved along these routes paid Sumanguru, and the revenue gave him the material power to enforce what his spiritual power claimed. The clans of the Manden who had once governed their own affairs, who had negotiated and agreed and relied on the jelilu to hold the terms, now answered to a king who had not been given authority by assembly but had seized it through force and forge-craft.

He killed the leaders of the founding clans. He took women from families that had not offered them. He captured Bala Faseke Kouyate, Sundiata's own jeli, because Sumanguru wanted the jeli's skill the way he wanted all skill: as a thing to be possessed, not a relationship to be honored.

The Manden's memory of Sumanguru is not hatred. It is diagnosis. His power was real and in its own terms coherent. A smith-king ruling through forge-knowledge and spiritual force was not an impossibility in the Manden world. It was a genuine alternative to the covenant model. But it violated the principle on which the Manden understood legitimate rule: that authority must be given, not seized; that the king serves the covenant, not the other way around; that power held in public, witnessed by the jelilu, is the only power that can be called just.

The messengers found Sundiata in Mema. Come back, they said. Only you can face what Sumanguru has become.

## IV. Kirina

The jelilu do not describe the gathering of the army the way a military historian would. They describe it as a homecoming.

Sundiata left Mema and moved south through the Sahel and into the savanna, and as he moved, the allies came. Tabon Wana Fran Kamara came from Tabon with his archers. Kamandjan came from Sibi. Tiramakhan Traore came from the west. Fakoli Koroma, Sumanguru's own nephew, came because Sumanguru had taken his wife, and this violation of the kinship obligations was the crack that split the Sosso alliance from within. The jelilu name each ally because each name is a lineage that still carries the story, and the descendants of those allies still carry the pride of having answered the call. To name them is to honor them. To forget one is to insult a family.

They gathered on the plains of Kirina, in the country between the Niger and the hills, where the savanna grass grew tall enough to hide a crouching man and the soil was red and the air tasted of laterite dust and dry grass. It was the dry season, and the ground was hard. The wells were low. The harmattan had passed but its haze still hung at the edges of the sky, giving the morning light a quality like tarnished bronze. Warriors from a dozen allied clans stood in companies defined by lineage, each company with its own jeli to sing its name and its own instruments to keep its courage. The drums had been speaking for days, the dundun carrying messages between the camps in the tonal language of the Mande, saying: we are here, we are ready, the lion has returned.

The jelilu give the year as what corresponds roughly to 1235 of the common reckoning, though the precision of the date matters less to the tradition than the meaning of what happened on that plain.

What happened at Kirina is told as a battle, but it is understood as a riddle.

Sumanguru's skin could turn to iron. Weapons could not touch him. The arrows broke against him. The spears bent. His power held as long as the source of his power remained intact. But every form of power has a point of failure, and the point of failure of Sumanguru's power was known, or discovered, or revealed, depending on which jeli carries the telling. The spur of a white rooster. An arrow tipped with the ergot of the rooster's claw. Some versions say Sundiata's sister Nana Triban learned the secret by going to Sumanguru's chamber. Some say Bala Faseke, the captured jeli, found a way to send word. Some say the knowledge came from the hunters' divination, from the consultation with the spirits of the bush where Sundiata had spent his exile years learning to read the language between the human world and the wild.

What the versions agree on: an arrow found its mark. The thing that could not be pierced was pierced. Sumanguru fell, or fled, or vanished into the stone of a mountain called Koulikoro, depending on which family tells it. The jelilu differ, and the difference is the tradition breathing.

The riddle at the heart of Kirina is the Manden's own way of encoding a truth about power: that every form of authority carries its own vulnerability, and the one who understands the vulnerability can defeat the one who holds the power. Sundiata's victory came not from superior numbers or better weapons. It came from knowledge, the right knowledge, found through the right relationships, applied at the right moment. The covenant defeated the sorcery because the covenant built alliances and the sorcery built only subjects. Sumanguru, for all his power, stood alone. Sundiata stood with Kamara and Koroma and Traore and Konde and a dozen other names the jelilu still sing. The man who held his power in secret was broken by the man who held his power in public, and the public power was greater because it was shared.

After Kirina, Sumanguru was gone. His Sosso scattered into the hills and the bush, and the clans that had served him by compulsion now had to choose whether to join the alliance or disappear into the countryside. Most joined. The power that had held them was broken, and the power that replaced it was offering something Sumanguru never had: terms. The Manden was free, and the question that rose from the dust of that battlefield was the question that would define the next three centuries: free to become what?

## V. The Great Covenant

The answer came at the assembly.

The jelilu describe the gathering after Kirina as the moment when the Manden was made. Not conquered, not unified by force, but made, by agreement, between free peoples who had fought together and now had to decide how to live together. They gathered at a place the tradition calls Kurukan Fuga, the clearing, the open space, and what they produced was not a legal document but a covenant spoken aloud before witnesses who would carry it in their memory for the generations to come.

The clearing itself was open ground, the kind of space where assemblies happened in the Mande world: flat, visible, a place where no one could hide and where the voice of the speaker could reach every listener. The clan leaders sat in their delegations. The warriors who had fought at Kirina stood behind their leaders. The jelilu stood between them, because the jeli's position is always between: between the speaker and the listener, between the past and the present, between the words being spoken and the memory that will carry them.

Sundiata spoke, and the clan leaders spoke, and the jelilu stood and listened with the attention of people whose life's work was about to begin, because everything spoken at this assembly would become their charge to carry, their burden and their purpose for the centuries to come. What was said became the structure of the new state.

The Kurukan Fuga named the categories. It named the horon, the freeborn nobles, the warriors, the farmers whose status derived from their lineage and their land. It named the nyamakalaw, the smiths, the leatherworkers, the jelilu themselves, the specialists whose skill gave them access to nyama and whose social position was powerful and circumscribed at once. It named the jon, those in servitude, whose position within the Manden's social order was a category with recognized roles, obligations, and paths of incorporation into the larger whole. And it named what each owed to the others.

The horon owed protection. The nyamakalaw owed their skill. The jon owed their labor. And the mansa owed the most of all. The mansa owed generosity. A king who does not give is not a king. A king who hoards is a king who has broken the covenant. This was the structural logic that would drive the Mali state for the next three centuries. It was the logic that would send Mansa Musa to Cairo with five hundred bearers of gold a hundred years later, spending so freely that the gold market in Egypt crashed for a decade, because the generosity was not personal extravagance. The generosity was the obligation. The obligation was the proof. The proof was the kingship. Every mansa who followed Sundiata would be measured against this standard, and the jelilu would do the measuring, and the jelilu would not forget.

The Kurukan Fuga named other things. It named the sanankuya, the joking relationships between certain clans, which defused tension through ritualized insult. A Keita and a Coulibaly could say unspeakable things to each other, and the saying of them discharged the pressure that might otherwise become violence. A Traore and a Diarra could trade mockeries that would be killing insults between strangers, and both would laugh, and the laughter was the mechanism that kept the peace. This was conflict resolution woven into the social fabric at the level of daily interaction, maintained not by judges or soldiers but by the web of clan relationships that the jelilu held in memory.

The covenant named the right of women to speak in the assembly. The versions collected by scholars record this explicitly, and the provision was not ornamental. Women in the Manden held property, managed trade, and wielded influence in the compound and beyond it. Sogolon Kedju herself had made the decision that saved Sundiata's life. The covenant recognized what the Manden already practiced: that the voice of women in council was not a gift to be granted but a reality to be affirmed.

It named the obligations of hosts to guests, under the jatigui system that had sheltered Sundiata during his exile years, encoding into the covenant the principle that hospitality is not optional generosity but structural obligation. It named what could not be taken by force and what could only be given freely. It named provisions relating to the welfare of all, the prohibition against certain forms of violence between clans, and the responsibility of the strong toward the weak.

The jelilu who carried these provisions did not carry a text. They carried an argument. And the argument could be adapted to new circumstances because it was held in living memory, not fixed on a surface. When a dispute arose between clans a generation later, the jeli who knew the Kurukan Fuga could speak to the specific obligation that applied. When a new mansa took the throne, the jeli who sang the genealogy placed him within the covenant's framework, reminding him, in public, before the assembled court, what the covenant required of a king. Written law can be hidden from those it governs, can be altered by those who hold power over the scribes. Spoken law, carried by a caste whose entire social identity depends on remembering accurately and performing publicly, is harder to silence without silencing the people themselves.

The assembly at Kurukan Fuga was the founding of a covenant-state, held together by spoken obligations, enforced by social memory, animated by the principle that legitimate authority flows upward from the consent of the governed, not downward from the power of the ruler. This was not a principle imported from outside. It was the principle the Manden had learned from its own history: from the suffering of Sundiata, who knew what it meant to have nothing, and from the tyranny of Sumanguru, who showed what happened when a king answered to no one.

Sundiata was mansa. The Lion of the Manden ruled from Niani, where the Niger carried trade and the jelilu carried the law. The gold of Bambuk and Bure flowed through his territory. The salt from the Saharan mines at Taghaza came south along routes his armies now protected. The Manden grew. Not by force alone, though there was force. By the logic of the covenant: join, and the covenant protects you; join, and the covenant obliges you; join, and your jeli and our jeli will remember the terms.

## VI. What the Jelilu Carry

The scholars who came later would look for Sundiata in documents and find almost nothing. Ibn Khaldun, writing in the 1370s, gives a genealogy that partially overlaps with the oral tradition but does not describe Kirina or the Kurukan Fuga. Al-Umari, writing in Damascus in the 1330s from informants in Cairo who knew the Mali court, describes the empire that Sundiata built but not the man himself. There is no contemporary written account of the Battle of Kirina. There is no written text of the Kurukan Fuga from the thirteenth century.

The record is the jelilu.

This is not a gap to be apologized for. It is the nature of the Manden's chosen form of institutional memory. The jelilu carry the Sundiata Epic not as a fixed text but as a living performance, and the variations between the versions of Djeli Mamoudou Kouyate and Fa-Digi Sisoko and Wa Kamissoko are not degradations of an original. They are the tradition doing what it was designed to do: preserving the essential structure while adapting the emphasis to the needs of the moment and the politics of the patron. A jeli who tells the story in Kangaba in the year of a Keita patron will shape the telling differently from a jeli who tells it in Kita for a Koroma audience. Both are accurate. Both are the tradition.

What the versions agree on across centuries and across jeli families and across the political shifts that remade the Manden again and again is this: that Sundiata could not walk, that he stood, that he was exiled, that he returned, that he defeated Sumanguru at Kirina, that the Kurukan Fuga was proclaimed after victory. This agreement is not less reliable than a written chronicle. It is differently reliable. Written records preserve the words of the moment but freeze them. Oral records preserve the structure of the meaning but allow the words to breathe. Each has what the other lacks. Neither is the shadow of the other.

The Kurukan Fuga itself survived in this way. In 2009, UNESCO recognized the Manden Charter, the oral tradition of the Kurukan Fuga, as an intangible cultural heritage of humanity. The recognition was of a living tradition, not a historical artifact. At Kangaba, in the heartland of the Manden, the Keita family still maintains the sacred grove where the covenant is renewed in ceremonies that occur on cycles measured in years, roughly every seven, and the jelilu still recite the obligations, and the recitation is not a museum performance. It is the covenant being kept alive by the act of speaking it. The roof of the sacred house is rebuilt, the words are spoken again, and the Manden is remade in the speaking.

Sundiata died sometime around 1255. The jelilu vary on the manner. Some say he drowned in the Sankarani River. Some say he was killed by an arrow at a festival. Some say he simply passed into the world of the ancestors, which is the Manden's way of saying that a man has completed what he was sent to do. The manner of the death matters less to the tradition than what he left behind. He left the covenant. He left the structure. He left the jelilu who would carry both.

And the jelilu carry them still. Not in a library, not in an archive, not on a shelf. In the voice. In the pause before the genealogy begins, the pause where the jeli gathers the silence and the silence gathers the listeners and the listeners become the vessel into which the words will be poured. In the sound of the bala, the same bala, the Sosso Bala, that Sumanguru once seized and that the Kouyate family has held for eight centuries in the town of Niagassola, playing it on the occasions the covenant requires. The instrument that was taken by force was returned by the covenant, and it has been speaking ever since.

The boy who could not walk stood. The man who stood walked into exile. The exile walked back. The one who came back defeated the one whose skin was iron. And the one who defeated the iron-skinned king did not make himself a tyrant. He called an assembly. He spoke the covenant. He asked the jelilu to remember.

They remember.
