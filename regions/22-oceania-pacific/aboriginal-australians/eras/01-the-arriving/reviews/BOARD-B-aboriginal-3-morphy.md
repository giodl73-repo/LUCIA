# B-aboriginal-3 — Howard Morphy — Board Review of "When the Ancestors Walked"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Aboriginal art as knowledge system; Yolngu epistemology; the relationship between visual representation, ceremony, and law.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The Gwion Gwion figures are accurately described: thin, dynamic, featuring elaborate headdresses, carrying boomerangs and spears. The dating (c. 17,000 BP) is consistent with current estimates, though the chapter correctly notes this is tentative.
- The Wandjina repainting tradition is accurately described as a living ceremony. The connection between repainting and maintaining the Ancestor's presence is correct.
- The X-ray art description (internal organs visible, bones and digestive system drawn inside the body outline) is accurate for the Arnhem Land tradition. The characterization of it as "simultaneously anatomical, spiritual, and practical" captures the multi-register nature of the art.
- The Lake Mungo evidence (Mungo Lady's cremation, Mungo Man's ochre burial) is accurately described. The c. 40,000 BP date is consistent with current scholarship.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter treats art as knowledge throughout. "A Wandjina painting on a Kimberley wall is not a historical document. It is a living ceremony in pigment." This is the correct framing.
- The Songline as a cross-language knowledge system is accurately described. The melody-constant/language-variable feature is well-attested.
- The kwertengerle role is correctly described as a secondary custodian with maintenance responsibilities, distinct from the primary custodian's authority.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The integration argument. Country as the point where song, fire, kinship, ceremony, and art converge. This is the most accurate single statement available about how Aboriginal knowledge works.
- The "dorsal fin" metaphor for the visible archaeological record. "For every painting on a rock wall, there were a thousand songs." This is exactly right. The material record is a fraction of the knowledge system.
- The triple-redundancy insight (song, landscape, kinship) as the mechanism of persistence. This is a genuine and defensible structural observation.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter says Arnhem Land has "X-ray paintings" and places the Kimberley art in a separate region, which is correct, but it then says "In the shelters of the southeast, different traditions" and immediately returns to Arnhem Land. The geography is slightly confused here. The "southeast" shelters should be specified (which southeast? New South Wales? Victoria?) or the label should be removed.
- The phrase "In Arnhem Land, X-ray paintings" should note that X-ray art is not exclusive to Arnhem Land, though it reaches its greatest elaboration there.
- The chapter does not mention bark painting, which is a major tradition in Arnhem Land and one of the primary vehicles for Yolngu knowledge transmission. This is an absence rather than an error, but the chapter's argument about art-as-knowledge would be strengthened by noting that the tradition is not confined to rock surfaces.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (fix the geography confusion in the art section; consider adding a note on bark painting)
