# Panel-2 Review — P-4 Wade Davis (Targeted: Notes / Production Bible)
**Era:** Jamaica Era 5 — The Sound of Freedom
**Date:** 2026-04-17

The notes' treatment of Rastafari as an epistemological system is correct. The section plan addresses my primary concern from Panel-1: Section 3 (What Rastafari Knew) and Section 4 (The Music at Method Level) together constitute the knowledge-system-at-method-level treatment the chapter requires. If both sections deliver at the depth the notes promise, the chapter will meet the v1.3 and v1.5 Evidence standards.

**What the notes need to add on knowledge transmission:**

The reasoning session needs specific description. Not "men sat in a circle and passed the chalice and spoke." Describe the structure: does the reasoning begin with scripture? Does each man speak in turn, or does the spirit move? What is the relationship between the herb and the reasoning? How long do sessions last? The methodology is the knowledge system operating. I need to be able to see it.

The answer to "what happened when Selassie died?" is partly in the notes (he became more fully divine by dying — "Jah does not die") but not at method level. How did the theological adaptation occur? Who reasoned it out? Was it a community consensus or did specific elders pronounce? The Rastafari tradition has no church hierarchy, no pope, no council. The theological revision was distributed and community-based. This is itself a fact about the knowledge system: decentralized transmission, which is why it could not be suppressed.

**The ital food and the body-as-site:**

The notes mention ital food and locks but the section plan does not fully integrate the body as the site of the theology. The Rasta body is not incidental. The refusal to eat flesh, the refusal to cut hair, the refusal to alter the body — these are not personal choices. They are a complete theory of what the body is for and what it means to live in Babylon without being of Babylon. Marley's refusal to amputate the toe is the most dramatic instance of this theology applied literally: the Nazarite vow makes the body whole, and no doctor's surgery supersedes Jah's design. The chapter should show this at method level in Section 7 (The Death of the Prophet).

**One thing the notes almost miss:**

The women. The notes' figures section does not include any named women. Carolyn Cooper's work (listed in Sources) is precisely on the gender politics of Jamaican popular culture. The Queens of Rastafari — their role in the yards, their specific exclusions (women were often not permitted to attend reasoning sessions during menstruation, per Levitical purity codes that the Rasta adopted), and their creative contributions — need at least one named figure. Rita Marley is the most documented (she was one of the I-Threes, the backing vocal trio; she was also shot in the assassination attempt and survived). She should appear in the chapter.

**Overall:** The knowledge-system treatment will be strong if Section 3 delivers at method level. The body-theology connection needs to be in Section 7. Add Rita Marley to the figures.
