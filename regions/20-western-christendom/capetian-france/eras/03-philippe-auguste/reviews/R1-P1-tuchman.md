# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman
## Panel-1 Review: Opening, Philippe Auguste et la Grande Expansion

**Round:** 1
**Reviewer:** Barbara Tuchman (P-1)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The rider arriving at dusk. That is how you open a chapter. Not "Philip II, born in 1165, came to the throne at the age of fifteen." A rider, dark with sweat, and men who can read the news in how he sits his horse before he dismounts. This is primary-source instinct — Joinville wrote this way, the chroniclers of Saint-Denis wrote this way, and the author has captured the method perfectly.

What I particularly admire: the arc of the opening is not Bouvines itself but the *meaning* of Bouvines. The battle is the hinge, not the subject. The real subject — which the author correctly identifies — is the transformation of France from a nominal kingdom into a dominant European power. The opening earns this claim by showing us what had to happen first: thirty-four years of patient work, the exploitation of feudal obligation, the siege of Château Gaillard. The reader understands the transformation because they have watched it accumulate.

The Château Gaillard paragraph is the chapter's best: "Philip's engineers had found the latrine shaft. The story was told with relish at the French court." That word "relish" does everything. It tells you this is a French story told from the French side, with French pleasure in English embarrassment. It tells you something about the culture's self-confidence. And the following line — "God, it seemed, had not been given the assignment" — is a sentence that Joinville himself might have written.

The final line — "He had eighteen months left" — is nearly perfect. It opens the abyss without editorializing, without "tragically" or "sadly." The reader has just watched Philip's triumph and is dropped, cleanly, into mortality. The desire to read on is not manufactured; it is earned.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening establishes Philip's political genius — the feudal exploitation, the coalition-breaking, the patient accumulation — but the sacred dimension of his kingship is present mainly as label. "Rex Christianissimus" is named, and the anointing at Reims is mentioned, but the reader doesn't yet FEEL the thaumaturgical weight. The chapter brief specifies sacred-monarchy, thaumaturgical, as a primary voice element. In the opening we have the institutional and the military. The chapter will need to let the sacred kingship breathe — not explain it but demonstrate it. What does it feel like to be in the presence of a king who heals by touch? What does it mean to Philip's subjects that he is anointed with the Holy Ampulla? The opening gestures; the chapter must deliver.

This is an opening that earns its chapter. The reader wants to go where Philip went.
