## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of The Age of Equals (Opening)

**Overall:** This is one of the strongest openings I have read in the project. It does what the best narrative history does: it makes a diplomatic system feel like a thriller.

**What works:**
- The opening line ("The messenger came from Egypt with gold") is clean, concrete, and forward-moving. It earns the second sentence.
- The inventory of gifts is not decorative. Each item (earrings, armlets, linen thread count) does double work: sensory grounding AND characterization of the diplomatic system. The linen "found acceptable" is particularly good, because it reveals the receiving court's standards without a lecture.
- The delayed reveal of the purpose ("The pharaoh wanted a woman") is excellent pacing. The reader has already absorbed the weight of the gifts before understanding their function.
- The Gilukhipa precedent (317 attendants, recorded on a scarab) is a brilliant structural echo: history repeating within living memory, raising the stakes for the present request.
- The closing image (horses worked in circles, the system running on the same logic) lands as both scene and metaphor without feeling forced.

**What doesn't:**
- At ~1100 words, the opening is about 300 words over target. The middle section where the system of five great kings is explained could be tightened. The Babylonian gold-leaf anecdote is vivid but could be held for the chapter.
- Tushratta "considering" the request is told rather than shown. I want to see him in the room. What is he looking at? Who is with him? The scene pulls back slightly into summary at that pivot.

**The question I'd push on:**
The structural logic of the system is beautifully laid out, but where is the *risk*? The reader understands the equilibrium but not what threatens it. A single sentence hinting that this system is not as stable as it appears would increase the pull. The reader needs to feel that this beautiful machinery could break.
