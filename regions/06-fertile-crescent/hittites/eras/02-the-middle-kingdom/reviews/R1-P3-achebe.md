## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Hittites Era 2 Opening

**Overall:** The voice is right. This opening does not explain the Hittites to a modern reader. It places the reader inside a world where the gods witness oaths, where royal blood pollutes the land, and where a clay tablet is a binding instrument of cosmic order. The culture owns its story here.

**What works:**
- The edict is quoted in its own voice, and that voice is allowed to stand without modern commentary. "Look at this tablet. Formerly, blood was common in Hattusa. And the gods took it out on the royal house." The writer does not then explain what this means. The reader understands, or does not. This is trust.
- The theological argument is presented as the Hittites would have understood it: divine punishment is real, observable, causal. The text does not bracket this as "belief." It does not say "the Hittites believed that the gods punished." It says the gods punished. This is the inside voice operating correctly.
- Telipinu is not romanticized. He is not presented as a visionary ahead of his time. He is presented as a survivor who tried a particular solution to a particular problem. The text does not admire him from outside. It presents his logic from within.
- No forbidden words. No "sophisticated." No "remarkably." No comparisons to other traditions. The Hittite solution is allowed to be itself without being measured against anything.

**What doesn't:**
- The phrase "constitutional drama" does not appear in the text, which is correct. But the analytical frame of the middle paragraphs edges close to modern political philosophy: "If a written covenant could hold an alliance between Hatti and Kizzuwatna, could a written covenant hold the royal family to itself?" This is an excellent question, but its framing is slightly more analytical than the treaty-voice the opening has established. It sounds like a historian's question, not a Hittite's question. A Hittite might ask: "If the gods bind the oath between kings, do the gods not also bind the oath within the house?"
- The final line, "whether the tablet on the wall was stronger than the knife in the dark," is vivid and effective but slightly literary in a way that sits outside the register. The treaty-voice does not deal in such metaphors. It deals in sworn statements and witnessed consequences. The chapter should find a closing image that belongs to the register.

**The question I'd push on:**
Whose voice tells this story? The opening is strongest when it quotes the edict directly or when it narrates the murders in flat, declarative succession. It is weakest when it pulls back to analyze the edict's mechanism in language that belongs to a modern historian rather than to the world of the text. The chapter should commit fully to the treaty-voice: the voice of a culture that wrote things down because the gods were listening and the words were binding.
