## 1. Sources

### Primary Sources
- **The Indian Act (1876, as amended)**: The foundational legal instrument. Key sections: definition of "Indian," reserve system, band governance provisions, prohibition of potlatch and sun dance (1885–1951), residential school authority. The Act as administrative architecture, not simply policy.
- **Truth and Reconciliation Commission, *Honouring the Truth, Reconciling for the Future* (2015)**: The definitive record. 94 calls to action. Survivor testimony. The TRC's framing of "cultural genocide." Extensive use in the residential schools section.
- **Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples, final report (1996)**: Five volumes. The RCAP documented abuse at residential schools 25 years before 2021. Key for establishing that the oral tradition's knowledge preceded and survived the institutional failure to act.
- **CPR construction records and immigration records (1880s–1900s)**: For the prairie settlement section; the human cost of construction (Chinese labor); the settler influx framed on their own terms.
- **Louis Riel's trial transcript (1885)**: Riel's own words at his trial. His self-defense is a remarkable document — he understood himself as defending the Métis nation's political existence.
- **Henri Bourassa's *Le Devoir* editorials (1910–1918)**: French Canadian nationalist perspective on the war, conscription, and the English-French fault line. Bourassa from inside his own logic.
- **Newspaper accounts of the Kamloops and other residential schools (contemporary)**: For sensory detail of the school buildings, routines, and official descriptions.

### Academic Sources
- **J.R. Miller, *Shingwauk's Vision: A History of Native Residential Schools* (1996, U of T)**: The standard academic account. Key for the mechanics of the system: the per-capita funding model (created an incentive to maximize enrollment regardless of student welfare), the church-state partnership, the inspectors' reports.
- **John Milloy, *A National Crime: The Canadian Government and the Residential School System* (1999, Manitoba)**: Documents government knowledge of abuse throughout the system's operation.
- **James Daschuk, *Clearing the Plains: Disease, Politics of Starvation, and the Loss of Indigenous Life* (2013, Regina)**: The deliberate starvation of Plains nations during the CPR construction period. Hawthorn Report documentation.
- **Olive Dickason, *Canada's First Nations* (1992)**: For the pre-Confederation indigenous nations and their legal/political status entering the Confederation era.
- **Ramsay Cook, on Quebec nationalism**: For the English-French dynamics, conscription crises, and Bourassa's framework.
- **Margaret MacMillan, *Paris 1919***: For Canadian agency at the Paris Peace Conference — the emergence of Canadian diplomatic independence as a discontent of empire rather than nation.

### Secwépemc-specific sources
- Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc community statements (2021) — the framing of the Kamloops discovery as confirmation, not revelation.
- Secwépemc Nation land-relationship documentation — for the watershed knowledge Davis identified as missing from the opening.

## 2. Key Figures

**Qwenqwent (composite, c. 1913 born)**: The opening figure. A Secwépemc child who arrives at Kamloops around 1920. Composite drawn from survivor testimony in TRC records. Her specific arc: arrival and name-stripping; the dormitory years; what she carries home. She reappears in the chapter's landing section — not as resolution, but as the transmission problem: what she was able to pass on, what she could not.

**Louis Riel (1844–1885)**: Métis leader, executed November 16, 1885. Two distinct moments: Red River Resistance 1869–70, when he negotiated Manitoba's entry into Confederation as the only moment when indigenous negotiation produced political results; and the Northwest Rebellion 1885, when the CPR's completion meant the government could send troops west within days. His trial is where he speaks most clearly from inside his own logic: he is not a rebel, he is a government. The execution is the hinge between English and French Canada's diverging narratives.

**John A. Macdonald (1815–1891)**: First prime minister. The National Policy as his conception. The CPR as his instrument. His documented statements about "Indian" peoples in Parliament — including the advocacy for residential schools — presented from inside his stated logic (assimilation as kindness) without endorsement or condemnation. His political genius and the uses to which he put it.

**Henri Bourassa (1868–1952)**: Founder of *Le Devoir*, the great voice of French Canadian nationalism. His opposition to Canadian participation in the Boer War and WWI: not pacifism, but the argument that French Canadians had no obligation to die for British imperial ambitions. His own logic is consistent: Quebec is a distinct nation within Canada, and its young men belong to Quebec, not to London. The conscription crisis of 1917 from inside his frame.

**Nellie McClung (1873–1951)**: Suffragist, the "Famous Five," Manitoba MLA. The other kind of discontent: the women who built the reformist Canada of the early twentieth century, who won the federal vote in 1918, who were declared "persons" by the Privy Council in 1929. Her story is the liberal-progressive story of this era — the one that produced the social welfare state — and it exists in the same era as the residential school system. McClung herself was not a critic of the residential school system; she shared the assimilationist assumptions of her class and time. This is part of the chapter's task: showing that the progressive reform tradition and the residential school system were not opposites but were simultaneous products of the same civilization.

**William Duncan Scott (1862–1947)**: Deputy superintendent of Indian affairs 1913–1932. The phrase quoted in the opening is his: "to do away with the tribal system." He is the administrative architect of the residential school system at its height. He wrote poetry and he ran a bureaucracy of cultural erasure. Both are true. He is presented from inside his own stated logic: he believed he was doing the right thing.

## 3. Narrative Arc — The Structural Engine

**Engine: The Indian Act as administrative machine that produces two simultaneous Canadas**

The engine is not the railway, though the railway is the instrument. The engine is the Indian Act of 1876, which established a legal framework that:
1. Defined "Indian" as a status to be administered and ultimately eliminated
2. Created the reserve system as a spatial containment
3. Established the residential school system as the mechanism of generational rupture
4. Ran simultaneously with, and as the hidden condition of, the liberal nation-building project

The machine produces two simultaneous Canadas from 1867 to 1945:
- **Canada-as-story**: the railway, the wheat, the peacekeeping, the progressive reforms, the two world wars, the Statute of Westminster independence (1931), the emergence of a distinct national identity
- **Canada-as-condition**: the residential schools, the starvation of the Plains nations, the suppression of ceremonies, the pass system confining First Nations to reserves, the deliberate interruption of cultural transmission

These are not sequential. They happen in the same years. The CPR construction uses Chinese labor and runs through treaties that are being violated as the rails are laid. The conscription crisis of 1917 runs through the same years as the residential school enrollment peak. The Famous Five win personhood for women in 1929 while Indigenous women in Canada are explicitly excluded from the Indian Act's status categories in ways that will be contested for the next century.

**Mechanism**: The Indian Act requires constant administrative expansion to manage its own contradictions. Each "Indian problem" produces a new amendment, a new policy, a new institution — which produces new problems. The residential school is itself an administrative solution to the problem created by the reserve system: confined to reserves, First Nations people do not interact with settler economy. The solution is to remove the children and train them for the settler economy while eliminating their cultural capacity to return to their own. The system is internally logical in its own terms. Its logic is the chapter's argument, not the chapter's verdict.

**Terminal condition**: Embodied-transmission vulnerability (v1.6 type 2). The residential school system's design assumes that culture is transmitted through children. The terminal condition of the counter-strategy — the oral tradition's survival — is that it depended on the same mechanism the school was targeting: human bodies and voices as the transmission medium. What survived survived because it was hidden in bodies. What was lost was lost because the bodies were separated from the community that held the knowledge. The 2021 discovery at Kamloops is not the terminal condition of the system; the TRC's 94 calls to action (2015) mark the moment when the oral tradition's knowledge is formally validated by the state. But the oral tradition had not waited for that validation.

## 4. Section Plan

### Section 1: The School (opening integrated, ~800 words)
Kamloops 1920. Qwenqwent's arrival. The name-replacement, the braid-cutting, the language prohibition. The school's physical character: the building, the dormitory, the food, the silence (Kapuscinski's pressure point — bring in the sensory detail of institutional life). This section establishes the machine's interface with a child's body.

### Section 2: The Machine's Design (~600 words)
The Indian Act 1876. Duncan Campbell Scott and the administrative logic. The per-capita funding model and its perverse incentive. The church-state partnership: the Oblates at Kamloops, their own theology of salvation. The machine is shown from inside the logic of its designers — not endorsed, but understood on its own terms (Ibn Khaldun's pressure point). The gap between "stated purpose" and practice visible in the inspectors' own reports.

### Section 3: The Land That Was Settled (~700 words)
The numbered treaties and what the Plains nations understood them to mean. The Blackfoot Confederacy at Treaty 7 (1877): Crowfoot's words at the signing, the Blackfoot understanding of "as long as the sun shines and the river flows." What happened after: the deliberate starvation policy during CPR construction (Daschuk). The pass system. The prairie from the Cree and Ojibwe perspective as the railway crosses it. Brings in the land knowledge that Davis identified as missing.

### Section 4: The Other Story (~700 words)
The National Policy as its builders understood it. The CPR's completion, 1885. The wheat economy, the settler influx, the prairie towns. Louis Riel's execution, also 1885: the hinge between English and French Canada's diverging interpretations of what Canada means. Bourassa's nationalism taking shape. The progressive reform tradition of McClung and the suffragists — genuine liberation for some, occurring simultaneously with imprisonment for others.

### Section 5: Two Conscriptions (~600 words)
The First World War conscription crisis (1917) and what it reveals about the English-French fault line. The Second World War conscription crisis (1944) and how it is different. Canada's emergence from WWI with Vimy Ridge as national myth — and the simultaneous exclusion of Indigenous veterans from the soldiers' land grants they were promised. The wartime story told on two tracks: the Canadian national story and the residential school enrollment records.

### Section 6: What the Language Carried (~600 words)
Return to the Secwépemc and the residential school survivors. What was transmitted despite the system: the whispered words, the ceremonies maintained in secret, the grandparents who taught what the schools could not reach. The RCAP documentation (1996) and its invisibility. The oral tradition as a survival mechanism for knowledge the land had taught for millennia. This section is where the chapter shows the residential school system's partial failure on its own terms: it did not destroy the transmission; it drove it underground.

### Section 7: Landing — The Mountains (~500 words)
2021 and the ground-penetrating radar. The flags. But the chapter does not end with 2021 as revelation; it ends with what the oral tradition already knew. Qwenqwent's granddaughter, speaking Secwepemctsin. The Thompson River watershed, which the word still names. The mountains, which never recognized the colonial naming. The chapter's landing must hold both the scale of what was lost and the fact of what survived. No verdict. No summary. The culture's own persistence mechanism as the closing note.

## 5. Panel Pressure Points (anticipated from PANEL-1)

**Tuchman's pressure point**: The dual-track structure must not let one track become context for the other. Solution: the chapter alternates anchor points — each section has a specific person or place that holds the sensory ground. Qwenqwent returns in sections 1 and 6–7. The specific landscapes of the numbered treaties hold section 3. The dual tracks are parallel, not hierarchical.

**Ibn Khaldun's pressure point**: Why residential schools specifically? The chapter must show the specific theory of cultural transmission the government was operating on, presented as the government's own logic. Scott's administrative documents provide this. The link between the National Policy (economic expansion) and the residential school (cultural suppression) must be theorized: settlement required clearing, clearing required removing not just bodies but cultural formation.

**Achebe's pressure point**: The National Policy section's framing must be marked as a perspective, not a neutral description. Solution: use phrases like "What the government called settlement" or frame Macdonald's policy language as his own self-understanding. The two tracks should not be ontologically equivalent — the chapter inhabits the Secwépemc interior as its default and enters the colonial administrative frame as a reported perspective.

**Davis's pressure point**: The land knowledge must appear. The Thompson River watershed, the salmon runs, the Secwépemc ecological knowledge. Section 3 (Land That Was Settled) and Section 6 (What the Language Carried) are where this goes. The Secwepemctsin word for water moving through land should be developed, not just invoked.

**Kapuscinski's pressure point**: The full sensory character of the school (Section 1) and the land before it (Section 3). Specific: the food in the school, the sound of the dormitory, the cold. Also: the sensory texture of the prairie — what it looked like, smelled like, felt like from the back of a wagon or a horse as the CPR line approached. The body in space as historical evidence.

## 6. Open Questions

1. **The Qwenqwent composite**: The TRC archives include extensive survivor testimony. For the chapter's Secwépemc interior sections, the figure must be drawn from actual testimony without attributing fictional interiority to named survivors. The composite approach (disclosed in figures.md) is the appropriate method.

2. **The Oblate cosmology**: Davis raises the question of what the Oblates believed. This should be in Section 2, drawn from primary sources: Oblate mission statements and correspondence. The belief that a child's soul could be saved by destroying their cultural formation is a specific theological position with a specific history.

3. **McClung and assimilation**: Nellie McClung's reformist tradition and the residential school tradition are simultaneous. The chapter must hold this without either endorsing McClung's assimilationism or flattening her genuine contributions to women's rights. Two genuine discontents of the National Policy era that do not agree with each other.

4. **The Chinese railway workers**: The CPR construction required approximately 17,000 Chinese laborers who were paid half the wages of white workers and bore the most dangerous work (explosives in mountain passes). Approximately 600–1,000 died during construction. The Head Tax (1885) was imposed the same year the CPR was completed. This is part of the conditions the National Policy required and concealed — a third track that the chapter should acknowledge even if it cannot fully develop.

5. **The 1931 Statute of Westminster**: Canadian diplomatic independence from Britain as a positive moment in the "Canada-as-story" track. The emergence of a distinct national identity between the wars. How does this sit alongside the residential school enrollment records from the same period?

**Structural engine confirmed**: Indian Act as administrative machine producing two simultaneous Canadas. **Terminal condition confirmed**: Embodied-transmission vulnerability — the school targeted the same mechanism (voice and body) on which the oral tradition's survival depended. The 2021 radar finds the bodies of children in the ground at the school. The oral tradition had not waited for the radar to know they were there.
