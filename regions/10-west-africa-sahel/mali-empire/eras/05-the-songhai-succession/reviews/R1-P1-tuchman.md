## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of "The Songhai Succession" Opening

**Overall:** This is the hardest opening in the Mali sequence, and it works. The tension between the unchanging genealogy and the contracting court is a genuine narrative engine that replaces the outward momentum of Era 3 with an inward, quieter pull.

**What works:**
- "The jelilu sang the full genealogy. They sang it in Niani as though nothing had changed." These two sentences establish the chapter's central irony without declaring it. The "as though" carries the full weight.
- The Songhai introduction is handled with real craft: "their own memory, their own river, their own reasons." Three nouns, three possessives, a whole people given their own gravity in nine words.
- "Sonni Sulayman Dama sat in Gao and did not send tribute. His son, Sonni Ali, was already listening to the river." This is the kind of sentence pair that makes a reader turn the page. The second sentence is perfect forward motion.
- The structural explanation of the covenant's self-consuming logic ("the performance required gold, and the gold required control...") is the best analytical passage in any LUCIA opening so far. It does what Ibn Khaldun always demands: it explains WHY without leaving the story.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening sentence of the first paragraph needs a body. In Era 3, the reader had dust and shea butter and woodsmoke within the first few lines. Here the reader has the genealogy but no physical ground until "the dust of the dry season" near the end of the opening. A single sensory detail in the first paragraph — the heat, the light on the mud-brick, the sound of the kora tuning — would anchor the reader's body before the mind is asked to follow the names.
- The passage beginning "This was not a fall" risks becoming a thesis statement that tells the reader what the chapter is about rather than showing it. The sentences that follow ("The covenant did not break...") are strong, but the initial declaration feels slightly authorial rather than narrative.

**The question I'd push on:**
Where is the complication in the Songhai sections? The opening introduces the Songhai with beautiful economy, but they are still a horizon, not a scene. Section 3 will need to give Sonni Ali the same embodied presence the opening gives the jelilu, or the chapter risks being Mali's story told from Mali's side with the Songhai as a distant force.
