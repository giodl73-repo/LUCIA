# LUCIA Style Guide

**The editorial voice of The Human Chronicle.**

Every culture in this project gets the same thing: their story, told from inside, with dignity. This guide is how we keep that promise — sentence by sentence.

---

## The Achebe Test

> **Every sentence must pass: Could a thoughtful member of this culture read this and feel their story was told with dignity — from inside — regardless of whether they agreed with every detail?**

This is not a final check. It is the first check, the constant check, the only check that never gets overridden by craft or cleverness. Before you ask "Is this well-written?" ask "Is this honest?" Before you ask "Is this vivid?" ask "Would they recognize themselves?"

Name it after Chinua Achebe because he spent a career showing what it looks like when someone else tells your story — and what it looks like when you tell your own.

If a sentence fails the Achebe Test, it does not matter how beautiful it is. Cut it.

---

## The 13 Principles

### 1. Inside, Not Above

The reader stands inside the culture, not above it looking down. Write as if the reader IS there — not as a tourist, not as a scholar observing, but as someone living in that world where its assumptions are simply true.

**BAD:**
> The Yoruba people of West Africa developed an elaborate pantheon of deities known as the Orisha, which served as a way to explain natural phenomena and enforce social norms.

This is a scholar on a balcony. "Developed," "served as a way to explain" — the writer stands outside, analyzing the system from above. No Yoruba person in Oyo would describe their world this way.

**GOOD:**
> When thunder cracked over Oyo, people knew Shango was speaking. His wives — Oya of the wind, Oshun of the river, Oba of the hearth — moved through the world as surely as the rain did. You did not "believe in" Shango. You heard him. The diviner cast the palm nuts and read what Ifa revealed, and you lived accordingly, because this was how the world worked.

Now the reader is inside. The Orisha are not a "system of belief" — they are reality. That is the difference between observation and immersion.

---

### 2. The World Before the Label

Describe the practice, the belief, the moment BEFORE naming it with an academic term. Let the reader experience it first. The label comes after — if it comes at all.

**BAD:**
> The potlatch ceremony was a practice of competitive gift-giving among the Kwakwaka'wakw people of the Pacific Northwest. Chiefs would distribute blankets, canoes, and copper shields to demonstrate their wealth and status.

The label arrives before the experience. The reader files it under "anthropology" and moves on.

**GOOD:**
> The chief stood before the fire and began to give everything away. Blankets — dozens, then hundreds. Carved boxes. Canoes. A copper shield worth more than most families would see in a lifetime. The more he gave, the larger he became. His rivals watched, calculating. To receive was to be diminished unless you could give back more. The whole village was the stage, and generosity was the weapon.
>
> Europeans who witnessed this called it "potlatch," borrowing the Chinook word. They could not understand why a man would bankrupt himself to prove his greatness.

Now the reader felt it before they labeled it. The term arrives as orientation, not definition.

---

### 3. No Time Travelers

No modern concepts projected backward. People in the past did not think in our categories. The Inca did not have "supply chain management." Song Dynasty merchants did not practice "venture capitalism." The Maya did not "struggle with sustainability."

**BAD:**
> The Khmer Empire's extensive hydraulic infrastructure at Angkor represented an early form of centralized urban planning, with sophisticated water management systems that demonstrate advanced engineering capabilities.

"Urban planning," "water management systems," "engineering capabilities" — these are modern categories superimposed on a world that understood itself differently.

**GOOD:**
> The barays held the rain. Jayavarman VII's builders carved channels from the great reservoirs through the city, feeding the rice paddies that fed the people that built the temples that honored the gods. Water was not a resource to be "managed" — it was the blood of the kingdom. When the barays silted and the channels failed, the rice failed. When the rice failed, Angkor emptied. Not all at once. Slowly, over generations, the people walked into the forest and did not come back.

The Khmer understood water through their own framework — sacred, royal, inseparable from the kingdom's life. Describe what they did and how they understood it.

---

### 4. Their Calendar, Their Seasons

Use the culture's own time markers first. Common-era dates go in parentheses for orientation, but the culture's calendar leads.

**BAD:**
> In 1453 CE, the Ottoman Empire conquered Constantinople, ending the Byzantine Empire.

This is the Western calendar doing all the work. The Ottomans had their own way of marking time.

**GOOD:**
> In the year 857 of the Hijra (1453 CE), Sultan Mehmed — twenty-one years old and already called "the Conqueror" by those who believed in him — brought his cannon to the walls of Konstantiniyye. The city that had held for a thousand years fell in a morning.

The Islamic calendar leads. The common-era date orients readers who need it. The event belongs to the people who lived it.

---

### 5. Names in Their Language

Use the culture's own names first. The reader learns the real name; the familiar Western name, if needed, comes second.

**BAD:**
> The Aztec capital, built on a lake in central Mexico, was one of the largest cities in the world in the early 1500s.

"The Aztec capital" is a description, not a name. "Mexico" is an anachronism. The city had a name, and the people had a name for themselves.

**GOOD:**
> Tenochtitlan rose from the lake. The Mexica had built it on islands and extended it with chinampa gardens until the city held perhaps 200,000 people — more than any city in Spain. When Cortés's men first saw it from the mountain pass, Bernal Díaz wrote that some of the soldiers asked if it was a dream.

Tenochtitlan, not "the Aztec capital." Mexica, not "the Aztecs" (unless the text has established the convention). The name is the first act of respect.

---

### 6. Earned Emotion

The text does not tell you how to feel. It presents the evidence, builds the scene, and trusts the reader to arrive at feeling on their own. Emotional stage directions — "tragically," "remarkably," "heartbreakingly" — are a form of cowardice. They substitute instruction for craft.

**BAD:**
> Tragically, the great library of Nalanda was destroyed by Bakhtiyar Khilji's forces in 1193 CE, a devastating loss to human knowledge that would be felt for centuries.

"Tragically," "devastating," "felt for centuries" — the writer is telling you what to feel instead of showing you what happened.

**GOOD:**
> Bakhtiyar Khilji's horsemen reached Nalanda in the autumn. The monks did not fight. The library held manuscripts that had been copied and recopied for seven hundred years — texts on logic, medicine, grammar, the movements of stars. The horsemen burned it. The fires lasted, some sources say, for three months, because there was that much to burn.

"There was that much to burn." The reader feels it because the detail earns it. No instruction needed.

---

### 7. No Verdicts

The text never says "this was wrong," "this was justified," or "this was inevitable." It shows what happened and why people did what they did. The reader is the jury. This is the hardest principle and the most important one.

**BAD:**
> The Zulu Kingdom's brutal military campaigns under Shaka caused widespread devastation across southern Africa, displacing countless peoples in what became known as the Mfecane.

"Brutal," "devastation," "countless" — the verdict is already in. The writer has decided what Shaka was before the reader has met him.

**GOOD:**
> Shaka rebuilt the Zulu army. He replaced the long throwing spear with a short stabbing blade, the iklwa — named, people said, for the sound it made when pulled from a body. He drilled his regiments until they moved as one. Neighboring peoples faced a choice: join the Zulu kingdom or move. Many moved. The ripples spread south and west, displacing nation after nation in a chain of movement that lasted decades. The Sotho call this period the Difaqane. The Zulu call it the building of a kingdom.

Same events. No verdict. The reader understands what Shaka did, how, and what followed — and can form their own judgment. Notice: even the name of the period differs depending on who is telling the story.

---

### 8. People First, Systems Second

Every era is told through the people who lived it. Start with a person. Systems emerge from what people did and why — not the other way around.

**BAD:**
> The Song Dynasty (960–1279 CE) was characterized by significant economic reforms, including the development of paper currency, expansion of maritime trade, and innovations in agricultural technology.

A textbook. No one lives here.

**GOOD:**
> In the markets of Kaifeng, a tea merchant named Zhang could buy Fujian oolong with paper — actual paper, stamped by the government, worth its face value in copper coins he would never have to carry. His grandfather would have needed a cart of iron cash for the same purchase. Zhang did not think of this as a "monetary revolution." He thought of it as convenience. But the paper traveled lighter and farther than metal, and so did he, and so did every merchant in the Song lands, and the economy that grew from this lightness eventually dwarfed anything the Tang had built.

Now a person leads, and the system emerges from his experience. The reader discovers paper currency through someone who used it.

---

### 9. Trust the Reader

Do not re-explain what a scene already showed. If the narrative conveyed a point, do not then write "This illustrates..." or "This shows that..." or "In other words..." The reader understood. Trust that.

**BAD:**
> The elder spoke for three hours without pause, recounting every ancestor back to the founding of the village. No one interrupted. No one left.
>
> This illustrates the central importance of oral history in Griot tradition and the deep respect accorded to those who carried the community's collective memory.

The scene was doing its job. Then the writer stepped in and explained it, like a museum guide talking over a painting.

**GOOD:**
> The elder spoke for three hours without pause, recounting every ancestor back to the founding of the village. No one interrupted. No one left. When he finished, a young girl brought him water, and he drank it slowly, and no one spoke until he set the cup down.

The silence after is more eloquent than any explanation. Let scenes land.

---

### 10. Simple When It Matters

Complex sentence structures undercut emotional moments. When the weight is heavy, the prose should be plain. Save your baroque constructions for passages where complexity serves the culture and the moment.

**BAD:**
> The systematic destruction of the irrigation canals upon which the agricultural productivity of the Mesopotamian floodplain had depended for millennia, carried out by the Mongol forces during their siege and subsequent conquest of Baghdad in 1258, constituted an infrastructural catastrophe from which the region, arguably, never fully recovered.

Forty-eight words before the main verb. The weight is buried under architecture.

**GOOD:**
> The Mongols broke the canals. The irrigation system that had fed Mesopotamia for four thousand years drained into the sand. The farms died. The region never fully recovered. What had been the richest land between the rivers became, over generations, dust.

Short sentences. Plain words. The weight lands because nothing is in the way.

---

### 11. Sensory Ground

Every page needs at least one sensory anchor — sight, sound, smell, touch, taste. History happened in physical places to people with bodies. Without sensory detail, narrative becomes abstraction, and abstraction is where cultures go to be flattened.

**BAD:**
> Timbuktu was an important center of Islamic learning in the 14th and 15th centuries. Scholars came from across the Muslim world to study at its universities, and the city's manuscript collections were among the largest in the world.

True, but you cannot see it, hear it, smell it. It could be anywhere.

**GOOD:**
> The sand got into everything in Timbuktu — into the ink, into the tea, into the folds of the manuscripts the scholars carried under their arms from lecture to lecture. The Sankore mosque's courtyard filled with voices at dawn: students reciting Quranic verses in a dozen accents, Berber and Songhai and Hausa and Arabic overlapping like market noise. The manuscripts themselves smelled of goatskin and the particular dust of the Sahel, and scholars handled them with a care that bordered on reverence, because some of these pages had traveled farther than any person in the room.

Now you are there. You feel the sand. You hear the voices. You smell the manuscripts. Timbuktu is a place, not a fact.

**v1.4 — Sensory-Analytical Fusion:** At its best, sensory detail does not merely ground the narrative. It carries the argument. When the smell of the dye pit tells you about the economic structure (Hausa), when the feel of the hull IS the navigational knowledge (Norse), when the cold of the butter-lamp room IS the urgency of the translation project (Tibet), the principle is operating at its highest level. A reader who skipped the analytical passages should still understand the argument from the sensory ones.

---

### 12. Rhythm Belongs to the Story

Prose rhythm should reflect the culture and the moment. Do not write every chapter in the same cadence. A steppe chapter moves differently than a court chapter. A maritime chapter has a different pulse than an urban chapter. Match the music to the world.

**BAD (steppe culture written in academic prose):**
> The Mongol Empire, which emerged in the early thirteenth century under the leadership of Temüjin, subsequently known as Chinggis Khan, represented a significant transformation in the political organization of the Central Asian steppe, characterized by the unification of previously disparate nomadic groups into a cohesive military and administrative entity.

This is a steppe culture imprisoned in a lecture hall. The rhythm is wrong for the world it describes.

**GOOD (steppe culture written with steppe rhythm):**
> They rode. That was the first thing and the last thing: they rode. Temüjin had come out of nothing — an abandoned boy eating roots on the Onon River — and he made himself Chinggis Khan, and then he made the steppe into an army. They could cover sixty miles in a day. They carried dried meat under their saddles. They slept in the saddle. The world had not seen anything like them, and by the time the walled cities understood what was coming, it was already too late.

Short. Forward. Relentless. The prose moves like the riders.

---

### 13. Honest Uncertainty

When the record is thin, say so. When scholars disagree, present the disagreement. "What we know..." is more compelling than false certainty. The reader respects honesty more than authority.

**BAD:**
> The Indus Valley Civilization collapsed around 1900 BCE due to climate change, which caused the Ghaggar-Hakra river system to dry up, forcing the population to migrate eastward.

This states one hypothesis as fact. The truth is, we are not certain.

**GOOD:**
> The great cities of the Sindhu valley — Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Dholavira — emptied. By 1900 BCE (if the dating holds), the careful grid streets were filling with sand. Why? The river may have shifted — the Ghaggar-Hakra, if that is what they called it, may have dried or changed course. The monsoons may have weakened. Some archaeologists read the evidence as gradual migration; others see decline and fragmentation. What is certain: a civilization that had maintained standardized weights and measures across a thousand miles of territory simply dispersed. The writing, which no one has yet deciphered, went silent. We are left with the bricks, the drains, the beautiful small seals with their unreadable script, and our questions.

The uncertainty becomes part of the story. The mystery is more compelling than a false answer.

## Mechanical Rules

| Rule | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Opening length** | ~800 words |
| **Full chapter length** | 4,500 – 5,500 words |
| **Figures section length** | 500 – 1,500 words |
| **Em-dash limit (opening)** | ≤ 5 |
| **Em-dash limit (chapter)** | ≤ 8 |

**On em-dashes:** The limit is 8. The benchmark is 0. Every chapter that has scored 80+ achieved zero. The em-dash's absence is not a goal in itself but a symptom of voice discipline: each register finds its own pause mechanism (the period for steppe and saga, the comma-chain for maritime and river, the colon for inscriptional, the semicolon for legal-public). If you reach for an em-dash, the voice may have slipped.

**On chapter length:** 4,500 words is not a suggestion to pad. 5,500 words is not a ceiling to approach. The chapter is as long as the story needs. The range exists to prevent both thinness (too little to immerse) and sprawl (too much to sustain voice). For chapters covering distributed events across 5+ locations, the range extends to 6,000 words without requiring justification.

**On openings:** The opening is the Gate. ~800 words is enough to establish a person, a place, a moment, and the pull that makes the reader continue. If you cannot hook the reader in 800 words, more words will not help.

## Forbidden Words

These words are forbidden in culture chapters. Not because they are always wrong in English, but because in historical narrative they almost always smuggle in a worldview we are trying to avoid.

| Word/Phrase | Why It Is Forbidden | Use Instead |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **primitive** | Places cultures on a ladder with "us" at the top. There is no ladder. | Describe what they built, how they lived, what they made. |
| **advanced** | The other end of the same ladder. | Describe the specific achievement. "They built X" is always stronger than "they were advanced." |
| **civilization** (as compliment) | "They achieved civilization" implies civilization is a club with membership requirements. | Use only if the culture used the term of themselves. Otherwise, describe what they built. |
| **discovered by [Europeans]** | The people who lived there had already discovered it. They had names for it. They had been there for centuries or millennia. | "Encountered." Or describe the meeting from both sides. |
| **the New World** | It was new to exactly one group of people, and naming it from their perspective centers them. | Use the specific geographic name: the Caribbean, Mesoamerica, the Andes, Turtle Island. |
| **tribe** (unless self-designation) | Often carries connotations of smallness and simplicity that do not apply. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy was not a "tribe." | "People," "nation," "group," "community," or their own term for themselves. |
| **Dark Ages** | A Eurocentric label that erases the rest of the world's 5th–10th centuries and misrepresents Europe's own. | Name the specific period and place: "Merovingian Gaul," "the Abbasid golden age," "the Tang Dynasty." |

**The test:** If removing the word forces you to be more specific, the word was doing harm. "They were an advanced civilization" becomes "They built a road system spanning 25,000 miles across mountains, desert, and jungle, with relay stations every fifteen miles." The second version is better in every way.

---

## Forbidden Framings

These are structural habits, not individual words. They are harder to catch because they feel natural to writers trained in Western historiography. That is exactly why they are dangerous.

| Framing | Why It Is Forbidden |
|---------|-------------------|
| **"While Europe was doing X, these people were doing Y"** | Implies Europe is the main timeline and other cultures are side plots. Every culture is its own main timeline. If you need to establish contemporaneity, anchor it in the culture you are writing about: "In the same generation that Mansa Musa ruled Mali, Europe was emerging from famine." The subject of the sentence is the protagonist. |
| **"Before contact with the West, they had already..."** | The word "already" implies the West is the expected catalyst and anything before contact is preamble. Their history before European contact is not "pre-contact history" — it is just history. |
| **"They were remarkably sophisticated for..."** | "Remarkably" reveals surprise, and surprise reveals an assumption about what you expected. If you expected less, that expectation is the problem, not the evidence. Describe the sophistication directly. Drop "remarkably." Drop "for." |

**The deeper pattern:** All three framings share a single error — they center one culture as the default timeline, the expected norm, the measuring stick. LUCIA has no default culture. Every chapter's protagonist is its own center.

## The Voice Spectrum

Not every chapter sounds the same. The prose rhythm should belong to the world it describes. Here is how voice shifts across culture types — not as rigid rules, but as tuning forks.

### Steppe and Nomadic Cultures
*Mongol, Scythian, Turkic, Comanche, Bedouin*

Short bursts. Forward motion. Space between the sentences like space between the camps. The steppe is movement, and the prose moves. Descriptions are spare — what you see from horseback. Verbs do the work.

> They wintered on the Tuul. When the grass came back, they moved. Sixty thousand riders, their families behind them, their herds a brown stain on the plain that took half a day to pass. Temüjin sent scouts ahead and riders behind and the army flowed between them like a river finding its way downhill. They did not march. They migrated. And everything in the migration's path either joined or was gone.

### Court Cultures
*Heian Japan, Mughal India, Versailles France, Tang Chang'an, Byzantine Constantinople*

Layered. Observational. Unfolds slowly, because court life unfolds slowly. Every gesture has meaning. Every silence is political. The prose lingers on fabric, on light, on the angle of a glance, because in court cultures these things are power.

> Murasaki Shikibu noticed that the Empress's sleeve, visible beneath the curtain, was layered — plum over white over the palest green, the combination called "plum blossom" and appropriate only to the second month. It was now the first month. The Empress knew this. Everyone in the room knew this. The choice was deliberate: a signal, or a provocation, or a test of who would notice and who would pretend not to. Murasaki noted it in her diary and wrote nothing else about the evening, which itself was a kind of comment.

### Maritime Cultures
*Polynesian, Phoenician, Swahili coast, Norse, Malay*

Rolling rhythms. Pull and release, like tide and swell. The prose breathes in longer phrases, gathers momentum, crests, and settles. Maritime cultures live by patterns — wave patterns, wind patterns, star patterns — and the prose should carry that sense of reading the world's rhythms.

> The navigator lay on his back in the hull and felt the canoe move beneath him — not the waves the crew could see, but the deep swell rolling from the south, the one that had been steady for three days now and meant the current was where it should be. He did not need the stars tonight. The ocean was telling him. His grandfather had taught him to read the swells the way a farmer reads soil: by feel, by memory, by the knowledge that the sea has structure if you are patient enough to learn it. Ahead, somewhere past the horizon, the island they were sailing toward was deflecting waves back toward them, and he could feel that too — a faint confusion in the pattern, a ripple in the rhythm. Land.

### Oral Tradition Cultures
*Griot traditions, Aboriginal Australian songlines, Native American oral histories, Celtic bardic tradition*

Cadence of the spoken word. Repetition as structure, because repetition is how memory works without writing. Proverb-weight — sentences that feel carved. Phrasing that sounds right when read aloud, because these traditions were made for voices, not pages.

> The Dogon elder said: the fox drew lines in the sand, and the lines were the future. He said: the Nummo came from the sky in an ark, and the ark was a granary, and the granary was the world. He said: everything that exists was spoken into being, and the word was seed, and the seed was water, and the water was the word again. You could write this down, but writing it down would miss the essential thing, which was the elder's voice — how he paused before "the word was seed," how the pause made the listeners lean forward, how the leaning forward was itself part of the teaching. The story was not separate from its telling. The telling was the story.

### Urban and Market Cultures
*Abbasid Baghdad, Song Dynasty Kaifeng, Renaissance Venice, Tenochtitlan, Axumite trade cities*

Density. Overlap. Voices. The sound of a crowd. Urban prose is busy — not rushed, but full. Multiple things happen at once. Descriptions layer sound over smell over sight because that is how a city hits you. Sentences can be longer, more packed, because cities are packed.

> In the Tlatelolco market, sixty thousand people on an ordinary day: the flower sellers near the eastern gate with armloads of marigold and dahlia; the cacao merchants in the shade of cotton awnings, their beans sorted by grade in baskets and every price negotiable; scribes painting accounts on amate bark; the smell of roasting maize and copal incense drifting over everything; a judge sitting on a raised platform at the market's center, resolving a dispute between two jade traders while a runner waited beside him for a verdict to carry back to the palace; the hum of Nahuatl and the dozen other languages that traders brought with them from the coast, the highlands, the southern forests — a sound that Cortés's men compared to the roar of a city twice the size of Seville, which it was.

### Saga-Spare (Nordic/Germanic)
*Norse, Anglo-Saxon, Icelandic*

Deliberate. Laconic. The prose says less than it means and trusts the reader to hear the rest. Understatement as structural principle: "The wind had been fair" after destruction carries more weight than any adjective. Short declarative default, with proverb-weight sentences spaced carefully. The voice lingers on material objects because things carry meaning that speech does not. Emotional register: cool. "He took that badly" = violence is coming. Distinct from steppe (more deliberate, stops to look at objects) and from oral tradition (cooler, records rather than performs).

### River Cultures
*Songhai, Nile kingdoms, Mekong polities, Mississippian cultures*

Forward-moving, current-driven. Longer sentences gather momentum like a current gathering tributaries, punctuated by short declarative stops where the current hits a bend. The rhythm is directional, weight-bearing, and steady. The river voice carries institutional analysis the way a river carries cargo: steadily, in one direction. Distinct from hydraulic (which pools and distributes); the river voice flows and carries.

### Hydraulic/Monumental Cultures
*Khmer, possibly Harappan, canal-state civilizations*

Sentences that gather, hold, and release. The prose moves like water through channels: long cumulative sentences that build pressure through serial clauses, interspersed with short declarative stops. Controlled, monumental, patient. The rhythm of water held in a reservoir, not water flowing freely. Distinct from river (which flows directionally) and maritime (which rolls and crests).

### Sedimentary/Administrative Cultures
*Sumer, Ur III, Shang oracle bones, temple-economy states*

Accumulative sentences that build like account entries, layer by layer. Short declarative theological statements land with the weight of carved pronouncements. The rhythm mirrors both alluvial deposits and a tablet archive growing. Heavy, wet, specific. The voice of a culture that kept meticulous records of the gods' grain and then used the same system to ask whether death was the end.

### Monument/Stone Cultures
*Old Kingdom Egypt, possibly Axum, megalithic cultures*

Gravitational, cyclical, certain. Heavy weight-bearing sentences accumulate like courses of limestone. The rhythm is cyclical, returning to the flood the way the flood returns annually. Short declarative sentences carry theological weight. The voice does not pause or qualify, because the cosmic order does not pause or qualify.

### Edict-Inscriptional
*Maurya, possibly Achaemenid, early Islamic inscription traditions*

Opens with the primary source itself: the inscription IS the voice. Declarative, weighted, carved. Each sentence feels placed on the page the way a mason places a chisel mark on stone. Modulates across modes (war/kinetic, policy/enumerative, material/descriptive, landing/elegiac) while maintaining the inscriptional foundation. The analytical mode is accounting: the voice counts, lists, specifies. Distinct from monument/stone (which builds upward); the edict voice carves outward, communicating rather than enduring.

### Inscriptional/Architectural
*Maya, possibly Aztec, cultures where the carved record IS the political act*

Carved, precise, placed. Each sentence a glyph block. The rhythm is deliberate and measured, built through commas and conjunctions, not through parenthetical interruptions. Because inscriptions do not pause mid-glyph-block.

### Argumentative/Text-Centered
*Jewish Talmudic, possibly Confucian commentary, scholastic traditions*

Questioning, cumulative, perpetually unfinished. Accumulative sentences mirror the sugya's method: one question opening the next. Dense, layered, argument-as-structure. The voice of a civilization that made sustained intellectual debate its defining activity.

### Legal-Public
*Roman Republic/Empire, possibly Athenian democratic, parliamentary traditions*

Declarative sentences that close like legal pronouncements. Measured rhythm shaped by the Forum's acoustics. The weight of inscription. Can progressively transform across eras: expanded in building periods, compressed under crisis, transformed under new dispensations.

### Philosophical-Devotional
*Tibetan Buddhist, possibly Chan/Zen, Sufi, Vedantic*

The prose moves at the speed of careful thought. Long accumulative sentences build meaning clause by clause, the way a translation builds vocabulary. No separation between intellectual and devotional registers: scholarship IS devotion. Silence between sentences mirrors silence between syllables in a translation session. Distinct from argumentative (which questions and accumulates); the philosophical-devotional voice builds and clarifies.

### Maritime Accumulative (Trade-Coast)
*Swahili coast, possibly Phoenician trade cities, Malay entrepots*

Rolling clauses that gather like monsoon swells, breaking into short declarative sentences like waves landing on a reef. The rhythm is tidal: accumulation, crest, resolution. The voice carries both atmosphere and argument in the same register. Distinct from oceanic maritime (Tongan/Polynesian), which reads the deep swell; the trade-coast voice reads the harbor, the storehouse, and the two horizons simultaneously.

### Vertical/Mountain Cultures
*Inca, possibly Tibetan highland, Andean, Ethiopian highland*

Ascending, descending, layered, weight-bearing. Prose moves through altitude zones the way the culture moves through its world. The ecological structure of the vertical archipelago becomes the prose's structural principle. Deliberate, shaped by thin air and long distances.

## A Final Note

This guide is not a cage. It is a set of commitments. The principles exist to protect the cultures we are writing about — to prevent the thousand small ways that well-meaning writers flatten, distort, or condescend to the human past.

When a principle feels like it is getting in the way of a story, stop and check: is the principle wrong, or is the story trying to do something it should not? Nine times out of ten, the principle is right and the story needs to find a different path.

The tenth time, the story has found something the guide did not anticipate. When that happens, write the story. Then come back and update the guide.

The guide evolves. The commitment does not.
