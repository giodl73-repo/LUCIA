# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski
## Panel Review: Opening — Canada Era 4, The National Policy and Its Discontents

"The scissors were cold."

Three words. This is how you write the body into history. Not "the procedure was dehumanizing" — the scissors were cold. The reader's neck knows it. This is what I mean when I say the body must be in the room for the history to be real.

The opening is sensorially excellent. Pine dust, horse sweat, September light on a cross, forty girls in a dormitory, words whispered "under their breath, the words growing smaller as the weeks went on" — this last is the best sensory observation in the piece. The sound of a language diminishing. Not a metaphor; a physical process, the volume turning down over weeks. I have covered famines and coups and assassinations, and the most haunting things are always the ones that happen slowly and at first sound like nothing.

The grandmother pressing her palms against Qwenqwent's shoulders is another body-moment that works: not an embrace, not a goodbye kiss — her palms, on the shoulders, once. Everything is in the restraint of that gesture. "No one cried because that would have made it worse." The logic of a family trying to hold together across a terrible threshold. I know this from other places, other borders, other separations.

**What I want more of:** The building itself. Red brick, three stories, a cross. But what is inside? What does the hallway smell like? What is the sound of forty girls who are not allowed to cry? What does the food taste like — is it the same food she ate at home, or is it different in ways that communicate, on the first night, how far she is? The body experiences the school through its material conditions, and those conditions are part of the violence. Cold floors, thin blankets, the particular silence of rooms where noise is punished: these are the sensory ground of the residential school's specific form of harm.

**The 2021 flash-forward:** I appreciate its purpose — to establish that the oral tradition was authoritative before the instruments confirmed it. But it comes at the cost of sensory immersion. We leave the school in 1920 and enter a news event in 2021, and the prose register shifts into journalism. The flags going to half-mast, the ground-penetrating radar: these are real and important, but they pull the reader out of the body and into abstraction. I would consider whether this material can be relocated or handled with a lighter touch in the chapter itself, keeping the opening more fully inside the 1920 moment.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter covers 1867 to 1945. This is almost eighty years of embodied experience. The residential school system, the prairie drought, the two world wars, the conscription crises. Each of these has a specific physical character — a smell, a sound, a texture of the body in a specific place under specific conditions. How does the chapter maintain this sensory ground across eighty years and multiple parallel stories without becoming a survey? What are the two or three moments of absolute physical specificity that will anchor the whole?
