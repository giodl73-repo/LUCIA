## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — PANEL-2 Review of Notes

**Focus:** Inside voice, the oral tradition, cultural ownership

**Overall:** The notes take my concerns seriously. The plan to rewrite the thesis paragraph from inside is correct. The plan to introduce specific figures with honest acknowledgment of the record's limits is appropriate. But I have two remaining concerns.

**What the notes get right:**

- The decision to maintain the anonymous builder and acknowledge the thin record honestly, rather than inventing specificity the sources do not support, is the right call. This is the technique the Mali chapter discovered with Inari Kunate: the gap as characterization. Use it.

- The plan to show Islam as the coast's own faith (Section 3) through the mosque and the dispute resolution is correct. Islam on the Swahili coast was not Arabian Islam transplanted. It was Swahili Islam, filtered through Bantu social structures and adapted to the coastal world. The Friday prayer scene must feel natural, not like a cultural event being observed.

**What still needs work:**

- The chronicle tradition must be present. The Swahili had their own historians — the town chroniclers who produced texts like the Kilwa Chronicle, the poets who composed utenzi, the genealogists who traced the ruling lineages. This tradition is the coast's own memory system. The Mali chapter had the jelilu; the Swahili chapter needs its equivalent. Not as a formal introduction, but as a presence in the world. Perhaps the dispute resolution in Section 3 involves reference to the town's chronicle — "as it has been since the days of..." — establishing that the coast remembers its own history.

- The Shirazi origin narrative. The Kilwa Chronicle traces the ruling dynasty to Shiraz in Persia. Modern scholarship treats this as a prestige genealogy — a political claim, not a historical migration. The chapter must handle this from inside. The genealogy is not a lie to be debunked; it is how the ruling family understood and legitimated its authority. Present it as the dynasty presents it, then let the reader see what the genealogy does (legitimates oceanic connections, claims prestige) without the narrator saying "this is a myth." The inside voice respects the claim even when the evidence complicates it.

- Watch the hinterland passage (Section 2). The ivory trader must not become a prop for the coast's story. He has his own world, his own commercial logic, his own reasons for making the journey. The coast is not the center of his universe — it is the end of his trade route, the place where he converts ivory into goods he needs. Make his perspective real, even briefly.
