# BOARD — James H. Sweet — The Middle Passage

**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Cultural Representation

The chapter's treatment of African cultural survival in the Americas demonstrates genuine scholarly engagement. The body-archive thesis is a powerful organizing concept that is well-supported by the evidence.

## Specific Notes

1. **Candomblé in Bahia:** The description of the terreiro, the mãe de santo/pai de santo leadership, and the orixas-to-saints syncretism is accurate. The claim that "the specific rhythm that calls Shango in a terreiro in Salvador today is recognizable as the alujá rhythm played in a compound in Oyo three centuries ago" is supported by the ethnomusicological literature (Xavier Vatin, Gerard Béhague), though "recognizable" is more defensible than "identical." The chapter uses "recognizable." Good.

2. **The body-archive thesis:** The claim that embodied knowledge systems survived because they required no material infrastructure is substantially supported by the scholarly record but requires nuance. The Candomblé houses of Bahia did eventually develop significant material infrastructure (terreiros, specific ritual objects, plant-based medicines from New World flora). The survival was not purely embodied; it also required the reconstitution of material culture in new forms. The chapter addresses this implicitly (the terreiro is described as a rebuilt Yoruba compound) but could be more explicit that the body-archive was the TRANSMISSION mechanism, not the PERMANENT form. Knowledge crossed in the body but was rebuilt in material and institutional forms. **P3: author decides.**

3. **What was lost:** The chapter's treatment of loss is honest and well-calibrated. The specific categories (plant-dependent herbalism, languages, names, genealogies, the dead left behind) are well-chosen. The acknowledgment that "some knowledge transferred: enslaved healers in Brazil and the Caribbean identified New World plants with similar properties and built new pharmacopoeias" is accurate and important: it shows adaptation, not merely loss.

4. **Judith Carney's "Black rice" thesis:** This is well-supported in the scholarly literature, though not without debate. Carney's argument that enslaved Africans from rice-growing regions brought the knowledge of *Oryza glaberrima* cultivation to South Carolina is the dominant position. Some scholars (David Eltis, David Richardson) have questioned aspects of the argument. The chapter presents it as established scholarship ("Judith Carney has shown"), which is a reasonable representation of the current state of the field.

5. **Composite figures:** The man from Oyo is a defensible construction. The demographic record supports the presence of Yoruba captives from the Oyo region at Ouidah in the early eighteenth century. The cultural knowledge attributed to him (Shango devotion, Ifa divination, bata drum rhythms) is standard Yoruba religious practice of the period. The disclosure in figures.md is thorough and honest. This is the right approach for a source record that documented commodities, not persons.

## Concern

The chapter's description of the Pelourinho neighborhood in Salvador ("named for the pillory where enslaved people were whipped") is correct as etymology but compressed. The pelourinho (pillory/whipping post) was a specific instrument of public punishment located in what is now the Largo do Pelourinho. The neighborhood's current status as a UNESCO World Heritage site and center of Afro-Brazilian culture creates a layered significance that the chapter's parenthetical only hints at. This is not an error but a missed opportunity for the kind of sensory-historical layering the chapter does so well elsewhere. **P3: author decides.**

## Verdict

Historically and culturally sound. No factual corrections required from this reviewer. The body-archive thesis is well-supported. The composite figures are defensible. The treatment of loss is honest. The plurality of African traditions is a significant strength.
