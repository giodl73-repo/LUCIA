# P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of "When the Empire Reached the Sea" Notes

**Overall:** The production bible has identified the correct structural engine — institutional resilience through succession crisis — and has mapped three distinct phases of the arc. I am satisfied with the analytical ambition. Two concerns remain.

**What works:**
- The three-phase arc (expansion, testing, furthest reach) is structurally sound. Each phase advances the argument rather than merely adding more material.
- Sakura is correctly identified as the era's most analytically important figure. A freed slave becoming mansa is not an anomaly — it is the covenant's logic made visible. The notes understand this.
- The Dyula network section (Section 4) is correctly framed as system, not color. The trade network as the Manden's nervous system is the right metaphor.

**What I'd push on:**
- Section 3 must distinguish between types of succession failure. Khalifa's deposition (covenant enforcement by assembly), the freed-slave period (covenant's elevation of competence over lineage), and Abu Bakr's voluntary departure (the system absorbing the king's absence by design) are three different institutional mechanisms. Each reveals a different feature of the covenant. The chapter must make these distinctions explicit.
- The economic mechanism of expansion needs more precision. The goldfields of Bambuk and Bure were not simply "brought under control." The Manden established specific institutional relationships with the Wangara mining communities — a form of controlled access rather than direct administration. The chapter must trace this.
