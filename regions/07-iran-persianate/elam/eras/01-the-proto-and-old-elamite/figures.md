# Figures
## Elam Era 1: The Proto-Elamite and Old Elamite Periods (c. 3200–1500 BCE)

---

## The Anonymous Scribe (composite figure, c. 3100 BCE)

No individual scribes from the proto-Elamite period are named in the surviving record. The tablets have not been deciphered. This is itself the most important fact about him: the institution he served was larger than any individual in it. The proto-Elamite sign conventions appear in the same form across hundreds of kilometers and six hundred years of tablets. The system was not one person's invention. It was a tradition maintained by training.

What the tablets tell us about the scribes collectively: they trained in structured sequences. Among the corpus, archaeologists have identified tablets that appear to be practice pieces — signs pressed repeatedly, sometimes with corrections alongside them, the evidence of learning made material. A young scribe pressed a sign incorrectly; beside it, or on another tablet from the same period, the correct form appears. The archive held both the student's attempt and the standard. The training was not separate from the archive; the archive was where training became legible.

They worked in the early hours. The Khuzestan plain in summer reaches temperatures that make midday handling of wet clay impractical — the clay dries too fast, before the scribe can press the full account. The morning hours, before the heat thickened, were when the work was done. The scribe sat in the temple precinct, in the shade before the sun crested the Zagros, and pressed the marks into river clay while the plain below was still cool enough to feel alive.

He was the god's clerk. His training was in service of the god's estate. His archive was the god's archive. He did not need a name in it because his name was irrelevant to the record. What mattered was the sign, pressed correctly, holding its count. The scribe was the system's instrument, not its author.

That system ran for six hundred years. No single scribe's hands pressed it for more than a working lifetime. But the tradition passed from hands to hands, from the teachers who pressed the correct form beside the student's incorrect one, through generations that no record names.

## Puzur-Inshushinak (c. 2100 BCE)

The governor of Susa who became king of Elam at the moment the Akkadian Empire collapsed.

His name means "protected by Inshushinak" — the lord of Susa's name embedded in his own, a daily carrying of the god who had watched over the city since before anyone's memory. This was not unusual for Elamite names. The gods were close enough to be worn.

What was unusual about Puzur-Inshushinak was his choice of script. When he declared himself king and began issuing royal inscriptions, he wrote in Linear Elamite — a script distinct from both proto-Elamite and from the Akkadian cuneiform that had been the language of external correspondence during the Akkadian imperial period. Linear Elamite appears to have been used for prestige texts: royal dedications, divine invocations, the records a king wanted to last rather than the records a clerk needed to file. Its surviving corpus is small — a few dozen inscriptions from this period, mostly from Susa. It also has not been deciphered, though recent work by François Desset and colleagues has proposed a partial reading.

What Puzur-Inshushinak intended by writing in Linear Elamite rather than Akkadian: to declare that the kingdom's deeds would be preserved in the kingdom's own script, by the kingdom's own scribal tradition. The Akkadian empire had folded. The world was reorganizing. In that moment of reorganization, Puzur-Inshushinak chose to declare Haltamti on its own terms.

He did not hold the kingdom long. The Third Dynasty of Ur consolidated its power within a generation, and Elam was again integrated into a Mesopotamian imperial system. But the inscription Puzur-Inshushinak pressed in Linear Elamite survived. It stands in the Louvre now. The words are still his, still in his script, still in the language he chose.

He chose correctly. His words lasted.

## Kindattu (c. 2004 BCE)

Kindattu's name appears in Sumerian sources as the agent of Ur III's destruction. The Lament for the Destruction of Ur names the Elamites among those who came against the city; the Sumerian king list and administrative texts place Kindattu as their leader. His own account of the campaign does not survive, or survives in tablets that have not been read.

He was not the only force that brought Ur III down. The Amorites pressed from the west at the same time. The empire was already straining — overstretched, drought-stressed, its administrative demands exceeding what its revenues could sustain. Kindattu did not break a healthy empire. He broke a weakening one, at the moment when the break was possible.

What the campaign achieved for the Elamite system: the removal of a dominant extractive power from the plain. Ur III had been demanding tribute, imposing its administrative conventions, integrating Elamite regions into its own redistributive network on Ur's terms rather than Elam's. Kindattu's campaign was the dual kingdom's engine asserting itself against a constraint it had borne for decades.

In Ur, the lament was performed in the ruins. "In Ur no one was spared." The temples were desecrated, the divine statues carried off, the city's treasury emptied. The Sumerians understood this as the gods' abandonment of their city — the destruction was theological before it was political. Kindattu appears in their text as the instrument of divine punishment, which is the way a Sumerian text would understand any military defeat.

How Kindattu understood it: we do not know. His name is preserved in the language of those he defeated. His own language remains unread. He is the conqueror whose victory is documented only by the victim, which is a condition that many conquerors in history have found themselves in, but rarely in quite this form — where the conqueror's own records exist in abundance but cannot yet be understood.

## Atta-hushu (sukkalmah, c. 1945–1905 BCE)

Atta-hushu is one of the earliest Old Elamite rulers who can be named and dated with some confidence. He appears in treaty texts — diplomatic correspondence in Akkadian between the Elamite sukkalmah and the city of Ur, which had reconstituted itself as a city-state after the empire's collapse and was still an important trading partner on the plain.

The treaty is not preserved in full. What is preserved: references to it in Mesopotamian administrative texts, enough to confirm that it existed, that Atta-hushu was a named signatory, and that the Elamite position in the treaty was that of an equal — a sovereign power conducting formal diplomacy, not a subordinate acknowledging an overlord.

This is what the sukkalmah title meant in practice. The great regent stood between the two poles of the kingdom and between the kingdom and the wider world. His court wrote in Akkadian for external correspondence — the world's diplomatic language — and administered the kingdom in Elamite for internal affairs. The sukkalmah's legitimacy was visible precisely in this capacity to maintain both registers simultaneously: Elamite in the god's precinct, Akkadian in the treaty text, a bilingual authority adequate to a kingdom that existed at the interface of two great civilizational worlds.

Atta-hushu's specific character — his personality, his interior life, his understanding of his own role — is not recoverable from the texts. What is recoverable is his institutional position: the hinge of a system that had organized the highland-lowland exchange for generations and would continue to do so for another century and a half. He stood at the axis of a kingdom that was, in his lifetime, one of the significant powers of the Near Eastern world, managing a surplus economy that connected the Iranian plateau to the Mesopotamian plain, maintaining a scribal tradition that had been running for over a thousand years, serving a god whose name the king before him had worn in his own.

The archive he maintained is gone, or buried beneath later layers of the Susa mound that have not been fully excavated. His name survives in the diplomatic record of another civilization. It is enough to know he was there.
