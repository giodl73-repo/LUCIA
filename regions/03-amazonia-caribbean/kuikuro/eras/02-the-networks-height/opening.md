# The Network's Height
## Kuikuro | Era 2: c. 1200–1500 CE

---

The road wants clearing again.

Iauatá can see it even from the edge of the plaza: the fringe of new shoots along the western margin, the way the young cecropia is already reaching its pale arms over the cut edge, feeling for the light that the road's opened corridor provides. Two weeks since the last crew worked it. The forest moves fast in the rains. If you stop cutting, the road stops being a road within a season. It becomes a memory of a road, and then it becomes forest, and then no one knows it was ever there.

He calls to the younger men. They come with their stone blades and their fire-hardened wooden tools, still eating from the morning's manioc cakes. The road crew is not ceremonial. There is no song for clearing roads, no ritual preparation. You wake up, you eat, you cut. The road does not care about ceremony. The road cares only about being a road.

They walk the corridor west. It runs straight. This is the thing about the road between the two towns, the thing that a stranger would notice first, if a stranger ever came this way, which they do not. The road runs straight through forest that does not want to be straight. The forest is always trying to be curved, growing in arcs toward the light, following the rivers' bends, tangling around its own fallen weight. The road cuts against the forest's nature. Someone chose this line. Someone maintained this line, generation after generation, and that choosing, that maintenance, is the fact of the road: not the soil beneath it but the decision to keep it open.

Iauatá is not thinking about any of this. He is thinking about the Quarup.

The ceremony is three moons away. Kuhikugu is hosting. Kuhikugu, the largest of the cluster towns, the one whose plaza can hold all the visiting groups at once, whose chief's house faces east across a space large enough for the wrestling matches and the mourning songs and the two days of dancing that accompany the egitsü. That is the Kuikuro name for the ceremony the whole network calls the Quarup, after the sacred hardwood log that stands at its center. The chief who died last dry season, Iauatá's own mother's brother's father-in-law, a lineage connection complex enough that Iauatá has spent time thinking through exactly how he should mourn, will be honored there. The egitsü for him will close the period of grief. The network will affirm itself.

This is why the road matters. Not in the way that Iauatá is thinking about it. Roads are simply infrastructure, they require maintenance, you clear them as you clear the fish weirs after the rains. The road matters in what it represents: a commitment. Every community in the network that maintains its road is announcing, in the medium of cleared earth, that it intends to arrive. The Mehinako arrive from the east along their road. The Kamayurá come from their forest to the south. The Kalapalo, the Aweti, the Wauja, each community's road is the physical expression of its intention to remain part of what they are all part of together.

The road crew reaches the worst section, where a tall Inga tree has dropped a branch across the corridor. The branch is half-rotted but still heavy, and the understory has grown up around it in the weeks since it fell, threading vines through the bark and seeding in the damp shadow underneath. They spend an hour on this section. The work is sweaty and specific: hacking the vine stems, rolling the branch to one side, cutting back the new growth to re-expose the straight line, the intentional line, the line that says someone lives at both ends of it and values the connection enough to keep it open.

When they finish, Iauatá stands at the cleared section and looks west toward Matuwa, the neighboring town. On a clear day, from the canopy's height, you would see the smoke of their morning fires. Here on the road, you can only see the corridor running forward through the green wall of the forest, the light at the end of it pale and inviting. The road looks like it leads somewhere important. It does. It leads to people who will come to the egitsü. It leads to the wrestling matches at which Kuhikugu's young men will prove themselves, at which the visitors' young men will prove themselves, at which the whole point of being a people with other peoples will be proven again, in public, through the body, through song, through the careful movement of food and gifts between the anetü of one town and the anetü of another.

It leads to the network, which is not a word any of the people of the upper Xingu use, because they do not need to name what simply is.

Three moons. The road needs to be clear when the visitors come. Iauatá picks up his blade and moves to the next section.
