# Panel Review — R1 / P-4 / Wade Davis
## French Empire Era 2: La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

What I look for is whether the knowledge systems of all parties — not just the French administrators but the people they administered — are treated as equally sophisticated. The opening manages this at the analytical level. The epistemological question it does not yet address is: what was the knowledge system that survived the plantation, that organized Bois Caïman, that produced Toussaint Louverture as a strategist and Jean-Jacques Dessalines as a general?

**On the classification system as epistemology:**

The opening correctly identifies Moreau de Saint-Méry's 128 categories as a knowledge system — not merely prejudice but a structured way of knowing the world, a taxonomy applied to human beings with the same methodological conviction that Linnaeus applied to plants. This is important: the chapter should treat it as a knowledge system, which means it should also trace how the system generated its own blind spots. What could the 128 categories not see? What knowledge was held in other bodies, other practices, other epistemologies — the Vodou ceremony that coordinated the insurrection, the network of kin relationships that moved information across the plantation system, the military knowledge that the Saint-Domingue insurrection's leaders had accumulated?

**The Vodou question:**

Bois Caïman is mentioned but not rendered. For a chapter that will spend most of its time inside the French administrative worldview, the ceremony at Bois Caïman is the moment where a completely different epistemology enters the frame. The opening wisely does not try to render it (that belongs to the Haiti chapter), but the chapter should at minimum register the French administration's encounter with a knowledge system it had no categories for. General Leclerc's dispatches from Saint-Domingue describe an enemy whose coordination he could not explain, whose supply lines he could not find, whose soldiers seemed to fight without fear of death. That bewilderment is the French Empire's encounter with its own epistemological limits.

**What the chapter must not do:**

It must not treat Vodou, or the knowledge of the insurrection's leaders, as exotic — as the Other against which the French Enlightenment system is measured. The ceremony at Bois Caïman was a sophisticated political act: it created collective commitment across language groups and plantation communities, built the trust that made coordinated action possible. The chapter should register this as what it was: a knowledge system operating at the level of political theory, at least as sophisticated as the Constituent Assembly debating the Declaration.

**The question I'd push on:**

Toussaint Louverture's military strategy — the use of terrain, the guerrilla tactics, the decision to retreat rather than hold fixed positions, the use of yellow fever (which killed 40,000 French soldiers) as a deliberate environmental weapon — was a form of knowledge. Where did it come from? Not from Raynal and Montesquieu, whatever Toussaint read. It came from the plantation landscape, from enslaved people's intimate knowledge of the northern plain's geography, from practices of resistance that had been developing since the first runaway (maroon) communities were established. The chapter should find a way to register this knowledge — even briefly — as knowledge.
