# P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of "When the Empire Reached the Sea" Opening

**Overall:** The opening honors the oral tradition as a knowledge system and presents the Atlantic voyage with the epistemic humility it demands. I want more depth in the full chapter on the Dyula network as an intellectual and commercial system.

**What works:**
- The jelilu's distinction between death and not-returning is an epistemological statement, not a literary flourish. The oral tradition is presented as having its own categories of knowledge, its own rules for what can be asserted and what must remain open. This is the kind of inside understanding I demand.
- Abu Bakr's expedition is not framed as "exploration" in the European sense. He is not "discovering" anything. He is following currents that the Dyula merchants have already described. The knowledge base is Mande, the initiative is Mande, the question is Mande. No outside validation is sought or needed.
- The phrase "the jelilu are precise in this way" — yes. This is the key insight. Oral traditions are not imprecise versions of written records. They are precise systems with their own protocols for handling certainty and uncertainty. The opening understands this.

**What doesn't:**
- The Dyula merchants are mentioned once, briefly. In the full chapter, the Dyula network must receive serious treatment. These were not simply traders. They were the information system of the Manden — carriers of news, prices, route knowledge, political intelligence. The Dyula network's expansion is one of the era's defining achievements, and it must be treated with the same depth and specificity as a chapter on the Silk Road would give to Sogdian merchants. Their knowledge of the Atlantic coast currents — the knowledge that made Abu Bakr's expedition conceivable — is a product of generations of commercial and environmental observation. Show the knowledge system.
- The opening's final paragraph mentions that the empire "had grown from the savanna of the upper Niger to the salt flats of the Sahara's edge and the humid air of the Atlantic coast." This is a geographical summary. In the full chapter, give the reader the ecology of each zone — the baobab savanna, the Sahelian semi-arid belt, the humid coastal forests — and show how the Manden's political and commercial systems adapted to each. The empire reaching the sea is not just a matter of territory. It is a matter of ecological knowledge, diplomatic flexibility, and commercial imagination.

**The question I'd push on:**
Will the goldfields of Bambuk and Bure receive treatment as knowledge systems, not just as economic assets? Gold mining in these regions involved specific techniques, specific social organizations (the Wangara mining communities), and specific relationships between miners and the state that protected the location secret. The goldfields are not just wealth. They are institutions. Show them as such.
