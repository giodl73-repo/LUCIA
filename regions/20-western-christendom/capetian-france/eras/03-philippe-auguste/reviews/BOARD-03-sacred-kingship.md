## Factual Accuracy

**Holy Ampulla:** The chapter describes it as "a vial of sacred oil said to have been preserved since the baptism of Clovis in the year 496." This is the Capetian tradition as documented in the coronation ordo. The "dove brought it from heaven" tradition is the standard Frankish account (attested from the 9th century onward). The chapter correctly presents this as the Capetians' own understanding rather than as historical fact.

**Royal touch for scrofula:** Accurate. The healing touch for *écrouelles* (scrofula) is documented in French sources from at least the 12th century. The phrase attributed to the king, "God heals you; the king touches you" (*Dieu te guérit, le roi te touche*), is the traditional formula documented in the sources.

**The chapter's characterization of the healing ceremonies:** The claim that the ceremonies sustained themselves across four centuries is correct. The royal touch for scrofula was practiced by French kings through Louis XVI. The chapter's approach (rendering the ceremony from within the Capetian worldview without requiring modern explanation) is exactly the approach Marc Bloch's *Royal Touch* implicitly calls for: to understand the institution as those who practiced it understood it, while maintaining analytical rigor.

**Anointing at Reims:** Correct. The coronation ordo at Reims included anointing with the Holy Ampulla. The rex Christianissimus title is documented from the 11th century onward as a specific Capetian claim.

## Cultural Authenticity

The healing ceremony section is the chapter's most important contribution to LUCIA's project of rendering cultures from within. Most historical accounts of royal thaumaturgy fall into one of two failure modes: (1) presenting it as naive superstition that modern readers will find charming or embarrassing, or (2) reducing it to "political theater" that the kings didn't really believe. The chapter avoids both. It presents the touch as what it was: a genuine institution, believed by those who practiced and received it, with its own internal logic and its own empirical record. The statement "The touch was real. The healing was real. God healed; the king touched. The logic was complete" is exactly the right way to render this from within.

The integration of sacred and legal authority is also handled correctly. The chapter argues that the three registers (sacred, legal, military) were not separate faces of royal power but one face: the king who healed was the same king who held Normandy and who broke coalitions. This is the argument that scholarship on Capetian ideology has advanced since Ernst Kantorowicz's *The King's Two Bodies* (1957) and that the chapter reaches without citing it.

## Corrections Required

No factual corrections required. The thaumaturgical material is handled with full scholarly accuracy and appropriate cultural depth.

**Suggestion (not a correction):** The chapter might note briefly that Philip himself was skeptical of the healing ceremony's efficacy in at least one documented instance (there is a passing reference in Rigord to Philip questioning whether God had actually healed a particular man). This would add a dimension to Philip's character that is consistent with his general pragmatism: the king who performed the sacred function because it was politically necessary, without necessarily believing it in the same way his subjects did. This is speculative and could go either way; it is offered as a possible enrichment, not a correction.

The integration of sacred, legal, and military authority as a unified system is analytically correct and narratively compelling.
