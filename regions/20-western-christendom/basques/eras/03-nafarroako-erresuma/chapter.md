# Nafarroako Erresuma — The Kingdom of Navarre
## c. 824–1234 CE

The pass does not care who crosses it.

Orreaga in August: the light comes in hard and flat from the west, throwing long shadows into the gorge below. The scrub grass between the rocks smells of dry earth and the particular coolness that comes off the stone even in summer, the smell of a place that holds winter inside itself. Eneko son of Aizarna lay flat on the upper ridge, where he knew the stone loosened near the lip and a careful man could see down without being seen. He had walked this ridgeline since he could walk. He knew which rocks moved in the thaw and which ones would hold a man's weight in August.

He heard the rearguard before he saw it. Metal on metal, the slow creak of wagon wheels on the stone track below, the sounds of an army not expecting trouble — spread too thin through the narrow gorge, the baggage train strung out and vulnerable, the end of it still not visible around the southern bend. He listened and counted what he heard. He had been counting since dawn.

Below, Charlemagne's rearguard was crossing.

The army of the Franks had spent the summer in Iberia. They had come south at the invitation of a Saracen governor who needed allies and then thought better of the offer. The campaign had accomplished little. Zaragoza had not opened its gates. The emperor had turned north, impatient, ordering the walls of Pamplona torn down — Iruñea, city of the Basques of Navarre — for reasons the Basques found sufficient to remember. Now the rearguard was strung out through Orreaga, the narrowest place in the Pyrenees, loaded with what the army had managed to take. The baggage train. The supply wagons. Twelve thousand, some said, or fewer, or more — the counts vary by who is counting and what they want the number to mean.

The men on the ridge had no interest in the count.

What happened next was quick. The Basques rolled boulders from the heights on both sides. The Frankish knights in their armor — useless armor, in a gorge, against stone falling from above — could not form a line, could not charge uphill into the broken terrain, could not bring their cavalry advantage to bear on enemies who stood above them and then did not stand there at all. The rearguard was destroyed. The commander died. The baggage was taken. The Basques withdrew into the mountains from which they had come, leaving the pass empty and quiet, the way mountain passes are always quiet after the difficult business is done.

The Franks called the dead commander Roland.

The poets would later have much to say about Roland. They would say the enemy was Saracens, not Basques, because Saracens were the proper enemy of the *chanson*, and the Christians dying against Saracens made a story that served the courts of France. The poem grew to 4,000 lines. It became one of the foundational works of French literature. The Basques, who had actually won at Orreaga, do not appear in it. This is not a complaint. It is simply the record: what happened, and what the poem said happened, are two different things, and the Basques have always lived in the distance between them.

## Before the Kingdom

Long before 778, before 824, before any of the names that the kingdoms of the north and south attached to the mountain people between them, there was the assembly.

The valley communities of the Basque country had a way of making decisions. They gathered — in the meadow, under a tree, at the flat ground where two valleys met — and they talked until they reached an agreement. Not a majority, not a decree: an agreement. What one valley claimed as pasture, another might also use; the boundary between them was not a line on a map but a memory held by the people who had grazed their animals on both sides of it for generations. When the memory was disputed, the assembly resolved it. When a new claim arose — a mill built on a shared stream, an iron-forge that needed forest for charcoal — the assembly considered it. The decision was not written down. It was remembered.

The Basque word for "knife" is *aizto*, which carries within it the element *haitz* — stone — a likely fossil from the period before metal, when cutting tools were made from flint. The word predates the metal it now names. When a Basque speaker says *aizto* for a steel blade, she is using a word that remembers the time before steel, that carries inside itself the memory of the people who made cutting tools from flint in these same mountains. Language is the oldest archive. Euskara, which has no traceable ancestor, which was already ancient when the Indo-European migrations remade the linguistic map of Europe and left Euskara standing exactly where it had always stood, carries inside its vocabulary the record of everything the history books have forgotten.

The valley assembly ran on this kind of memory. The specific legal procedures — who spoke first, how a disputed inheritance was adjudicated, what the penalty for violating a boundary agreement was — were held in living minds and transmitted through the oral tradition. The *bertsolaritza* played its role here: the improvisational verse-maker who could summarize a decision, a boundary, an agreement in the formal meters the tradition required. The meter was not decoration. It was constraint — the formal requirement that bound the composition and made it repeatable, memorable, resistant to variation. Not entertainment. An archive.

By the time the Visigoths came, the valley assemblies had been running this way for longer than anyone could trace. The Visigoths built their kingdom in the south of the peninsula and sent administrators north. The administrators encountered the valley assemblies and the customs those assemblies enforced — the *foru* — and found them inconvenient. The Basques found the Visigoths inconvenient in return. The standoff lasted until the Umayyad armies crossed from Africa in 711 and the Visigoths had larger problems. The Basques watched from their ridges. When it was over, they were still there.

The Franks came next. After them came Iñigo Arista and the kingdom with a name.

## Iñigo Arista and the Kingdom's First Form

The year 824 is an approximation. What is clear is that by the third decade of the ninth century, a lord named Iñigo Arista held Iruñea and the surrounding valleys with enough authority that both the Frankish Carolingians and the Umayyad Emirate of Córdoba treated him as a negotiating partner rather than a subject. He was not a great military commander. He was something more useful: a man who understood that Navarre's survival depended on never becoming fully useful to either of its powerful neighbors, because a fully useful ally can be incorporated and a fully incorporated ally is no longer separate.

His name is a map of the world he lived in. Iñigo is Basque; Arista is possibly Latin or Iberian. His family had marriage ties to the Banu Qasi, the Arabicized Iberian family that controlled the Ebro valley to the south and served as the Emirate's border managers on the upper Ebro. The Banu Qasi called themselves "the sons of Count Cassius," tracing their lineage to a Visigoth who had converted to Islam after the conquest. They were Christian by ancestry, Muslim by practice, Iberian by location, and pragmatic by temperament. Iñigo's connection to them was not apostasy — it was geography. The Ebro valley was the road south. The Banu Qasi controlled the Ebro valley. A kingdom that needed to trade south had to deal with the Banu Qasi.

The Frankish chronicles record Iñigo as a troublemaker. The Umayyad chronicles record him as a sometimes-client who occasionally paid tribute and occasionally did not. Neither set of records captures what Iñigo was doing, which was holding a mountain pass between two greater powers and calling that holding a kingdom. He was not wrong to call it that. A kingdom is, at minimum, a place where one set of laws obtains and where the people who live there have agreed — however provisionally — to recognize the same authority. The *foru* was already there. What Iñigo provided was the external-facing form: a lord who could sign a treaty, receive an embassy, and give the mountain people a name in the documents of the powers that surrounded them.

The valley assemblies continued to meet. The *foru* continued to operate. What the kingdom added was legibility.

## The Foru at Work

In the autumn of the year that Iñigo's grandson held Iruñea, a dispute came before the valley assembly of the upper Arga. A ferrería — an iron forge on the mountain stream above the village of Lanz — had been claimed by two families, both of whom traced their right to it through different branches of the same grandfather's lineage. The forge was not idle speculation. It was the largest ironworking site in the valley: a water wheel powered the bellows that fed the charcoal fire that reached the temperature needed to work iron ore from the nearby hills. The sound of its hammering carried to the valley floor all morning; the smoke of its charcoal fire was visible from two ridges away; the smell of hot metal and soot was the smell of prosperity.

Both families worked the forge. Neither would yield. The eldest son of the first family had been there longest; the eldest daughter of the second had married into the forge through her father's arrangement with the original owner. The claims crossed. The assembly gathered on a clear October morning, twenty or thirty people sitting in the meadow above the forge, the hammer sounds coming up the hill to remind them what was at stake.

The proceedings were in Euskara. No Latin. No Frankish. The language of the valley, the language of the agreements, the language in which the memory of the forge's founding and its subsequent history had been transmitted from mouth to ear for three generations. The assembly heard both claims. It asked questions. It consulted the oldest member present, who had been a child when the grandfather in question made his original arrangements. The process was not fast. Basque assemblies were not designed for speed; they were designed for agreements that would hold.

The bertsolar at the assembly — a young man named Otsoa who had already made a name in the valley competitions — waited. When the assembly had finished its deliberations and reached its decision, he composed: four stanzas summarizing the finding, the reasoning, the boundary of the right, and the obligation each family carried forward. The stanzas rhymed. They were written in the meter the tradition required. They were spoken aloud, and the assembly listened, and they were correct — the people who had been there would remember them, and the people who learned them later would know the same thing. The decision existed in the form the community trusted.

This is what the *foru* looked like when it worked. Not a document in a scriptorium. A memory in a community's mouth.

When the king's representative came through the valley six months later — collecting what the kingdom now called a tax and what the valley assembly recognized as a negotiated contribution — he asked who owned the ferrería. The answer came back in Euskara, citing the assembly's decision. The representative did not speak Euskara well. He wrote down what he understood in Latin, which was approximate. The Latin version exists in the archives. The Euskara version, which was more precise, lived in the community's memory for three more generations before it was written down, and by then the Latin approximation had been taken as the authoritative record.

This is how the archive works when the writing belongs to someone else.

## Sancho the Great and the Peak of Navarre

By the end of the tenth century, the Kingdom of Navarre had survived six generations of Frankish pressure, Umayyad raids, and the general turbulence of a peninsula reorganizing itself after the fracture of the Umayyad Emirate into the smaller *taifa* kingdoms. Navarre had done this by being, consistently, more trouble than it was worth to absorb and more useful than it was convenient to destroy. It had developed the Camino de Santiago.

The Camino was already a pilgrimage route before Sancho III Garcés made it Navarrese policy, but it was Sancho who understood what the route could do for his kingdom if properly organized. The road from the Frankish kingdoms to the tomb of Saint James at Santiago de Compostela passed through the most accessible crossing of the Pyrenees — at Orreaga, the same pass where Charlemagne's rearguard had died two centuries earlier. The irony was not lost on anyone who knew both histories, and most people in the mountains knew both.

Sancho ordered bridges built, hospices maintained, and the road through Navarrese territory kept safe. The work was practical: a bridge at Puente la Reina where the roads from three Pyrenean crossings joined into one; a hospice at the pass itself, where pilgrims arriving over the mountains could rest before descending into the valley; a market at Pamplona, reorganized on the pilgrimage-road logic that made the city a stopping point for travelers from a dozen kingdoms.

In October, when the pilgrimage season was moving toward its end before the winter closed the pass, Pamplona's market smelled of tallow candles and damp wool and the bread baking in the hospice ovens. Pilgrims arrived speaking Occitan, Castilian, Flemish, German, and the Basque of the local guides who helped them negotiate the mountain paths. The market stalls sold the things pilgrims needed: rope, bread, leather for shoe repair, iron nails for the boots that wore out on the stone tracks. Basque ironmongers sold to German pilgrims; Navarrese merchants sold wine to Flemish traders who had never tasted anything that red. The whole city was organized around the fact of movement — everyone passing through, everyone needing something for the next stage of the road.

The mountain pass that his great-great-grandparents had used to destroy Charlemagne's rearguard was now the gateway to the most sacred destination in Western Christendom. The King of Navarre was not merely a border lord between competing powers. He was the caretaker of the road to Santiago. Popes wrote to him. Abbots corresponded with him. The King of France sent embassies. Sancho styled himself "Emperor of all Spain" — a claim far larger than his actual territory, but an accurate description of his ambition and his standing in the network of Christian kingdoms that used his road.

At his death in 1035, his territory stretched from the Atlantic to the eastern borders of Castile and Aragon. He had controlled more of the Iberian peninsula than any Christian ruler since the Visigoths. He divided it among his heirs.

This was not a failure of judgment. It was the *foru*'s logic operating at the scale of a kingdom. The covenant was made with each valley separately; the loyalty it generated was to the specific agreement, not to the dynasty. A kingdom built on distributed covenant cannot consolidate as a centralized hereditary empire without breaking the covenant at its roots. Sancho's heirs would rule Castile, Aragon, and Navarre as separate kingdoms. The *foru* in each territory would continue to be sworn separately, by whichever king happened to hold the territory that week. The mechanism had outlasted its first host. It would outlast more.

## García Ramírez and the Restoration

In 1076 Navarre was absorbed into Aragon when its king was murdered by his own brothers and Alfonso I of Aragon seized the opportunity. Navarre disappeared as a separate kingdom for sixty years, swallowed by a neighbor that had its own dynastic ambitions and its own view of where borders should run.

In 1134, Alfonso I died without heirs. Navarre and Aragon, which had been one kingdom under him, divided again along lines that the Navarrese nobility insisted upon. García Ramírez, "the Restorer," was elected king by an assembly of Navarrese lords who chose a domestic candidate over the foreign alternatives. He was not elected because he was a great warrior or a brilliant administrator. He was elected because he was there, and because the assembly had the right to choose.

The election itself was the statement. The Navarrese nobility did not wait for a dynastic heir. They assembled and decided. This was the *foru* operating at the highest political level: the community's prior claim to its own governance, expressed as a right of election rather than a right of swearing. García Ramírez's legitimacy came from the assembly, not from bloodline. He understood this. His first act as king was to travel to each of the Basque provinces and swear the *foru* under its specific terms. He did not do this once. He did it five times, once for each territory, once for each community's specific agreement.

His restoration held. Not because he was powerful — he was not — but because the mechanism that had generated the kingdom's identity in the first place was still operating. The kingdom could be extinguished and re-lit. The *foru* did not need the dynasty to survive.

## The Slow Passing

After García Ramírez, Navarre shrank. Castile took territories to the south and west; Aragon pressed from the east. The kingdom that had briefly extended from the Atlantic to the borders of two other kingdoms became a smaller and smaller thing, pressed back toward the mountain heartland that had always been its core.

By the end of the twelfth century, Navarre was effectively surrounded by the kingdoms that had grown faster and consolidated more successfully. Sancho VII, called "the Strong," fought against the Almohads at Las Navas de Tolosa in 1212, where the chroniclers recorded him breaking through the chains that guarded the Almohad caliph's tent — a story that gave Navarre's arms their most distinctive element, the chain motif that appears on the kingdom's seal. Whether the story is literally true is less important than what it reveals about how Navarre wanted to be seen: present at the decisive moments of Iberian history, still relevant, still fighting.

Sancho VII died without heirs in 1234. The succession went to the son of his sister Blanche — Theobald of Champagne, Count of Champagne and Brie, a French lord whose connection to Navarre was entirely dynastic, whose language was Occitan French, and who had never stood on Basque ground.

He came to claim his inheritance in the autumn. The oak at Guernica was very old by then — its trunk wider than a man's armspan, its crown throwing shade over the assembly ground. The valley people gathered as they had gathered for four centuries, for longer, under trees that had stood before any of the kingdoms arrived to organize them. Theobald stood in front of the oak. He heard the terms of the *foru* read to him. He did not understand all of it. He swore it anyway, in Euskara words that had been written out for him phonetically, in the presence of the assembled community, on Basque ground.

The price of governance was what it had always been. He paid it.

## What Endured

The kingdom passed. What the kingdom was a vessel for did not pass.

The *foru* continued. Each new lord of Vizcaya, each new count of Álava, each king who acquired Navarrese territory through marriage or inheritance had to travel to the Basque lands and swear the oath under its specific terms. The ceremony changed its external trappings — the language of the court around it, the titles of the witnesses, the political context in which the swearing took place — but the core remained. The community's prior claim to its own law, expressed as the condition of governance, recognized by whoever wanted to govern.

Euskara continued. The language that had survived the Romans and the Visigoths and the Frankish incursions and the Umayyad advances and three centuries of a kingdom that conducted most of its official business in Latin continued to be spoken in the valleys, sung in the competitions, used in the assembly deliberations, and taught from parent to child in the farmsteads of the mountains. The word *aizto* continued to carry *haitz* — stone — inside itself even as it named a steel blade. The language remembered what the history books forgot.

The bertsolaritza continued. Long after the Kingdom of Navarre became a possession of French counts, the assembly tradition's memory system continued to operate. Debates were composed in verse and remembered communally. Legal decisions were summarized in the meters the tradition required. The oral archive held what the written archives approximated.

Three centuries after Theobald of Champagne swore his oath under the oak at Guernica, the Castilian kings would absorb the Basque territories directly into the Castilian crown. They would also come to Guernica. They would also swear. The mechanism that Iñigo Arista had inherited from the valley assemblies that preceded him would outlast the kingdom that had given it a name on the maps of neighboring powers.

This is what it means to be the mountain rather than to cross it. The people who know where the stone loosens and where the path narrows to the width of a single horse do not need to hold the pass against every traveler. They only need to know that anyone who wants to stay must come to them. And swear.
