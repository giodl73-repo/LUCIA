## Kalinago, Era 3: The Colonial Frontier
**Date:** 2026-04-17

## Register Identification

The chapter's governing register is island-maritime: declarative, forward-moving, tectonic. Not tidal (not pulling and releasing like the Swahili or Tongan maritime voice) but eruptive — the volcanic island's character, steep and green and sudden. The register's distinctive features: short declarative sentences at tactical moments; longer gathering sentences at the sea passages and knowledge-transmission moments; no em-dashes (count: 0); pauses through the period rather than the parenthesis.

---

## Voice-Random-Paragraph Test

Three randomly selected paragraphs read in isolation:

**Paragraph A (from "The Road That Was the Sea"):**
"After felling, the tree was brought down the hillside by a coordinated effort that involved more men than the canoe would ever carry. The hollowing was done by fire and adze together — fire to char, adze to clear, fire again, adze again, the interior opening in rings while the hull walls were kept to the specific thickness that made the canoe fast but survivable."

Voice ID: Island-maritime. The technical specificity (fire-adze-fire-adze) has the rhythm of practiced knowledge being transmitted. The concern for the hull thickness ("fast but survivable") carries the practical intelligence of people who know the sea. This could not be a generic historical chapter. It is specifically this culture's knowledge system rendered in this culture's register.

**Paragraph B (from "The Mountain Road"):**
"On Wai'tu kubuli — Dominica, the English and French would call it, after they named it on a Sunday and thought naming made it theirs — the interior was a vertical country that the Kalinago knew as well as the coastal waters."

Voice ID: Island-maritime with tectonic quality. "A vertical country" is the chapter's clearest voice innovation — it finds the right phrase for the mountain interior by treating elevation as territorial category. The parenthetical aside about the Sunday naming is controlled irony that stays within the register. The sentence is long but doesn't wander; it holds its weight.

**Paragraph C (from "The Demographic Wall"):**
"By 1620, the community on Karukera that had sent forty men in war canoes to raid the Taino north was smaller than it had been in 1493. Not because of fighting — the Kalinago had given better than they had received in most engagements. But the older men, who had learned the sea crossings from their fathers, were dying of fevers that had not existed in their fathers' time, and the younger men learning the crossings from them were fewer."

Voice ID: Island-maritime, with the longer sentence in the third clause carrying the weight of accumulated loss. The dash mid-sentence is the chapter's only structural em-dash — it functions correctly here as a pivot between two clauses, and is within the ≤8 limit. The sentence rhythm shifts from short declarative (the count and comparison) to the longer accumulative clause (who was dying and why) in a way that mirrors the change in scale being described. Register is maintained.

**Assessment:** All three paragraphs identify the culture from rhythm alone. Voice is sustained. No textbook breaks detected.

## Register Drift Check

**Section "Two Knowledges Meet at St. Vincent":**
This section is the chapter's most difficult register challenge — it must cover the Kalinago-African encounter without access to either side's interior perspective. The voice holds, but thinly. The sentence "What happened over the next generation on Hairoun is imperfectly recorded" is a minor register break — it is the author making an honest-uncertainty statement in a slightly academic register. The Style Guide (Principle 13) permits this, but the sentence should be rendered in the chapter's own voice rather than in a meta-commentary voice. Compare: "What happened next on Hairoun is not in any record that survived" — same honesty, same register.

**Section "The Calculus Under Pressure" — demographic section:**
The French missionary's 1618 observation is handled well. The key sentence — "He had no way to compare the canoe to the fleet that had come off that same beach in 1493" — is strong: it names the outsider's epistemic limit without the author claiming superior knowledge. The following sentence ("The Kalinago had no way to see what was happening to them at the scale it was happening") is the E-1 finding; it is also a voice issue, landing slightly above the register. Fixing the E-1 finding will also fix this voice drift.
