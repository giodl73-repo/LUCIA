# PANEL-1 Review — Barbara Tuchman
## Bakumatsu Opening

**Reviewer:** Barbara Tuchman (narrative craft)  
**Date:** 2026-04-16  

---

The first line is perfect. "The ships were black, and they came in the morning." This is how you open. Not with explanation, not with context, but with the image — the color, the action, the time. By the third paragraph, I understand I am watching the boundary break, and I am watching it from inside.

The narrative architecture here is exceptional. You have Tōjirō as the observer, and as the ships arrive, the power to decide shifts away from him toward Nakajima. This is not told; it is shown through the mechanics of command. The civilian authority is — in real time, in this scene — becoming insufficient. That is craft.

The restraint is the thing that works best. In the moment when the officer arrives and says "We fire on the signal," Tōjirō hesitates. Not because he is weak, but because this hesitation contains everything: thirty years of no precedent, a system designed for boundaries that no one had tested, and now the test has arrived. The "Not a question" parenthetical does crucial work. Nakajima is not asking. But Tōjirō does not answer immediately. That pause is worth its weight in gold.

The movement of the ships themselves — "maneuvered with an ease that defied the mathematics of its size" — is the image that carries the argument. You do not say the Americans are more advanced. You describe what their ships do. The reader arrives at "advanced" on their own, through observation.

Three scenes within the scene: (1) Tōjirō watching the ships arrive, (2) the officer arriving and authority shifting, (3) the messenger bringing news of Perry's letter. The progression is clean — each moment adds pressure without repeating itself.

**One question:** The ending circles back to "what do we do?" — it is the same question Tōjirō asked in the middle. Is this repetition intentional (the question cannot be answered, only restated), or would a different landing carry more momentum into the full chapter? The signal fires burning on — that's a strong image, but does it move the narrative forward, or does it hold the scene in place?

**Pull:** Absolute. By the time I finish, I am desperate to know what Tōjirō does, what the shogunate decides, what this rupture produces. The opening has created a situation that cannot be un-created. That is the definition of pull.

This chapter is ready to go to the panel.
