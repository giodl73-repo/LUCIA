## Archaeological and Material Culture Review

**Kumbi Saleh identification:** "The site that archaeologists have identified as the probable capital" — correct hedging. The Kumbi Saleh identification with Al-Bakri's capital has been the dominant scholarly view but is not archaeologically confirmed beyond doubt. The chapter's "probable" is the right qualifier.

**The twin-city structure:** Al-Bakri's six-mile separation between royal enclosure and merchant quarter is supported by the Kumbi Saleh excavations, which have revealed a substantial stone town (the merchant quarter) distinct from the pattern expected of the royal enclosure. The stone houses with flat roofs in the merchant quarter are consistent with the archaeological findings. The chapter's description matches the evidence.

**The merchant quarter's material evidence:** "Fragments of painted plaster, glass weights, and small gold pieces have surfaced from the sand" — consistent with the Kumbi Saleh excavation record (Berthier, Thomassey, and subsequent work). Glass weights are particularly significant as archaeological evidence of standardized commercial practice; the chapter is correct to include them.

**The royal enclosure's archaeology:** "The royal quarter, built of wood and thatch within its grove of trees, has left less trace, because wood and thatch return to the earth in ways that stone does not." This is archaeologically accurate and appropriately honest about the excavation record. The royal town's wooden construction means its physical evidence is substantially thinner than the merchant quarter's stone remains. The chapter's "The archaeological silence where the royal town stood is its own evidence" is a valid and perceptive reading of the record.

**Salt blocks from Taghaza:** The chapter describes the salt blocks as "cut at Taghaza, wrapped in reed matting and then in goatskin against the desert's wear." This is consistent with documented caravan practice for Taghaza salt. The mineral character of the salt, its smell after transit, the weight loss to evaporation — these are plausible material-culture details grounded in what we know of trans-Saharan salt commerce.

**Gold dust and scales:** "Small brass balance... the dust was a particular color in midday light — not the bright yellow of worked gold jewelry but the pale, warm, almost-grey of fine dust." This is accurate: alluvial gold dust has a different appearance from refined worked gold. The color description is consistent with documented appearances of Sahelian alluvial gold. Brass balance scales are archaeologically attested in Sahelian commercial contexts.

**The harmattan and ecology:** The chapter's ecological description — "the Sahel was likely wetter then than now, the margins broader, the grass reaching farther toward the sand" — is consistent with paleoclimatic evidence for the western Sahel in the 7th–11th centuries CE. The chapter correctly notes this with appropriate epistemic hedging ("likely").

**Guard dogs with gold and silver collars:** Al-Bakri's report. The chapter does not add archaeological confirmation because there is none — this is documented only through the Arabic account. Correct to present as an Al-Bakri report without claiming material evidence.

## One Observation

The chapter does not address the Tichitt Tradition archaeology, which is appropriate given this chapter covers the High Wagadu period (700–1076 CE). The Tichitt material belongs to Era 1. No gap here.

## Corrections Required

None. The material culture claims are consistent with the archaeological record and the chapter is appropriately hedged where the evidence is thin or absent.

## Endorsement

The chapter's physical descriptions are grounded in what the archaeology and the documentary record actually show. The handling of the Kumbi Saleh identification — "probable," with the royal town's absence from the archaeological record honestly noted — is exactly the care this site's complex excavation history requires.

**Archaeological accuracy: ENDORSED.**
