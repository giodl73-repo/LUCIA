# PANEL-1 Review — P-3 Chinua Achebe
## The Long Struggle — Qajar Era 2
**Round:** R1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The voice is right. That is the first thing, and it matters more than the rest.

The opening never steps outside to explain Iran to us. Naser al-Din Shah does not appear as a figure of sympathy or a figure of failure — he appears as a man reading a situation according to his own categories. His calculation ("Iran could survive by concession or by war, and war was not possible") is stated in his own terms, not in terms that invite the reader to judge whether it was the right calculation. The cold pipe is not "a symbol of Iranian resistance to colonialism" — it is what it is, rendered exactly: an object that has gone cold because the country has stopped using tobacco, which proves something the shah did not expect to be proved, at least not this publicly.

**What I want to register as working:**

The rendering of Mirza Hasan Shirazi is exemplary: "the man who had not set foot on Iranian soil in forty years and yet whose word traveled faster than the telegraph." This sentence explains the *marjaiyyat*'s power without explaining it — it shows the fact and trusts the reader to feel its weight. The scholar's authority does not depend on proximity. It travels through fidelity, not through force. The sentence captures that without saying so.

The passage on the Tobacco Protest's spread — the harem, the bazaars, the coffee-houses — is told without any tone of admiration or surprise. It simply happened. The shah's wives put down their pipes. The merchants closed the bazaars. The coffee-houses sat empty. The text does not say "remarkably" or "extraordinarily" or "despite the shah's power." The spread of the fatwa's reach is treated as a natural consequence of how the system worked, because within the world of this opening, that IS how the system works.

**One sentence I want to flag:**

"He had gone to Europe three times and seen what the Europeans had built with their factories and their railways and their naval academies and their maps — maps on which Iran appeared smaller than Naser al-Din Shah believed it should appear."

This is very close to correct. The last clause — "maps on which Iran appeared smaller than Naser al-Din Shah believed it should appear" — is told from inside the shah's perspective, which is right. But "the Europeans" in the first clause risks a slight flattening. Not a P1 violation, but the chapter should be careful not to turn "Europe" into a monolithic force that acts on Iran. Britain and Russia had very different interests and very different methods. The chapter should keep them distinct throughout.

**The question I'd push on:**

The opening centers Naser al-Din Shah, which is right — his fifty-year reign is the era's spine. But the chapter must also give voice to the people who are not the shah. The merchants in the bazaars had their own reasons for joining the tobacco boycott, and they were not simply obeying the fatwa — they had economic grievances against the concession system that had been building for decades. The clerics had their own institutional interests in establishing independent authority from the state. These are not cynical framings; they are the full human reality of why the protest worked. The chapter should show multiple motivations without any of them canceling the others.

**The word "the West" (warning for the full chapter):**

The opening does not use this phrase, which is correct. The chapter must continue to resist it. Russia is not "the West." Britain is. They are not interchangeable. The Qajar world knew the difference keenly.

**Verdict:** The culture owns its story here. The shah is the protagonist, not a victim, not a symbol. Proceed with care.
