# P-5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Review of "When the Empire Reached the Sea" Opening

**Overall:** The opening has a magnificent first line and a strong narrative arc, but the courtyard at Niani needs a body. I can hear the captain's report. I cannot yet feel the heat of the stone where Abu Bakr sits.

**What works:**
- "The canoes came back wrong." Five words. The reader is hooked. This is how you begin — not with context, not with geography, but with something that has gone wrong and demands explanation.
- The captain's account of the water changing color — "from brown to green to a blue so deep it was almost black" — is sensory writing at its best. The reader sees the ocean through the captain's memory. The canoes disappearing "not capsized, not swamped by waves, but pulled forward into something the captain could not name" has physical dread.
- "The jelilu do not say he died. They say he did not come back" — this has the weight of something carved. It will stay with the reader.

**What doesn't:**
- The courtyard scene needs grounding. Abu Bakr sits on a raised platform. The captain kneels in "the red dust." Good — that is a color. But I want more. What does the courtyard smell like? Is it morning or afternoon? What is the temperature? Who else is present as physical bodies, not just as "the court"? The jelilu "stood at the edges of the courtyard" — are they in shade? Is there shade? When the captain finishes, is there silence? What fills it? The era 3 opening gave us "dust and shea butter and woodsmoke" in the first paragraph. This opening needs an equivalent sensory constellation.
- Abu Bakr's departure ("He loaded the canoes with provisions for years, not months") needs one physical image. What do the canoes look like loaded? How heavy do they sit in the water? What does the Niger look like at the point where the fleet departs? The departure is narrated but not felt.
- The final paragraph is analytical — the chapter's structural argument stated in plain terms. It works as architecture, but it has no body. Can the final image be grounded in something physical? The gold moving north, the salt moving south — these are abstractions. One image of a loaded camel, one sound of a jeli singing, one smell of the river, and the paragraph lands harder.

**The question I'd push on:**
In the full chapter, every section must have temperature. The Sahel has a specific quality of heat — a dry, pressing heat in the harmattan season, a humid weight before the rains. The Niger has a smell — earth, vegetation, the slightly metallic tang of laterite mud. The goldfields have a texture — the laterite soil, the alluvial gravel in the riverbeds. Give me the body of this empire, not just its politics.
