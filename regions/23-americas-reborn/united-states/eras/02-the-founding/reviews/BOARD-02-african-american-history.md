# Board Review — B-02: African American History
## United States Era 2: The Founding — The Experiment Begins

**Reviewer profile:** Specialist in African American history from the colonial period through Reconstruction; has published on the founding era's free Black community in Philadelphia and on Richard Allen's political thought.

---

### Factual Accuracy Assessment

**Richard Allen and the Free African Society:** The Free African Society founding date (April 12, 1787) is correct. The characterization of the Society as "the first mutual aid organization in America run by and for Black people" is generally accurate; some historians would note that informal mutual support networks existed earlier among Black Philadelphians, but the Free African Society is the first formally organized and incorporated institution of this type with surviving records. The characterization is fair to the historiography.

**The St. George's incident:** The chapter quotes Allen's account directly from his autobiography. The dating of the incident to 1792 in the chapter's notes is consistent with most historical accounts. Allen's account in his autobiography is the primary source; the chapter handles it correctly. Note: the exact date within 1792 is disputed in secondary literature — some accounts place it in 1791 — but the chapter does not specify a month, which is the correct approach given the ambiguity.

**The AME Church:** The chapter states that the African Methodist Episcopal denomination was "formally organized, with Allen as its first bishop" — correctly, in 1816. The chapter does not overstate Allen's independence from the white Methodist Episcopal structure during the Bethel period, which is accurate; the legal battles over Bethel's independence lasted from the 1790s through 1816. This nuance is important and the chapter handles it correctly.

**One addition recommended:** The chapter notes that Allen "spent years in legal battles to establish Bethel's independence and won" without naming the legal mechanism. The Pennsylvania Supreme Court ruled in Allen's favor in 1816 (the "Allenites" case), establishing that the white Methodist conference could not control property held by the Black congregation. For historical precision, the chapter might note that the independence was won through litigation, not simply by organizing. Current text implies this but could be marginally clearer.

---

### Cultural Representation Assessment

**The "Other Philadelphia" section:** The chapter does precisely what the best scholarship asks for: it places the Free African Society's founding in temporal and geographic proximity to the Constitutional Convention without subordinating it to the Convention's narrative. "Both were founded on the same principles. Both are still in operation. This is not a coincidence." — this is the right framing. The Free African Society is not positioned as a response to the Convention; it is positioned as a parallel act of self-governance. Correct.

**Sally Hemings:** The chapter handles Sally Hemings with appropriate complexity. It does not use her as a symbol of Jefferson's hypocrisy, which is the most common failure mode in popular history. It does not romanticize or dramatize the relationship. It states what is known, acknowledges what is not known, and honors the gap. The phrase "what she thought about this we do not know, because no record of her voice survives" is the right approach.

The chapter also correctly notes that four of the Hemings-Jefferson children survived to adulthood and were eventually freed (Beverly, Harriet, Madison, and Eston), while "others" were sold. For precision: Jefferson sold others of his enslaved people to pay debts, but the specific documentation of which children of Hemings he sold (as opposed to never having, or who died young) is contested. The sentence "He sold others" is sufficiently general to be accurate without overclaiming.

**Benjamin Banneker:** The chapter places Banneker correctly — free Black Marylander, self-taught, almanac publisher, DC surveyor, letter-writer to Jefferson. The characterization of his letter as using "the republic's own language to argue for what the republic's own language promised" is historically accurate and is the interpretation that most scholars of the Banneker-Jefferson correspondence hold. The chapter does not overstate Banneker's isolation or understate his social network; the brief characterization is accurate.

**One concern:** The chapter does not mention Phillis Wheatley, who appears in the production notes as a candidate for inclusion. The decision not to include Wheatley in the chapter is defensible — she died in 1784 before the founding era period, and her work was more associated with the Revolutionary period than the constitutional era — but the figures.md might briefly note her absence with a sentence acknowledging that the founding era's argument about Black intellectual capacity had a prior chapter. This is a suggestion, not a required change.

---

### Recommendation

**The chapter handles the African American founding-era material with historical accuracy and appropriate complexity.** Richard Allen, the Free African Society, Sally Hemings, and Banneker are each treated with specificity and without instrumentalization. The "Other Philadelphia" section is the chapter's strongest contribution to the period's historiography — it is rare to see the Free African Society given coequal narrative space with the Constitutional Convention without making the comparison polemical.

**Endorsement with one minor recommendation:** Consider one sentence in figures.md acknowledging Phillis Wheatley's prior contribution to the founding-era argument about Black intellectual life. No other substantive revisions required. Approved for FINAL.
