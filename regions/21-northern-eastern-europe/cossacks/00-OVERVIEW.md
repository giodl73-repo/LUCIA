# Козаки — Вільні Люди — "The Cossacks — The Free People"

The word *kozak* comes from the Turkic for "free man" or "adventurer" — one who lives outside the settled order, who answers to no lord. This is what we were and what we chose to be. In the late fifteenth century, the borderlands between the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, the Muscovite state, the Crimean Khanate, and the Ottoman Empire produced a frontier zone — the *dikoe pole*, the Wild Field — where the authority of every state thinned and eventually vanished, where fugitive serfs, escaped slaves, bankrupt nobles, and adventurers of every description could make a life beyond the reach of the courts and the tax collectors. The Cossacks organized this freedom into something durable: the *Sich* (the fortified camp), the *Kosh* (the community), the elected *hetman*, and the council of the *rada* where every Cossack had an equal voice.

The Zaporozhian Sich — "Below the Rapids," in the lower Dnieper, below the cataracts that made the river impassable to the armies of the north — was the institutional center of Cossack independence. Its location was its charter: armies could not easily reach it, and anyone who could reach it had demonstrated the qualities the Cossacks valued. The Sich operated as a military republic — no women were permitted (though Cossack communities on the river banks above the Sich included families), decisions were made by assembly, the hetman served at the rada's pleasure and could be deposed by it. This was not democracy in any modern sense, but it was a form of self-governance that neither Poland, Russia, nor the Ottomans could replicate and that all three simultaneously needed (as a frontier buffer) and feared (as an independent military force that could turn in any direction).

Key places:
- **Zaporozhian Sich** — the fortified camp below the Dnieper rapids; moved several times; the center of Cossack military life
- **Chyhyryn** — Bohdan Khmelnytsky's capital; the seat of the Hetmanate after 1648
- **Baturyn** — later Hetmanate capital; destroyed by Peter the Great's forces in 1708
- **Sich on Khortytsia Island** — the first recorded permanent Sich, on the island in the Dnieper; archetypal Cossack stronghold

## What Defined Them

**Freedom as a way of life.** The fundamental Cossack value was *volya* — freedom, will, the absence of the master's claim. This was not abstract political philosophy; it was a lived condition. A man who had been a serf in the Polish commonwealth, who had run to the steppe and survived a season and reached the Sich, was free — legally, practically, permanently. The Cossack community did not ask where you came from. It asked whether you could fight and whether you would follow the rada's decisions. The fierce resistance to any attempt to register the Cossacks, to define their numbers, to reduce their autonomy, or to treat them as ordinary soldiers was the defense of this foundational freedom. They had paid for it in hardship, and they would pay for its defense in blood.

**The rada — equality in decision.** The Cossack *rada* (council) was the assembly of all Cossacks where major decisions were made: the election of the hetman, the declaration of war, the negotiation of treaties. Every Cossack had an equal voice, regardless of origin or wealth. The hetman who was elected served at the rada's pleasure and could be deposed by it — several were. This radical equality within the Cossack community coexisted with the hierarchical military organization that made the army effective and with the brutal exclusion of everyone who was not a Cossack. The rada's democracy was the democracy of the brotherhood, not of the nation.

**The Sich as military monastery.** The Zaporozhian Sich combined the functions of a military fortress, a trading post, and something resembling a monastic community — celibate (no women in the Sich proper), organized around a shared identity and a shared purpose, governed by its own rules, and deeply suspicious of the outside world. The Sich had its own church, its own officers, its own treasury, and its own judicial process. Men who entered the Sich left their former identities behind — married men were required to leave their families above the rapids. This total community had a spiritual dimension: the Cossacks understood their frontier service as a kind of sacred obligation, defending Orthodox Christianity against the Muslim Tartars and Ottomans.

**Khmelnytsky and the question of belonging.** Bohdan Khmelnytsky's great uprising of 1648 was simultaneously a Cossack revolt for autonomy, a Ukrainian peasant uprising against Polish nobility, an Orthodox Christian war against Catholic Polish rule, and a bid for independent statehood. Khmelnytsky won stunning victories, established the Hetmanate, and then faced the fundamental question: the Cossack state was not strong enough to survive alone against Poland, Muscovy, and the Ottomans simultaneously. The Pereyaslav Agreement of 1654 — in which the Zaporozhian Host accepted the Tsar of Moscow's "protectorship" — was an attempt to find a powerful patron without surrendering autonomy. Both sides understood the agreement differently; their different understandings led directly to the wars of the Ruin.

**The steppe as freedom and threat.** The Cossack world was created by the steppe and constantly menaced by it. The Crimean Khanate's cavalry raids into Ukrainian territory — the *chababan* raids that seized thousands of people for the Ottoman slave markets — were the constant backdrop of Cossack life. The Cossacks raided in return, sometimes reaching Constantinople's suburbs by sea, sometimes descending on Crimean slave markets to free Ukrainian captives. This mutual raiding was both warfare and trade; Cossacks sold captured Tatars and Tatars sold captured Ukrainians, and the line between raiding and commerce was permanently blurred in the Wild Field. The steppe was the Cossacks' home and their perpetual war.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Emergence:** A former serf, who has been running for six months, reaches the Dnieper rapids and finds the Sich. The men there look him over and ask: can you use a saber? He says yes, which is partly true. They let him in. He will never be a serf again. He does not know yet what he will be instead.

**2 — The Cossack Uprisings:** Hetman Petro Sahaidachny leads the registered Cossacks in service of the Polish king against Moscow, against the Ottomans, against whoever will pay and whoever will expand the number of registered Cossacks. He is the most successful Cossack politician of his generation: using service to Poland to extract concessions. He dies having won more for the Cossacks than any uprising produced. The next generation will not remember his method.

**3 — The Khmelnytsky Era:** Khmelnytsky stands on the battlefield of Zhovti Vody in May 1648, looking at the destroyed Polish army. He has just won the first of what will be five major battles in a single year. He does not yet know what he is building. He knows what he is destroying. He sends a letter to Warsaw: this is what happens when you try to turn free men into serfs.

**4 — The Ruin:** Two hetmans, commanding two Cossack armies on opposite banks of the Dnieper, are receiving the same instruction from different masters — Muscovy says fight Poland, Poland says fight Muscovy, the Ottomans say fight both. The Ruin has no villain. It has only the logic of a small state caught between three empires, all of whom need it and none of whom will let it be.

**5 — The Hetmanate and Its End:** Ivan Mazepa meets Charles XII of Sweden at Baturyn in 1708, having just bet Ukrainian Cossack independence on Sweden defeating Russia. Peter the Great's army reaches Baturyn first. The city is destroyed and its population massacred as a warning. Poltava ends the bet. The Sich is destroyed in 1775 by Catherine the Great. The last hetman is exiled. The freedom that lasted three centuries is over. The songs that remembered it are not.
