# Tiv — "Tar / The Land"

The Tiv live in the Benue River valley of central Nigeria and organize their world from the ground up: from the compound, to the lineage segment, to the lineage, to the clan, and outward until you reach Tiv itself — the name of the founding ancestor from whom all Tiv people claim descent. There is no chief above this structure. There is no king. There is no single center. The entire Tiv world radiates outward from the genealogical principle, and legitimate authority at any level belongs to the senior lineage member at that level. This is not a simple system; it generates enormous complexity in practice, because the segments are always shifting, fissioning, and reconstituting as people move and populations grow and disputes arise. But the principle is clear: authority belongs to genealogy, not to conquest.

The Tiv practice of egalitarianism is embedded in their exchange system. Tsar — the principle of balanced exchange — requires that what is given must be returned in kind. Gifts create obligations; obligations must be discharged; the discharge creates the relationship. The akombo ritual exchange system through which Tiv men acquire the medicine bundles that protect their compounds and their fertility is organized around this principle of balanced obligation. No one becomes wealthy at another's expense without creating a debt that will eventually come due. The Tiv explanation for misfortune — illness, death, crop failure — is typically that the akombo have not been maintained, that the obligations have not been honored, that the balance has been broken. This is simultaneously a cosmological system, a social ethic, and an economic philosophy.

Key places:
- **Gboko** — the principal modern Tiv town; administrative center
- **Benue River** — the defining geographic boundary; the river the Tiv crossed in their westward migration
- **Swem** — the sacred mountain in the traditional Tiv homeland; the center of the founding migration myth
- **Ife and Idoma border zones** — the zones of interaction and exchange with neighboring peoples

## What Defined Them

**The genealogical constitution.** The Tiv do not have chiefs or kings; they have elders — the senior men and women of each lineage segment, whose authority extends precisely as far as their genealogical seniority extends and no further. This is not an informal or weak authority structure; it is a constitution, understood by everyone and enforced by social pressure and ritual means. The British attempt to rule the Tiv through appointed chiefs — the indirect rule policy — failed repeatedly because the appointed chiefs had no genealogical basis for their authority and the Tiv did not recognize it. The "Tiv riots" of 1929, 1939, and the 1960 Tiv crisis were partly reactions to the imposition of a governance model that was structurally incompatible with the Tiv political constitution.

**Akombo and the maintenance of balance.** The akombo are medicine bundles — assemblages of materials charged with spiritual power — that protect the Tiv household, lineage, and community from the forces of harm. Each akombo requires maintenance: regular ritual attention, offerings, and the observance of taboos. When something goes wrong — illness, crop failure, a run of bad luck — the interpretation is typically that an akombo has been neglected or that the obligations surrounding it have not been met. The system creates a continuous audit of social relationships: every misfortune prompts inquiry into which obligations have been violated, which debts have not been repaid, which balances have been broken. Akombo is simultaneously cosmology, social accounting, and therapeutic practice.

**Tsar and the exchange ethics.** The Tiv word tsar encompasses the principle of balanced exchange, the ethic of equivalence, and the standard of moral conduct in all transactions. A gift requires a return of equal value; hospitality requires reciprocal hospitality; a marriage requires exchange of women between lineages (a system called "woman exchange" in the anthropological literature, though this translation flattens its actual cultural logic). Paul Bohannan's classic economic anthropology of the Tiv identified three "spheres of exchange" that could not be crossed without moral condemnation — subsistence goods, prestige goods, and rights in persons — and the social crises that followed colonial money's disruption of those spheres. The Tiv understood their economy as a moral system, and the colonial cash economy's introduction of a universal medium that crossed all spheres simultaneously was understood as a kind of moral pollution.

**The honest limits.** The Tiv historical record is thinner than most of their neighbors' because the society did not develop writing and the colonial encounter produced relatively little documentation compared to the coastal states. Much of what we know about Tiv history comes from the anthropological fieldwork of the 1940s–1960s — primarily Paul and Laura Bohannan — which captures mid-twentieth century Tiv life more confidently than pre-colonial history. The founding migration tradition from Swem is preserved in oral tradition; the pre-nineteenth century history is largely a matter of inference from the contemporary lineage structure and from comparative evidence. This must be stated honestly rather than papered over with confident historical narrative.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Migration:** The founding ancestor crossed the Benue. The tradition does not give the year; it gives the crossing, the new land, the beginning of the genealogical tree. Every Tiv person alive can trace their descent from this moment, through a genealogy they know.

**2 — The Compound:** A Tiv elder sits at the center of his compound. His sons' compounds are built around his. His grandsons' compounds will be built around theirs. The constitution of the Tiv world is spatial as well as genealogical.

**3 — The Akombo Ceremony:** The medicine bundle has not been attended to since the last planting. The old man who maintained it has died. His son is learning the obligations now, from a neighbor who was there when the bundle was last charged. The knowledge is being transferred. The balance must be maintained.

**4 — The Colonial Chief:** The British district officer has appointed a Tiv man as "chief" of his area. The man accepts the appointment. He knows, and the district officer knows, and the Tiv of the area know, that this is not what a chief is. The question is what the appointee does with the position. Some use it to help their community. Some use it for themselves. The system creates neither kind automatically.
