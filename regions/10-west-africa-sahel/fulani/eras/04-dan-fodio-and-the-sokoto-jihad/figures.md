# Figures — Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad

## Usman dan Fodio (the Shehu)

He was born in Maratta, in the territory of Gobir, in 1754, to a Torodbe family whose business was knowledge. His father taught him. His uncle taught him. Jibril ibn Umar, who had studied in the scholarly centers of the central Sahara and, by some accounts, in the North African tradition, taught him the Maliki methodology that would become the foundation of everything he wrote. By his twenties, he was teaching at Degel, and by his thirties, thousands of students moved through his compound each year, sitting on reed mats in the shade of the awning while the Shehu spoke in a voice that witnesses described as quiet but carrying, the voice of a man accustomed to being listened to rather than a man who needed to be loud.

His hands were stained with gum-arabic ink. This is a small detail, but it matters: the Shehu wrote constantly, producing over a hundred treatises in Arabic and Fulfulde, his reed pen wearing grooves into the wooden writing boards his students would later copy and carry across the Sahel. The *Kitab al-Farq*, the *Ihya al-Sunna*, the *Bayan Wujub al-Hijra*: these were not private meditations. They were political documents that circulated through the scholarly network and built, over two decades, the intellectual case for the jihad he would eventually call.

He did not want a state. This is the fact his legacy turns on. He wanted a community that lived according to the Prophet's example, governed by the Sharia as the Maliki jurists understood it, led by scholars whose authority derived from learning rather than from armies. When the jihad he called produced a state, he withdrew from its governance and continued writing. His later treatises document, with the same juridical precision he had applied to the Hausa sarakuna, the ways in which the Caliphate he had created was failing its own standards. He did not see this as contradiction. He saw it as the scholar's permanent obligation: to hold the ruler to the law, even when the ruler claims to govern in the law's name.

He died in 1817, in his room near Sokoto, his books around him. He had been writing to the end.

## Muhammad Bello

The Shehu's son was the man who built what the Shehu called into being. Born around 1781, he grew up in the compound at Degel, educated by his father and by the visiting scholars who passed through the Torodbe network. He was a scholar; his *Infaq al-Maysur*, a history of the jihad, remains one of the primary accounts. But where his father thought in terms of community, Bello thought in terms of institutions.

At the battle of Tabkin Kwotto, he was twenty-three and fighting. In the years that followed, he organized military campaigns, managed the flag-holder network, and negotiated the submission of Hausa states that fell without a fight. When the military phase ended, he founded Sokoto in 1809, chose its site for strategic and sanitary reasons (higher ground, cleaner water, defensible approaches), and designed the administrative system that would link thirty emirates to a central authority. Emirs owed allegiance and tribute to the Sultan; the Sultan owed the emirs military support and legal guidance; the qadis administered the Sharia in the courts.

He was pragmatic where his father was principled, which is not a criticism of either. The Caliphate required someone who could manage the transition from revolutionary movement to functioning state, and Bello managed it. He corresponded with the emirs, adjudicated disputes between them, organized military responses to external threats, and wrote his own treatises on governance. He governed until his death in 1837, having held the Caliphate together through its most fragile years.

## Abdullahi dan Fodio

The Shehu's brother led the western campaigns that brought the lands between the Niger and the Hausa states under the Caliphate's authority. He governed from Gwandu, where he established a parallel administrative center for the western emirates.

Abdullahi was also a poet, and his *Tazyin al-Waraqat* is the most candid primary source on the jihad's internal tensions. He documented disagreements with Bello over the distribution of tribute and the appointment of emirs. He recorded his own frustrations with the gap between the jihad's ideals and the state's practice. Where Bello's accounts are diplomatic, Abdullahi's are honest in a way that approaches pain. He believed in what his brother had started. He was not always certain that what his nephew was building was the same thing.

The tension between Gwandu and Sokoto was a structural feature of the Caliphate, not a personal failing. Two centers of authority, each claiming the Shehu's legacy, each interpreting the founding texts slightly differently. Abdullahi's Gwandu was more scholarly and less administrative; Bello's Sokoto was more institutional and less theoretical. The Caliphate held together because both men were alive and the Shehu's memory was fresh. Whether it would hold under lesser figures was a question Abdullahi did not live to see answered. He died in 1829.

## Nana Asma'u

She was eleven years old at the hijra and present at the founding of the community that became the Caliphate. She grew up in the Shehu's household, educated in Arabic, Fulfulde, and Hausa, reading the same texts her father taught his male students and producing her own scholarship from an early age. By the time she was in her twenties, she was writing poetry that circulated through the same networks her father's treatises had traveled.

Her contribution was not merely literary. After the jihad's military phase, when the Caliphate's administration was concentrated in cities and the rural Fulani communities were receiving less of the scholarly attention the movement had promised, Nana Asma'u organized the *yan taru*. These were associations of women teachers, trained by Asma'u herself, who traveled on foot to rural communities to educate women in the faith, in practical matters of law and hygiene, and in the obligations the jihad had committed the community to uphold. Each *yan taru* teacher was identified by a distinctive hat, the *malafa*, that marked her authority. The system was a second scholarly network, carried on women's feet across the same territory the flag-holders had claimed.

She wrote over sixty works. Her elegies, composed in the decades after the jihad's military phase, for her father, her brother, and the community's martyrs are among the most significant bodies of women's poetry in the Hausa-Fulani tradition. Her instructional verses in Fulfulde and Hausa reached audiences the Arabic treatises could not. She died in 1864, having outlived the jihad's founders by decades and having built, in the *yan taru*, an institution that embodied the Shehu's original vision more faithfully than many of the emirates his jihad had created.

## Umaru Dallaji

The flag-holder for Katsina appears in this chapter as the opening's human entry point: a scholar in a compound, receiving a letter and a flag, the revolution arriving in his hands. The historical Umaru Dallaji was a Torodbe scholar of local standing who had been part of the correspondence network for years before the jihad call. He led the conquest of Katsina and became its first Fulani emir.

The record does not preserve his inner life. What it preserves is his function: the point where the Shehu's scholarly argument met a specific city, a specific market, a specific population of herders and traders and craftspeople who would live or die by the flag's outcome. He was one of perhaps thirty flag-holders, each making the same calculation in a different city. The flags went up because men like Umaru Dallaji believed the arguments they had studied were worth more than the safety they would lose. Whether each of them, in the quiet of their compounds before the decision was made, felt the weight of that calculation the way this chapter imagines it, the sources do not say. The sources say the flags went up.

## Sarki Yunfa of Gobir

The man who forced the Shehu's hand had been the Shehu's student. This is the detail that makes the political drama personal. Yunfa came to power in Gobir and inherited a Torodbe community that his predecessor had accommodated. He chose confrontation: the demand to disarm, the restriction on new converts, the revocation of privileges the community had held for a generation.

Whether Yunfa understood what he was provoking is unknowable. He may have calculated that the Torodbe, being scholars, would not fight. If so, the calculation was wrong. He died at the fall of Alkalawa in 1808, killed in the fighting, the teacher's former student dying in defense of the order the teacher had spent thirty years arguing was illegitimate. The Torodbe sources do not mourn him. They record his death as the fulfillment of a legal process that began with the *Kitab al-Farq* and ended with a siege. The student who had sat in the back row of the Shehu's classroom and taken notes had, in the end, become the evidence for the very argument he had once been taught.
