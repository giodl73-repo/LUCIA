# La Conquête — The Conquest
## Norman Kingdoms, Era 2 | 1066–1087 CE

---

The ravens came first.

They were always ahead of the men, following the smell of blood east across the ridge, down into the hollow where the English had made their last stand. William walked behind them in the grey of the fourteenth of October, 1066, and the ground was not ground. It was men. English men, most of them. Some of his own. His horse did not like it and he went on foot.

He had won. He knew this. The king was dead somewhere in the press, shot through the eye or hacked down at the end; the accounts would differ for a century. Harold Godwinson, the last Anglo-Saxon king of England, was dead on the hill called Senlac, and William, Duke of Normandy, was the last man standing with an army behind him.

England was still there.

This was the problem he had not thought through. You ride to a battle and you win or you lose, and losing is simple. You are dead or you run. But winning a battle and winning a country are not the same thing. He had conquered a duchy before: not like this. Normandy was his by inheritance, fought over and eventually settled, its lords familiar, its language his. England was a country of two million people who did not speak his language, who had their own bishops and their own law courts and their own understanding of who should rule them, and the man who had held all that together was lying in the field in front of him.

He stood there in the October light, and the ravens worked.

William was forty-three years old. He had been fighting since he was eight, when his father Robert died on pilgrimage to Jerusalem and left him the duchy and three guardians who were each murdered in succession, and the young duke learned early that survival was its own philosophy. He was broad and heavy now, with a neck that contemporaries called like a bull's, and a temper that men who served him described in careful, neutralizing language: *impetuous*, one would write; *severe*, another. What they meant was that he could be very frightening and that this was sometimes useful and sometimes not.

He understood legitimacy. He had had to. His mother was not his father's wife but a tanner's daughter from Falaise, and the Normans had called him *bastard* to his face before he was old enough to know what it meant and afterward when they thought he could not hear. The word had hardened into something else: a refusal to let his claim rest on what he was born and an insistence that it rest on what he built. He had come to England not as a conqueror merely but as a king asserting a right. Pope Alexander II had blessed his banner. The Church of Rome was behind him. Harold, he maintained, had sworn an oath on sacred relics to support William's claim and had broken it. He was here to collect a debt.

The debt was collected. Now he had to invent the payment.

He had perhaps eight thousand men, knights and infantry. England had perhaps two million people. The great northern earls of York, Northumbria, and Chester had not come south to fight him. They were watching from behind their walls and their winters, and they owed him nothing. The men who had governed England before he came were still alive, still on their lands, still sending riders south to see what the Norman would do next.

In the weeks that followed, he moved. He did not march on London immediately; he circled, burning the villages of Kent and Essex, and this too was calculation: he wanted the city to feel his approach before it felt his weight. A column of smoke visible for twenty miles was a sentence in a language every people in Europe could read. It said: we are here, we can reach you, and we have not reached you yet.

London submitted in December. The great men brought the crown to him at Berkhamsted: the Archbishop of York, the remaining earls, the representatives of the city. On Christmas Day, 1066, in the newly built church at Westminster, William was crowned King of England. The coronation shout of acclamation was in two languages. The Normans outside the building, hearing the English shout, thought it was a riot and set fire to the houses. William was crowned in a burning city, trembling, according to Orderic Vitalis, throughout the ceremony.

He was right to tremble. The crown was a beginning, not an end.

## The Castle Grammar

The castle came first. Always the castle.

He had brought the idea from Normandy: the motte-and-bailey, a timber tower on a raised earthen mound, ringed by a wooden palisade enclosing a yard where horses and supplies could be kept. It was not elegant. It was fast. A competent crew could raise one in two weeks. And it was visible. His men built them across England the way a language builds a sentence: one word at a time, until the sentence could be read.

The first fortification at London went up within the year: timber on earthwork, fast and functional, raised on the eastern edge of the old Roman city wall where it faced the river. The permanent White Tower would take more than a decade to complete, its Caen stone arriving by ship up the Thames. The choice of stone was itself a statement: here is something brought from Normandy, placed in the center of your city, which will outlast everyone who can see it. The Tower's first purpose was not defence. There was no enemy fleet in the Thames in 1067. Its first purpose was to dominate, to stand above the city's wooden rooflines and be unmistakable from every approach. Everyone who came to London by water or by road would see it before they saw anything else.

In the years that followed, the castles multiplied. Winchester. Pevensey. Dover. Hastings. Then, after the first northern rebellions, York, Lincoln, Durham. Then Chester, Carlisle, the Welsh marches. Each one placed by calculation: at a river crossing, at a coastal landing point, at the junction of roads, at the edge of hostile territory. By 1086, there were perhaps eighty significant fortifications in England, each one a Norman lord anchored in a landscape he was learning to read.

The English had understood their landscape in different terms. The thegn who held a village held it through a web of obligation and custom: to his lord above him, to the freemen and villeins below him, to the king who was the theoretical apex and the Church that was the institutional constant. This web was old and had its own grammar. The Norman did not tear it out. He sat inside it, and the castle sat above it, and gradually, not immediately, not without friction, but gradually, the web adjusted its load-bearing points.

The Norman lord learned to hold his English court alongside the inherited shire court. He sat where the English had sat, administered the pleas the English had administered, collected the taxes the English had owed. The writ, that specifically Anglo-Saxon administrative instrument, a letter from the king to the sheriff recording a command or a grant, carried by a rider who could cover forty miles in a day, the Normans kept. They wrote it in Latin now, not Old English, and the commands inside it served Norman ends, but the form itself was English. A tool that worked was kept. A tool that could be repurposed was repurposed.

By 1071, the surviving English earls of the old order were dead, exiled, or reduced. Edwin of Mercia was dead. Morcar was in prison. Edgar Ætheling, the last of the Cerdicine line, had submitted. The great English church sees were filling with Normans: Walkelin at Winchester, Thomas at York, and most important, the Italian lawyer-turned-monk who arrived at Canterbury in 1070 and became, within a decade, the most important administrator in England after the king himself.

And somewhere in Canterbury in those same years, English embroiderers were at work on a strip of linen seventy meters long. Odo of Bayeux, William's half-brother and Earl of Kent, had commissioned a record of the Conquest. The embroiderers were English; the craft tradition was theirs. The story they were stitching was not. It was the Norman story: Harold the oath-breaker, William the rightful king, the battle at Hastings rendered in wool thread against cream linen in the colors of Normandy's own aesthetic. They stitched it carefully, with the skill their tradition gave them, and they left traces of themselves in it: the animals in the lower border who behave and misbehave independent of the main narrative; the faces of the English dead rendered with the same care as the Norman victors; a woman named Ælfgyva, inserted without explanation into the Norman story, her name written in Latin above her, her context unrecorded. The English hands are in the work. What they thought of it, stitching the story of their own country's conquest, is not recorded anywhere.

## Lanfranc at Canterbury

Lanfranc of Bec was sixty-five years old when William insisted he become Archbishop of Canterbury. He did not want the appointment. He wanted his monastery. He had already refused to become Archbishop of Rouen, and he refused William's first approaches for Canterbury as well, on the grounds that he was too old and the task too large. William was not asking.

Lanfranc arrived at Canterbury in 1070 and found a cathedral chapter in disarray: the old English monks at odds with each other, the records incomplete, the properties disputed, the theological training thin. He also found a library. The Anglo-Saxon church had been, in its own way, a center of learning. Bede's tradition, the illuminated manuscripts, the careful copying that had gone on in English scriptoria since the seventh century. Lanfranc looked at the library and understood that the church he was inheriting had been doing something, and that what it had been doing could be used.

He reorganized the scriptorium. He brought monks from Bec, trained in the continental school tradition, to work alongside the Canterbury monks. He commissioned new copies of the theological texts the English had not had, the works of Anselm, the canon law collections that were reshaping the continental Church. He kept the English texts and kept the English monks copying them, because the English liturgical tradition was not his to discard. It was the tradition his cathedral had been built to serve. He added to it rather than replacing it, which was also, not coincidentally, the most effective way to maintain the goodwill of an English monastic community that could have been much more difficult.

The new books arrived on Caen parchment and Canterbury parchment alike. The scriptorium produced writs, charters, records of property, records of pleas. Lanfranc wrote to William in Normandy and to Rome, managing the two allegiances simultaneously, keeping England inside both orbits without colliding them. He wrote to his former pupil Anselm, who would eventually succeed him. He wrote to the English bishops he had replaced, when they were still alive, with a firmness that was not unkind.

What Lanfranc built at Canterbury was the literate infrastructure of the Norman state. Every sheriff's writ, every royal grant, every plea in the king's court required a clerk who could write and read Latin. Those clerks came from the Church. Lanfranc trained them, organized them, and directed them outward into the kingdom. The Church was not a spiritual comfort to the Norman conquest. It was its central administrative mechanism.

When William was in Normandy, which was frequently, Lanfranc governed England. He convened councils, settled disputes, quelled minor rebellions with a quiet authority that had nothing to do with military force. He was, in the fullest sense, a co-king. William knew this and trusted him. When William's half-brother Odo overreached in 1082, it was Lanfranc who managed the aftermath of the arrest, who kept the Church's institutional structures from fracturing along with the family's. The archbishop was the structural guarantee of the kingdom's continuity.

## The Harrying of the North

In the autumn of 1069, the north rose.

A Danish fleet had entered the Humber. Sweyn Estridsson of Denmark had a claim to the English throne; more than one king of Denmark had sat on it in the previous century. His fleet was large enough that the northern English lords, who had never quite accepted Norman authority, saw their moment. York fell to the combined English-Danish force. The Norman garrison was killed.

William moved north in the winter.

Orderic Vitalis was a monk at Saint-Évroul in Normandy. He was born in England in 1075, the son of a Norman father and an English mother, and he wrote his *Historia Ecclesiastica* over decades, incorporating accounts from men who had been present. He was careful, he said, to have avoided being present himself in that winter. The phrase is not logistical. He does not mean he could not travel north. He means he did not want to see it. But he had heard the survivors relate it, and he wrote down what they said.

The survivors said: William sent his men through the villages of Yorkshire and the lands beyond, burning the stores of food, the farm equipment, the seed grain. The animals were killed or driven off. The mills were burned. The houses were burned. The people who remained after the fighting had no food source and no means of producing food, and the winter of 1069–70 was severe. Modern estimates, drawn from reading Orderic's account alongside the Domesday waste entries, have put the dead in the tens of thousands. Orderic himself does not count. He says only that the roads were strewn with the starved, that the survivors could not bury the dead. What is agreed: the roads of Yorkshire were, the following spring, strewn with the starved. Travelers found no houses standing, no human presence for miles. The land took a generation to recover. Some manors in the Domesday survey of 1086, seventeen years later, were still recorded as *waste*: the Domesday term for land that produced no revenue and sustained no population.

William knew what he was doing. He wanted the north to understand that rebellion was more expensive than submission, not as an abstraction but as a physical fact. The calculation was cold and it was deliberate. Orderic, who admired William, recorded that the king expressed remorse on his deathbed, that he had "massacred by slow famine" the innocent with the guilty, and that this weighed on him at the end. Whether William actually said this, or whether Orderic constructed a deathbed that fit the conventions of medieval penitential writing, is not fully knowable.

What is knowable: the north did not rise again in William's reign.

The Danish fleet withdrew. The northern lords submitted. The Norman castles went up in York, Durham, Carlisle. The land that had been burned was parceled out to Norman lords who were expected to build it back, which they did, slowly, at the cost of the English villagers who had survived and who now held their land under different lords and different terms. The survivors rebuilt. They kept their place names, Rievaulx, Pickering, Helmsley, and they kept their customs in the parts that the Norman lords had no interest in controlling. And they were watched from above by timber towers on earthen mounds, which eventually became stone towers on stone bases, which became the castles of the English north.

## The Domesday Survey

Christmas 1085. William held his court at Gloucester. He had been in England for several weeks after a long absence in Normandy, and he was fifty-seven years old and heavy in a way that was no longer merely imposing. His son Robert had revolted and been reconciled. His half-brother Odo was in prison in Normandy. The kingdom was stable, and William wanted to know what he owned.

He called it, according to the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, a "deep speech" with his council: a consultation, a thinking through. He sent men across England in the following year, commissioners who rode into each county, convened the local men, and asked questions. The questions were the same everywhere. Who held this manor before the Conquest? Who holds it now? How many villagers? How many smallholders? How many slaves? How many hides of land? What is it worth? What was it worth in King Edward's time?

The answers came back to Winchester, where scribes organized them by county, then by the tenant-in-chief who held the land from the king, then by the subtenants who held from the tenant-in-chief. The result was a survey of virtually every landholding in England, organized in a single coherent system that no equivalent document in Western Europe attempted before or would attempt again for centuries.

In a village in Essex, a man named Æthelric appeared before the commissioners. He held sixteen acres in the village of Writtle, a modest holding that had been in his family before the Conquest. He held it now, the record would say, of the sheriff. Not of the lord who had held Writtle before, because that lord was gone. Not by right of inheritance acknowledged in writing, because he had no document that the Norman commissioners would recognize. He held it at the sheriff's convenience, which meant he held it because it suited someone with power to allow him to hold it, and could lose it when it ceased to suit.

He answered the questions. The commissioner asked him in French, probably, or the interpreter translated; Æthelric answered in English. The clerk wrote down what the interpreter said, in Latin, in the careful hand that would be reproduced in the Winchester manuscript. The record of Writtle in Domesday does not name Æthelric. It does not name the interpreter. It names the tenant-in-chief, the subtenant, and the assessment in hides. Æthelric's testimony is present in the document; Æthelric himself has been translated out of it.

He knew what the record said because someone would have read it back to him in English, probably, or told him its content. He understood that this document now governed his claim to his sixteen acres more definitively than his family's memory of holding them, more definitively than his neighbors' knowledge of the boundary markers, more definitively than the unwritten custom that had organized his father's tenure. A document he could not read, in a language he did not speak, produced by a system he had not chosen, was now the authoritative account of his place in the world.

This was the Domesday Book's full achievement and its full weight. Nothing like it existed in England before, and nothing like it would appear in France or the Holy Roman Empire in William's lifetime. It was also a replacement: of customary memory by documentary record, of English categories by Norman-Latin categories, of the knowledge that lived in the village by the knowledge that lived in Winchester. The village's knowledge was necessary to produce it; the commissioners could not have assessed anything without the testimony of people who knew the land. But the knowledge was organized and preserved in a form that served the crown, not the village. The village knew what it held. The document said what it held.

William had wanted to know what he owned. He found out. The book was presented to him, according to later sources, at Salisbury, where he also extracted a sweeping oath of loyalty from every significant landowner in England: not just the great lords, but the lords of the lords. Every man who held land of any consequence swore directly to the king. This was unusual. In the standard feudal arrangement, a man owed loyalty to his immediate lord, who owed it to his lord, and so upward. William wanted the chain shorter. He wanted every significant landowner in England to owe him personally, which meant that no great lord could raise a rebellion without the king knowing in advance exactly who would follow, and on whose land they would assemble, and what those lands were worth.

The Domesday Book and the Salisbury Oath were the same instrument: the king making himself legible and making his kingdom legible to him. He could now see England whole. He had built himself a pair of eyes.

## The Body at Caen

William died in September 1087 at the priory of Saint-Gervais, outside Rouen.

He had been raiding into the Vexin, a border territory between Normandy and France, perpetually contested, and his horse had stumbled on debris inside the burning town of Mantes. William was thrown forward against the saddle pommel. The internal injuries were severe. He was taken to Rouen and then to the priory, where he lingered for several weeks and died on the ninth of September at dawn, when the bells of the city began to ring for prime.

Orderic Vitalis records a deathbed speech. It is long and careful and covers William's life as William, or Orderic, understood it. He speaks of his father's death and his early survival. He speaks of Hastings. He speaks of what he did in England: the lands he took from the English and gave to his followers, the castles he built, the men he imprisoned. He says he imposed harsh laws, and that he is not certain he acted rightly. He speaks of his sons and distributes the kingdom between them: Normandy to Robert Curthose, who had revolted against him but whom he could not bring himself to disinherit; England to William Rufus, his second son, who had stayed loyal; to Henry, the youngest, five thousand pounds of silver from the treasury and the assurance that he would have lands enough in time.

Medieval deathbed speeches are always reconstructions. What Orderic gives us is not a transcript but a characterization: William as Orderic understood him, filtered through the conventions of penitential narrative, organized by what Orderic thought a king ought to say when he was dying. Whether William said these words, or words like them, or whether Orderic built the speech from the facts of William's reign and the traditions of his genre, cannot be settled. What is certain is that Orderic wrote it, and that it rings true in its particulars, in the specific things it says William did and expressed uncertainty about, in a way that suggests access to something real.

After the speech, William died at dawn, and the men who had been tending him scattered immediately to secure their own interests. The great lords rode for their estates. The attendants took what they could carry. The king's body lay in the priory church without proper care for most of the day.

They brought him to Caen for burial at his own abbey, the Abbaye aux Hommes, the Abbey of Saint-Étienne, which he had founded as part of his penance for marrying his cousin Matilda without papal dispensation. The penance was itself one of the many arrangements he had made with the Church, combining genuine piety with political calculation so thoroughly that it was not possible then, and is not possible now, to separate them.

The September heat had done what September heat does. When they tried to lower the king into the stone coffin, the body had swollen and it did not fit. The attending clergy and dignitaries pressed. The coffin was too small, or the king was too large, or both, and the pressing ruptured the body, and the church at Caen filled with the smell of it.

The funeral was conducted anyway. The king was interred. The inscription carved over his grave said that he had ruled England and restored it to its proper lord, a characterization of the Conquest that reflected Norman legal theory, which held that Harold had been a usurper and William's kingship a rightful restoration. Whether the English agreed with this theory, and in what numbers, and how their opinions changed over the twenty years of William's reign, is not fully recorded. The Domesday Book does not ask that question.

What remained at his death: the Domesday Book, complete in Winchester. The great castles, stone now in the major centers, timber still in the smaller stations but permanent enough. The network of Norman lords holding English land, tied to each other and to the king by the feudal hierarchy the Conquest had imposed. The Church, reorganized, Latin-literate, administratively capable. The common law, the system of royal courts that would develop in the following century into English common law, with its foundations in the king's right to adjudicate disputes across the kingdom. The writ, now standard, now the administrative form of royal command, carrying Norman decisions in Anglo-Saxon format through the country at the speed of a horse.

Orderic Vitalis, writing forty years later, thought William was a great king who did terrible things. He did not put it in those terms; the moral vocabulary was different. But that is what his account adds up to: a man of exceptional capacity, genuine religious conviction, savage political will, and a legacy so deeply embedded in English institutions that it could not be separated from England itself. The Normans built it that way. They built it to be permanent, because they were always a small group in a large country, and permanence was what they had instead of numbers.

England was reorganized from the ground up in twenty years, by a man who had stood on a field of ravens and understood that the battle was the easy part.

The machine he built outlasted him by centuries, outlasted the Norman name, outlasted the distinction between Norman and English that was the conquest's premise. By 1200 the barons of England called themselves English and the lords of Normandy called themselves French, and the castle and the writ and the survey were simply how England worked.

He did not know this would happen. He had wanted to know what he owned, and to secure it, and to pass it to his sons. He got two of those three things. The kingdom did not divide as he intended; it reunited, under different men, into something he had not intended to build.

The book remained. In Winchester, in its two volumes, it stayed. Clerks consulted it for generations, for centuries, because the land records it contained were legally operative long after the men who gave testimony were dead. The English called it the Domesday Book, the Book of Judgment, because its word was final and there was no appeal. William had not given it that name. He had called it the survey, *descriptio*, a describing of the kingdom.

He described it, and the description outlived the man, as he had perhaps known it would, as he perhaps intended.

The ravens were still working the fields of Yorkshire, seventeen years after Hastings, in the places the Domesday record called *waste*.
