# The Principate — The Age of Augustus

The marble was new. That was the point.

Augustus, who had been Octavian, who had been the boy who inherited a name and used it to end a world, stood in his new Forum and looked at what he had built. The temple of Mars Ultor, Mars the Avenger, rose at the far end of the enclosure, its columns Greek marble from the quarries of Luna, white and sharp-edged in the Roman sun, the first time the city had dressed itself so thoroughly in stone that was not tufa, not terracotta, not the dark volcanic rock of the Alban hills, but imported marble that caught the light and held it. Augustus found Roma a city of brick, the tradition says, and left it a city of marble. The tradition exaggerated, as traditions do, but the direction was right. The brick was the Republic. The marble was what came after.

He was not a king. This was the essential fiction, and every stone in the Forum Augustum was shaped by it. The Senate still met. The consuls still served their year. The tribunes still stood in the Forum Romanum, the old Forum between the hills, and said veto, though the things that mattered were no longer decided there. The assemblies still voted, though the outcomes were known before the voting began. Every form of the res publica was maintained because Augustus understood what Caesar had not: that the Romani could accept one-man rule if one-man rule looked like the Republic. The fiction was not a deception. It was a settlement, and both parties, the princeps and the Senate, sustained it because both needed it. Augustus needed the Senate's legitimacy. The Senate needed Augustus's order. The fiction was the knot that bound them.

The Forum Augustum was designed as an argument in stone. The niches along the colonnades held statues of the summi viri, the greatest men of Roman history, each with an inscription listing his achievements. On one side, the descendants of Aeneas and the Julian line. On the other, the great men of the Republic: Scipio Africanus, Fabius Maximus, the men whose names the previous chapters of this story have carried. The argument was visible: Augustus stood at the culmination of both lines, the mythic and the historical, the Trojan ancestor and the Republican hero merged in a single figure who was neither king nor consul but something the tradition had not yet named. The inscriptions told the visitor what Augustus wanted them to know: that the Roman past led here, to this Forum, to this temple, to this man.

The smell of the new marble was sharp and slightly sweet, the dust of cut stone mixing with the incense from the temple and the olive oil that was rubbed into the stone surfaces to seal them. The sound of the Forum was different from the old Republic's acoustic bowl. The colonnades directed and contained the voice. A man speaking in the temple portico was heard by the people in the court below, and the hearing was managed, architectural, deliberate. Augustus did not shout in the Forum the way the tribunes had shouted. He spoke in the Senate. He spoke through edicts. He spoke through buildings that said what he wanted said in a voice louder than any human throat: that the disorder was over, that the civil wars were finished, that the res publica was restored, and that the man who had restored it was here, in marble, and the marble would not argue.

The Pax Romana began. The roads were built. The law was codified. The legions held the frontiers. For two centuries the interior of the empire knew a stability the Mediterranean had not experienced before and would not experience again for a very long time. And the question that hung over all of it, the question the marble could not answer, was whether what Augustus had built was the Republic saved or the Republic finished.
