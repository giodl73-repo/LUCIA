# Panel Review — R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe

**Chapter:** Ghana Empire — Era 2: The Age of Gold (opening.md)
**Voice:** Chinua Achebe — Cultural voice, dignity, authenticity, ownership
**Stage:** PANEL-1

---

The opening passes the primary test. The two gestures of recognition — dust poured on the head, hands clapped — are presented as equal in meaning and origin. Neither is "explained" as custom; both are understood from inside as the same act of acknowledgment. This is the kind of detail that tells me the writer has grasped something about how coexistence actually works in a culture like this: not through tolerance as an abstract value, but through shared recognition of shared order.

The merchant's outsider position is handled correctly. He cannot enter the royal city. He glimpses the Ghana from a distance. The chapter does not apologize for this — it uses it: the distance is what produces the longing that pulls the reader toward the next section. What he cannot enter is what we want to understand.

**The question I'd push on:** The full chapter must give the Soninke their own voice in the royal city. The opening establishes the merchant as a viewpoint character because discovery requires someone who is discovering. But the chapter cannot remain in that position. The sacred grove, the Ghana's covenant with the land, the priests and their rites — these things belong to the Soninke, and they must be told from inside their own framework, not observed from outside it. The opening's merchant perspective is valid as an entry point. The chapter's Soninke sections must not be observational; they must be inhabited.

The Bida legend, when it comes, should not be explained. It should be told in the Soninke voice, as the Soninke told it: because it is true in the way things are true when a people have lived by them for generations.
