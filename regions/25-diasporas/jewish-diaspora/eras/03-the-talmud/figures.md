# Figures — The Portable Homeland

## Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai

He chose the text over the city. This is the fact that defines him, and the tradition has argued about it for two thousand years.

When Jerusalem was besieged, others fought. The zealots held the walls and burned the grain stores and prepared to die rather than surrender. Yochanan was old, a student of Hillel, and he saw that the city would fall and that dying inside it was not the same as saving what the city held. He was smuggled out in a coffin. The tradition records this detail without embarrassment, because the coffin is the point: to leave Jerusalem alive, Yochanan had to pass through the gate as a dead man. He died to the city so that the tradition could live beyond it.

What he asked Vespasian for was not Jerusalem, not the Temple, not the priesthood. He asked for Yavneh and its sages. The request has been debated ever since. Was it strategic brilliance, asking for the one thing that would ensure survival? Was it a failure of imagination, the old scholar unable to conceive of asking for more? The Talmud itself preserves the debate. Rabbi Yosef, generations later, applied the verse: God "turns the wise backward and makes their knowledge foolish." Yochanan should have asked for Jerusalem. But he did not, and what he asked for instead was the seed from which rabbinic Judaism grew, and the fact that we are still arguing about whether he was right is itself proof that his request was granted.

He died at Yavneh. The tradition says that on his deathbed he wept. His students asked why, and he said: Two paths are before me, one to Gan Eden and one to Gehinnom, and I do not know on which I will be led. Even the man who saved the tradition did not know if he had done enough. The tradition records his uncertainty, because uncertainty, in this tradition, is not weakness. It is the honest condition of a human being who has done what he could and cannot know whether it was sufficient.

## Rabbi Yehudah ha-Nasi

They called him simply "Rabbi," as if there were only one. He was wealthy, politically powerful, and deeply learned, a combination that the tradition rarely produced in a single person. He was nasi, the president of the Sanhedrin, the highest Jewish legal authority in the Land of Israel, and his friendship with the Roman administration gave the Jewish community a degree of autonomy that other subject peoples did not enjoy.

His great work was the Mishnah, completed around the year 200, and the decision to compile it was itself a legal revolution. The Oral Torah was oral for a reason: writing it down would fix what was meant to remain fluid. Yehudah ha-Nasi justified the revolution on emergency grounds. The chain of transmission was fraying. The scholars were scattered. The memory was at risk. Better to violate the prohibition against writing the oral law than to lose the law itself. This reasoning became a principle in its own right: when the Torah is in danger of being forgotten, extraordinary measures are permitted. The man who wrote down the Oral Torah did so by applying the oral tradition's own logic to the crisis of its survival.

The Mishnah he produced is compressed to the point of enigma. It states laws and omits reasoning. It records disputes and does not always resolve them. It organizes an immense body of tradition into six orders and sixty-three tractates, and the organization is itself an argument about what matters and in what order. Zeraim first, because the land and its produce are the foundation. Tohorot last, because purity is the most complex and the most contingent on the Temple that no longer stands. The Mishnah is not a code to be applied. It is a framework to be argued with, and the next three centuries of argument are the proof that the framework did what it was designed to do.

## Rav (Abba Arikha)

He brought the Mishnah to Babylon. He was tall, the sources say, and his learning was so great that the tradition gave him the simplest and most imposing of titles: Rav. The teacher. As if the word needed no other name attached.

He came from the Land of Israel around 219 of the common era and founded the academy at Sura, in southern Mesopotamia, in a community that had been Jewish for seven hundred years. The Babylonian Jews had their own traditions, their own customs, their own ways of reading the Torah. Rav brought the Mishnah into this world, and the meeting between the Land of Israel's compiled text and Babylon's independent tradition is the meeting that produced the Babylonian Talmud.

His debates with Shmuel fill tractate after tractate. In ritual matters, the law follows Rav. In civil matters, the law follows Shmuel. The tradition did not need to choose one over the other. It held both, drew a boundary, and let each voice carry authority in its own domain. Rav died around 247. The academy at Sura continued for eight centuries after him. The arguments he began are still being studied, and the title the tradition gave him is still the title by which he is known, because in a tradition that produces scholars by the thousand, one man was simply the teacher.

## Shmuel

He led the academy at Nehardea and later the tradition moved to Pumbedita, and his contribution to Jewish civilization was a sentence of six words: dina d'malkhuta dina. The law of the kingdom is the law.

This was not submission. It was the architectural principle that made diaspora life possible. Shmuel drew a line: in matters of civil governance, taxation, and public order, the law of the host country is binding. In matters of ritual, family law, internal community governance, Shabbat, kashrut, and the calendar, the Torah's law governs. The two legal systems do not compete because the boundary is drawn clearly. A Jew living in Sasanian Babylon could be a loyal subject of the Sasanian king and a faithful member of the Jewish community simultaneously, because the obligations did not conflict. Shmuel made this possible with a principle, and the principle made the diaspora possible as a legal reality, not merely an accident of history.

He was also an astronomer. The Talmud records his claim that he knew the paths of the stars as well as he knew the streets of Nehardea. The tradition did not see this as a departure from Torah study. Understanding the movements of the heavens was necessary for calculating the calendar, and the calendar was the scaffolding of Jewish time. Shmuel's astronomy served the same project as his jurisprudence: building the infrastructure of a civilization that had to function without territory.

## Beruriah

She appears in the Talmud as a voice, not as a biography. The tradition records her arguments, her interpretations, her corrections of other scholars, and these records are themselves exceptional, because the Talmud names very few women as participants in halakhic debate.

In Tractate Berakhot, when Rabbi Meir prays for the death of neighbors who torment him, Beruriah reads the verse more carefully: let sins cease, not sinners. The distinction is not merely semantic. It is a legal and moral argument about the nature of wrongdoing and the possibility of repentance, and the Talmud records it because the argument was sound. In the beth midrash, a sound argument carries its own authority regardless of who speaks it.

Later traditions about Beruriah are more complicated and less reliable, and the chapter does not pursue them, because the Talmud itself presents her as what she was in the study house: a scholar whose reading of the text was sharp enough to correct her husband, and whose correction was preserved because the tradition's commitment to recording the best argument overrode its own social conventions about who was expected to produce one.
