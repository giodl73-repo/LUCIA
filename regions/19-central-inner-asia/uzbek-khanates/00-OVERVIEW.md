# Uzbek Khanates — The Settled Heirs of the Steppe

We are the Uzbeks — and our identity was forged at the intersection of the Mongol inheritance and the sedentary civilization of the Central Asian oasis cities. We trace our name and our political lineage to Öz Beg Khan (Khan Uzbek) of the Golden Horde, who ruled in the early fourteenth century and under whom the Kipchak steppe definitively converted to Islam. The Shaybanid dynasty — the Uzbek ruling house that expelled Babur from Samarkand in 1500 and dominated Transoxiana until the seventeenth century — was of direct Chinggisid lineage, descendants of Chinggis Khan's eldest son Jochi. We are the Mongol inheritance in Muslim, Persianate, sedentary form.

The cities we inherited — Samarkand, Bukhara, Khiva, Kokand — were already ancient when the Uzbeks arrived. They had been Zoroastrian, then Buddhist, then Nestorian Christian, then Islamic; they had been administered by the Achaemenids, the Greco-Bactrians, the Kushan, the Sassanids, the Arab caliphate, the Samanids, the Qarakhanids, the Mongols, and the Timurids before we arrived. We did not build these cities; we became their rulers and, eventually, their people. The process by which nomadic steppe people become the cultural custodians of sedentary civilization is one of the most important dynamics in Central Asian history, and the Uzbeks are its most complete expression. By the time the Russians arrived in the nineteenth century, the Uzbek khanates of Bukhara, Khiva, and Kokand were thoroughly sedentary Muslim polities whose ruling dynasties still claimed Chinggisid descent as legitimating genealogy while living in palaces and patronizing Islamic scholarship.

## What Defined Them

**The Chinggisid legitimacy problem.** The Uzbek rulers faced a recurring legitimacy challenge: they were Turkic-speaking Muslim rulers of ancient Persian-Islamic cities, and their claim to authority rested on descent from Chinggis Khan — a pre-Islamic, steppe-nomadic identity that seemed at odds with their actual role as patrons of Islamic scholarship and Persian literary culture. They resolved this through genealogical emphasis: the Shaybanid khans maintained elaborate Chinggisid genealogies while presenting themselves publicly as Muslim rulers and patrons of Islamic law. The dual identity — Mongol descent, Islamic rule — was the Uzbek governing formula.

**Bukhara as the center of Islamic learning.** Bukhara's madrasa network was one of the densest concentrations of Islamic scholarship in the world: students from across Central Asia, India, and the Ottoman Empire came to study fiqh, theology, and the Islamic sciences in the city that had been a center of learning since the Samanid period (9th–10th century). The Mir-i-Arab Madrasa, the Kalon Mosque and minaret, and the labyrinthine bazaars made Bukhara simultaneously a physical and intellectual architecture of Islamic civilization. The Uzbek emirs patronized this scholarship not from personal piety alone but because control of the scholarly establishment was control of the city's moral authority.

**The Persianate cultural medium.** Despite being Türkic speakers, the Uzbek ruling class governed, patronized, and expressed its cultural identity through Persian: Persian was the language of administration, poetry, and high culture in the oasis cities, and any ruler who aspired to be taken seriously as a Muslim monarch needed to demonstrate competence in the Persian literary tradition. The result was the same synthesis that characterized all the post-Mongol successor states from Mughal India to Ottoman Istanbul: Türkic military identity married to Persian cultural form, legitimated by Islamic religion and Chinggisid genealogy.

**The Russian encounter.** The Russian conquest of the Central Asian khanates in the 1860s-1870s was one of the largest territorial acquisitions of the nineteenth century, driven by strategic competition with Britain (the Great Game), economic interest in cotton cultivation, and the ideological conviction that "civilizing" the khanates was Russia's destiny. The Emirate of Bukhara survived as a formal protectorate until 1920, when the Red Army ended it. The Soviet national delimitation of 1924 created the Uzbek Soviet Socialist Republic from territories that had never been governed as a single unit — a political geography that became the basis for the independent Republic of Uzbekistan in 1991.

## The Chapters

**1 — Muhammad Shaybani Enters Samarkand:** Babur has fled. The Shaybanid army occupies the city that Timur built. The blue domes are intact. The bazaars are intact. The libraries are intact. The new rulers claim the Timurid inheritance — not to preserve it unchanged, but to become its next expression. The question is whether the Uzbek princes can be what the Timurid princes were: patrons, scholars, builders. Some of them will be.

**2 — The Mir-i-Arab Madrasa:** The endowment document for the new madrasa specifies the number of students, the curriculum, the living stipends, the teaching staff. Bukhara already has dozens of madrasas; this one is larger and better endowed than most. The emir who built it is performing both piety and political calculation: the scholars he educates will fill the administrative positions of his state, preach his legitimacy in the mosques, and validate his rule as an Islamic monarchy. The genealogy is Mongol; the governance is Islamic.

**3 — The Silk Road Contracts:** The great overland trade routes that once made the oasis cities rich are failing. The Portuguese have opened the sea route around Africa; the oceanic trade has redirected the luxury goods traffic away from the overland Silk Road. Bukhara still trades, but it is increasingly trading within the Central Asian region rather than as a node in a continental network. The cities will survive; the trade volumes will decline; and when the Russians arrive with their ideas about cotton monoculture, the oasis economy will be restructured completely.

**4 — Bukhara's Last Emir:** Alim Khan, the last Emir of Bukhara, watches the Red Army approach the city in 1920. He has modernizers in his court who argued for reform; he has conservatives who argued for resistance. He chose neither consistently. He flees to Afghanistan. The Bukharan People's Soviet Republic is proclaimed. The ancient city's political tradition — continuous in some form since the Achaemenid period — ends. The madrasas close. The libraries are redistributed.

**5 — The Cotton Republic:** Soviet planners have decided that Uzbekistan will grow cotton for the Soviet textile industry. The Amu Darya and Syr Darya are diverted for irrigation. The Aral Sea — the great inland sea that once sustained the region's ecology — begins to dry. By the time the Soviet Union collapses in 1991 and the Republic of Uzbekistan declares independence, the Aral Sea is a fraction of its former size, and the Uzbek people inherit both the ancient cities of Samarkand and Bukhara and the ecological catastrophe of the cotton program.
