# The Great Hajj

Bala Faseke Kouyate, who carried the name of Sundiata's own jeli, stood before the mud-brick gate of Niani in the early heat and sang the names.

He sang Sundiata, the Lion, the hungering lion, who crawled and then walked and then ran and then conquered. He sang Wali, who had made the pilgrimage before his father's body was cold. He sang Khalifa, who shot arrows into the crowd and did not last. He sang Abu Bakr, who went to sea and did not return. He sang those who held the Manden together and those who nearly lost it, because a jeli does not choose which ancestors to remember. A jeli remembers them all. And then Bala Faseke sang Musa, son of Faga Leye, ninth Mansa of the Manden Kurufaba, who stood now in the morning light at the head of a column that stretched from the palace gate to the river and beyond where any eye could follow.

It was the dry season, and Niani smelled of dust and shea butter and woodsmoke. The harmattan had come and mostly gone, leaving a haze over the Niger that the sun was burning through. Musa stood still while the jeli sang. He was tall. He wore white, the cloth of a pilgrim, though the gold was already loaded and the gold would speak louder than cloth. Around him, the court waited. Behind the court, the column. Behind the column, the city had emptied itself to watch.

Five hundred jon carried gold. The jon were not chattel — within the Kurukan Fuga's framework, they held recognized roles, rights, and social identity among the Manden's three orders. The Kurukan Fuga named the categories — horon, nyamakalaw, jon — and it named what each owed. Each bore a staff of the metal weighing six mithqals, and behind them came a hundred camels loaded with what the chroniclers say was three hundred pounds of gold dust in saddlebags that sagged with the weight. There was more. Attendants and soldiers and cooks and the men who carried the tents and the women who carried the food. No one who was there could agree on the number. Eight thousand, some said. Sixty thousand, others said. What they agreed on was that they had never seen anything like it.

Musa was going to Mecca.

He was going because a man who can make the journey and does not has left something unfinished before God. The camels grunted under their loads while the dust rose ankle-high around the column. But Musa was also going because he was mansa, and a mansa does not travel like other men. The Kurukan Fuga made a mansa through generosity — a king who does not give is not a king. A king who gives so that his name reaches the corners of the earth — that is a king whose children's children will be named in the songs of the jelilu.

So the gold was not extravagance. The gold was the point.

Musa's senior wife, Inari Kunate, rode at the head of her own column, the indigo of her wrapping cloth dark against the morning haze, five hundred women of her household behind her. Scholars from Timbuktu and Djenne joined with their books, because Musa intended to return with men who could build in stone and teach in Arabic and help him reshape the Manden.

The route was north. North through the savanna, where the baobabs thinned and the grass shortened and the air dried until the skin cracked. North to Walata, the gateway of the Sahara. North across sand where water was carried or water was death. Then east to Cairo, and east again to Mecca.

But that was months away. Today there was the gate, and the jeli, and the morning.

Bala Faseke finished the genealogy and began the praise song. His balafon held the low notes while his voice climbed. The crowd knew the words — the jelilu had been singing them since Sundiata's time. They belonged to the horon who stood near the mansa, to the nyamakalaw who made the craft of the empire, to the children who had climbed the compound walls to see.

The drums began. First the dundun, which speaks in the tonal language of the Mande. Then the djembe, which sets the body moving. The sound built. The horses stirred. The camels, loaded and patient, turned their heads north as if they already knew.

Musa raised his hand, and the caravan began to move.

It would take them the better part of a year. They would cross the Sahara, and the world beyond the sand would learn what the jelilu had always known. But the jelilu did not need the world to confirm this. They had been singing it since Sundiata's time, and they would sing it seven hundred years more, long after the chroniclers who tried to write it down had themselves become part of the song.

All of that was coming. But on this morning in Niani, in the dry season of the year 724 of the Hijra, there was only the gate, the jeli, the gold, the drums, and the long column moving north, raising dust that hung in the haze and did not settle for hours after the last of them had passed.
