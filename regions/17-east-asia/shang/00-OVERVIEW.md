# Shang — The Oracle and the Ancestor

We are the Shang — the people of the middle — and our world is organized around the living and the dead. The dead are not gone. Our Di, our Lord on High, and the long chain of royal ancestors who have joined him are active presences in our world: they send harvest and drought, victory and defeat, disease and health, according to their pleasure. Our king, the Son of Heaven, communicates with them through the oracle: the hot bronze stylus touches the shoulder blade of an ox or the plastron of a turtle, and the crack that forms is the ancestor's answer. The diviners read the cracks; the scribes inscribe the question and the answer; the shell or bone is stored. This is the origin of Chinese writing.

We built our cities at the bend of the Yellow River, on the loess plain where the soil is deep and the flood is both gift and danger. Our capital Anyang — called "the Great Settlement Shang" — was a city of palaces and workshops: the royal compound with its stamped-earth foundations, the bronze foundries where craftsmen poured the alloy into clay molds, the royal tombs where kings were buried with chariots, jade, bronze vessels, and the humans who would serve them in the afterworld. We are the ancestors of the Chinese tradition in the most literal sense: the oracle bones they dug up in Anyang in the late nineteenth century were our words.

## What Defined Them

**The ancestor cult.** The Shang universe was animated by the royal ancestors — Di, the High God, and the line of deceased Shang kings who had become powerful spirits. Ancestor spirits required regular sacrifice: grain, wine, animals, and sometimes humans. In return they provided protection, counsel (through the oracle), and the blessings of harvest and victory. The king's primary function was to maintain this relationship. Governance was ancestor management.

**Oracle bone divination.** The systematic use of oracle bones — ox scapulae and turtle plastrons — for royal divination is the defining practice of the Shang state. Tens of thousands of inscribed oracle bones have been recovered from Anyang, recording the questions the king asked his ancestors (Will this year's harvest be good? Should we attack the Fang people? Is the queen's pregnancy auspicious?) and the answers the diviners read. These inscriptions are the oldest body of readable Chinese writing and the primary source for our knowledge of the Shang.

**Bronze as sacred technology.** The Shang bronze ritual vessels — the ding cauldrons for cooking sacrificial animals, the gui bowls for grain offerings, the jue and jia cups for wine libations — were among the most technically sophisticated metal objects made anywhere in the world at this time. They were not utensils; they were the material bodies through which the ancestor relationship was performed. The taotie (monster mask) that decorates so many Shang bronzes is not purely decorative; it is an apotropaic image whose full meaning we have not recovered.

**The royal chariot.** The Shang elite used chariots — a technology that appears in the Shang heartland around 1200 BCE and may have arrived via Central Asian intermediaries. The chariot was simultaneously a military technology and a symbol of elite status: the royal tombs at Anyang contain chariot pits with horses and drivers, all sacrificed to accompany the king. The Shang military conducted raids against surrounding peoples for slaves, animals, and the prestige that fueled the sacrificial cycle.

**Writing as ritual.** The Chinese script that begins with the oracle bone inscriptions was not invented for commerce or administration (as in Mesopotamia) but for divination. The earliest Chinese writing records the king's questions to his ancestors and the ancestors' answers. This ritual origin shaped the Chinese script's subsequent development: a logographic system in which the character is both sound and meaning, memorized through sustained study, and used as the currency of elite legitimacy across three millennia.

## The Chapters

**1 — Pre-Dynastic:** The traditional account says Tang the Successful overthrew the Xia. The archaeologists have found Erlitou, which may be the Xia capital. The line between myth and history is the line the oracle bone scribes drew, and they drew it in their favor.

**2 — Middle Shang:** The bronze foundry at Zhengzhou is working around the clock. The clay molds are prepared with precision. The alloy is poured. The ritual vessel emerges. It will be used to feed the ancestors for generations and then buried with a king.

**3 — Anyang:** The diviner heats the bronze stylus. The king's question has been written on the bone. The crack forms. The diviner reads: auspicious. The scribes inscribe the answer. In three thousand years, a farmer will plow this field and find the bone.

**4 — Zhou Conquest:** Di Xin, the last Shang king, has lost the Mandate of Heaven by cruelty and excess. This is what the Zhou will say. At the battle of Muye, the Shang army collapses. Di Xin burns himself in his palace. The Zhou inherit the ancestors and invent the concept that explains why inheritance was legitimate: Tian Ming, the Mandate of Heaven.
