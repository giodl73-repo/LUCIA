# The Atlantic Corridor — Opening

*Era 2: c. 1000–1400 CE*

---

He woke before the birds.

This was how you knew the word had come: you were awake before the forest thought to speak. Arapoty lay still on his mat of woven grass and felt the dream departing the way water leaves cupped palms — not slowly, not all at once, but in a succession of small abandonments. A field of maize. Light without shadow. A sky the color of the first breath a newborn takes. And no death anywhere. Not the rot-smell of the cut tree. Not the sound the peccary makes when the arrow finds it. Nothing dying. Everything simply there, the way a river is simply there — not because it was made but because it has always been.

He rose before his wife woke. He walked to the edge of the clearing where the manioc grew in careful rows and stood facing east, where the tree canopy opened slightly toward the sky's first lightening. The air was thick with the smell of wet earth and the particular sweetness of the bromeliad flowers that opened at night and closed at sunrise, as if they were keeping something for the darkness alone. He could feel the river behind him — not hear it, feel it — the way you feel a large body breathing in the same room.

He had been a karai for eleven years. He had known the word before he knew what to name it: this pressure in the chest that was not sickness and not fear but something closer to the way a woman's belly feels when the child inside has turned and is now descending. An urgency that was not his own. Something wanting to move through him.

He did not speak that morning. He waited until the men had finished their first meal, until the women had begun the work of processing the manioc — scraping, pressing, the flat cakes already forming on the clay griddles that still held last night's heat — and then he walked to the center of the village and sat on the speaking-log, which was not a log but a carved seat that the oldest karai in memory had shaped from a single piece of dark wood, and he began.

He told them about the field. He told them about the light without shadow. He said: there is a place where the maize grows without our hands. He said: there is a place where no one descends into the earth and fails to return. He said: this is not a story. I have seen it. I have walked in it with my sleeping body while my waking body lay here among you. The land is real. It is east. It is where the sun rises from.

Several of the older men nodded. They had heard this before — not this specific dream, but the shape of it. Their fathers had heard it. The karai of their fathers' time had spoken of the same eastern light, the same abundance that required nothing from the hands, the same absence of the tooth-in-the-belly that is aging, that is sickness, that is the body's long argument with itself before it surrenders. Yvy Maraey. The Land Without Evil. It was not a hope in the way that drought makes people hope for rain. It was not a wish. It was a fact that existed in a direction — east, toward the rising — that had not yet been reached.

Three families began preparing that week. They did not announce departure. They did not hold a council. They simply began the work of it: the selection of seed maize that would survive a journey, the careful wrapping of the clay vessels that held fermented manioc, the conversations with children about which things could be carried and which things would need to stay. The community watched and understood. Some would go. Some would follow when the time was clear. Some would remain until the karai's words deepened into certainty.

This was how it worked. This was how it had always worked. Not the sudden departure of the frightened but the gradual gathering of the called. The ñe'ë — the sacred word that lived in each person, the fragment of divine speech that was the truest name of the self — moved in the people the way a current moves in a river. It was not visible at the surface. It was felt in the direction of things, in the pull that could not be reasoned about or refused, only followed or resisted until the resistance broke you.

Arapoty had not said: we must go. He had said: I have seen it. The difference was everything.

The Atlantic coast lay perhaps forty days to the east through forest that no one in his community had walked. They had heard of it from traders who brought shells and stories from the coastal people who lived where the great water was so large it had no other side. Some said the land was in that water. Some said the land was past that water. Some said the land was nowhere and everywhere and the movement toward it was itself the thing.

Arapoty did not know which of these was true. He knew only what the dream had shown him, and the dream had shown east, and east was a direction that could be followed.

The manioc cake on the griddle made its particular sound — the soft hiss of moisture leaving — and somewhere behind him a child laughed at something.

He stood and faced the rising light.
