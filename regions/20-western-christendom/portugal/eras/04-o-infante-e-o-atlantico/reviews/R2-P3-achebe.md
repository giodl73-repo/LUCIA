# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Panel Review Round 2 (NOTES)

**Chapter:** Portugal Era 4 — O Infante e o Atlântico
**Stage:** PANEL-2 (notes.md review)
**Reviewer:** P-3 Chinua Achebe
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The notes have taken my challenge seriously. Section 4 proposes to render the Lagos 1444 scene from both Zurara's divided witness and from the Wolof coast. The commitment to "both sides of the encounter" is present. Now I need to test whether the execution plan is adequate.

**On Zurara as divided witness:** The notes identify Zurara as a structural element, not just a citation — someone whose divided attention is itself evidence. This is the right approach. Zurara's Chapter XXV of the *Crónica de Guiné* describes the scene at Lagos with unexpected emotional honesty: "to increase their misery," he writes of the separated families, and he records that he himself was moved to tears. But he then continues the chronicle without resolving the tension — he notes the grief and then returns to celebrating the enterprise. The chapter should not resolve this tension either. Zurara's unresolved witness is the honest form of the moment. Do not make him more tortured than he was. Do not make him more comfortable with it than he was. Use him as he left himself.

**On the Wolof presence:** The notes identify the Jolof Empire as "at its height" in the 1440s. This is accurate: the Jolof Empire controlled the northern Senegambian coast and interior and was a major political entity. The people the Portuguese encountered on the coast were not stateless individuals; they were subjects of a state, traders in a trade network, participants in an existing coastal economy. The chapter should give at least one of them a name and a context — not invented but drawn from the historical record. If Zurara names any of the Africans in his chronicle, use those names. If not, note the absence honestly: these were specific people, and the record does not preserve their names.

**Critical question:** The notes say the chapter will render the encounter "from the coast as well as from the ship." How? There are no surviving African sources from this moment. The chapter cannot fabricate an African perspective. What it can do is: (1) note that the Wolof coast had an existing economy, trade, and political structure that the Portuguese were disrupting; (2) use the detail Zurara provides about African responses to Portuguese contact (some people ran, some tried to trade, some resisted) to show that the Africans were reading the situation and responding, not merely reacting; (3) be honest that the African perspective is structured absence, not ignorance.

**What I will not accept:** A chapter that describes the slave trade from the Portuguese vantage point and then adds a paragraph "acknowledging the human cost." The human cost is not a postscript. It is the other half of the story. If the chapter gives 700 words to the caravel's mechanics and 200 words to the 235 enslaved people at Lagos, it has not held both things simultaneously. It has told one story and mentioned another.

**The Achebe Test for this chapter:** The question is whether a Wolof historian reading this chapter would feel that their ancestors' story was told with dignity — not idealized, not condemned, but present and specific. The notes have the right intention. The chapter must execute it.

Proceed with caution and honesty.
