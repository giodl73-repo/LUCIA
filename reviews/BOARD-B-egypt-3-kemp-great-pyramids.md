## B-egypt-3 — Barry Kemp — Board Review of The Age of the Great Pyramids

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Old Kingdom social structure, provincial administration, centralization and fragmentation, economic systems.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The centralization-to-fragmentation arc is correctly described. The nomarchs' evolution from royal appointees to hereditary rulers across the Sixth Dynasty is accurate.
- Harkhuf's autobiography at Aswan is correctly cited, and the Pepi II letter about the dancing dwarf is a real document inscribed on Harkhuf's tomb wall. The chapter correctly uses it as evidence of both the child-king's personality and the nomarchs' increasing autonomy.
- The First Intermediate Period described as "diffusion" rather than "collapse" is accurate and avoids the common error of treating decentralization as catastrophe. Good.
- The economic feedback loop (flood -> grain -> surplus -> state revenue -> construction) is structurally accurate.
- The Nile-based calendar (Akhet, Peret, Shemu) is correctly described and correctly linked to the labor rotation system.
- The forty-two nomes are correctly numbered.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter avoids treating decentralization as moral failure. The nomarchs are not villains. The fragmentation is structural, not personal. This is correct.
- The phrase "This was not a collapse. It was a diffusion" is the right framing.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The structural engine (ma'at feedback loop running forward, then in reverse) is the chapter's strongest analytical contribution. The reversal in Section VIII — "the feedback loop that had built the pyramids was running in reverse" — is a genuine structural insight.
- The economic base (bread, beer, grain, cattle) connected to the theological superstructure (ma'at, solar ascent) throughout, not as separate topics.
- The labor rotation tied to the Nile flood cycle, making the pyramid a product of the same rhythm that produced the agriculture.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter mentions "the Nile no longer floods" and the Aswan High Dam in the landing. This is factually accurate but the date (1970) should be noted as the completion of the High Dam, not the original Aswan Dam (1902). The chapter does specify "Aswan High Dam, completed in 1970," so this is correct as written.
- The chapter could note that the grain-tax system was not uniform across the Old Kingdom but evolved. In the Fourth Dynasty, the system was more directly controlled by the crown; by the Sixth Dynasty, provincial collection had become more autonomous. The chapter gestures at this with the nomarch discussion but does not specify the tax mechanism's evolution. This is a compression issue, not an error.
- One structural observation: the chapter's treatment of the Fifth Dynasty sun temples as a "rebalancing" is interesting and broadly supportable, but some scholars (Stadelmann, Verner) would argue the shift also reflects a political change in the relationship between the king and the sun-priest establishment at Iunu (Heliopolis). The chapter's framing as theological continuity with architectural change is defensible but not the only reading.

**Verdict:** APPROVED
