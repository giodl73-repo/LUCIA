# P-3 — Chinua Achebe — R2 Review of Notes (Production Bible)

**Overall:** The production bible shows awareness of the voice problem I identified. The proposed solutions are sound but require execution discipline. The sacred-knowledge boundary note is excellent and essential.

**What works:**
- "The Tjukurpa does not count years. The Ancestors walked when they walked, and they are still walking." If the chapter can sustain this reframing of deep-time uncertainty through the Dreaming's own temporal logic, it will solve the outside-voice problem. The Dreaming is not time-bound. Let the Dreaming's timelessness be the answer to the archaeological record's uncertainty.
- The sacred-knowledge boundary principle is exactly right. "What happens at certain sites along the Songline is not for this account. The custodians hold it." This is not evasion. It is respect for a knowledge system's own access protocols. It will also read, to the right reader, as evidence of the system's seriousness.
- The Country-as-integration-pivot is stronger than the eland pivot for this culture. Country IS the integration point. The San's eland was a single species through which all threads passed. Country is the ground on which all threads stand. This is the correct structural choice.

**What doesn't:**
- The notes list "The Ancestors (Tjukurpa beings)" as figures but do not yet grapple with how to narrate them. /Kaggen in the San chapter was told through a storytelling scene (N!aisa telling children). How will the Ancestor beings be present in this chapter? Will they be narrated in the Dreaming's own voice? Told through an elder's teaching? Painted on rock and read? The method matters.
- The astronomical section (Section 3, Jirri) risks becoming a catalog of star knowledge. "The Pleiades as the Seven Sisters. The Dark Emu in the Milky Way." This is a list. It needs a scene: Jirri looking at the sky with an elder, the elder pointing, the knowledge arriving through the act of being shown. The knowledge must be TAUGHT, not listed.

**The question I'd push on:**
The naming of the Dreaming as "Tjukurpa" is Pitjantjatjara. The opening uses it consistently. But the chapter covers multiple nations. Will it acknowledge that different nations use different words for the same underlying reality? The chapter risks homogenizing hundreds of nations into a single "Aboriginal" experience if it stays within one nation's terminology throughout.
