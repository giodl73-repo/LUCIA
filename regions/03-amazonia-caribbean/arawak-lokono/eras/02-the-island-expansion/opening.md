# Era 2: The Island Expansion
## Opening

The canoe was loaded before the sun reached the second mangrove island.

Kurima had counted the trade goods three times — dried fish wrapped in palm leaf, two clay vessels from his mother's firing, a coil of cord braided from the fibers his sister had stripped from the inner bark of the tirite palm. Small things. A young man's things. His uncle, who had made this crossing twice, had told him the islands were not like the delta. The people there spoke the same root of words but with sounds that had shifted, the way a river sounds different at its mouth than at its source. They would understand him. He would understand them. But the mangrove coast would be gone from sight before the island appeared, and in that gap between knowing where you had been and seeing where you were going, a man learned whether he was a river person or an ocean person.

He was not yet sure which he was.

The Orinoco mouth at the dry season: a hundred channels braiding through mud and root and green water, herons lifting one leg at a time in the shallows, the smell of rot and salt and the fresh silt the river brought down from the mountains he had never seen. His people called themselves Lokono — the people — and the river was not background to their lives but the medium of it, the way water is the medium of fish. You did not travel the river. You lived it. Every bend had a name. Every current had a history. The channel that ran fast beneath the second sandbar where the anaconda had taken his grandfather's dog — that current was local knowledge, passed through three generations of paddlers who knew it by feel in the dark.

But Kurima had been listening to his uncle's stories about the islands since he was small enough to sit in the bow and watch the water. The Lesser Antilles were a day's crossing and then another half-day if the wind favored. His uncle's uncle had gone before that. Other families had gone before them, whole households moving up the island chain in the years his great-grandparents had been children, leaving kin nodes behind at each island like seeds pressed into new soil. A person did not disappear when they went to the islands. They became a point in the network. They sent back things the mainland did not have — certain shells, the dried flesh of sea animals, bird feathers from species that did not reach the continent — and the mainland sent them things the islands could not make: the clay vessels, the cord, the knowledge of the river forest.

The network was the world. This was something Kurima understood not as an idea but as a physical sensation, the way you understand weight by lifting it. His family's obligations ran in every direction: to his mother's brother, who was owed labor at planting time; to the family three river-bends east, where his father had traded a canoe for river-passage rights through a contested channel; to the downstream community that processed the bitter manioc and whose cassava bread his household ate each season when the rains came. Obligation was not a burden. It was what made you real in other people's worlds. A person with no obligations was a person no one would help when the dry season stretched long.

He settled into the paddling rhythm. Behind him, in the stern, his cousin matched his stroke. They did not speak. You did not need to speak when you paddled with someone you had paddled with since childhood; the communication was in the shift of weight, the slight lean before a turn, the pause when a current took the bow and you felt together whether to let it carry you or push against it.

The delta mangroves thinned. The water changed color from brown-green to blue-green. The smell changed. Something opened in the air — salt, but not the salt of brackish river mouth, a cleaner salt, a salt that carried no memory of mud.

Kurima had heard that the islands held different things. Not just different goods — different ways of growing and keeping. The island families had adapted the manioc gardens to soil that was not delta soil, had learned to build their settlements above the storm surge line, had worked out the fishing of reef waters where the fish moved in patterns nothing like the river's patterns. When he brought back those differences — not just the goods but the knowledge of how the island people lived — his community would act on them. Some would decide to go and see. Some would go and stay. The network would extend another increment northward.

This was how the Lokono world grew: not through conquest, not through command, but through the accumulated decisions of young men and women who went to see what the islands held and came back knowing.

The first island appeared at the horizon as a low darkness, a thickening of the line where sky met sea. Kurima watched it rise as the canoe crossed the open water, felt his cousin lean slightly forward in the stern, the same lean Kurima felt in his own body — the instinct of the paddler when the destination becomes visible, when the crossing becomes a certainty rather than a faith.

He thought about what his uncle had told him: the island people would receive him through their network, not as a stranger but as kin at one remove. He carried a name his uncle's contacts knew, and that name was a door. You did not arrive at the islands empty. You arrived carrying the thread that connected you back to the mainland, and the island people took that thread and added it to their own web of threads, and the network grew by one more connection, and the next crossing would be slightly less uncertain than this one.

The island was real. He could see the dark green of the hilltop trees now, and below them the white line of surf on the reef.

He paddled.
