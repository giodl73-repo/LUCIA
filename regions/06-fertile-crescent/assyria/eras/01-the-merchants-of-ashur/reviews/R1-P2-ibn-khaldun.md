## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of The Merchants of Ashur (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening identifies the correct mechanism: the karum network as a system of organized long-distance trade that preceded and structurally enabled the later state. But it does not yet explain WHY Ashur specifically produced this system, and it risks presenting the merchant network as a charming prelude rather than a causal engine.

**What works:**
- The description of the karum assembly as a self-governing judicial body with elected judges and recorded precedents is the most analytically valuable element. This is not merely a trade network; it is a proto-institutional structure. The opening correctly places the institution, not the individual merchant, at the center.
- The closing paragraph's restraint is well-judged: Lamassatum "knew what she knew." The opening does not claim she was building an empire without knowing it. It lets the commercial world be its own explanation.
- The identification of profit as the product of geographic arbitrage ("the difference between the price of tin in the east and the price of tin in the west") is precise. This is the mechanism.

**What doesn't:**
- WHY Ashur? The opening tells me that Ashur's merchants traveled to Anatolia with tin and textiles, but it does not explain why Ashur occupied this position. What about the city's geography, its location on the Tigris, its relationship to the tin routes from the east, made it the node in this network rather than Babylon or any other Mesopotamian city? The structural cause is missing.
- The karum arrangement ("the right to buy and sell and adjudicate their own disputes, so long as they paid the taxes") is described but not analyzed. What was the Anatolian ruler's interest in this arrangement? What did the local polities gain? A trade network is a relationship, not a one-sided venture. The opening presents it from Ashur's side only.
- The relationship between the temple of Ashur, the city's political authority (the rubā'um), and the merchant houses is mentioned but not explained as a system. How did these three nodes of authority interact? The chapter will need to answer this.

**The question I'd push on:**
What was the structural logic that made the karum system sustainable for two centuries? A private merchant diaspora operating in foreign territory under local rulers' sufferance is fragile by definition. What held it together? If the chapter cannot identify that mechanism, it will narrate events without explaining the system.
