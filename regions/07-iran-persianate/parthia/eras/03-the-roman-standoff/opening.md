# Opening: The Roman Standoff

*Carrhae, 53 BCE. The ninth hour.*

The camel-driver did not stop when the arrows began to fall.

His name was Abgar — so the Romans later said, though the name tells us only that he was from somewhere east of the Euphrates, not who he was or what he understood about the morning he was earning his fee. He had led the Roman column through the Balikh valley, through scrub and red dust, away from the river roads where an army could drink and resupply, out onto the Syrian plain where the sky sat on the horizon like a bronze lid. He knew the horses were coming. He had been well paid to know this and to say nothing.

Crassus, the richest man in Rome — the man who had crushed Spartacus and now wanted a war to call his own — had brought forty thousand soldiers east to take Parthia. His allies had told him to hug the Euphrates, to keep water within reach, to let the river roads guide him north toward Armenia where the terrain would give his infantry the footing they needed. He had ignored this advice. He was sixty years old and he knew what a Roman army was worth.

What he did not know was how arrows could come from three directions at once without the archer being in range of a spear.

The Suren had arranged this. The general whose name the Romans could not quite transcribe — Surena in their accounts, but the clan-name was what mattered, not the personal name, because the Suren family had provided Parthian armies for a century and would provide them again after this man was dead — had brought ten thousand cavalry to meet seven Roman legions, and he had understood from the beginning that the question was not whether the Romans could fight. It was whether they could do anything at all.

The horse-archers surrounded the Roman formation and began. They were not charging. They had no reason to charge. The camels moved in a wide circle behind the cavalry, carrying baskets of arrows, and when a quiver emptied the rider would swing back to refill and another would take his place, and the fire was continuous, a rotation, a system, a machine built from living horses and living men who had practiced this since before they could read. The Romans formed a testudo and the arrows found the gaps. The Romans opened out to charge and the horse-archers opened their circle, staying beyond spear-range, keeping pace with a Roman sprint without effort, and when the Romans stopped the archers closed again. Crassus's son led a charge with a Gallic cavalry escort and they disappeared into the dust and did not come back.

By midday, twenty thousand Romans were dead. The rest were retreating north through the night, and in the dark Crassus was killed — the exact circumstances disputed, as they always are when a great man dies badly. The Parthians sent his head to the Arsacid king's court at Ctesiphon, where it arrived, the story went, during a performance of Euripides. The actor playing Agave, who carries the head of her son thinking it a lion she has killed, exchanged the theatrical prop for the real head of Rome's wealthiest man. The audience laughed, or wept, or both.

What the Suren had demonstrated was not simply a military tactic. It was a proposition about how an empire holds its shape when the territory it governs cannot be held by the methods that built it. The Parthian empire stretched from the Euphrates to the eastern plateau, from Armenia to the edge of India, and it was governed through the great noble houses — the Suren, the Karen, the Mihran — who ran their territories with their own armies, their own revenues, their own courts, as a condition of their loyalty to the Arsacid dynasty. The king held the center not through bureaucratic machinery but through the network of obligations that the great houses maintained with the throne. It was not a weakness. It was the only system that worked on this terrain, at this scale, without the roads and written law and city-grain the Romans depended on.

The Euphrates became the border neither side could cross permanently. Rome attacked again, and again, and again — Antony failed, Trajan's campaigns half-succeeded and then reversed, the frontier fluctuated by a hundred miles in each direction over two centuries — and the Parthian answer was always the same: let them come, let them take what they can hold, follow when they turn back. Hold the plateau. Let the distance do the work. The horse-archers could fire backward at full gallop, and this fact had entered the Latin language — *Parthian shot* — as a metaphor for the parting blow of a departing enemy, which misunderstood the point entirely. The Parthian shot was not a parting blow. It was a statement about terrain.

Between the attacks, the silk moved. From Chang'an to Ctesiphon to Antioch to Rome, through the great caravanserais of the plateau, through the customs-points the Arsacid governors maintained at every pass. The Parthians collected their percentage. The empire that could not be held from the front paid for itself at the crossing.

Abgar rode out of the Roman encampment before dawn. He had been hired to lead them to water. He had led them somewhere else. Whether he understood what he had done, whether he thought about the men who would die in the heat of the Syrian plain, whether he considered himself a traitor or a servant or simply a man who had been paid: this the sources do not say. He disappears from the record as he entered it — a name attached to a function, a guide at the moment of arrival, already turning east before the arrows began.
