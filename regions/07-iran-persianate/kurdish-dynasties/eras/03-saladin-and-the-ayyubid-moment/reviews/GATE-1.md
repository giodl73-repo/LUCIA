## Notes

The opening is exceptional in its handling of the central interpretive challenge: Saladin as Kurd, not as Arab hero or Muslim symbol. The prose never hedges — it names him Kurd, names the Kurdish military networks, names the highland instinct — without making him a Kurdish nationalist anachronism. The v1.2 Honesty amendment is met: the restraint after al-Quds is attributed to the mountain-lord's logic, not to authorial admiration.

The one point deducted in Texture reflects that the opening's first paragraph, while rich in named places and relationships, is more genealogical than sensory. A single physical detail — the temperature of the Tikrit house, the smell of the garrison yard — would have given the ancestry scene the embodied anchor it earns on its own terms.
