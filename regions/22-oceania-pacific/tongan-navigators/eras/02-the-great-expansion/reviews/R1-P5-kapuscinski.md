## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Review of The Great Expansion (Opening)

**Overall:** I can feel the hull. I can feel the water. The physical world is present and it carries the meaning. This is sensory writing that earns its abstractions.

**What works:**
- "His back against the curved planking, his body reading the water through the wood the way a hand reads skin." I am on this canoe. The physical contact between body and hull is the opening's primary instrument, and it works because navigation IS physical in this culture. The sensation is the method.
- The transition from deep blue to pale green as the kalia enters the lagoon. This is a specific visual that anyone who has sailed into a Pacific lagoon would recognize instantly. The color shift is not decorative. It is navigational information rendered as sensation.
- The sound of the hull moving through swells "with a sound like breathing." This is the maritime voice the Style Guide describes. The prose breathes with the canoe. The rhythm of the sentences matches the rhythm of the swells.
- The cargo is embodied: "polished to the color of old cream, heavy in the hand." I can hold the tabua. The fine mats are "months of labor." The material culture has weight and texture.

**What doesn't:**
- I want more senses. The opening is strong on touch and sight but thin on sound and smell. What does the ocean sound like from inside a kalia hull? What does the pandanus sail smell like when wet? What does the sennit cord smell like? The Pacific ocean has a smell, and a Tongan navigator would know it the way a farmer knows the smell of soil before rain.
- The people on shore at the end are generic. "The people on the shore came down to meet it." Who are they? What do they look like? What are they wearing? What sounds do they make? The arrival is the opening's climax, and it needs more physical presence at the moment of contact.
- Night is missing. Polynesian navigation is fundamentally a nighttime practice. The stars are the primary reference system, and the opening mentions them but does not put the reader under the night sky. A passage of night sailing, with the stars and the sound of the ocean and the feel of the wind, would deepen the sensory world enormously.

**The question I'd push on:**
Where is the night? The stars are mentioned as data points, but the experience of sailing under a Pacific sky, where the stars are so numerous and so bright that they light the water, is absent. This is a sensory experience that defines Polynesian seafaring. The chapter cannot be complete without it.
