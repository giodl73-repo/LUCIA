# Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad

The letter arrived in Katsina before the rains.

It came on the road from Degel, carried by a student who had walked three days through the dry scrub of Gobir and crossed the boundary into Katsina territory without stopping at the sarki's gate. He carried no weapon, only the letter, folded and sealed, and a green cloth bundle that he guarded against his chest. The letter was in Arabic, written in the careful hand of a man whose fingers were permanently darkened with gum-arabic ink, and it was addressed to a scholar named Umaru Dallaji, who taught Maliki jurisprudence in a compound near the central market where the smell of tanned leather and ground pepper hung in the air from dawn to dusk.

Umaru Dallaji broke the seal and read. The letter was from Usman dan Fodio, Shehu of the Torodbe community at Degel, and it said what Umaru already knew it would say, because the arguments had been circulating for twenty years. The Hausa sarakuna had departed from the path. They collected taxes the Sharia did not sanction. They mixed Islamic law with customs that had no basis in the Quran or the Sunna. They tolerated practices among their courts that any student of fiqh could identify as bid'a, innovation against the Prophet's way. The letter said more: the time for teaching had ended. The time for patience, for munyal, the Fulani virtue that had kept the Torodbe scholars in their compounds and their students at their books while the sarakuna grew fat on unlawful taxes, that time was over. The Shehu had made the hijra. He had left Gobir. He had raised the flag.

And inside the green cloth bundle was a second flag.

This was the mechanism. Not an army marching from a single capital, not a general sending orders down a chain of command. A letter. A flag. A scholar in a distant city who had been reading the same texts, attending the same arguments, arriving at the same conclusions for years. Usman dan Fodio did not need to build an army from scratch. The army already existed. It was the network of Torodbe scholars and their students, scattered across every Hausa city-state from Gobir to Zaria, connected by decades of correspondence, shared teachers, shared texts, and a shared diagnosis of what had gone wrong.

Umaru Dallaji unfolded the flag. Green cloth, the color of the Prophet. It was not large. It did not need to be. What mattered was that it existed, that the Shehu had sent it, and that an identical flag had been sent to Kano, to Zaria, to Daura, to every place where a Torodbe scholar had students and those students had families and those families had cattle and those cattle meant wealth that the sarakuna taxed and the scholars said the sarakuna had no right to tax.

This was the year 1219 of the Hijra (1804 CE). The Shehu was fifty-four years old. He had been teaching for more than thirty years, and he had written more than a hundred treatises, his reed pen wearing grooves into the wooden writing boards at Degel that his students copied and carried across the Sahel. In the *Kitab al-Farq*, which every scholar in the network had read, he had drawn the line between a ruler who governs by Islamic law and a ruler who governs by custom and calls it Islam. The line was precise, drawn with the specificity of a jurist who had studied al-Maghili and al-Suyuti and the Maliki scholars of the Sahel for a lifetime. It was not a line you could straddle. The Hausa kings were on one side of it. The Shehu was on the other.

In Kano, the flag went to Sulaiman, who received it in his compound near the dye pits where the sour ferment of indigo hung in the air and the workers' hands were stained blue to the elbow. He read the letter and sent word to the pastoral Fulani in the hinterland, the cattle owners whose herds moved through Kano's territory each dry season and whose grievance against the sarki's taxes was as old as the taxes themselves. In Zaria, the flag went to Musa, a scholar whose students numbered in the hundreds and whose copy of the *Kitab al-Farq* was so annotated that the margins held more ink than the text. Not every scholar who received the flag was certain. In one city, the sources say, a flag-holder turned the green cloth in his hands for days before he could bring himself to raise it. The cost of failure was not theological. It was his family, his students, his compound burned.

They did not all rise at once. The coordination was not military precision. It was scholarly consensus. Each flag-holder read the letter, consulted with his own students, assessed the local situation, and chose his moment. In some places, the jihad began within weeks of the flag's arrival. In others, months passed. But the flags went up, one after another, across the distance from Gobir to Adamawa, and the sarakuna of the Hausa Bakwai discovered that the quiet scholar in Degel, the man they had tolerated and sometimes patronized and sometimes threatened, had built something they had never seen: a revolution distributed across a network of books.

Umaru Dallaji looked at the flag in his hands. The compound was quiet. His students would arrive for the afternoon lesson soon, and among them the young women of the Torodbe households who studied Maliki law alongside the men, as the Shehu had insisted they must. The leather-tanners in the market outside were beating hides, and the rhythmic thud of the mallets carried over the mud-brick walls. On the shelf above his books sat his own copy of the *Kitab al-Farq*, its binding cracked, its arguments long since committed to memory. He had a choice to make, but in truth the choice had been made years ago, in the classrooms and the correspondence and the slow accretion of conviction that what the sarakuna called governance, the Sharia called oppression.

He placed the flag on the shelf beside the book. Not yet. But soon.
