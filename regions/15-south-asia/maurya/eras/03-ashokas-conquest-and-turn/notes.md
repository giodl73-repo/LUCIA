# Notes — Ashoka's Conquest and Turn

## Production Bible

### Sources

**Primary (Tier 1):**
- Ashoka's Rock Edicts (RE), especially RE 13 (Kalinga edict), RE 1-6 (dhamma principles), RE 12 (tolerance), RE 9 (ceremonies vs. true dhamma)
- Pillar Edicts (PE), especially PE 7 (summary of reign), PE 6 (welfare of all beings)
- Separate Kalinga Rock Edicts (SRE 1-2, found at Dhauli and Jaugada)
- Minor Rock Edicts (MRE), especially MRE 1 (personal Buddhist declaration)
- Kautilya, Arthashastra (Books 6-7, theory of the vijigishu and the mandala system of interstate relations)

**Academic (Tier 2):**
- Romila Thapar, Ashoka and the Decline of the Mauryas (1997 rev. ed.): standard work on Ashokan chronology, edict interpretation, and the nature of dhamma
- Patrick Olivelle, Ashoka: Portrait of a Philosopher King (2021): recent reassessment, argues dhamma as philosophical governance rather than Buddhist sectarianism
- Upinder Singh, A History of Ancient and Early Medieval India (2008): archaeological context, Pataliputra excavations, Maurya material culture
- Harry Falk, Asokan Sites and Artefacts (2006): epigraphy, site locations, script analysis

**Scholarly Secondary (Tier 3):**
- Megasthenes' Indica (fragmentary, via McCrindle): Greek ambassador's account of Pataliputra and Maurya governance
- Mudrarakshasa (Sanskrit drama): literary account of Chandragupta's rise, useful for court atmosphere

### Key Figures

**Ashoka Devanampiya Piyadassi (c. 304-232 BCE):**
Third Maurya emperor. Name means "without sorrow," a name he may have taken rather than been given at birth (Thapar). Title Devanampiya Piyadassi means "Beloved of the Gods, of Gracious Mien." The edicts consistently use this title, not the name Ashoka, which appears only in the Minor Rock Edicts. The record cannot fully support his interiority. We know what he carved, not what he thought. Handle the gap honestly: the edicts are a public performance of moral transformation; whether they reflect private conviction is unknowable and should be named as unknowable.

**Kautilya/Chanakya:**
Minister attributed with the Arthashastra. Historical existence debated but the text itself provides the intellectual framework Ashoka was working within and against. Present as the tradition from which Ashoka both descended and departed.

**The dhamma-mahamatras:**
Officers of dhamma, created by Ashoka (RE 5). A new class of official with no precedent in the Arthashastra. Their job was to promote dhamma among all people, visit prisoners, ensure fair treatment. Present as the institutional innovation that made dhamma more than a personal ethic.

**The peoples of Kalinga:**
The conquered. The edicts give numbers. They do not give names. Archaeological record at Dhauli and Jaugada includes the Separate Kalinga Rock Edicts, which are the most personal of all Ashoka's inscriptions. The Kalingans must have voice in this chapter even though the record is thin. Handle through landscape and through the edict's own address to them (SRE 1 addresses the Kalingans directly).

**Megasthenes:**
Greek ambassador at Chandragupta's court, c. 300 BCE. His Indica survives only in fragments. Useful for Pataliputra description. Handle as an outside observer whose biases are his own.

### Narrative Arc

**Structural engine:** The stone paradox. Power carved into stone becomes vulnerability carved into stone. The same medium (the edict) that proclaims sovereignty also proclaims its limits. Dhamma is the answer to a question the Arthashastra could not ask: what does the king owe the people he has conquered? The arc moves from conquest through crisis to transformation, but the transformation is always tethered to power.

**Movement:**
1. **The Edict** (opening integrated) — The stone at Dhauli. The edict's voice. The act of inscription as the entry point. Establishes the voice.
2. **The War** — Kalinga itself. The Maurya army descends. The Arthashastra logic in action. The vijigishu calculates. The landscape of eastern India. The battle (what can be reconstructed). The cost.
3. **The Turn** — RE 13 in full. What Ashoka says happened to him. The Buddhist textual tradition vs. the edicts' own account. The gap between the two. What can be known and what cannot.
4. **The Policy** — Dhamma as governance. The dhamma-mahamatras. The edict network across the empire. The multilingual strategy. Non-violence as state practice. The animal hospitals, the herbal gardens, the roadside trees, the wells.
5. **The Stone** — The edicts as physical objects in landscape. The pillar at Sarnath (four lions). The material infrastructure of moral governance. The edicts' afterlife: buried, forgotten, rediscovered by James Prinsep in 1837 who deciphered the Brahmi script.
6. **The Landing** — The edicts still stand. The stone holds. What endures is not the empire (which fell within fifty years of Ashoka's death) but the inscription. The medium outlasted the power.

### Panel Pressure Points Anticipated

**Tuchman** will push on whether the inscriptional voice can sustain 5,000 words without becoming monotonous. **Solution:** Modulate within the register. The war section uses shorter, more kinetic sentences. The policy section uses accumulation and listing (mirroring the edicts' own enumerative style). The landing slows.

**Ibn Khaldun** will push on the mechanism of the turn: why Ashoka and not other conquerors? **Solution:** Present multiple explanations (Buddhist awakening, third-generation imperial fatigue, administrative pragmatism for diverse empire). Let the edicts be the primary evidence. Do not claim to know what the edicts do not reveal.

**Achebe** will push on the Kalingan voice and on the Nile-to-Tigris comparison. **Solution:** Fix the comparison in FIXES (reframe from within Indic tradition). Give the Kalingans presence through landscape, through the Separate Kalinga Rock Edicts (which address them directly), and through honest acknowledgment that their story is told only through their conqueror's words.

**Davis** will push on embodied inscription, multilingual ecology, and the monument/medium distinction. **Solution:** The stone section (V) will be densely material. The war section (II) will carry the body. The policy section (IV) will develop the multilingual strategy. Edict as medium (communication), not monument (commemoration).

**Kapuscinski** will push on sensory ground. **Solution:** War section gives battlefield body. Stone section gives inscription body. Policy section gives the sound of the edict read aloud. Every section has at least one sensory anchor.

### FIXES Applied (Stage 6)

1. **Nile-to-Tigris comparison removed** (Achebe P3). Replaced with internal Indic frame: rajashasana tradition, the grammar of Indian royal inscription. The comparison now comes from within.
2. **"No king before him" rephrased** to "The tradition of rajashasana was older than the Maurya" followed by the contrast. The novelty claim is now contextual, not absolute.
3. **Dhauli landscape grounded** (Kapuscinski P5). Added laterite hill, wet-earth smell, Daya River running brown through rice fields, stone warm under the hand. Sensory anchoring.
4. **Succession question: include briefly** (Achebe P3 counsel). The Divyavadana tradition of violence against brothers grounds the turn without dominating it. Brief mention in the Turn section.
5. **Kandahar edicts: include in Policy section** as evidence of multilingual reach. Greek and Aramaic on the northwestern frontier.
6. **Prinsep: include in landing** as a sensory moment per Kapuscinski's counsel. The man in Calcutta, the rubbing, the moment of reading.

### Open Questions (Resolved)

1. Battle of Kalinga reconstruction: acknowledge limits honestly. Use Arthashastra for army composition, edict for numbers, landscape for body. Do not fabricate.
2. Succession: include briefly in the Turn section. Honest about legendary status of Divyavadana account.
3. Kandahar: yes, in Policy section. Evidence of reach.
4. Prinsep: yes, briefly in landing. Sensory connection between mason and decipherer.
