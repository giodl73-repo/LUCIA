# R2-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Panel-2 Review: Notes, Philippe Auguste et la Grande Expansion

**Round:** 2
**Reviewer:** Ryszard Kapuściński (P-5)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The notes plan sensory ground for each major section, and I am glad the southern landscape is specifically flagged: "the Languedoc heat," the walled cities, Carcassonne. The chapter must give us the physical reality of the south as a distinct world from northern France — the Occitan language in the air, the limestone plateau, the dry summer hills around Béziers. The crusaders who came from the north were entering foreign terrain in every sense: geography, language, theology, climate. The reader should feel that foreignness.

The healing ceremony section (Section 4) is the one I am watching most carefully. The notes plan one ceremony rendered in the body — "the physical reality of it (the sick brought in, the long lines, the king moving through them, his hand on each neck or face or swollen gland)." This is correct. I would add: the smell. Medieval healing ceremonies happened in spaces with specific smells — unwashed bodies, incense, possibly the outdoor air of an abbey courtyard. The sick who came for the royal touch had often traveled days. They brought the dust of the road with them. Philip moved through this cloud of bodies. This is what the ceremony looked like from the inside, and the chapter needs to be inside it.

The Château Gaillard siege could use more physical texture in the chapter. Eight months of siege: the engineering of the approaches, the cold of a Norman winter, the smell of a besieged castle — these are the details that make the latrine-shaft story land harder. The latrine shaft is funny in the opening because it is surprising; in the chapter it should be both funny and grimly physical, because the reader will know by then what a siege costs in bodies.

Paris as a capital is a section I want to see rendered physically: Philip paving the streets of Paris is not bureaucratic history, it is a sensory event. The unpaved streets of medieval Paris were notoriously foul — mud, refuse, the smell of the river at low tide. To pave them was to change what the city smelled and sounded like. The chapter should give us the old Paris briefly and then the new, so the change registers in the body, not just in the mind.
