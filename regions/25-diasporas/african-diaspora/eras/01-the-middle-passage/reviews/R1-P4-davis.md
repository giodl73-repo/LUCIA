## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of The Middle Passage (Opening)

**Overall:** This opening does something I have rarely seen: it treats the Middle Passage as a knowledge-transfer event. Not a tragedy with cultural footnotes, but a transmission of intellectual systems under conditions that should have made transmission impossible. The African knowledge systems are treated with the same seriousness this project gives to Greek philosophy or Chinese governance. That is exactly right.

**What works:**
- The Yoruba knowledge system is presented at method level. The Ifa divination system is not described as a "spiritual practice" (the vague phrase that flattens everything non-Western into a single category). It is described as "the sixteen principal odu, the binary figures cast in palm nuts or cowrie shells that mapped the structure of fate." This is a specific intellectual system with a specific methodology. The reader learns how it works, not just that it exists.
- The opening makes the extraordinary claim that a human body can carry a civilization, and it proves it. The rhythms the hands knew how to play. The call-and-response patterns the voice fell into without thought. The cosmological framework. None of this required paper or buildings or territory. It required a living human being. This is the most powerful statement I have seen in this project about the nature of embodied knowledge.
- The cultural mixing strategy of the traders is revealed as a failure of understanding: the Portuguese mixed language groups to prevent communication, but the deep structure of West African music operates below language. Call-and-response is not a linguistic act. It is a musical and social architecture that transcends the specific languages of the people who practice it. The traders could not see this because they did not understand what they were dealing with.
- The list of what survived (Candomblé, Vodou, the ring shout, the blue notes) is not presented as a miracle or as evidence of "resilience" (a word that can patronize). It is presented as the predictable result of a knowledge system that was stored in a medium the trade could not confiscate.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening focuses on Yoruba knowledge systems. The Fon and Igbo traditions are mentioned but not given the same depth. In the chapter, the Fon cosmology (Vodun, the relationship between Mawu-Lisa and the vodun, the Fon contribution to Haitian Vodou) and the Igbo traditions (Odinani, the chi, the complex decentralized political structures that resisted capture differently than the centralized Oyo system) will need equivalent depth. The African diaspora was not only Yoruba. The chapter must represent the plurality of the knowledge systems that crossed.
- The phrase "purchasing a library" is a compelling metaphor, but it risks romanticizing the knowledge. A library is ordered, catalogued, preserved. What the man from Oyo carried was not a library but a living system, a way of knowing that required practice, community, and continuation to survive. The difference matters. A library survives on a shelf. A living system survives only if someone practices it. The chapter should acknowledge the fragility of embodied knowledge alongside its portability.

**The question I'd push on:**
What happened to the knowledge systems that did NOT survive? Not everything crossed. Some ritual practices required specific plants, specific landscapes, specific community structures that could not be replicated in the Americas. Some knowledge was carried by specialists (herbalists, diviners, smiths) who died in the crossing or whose specific knowledge required materials unavailable in the destination. The chapter should acknowledge what was lost as well as what survived, because honest wonder includes honest loss.
