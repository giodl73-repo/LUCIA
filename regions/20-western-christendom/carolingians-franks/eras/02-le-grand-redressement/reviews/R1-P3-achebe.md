# Panel-1 Review — P-3 Chinua Achebe
## Le Grand Redressement — Opening

**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (P-3)
**Stage:** PANEL-1
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The voice is mostly right. Let me be clear about where it is and where it
briefly is not.

The Carolingian world-logic — *correctio*, the theological theory of
governance, the chain of clergy as the empire's administrative spine — is
rendered from inside. Charles learns his letters in the same sentence that
tells us he has remade more of the world than anyone since the Caesars, and
neither fact is used to diminish or diminish the other. The king is a full
human being: the grip trained on a sword before a pen, the pillow-books,
the midnight work. Alcuin is not a saint of learning but a practitioner with
a diagnostic habit of mind. These are men, not archetypes.

But there is one place where the author steps outside — briefly, quickly, but
the step is visible: "not education as the modern world means it — not the
cultivation of individual minds for individual flourishing." This sentence
addresses the modern reader. It exists to prevent a misreading that a Frankish
reader would never make, because a Frankish reader would not have the modern
concept of individual cultivation to misread into the text. The sentence is
a sign that the author has noticed the modern reader's expectation and decided
to correct it. The correction is accurate. But the fact of the correction
breaks the inside voice.

How to fix it: do not negate the modern assumption. Instead, make the
Carolingian assumption so fully present that there is no room for the modern
one to enter. The passage about the *Admonitio Generalis* does part of this
work — "every monastery must have a school" — but then the author reaches
for the gloss. If the royal directive's own language can carry the argument,
let it. The capitularies themselves are quite clear about why literacy matters
to Charles: it is about the accuracy of prayer, the consistency of doctrine,
the reliability of administration. Let Charles and Alcuin explain this in
their own terms rather than clearing space for it by waving away what the
modern reader might think.

**What the voice does well:**
- The phrase "a shape that was no longer a word in any tongue" — this is from
  inside the moment, the inside of Alcuin's reading experience.
- "The empire was vast and the men who could read were few and the distances
  between here and the frontiers of Saxony or the Spanish March were measured
  in weeks of travel through forest and mud" — the voice stays grounded in
  the physical reality of the Carolingian world, not in a modern assessment
  of its administrative challenges.
- The close — "The manuscripts were accumulating. The trained scribes were
  dispersing to their monasteries." — is quiet and correct. No triumphalism,
  no modern verdict on whether the project succeeded.

**What I would push on:**
The chapter will need to address the fact that many of the people being
corrected did not experience *correctio* as reform. Saxon hostages at the
Frankish court, priests who had their liturgy standardized by royal directive,
abbots whose monasteries were reorganized by *missi* they had not asked for —
these are people for whom the reform was imposed, not invited. The chapter
must hold this complexity without stepping outside the Carolingian worldview
to judge it. The Carolingians believed they were correcting the empire for
God. That belief is the voice. The resistance — and there was resistance —
must be rendered in the same register.

**The question I'd push on:**
Who is the "we" of this era? The overview is written in first person plural
("We inherited a world half in ruins"). The opening is in close third on
Alcuin. For the chapter, the voice needs to decide: are we inside the
reform project (Alcuin's perspective, the palace school's), or can we move
inside the perspective of those the reform reaches — the Saxon noble whose
son is educated at Aachen, the Frankish priest learning to read at forty?
The chapter's voice will be strongest if it holds multiple inside perspectives
without ever stepping outside any of them.
