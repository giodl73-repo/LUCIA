## Anachronistic Morality Check

The reconcentración camps passage: "In the camps, the specific cruelty was not violence but deliberate deprivation." The word "cruelty" risks stepping outside to judge. However: the chapter does not say "Spain's cruel policy" from an authorial vantage point. It says the specific character of the cruelty, from inside the experience — a descriptive statement about the method, not an authorial verdict on Spain's moral status. The sentence immediately following ("the Spanish did not shoot the people they concentrated. They simply provided them with nothing and waited") is a statement of fact that earns the word "cruelty" by showing exactly what it consisted of. This is acceptable.

The Platt Amendment passage: "The United States had decided that Cuban sovereignty would be a managed condition, not an achieved fact." This is a characterization of the United States' position, stated factually. No "wrong" or "unjust" or "hypocritical" appears. The chapter states what happened and what it meant without announcing a verdict on whether it was right. Passes.

Maceo's death passage: "His death, like Martí's, was mourned as a military loss. It was also the removal, eighteen months before the war's end, of the figure most insistently committed to the racial terms of independence." The chapter notes the structural observation without attributing bad faith or conspiracy: "The chapter should be honest about this without verdict" was the production bible's instruction, and the chapter honors it. Passes.

## Romanticization Check

The manigua republic is described with clarity about what it was: "A republic without a capital, without a treasury, without a flag that any other nation would recognize." This is not romanticization; it is accurate description of the manigua's political status, followed by a serious account of how it actually functioned. The Lucumí and orixá passage is rendered as knowledge and function, not as mysticism or romantic spirituality. Passes.
