# Board Review — Wang Gungwu

**Chapter:** Chinese Overseas Era 1: The Tang-Song Nanyang
**Reviewer:** Wang Gungwu (institutional/structural specialist)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Overall Assessment

The chapter captures the structural logic of the Nanyang Chinese network correctly. The sojourner-to-settler transition, the huiguan system, the guanxi mechanism, and the dialect-community basis of the network are all accurately presented. The chapter's central insight, that the network functioned as a pre-legal infrastructure replacing the absent Chinese state, is sound and well-demonstrated.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

1. **The dialect-community basis.** Lin is Hokkien, not generically "Chinese." The Teochew trader who cheats a Hokkien merchant and is punished by the Hokkien network is a correct and important detail: the network was dialect-specific, not pan-Chinese. The solidarity of the dialect group was the mechanism; the broader "Chinese" identity came later and was always contested.

2. **The huiguan's institutional functions.** The chapter correctly identifies the huiguan's roles: dispute resolution, cemetery maintenance, school funding, welfare, and credit management. The detail about the cemetery facing northwest toward Fujian is culturally accurate and carries the emotional weight of the sojourner's persistent orientation toward home.

3. **The Song state's role.** The shibosi system is accurately described as a mechanism that taxed and enabled simultaneously. The chapter correctly avoids the common error of treating the Song state as either the architect or the enemy of the overseas trade; it was a participant with its own interests.

4. **The sojourner-to-settler transition.** Chen's decision to stay is the correct structural pivot, and the chapter presents it as an economic calculation that becomes a demographic fact. This is precisely how the transition worked.

## What Needs Correction or Clarification

1. **The Pu family.** The chapter describes the shibosi Pu as "Arab merchants who had married into the local community." The Pu (Abubakar) family's origins are Arab-Muslim, this is correct, but the family's rise to the shibosi position was more complex than "converting commercial knowledge into bureaucratic authority." The Pu family held the shibosi post through political connections to the Song court, not simply through commercial expertise. Suggest adding that the appointment reflected the court's pragmatic use of foreign-origin families whose trade connections served Song interests.

2. **Guanxi terminology.** The term "guanxi" as used in the chapter is anachronistic for this period. The concept of relationship-based commercial networks existed, but the term guanxi in its modern commercial sense is a later development. The chapter should either use the term with a note that it names a practice older than the word, or describe the practice without the label.

3. **The Mongol-Yuan section.** The attempted invasion of Java in 1293 is correctly dated, but the chapter's claim that the Champa campaigns were in "the 1280s" should be more precise: the major Champa campaign was 1283-1285. The relationship between the Yuan military campaigns and the Chinese commercial communities in the Nanyang is more nuanced than the chapter suggests. The Chinese merchants in some Nanyang ports actively supported Mongol-Yuan interests; in others, they were caught between Yuan and local loyalties. The chapter should acknowledge this complexity.

4. **The Peranakan pattern dating.** The chapter is appropriately cautious about the Tang-Song evidence for intermarriage, but the wedding scene (Huang in Palembang) may imply a more institutionalized pattern than the evidence supports for this period. The Peranakan culture as a recognized identity is a much later development (18th-19th century). Suggest framing the wedding more explicitly as the beginning of a process, not as an established pattern.

## Recommendation

The chapter is historically sound and structurally well-argued. The corrections above are matters of precision, not of direction. The central argument, that the guanxi network functioned as institutional infrastructure in the absence of state authority, is the correct analysis of the Nanyang Chinese formation, and the chapter demonstrates it through concrete scenes and specific figures. **Approve with minor revisions.**
