The notes address my key concern — knowledge at method level — in the section plan's Section 2. I am mostly satisfied with the intent. My focused concern is about execution.

The section promises: "The pluriarc tradition: the instrument's construction (gourd body, eight strings, tuning), the training relationship between master musician and apprentice, what specific ceremonies required specific musical forms." And then immediately: "Source caveat: the repertoire is poorly documented."

This is the correct acknowledgment. But the source-asymmetry technique requires more than acknowledging the gap — it requires doing something with it that makes the limitation itself part of the intellectual content. Here is what I want to see in the chapter:

The pluriarc tradition survives the Mangbetu court's destruction only in fragments — the instruments themselves (now in museums), the descriptions of their sound in explorers' accounts, and whatever ethnomusicological work has been done since. The chapter should show one of those fragments: a specific description of what Schweinfurth heard, or what the instrument felt like to hold (describable from the surviving instruments), or what the training relationship between master and apprentice looked like in the records we have. Even if the specific pieces are lost, the fact of the training relationship — that there were apprentices who observed masters for years — can be shown.

For the coiffure: Schildkrout & Keim will have detailed descriptions. The chapter should show the physical act at method level — not "a complex process of binding and building" but the specific sequence: what cloth, how tight, for how long, what the specialist's hands know that the untrained person's hands do not. This is knowable from the record and it is the difference between "they had a sophisticated tradition" and "here is the tradition operating."

On the architecture: the notes appropriately flag the archaeological gap. Use honest-uncertainty there — what Schweinfurth measured and described, what the materials would have required, what the forest-building knowledge implies even when the buildings no longer exist. The buildings are gone; the logic of building them in forest materials at that scale is recoverable.
