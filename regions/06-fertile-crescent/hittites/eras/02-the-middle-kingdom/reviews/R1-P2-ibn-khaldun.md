## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of Hittites Era 2 Opening

**Overall:** The opening identifies the correct structural problem: the cyclical self-destruction of dynastic asabiyya through succession violence. Telipinu's edict is an attempt to break a cycle that I have observed in every dynasty I studied. The question is whether the chapter will analyze the mechanism deeply enough or remain at the level of narrative drama.

**What works:**
- The causal chain is clear: royal murder leads to divine punishment (drought, military defeat, territorial shrinkage), which leads to further instability, which leads to further murder. This is not merely a sequence of events. It is a system, and the opening presents it as one. The phrase "as certain as seed and harvest" grounds the divine-punishment logic in agricultural reality, which is the correct move.
- The structural insight that the Hittites turned the treaty form inward is exactly the kind of institutional innovation that most historians pass over. Treaties bind foreign kings. Can the same contractual mechanism bind the royal family? This is the analytical core of the chapter, and the opening names it clearly.
- The honest uncertainty of the closing ("Whether it worked is the question the edict itself could not answer") respects the limits of the evidence and sets up a genuine analytical question rather than a predetermined conclusion.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening does not yet address the role of the *panku* (court assembly) as an institutional body. The edict assigns enforcement to the assembly, not to the gods alone. This is a critical distinction: the mechanism is not merely theological (the gods will punish) but institutional (the assembly has the authority to act). The opening mentions the assembly but does not yet analyze its function. The chapter must do this.
- The economic and military context of the Middle Kingdom period is gestured at ("The harvests failed. The borders shrank.") but not specified. What were the specific pressures? The Kaska raids from the north? The loss of Syrian territories? The Hurrian pressure from the east? These pressures are not background; they are part of the causal system that made the edict necessary.

**The question I'd push on:**
Every dynasty I have studied that attempted to formalize succession rules faced the same problem: the rules work only as long as the power behind them is credible. What was the enforcement mechanism? Was it genuinely the court assembly, acting as a constitutional body? Or was it the threat of divine sanction, which is to say, the threat of bad harvests and military defeat attributed to divine anger? The chapter should distinguish between the theological claim (the gods punish) and the institutional reality (who actually enforces). The edict may be both a constitutional document and a theological argument, but these are different instruments, and the chapter should know the difference.
