# BOARD-HRE-1 — Horst Fuhrmann | Domain Review

**Chapter:** Holy Roman Empire, Era 2 — Der Investiturstreit (1024–1122 CE)
**Reviewer:** Horst Fuhrmann (B-HRE-1) — High Medieval Germany, MGH
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Historical Accuracy

The chapter is historically sound in its main lines. The Canossa narrative is accurate — the three days, the hair shirt, Hugh of Cluny's intercession, the specific canonical-law reason for Gregory's compliance (contrition requiring mercy). The *Dictatus Papae* propositions are rendered correctly, with appropriate emphasis on the explosive proposition about releasing subjects from their oaths.

**Specific corrections required:**

1. **The Concordat of Worms characterization.** The chapter describes the two documents as "the papal *Calixtinum* and the imperial *Heinricianum*." The usual scholarly designations are reversed: *Calixtinum* refers to the document issued under Pope Calixtus II; *Heinricianum* refers to the document issued by Heinrich V (not Heinrich IV, who had died in 1106; this is his son). The chapter does not make this error explicitly — it doesn't say which Heinrich — but the context of the chapter may lead a reader to associate the Worms settlement with Heinrich IV. A clarifying note that the settlement was signed under his son Heinrich V would prevent confusion.

2. **The "Anno II kidnapping" story.** The anecdote of the young Heinrich being kidnapped by Anno of Cologne while being lured aboard a ship is attested in the sources (Lampert of Hersfeld's chronicle), but its accuracy is disputed by modern scholarship — Lampert is considered a tendentious source with a strong anti-Salian bias. The chapter presents the anecdote as fact in the figures section. It should be presented as "Lampert of Hersfeld records that..." or "the chronicles say..." rather than as established fact.

3. **Ariald's death date.** The chapter gives Ariald's death as June 1066. This is correct. The detail that his body was found "mutilated" is also attested. No correction needed here.

4. **Gregory's last words.** The attribution of "I have loved righteousness and hated iniquity; therefore I die in exile" to Gregory VII is attested in the sources — specifically in a contemporary chronicle. The words are a quotation of Psalm 44(45), verse 8, which Gregory adapted. The chapter renders them correctly, but note that they echo a biblical text — the choice of words is not original to Gregory but is his adoption of Scripture, which is itself a characterization worth noting as it reveals his self-understanding.

**No other factual errors identified.**

## Constitutional Accuracy

The chapter's characterization of the Concordat of Worms as a "vocabulary compromise that constitutionalized the disagreement rather than resolving it" is precisely correct and represents the current scholarly consensus. The two-document structure (each side issuing its own version) is an important detail that the chapter handles well.

The characterization of the German princes' autonomy gains during the controversy is also correct. The structural mechanism — that a divided emperor and pope each needed princely support and each paid for it in autonomy — is historically accurate and represents good analytical history.

The chapter's observation that the prince-bishops "owed loyalty to two masters" is a precise characterization of the structural problem. The Reichskirchensystem of the Ottonian period had functioned because the emperor controlled both appointments (temporal) and, effectively, the spiritual machinery of the Church in his territories. The Investiture Controversy disrupted this functional unity. The chapter correctly identifies this.

---

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter presents the investiture ceremony, the excommunication, and the canonical procedures with appropriate seriousness — treating them as real mechanisms with real consequences in the world these people inhabited. The observation that excommunication was "not a metaphor" and "not a bureaucratic sanction" is exactly right. The chapter does not condescend to the medieval world by treating theological argument as a mask over "real" political interests. It correctly presents the theological and the political as genuinely fused.

---

## Recommendation

Minor factual adjustments required (Heinrich V at Worms, Lampert source qualification for Anno kidnapping). All other historical content is accurate. The constitutional and institutional analysis is sophisticated and current. The chapter is endorsed.
