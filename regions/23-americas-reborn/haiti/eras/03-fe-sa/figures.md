# Figures — Haiti, Era 3: Fè Sa — The Revolution

---

## Boukman Dutty (d. December 1791)

The historical record gives us the outline of a man: a coachman on a plantation in the north plain, which meant freedom of movement across the region; a figure in the Vodou religious tradition, which meant spiritual authority and access to the network of ceremony and community that Vodou sustained; a man who gathered people in a forest at night and called on the ancestors.

The French colonial record gives us his head on a pike at Cap-Français, and the notation that the uprising he started did not end when he died.

Haitian tradition gives us something the colonial record could not: Boukman as founding ancestor, as the one who opened the covenant. The prayer attributed to him — "Listen to the voice of liberty that speaks in the hearts of all of us" — has been translated and retranslated, disputed and claimed. The words themselves may have shifted in the two centuries of their transmission. The weight they carry has not. He is not remembered as a general or a strategist. He is remembered as the one who called to the ancestors, and as the proof that when you call them correctly, they come.

What we cannot know — and the tradition itself does not claim to know — is what he thought in the moment, what he felt when Cécile Fatiman came into possession, whether he believed the uprising would succeed or simply that it had to happen regardless. The thinness of the record here is not a gap to be filled with invention. It is the shape of the event: the man who summoned the revolution is remembered for the summoning, not for his private psychology. That is how this tradition honors its founders.

## Cécile Fatiman (c. 1771–1883)

She is one of the revolution's most vivid presences and one of its least documented. What we know: she was at Bois Caïman on August 14, 1791. She came into possession — the lwa arrived in her body. She danced. She cut the throat of the black pig. She was, by various accounts, the daughter of an enslaved mother and a French planter — a *métisse*, a person of mixed race, which in the social architecture of Saint-Domingue placed her in a category that itself was in revolt against the colonial order.

What we know that the colonial record did not center: she lived to approximately 112 years old. She was alive to see the revolution succeed, to see the republic declared, to see the indemnity paid, to see the decades that followed. She died in 1883, nearly a century after the night in the forest.

The Vodou tradition has a name for the specific lwa who rode Cécile Fatiman at Bois Caïman: Ezili Dantor, the dark-skinned manifestation of Ezili, the lwa of love and fierce protection of women and children, associated with the Petro nation of lwa — the lwa born from the specific spiritual crucible of the plantation, fiercer than the Rada lwa of West Africa, forged in the conditions of Saint-Domingue. If the tradition is right about who arrived through Cécile Fatiman that night, then the covenant at Bois Caïman was not simply martial. It was also a covenant of protection, of the fierce love that is willing to wound.

Her longevity is part of how the tradition holds her: she was there at the beginning, and she endured to witness what the beginning had made. She carries the revolution's arc in her body.

## Toussaint Louverture (c. 1743–1803)

He was born into slavery in the north plain of Saint-Domingue, around 1743. By some accounts he was the son of an African prince — this may be hagiography, may be fact; the question of his parentage runs through the tradition as one of the things that shaped how he understood himself. What is documented: he was freed from slavery before the revolution, probably in his late twenties or early thirties; he was literate; he managed the livestock on the plantation where he had been enslaved and where he remained after his freedom, which was how freedom sometimes worked.

He joined the uprising after Bois Caïman. What he brought to it was not mystical authority (that was Boukman's, and it passed to the tradition) but something rarer in the circumstances: the ability to organize a military campaign, to think strategically across a theater of operations, to negotiate with multiple foreign powers simultaneously while keeping his primary objective — permanent abolition — fixed.

His is the most complicated life in this era's story. He was brilliant and authoritarian. He freed his people from slavery and then instituted labor contracts that chained them to the plantations by a different name. He was betrayed by the French and died in a cold cell in the mountains of eastern France, having trusted an assurance that any man with his experience of French promises should not have trusted. The tradition does not resolve these contradictions. It holds them.

What his own account gives us, written in captivity at Fort de Joux: a man who understood himself as the instrument of something larger than himself. He did not write "I freed Saint-Domingue." He wrote: "In overthrowing me, you have done no more than cut down the trunk of the tree of Black liberty in Saint-Domingue. It will spring back from the roots, for they are numerous and deep." He knew he was not the tree. He was the trunk. The roots were in the people.

He was right about that. He died in April 1803. Nine months later, Dessalines declared independence.

## Jean-Jacques Dessalines (c. 1758–1806)

He did not have Toussaint's literacy or his diplomatic finesse. He had survived the north plain plantation system under brutal conditions — the scars were visible on his body for the rest of his life — and he had concluded from that survival that certain things were non-negotiable.

His brutality in the revolution's later stages is documented and should not be minimized. His order to massacre the remaining French white population in early 1804 was carried out. He knew what he was ordering. He had a logic: the French presence was a structural threat to the republic's survival, and he had watched Toussaint's trust in French negotiations result in Toussaint's death. He would not repeat that calculation.

He was also, in the January 1, 1804 declaration, the man who named the country Ayiti. He tore the white from the French flag. He named the land by its oldest name — the Taíno name, the name that predated the colony, the name the land had given itself. This was a political-theological act of the first order: not merely declaring independence from France but declaring continuity with the land's own identity before France had arrived.

He was made Emperor Jacques I in 1804. He was assassinated in 1806, killed by a coalition of rivals including Pétion, who found his economic vision (the equitable division of assets) as threatening as the French had found the revolution itself. His body was desecrated after his death.

Haitian memory did not let him stay dead. He is honored now as the father of independence, the man who closed the door, the one who understood that *libète* was not a position paper but a fact that had to be defended at maximum cost if necessary.

He understood this because he had paid the cost personally, over decades, before he ever led an army.

## Marie-Jeanne Lamartinière (dates uncertain)

She fought in battle. This is documented. The defense of Crête-à-Pierrot in 1802 — one of the revolution's most celebrated military engagements, in which a Haitian force held a fortification against Leclerc's troops — includes her. She is described as fighting alongside her husband and continuing after he fell wounded. She is one of the women whose presence in the revolution's military campaigns has been systematically underweighted by the masculine historiography of the period.

The tradition knows what she represents: the revolution was not an event that happened to women, or that women witnessed from the margins. Women were in the ceremony at Bois Caïman. Women were in the battles. Women organized the networks of communication and supply that a guerrilla campaign against three European armies requires. Lamartinière is the name the record preserved; she represents the larger population of women whose names it did not.

## Sanité Bélair (c. 1781–1802)

She was a sergeant in Toussaint's army. This is documented — her rank, her service, her capture. She was twenty-one years old when the French arrested her in 1802 as part of the campaign to decapitate the revolution's military leadership in the period following Toussaint's own capture.

She was condemned to be shot. At her execution, she refused the blindfold.

The tradition remembers this refusal as the act that defines her: not her military service, not her rank, but that final refusal to not see the moment of her death. She looked at it. She had spent her adult life in a revolution organized around the claim that the people of Saint-Domingue would not avert their eyes from what was being done to them — that they would see the ledger, see the calculation, see the seven-year math — and act accordingly. At the end, she looked at what was coming and did not flinch.

Her name is on the Arc de Triomphe in Paris, listed among the defenders of French liberty. She was a Haitian revolutionary executed by the French army for fighting for Haitian liberty. The irony is not accidental. It is the record.

## A Composite Figure: A Woman of the North Plain

The following figure is drawn from multiple documented accounts in Carolyn Fick's *The Making of Haiti: The Saint Domingue Revolution from Below* (1990) and is presented as composite rather than as a named individual, in keeping with the project's practice for figures drawn from aggregate evidence.

She was born on a north plain plantation in the 1760s. She knew the bell — the plantation bell that marked the hours of forced labor, that sounded at dawn and did not stop sounding until dark. She knew the names of the lwa because her mother had taught them to her in the hours after the bell. She was at the uprising of 1791 or near it — the records show women in the crowd, women in the burning of the cane fields, women carrying water and children and news.

She survived the years of military campaign — survived yellow fever and the violence of Rochambeau's dogs and the back-and-forth of a war that crossed every road she had ever walked. She was alive on January 1, 1804, in the year her daughter was two years old. She heard that it was over. That there was a name for what they were now: Haitian. That the country had a name that was older than the colony. That it was called Ayiti.

The bell did not ring on January 1, 1804. She noticed that it did not ring.

What she thought about the contract system, about the labor arrangements Toussaint and then Dessalines instituted to keep the coffee and the cane from rotting unharvested — about whether the freedom that had been declared was the freedom she had been fighting for — is not preserved in the record. It is preserved in the history of subsequent decades, in the decisions that ordinary people made about how to live on the land they had freed. They moved away from the plantation rows. They planted subsistence gardens. They chose smallholding over export agriculture. They chose the kind of freedom they understood over the kind the republic's leadership had in mind. This was not ingratitude. It was the assertion of *libète* against a new set of conditions that were trying to put conditions on it.

She had learned, over the course of a life, that *libète* is not given. It is taken. And held.
