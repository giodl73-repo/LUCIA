## Spanish Kingdoms, Iberian History 1250–1516

**Reviewer:** J.N. Hillgarth — historian of the Spanish kingdoms, author of *The Spanish Kingdoms 1250–1516* (2 vols., Oxford University Press, 1976–1978) and *The Mirror of Spain: Catalan Humanists and the Medieval Past* (Michigan, 2000)
**Chapter:** Aragon Era 2 — La Reconquesta Aragonesa
**Stage:** BOARD — Domain Expert Review

---

## Historical Accuracy Assessment

**General assessment:** The chapter is historically accurate in its major claims and appropriately qualified in its areas of scholarly uncertainty. The events covered — the 1229 Majorcan campaign, the Valencia siege of 1238, the mudéjar arrangement, the constitutional framework of pactisme — are accurately described against the available primary and secondary literature.

**The island strategy:** The chapter's characterization of James I's strategic intelligence regarding the western Mediterranean island chain is accurate and supported by Aragonese archive evidence showing the Barcelona merchant community's involvement in identifying the commercial value of Majorcan control before the 1229 campaign. The *Consolat de Mar* is correctly identified as the institutional expression of this commercial empire.

**The Catalan commercial networks:** Accurate. The Barcelona banking families' financing of the Valencia campaign is documented. The characterization of the Crown of Aragon as "a business with a constitution" is pointed but defensible — Hillgarth's own work on Catalan humanists documents the degree to which the commercial culture of Catalonia shaped the Crown's political imagination.

**Zayyan's exile and the Hafsid connection:** The chapter correctly notes that Zayyan made alliances with the Hafsid dynasty after his departure. This is confirmed in Arabic sources. The characterization of him as still calling himself king of Valencia in exile is accurate; the legal fiction of his authority was maintained in the diplomatic correspondence of the period.

**The *Furs de València* (1261):** The chapter notes that the *Furs* were established in 1261. This is correct; the law code was promulgated in 1261 though the juridical foundations were established immediately after the conquest. The chapter handles this correctly.

**Minor factual flag:**
The chapter refers to the Valencia *capitulació* as having been negotiated "in late summer of 1238" — more precisely, the terms were settled in August and the formal entry was in late September (the *Llibre dels Fets* gives September 28, 1238, as the date of James's entry into Valencia). This is not an error but an imprecision; "late summer" is defensible for August negotiations, and the entry in late September could be called early autumn. No correction required, but the chapter could be more specific if precision is preferred.

---

## Context Assessment — Broader Iberian Picture

The chapter correctly situates the Valencia conquest within the framework of the Almohad collapse after Las Navas de Tolosa (1212). This context is important and correctly rendered: the Aragonese advance into Valencia was possible not solely because of Aragonese military strength but because the Almohad defensive capacity had already been broken. The chapter's treatment of Abu Zayd's conversion and alliance illustrates this fragmentation from within the Muslim political world.

One element the chapter might have noted, though not required: the roughly simultaneous Castilian advance into Andalusia — Córdoba fell to Ferdinand III in 1236, Seville in 1248 — which constrained any possible Muslim coalition against the Aragonese in Valencia. The *Reconquista* was not solely an Aragonese enterprise, and Valencia's fall in 1238 was partly the consequence of Castilian pressure in the south preventing any coordinated Muslim response in the north. This is not an error in the chapter — it correctly focuses on the Aragonese dimension — but a broader reader might benefit from knowing that the Valencian Muslim position was also compromised by developments in Castile.

---

## Cultural and Historiographical Representation

The chapter avoids the standard romanticized framing of the *Reconquista* as a simple Christian liberation narrative. It renders the *capitulació* and the mudéjar arrangement as what they were: pragmatic, economically driven, imperfect, and conditional on the Aragonese needing the people they had conquered. This is the correct historiographical position and represents the best recent scholarship on mudéjar communities.

The treatment of Zayyan as a full figure — his family history, his exile, his continued legal claim — is the strongest element from a historiographical perspective. Too much writing about the 1238 conquest renders Zayyan as a door opening. This chapter renders him as a ruler making the best available decision in impossible conditions. This is the accurate picture.

## Required Revisions for FINAL

No required revisions. The chapter is historically sound.

**Optional note:** If precision on the September 28, 1238 entry date is preferred, "late summer" in the capitulació passage could be revised to "August" (for the negotiations) and the entry date confirmed as September 28. Author decides.

---

## Overall Assessment

A chapter that accurately represents one of the most important episodes in medieval Iberian history, with particular strength in its constitutional analysis and its treatment of the conquered population's situation. The Tribunal de las Aguas landing is an elegant and historically defensible choice: the living institution carries the chapter's persistence-mechanism argument without requiring authorial assertion.

Endorsed for publication without required revision.
