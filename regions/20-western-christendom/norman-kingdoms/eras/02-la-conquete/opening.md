# La Conquête — The Conquest
## Norman Kingdoms, Era 2 | 1066–1087 CE

---

The ravens came first.

They were always ahead of the men, following the smell of blood east across the ridge, down into the hollow where the English had made their last stand. William walked behind them in the grey of the fourteenth of October, 1066, and the ground was not ground — it was men. English men, most of them. Some of his own. His horse did not like it and he went on foot.

He had won. He knew this. The king was dead somewhere in the press, shot through the eye or hacked down at the end, the accounts would differ for a century. Harold Godwinson, the last Anglo-Saxon king of England, was dead on the hill called Senlac, and William, Duke of Normandy, was the last man standing with an army behind him.

England was still there.

This was the problem he had not thought through. You ride to a battle and you win or you lose, and losing is simple — you are dead or you run. But winning a battle and winning a country are not the same thing. He had conquered a duchy before: not like this. Normandy was his by inheritance, fought over and eventually settled, its lords familiar, its language his. England was a country of two million people who did not speak his language, who had their own bishops and their own law courts and their own understanding of who should rule them, and the man who had held all that together was lying in the field in front of him.

He stood there in the October light, and the ravens worked.

William was forty-three years old. He had been fighting since he was eight, when his father Robert died on pilgrimage to Jerusalem and left him the duchy and three guardians who were each murdered in succession, and the young duke learned early that survival was its own philosophy. He was broad and heavy now, with a neck that contemporaries called like a bull's, and a temper that men who served him described in careful, neutralizing language — *impetuous*, one would write; *severe*, another. What they meant was that he could be very frightening and that this was sometimes useful and sometimes not.

He understood legitimacy. He had had to. His mother was not his father's wife but a tanner's daughter from Falaise, and the Normans had called him *bastard* to his face before he was old enough to know what it meant and afterward when they thought he could not hear. The word had hardened into something else: a refusal to let his claim rest on what he was born and an insistence that it rest on what he built. He had come to England not as a conqueror merely, but as a king asserting a right — Pope Alexander II had blessed his banner, the Church of Rome was behind him, and Harold, he maintained, had sworn an oath on sacred relics to support William's claim and broken it. He was here to collect a debt.

The debt was collected. Now he had to invent the payment.

He had perhaps eight thousand men, knights and infantry. England had perhaps two million people. The great northern earls — York, Northumbria, Chester — had not come south to fight him. They were watching from behind their walls and their winters, and they owed him nothing. The men who had governed England before he came were still alive, still on their lands, still sending riders south to see what the Norman would do next.

In the weeks that followed, he moved. He did not march on London immediately; he circled, burning the villages of Kent and Essex, and this too was calculation: he wanted the city to feel his approach before it felt his weight. The Normans understood the grammar of fire. A column of smoke visible for twenty miles was a sentence in a language every people in Europe could read. It said: we are here, we can reach you, and we have not reached you yet.

London submitted in December. The great men brought the crown to him at Berkhamsted — the Archbishop of York, the remaining earls, the representatives of the city — and on Christmas Day, 1066, in the newly built church at Westminster, William was crowned King of England. The coronation shout of acclamation was in two languages. The Normans outside the building, hearing the English shout, thought it was a riot and set fire to the houses. William was crowned in a burning city, trembling, according to Orderic Vitalis, throughout the ceremony.

He was right to tremble. The crown was a beginning, not an end. He knew what a Norman duke was; he did not yet know what an English king required him to be. He would spend the next twenty years finding out, and the finding out would be violent — rebellions in the north, rebellions in the east, his own half-brother in revolt, his eldest son in revolt before the end — but it would also be systematic, precise, and in some ways unlike anything England had seen.

The castle came first. Always the castle.

He had brought the idea from Normandy: the motte-and-bailey, a timber tower on a raised earthen mound, ringed by a wooden palisade. His men built them across England the way a language builds grammar — one structure at a time, each one a statement, until the statements accumulated into a system. Within a year of Hastings there were castles at London, at Winchester, at Pevensey, at Dover. Within five years, at York, at Lincoln, at Durham. Within twenty, at Chester, at Carlisle, at the Welsh frontier. The Domesday survey of 1086 does not count them all; it does not need to. By then they were everywhere, and every Norman lord who held land held it beneath a structure of timber or stone that said the same thing the ravens had said over Senlac: we are here, and here is permanent.

England was about to be reorganized from the ground up. The Normans did not know how to do this yet. They were going to learn.
