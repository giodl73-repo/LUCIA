# La Conquista — La Espada y la Cruz
## Era 1: 1492–1550 CE

---

## I. The Form

The notary unrolled the document in the dark.

There was no one to hear it. The village had been empty for three days — the people had gone into the forest when the ships appeared, which was what people did when the ships appeared, because the ships always brought the same thing. Rodrigo de Colmenares, captain, held the torch. The notary read in Spanish, as the law required, into the dark trees and the sound of the sea.

He read that there was one God, and one Pope, and that the Pope had given these lands and their people to the Crown of Castile and Aragon by right of the donation of Alexander VI, which had been made in the year of Our Lord 1493 and was recorded in a papal bull. He read that if the people of these lands did not accept this gift — if they did not come forward, if they did not submit to the Church and to the Crown — then any war made upon them, any death that followed, any enslaving of their persons and their children's persons, would not be the fault of the captain or the soldiers or the Crown of Castile, but their own fault, for having refused the gift.

He read this to the empty air. The notary noted in his record that the Requerimiento had been read. The law was satisfied.

In the morning, the soldiers went into the forest.

The document was called the *Requerimiento* — the Requirement. It had been drafted in 1510 by Juan López de Palacios Rubios, a jurist of the Council of Castile who had studied at Salamanca and Bologna. He wrote it in response to a crisis: a Dominican friar named Antonio de Montesinos had stood in the church at Santo Domingo on the fourth Sunday of Advent in 1511 and told the assembled encomenderos that they were in a state of mortal sin. That they had no right to wage war on innocent people. That they were killing people who had done nothing to deserve it. His voice, he said, was the voice crying in the wilderness.

Palacios Rubios was called upon to solve the problem that Montesinos had named. The problem was theological and legal simultaneously, which in 1510 was the same thing. The Catholic faith held that war required just cause. The Crown of Castile was at war with the people of the Indies, and was conducting that war at great expense and at considerable moral risk to the souls of its soldiers and administrators. It was necessary to establish the just cause.

This was not cynicism. Palacios Rubios believed what he wrote.

The faith that organized the conquest was the same faith that made the conquest's defenders argue, in writing, before the King, about whether what they were doing was right. The Requerimiento transferred culpability: if the people submitted, they would be received with love; if they resisted, the consequences were their own responsibility. The document made conquest conditional and war a just response to refusal.

Bartolomé de las Casas, who had himself been an encomendero before he became a Dominican priest, who had watched the conquest of Cuba with his own eyes, read the Requerimiento and laughed. He could not help it, he wrote later. He laughed because it was so impossible to take seriously. Then he stopped laughing, because it was very serious indeed.

## II. The Third Act

The year 1492 was not one event. It was three.

On January 2, the last Nasrid sultan of Granada, Muhammad XII — called Boabdil by the Spanish — surrendered the Alhambra to Ferdinand and Isabella after seven hundred and eighty years of Muslim presence in the Iberian peninsula. On March 31, the Alhambra Decree expelled every Jew who would not convert to Christianity from the territories of the Crown. On October 12, the ships of Christopher Columbus made landfall in the Caribbean.

These three events were not separate. They were the same event, understood differently. The Reconquista — the re-conquest of the Iberian peninsula from Muslim rule — had been the organizing project of the Spanish Christian kingdoms for seven centuries. It was a holy war. Holy war required theological sanction: the blessing of the Church, the declaration of just cause, the formal offer of peace before the initiation of hostilities. The form had been refined across centuries of fighting. It was the form Palacios Rubios had inherited.

When Isabella stood before the walls of Granada in January 1492, she was completing a project her ancestors had begun in the eighth century. When Columbus arrived in the Caribbean eight months later, the Crown was in the habit of holy war — of conquest that required God's sanction and the Church's blessing and the legal record of having offered terms. The Requerimiento was not invented for the Americas. It was the Reconquista's logic applied to a new theater.

The Americas were the third act. The first act was the Moorish kingdoms. The second was the Jewish communities — expelled for refusing conversion, absent from the Spain that discovered the new world. The third was the people of the Indies, who received the document that had been written for all of them: submit or bear the consequences of your refusal.

## III. Tenochtitlan

In November 1519, Hernán Cortés and his soldiers came down from the mountain pass and saw the city.

Bernal Díaz del Castillo, a soldier who had been on the campaign and wrote its history in old age, remembered the moment clearly. He remembered the soldiers asking whether what they were seeing was a dream. The city sat in the middle of a lake, connected to the mainland by three great causeways. It was the size of Córdoba or Seville, or larger — some said two hundred thousand people, others said more. The great market at Tlatelolco held sixty thousand people on an ordinary day: the flower sellers, the cacao merchants, the scribes painting accounts on amate bark, the judges sitting on raised platforms resolving disputes while runners waited to carry verdicts back to the palace. The air smelled of maize and copal incense, and the sound of a dozen languages mixed in the market like the sound of a river. When the Spanish soldiers first heard the noise, Díaz wrote, they compared it to the roar of a city twice the size of Seville, which is what it was.

This was Tenochtitlan, the capital of the Mexica, founded in 1325 on the islands in the lake that would become Mexico City. The Mexica called themselves the people of the sun. Their cosmology required feeding the sun with the blood of sacrifice — not as cruelty, but as maintenance. The sun had been created through the self-sacrifice of the gods; the gods had given their blood for the world; the world owed a debt that had to be serviced. Without the debt's servicing, the sun would not rise. Cortés's soldiers, watching the flower sellers in Tlatelolco, did not know this. They knew they were looking at something extraordinary. They did not have a category for it.

Moctezuma II, the *tlatoani* — the speaker — of the Mexica, had known the Spanish were coming. He had received reports for two years. The year 1519 was the year One Reed in the Mexica calendar, the year in which the god Quetzalcóatl — the feathered serpent, who had gone east across the sea — was prophesied to return. Moctezuma was not a passive man. He was a sophisticated political actor who had governed the most powerful state in Mesoamerica for seventeen years. He had received Cortés not because he was deceived, but because his cosmological framework required that he manage the arrival with care. He had sent gifts. He had sent envoys. He had tried, repeatedly, to prevent Cortés from reaching Tenochtitlan. He had failed. He now received the Spanish in his city because his own theology demanded that the situation be handled through the appropriate protocols of reception.

Cortés had not come alone, and the story is not simple. His force included tens of thousands of Tlaxcaltec warriors — people who had their own reasons to oppose Mexica dominance, who had fought their own wars against Tenochtitlan's expansion, and who made the eventual siege of the city possible. The fall of Tenochtitlan was not a Spanish defeat of the Mexica. It was a realignment of the politics of Mesoamerica, in which the Spanish were the new force that made other political arrangements newly possible.

The protocols of reception that Cortés was following were the protocols of the Reconquista. He had read the Requerimiento from his ships before landing. He had notified the Mexica that they were required to accept the Pope's authority and the Crown's sovereignty. He had notified them in Spanish, which they did not speak. He had been told they had refused, which they had not, because they had not understood what they were refusing. He proceeded accordingly.

The two men met in the middle of the causeway. Moctezuma wore a headdress of green quetzal feathers and gold, with turquoise and jade. Cortés wore plate armor. Each was operating from a framework entirely incomprehensible to the other. Each understood that the meeting was significant. Each was wrong about what it meant.

## IV. The Debate

Forty years after the notary read the Requerimiento to the empty air, the Crown of Castile convened a formal proceeding to determine whether what its soldiers had done in the Americas was just.

The Valladolid Debate met in 1550 and 1551 in the College of San Gregorio in the city of Valladolid, before a panel of jurists and theologians appointed by Charles V. The question was not whether the conquest had happened. It had happened. The question was whether it had been lawful — whether the indigenous peoples of the Americas could be legitimately subjugated, or whether subjugation required their consent, or whether (as one position held) they were by nature suited to be governed by others and consent was therefore not relevant.

The man arguing that subjugation was lawful was Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda, the most learned Aristotelian scholar in Spain. He invoked the doctrine of natural slavery from the *Politics*: Aristotle had held that some human beings, by nature, were better fitted to obey than to govern, and that their subjugation was for their own benefit. The indigenous peoples of the Americas were, in Sepúlveda's argument, such people. The evidence was their practices: human sacrifice, idolatry, the violation of natural law. War against such people was not merely permissible — it was required. The obligation to bring them into the framework of civilization and Christianity was itself the justification.

The man arguing against subjugation was Bartolomé de las Casas, now seventy-six years old, a bishop without a diocese, who had been making this argument for forty years. He had the *Brevísima relación*, his account of what the Spanish had done, which he had addressed to the Crown Prince fifteen years earlier. He had his theology: the indigenous peoples were fully rational, fully human, possessed of the same souls that every person possessed, created in God's image, entitled to the same rights as any Christian. The conquest was not holy war. It was murder. The souls being lost were not only indigenous; the souls of every Spaniard who had participated were also at risk.

Las Casas read from his account. He read about the island of Hispaniola, which had held, by his estimate, three million people when Columbus arrived. By 1541, there were two hundred. He read about the cacique Hatuey, who had fled from Hispaniola to Cuba warning the people there about what was coming, and had been captured, and had been condemned to death by burning for his resistance. At the stake, a Franciscan friar offered him baptism. Hatuey asked: do Christians go to heaven? The friar said yes. Hatuey said he would rather go to hell than go to a place where he would find such people. He was burned. Las Casas recorded this and drew the theological conclusion: Hatuey had understood Christianity, in practice, correctly. He had looked at what Christians did and made a rational judgment. The judgment damned not Hatuey but the Christianity that had produced this result.

The panel listened. They took notes for weeks. When Sepúlveda had finished and Las Casas had finished, the panel deliberated.

The panel did not verdict.

It was not incompetence. The panel was composed of learned men facing a genuine logical impossibility. If the indigenous peoples had rational souls, the conquest required their consent, which had not been given. The Requerimiento was the performance of consent-seeking — but it had been read in Spanish, in the dark, to empty forests. If they did not have rational souls, then the Requerimiento was unnecessary; you do not require the submission of animals. If the conquest was just war, it required a just cause; but if the just cause was their rational incapacity, then just war was not the correct category, because just war was a category that applied to persons. Either the indigenous peoples were persons, in which case the conquest was unjust; or they were not persons, in which case the framework of just war did not apply and the entire theological-legal enterprise was irrelevant. The panel could not verdict because both possible verdicts undermined the enterprise. It sat with the impossibility. It produced nothing.

The conquest continued.

## V. The New Laws and the Geography of Failure

The New Laws of the Indies were promulgated in 1542. They were the fruit of forty years of Las Casas's argument, the most significant reform legislation the Spanish Crown produced for the Americas, and a direct response to the documented abuses of the encomienda system. They declared that no new encomiendas could be granted. Existing encomiendas would revert to the Crown when the current holder died. The labor draft system that had organized indigenous work in the mines and on the plantations would be restricted and eventually abolished. The indigenous peoples were declared free vassals of the Crown, entitled to the Crown's protection.

The Crown wrote these laws in the palace at Valladolid. The encomienderos read them in Lima.

The man sent to implement the New Laws in Peru was Blasco Núñez Vela, the first viceroy of Peru. He arrived in 1544 and began to enforce the laws immediately, as the laws required. The encomenderos of Peru responded with something between a lawsuit and a rebellion. Gonzalo Pizarro — the brother of Francisco, the conqueror — led the resistance. The encomenderos were not merely wealthy landowners arguing about property rights. They were the men who had conquered Peru. They had risked their lives, spent their fortunes, and lost their health in the service of the Crown. They had been promised the encomiendas as the reward for conquest. The Crown, eight weeks away by ship, was now proposing to take the reward back.

Blasco Núñez Vela was killed in battle in January 1546. He had been viceroy for two years.

The New Laws were suspended.

The distance between Madrid and Lima was not only oceanic. It was administrative, communicative, and temporal. The Crown could write laws. It could appoint viceroys. It could send instructions, decrees, and new laws in response to conditions that had changed by the time the reply arrived. The encomenderos were present. The gap between the law and its enforcement was not moral cowardice. It was eight weeks by ship, and everything that happened in those eight weeks was beyond the Crown's control, and the Crown knew it, and could not solve it, and the empire ran on that gap for three hundred years.

Las Casas was still writing at age eighty-two. He had outlived the New Laws' implementation and witnessed their failure. He had outlived the Valladolid Debate and its non-verdict. He was still arguing, in the language of Catholic moral theology, that what his civilization was doing in the Americas was a sin, that the indigenous peoples were rational beings with souls, that the Crown had an obligation it was not meeting, that the documentation of the atrocity was not the same as its cessation.

He died in 1566, still writing.

## VI. The Letter

Sometime around 1615, a man named Felipe Guaman Poma de Ayala completed a letter to the King of Spain.

He had been writing it for twenty years. It was 1,200 pages long. It was illustrated with nearly four hundred drawings — Guaman Poma was not a trained artist, but his drawings showed, in sequence, the history of the Andean world before the conquest and the conditions of colonial life after it: the Inca kings and queens in their regalia; the Spanish administrators collecting tribute; the indigenous people in the mines; the priests who protected them and the priests who did not; the whole social order of the viceroyalty as Guaman Poma had observed it, from inside, for sixty years.

Guaman Poma was an Andean nobleman, the son of a man who had served as an interpreter for the Spanish and had been given a position in the colonial hierarchy that allowed him to observe both worlds. He had learned Spanish, and learned it well enough to write 1,200 pages of it. He had learned the Spanish legal and theological framework well enough to use it. He addressed his letter to Philip III, King of Spain, and argued, within the framework of Spanish law and Catholic theology, that the indigenous peoples of Peru were legitimate subjects of the Crown, that the administrators and priests responsible for their governance were systematically violating the Crown's own laws, and that the Crown had a legal and moral obligation — an obligation Guaman Poma derived from the same sources Palacios Rubios had used — to intervene.

He addressed this letter to the King because he believed the King did not know. He believed that if the King knew what was being done in his name, the King would stop it. He had spent twenty years gathering testimony, documenting abuses, constructing the legal argument, and writing it down. He had done what Las Casas had done, from the other side: he had learned the framework of the people who held power and used that framework to argue for his people's rights.

He sent the letter to Spain. It arrived. It was received. It was filed.

It was lost.

For three hundred years, the letter sat in the Royal Library of Copenhagen, among manuscripts from other collections, uncatalogued and unread. A Danish scholar found it in 1908. It was published in a facsimile edition in 1936. By then, the empire whose laws it invoked had been gone for a century.

The Requerimiento is in the archive. The New Laws are in the archive. Las Casas's testimony is in the archive. The records of the Valladolid Debate, both sides, are in the archive. The mita labor rolls from Potosí, recording the names of the men who went into the mine and noting which ones did not come back, are in the archive. The Archivo General de Indias in Seville holds eighty million pages of records — every transaction, every trial, every instruction from the Crown, every reply from the viceroyalties, every Requerimiento read and notarized and filed.

Guaman Poma's letter is in the archive now. It says: I have told you what is being done. It says: you have an obligation, within your own framework, to respond. It says: I am asking you directly, as a subject of the Crown, in the language of the Crown, in the law of the Crown, to answer.

The answer is not in the archive. The answer was not given.

What remained was the letter, and the argument it contained, and the man who spent twenty years making it, in the language of the people who held the power, using their own framework to argue for his people's dignity, trusting that the framework meant what it said.

He was not wrong about the framework. He was wrong about what happened when it was applied.
