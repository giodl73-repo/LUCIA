## First Read

I read it straight through and did not look up. That is my first test, and this opening passes it.

The opening sentence does what opening sentences are supposed to do: it drops you into the impossible. "The notary unrolled the document in the dark." Six words that tell you everything — the bureaucracy of it, the performance, the darkness — before the reader knows anything. And then, withheld just long enough: there is no one to hear it. The village has been empty for three days.

That is scene construction at the level I care about. Not a setting, not a summary — a scene. A person performing an act that is absurd and yet legally required. The reader is trapped in the absurdity before they are told to feel anything about it.

## What Works

**The arc is present from the first paragraph.** The opening is not just a vivid moment — it is a structural statement. The notary reads to no one; the law is satisfied; the soldiers go into the forest. Three sentences. An entire logic. The reader understands before the chapter names it that this is a civilization capable of manufacturing justification and recording it carefully.

**Palacios Rubios and Las Casas in the same frame.** The decision to introduce both figures in the opening — the author of the Requerimiento and the priest who laughed at it — is exactly right. Both are inside the same theological framework. The opening does not resolve the tension between them. It presents it and leaves it open. That is the pull.

**The last paragraph.** "Somewhere in the Archivo General de Indias in Seville, the record of that night still exists." This is the landing note for the opening, and it lands. The reader understands: the civilization documented what it did. The documentation is part of the method.

## What to Watch in the Chapter

**The chapter must sustain the inside voice through the violence.** Las Casas's account involves specific, documented atrocities. The *Brevísima relación* is relentless. The temptation at full chapter length will be to either soften (which loses honesty) or to editorialize (which loses the inside voice). The chapter needs to find the way Las Casas would narrate it — as a theologian, not as a modern human rights reporter.

**Cortés and Pizarro are not yet present.** The opening establishes the legal-theological framework but does not introduce the specific conquests. The chapter must anchor in Cortés's Mexico and Pizarro's Peru with the same specificity that the opening anchors in the notary's night. Named moments. Real documents. Not a survey.

**Pacing the transition from Requirement to practice.** The opening establishes the gap between the law and the act. The chapter needs to earn that gap through specific scenes — not by asserting it again, but by showing it in a different register. The Potosí chapter handles the material consequence; this chapter needs to handle the initial violence.

## The Question I'd Push On

**Where is the indigenous voice within the theological framework?** Las Casas is inside the system. Palacios Rubios is inside the system. The people being read to are not. The opening handles this by making their absence the scene itself — the empty village is the indigenous response, rendered without a word. That is powerful. But the chapter must decide: does it stay entirely inside the Spanish theological world, or does it find a way to hold the indigenous perspective without stepping outside the Spanish frame to explain it? I would push the chapter toward finding someone — Guaman Poma de Ayala, or an indigenous account of the Requirement being read — who operated at the intersection of both worlds.
