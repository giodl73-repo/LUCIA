# Jamaica — "Out of Many, One People"

Small island, large idea. Jamaica is 10,990 square kilometers — about the size of Connecticut — and it has given the world reggae, Rastafari, the political philosophy of Marcus Garvey, and a moral vision of liberation whose global reach is disproportionate to any measure of the island's size. This is not an accident. It is the result of a specific historical experience: the furnace of plantation slavery that burned for three hundred years, the emancipation that came in 1838 without land or compensation, the working-class and peasant culture that built something extraordinary in the conditions of poverty and postcolonial struggle that followed. The creativity that emerged from the Kingston yards in the 1960s and 1970s — the ska and rocksteady that became reggae; the sound systems that invented the DJ; the Rastafari theology that saw in the Ethiopian emperor Haile Selassie the fulfillment of Marcus Garvey's prophecy of a Black king — was not despite the hardship. It was through it.

Jamaica's motto — "Out of many, one people" — acknowledges the island's African, European, South Asian, Chinese, Sephardic Jewish, and indigenous Taíno heritage without quite resolving the question of how the many became one or at what cost. The African majority — brought forcibly from West and Central Africa, subjected to one of the most brutal plantation systems in the Caribbean, surviving through marronage (escape to the mountains), obeah (spiritual practice), and the constant maintenance of African cultural elements that the plantation tried to suppress — is the foundation. The Maroon communities of the Cockpit Country, who fought the British to a standstill in the eighteenth century and secured treaty recognition of their independence, are the proof that the foundation was not passive.

- **Port Royal** — the seventeenth-century pirate and privateer capital that sank into the sea in the 1692 earthquake; still partly underwater

## What Defined Them

**Marronage as the founding act of resistance.** The Jamaican Maroons — enslaved Africans who escaped the plantations and established free communities in the island's interior — fought the British colonial forces to a standstill across the eighteenth century and secured, by treaty, recognition of their autonomy. The Leeward Maroon Treaty (1739, with Cudjoe) and the Windward Maroon Treaty (1740, with Queen Nanny) are foundational documents of Jamaican history. Nanny of the Maroons, a legendary military and spiritual leader, is one of Jamaica's seven National Heroes. The Maroon tradition — of resistance, of cultural preservation, of the refusal to accept the terms imposed by power — is the deep root of Jamaican culture.

**Rastafari and the vision of Africa.** Rastafari emerged in Kingston in the early 1930s, drawing on Marcus Garvey's Pan-African philosophy and the coronation of Haile Selassie I as Emperor of Ethiopia in 1930. Selassie, in the Rastafari reading, fulfilled Garvey's prophecy of a Black king who would lead the African diaspora to liberation. The Rastafari theology is at once spiritual and political: Babylon (the system of white supremacy and capitalist exploitation) is the enemy; Africa (specifically Ethiopia) is the promised land; the reggae musicians who carried this theology to the world — Bob Marley above all — are its prophets. The dreadlocks, the ital food, the reasoning sessions, the use of cannabis as a sacrament — all are expressions of a complete spiritual and political philosophy that emerged from the poorest communities of Kingston.

**Reggae as resistance philosophy.** Reggae is not primarily dance music. It is, as Marley put it, "soul music" in the most literal sense — music about the soul's condition under Babylon and the soul's aspiration to freedom. The music that developed from the Kingston sound systems through ska and rocksteady into reggae in the late 1960s encoded a complete political philosophy in rhythm: the offbeat emphasis (the "skank") that inverts the Western rhythmic hierarchy; the bass as the primary melodic instrument; the lyrics that addressed poverty, injustice, spiritual liberation, and love simultaneously. Bob Marley's global popularity was not a triumph of marketing. It was a recognition by people across the world that this music was speaking directly to their experience of injustice and aspiration.

**The garrison and the limits of independence.** The political history of independent Jamaica is inseparable from the garrison communities — neighborhoods in Kingston where the two major political parties (the PNP and the JLP) built client relationships with local strongmen who delivered votes and received patronage. By the 1970s, these strongmen had become armed dons with significant political power, and the violence between garrison communities aligned with competing parties had turned parts of Kingston into war zones. The garrison system is not a pathology external to Jamaican democracy; it is the product of a democratic system built on patronage in the context of poverty and underdevelopment. It is the shadow side of independence.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Sugar and the Sea:** A plantation on the north coast of Jamaica in 1750: the enslaved workers cut cane twelve hours a day in the tropical heat. The ratio of enslaved to free is nine to one. The planters calculate that the island's productivity requires constant replacement of those who die. The calculation is the moral foundation of an entire economic system.

**2 — The Maroon Wars:** In the Cockpit Country, Nanny of the Maroons is organizing the Windward Maroons against the British. The British cannot penetrate the karst limestone labyrinth of the Cockpit Country, cannot adapt their formation warfare to guerrilla terrain, and eventually negotiate. The treaty of 1740 gives the Maroons their freedom. The cost is an obligation to return escaped slaves — a clause the Maroons have always contested and sometimes simply ignored.

**3 — The Baptist War:** December 27, 1831: a Baptist deacon named Samuel Sharpe leads what he intends as a passive strike — enslaved workers refusing to return to the fields after Christmas. The planters' response turns it into an armed uprising. Sharpe is hanged. The rebellion terrifies the British Parliament into the Emancipation Act of 1833. Freedom comes in 1838. The land does not.

**4 — Marcus Garvey:** In 1914, Marcus Garvey founds the Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA) in Kingston. By 1920, the UNIA has six million members across the Americas, Africa, and beyond. Garvey's message — Black pride, economic self-sufficiency, and the return to Africa as spiritual and political horizon — is the most influential Black political program of the twentieth century's first half. He is deported from the United States on a contested mail fraud conviction. He dies in London in 1940. He does not live to see the coronation of Haile Selassie inspire the movement his prophecy helped create.

**5 — Trench Town:** 1963: in a government yard in Trench Town, Kingston, three young men from the hills and the yards are playing music. Bob Marley, Peter Tosh, and Bunny Wailer are developing a sound that will carry the theology of Rastafari, the politics of the Black Power movement, and the rhythm of Kingston's streets to every inhabited continent on Earth. The music they are making is not yet reggae. It will be.

**6 — The Reparations Argument:** CARICOM's 2013 call for reparations from Britain, France, the Netherlands, and other European states for the slave trade is led in part by Jamaica. Britain's response is to offer development aid — infrastructure, not reparations. The distinction is precise and contested. The slave trade generated wealth that built the British economy; the calculation of what is owed is not abstract. It is arithmetic.
