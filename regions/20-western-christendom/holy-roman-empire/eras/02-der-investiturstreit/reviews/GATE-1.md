**Notes:**

The opening achieves what very few do: it makes the political theological and the theological political, simultaneously, without explaining either. The reader understands why bishops matter (they hold literate administration) and why excommunication matters (it severs the soul from God and the king from his grounds of authority) before either is explained — because the narrative inhabits a world where these are simply true.

The Canossa scene is well-worn in Western historiography, but this opening earns its approach: it does not treat the image as iconic but excavates the mechanics. Why three days? Because canon law required contrition before absolution, and duration is the only visible measure of sincerity. Why barefoot? Because the penitent's body performs the soul's submission. The text makes the reader understand these things from inside.

Zero em-dashes is not a technical achievement but a voice achievement. The legal-public register generates its own pauses.

One note for the chapter: the opening promises that the question "will outlast the empire." The chapter must fulfill this promise — not just narrate 1024–1122, but show how the Concordat of Worms produces a settlement that is not a settlement. The structural engine must be: the investiture argument as permanently irresolvable, and every apparent resolution as a new opening of the same wound.
