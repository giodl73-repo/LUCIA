# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Wolof / Era 2: The First Atlantic Contact
**Round:** 1 (Opening review)

## What Works

The opening line is perfect. "The horses came off the ship wrong." Five words, and the reader is there. The scene has a physical anchor before any context arrives, and the strangeness of the moment (horses from the ocean) is felt before it is explained. This is how you begin.

The structural pivot, "The Wolof knew more," is excellent. It flips the power dynamic in three words and earns the reader's trust that this story will not follow the expected script. The reader who picked this up expecting the standard European-arrival narrative has just been told, firmly and without fuss, that the Wolof are the protagonists.

The final paragraph is strong. The repetition of "would be made again and again" gives the closing genuine pull. The reader wants to know how the balance shifts, and the question is earned by the political geometry the opening has built.

## What Needs Work

**The middle section sags.** Between "The Wolof knew more" and the Burba Jolof's decision to send men, there is a passage that surveys the Wolof trade network and the Portuguese learning curve. This is necessary information, but it reads like a competent paragraph of context rather than a scene. Where is the body? Who is speaking? The Portuguese chroniclers are invoked abstractly ("the papal bulls imagined") rather than through a specific person. Give me a person at this moment, or anchor the passage in a physical action.

**The Burba Jolof needs a scene.** "He sent men to see" is a good plain statement, but the Burba Jolof arrives as a title, not a person. I do not need his interior life, but I need one physical detail. Where is he when the message arrives? What does the court look like? A single sensory anchor would transform this passage from report to narrative.

**The ambassadors-to-Lisbon moment is told, not shown.** "Wolof ambassadors had traveled to Lisbon, had been received at the Portuguese court" is a summary of what should be the opening's most dramatic moment. These are Wolof nobles in a European court. Give this a beat. Even one sentence of scene (what they saw, what they wore, how the Portuguese court reacted) would earn the significance the passage claims.

## The Question I'd Push On

The opening establishes the calculation (horses for captives) and the axis shift (north-south to east-west). But where is the human cost in this calculation? Not as a moral judgment, but as a narrative fact. Someone was traded for those horses. I do not need the opening to dwell on this, but if the chapter is going to track this exchange across a hundred years, the human weight of the transaction should be present from the beginning, even if only in a single sentence. Not sentiment. Not judgment. Just the weight.
