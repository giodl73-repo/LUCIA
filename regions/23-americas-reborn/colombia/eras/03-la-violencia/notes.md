## 2. Key Figures

### Gaitán, Jorge Eliécer (1903–1948)
Liberal party leader and presidential candidate. Law degree from Rome. Built a mass movement — gaitanismo — outside the traditional party structure, which is why the oligarchy on both sides feared him. His assassination was not simply a Liberal/Conservative act; it was a class act — the elimination of the man who threatened the closed world of the país político. His voice is documented in recordings. His "Oración por la Paz" is one of the great documents of Colombian political life.

### A Campesino Figure — NEEDED
**The chapter's central gap, identified by Tuchman, Achebe, Davis, and Kapuściński.** The opening is Bogotá. The chapter must deliver a figure from the interior. Options from the scholarly record:
- **"El Cóndor"** (Efraín González) — a bandolero from the coffee zone who began as a partisan killer and became something more complex: a regional power in his own right, with codes of conduct and a following that crossed party lines. Sánchez and Meertens document him in detail. He is the figure who shows how La Violencia morphed from partisan conflict into social banditry.
- **A composite campesino figure** — drawn from the CNMH testimonial archive; a Liberal family in a Conservative municipality or vice versa. Explicitly composite in figures.md. This preserves specificity without inventing a named individual.

Recommendation: use both — the bandolero as the chapter's structural hinge, the composite campesino as the intimate ground-level experience.

### García Márquez, Gabriel (1927–2014)
Born Aracataca, Caribbean coast. Twenty years old at the Bogotazo; watched from a boarding house window. His grandmother's narration of miracles as weather events is the model for the register. His columns in *El Heraldo* (Barranquilla, 1950s) covered La Violencia from the Caribbean periphery — a different register than Bogotá's. *Cien Años de Soledad* (1967) encodes the entire Liberal-Conservative cycle in the Buendía family wars.

### Rojas Pinilla, Gustavo (1900–1975)
The general who ended the first phase. His 1953 coup is the pivot of the era: the military as the only institution with enough authority to halt the partisan machinery. His amnesty of 1953–1954 worked, briefly. Why it stopped working — why the violence resumed after 1954 — is the chapter's terminal-condition problem.

## 3. Narrative Arc & Structural Engine

### The Structural Engine
**La Violencia's engine is the inheritance mechanism of partisan identity combined with state absence in the periphery.**

The mechanism: Colombian political identity was not ideological but ancestral — Liberal or Conservative was written into villages the way surnames are written into families. When the state retreated from the periphery (which it did structurally — the Colombian state was strong in the cities and absent in the countryside), this ancestral identity became the only available organizing principle for local power. Violence was not the exception to the political order; it WAS the political order at the local level, because no other mechanism of adjudication existed.

Mechanism specifics:
1. **Concentrated land tenure** — the hacienda system left a large landless peasantry with no economic independence, therefore total vulnerability to landlord/patron political dictates.
2. **Coffee boom asymmetry** — the coffee zones (Antioquia, Caldas, Risaralda, Tolima) had some smallholder ownership, which made violence more lethal there, not less: when small property-holders align with party, defending property becomes defending party becomes killing neighbors.
3. **The pájaros system** — Conservative elites hired professional killers (pájaros, "birds") to systematically kill Liberal leaders in specific municipalities, consolidating political control through targeted assassination rather than mass war. This is not chaos; it is a machinery.
4. **Liberal guerrillas** — responding with their own armed groups, which over time transformed from partisan defense into autonomous banditry as the conflict's origins became less relevant than the conflict's own logic.

### The Terminal Condition
**Legitimacy gap meeting institutional exhaustion.**

La Violencia ended not through victory but through exhaustion and institutional substitution. The military coup of 1953 (Rojas Pinilla) worked briefly because neither party had enough legitimacy left to stop the killing through political means. But Rojas Pinilla's own legitimacy was borrowed — from the army's institutional authority — and when he tried to convert borrowed authority into permanent power, the parties reasserted themselves. The National Front (1958), the bipartisan power-sharing agreement, was the terminal condition's institutional resolution: the two parties agreed to take turns governing for sixteen years, explicitly acknowledging that they could not govern separately without killing each other. The agreement was a recognition that the engine had consumed its own fuel.

This is a **legitimacy gap** terminal condition (v1.5 framework): neither Liberal nor Conservative military authority could generate the political legitimacy required to end partisan violence, so the parties agreed to suspend genuine competition in order to stop the killing. The engine ends not with one side winning but with both sides agreeing that the game's rules were lethal.

## 4. Section Plan (~5000 words)

**Section 1 — El Bogotazo (integrated opening, ~800w)**
The assassination, the riots, the city burning. Castro watching. García Márquez at the boarding house window. The ten-minute / ten-year compression. The opening as written integrates here.

**Section 2 — La Máquina (the machine, ~800w)**
How the violence actually worked. Not the Bogotazo but what followed it into the countryside. The pájaros system. The Conservative municipality apparatus: the alcalde, the police, the curas. The landlord who is also the party boss who is also the man who controls the credit. The violence is not random; it is structural. We meet a specific municipality — suggest Líbano, Tolima, well-documented by Sánchez and Meertens — in its specific configuration of local power.

**Section 3 — La Tierra (the land question, ~700w)**
Why the coffee zones burned hottest. The relationship between small property ownership and partisan violence: when you own your land, defending it is also defending your party affiliation. The campesino experience — through the composite figure — of watching neighbors you have known your whole life become threat or ally purely on the basis of inherited color. The asymmetry: what it meant to be Liberal in a Conservative zone vs. Conservative in a Liberal zone.

**Section 4 — El Bandolero (the bandit, ~800w)**
"El Cóndor" or another documented bandolero figure. The transformation of partisan violence into social banditry: the moment when the killers stopped being party agents and became their own thing. The codes of conduct — some bandoleros had rules about who could be killed and who could not. The way the communities related to bandoleros — sometimes as protectors, sometimes as predators. This is the section where the engine's own logic becomes visible: the violence has become self-sustaining.

**Section 5 — La Gaitanada (the promise and its absence, ~500w)**
A brief section on what Gaitán had actually proposed — his economic program, his vision of the país nacional. Not to mourn him sentimentally but to show what the engine eliminated. The pájaros killed not only people but the possibility of a political alternative that might have broken the partisan inheritance cycle. This is the counter-factual as texture.

**Section 6 — El General y el Acuerdo (the coup and the pact, ~600w)**
Rojas Pinilla's 1953 coup. Why it worked briefly — the amnesty of the guerrillas, the return of some displaced families. Why it stopped working — Rojas Pinilla's own authoritarian consolidation, the parties' reassertion. The National Front negotiated at Sitges (1957): the extraordinary agreement between Liberal and Conservative elites to share power for sixteen years. The engine's terminal condition: both sides acknowledged the rules were lethal and agreed to change them.

**Landing (~400w)**
The National Front is signed. The killing stops, mostly. But what has not been settled: the land question, the state absence in the periphery, the bandolero successor organizations. García Márquez publishes *El Coronel No Tiene Quien Le Escriba* in 1961 — the colonel who waits for his pension from a civil war that ended years ago, who waits every Friday for a letter that never comes. The persistence mechanism: the unresolved continues in the form of people waiting. The FARC will emerge from exactly the zones where La Violencia was most intense. The National Front agreement closes the era; it does not close the conditions that produced it. The culture's own persistence: the waiting that outlasts the agreement.

## 6. Voice

**Register: Discover (Magical-Realism-as-Realism)**

This is the voice confirmed for the opening. García Márquez's universe as actual description: matter-of-fact about the extraordinary, accumulative, without explanation. The fantastic and the murderous sitting side by side without either canceling the other.

The voice is not García Márquez's literary voice. It is the voice of the world he was trying to describe. The grandmother narrating miracles with the same tone as weather reports — that is the model. La Violencia was experienced by its survivors not as an exceptional catastrophe but as a condition, like altitude. The voice must hold this.

Pause mechanism: the period. Short declarative stops, no em-dashes, no parenthetical interruption. The sentences accumulate the way the violence accumulated: one thing and then another and then another.

**Em-dash limit:** 0 target / 8 maximum. Opening achieved 0. Chapter must hold this.

## 7. Open Questions

1. **Juan Roa Sierra** — Gaitán's assassin. His motives remain disputed (was he a lone actor? hired? by whom?). The opening treats him as a fact, not a mystery. The chapter should honor this ambiguity without making it the chapter's focus. One sentence acknowledging the dispute.

2. **The Bogotazo's geography outside Bogotá** — the riots spread to other Colombian cities (Cali, Barrancabermeja). The chapter currently focuses on Bogotá. Whether to include one moment from another city's experience in Section 1.

3. **Women in La Violencia** — the scholarly record includes documentation of sexual violence as a weapon of partisan politics (documented in the CNMH reports). María Victoria Uribe's work is essential here. The chapter should not elide this dimension; it should find the right register for including it without making it sensational.

4. **The National Front's cost** — the agreement that ended La Violencia also closed Colombian democracy to third parties for sixteen years. The FARC (founded 1964) argued that the National Front made armed insurgency the only alternative to the closed political system. The landing section must acknowledge this without turning it into a verdict on the National Front.
