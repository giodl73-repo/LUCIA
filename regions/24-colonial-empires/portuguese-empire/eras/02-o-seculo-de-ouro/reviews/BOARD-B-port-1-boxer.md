## Domain Assessment

### Historical Accuracy

The chapter's core account of the Estado da Índia is historically accurate and reflects current scholarship.

**Albuquerque's campaigns:** The chapter correctly places Goa's fall in November 1510 (second assault, after the first was repelled), Malacca in 1511, and Hormuz in 1515. The characterization of Albuquerque's death in the roadstead off Goa, having been informed of his replacement, is accurate and well-sourced in the primary accounts (Gaspar Correia, João de Barros).

**The "hand on the throat of Venice" formulation:** This is from Albuquerque's letters to King Manuel, and it is correctly attributed. The chapter captures the dual commercial-religious logic of Albuquerque's strategy accurately.

**The Carreira da Índia mortality:** The chapter's statement that mortality was "one in three" over a ten-year period is consistent with Boxer's research. Boxer calculated mortality on the India run at approximately 25-30% of men who sailed, factoring in all causes. The chapter's formulation is accurate.

**Fernão Mendes Pinto:** The chapter correctly identifies the *Peregrinação* as written around 1578 and published posthumously in 1614. The characterization of its reliability issues is accurate: Pinto's China accounts are demonstrably embellished, his Japan accounts are more reliable, and his general reliability as a factual record is questioned while his value as an experiential witness is genuine. The Portuguese proverb "Fernão, mentes?" is accurate.

**Alcácer Quibir:** The chapter correctly gives the battle as August 1578, with approximately 15,000 men in Sebastião's force and approximately 8,000 Portuguese dead. The three-kings characterization (Moroccan name: Battle of the Three Kings) is accurate.

### Factual Corrections Required

**Da Gama's mortality numbers on the first voyage:** The chapter states 55 of 170 men returned. Current scholarship puts the numbers at approximately 55 survivors out of around 170 who sailed, which is correct. However, the fleet on the first voyage comprised four ships with approximately 170 men total, not including the supply ship that was scuttled at the Cape on the return. The chapter's numbers are accurate.

**The Zamorin:** The chapter uses "Zamorin" throughout, which is the Portuguese corruption of "Samutiri" (lord of the sea). This is historically appropriate — the Portuguese used this term, and the chapter is written from the empire's self-understanding, so the Portuguese rendering is correct in context.

**Minor:** Camões's birth date (c. 1524) is disputed; some scholars place it as early as 1517. The "c. 1524" convention in the figures is acceptable as it follows the consensus used in most recent scholarship.

### Cultural Authenticity

The chapter's handling of the Lusotropicalism question is historically sophisticated. The myth's emergence in the 1930s under Salazar is correctly placed; the chapter does not anachronistically apply it to the Golden Century itself but reserves it for the retrospective treatment in Section IV. This is the correct chronological placement.

The casado composite figure is accurate to the social history. Boxer's *The Portuguese Seaborne Empire* provides the basis for the characterization given here, and the chapter does not overstate the casado's either positive (cultural mixing) or negative (private trade, monopoly violation) dimensions.

### Verdict

**APPROVED.** No factual corrections required. Two minor notes logged above (Samutiri/Zamorin convention, Camões birth date dispute) — neither requires revision.
