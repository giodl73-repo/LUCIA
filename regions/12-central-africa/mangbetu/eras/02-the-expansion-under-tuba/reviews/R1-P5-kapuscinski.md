# PANEL-1 — P-5: Ryszard Kapuściński

**Chapter:** Mangbetu — Era 2: The Expansion Under Tuba
**Round:** 1
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

I can feel where I am. That is the test and it passes.

The room has heat — the Uele valley's humidity, the "heavy and wet" air of the forest-edge that comes through the analogy to wax being worked. The room has sound — the pluriarc filling the chamber the way incense smoke fills it. The room has smell — I infer the incense from the smoke comparison, and the woven palm-leaf panels carry their own smell if you have been in spaces like this. The room has texture — the ivory pins, the packed clay walls, the residue blackening the craftsman's fingers. The room has spatial logic — the long hall, the seat at the center, the precise distances. I am placed.

Nasakara's body is specific. "The long oval rising from the back of the neck, the gentle taper at the crown." This is not vague description. The author has looked at images of the Mangbetu aristocratic form and has rendered it with the precision of a sculptor. I can see this woman. Her posture — "held still" — gives her a physical discipline that the reader feels.

The musician's body is present. His hands are doing different things. His body moves "almost imperceptibly." His eyes are closed. He is in the room.

**What works:**

The Uele River outside is a brief but essential ground. The river moving "in its slow, permanent way, indifferent to what the humans along its banks had built" does real work: it establishes the landscape as a force that exists regardless of the court's ceremony, which is exactly right for a forest environment where the river is the territory's organizing fact. I would have liked one more physical detail of the exterior — the sound of the water, the smell of the forest — but the gesture is there.

The incense mention is sensory gold. Incense burning in a hall is simultaneously smell and sight (the smoke) and time (it burns slowly, marking the passage of ceremony). In a single word the author has grounded the scene in three registers.

**The question I'd push on:**

The opening is almost entirely interior. The chapter will need to take us outside — to the avenues of palms, to the scale of the Mangbetu capital as a physical place, to the sound and smell of the surrounding settlement. What did the capital sound like when the ceremony was complete and the hall was emptied? What did the forest on the edge of the cleared land look like, smell like, sound like? What did it feel like to walk the avenue toward the hall for the first time?

The expansion under Tuba — territorial growth, new populations incorporated — must be grounded physically. Expansion is not an abstraction. It is people moving through terrain, armies crossing particular rivers, new foods and materials arriving from particular directions. I want to feel the geography of the Uele valley not just as backdrop but as determining force. The chapter should address this.

Also: the great hall's scale. "Two hundred paces in length" is a measurement, not a sensation. I want to know what it felt like to be inside that building — the quality of the light through whatever openings it had, the acoustic effect of that space on the pluriarc's sound, the way the decorated palm-leaf panels moved or did not move in the air of a forest environment. This is achievable from the available record and from genuine immaginative engagement with the materials.
