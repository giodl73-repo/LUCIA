# Be'ena'a — "The Cloud People"

They called themselves the Be'ena'a — the Cloud People — because they lived on the mountain above the clouds. Monte Albán (Dani Baan in Zapotec, "the Sacred Mountain") rose above the three converging arms of the Oaxaca Valley and the Zapotec built their city on its summit not despite the wind and the thin air but because of it: a mountain top is the place closest to the forces that govern the rain, the harvest, and the fate of the dead. Their city was not hidden from the gods. It was as close to the gods as the living could build.

For a thousand years, Monte Albán was both a political capital and a place of the dead — the Zapotec did not separate these categories. The urns placed in the tombs beneath the city's plazas were shaped as deities and rulers alike, and the line between the two was deliberate and thin. The first writing in the Americas that we can recognize as a formal system appeared on the carved stones at Monte Albán's base, the danzantes — figures that likely represent defeated lords from neighboring valleys, their names and capture dates written in glyphs that the Zapotec scribes were still refining. Writing began, here, as the record of conquest and the memory of the dead.

Key places:
- **Dani Baan (Monte Albán)** — the sacred mountain capital; occupied c. 500 BCE–700 CE at monumental scale; tombs beneath every major building
- **Yagul** — major valley center, post-Monte Albán prominence
- **Lambityeco** — late Classic center in the Tlacolula arm; notable for carved ancestral portraits
- **Mitla (Lyobaa)** — "Place of Rest"; the post-Classic sacred center; burial site of the high priests; famous for its mosaic stonework

## What Defined Them

**The mountain as the world's center.** To build on Monte Albán's summit was a declaration that this place stood above the ordinary world. The wind that swept the summit was not inconvenience; it was closeness to the forces the city was built to address. Every major structure on the hilltop was oriented to celestial events — solstices, the zenith passage of the sun — because the mountain was an observatory as much as a city, and the Zapotec priests who watched the sky from it were reading the schedule by which the rains would come and the maize would grow.

**The dead beneath the living.** In most of the world's traditions, the living and the dead are separated. In the Zapotec world, they shared the same ground. The tombs beneath Monte Albán's buildings were not sealed away; they were accessible, periodically reopened, the vessels of ashes and offerings refreshed. The funerary urns — those extraordinary ceramic containers shaped as deity-rulers — were not simply grave goods. They were presences: the ancestor in the form of the sacred, the sacred in the form of the ancestor, placed in the earth beneath the city to hold the world upright from below.

**The first writing in service of the state.** The danzante carvings at Monte Albán's base are among the oldest writing in the Americas. They record, most scholars now believe, the names and capture of defeated rival lords — a calendar date and a name glyph carved beside a body shown in the sprawled posture of the conquered. Writing began here not as literature or accounting but as political memory: this person, on this date, submitted to the mountain. The Zapotec developed this system across centuries into one of the most sophisticated writing traditions in Mesoamerica, used for dynastic records, ritual calendars, and the fates of the dead.

**Ceramic as the art of transformation.** The funerary urn is the Zapotec art form that speaks most clearly across time. In these vessels — some showing the rain deity Cocijo in his lightning-bolt and cloud headdress, others showing long-eared maize deities, others showing ruling figures whose identity blurred into the divine — the Zapotec articulated their understanding of power: it moves through transformation. The ruler becomes the ancestor becomes the deity becomes the rain becomes the maize. The ceramic urn held that chain of transformation in fired clay.

**The valley as a managed world.** Monte Albán's power rested not on military dominance alone but on water. The Etla arm of the Oaxaca Valley was one of the most agriculturally productive zones in highland Mexico, and the Zapotec managed it through canal irrigation, terraced hillside farming, and the political subordination of the valley's smaller centers. Their dominance was hydraulic before it was political — those who managed the water managed the food, and those who managed the food managed everyone else.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Founding on the Mountain:** The valley has three armed rivals. One group climbs to the hill between them and begins to build. The danzante carvings mark who has already been defeated. This is how a city announces itself.

**2 — The Time of the Urns:** In the tomb beneath the plaza, the potter shapes Cocijo's lightning-bolt headdress from the clay. The urn will go into the ground with the lord whose fate it records. The dead are not going anywhere — they are staying precisely where they lived.

**3 — The Age of Teotihuacan:** Zapotec merchants live in a walled quarter in the City of the Gods, far to the north. Teotihuacan merchants live in a quarter at Monte Albán. Both cities are studying each other, carefully, across a distance they are choosing to maintain.

**4 — The Late Flowering:** Monte Albán's administrative reach is shrinking. But in the valley's secondary centers, the funerary urns are more elaborate than ever. The political center is weakening; the sacred tradition it carried is not.

**5 — The Valley's Own Time:** The great city on the summit has been let go — a sacred site, still, but no longer a capital. At Mitla, the mosaic-stone temples rise. The cloud people are still on their mountains. The Spanish will find them there.
