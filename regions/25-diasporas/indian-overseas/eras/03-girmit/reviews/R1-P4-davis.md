## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of Indian Overseas Era 3 Opening

**Overall:** The opening treats the girmitiya's knowledge system with the seriousness it deserves. The Ramcharitmanas is not an artifact; it is a technology of survival. This is the right frame.

**What works:**
- The Ramcharitmanas as portable homeland. The parallel with the Jewish diaspora's Torah is not stated but is structurally present: a text carried in the body, reconstituting a world in a place that has no physical connection to the original landscape. This is a knowledge system operating at the highest level of cultural technology: not literacy (Dulari cannot read the text), but embodied oral knowledge that can reconstruct a sacred geography anywhere.
- The brass lota and the Hanuman murti are not decorative details. They are the material culture of a portable religious practice. The lota is for ritual washing. The murti is for prayer. These are the minimum viable kit for maintaining dharmic practice in exile, and the opening knows this without explaining it.
- The failed monsoon is presented as a natural event, not a metaphor. This is important. The girmitiya's world is an agricultural world, and the agricultural world is a world where rain determines everything. The opening understands that the push factor is not abstract poverty but specific agricultural failure.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening describes a North Indian, Hindi/Bhojpuri experience. Approximately one-third of the indentured came from Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, and other South Indian regions. Their experience was different: different language, different religious tradition (more Shaivite, different caste configurations), different recruitment networks. The chapter must acknowledge this diversity or it will present the girmitiya as monolithically North Indian.
- The sensory world of the ship is absent from the opening, which is appropriate for the scene's scope. But the chapter must render the kala pani (the black water, the ocean crossing) as the girmitiya experienced it. For a Hindu, crossing the ocean was not merely a journey; it was a caste violation, a loss of ritual purity that could not be recovered. The terror of the kala pani was spiritual as much as physical. This must be shown, not stated.

**The question I'd push on:**
The girmitiya carried more than texts and objects. They carried agricultural knowledge, medical knowledge (especially around childbirth and herbal medicine), cooking techniques, and social structures (the jahaji-bhai, the ship-brother system). The chapter must show these knowledge systems operating, not merely name them. How does Bhojpuri folk medicine work on a plantation in Trinidad? How does the jajmani system reconstruct itself when there are no Brahmins? What happens to caste when the ship puts everyone in the same hold?
