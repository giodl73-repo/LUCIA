# Somali Sultanates — "The Horn's Command of the Crossings"

The Horn of Africa is the blade that cuts into the Indian Ocean between the Gulf of Aden and the Arabian Sea, and the Somali-speaking peoples who have lived there for millennia understood their geography as a commercial and military asset. The Gulf of Aden is one of the world's most strategic waterways — the passage between the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean, the route through which incense, slaves, ivory, and later oil have always moved. The Somali sultanates — Ifat, Adal, Ajuran, Majeerteen, Hobyo — controlled different portions of this geography at different times, and their power derived partly from this geographic position and partly from the Islamic commercial networks that the Horn sat at the center of.

The Ajuran Sultanate (c. 1200–1620 CE) was the most powerful of the Somali polities in the medieval and early modern period — a hydraulic empire that built wells and cisterns across the semi-arid interior and taxed the water access that made pastoral and agricultural life possible in an extremely arid environment. The Adal Sultanate (c. 1415–1559 CE) launched the most serious military challenge to the Ethiopian Solomonic empire that it ever faced: Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi, known as Gran ("the left-handed"), led a jihad that temporarily conquered most of the Ethiopian highlands in the 1530s–1540s and was only reversed with Portuguese military assistance. The Somali contribution to the history of the Horn is not simply the story of states that rose and fell; it is the story of a pastoral, maritime, and commercial culture that maintained its distinctiveness across centuries of engagement with the Islamic world, the Ethiopian highlands, and the Indian Ocean trade system.

## What Defined Them

**The Gulf of Aden as strategic resource.** The Somali command of the Gulf of Aden crossing — the passage between the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean — gave the Horn's political powers a structural commercial advantage. Every ship passing between the Mediterranean world and the Indian Ocean trade system had to cross the Gulf of Aden; the ports of Zeila, Berbera, and later Mogadishu were the service stations of this crossing, providing water, food, repairs, and trade goods. The Somali sultanates that controlled these ports were not peripheral powers; they were nodes in one of the world's most important commercial highways.

**The Ajuran hydraulic state.** The Ajuran Sultanate's distinctive form of power was the control of water in an arid environment. The Ajuran rulers built wells, cisterns, and water-management systems across the Somali interior and taxed access to them. In a land where water is the primary limiting factor on pastoral and agricultural life, the entity that controls the water controls the economy. The Ajuran hydraulic state is a distinctive African solution to the problem of governing a nomadic and semi-nomadic pastoral population: don't try to control the people's movement; control the water and the people will come to you. The engineering infrastructure of wells and cisterns that the Ajuran built is still partially visible in the Somali landscape.

**Ahmad Gran and the jihad.** Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi — Gran — was not simply a military commander. He was the leader of a religious reform movement that sought to establish Islamic governance across the Horn and to end what he saw as the Ethiopian Christian empire's domination of Muslim populations in the highlands and lowlands. His military campaigns of the 1520s–1540s, supported by Ottoman firearms and Ottoman military advisors, came close to destroying the Ethiopian state entirely. The Portuguese intervention that reversed them — a force of 400 musketeers under Cristóvão da Gama, the son of Vasco da Gama — changed the balance of military technology on the Horn in ways that had long-term consequences for all parties. Gran was killed in battle in 1543, and the jihad collapsed, but the political landscape of the Horn was permanently reshaped.

**The xeer system and Somali governance.** The Somali xeer — the customary law that governs relationships between clans, the resolution of disputes, and the obligations of pastoral hospitality — is one of the most sophisticated bodies of customary law in Africa. It developed without a central state authority and has been maintained by clan elders across centuries of pastoral migration and political change. The xeer covers everything from blood compensation (diya) for deaths to grazing rights to marriage obligations. It is not written law; it is oral, maintained by specialists and adjudicated by elder consensus. Its resilience — it has functioned even during the periods of state collapse that Somalia experienced in the late twentieth century — testifies to the depth of its social roots.

**Harar as sacred city.** The city of Harar — walled, labyrinthine, the center of a distinct culture — is one of the holiest cities in Islam outside the Arabian Peninsula, with 99 mosques within its old walls. It was the capital of the Adal successor emirate for centuries and maintained its distinctive Harari culture and language (Harari, also called Adare) despite the pressures of surrounding Oromo and Amhara populations. Harar was the destination of the first European to enter it — the British explorer Richard Burton, who disguised himself as a Muslim to enter in 1854 — and its distinctiveness has been maintained to the present.

## The Chapters

**1 — Zeila Port:** A merchant from Arabia is unloading at Zeila. Behind him, the docks are stacked with ivory from the interior. Somali traders are setting prices. The Gulf crossing is three days in good wind. He has done this twenty times.

**2 — The Ajuran Well:** A pastor has come three days to reach this well. The Ajuran tax collector is there before him. He pays in cattle. The well is deep and clean and the water is cold. He thinks the price is worth it. His children will come back.

**3 — Gran's March:** Ahmad Gran has 12,000 fighters and Ottoman cannon. He is moving into the Ethiopian highlands. The emperor has fled to the mountains. The monasteries are burning. This has never happened before.

**4 — The Diya Council:** Two clans are in dispute over a death. The elders have convened. The xeer requires blood compensation. The numbers are being calculated: how many camels, how many years of payment, who is responsible for what portion. The resolution will hold because both sides know the xeer and know there is no alternative to it.

**5 — Harar:** Richard Burton sits in a Harari courtyard in 1854. He is the first European to enter the city without being killed — or so the British press will claim. He is observing carefully. What he is observing is a city that has been itself, distinctively and completely, for five hundred years.
