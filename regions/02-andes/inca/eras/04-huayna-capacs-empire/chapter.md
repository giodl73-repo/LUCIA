# Huayna Cápac's Empire

## I. The Peak

The quipucamayoc in Qusqu were counting, and the numbers had never been larger.

In the administrative quarter of the capital, where the stone buildings stood in precise rows along streets so straight the morning sun entered each doorway at the same angle, the senior quipucamayoc held their accounting cords across their laps and read the state of the world. The pendant strings hung in clusters of color, and each cluster was a province, and each cord a category, and each knot a quantity, and the quantities were at their peak. More stored maize than any previous generation had recorded. More textiles in the qollqa. More mit'a workers committed than in the time of Pachacuti or Topa Inca. The empire Huayna Capac inherited in 1493 governed perhaps twelve million people across 4,000 kilometers of territory, and the quipucamayoc at the center could, by assembling the reports that arrived through the administrative hierarchy, construct a picture of this world with a precision no other American state could match.

The picture showed a system functioning. In the highlands, the terraces stepped down the mountainsides in neat increments, their walls maintained by the mit'a labor that came in annual shifts, the irrigation channels cleaned and cleared, the potatoes and quinoa planted according to the agricultural calendar the ceque system tracked. In the qollqa above the provincial centers, the stored goods waited: chuño that could last for years, dried meat, sandals, wool, the cumbi cloth that served as the state's highest-denomination currency of obligation. At Huanuco Pampa, the great northern administrative center, the storehouses on the hillside held supplies for thousands of workers, and the central plaza, where the state feasts were held, was swept and ready.

The feasts were the system's visible proof. When a mit'a labor shift began, the Sapa Inca's representative, or the Sapa Inca himself, hosted the workers with chicha brewed in quantities the aqllawasi had prepared, served in vessels whose size proclaimed the generosity of the giver. The workers drank. The administrator drank with them. The chicha sealed the compact: you are giving your labor, and the state is giving its thanks, and the exchange is witnessed by everyone present and recorded by the quipucamayoc standing at the edge of the plaza with his cords. The reciprocity was performed, witnessed, and filed. The system ran on this performance.

Huayna Capac, eleventh Sapa Inca, son of Topa Inca Yupanqui, inherited this machine and chose to extend it. His campaigns in Ecuador, building on his father's incomplete conquest of the north, consumed the better part of his reign. The Caranqui resisted with a ferocity that cost Inca lives and mit'a resources on a scale the quipucamayoc dutifully recorded. The final battle, at Yahuarcocha, produced, the chronicles say, a lake of blood. The name itself means this: Yawar Qucha, the lake of blood. Whether the account is literal or shaped by the needs of the narrative, the message was consistent: the north was expensive to hold.

## II. The Second Capital

Huayna Capac built Tomebamba and stayed.

In the highland basin that is now the Ecuadorian city of Cuenca, the Sapa Inca constructed an administrative center that rivaled Qusqu in ambition. The archaeology shows the characteristic Inca features: the central plaza, the Coricancha, the qollqa, the aqllawasi, the finely fitted stone walls that declared the state's presence. Huayna Capac did not simply build a provincial center. He built a court. He married Canari women, integrating the conquered people into the royal lineage through the kinship ties that had been the Inca's method since the pre-imperial valley days. He held the festivals of the Inca calendar at Tomebamba, performed the sun rituals, hosted the feasts, received the provincial governors.

The court at Qusqu did not cease to function. The ceque rituals continued. The panaqas of the dead Sapa Incas maintained their estates, tended the royal mummies, spoke for the dead in council. But power had shifted north. The army was in the north. The active campaigns were in the north. The Sapa Inca's presence, which in the reciprocal system was the ultimate legitimating force, was in the north. Qusqu became a capital with the ceremonies but without the center.

This created a structural problem the system had not previously faced. Tawantinsuyu was organized around a single navel, Qusqu, the center from which the ceque lines radiated and toward which the reciprocal obligations flowed. The system assumed one center. Two centers created two gravitational fields, two courts, two sets of loyalties, and when Huayna Capac fathered sons in both places, the gravitational split became dynastic.

Huascar was born in Qusqu. His mother was of the royal Inca lineage, and his claim to the succession rested on the traditional framework: the legitimate son of the legitimate lineage in the legitimate capital. Atahualpa was born in the north, possibly in Quito, his mother a woman of the conquered northern peoples, and his claim rested on a different foundation: the presence of the father, the loyalty of the northern army, and, according to some accounts, the expressed preference of Huayna Capac himself.

The succession practices of the Inca did not follow European primogeniture. The Sapa Inca did not automatically pass the title to his eldest son. The succession was determined by the outgoing ruler's choice, validated by the support of the panaqas and the major ayllus. This flexibility had allowed the system to select capable rulers. It had also allowed ambiguity, and ambiguity, when combined with the geographic split between Qusqu and Tomebamba, became a fracture line.

## III. The System Working

Before the fracture, the system worked. And because this chapter is about the moment before the crack, it is worth seeing what a working Tawantinsuyu looked like from the inside.

A kuraka in a highland province, one of the thousands of local leaders who mediated between their ayllus and the state, woke in his compound in the pre-dawn cold and planned his day around the obligations the quipucamayoc tracked. A section of the road in his territory needed repair. The mit'a labor crew for this season, drawn from the ayllus under his jurisdiction, would assemble at the tambos along the damaged section, and the chicha for the work-feast was already brewing in the ceramic vessels his wives had prepared. The quipucamayoc attached to his administration would record the labor: how many workers, how many days, what was provided. The balance of the reciprocal account would shift: the ayllus gave their labor, the state provided food and chicha and the assurance that when their own needs arose, the state's resources would flow in their direction.

The kuraka walked to the qollqa above his compound and checked the stores. Chuño, the freeze-dried potato, stacked in bundles. Dried llama meat, charki, the word that would one day enter English and lose its etymology. Sandals of camelid leather. Wool for spinning. The maize was lower than it should be, because the last growing season had been poor, the rains insufficient, the frost early. But the system was designed for this. A message on a quipu, sent by chasqui to the provincial governor, would request a redistribution from the qollqa at the nearest administrative center. If the center had surplus, the surplus would come. If it did not, the request would travel up the chain to Qusqu, where the senior quipucamayoc would assess the empire-wide balance and allocate from wherever the stores were fullest.

This was the system at its daily scale. Not the great conquests, not the golden Coricancha, not the road stretching to the horizon. The kuraka checking his stores. The quipucamayoc adjusting a knot. The chicha brewing for the work-feast. The mit'a crew assembling at the tambo. The chasqui running the message between the province and the center. The system was a web of these small reciprocal transactions, and its strength was their accumulation, and its weakness was that the accumulation depended on every node functioning.

## IV. The Epidemic

The disease arrived before any European.

Moving south along the trade routes and the qhapaq nan itself, traveling in the bodies of people who did not yet know they were carrying it, the epidemic reached Tawantinsuyu sometime around 1524 or 1527, the precise date uncertain because the quipucamayoc, for all their precision, had no category for an epidemiological event without precedent. The disease was probably smallpox, though some scholars argue for a combination of pathogens. What is certain: it killed on a scale the Andes had not experienced in living memory.

The epidemic moved along the road. The same road that carried the chasqui and the chicha and the mit'a workers now carried the disease, because the road was the nervous system and the disease traveled the nervous system the way information traveled it: quickly, efficiently, from node to node, tambo to tambo, provincial center to provincial center. The density of the road network, which was the empire's greatest administrative achievement, became the disease's greatest advantage. The storehouses that gathered people for distribution became gathering points for infection. The mit'a labor crews that assembled at the tambos became clusters of vulnerable bodies pressed together in compounds designed for shared work and shared chicha, not for isolation.

The quipucamayoc in the provincial centers recorded deaths. They recorded the decline in tribute. They recorded the labor shortages as ayllus that had previously sent their full complement of workers sent fewer, or sent none, because the workers were dead or caring for the dying. The knots registered the symptoms: declining numbers, unfulfilled obligations, stores drawn down faster than they were replenished. But the knots could not explain why. The category "unprecedented epidemic killing people who have no immunity to a disease they have never encountered" was not a category the quipu held.

Huayna Capac was in the north when the epidemic reached him. The chronicles say he received reports of the dying before the disease reached Tomebamba, and the reports described something the empire's administrative system had no framework for: people dying in numbers that exceeded the variation the system was designed to absorb. Crop failure was manageable. Localized drought was manageable. The death of a fraction of the population across an entire province, then another province, then another, was not.

Huayna Capac died of the disease at Tomebamba, probably in 1527, though the date, like so much of this period, is contested. The chronicles describe his awareness of the approaching end. He died with the succession unresolved. Some accounts say he named Ninan Cuyuchi, another son, as his heir, and Ninan Cuyuchi died of the same epidemic before the message could reach him. Some say he favored Atahualpa. Some say he left the decision to the oracle at Pachacamac. What is certain: when the Sapa Inca died, there was no clear successor, and the empire was already weakened by a disease that was killing its people faster than the quipucamayoc could update their records.

## V. Two Sons

The split was geographic, dynastic, and structural.

Huascar was in Qusqu. He was the son of the capital, the son of the traditional lineage, the son the panaqas and the ayllus of the heartland recognized. He took the borla, the crimson fringe, and claimed the succession on the grounds that the legitimate heir ruled from the legitimate capital. The ceque system radiated from his city. The Coricancha was his. The mummies of the dead Sapa Incas, tended by their panaqas, were in his city, and the dead had always been consulted on the living's decisions, and the dead were his ancestors, and this was his ground.

Atahualpa was in the north. He had the army, or much of it, because the army had been campaigning in Ecuador for years and the soldiers knew Atahualpa as the prince who had fought alongside them in the field. He had the loyalty of the generals who had served Huayna Capac's northern campaigns. He had Tomebamba, the second capital, and the provinces of the north that had been incorporated under his father's direct supervision. He did not have the mummies. He did not have the ceque system. He did not have the Coricancha. He had the army and the north.

The civil war that followed would be told in Era 5. What belongs to this chapter is the structure of the split. The empire that Pachacuti had built on a single center, Qusqu, the navel, the point from which all ceque lines radiated and toward which all reciprocal obligations flowed, had developed a second center during Huayna Capac's reign, and the second center had its own gravitational pull, and when the Sapa Inca who had held both centers together died, the two centers pulled apart with a force the system had no mechanism to resist.

The reciprocal system required a single Sapa Inca. The mit'a flowed toward one center. The chicha was poured by one hand. The quipucamayoc reported to one hierarchy. Two Sapa Incas meant two reciprocal systems, two claims on the mit'a, two sets of quipucamayoc recording two sets of obligations. The empire did not have the institutional infrastructure for a divided center, because the empire's institutional infrastructure was designed around the premise that there was only one sun, and the Sapa Inca was the sun's son, and there could be only one son, and the son sat at the navel. When the navel doubled, the system tore.

## VI. The Numbers

In Qusqu, the senior quipucamayoc continued to hold their cords.

The numbers they recorded were already historical. The census data reflected a population that the epidemic had reduced by a percentage scholars estimate, with wide uncertainty, between twenty-five and fifty percent. The tribute records reflected obligations that could no longer be fulfilled because the households that owed them were gone or diminished. The storehouse inventories reflected qollqa that were being drawn down to feed the civil mobilization without being replenished, because the mit'a labor that should have replenished them was being diverted to war.

The quipucamayoc did not editorialize. They recorded. They recorded the deaths. They recorded the declining contributions. They recorded the stores diminishing. The knots adjusted, cord by cord, knot by knot, the quiet arithmetic of contraction. A quipucamayoc's hands moving along the cords in the quiet of the administrative quarter, untying a knot that represented a thousand workers who were no longer a thousand, retying it at a lower number, adjusting the balance, noting the shortfall. The work was the same as it had always been. The numbers were different.

The system Pachacuti had built was designed to manage abundance through redistribution. It had mechanisms for localized scarcity: the qollqa network, the lateral redistribution, the chasqui-speed reallocation of resources from surplus areas to deficit areas. It did not have a mechanism for general scarcity, for a decline that hit every province simultaneously, that reduced the labor pool and the food supply and the tribute base all at once, across the full extent of the road. The epidemic was the first general scarcity the system had faced, and the civil war that followed it was the second, and the two together were consuming the reserves the system needed to function.

The quipucamayoc held their cords. The knots were precise. The numbers were accurate. The empire was dying, and the record of its dying was kept with the same care that had been given to the record of its peak. The quipu did not distinguish between growth and decline in its method. It recorded quantities. The quantities told the story.

Outside the administrative quarter, the road still ran. The tambos still stood. The qollqa, emptier now, still held what they held. The ceque lines still radiated from the Coricancha. The sun still rose over the eastern ridge and struck the gold plate of the temple and filled the chamber with the light that had been the foundation of everything the Inca had built. The system's forms were intact. The substance behind the forms was thinning, cord by cord, knot by knot, in the hands of the quipucamayoc who recorded everything and judged nothing, because judgment was not their function. Their function was to remember. And they remembered.
