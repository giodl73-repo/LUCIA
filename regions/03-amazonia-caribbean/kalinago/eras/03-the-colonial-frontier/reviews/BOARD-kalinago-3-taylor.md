## Linguistic Accuracy Assessment

**The Breton dictionary as source:** The chapter's use of Breton's dictionary is appropriate and the technique of letting dictionary words serve as inside-voice evidence is linguistically sound. The Kalinago language as Breton recorded it is Island Carib — specifically the Island Carib of Dominica in the 1640s. My analysis (*Languages of the West Indies*, 1977) establishes that this language is a creolized form: its vocabulary is heavily Arawakan (specifically Lokono, the Arawak language of the mainland coast), while its grammar reflects a substrate of Kalinago (a Cariban language). The men's speech and women's speech were differentiated, with women using more Arawakan vocabulary (reflecting the historical integration of Arawak-speaking women through the raid-integration economy).

This has implications for the chapter's claims. When the chapter refers to Kalinago words in Breton's dictionary, it should be understood that Breton's primary sources were men — so the dictionary primarily reflects men's speech. The words for navigation, warfare, and sea knowledge are primarily men's vocabulary and are likely more accurate. The words for agricultural practice, domestic knowledge, and healing plants are primarily women's vocabulary — and Breton's access to this vocabulary was more limited. The chapter should not assume the dictionary is equally reliable across all domains.

**Specific vocabulary claims:** The chapter attributes to Breton's dictionary: a word for specific navigation stars, a word for the sea-swell indicating deflected waves from land, a word for fever-reducing plant preparation, a word for mosquito-breeding water, a word for the captive-integration relationship, and a word meaning "those who have come to stay without being asked."

The navigation vocabulary and sea-knowledge vocabulary attributed to Breton are consistent with what the dictionary contains — Island Carib had a rich maritime vocabulary. The plant-knowledge vocabulary (fever-reducing plant, mosquito-breeding water caution) is plausible but the specific entries the chapter implies Breton recorded should be verified against the actual dictionary text. The captive-integration relationship term is consistent with the kinship vocabulary Breton recorded, which did include nuanced terms for people of different origins and statuses within the community.

The word for "those who have come to stay without being asked" — rendered by the chapter as a Kalinago term for Europeans — is where I want to be cautious. Breton does record terms that appear to distinguish Europeans from other people the Kalinago encountered, but the specific gloss the chapter provides ("those who have come to stay without being asked") may be a scholarly reconstruction rather than Breton's direct translation. If this is the chapter's own interpretation of the dictionary rather than a direct quote from Breton, that distinction should be visible somewhere in the source documentation.

## The Gender-Language Issue and the Women's House Figure

The chapter's women's-house figure is described as knowing "the forest garden" — plants, preparation, their uses. The chapter says "Breton's dictionary preserves a significant number of plant names in Kalinago" and attributes specific plant-knowledge vocabulary to the dictionary. My work establishes that the women's vocabulary in the dictionary is less completely recorded than the men's. The chapter's use of dictionary evidence for women's knowledge domains should be calibrated against this limitation — not abandoned, but noted in SOURCES.md with the caveat that women's speech is underrepresented in Breton relative to men's.

The phrase "she knew which plants the European settlers had brought that did not belong in this forest" is a plausible inference from what we know about Kalinago botanical knowledge, but it is not directly supported by Breton. It is in the register of reasonable reconstruction rather than documented practice.

**Note on the Garifuna connection:** The chapter correctly states that "Kalinago words he preserved are still present in Garifuna." This is accurate. The Garifuna language retains the Island Carib core vocabulary — including the maritime and navigation terms — in a form that connects directly to Breton's dictionary. The chapter's landing, which implies the language survived through the Garifuna, is linguistically correct and one of the chapter's most important historical claims.
