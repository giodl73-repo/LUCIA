# The Model Parliament
## Era 4 — Plantagenet England, 1272–1327 CE

---

The writ went out in the king's name, as writs did. The clerk who copied it had copied a hundred like it. The formula was old. But one phrase had not appeared in quite this form before, and the clerk who read it aloud in the chancery that autumn of 1295 paused, perhaps, at the words — or perhaps he did not. The men in the chancery dealt in phrases. This phrase would be remembered.

*Quod omnes tangit ab omnibus approbetur.* What touches all must be approved by all.

Edward I needed money. He needed it for Wales, already taken. He needed it for Scotland, not yet taken. He needed it for Gascony, held by exhaustion and negotiation against the French king's appetite. He had been king for twenty-three years. He understood that money came from taxation, that taxation required consent, that consent required assembly, and that assembly required the right bodies in the room. The barons and bishops had always been there. What had not always been there — what was not reliably there, not yet — were the knights of the shires and the citizens and burgesses of the towns.

He put them in the summons. He needed the money enough to put them in the summons.

This is how constitutional precedents are made. Not by vision. By need.

---

The knights came to Westminster from forty English shires. Two from each, elected by the county court, carrying the county's instructions — its grievances, its requests, its figure for what it could afford. The burgesses came from the boroughs, men who ran market towns and knew the price of wool, the cost of a cartload, the rate at which a tallage would break a merchant's year. They sat separately from the great lords, separately from the bishops and abbots, at the far end of the hall where the acoustics were worse and the ceremony less gilded.

They were not there as equals. They were there because the king needed their money and the law of the land was already clear on this point: you could not take a free man's property without his consent. Henry III had confirmed it, revised and reconfirmed, the Great Charter's principle worked and reworked until it was no longer a baron's instrument but a common man's right. The barons at Runnymede in 1215 had not meant to include the burgesses. What they had established, because they were lawyers' men and knew what principle meant, was the principle. The principle included everyone.

Edward understood this. He had spent his reign turning principles into statutes, and statutes into machinery. The Statute of Westminster in 1275, the Statute of Mortmain in 1279, the Statute of Winchester in 1285 — each one taking a custom, a practice, a feudal obligation that had existed in the memory of men and the pages of Bracton, and hammering it into law that could be cited, appealed, enforced. The common law courts multiplied under his reign. The judges who rode the circuits were his men, trained in his principles, applying a body of decided cases that was already a century old and growing.

The law was not something Edward had invented. It was something he had found — alive, growing, resistant to his own will when he tested it — and had learned to use.

---

Twenty-three years earlier, in 1265, a different man had called a different Parliament. Simon de Montfort, Earl of Leicester, had summoned knights and burgesses alongside the magnates. He was the king's prisoner's keeper, fighting a civil war against a king he had already defeated and a prince who was about to destroy him. He needed the support of the commons to legitimize a government the barons were beginning to desert. He got the Parliament he summoned. He lost the war six months later. His body was quartered, his head sent to his enemies.

But the Parliament had been called. The precedent existed. Edward I, who had cut de Montfort down at Evesham, inherited the precedent. Twenty-seven years later, he used it.

This is also how constitutional precedents are made. The man who dies for the idea does not need to win the war for the idea to survive. The idea has been put into practice. No one can un-practice it.

---

The men who gathered in November 1295 would not have used the word "parliament" the way it would be used later. *Parliamentum* was still a word for a discussion, a colloquy, a talking-together. They were there to grant the king money and to present their counties' grievances, which was the deal: consent in exchange for audience. The king would hear what touched all. All would approve what the king required.

The hall was cold. The great lords sat nearest the king's dais, where the candles were thickest and the draft least. The knights of the shire and the burgesses sat at the far end, where the November air moved freely through the stones and the ceremony was thinner. They had ridden long distances. Some of them had never been to Westminster. The formula for why they were there had been copied into the writ by a clerk who had copied a hundred writs before it. Not one of them in that hall understood that this particular gathering, in this particular configuration, would still be cited by historians seven hundred years later as the moment the institution found its form.

The formula was transactional. It became, through repetition, a custom. The custom, challenged and confirmed and hammered into writs, became the law. The law, old enough, became the ground on which everything else stood.

Edward got his taxes. The knights rode home to their shires with the king's answers to their counties' petitions — rolled parchment, sealed, carried in a saddlebag against the winter road. The burgesses went back to their market towns. The clerks in the chancery filed the writs.

The Parliament had been called. The precedent had been made. No one who had been in that hall would live to understand what they had done.
