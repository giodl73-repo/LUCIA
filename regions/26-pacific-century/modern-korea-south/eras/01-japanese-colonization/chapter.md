# Chapter: Iljegangjeongi — The Japanese Colonization (1910–1945)

## **The Haegeum and the Name**

The woman's fingers found the haegeum's two strings in the darkness before the song found her voice. This was how the music worked: the strings came first, waiting for the voice to know what they meant. She had been playing since she was seven — forty-three years now — and her fingers still asked the strings the same questions they had asked then: *What do you know that I don't?*

The Japanese administrator sat across from her in the small room, his boots catching the lamplight. He had a document folded on his lap. He had not come to hear the haegeum. The music had been playing only as she waited for him, the way a person hums while the kettle boils.

"Your name," he said, in Korean that was precise and untouched by the accent of home. "Your family name."

"Kim," she said.

"Your given name."

She told him. It meant *brightness* — the character her father had given her, written the way the aristocracy wrote, with the weight of generations in its structure.

The administrator unfolded the document. He did not show it to her. He was reading it to himself, as if confirming something he already knew. "This name," he said, "will need to be replaced."

The haegeum was still in her lap. She could feel it warming from her hands.

"Not today," he said. "Not yet. But when the policy comes — and it will come — you will choose a Japanese name. Or one will be chosen for you." He refolded the document, exactly as it had been. "The music you play. What is it called?"

"Sanjo," she said. "Or jangdan, if there is dancing. This is neither — this is just the strings asking what they need to ask."

The administrator stood. He had not called her by her name. He had only told her it would be gone.

## **The System of Names**

When the soshi-kaimei mandate came in 1939, Kim Min-jeong was seventy years old. The policy required all Koreans to register with Japanese family and given names. The notice came to her door. She had been waiting for it since 1912. The administrative machinery had taken twenty-seven years to build, but when it arrived, it was thorough.

The registration office was in Seoul, in a government building that had been constructed by Japanese architects. The walls were clean. The forms were clean. Everything was systematic and precise.

The clerk who processed her registration was named Tanaka Reiji. He had been in Korea since 1910, the year of annexation, when he arrived as a young man to help with the cataloguing of Korean cultural practices. He had moved through the ranks of the colonial administration with steady competence. He believed in the righteousness of what he was doing. He genuinely thought that Japanese names, Japanese language, Japanese education — these were not erasure but *evolution*. Korea had been a kingdom in stasis. Japan was bringing it into modernity.

Tanaka sat at his desk with a pen in his hand. The pen was his instrument of power. He used it to transform people from Koreans into Japanese subjects.

"What name do you choose?" he asked Kim Min-jeong.

She did not answer immediately. In a Confucian society, a name is not a label. It is genealogy. It is the visible record of descent and inheritance. To change her name was to sever herself, officially and legally, from her ancestors. The records would show that Tanaka Minako (the name he had suggested to her) was descended from Japan, not from Korea. The continuity was broken. She existed now as a product of the system, not as a child of her father.

"Tanaka Minako," she said.

Tanaka wrote it down carefully. He recorded her thumbprint. The forms were filled out in triplicate. The machinery processed her into the system. When she left the building, she was still Kim Min-jeong in her own understanding. But in the records, in the documents, in the identity that could be checked and verified and tracked, she was now Tanaka Minako.

She walked back through Seoul's streets. The city was changing. Japanese signs predominated. Japanese language was heard more than Korean in the public spaces. The schools taught Japanese. The newspapers were censored. But she heard Korean being spoken in the markets, in the quiet corners, in the homes. The language was still there, spoken more quietly now, but still alive.

At home, her granddaughter Young-Sook was waiting. Young-Sook was fifteen years old and caught between two worlds. At school, which was mandatory and taught entirely in Japanese, she was "Tanaka Yuki." Her teachers called her Yuki. Her classmates knew her only as Yuki. Her official name was Yuki.

But at home, with her grandmother, she was Young-Sook.

"Did they take your name?" Young-Sook asked.

"Tanaka Minako," Kim Min-jeong said.

"But you're still Kim Min-jeong," Young-Sook said.

"Yes," her grandmother replied. "I'm still that too."

This was the knowledge Young-Sook lived in: *I am two people at once.* At school, she thought in Japanese. Her mind moved in Japanese sentence structures. Japanese logic governed the classroom. But when she came home, she became Korean. Her grandmother taught her in Korean. The stories were Korean. The music was Korean. The pansori that her grandmother was teaching her — the haegeum wailing in the evening when the neighbors would not hear — that was purely Korean, untranslatable into Japanese, impossible to document in the administrative system.

Young-Sook was becoming bilingual in more than language. She was becoming bilingual in identity. She knew that these two selves could not be merged. They had to exist in separation, one for the world outside, one for the world inside.

## **What the Schools Cannot Teach**

The teacher at Young-Sook's school was named Yamamoto Sachiko. She had come to Korea in 1932, fresh from a teacher-training college in Tokyo, full of idealism about her mission. She believed that Japanese language was more modern than Korean. Japanese education was more systematic. Japanese ways of thinking were more logical, more civilized. Korea had been stagnant for centuries. Japan was bringing it into the twentieth century.

Yamamoto was not a bad teacher. She was a careful teacher. She planned her lessons thoroughly. She cared about her students. She wanted them to succeed in Japanese. She wanted them to think clearly, to write properly, to understand the logic of Japanese grammar and Japanese thought. She was participating in a system of cultural erasure, but she was participating in it with genuine conviction that she was doing good work.

In Yamamoto's classroom, Korean was forbidden. If a student used a Korean word, she would correct them firmly: "Japanese, please." The logic was that the students needed to think in Japanese to truly become modern. To think in Korean was to think in old patterns, provincial patterns, patterns that belonged to a stagnant past.

The irony was this: Yamamoto was right about one thing. Language shapes thought. The students who learned to think in Japanese *did* begin to think differently. Their minds moved in Japanese patterns. They began to see Korean not as inadequate but as simply *different* — and in the colonial context, different meant *backward*.

But every evening, in the homes of Seoul, Korean-speaking teachers were teaching the students something else. Pansori masters were teaching the haegeum. Storytellers were teaching the history that Japanese history books did not include. Grandmothers were teaching their granddaughters the songs that could not be sung in the schools.

Kim Min-jeong's teaching of pansori was not systematic in Yamamoto's sense. There were no textbooks. There was no curriculum. There was only the strings of the haegeum, the voice of the student, and the knowledge that lived in the body and could not be written down.

"Listen," Kim Min-jeong would say to Young-Sook. "The haegeum asks a question. Your voice answers. But you do not answer with words. You answer with melody. You answer with feeling. The answer lives in the space between the question and the response."

This way of knowing could not be documented. It could not be inspected. It could not be verified by an administrator with a clipboard. It existed only in the moment between the strings and the voice, in the body of the person who had learned it, in the memory of the person who heard it.

Young-Sook walked the city as two people. At school, she thought in Japanese and wrote in Japanese and learned history as the Japanese told it. But every evening, she was in her grandmother's room, learning a way of knowing that the schools had outlawed.

The colonial system could mandate education. It could require Japanese language. It could forbid Korean in the schools. But it could not forbid the haegeum in the home. It could not forbid the grandmother's voice singing pansori in the night. The system had tremendous power, but there were forms of knowledge that it could not fully contain. The oral tradition was one of them. The embodied practice was another. The things that lived in the body and were transmitted from body to body, from hand to hand, from ear to ear — these things the administrators could count and catalogue and forbid, but they could not erase because erasure requires documentation and these forms of knowledge left no documents.

## **The March First Spirit**

In 1919, when Young-Sook was still unborn and Kim Min-jeong was sixty years old, Korea attempted its first great liberation movement. On March 1, 1919, hundreds of thousands of Koreans gathered in streets across the peninsula, demanding independence from Japan. The movement was called Samil Undong — the March First Movement. It was largely peaceful. It was also crushed.

The Japanese military response was brutal. The police and soldiers killed at least 7,000 Koreans, perhaps more. Tens of thousands were arrested. The violence was documented. The photographs exist: Korean families lying dead in the streets, their names and faces now archived in histories that Yamamoto would never teach.

A man named Park Min-jun participated in the movement. He was thirty-two years old, a nationalist intellectual who had been writing underground newspapers calling for Korean independence. When the Japanese arrested him in the aftermath of March First, they beat him. They beat him for days. They broke his ribs and his teeth. They wanted him to betray the other members of the independence network. He refused, and they beat him again.

He was released in 1920, broken. His body never fully healed. He moved through the world with pain in his chest, with teeth missing, with a limp that had no name because his legs had been beaten but not broken. He was physically alive, but something had been crushed in him that could not be repaired.

But the March First Movement changed the colonial system in ways the Japanese did not anticipate. The movement showed that Koreans would not accept the annexation. The Japanese response was to accelerate the system of assimilation. If Koreans would not accept Japanese rule voluntarily, they would be *made* Japanese through force.

This was the perverse logic that followed March First: *The more Koreans resist, the more thoroughly we must erase them.* The system intensified. The mandatory education in Japanese became more strict. The forced labour programs expanded. The surveillance became tighter. Park Min-jun's sacrifice — his broken body — was one of thousands that showed the cost of open resistance.

Kim Min-jeong understood this. After March First, she stopped thinking about organized rebellion. She understood that the system was too powerful. What she could do was *persist*. She could keep teaching the haegeum. She could keep speaking Korean at home. She could keep transmitting the knowledge that the schools could not erase.

## **The War That Reaches Into Everything**

By 1937, Japan was at war with China. By 1941, Japan was at war with the United States. Korea became a supply base for the Japanese war effort.

Young-Sook was seventeen years old when Japan went to war with America. She was still in school, but school had changed. The teachers were teaching military science. Young men were being conscripted. The factories were being converted to war production. Young-Sook's father had already been conscripted, drafted into the Kempeitai — the Japanese military police — and stationed in Manchuria.

In 1942, Young-Sook was offered a choice. She was told that there was a need for "comfort women" — young women to work in the military facilities. The term was euphemistic. The work was sexual slavery.

She was not told this directly. She was told that she could volunteer for a "work service program" that would support the war effort, and that it would be rewarded with higher wages. She was given a form to sign. She was seventeen.

She could "choose" to sign the form and become a comfort woman, or she could "choose" to stay in Korea and be assigned to factory work. The second choice was not safety. Factory work during wartime was backbreaking and dangerous. Young women died in the factories from exhaustion and industrial accidents. There was no choice that was not a trap.

She chose the factory.

She was assigned to a munitions plant in Seoul, where she worked twelve-hour shifts, standing at a conveyor belt that moved explosives through her hands. The work burned her fingers. Her nails fell off. The chemicals in the air made her cough blood.

Meanwhile, Kim Min-jeong was still alive, still seventy-three years old, still playing the haegeum in the evenings when no one could hear her. She had stopped teaching. Young-Sook had no time for lessons anymore. She came home from the factory destroyed.

But the haegeum was still in Kim Min-jeong's hands. She played it for herself, alone. The music had become a conversation with her own mortality. The strings asked questions that she no longer had words to answer. But her fingers still moved. The haegeum still made sound.

The war was reaching into everything. There was no corner of Korean life that was not touched by the war effort. The rice that Koreans grew was being shipped to Japan. The young men were being drafted. The young women were being coerced. The forests were being cut. The resources were being stripped.

But still, in the darkness, the haegeum played.

In the homes where grandmothers had taught the pansori, the tradition was being kept alive not by performance but by memory. The elderly musicians continued playing in private. They could not perform publicly. The music was banned in the concert halls. But in the privacy of home, the haegeum sang the songs that the system had tried to erase.

## **What Cannot Be Written Down**

The war lasted from 1937 until Japan's defeat in 1945. For those eight years, Korea was transformed. The population was decimated by hunger, by disease, by the conscription and coercion of war. The countryside was emptied of able-bodied people. The cities were bombed. The resources were stripped.

But something persisted that the Japanese could not suppress: the memory of who they were.

This memory was not written down. It could not be written down, because the schools were controlled and the newspapers were controlled and the books were controlled. The memory persisted in the things that could not be documented: the language spoken at home, the stories told to children, the music played in the night, the names that people called each other in private.

Young-Sook survived the factories. In 1945, when Japan surrendered, she was twenty years old. She had lost the years from seventeen to twenty in the factory. Her hands bore the scars of the chemicals. Her lungs bore the damage of the air she had breathed. She would never fully recover from those years.

But she had survived. She was alive on August 15, 1945, when the radio announced that Japan had surrendered. Korea was liberated.

It was not a liberation that Koreans had won for themselves. It was a liberation that came because Japan had lost a war that was not Korea's war. Korea was freed by the defeat of the colonizer, not by the victory of the resistance.

This is the complexity of Korean history: *liberation through external event, not earned by internal agency.*

Koreans had fought. They had resisted. They had suffered. Park Min-jun had been tortured and broken. Thousands had been killed in March First. But in the end, what freed them was not their resistance but the collapse of Japan's empire in the face of American military power.

## **The Music Opens**

On August 15, 1945, the day of liberation, there was a celebration in Seoul. People gathered in the streets. They sang. They danced. And in the evening, a very old woman came to the center of the city with a haegeum in her hands.

Kim Min-jeong was seventy-six years old. She had not played in public for thirty-five years. The haegeum was no longer banned. The music could be played openly again.

She sat in the center of the gathering and began to play. The strings of the haegeum had not changed. They still asked the same questions they had asked sixty-nine years ago, when she was a girl learning to play from her mother. The music that came from her fingers was the same music that had been sung in secret during the war, in the darkness of the factories, in the homes where the young were being taught the tradition that would not die.

Young-Sook stood at the edge of the crowd and listened to her grandmother play. For five years, she had not heard the haegeum. She had almost forgotten the melody. But as soon as the strings began to ask their questions, the melody came back. Her body remembered what her mind had suppressed.

The music was not just joy. It was not triumphant. It was something more complex: it was the sound of a culture that had been systematically attempted to be erased, and had persisted despite everything. It was sorrow and strength combined. It was the weight of thirty-five years of silence, and the immense relief of being able to speak again.

The war was over. Korea was free. But the cost was visible in every face that heard the music. The cost was in Young-Sook's scarred hands. The cost was in Park Min-jun's broken body, broken by torture in the years after March First. The cost was in the millions of Koreans who had died in the war, in the factories, in the comfort women stations, in the military conscription.

The haegeum opened. The music poured out into the Seoul night. And Korea, which had been told that its name would be taken, which had been told that it would become Japan, which had been systematically attempted to be erased for thirty-five years, was still itself. It was wounded. It was traumatized. But it was still Korea.

The strings knew this. The voice knew this. And tomorrow, the country would be divided at the 38th parallel, and the war that had just ended would be replaced by a different war that would last another seventy-five years and counting. But tonight, for this moment, the haegeum played and Korea listened to itself singing.
