# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Review — PANEL-1
**Chapter:** Se Dena Tíd — The Danish Time (Anglo-Saxons, Era 4)
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (opening review)

---

The knowledge system this chapter promises to explore is the most interesting thing about it: the decision to make the vernacular — Old English, the spoken tongue of farmers and fishermen and warriors — the vehicle for administrative, legal, and sacred text.

In most of the world where we observe this choice, the literate language and the spoken language are different. Sanskrit and Prakrit. Latin and the Romance languages. Classical Arabic and the spoken dialects. The prestige of the written form derives in part from its separation from ordinary speech. Alfred's decision reverses this. He chose to close the gap — to make the language of the poem and the prayer also the language of the law and the translation. This is a profound intellectual decision about what knowledge is for. Knowledge in this framework is not the property of those who can read Latin. It belongs to everyone who speaks English.

The opening does not quite name this. It gestures at it — "the will to write your laws in your own tongue, to teach your children to read them, to insist that the language you speak is worth the trouble." But the chapter should develop the full intellectual weight of this choice. What did it mean to translate Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy* into Old English? What was Alfred doing when he sat in his hall and turned Latin periods into West Saxon prose? He was making a claim: that the tragedy of Boethius — a Roman philosopher awaiting execution, writing about the nature of fortune and divine providence — was a tragedy that mattered to the herdsman's son in Dorset. That the distance between Rome and Somerset was crossable in a sentence.

This is not a small thing. This is a theory of knowledge: that understanding should be available to the people who need it, in the language they have, not hoarded by those with access to the language of the learned.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter must show Alfred's translation project at method level. Not "he translated Boethius and Bede" — show a specific passage, show what he changed, show what he added. His preface to the *Pastoral Care* is a primary source that explicitly states his theory of knowledge: he laments that learning has decayed because there are no men who can translate Latin, and he proposes to restore it by bringing the most necessary books into English. Quote it. Let Alfred state his own epistemology in his own words. That is not description — that is evidence of the knowledge system operating.

This opening earns what follows. The chapter should make good on its implicit promise.
