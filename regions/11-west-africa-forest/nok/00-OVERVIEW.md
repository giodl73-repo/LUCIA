# Nok — "The First Iron-Workers of the Forest-Savanna Edge"

The people of the Jos Plateau in what is now central Nigeria left behind terracotta figures of such technical and aesthetic accomplishment — hollow-built, with triangular eyes and pierced pupils, anatomically detailed and emotionally present — that when European archaeologists first encountered them in the 1930s, they refused to believe they were African in origin. This refusal tells us more about the archaeologists than about the Nok. The terracottas are African in origin, in context, and in tradition. They were made by people of the Jos Plateau from roughly 1500 BCE to 500 CE — a two-thousand-year tradition of ceramic sculpture unmatched in sub-Saharan Africa for that period — and they were made alongside the earliest evidence of iron smelting in sub-Saharan West Africa.

The Nok people — we call them by the name of the village where the first figures were found, not by any name they gave themselves, which is one of the honest uncertainties this overview must state clearly — were not a single unified culture but the shared material tradition of communities spread across the Jos Plateau and its margins. What connected them was the clay tradition, the iron tradition, and the plateau ecology of tin-bearing rock, iron ore, and hardwood fuel. What they thought of themselves, what language they spoke, what political institutions they maintained — these are questions the archaeological record has not yet answered. The honest position is that we have their faces in clay and we do not yet have their words.

Key places:
- **Nok village** — the type site; the first terracottas recovered, in tin-mining spoil
- **Taruga** — the most extensively excavated Nok iron-smelting site; multiple furnace complexes
- **Jos Plateau** — the geographical heartland; 1,200–1,400 m elevation; tin and iron ore bearing geology
- **Samun Dukiya** — another major excavated site; terracotta and iron together

## What Defined Them

**Iron as foundation.** The Nok ironworking tradition is among the earliest documented in sub-Saharan Africa, contemporary with or preceding the iron traditions of Meroe in the Nile valley. The furnace complexes at Taruga, dated to the fifth century BCE, were not experimental — they were the remains of a mature smelting technology. Iron at the Jos Plateau meant tools for agriculture (yam cultivation in the forest-savanna transition zone), for hunting, and for clearing land. It also meant exchange: iron goods moved from the plateau into the surrounding region. The Nok iron tradition is almost certainly the ancestor of the iron traditions that spread south into the forest zone and shaped every subsequent West African culture.

**Terracotta as record.** The Nok terracottas are not decoration. They are figures of humans, animals, and possibly spiritual beings, made with technical sophistication — built hollow to prevent explosion in firing, with consistent anatomical conventions (the triangular eye, the elaborate coiffure, the detailed jewelry) that speak of a tradition maintained across generations and sites. The figures appear to have been placed at shrines and in ritual contexts, though the looting of Nok sites by tin miners and antiquities collectors before systematic archaeology could document their contexts has left many questions about their specific ritual function unanswered. They were meaningful objects in a meaningful system; the specific meaning is honest uncertainty.

**The ancestor of the southern artistic traditions.** There is no proven direct genealogical connection between Nok terracotta and the later Ife and Benin bronze traditions of the forest zone to the south. But the structural similarities — the hollow building technique, the naturalistic but conventionalized figural style, the ritual context — are strong enough that most scholars see a connection, whether through direct transmission or through a shared tradition. The Nok tradition may be the upstream source from which the extraordinary forest-zone artistic tradition of Yoruba and Edo developed across two millennia.

**The honest limits.** The Nok cannot be placed within a named linguistic or ethnic tradition with current evidence. We do not know what language they spoke, what political institutions they maintained, or what they called themselves. The terracottas give us faces without names. The iron furnaces give us technology without the names of the workers. The archaeological record is rich in material and thin on social and political context. Writing about the Nok honestly requires saying this: they were real people with a sophisticated culture, and they are also, in many respects, still strangers to us.

## The Chapters

**1 — The First Furnace:** A clay-lined pit packed with charcoal and iron ore is being lit on the Jos Plateau. The people who built it have done this before — this is the third furnace this season. What comes out will be traded south, where the yam farmers need hoes.

**2 — The Clay Face:** A craftsperson is coiling a hollow head in stages, drying each layer before adding the next. The triangular eyes are made with a tool. The elaborate coiffure is being constructed detail by detail. This will stand at a place of power. Who it represents is a question we cannot yet answer.

**3 — The Long Tradition:** A thousand years have passed since the first furnace. The terracotta tradition continues. South of the plateau, in the forest zone, younger traditions are beginning that will carry something of this plateau knowledge into the future. The connection is there. The thread is thin.
