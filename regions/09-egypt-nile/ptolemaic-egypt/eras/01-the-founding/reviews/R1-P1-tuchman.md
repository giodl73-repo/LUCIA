## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of Ptolemaic Egypt, Era 1 Opening

**Overall:** This is a superb piece of narrative construction. The opening line is one of the strongest in the manuscript: a body, a coffin, a man waiting. The arc from stolen corpse to crowned pharaoh is built with the inevitability of a well-made argument.

**What works:**
- The opening line ("The body arrived in a coffin of hammered gold") is perfect economy. The body before the name. The gold before the context. The reader is caught before the reader knows why.
- The pacing of the middle section, where Ptolemy's calculation is set against the other Successors in three short sentences ("Demetrius wanted glory. Antigonus wanted dominion. Seleucus wanted territory."), creates the kind of rhythmic contrast that makes a reader lean forward.
- "The body was the first stone." This is the pivot sentence of the opening, and it earns its weight. Everything before it is scene; everything after it is architecture. The sentence does structural work.
- The close is restrained and right. "The construction continued. It would not stop." Two sentences that carry the entire opening's argument without summarizing it.

**What doesn't:**
- The paragraph about Alexandria's physical description (limestone ridge, Lake Mareotis, Pharos) is the one moment where the pacing slackens. It reads more like orientation than narrative. The city needs to be felt, not described.
- The Library paragraph is dense with names (Demetrius of Phalerum, Strato, Euclid, Herophilus) in a way that risks becoming a roster. The names work if the chapter develops them; in the opening, they crowd each other.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening is built almost entirely around Ptolemy's calculation. He is a strategist, a builder, an architect of legitimacy. But where is his body? He waits in Memphis, he intercepts the cortege, he recruits scholars, he orders temples. All of these are acts of mind. I want one moment where he is physical: standing somewhere, seeing something, feeling the heat or the stone or the weight of what he is building. The greatest openings in this manuscript ground their thinkers in their bodies. Ptolemy needs a body.
