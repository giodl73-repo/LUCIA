# Neshili — The People of the Great King

We called our language Neshili — the speech of Nesa — and our king was the Great King, the *Labarna*, whose title became a word for greatness in the same way that Caesar became a word for emperor. The Great King ruled not merely by force but by covenant: the *international treaties* that the Hittite kings carved in stone and bronze and distributed to their treaty partners were legally binding contracts between rulers, invoking the gods of both kingdoms as witnesses and guarantors. The world was held together by sworn agreements, and the gods punished those who broke them.

We built our empire in the mountains of Anatolia — the high plateau that rises from the coasts of the Black Sea and the Aegean to the central plain where our capital Hattusa stood, surrounded by the massive carved stone walls and the great rock sanctuary of Yazılıkaya where the gods processed in relief. From Hattusa we contested with Egypt for the Levant, fought Mitanni for Syria, withstood Assyria in the east, and organized a diplomatic system so sophisticated that the treaty Ramesses II signed with Hattusili III after the Battle of Kadesh — the first international peace treaty in history whose text survives from both sides — was later displayed in the United Nations headquarters as a model of diplomatic achievement. We were destroyed around 1180 BCE by the same Bronze Age collapse that ended the Mycenaean palace world, and we disappeared so completely that for two thousand years no one knew we had existed.

## What Defined Them

**The Great King and the treaty system.** The Hittite Great King governed his empire not through direct administration of all its territory but through a hierarchy of relationships: vassal kings who swore loyalty oaths to the Great King, received protection and confirmation of their thrones, and were required to provide military service and tribute. These relationships were formalized in detailed treaty documents that specified the history of the relationship, the obligations of each party, and the divine witnesses who would punish violations. The gods of both kingdoms were invoked — Hittite gods and the vassal's own gods — because the treaty was a covenant in the deepest sense: a sworn agreement under divine witness whose violation was not just political disloyalty but cosmic offense.

**The Telipinu Edict and the problem of succession.** The early Hittite kingdom was destroyed repeatedly by succession crises: kings killed by brothers, brothers killed by sons, factions forming around competing claimants. The Telipinu Edict — issued by the king Telipinu around 1525 BCE — was the Hittite solution: a formal codification of the succession rules, making the eldest son the heir, specifying what happened in the absence of a son, and explicitly prohibiting the killing of royal family members who were not guilty of crimes. The Edict was not merely a constitutional document. It was the recognition that the kingdom had nearly destroyed itself through political violence, and that written rules might prevent it.

**Yazılıkaya — the gods' assembly in stone.** The rock sanctuary of Yazılıkaya, cut from the limestone outcrop outside Hattusa, contains the largest surviving procession of Hittite gods — over sixty deities carved in relief on the rock walls, the male gods on one wall, the female gods on the other, meeting in the center at the great god Teshub and great goddess Hepat. This was not simply an outdoor temple. It was the physical representation of the divine assembly, the council of gods whose decisions shaped the cosmos, made permanently visible in the living rock. The Hittite king's access to this sanctuary — his ability to perform rituals in the presence of the assembled divine — was a demonstration of his unique position as the gods' earthly representative.

**The chariot and the composite bow at Kadesh.** The Battle of Kadesh (1274 BCE) was the largest chariot battle in the ancient world: Ramesses II of Egypt and Muwatalli II of Hatti with thousands of chariots on each side, the Orontes River as the pivot, and a tactical situation so complex that both sides claimed victory. What makes Kadesh historically remarkable is not the battle but what followed: the Egyptian-Hittite peace treaty of 1259 BCE, in which both kings agreed on a border, mutual defense obligations, and extradition arrangements. Both versions of the treaty survive — the Hittite silver tablet version (known from copies) and the hieroglyphic Egyptian temple inscription. The two texts are not identical; each is shaped by its audience. But the existence of a negotiated, written, witnessed peace agreement between two great powers is itself the achievement.

**The Hittite legacy in the Neo-Hittite states.** The Bronze Age collapse destroyed Hattusa and the empire, but it did not erase the Hittite tradition. The Neo-Hittite city-states of southern Anatolia and northern Syria — Carchemish, Malatya, Aleppo, Hamath — continued Hittite cultural and artistic traditions for another five centuries, until the Assyrian conquests of the 8th century BCE absorbed them. The Hittite hieroglyphic script — distinct from the cuneiform used for administrative texts — continued in use in these states and is one of the key records of the Luwian language and culture that underlay much of Anatolian civilization.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Old Kingdom:** Hattusili I leads his army south toward Babylon. The city that has stood for three centuries will be sacked by a people that Egypt has barely heard of. The king returns to Hattusa with the plunder and immediately faces a succession dispute. The pattern of Hittite power — great reach abroad, fragility at home — is established.

**2 — The Middle Kingdom:** Telipinu publishes his edict. The text is carved and distributed to the provinces. The king who made it will be remembered not for conquests but for the document that tried to stop Hittites from killing each other over the throne. Whether it works depends on successors he cannot control.

**3 — The Empire Age:** Suppiluliuma sends his army into Mitanni. In one campaign the dominant power in the Near East is destroyed. The vacuum this creates pulls the Hittite empire further south, toward Egypt's sphere. At Kadesh, the two empires collide. Both claim victory. Both accept the treaty. Both know the world has changed.

**4 — The Collapse:** The Sea Peoples are moving. From the west, from the islands, from somewhere, armed groups are attacking the Aegean coast, the Levant, Egypt. Hattusa burns around 1180 BCE. The great walls, the thousands of clay tablets in the archives, the rock carvings at Yazılıkaya — all of it is abandoned. For two thousand years, no one will know these people existed.
