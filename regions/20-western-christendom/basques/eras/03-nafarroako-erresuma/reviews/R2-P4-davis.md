# R2-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel-2 Review: Notes (Production Bible)

The *bertsolaritza* figure is the chapter's most important intellectual gesture and the notes handle it with the right level of care. "The improvisational oral poet who competed at public gatherings and in doing so preserved the community's memory, legal precedents, and cultural identity" — this is exactly the kind of knowledge-system description that takes the tradition seriously at the level of what it does, not just what it is. The disclosure that this is a composite figure drawn from ethno-historical sources is also correct and honest.

**What works in the plan:**

The method-level treatment of the *foru* — "show the assembly operating: one specific disputed matter" — is the right approach. What the notes need to specify is what kind of dispute will be shown. I recommend showing an inheritance dispute over an ironworking site (a *ferrería*), because this brings together the three characteristic elements of Basque material culture: the iron technology (which requires specific technical knowledge passed through family lines), the water-power system (which requires shared rights to a stream), and the forest (which provides charcoal and is a shared resource managed by the valley community). A single disputed *ferrería* contains the entire ecological logic of the *foru*.

**Where I would push harder:**

The notes mention the linguistic fossil — *aizto*, "knife," meaning "stone tool" — but do not plan to use it in the chapter. This should appear in the chapter. Not as a footnote or an aside but as a structural moment: the chapter pauses on this word as evidence that Euskara carries a memory that predates any written record, any kingdom, any administrative apparatus. The language is not a survival — it is an archive. An archive that continues to be written in spoken form through the *bertsolaritza* tradition. The linguistic fossil and the oral poet are the same phenomenon: the community's method for storing what cannot be stored in stone or parchment.

The chapter should also engage with what Euskara's isolate status means for the *foru*. The covenant is in a language that no outsider speaks. This is not accidental. When the king swears the *foru*, he swears in a language that belongs to the community he is swearing to serve. He can swear in Latin, but the community will not remember it in Latin; the memory of the oath will circulate in Euskara, in the oral tradition, in the verses of the bertsolarar. This means the community has control over what the king promised. If the king later claims he swore something different, the community's memory — in its own language — is the counter-archive. This is a knowledge-system as political technology.

**The question I would still push on:**

The *bertsolaritza* tradition is planned for figures.md but I think it needs a moment in the chapter proper. Not a full description but a single scene: a bertsolar at an assembly, composing in public on the question of a disputed inheritance or the conditions of the king's oath, with the crowd hearing and responding. The tradition's power is that it is public, participatory, and competitive — the composition is witnessed, challenged, and remembered by the whole community. This is the method level. Describe it at the method level: show the bertsolar doing it, not the tradition existing.

**Verdict:** The knowledge-system framing is well-planned. Add the *ferrería* dispute for the *foru*-at-method-level scene; integrate the linguistic fossil as structural evidence; add one bertsolar scene in the chapter (not just figures.md).
