# Haebang — The Return

## Part 1: The Arrival

The ship cut through the cold water of Wonsan harbor on September 19, 1945, and a man stood at the rail watching the Korean coast rise toward him. The war with Japan had ended four days before. Soviet troops had already moved into the northern districts, and now the Americans held the south across an arbitrary line drawn by two powers who had never set foot on this peninsula before the war ended. The man at the rail was thirty-three years old. He had spent the last ten years in Yan'an with the Chinese communists. No one in Korea had seen him since the 1930s. No one in Korea knew if he was still alive. No one in Korea knew him at all.

The dock at Wonsan smelled of salt and diesel fuel. Soviet uniforms moved among the gathering crowd — Korean communists, some who had fought in Yan'an, some who had survived the occupation, some who had simply appeared from the underground cells of Pyongyang and the mining towns of the north. They stood waiting, not knowing what they were waiting for, knowing only that the Soviets had promised something.

When the Soviet commander presented him to the waiting crowd, he gave a name: Kim Il-sung.

The crowd's breath caught. A man named Park Hon-yong, who had led the underground Communist Party through the occupation, felt something crystallize in his chest. Kim Il-sung was the name of a legendary guerrilla fighter — the man who had fought the Japanese occupation for years, who had become a symbol of resistance when the occupation seemed total, who had died or disappeared into China or into legend itself. Park knew this name. Everyone in the crowd knew it. They believed the story it carried. The Japanese had tried to erase Korean history, but this name had survived in whispers. This name meant No. This name meant they had not been erased entirely. This name meant that somewhere in the world, a Korean had stood against the occupiers. And now he was here.

But in the corner of the crowd, a schoolteacher named Im Hae-jo stood watching. He had grown up near the home village of the original Kim Il-sung, the real one, the one people remembered. He watched the man descend from the ship — the set of his shoulders, the way he held his hands, the line of the cheekbones. Im Hae-jo's mouth opened slightly, then closed.

The cheekbones were wrong.

The movement was wrong too — trained rather than native, the walk of a man who had been taught to look a certain way. Or maybe Im Hae-jo was wrong. Maybe memory had shifted the original Kim into something clearer than the human being who had actually lived. Maybe this was the same man, aged by ten years of exile. Maybe Im Hae-jo's uncertainty was itself the machinery of doubt, and he should trust the name instead of his own eyes.

He said nothing. Around him, the crowd was crystallizing into belief. The name was doing its work. The story that the Soviets were offering was true because Korea needed it to be true, and what Korea needed was stronger than what Im Hae-jo could know from a schoolyard childhood. Later, when Im Hae-jo would speak these doubts aloud, he would be arrested for them. He would spend the next five years in prison for the crime of noticing that the emperor was wearing someone else's face. The founding of North Korea could not tolerate the question he had asked. And so it would erase him, and then erase the question, until everyone who might have asked it had learned not to see what Im Hae-jo had seen.

## Part 2: What Was Lost

The Japanese occupation of Korea had lasted thirty-five years — a generation and a half. No one under the age of thirty-five had ever lived in an independent Korea. The occupation was not a military regime with civilian government. It was total. The language was suppressed: Korean children were beaten in schools for speaking it. Korean newspapers were closed. Korean history was rewritten to be a prologue to Japanese greatness. Korean place names were replaced with Japanese ones. Korean women were conscripted into the comfort women system — forced to serve as sex slaves to Japanese soldiers.

In a schoolroom in Pyongyang, a child spoke Korean to a friend during a lesson. The Japanese teacher heard. In front of the entire class, the child was struck — hard enough to draw blood. The message was clear: Korean words carried a physical price. For years after the occupation ended, that child did not speak, even in safety. The silence had become part of her body. Other children learned faster. They learned to keep Korean words in their mouths, held unexpressed, made private. Some forgot the language entirely. When liberation came, many Korean children would have to be retaught their own language, as if it were a foreign thing.

In the countryside, Japanese agricultural officers had reorganized farming to feed Japan. Korean farmers worked Japanese land, grew Japanese crops, sent the harvest to Japan. Starvation was not unusual. The Japanese were more interested in extraction than in keeping the colonized population alive. When Korea suddenly became free, it was free but starving.

On August 15, 1945, the Japanese Emperor announced surrender. In Seoul and Pyongyang and every city in between, Koreans poured into the streets. The celebration was authentic and overwhelming. People wept. People sang. For thirty-five years, they had lived under a foreign flag, forbidden to speak their own language, told that they were subjects of the Japanese emperor and always would be. Now they were free. The colonizers were gone. Korea was theirs again.

But freedom is not the same as sovereignty. The Soviets had entered Korea from the north — they had invaded Japanese-occupied Manchuria and were sweeping down the peninsula. The Americans, in a hastily made decision that would shape the next seventy years, drew a line at the 38th parallel. Above that line: Soviet occupation. Below: American occupation. No Korean had been asked where the line should be drawn. No Korean had asked for division. The people who celebrated liberation on August 15 did not yet understand that they were celebrating the end of one occupation and the beginning of another.

## Part 3: The Soviet Installation

The Soviets who arrived in Korea did not come to end colonialism. They came to secure their sphere of influence in East Asia and to prevent the Americans from controlling the entire peninsula. The Soviet commander in the north, General Chistiakov, understood his task clearly: install a communist government that would be obedient to Moscow, keep the Americans from expanding their zone, and establish the infrastructure of a Soviet-style state.

The communists of Korea — the handful who had survived underground, and the larger number who had simply emerged from hiding or joined from the streets — saw the Soviets as allies. Not as occupiers. As allies. The Japanese were gone. Here were powerful people who said they believed in communism, in revolution, in the liberation of the working class. For a generation starved of hope and agency, this looked like possibility.

Park Hon-yong, the Communist leader, understood the geometry of power. The Soviets would not leave unless a Korean communist could be installed who would do what the Soviets wanted. The Soviets had brought a man and a name. Park's job was to make them both real.

In October 1945, one month after the ship arrived, land reform was announced. The largest redistribution of property in Korean history. In the countryside, Japanese landlords and Korean landlords who had collaborated with the occupation were stripped of their estates. The land was given to peasants who had nothing. In one village, a man named Lee stood in the field that was now declared to be his. The earth was red. The boundaries had been walked by a Soviet officer and two Korean communists. The land was his. The weight of this fact — ownership, after a lifetime of landlessness — pressed into his chest. He had no tools. The land would need work. But it was his. His children would eat from this earth.

The mother, Lee's wife, understood differently. She walked the boundaries behind her husband. She understood that the state had given them this land, and the state could take it back. She understood that survival now depended on performing gratitude, showing loyalty, proving that they were not enemies of the revolution. The gift of land was also a test.

In the cities, Communist Party cells were organized. A study group would meet in a room, maybe a basement, maybe a school building after hours. A leader would arrive with a text — maybe one of Kim Il-sung's proclamations, maybe a passage from Marx that had been interpreted through the Korean situation. The group would sit. The leader would read. Then the discussion would begin.

"What does this mean?" the leader would ask.

A worker would offer: "It means the rich are exploiting us."

"Yes, but how?" the leader would press.

Another would answer: "They take the surplus value of our labor."

"Correct. And what must we do?"

The answer everyone knew: "We must organize. We must overthrow them."

This happened in study groups across the north. Not as indoctrination — not yet. As education. As the installation of a framework for understanding the world. The framework said: the capitalists are your enemies. The workers are your class. The Communist Party represents your class interest. You are the future. History is on your side. This is not propaganda in the sense of falsehood. This is mythology in the sense of a story that structures how you see reality.

By late 1945, the institutions were in place. Land reform was complete. The Party was organized. A provisional government had been established, with Soviet approval. The boundaries between what was happening and what the Soviets wanted were becoming increasingly blurred. The Soviets gave an order; a Korean communist would implement it, and afterward it would be presented as a Korean decision. The Soviets announced policy; Korean media would carry it, framed as Kim Il-sung's thought. The machinery was being built at speed, with a clarity of purpose, and with the consent of Koreans who understood that this was the price of remaining in the Soviet sphere and remaining protected from American dominance.

## Part 4: The Name and the Legend

Who was Kim Il-sung?

The legend said: a guerrilla fighter who had resisted the Japanese for years, who had fought in Manchuria, who had become so dangerous that the Japanese had hunted him relentlessly, who had disappeared into China. The legend was real. There had been a Kim Il-sung like this. He had lived. He had fought. He had mattered.

But by 1945, no one in Korea knew if he was alive or dead. No one had heard from him in a decade. The name was powerful. The name carried history. The name meant resistance. But the man — that was uncertain.

In the crowd at Wonsan dock, people had different reactions. Park Hon-yong believed that this was the real Kim Il-sung returned. Or he believed it because belief was necessary. Or he knew it was probably not the real Kim Il-sung but believed that it did not matter. These states of consciousness — belief, necessary fiction, pragmatic acceptance — were becoming indistinguishable from each other.

A worker who had hidden from the Japanese police heard the name and felt hope bloom in his chest. The legendary resistance fighter had come home. Korea would be free.

A peasant who had lost family to the occupation heard the name and thought of revenge. The man who had resisted would now lead them to victory.

A young student heard the name and felt something crystallize: this was the future. This man represented the future. The future had arrived.

The machinery of making the mythology true was set in motion immediately. In schools, children began to learn about Kim Il-sung's life — the version of his life that had been decided upon. The Japanese had tried to erase him. The Japanese had hunted him. He had survived. He had come back. The lesson was clear: Korea's enemies cannot erase what Korea believes in. The name survives. The story survives. As long as the story survives, Korea survives.

In homes, photographs appeared. How had photographs appeared so quickly? The Soviet command had them. Or they had been manufactured. The face looking out from these photographs was the same face that had stepped off the ship. Or maybe it was a face similar enough that people's memory, combined with their hope, would make it the same face. The photograph was placed in a position of honor. A family would gather before it. A mother would say: "This is Kim Il-sung. He fought for Korea. He will lead us now." A child would bow, the way it was done in school, the way everyone was learning to do.

The statues came next. In Pyongyang, in public squares, bronze figures rose. Kim Il-sung in heroic posture. Kim Il-sung with his arm raised. The statues were so much more solid than memory. The statues did not flicker or change. The statues made the mythology permanent.

## Part 5: The Bargain

By 1950, five years of repetition had shaped a population. The study groups met every week. The lessons were constant. The photographs, the statues, the stories, the songs — they formed a dense mythology that was woven into every institution. To learn to read was to learn to read about Kim Il-sung. To join the Communist Party was to study his thought. To be a good Korean was to serve the revolution that he led.

It is not clear how many people believed, in their hearts, that this man was truly the legendary guerrilla fighter. It is not clear how many people performed belief while privately doubting. What is clear is that by 1950, the question itself had become dangerous. The state could not afford doubt. The state had been built on mythology, and mythology is fragile. To question the mythology is to threaten the state itself.

The woman who had learned not to speak Korean because of pain now spoke Korean freely, and it carried the grammar of the revolution. Her children had learned revolution as a mother tongue. They did not know any other language. They did not know that there was a choice. The mythology had become their reality.

The peasant who had received his land worked it in the name of Kim Il-sung. The surplus was taken by the state in the name of building the nation. He performed loyalty not because he had been convinced that loyalty was right, but because loyalty was the condition of survival. Over time, the line between performed and genuine loyalty blurred. He told his children the stories. He believed them, or believed that he believed them, or had forgotten the difference between the two.

The young student of 1945 was now a young soldier in a new army. The army was organized, disciplined, trained by Soviet officers. The young soldier understood his role: to defend the revolution. To defend the nation. To defend the Supreme Leader. When the Korean War began in June 1950, he was ready. He did not question. He did not doubt. He had been trained not to doubt.

In 1950, the country had a government, an army, a mythology, and a leader. Whether the leader was authentic had become irrelevant. The leader was real because the state had made him real. The mythology had become truth through the simple mechanism of making people live as if it were true. A state that required the mythology to function — a state whose entire legitimacy rested on a founding story — could not afford to question that story. And so it did not. It built institutions around the requirement of belief. It installed machinery that made doubt painful, dangerous, impossible. By 1950, millions of Koreans had internalized this requirement so thoroughly that they did not experience it as external pressure. They experienced it as love — love of the leader, love of the revolution, love of the nation.

This is what a people offers itself when colonization has destroyed other forms of meaning-making: mythology as the ground of survival. Whether or not you can know the truth about the foundation of your state, you can live as if the mythology is true. You can organize your life around it. You can raise your children in it. You can go to war for it. You can build a nation on it.

And the nation that is built on mythology will do whatever is necessary to protect the mythology. Because the mythology is not separate from the nation. The mythology is the nation.

## The Founding and What It Made

On September 19, 1945, a ship arrived in Wonsan harbor. The man who stepped off — whether he was the legendary Kim Il-sung or a Soviet appointee groomed to play the role — accepted a name and a nation's hope. Over five years, that hope was systematized into institutions. Study groups. Schools. Statues. Photographs. A party. An army. A government. The machinery was relentless and efficient.

By 1950, when the Korean War began, the mythology had taken hold so thoroughly that North Koreans would fight and die for the leader they had been taught to worship. They would do it sincerely, not as performance. The line between the founding fiction and the truth had been erased. What remained was a state built entirely on mythology — not cynically, but desperately. A traumatized people had grasped a story that allowed them to survive, to rebuild, to become a nation. The story had become their nation.

This is the paradox of the founding of North Korea: it was both necessary and total. It was necessary because colonization had shattered other structures of meaning. It was total because a state built on mythology must control every institution, every narrative, every voice, in order to maintain the fiction that the mythology is true.

And it worked. For seventy years and counting, a nation has endured, organized entirely around the worship of a family and the mythology that justifies that worship. The children of those who heard Kim Il-sung's name for the first time at Wonsan dock have grandchildren who have never questioned whether the mythology is true. For them, it is not a mythology. It is simply reality. The nation. The only frame of understanding they have ever been given.

This is the power of the state built on mythology: not that it is fragile, but that it is stronger than truth. As long as every institution — school, party, military, government — requires the mythology, as long as all meaning-making flows through it, as long as doubt is impossible because the cost is too high, the mythology cannot be destroyed. It can only be lived inside, as if it were solid ground. And for a people who had lost all other ground, it was solid enough to build a nation on.
