# B-wolof-2 — Boubacar Barry — Board Review of The First Atlantic Contact

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Senegambian history and the Atlantic slave trade; the structural effects of Atlantic commerce on West African political systems.

## Historical Accuracy

- **The structural argument is correct.** The Atlantic trade did empower coastal Wolof states at the expense of the inland Jolof heartland. This is consistent with the scholarly consensus and with my own analysis in Senegambia and the Atlantic Slave Trade.
- **The horse-captive cycle is correctly identified as a self-reinforcing mechanism.** The chapter's analysis of horses requiring captives, captives requiring raids, raids requiring horses is sound and reflects the historical reality of the Senegambian horse trade economy.
- **The Bemoim episode is handled well.** The framing of his journey as a political strategy rather than a tale of cultural encounter is historically defensible and avoids the patronizing narratives that have characterized some Portuguese historiography.

## Cultural Authenticity

- The chapter correctly presents Wolof agency throughout the Portuguese encounter. The Wolof are not passive recipients of European trade but active negotiators who calculate costs and benefits within their own political framework.
- The treatment of the jon is balanced. The chapter presents the Wolof legal framework for captive status without apologizing for it, while also giving the jon physical presence as human beings. The distinction between jon status within the Wolof world (where it was bounded) and what awaited captives in the Portuguese system (where it was not) is an important and correct observation.
- **One concern:** The chapter states that the jon "had been someone before the battle that captured them. They had farmed millet or fished in the river or woven cotton in a village compound." This is well-intentioned and humanizing, but it risks implying that most jon in this period were Wolof farmers and fishermen. In practice, many of the captives traded to the Portuguese were taken in raids on neighboring non-Wolof peoples (Serer, Toucouleur, peoples along the Gambia). The chapter should clarify the geographic and ethnic diversity of the captive population.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The presentation of the Saharan and Atlantic trade systems as coexisting rather than the Atlantic immediately replacing the Saharan is an important and often overlooked nuance. The salt trade continued to give the Saharan merchants relevance even as the horse trade shifted to the Atlantic.
- The women in the coastal compounds counting absences (Section IV) is a powerful and historically grounded detail. Women in Senegambian societies managed household economies and were acutely aware of the demographic effects of the raiding-and-trade cycle.
- The chapter avoids the trap of treating the confederacy's dissolution as a tragedy caused by European contact. It correctly identifies internal structural dynamics as the primary driver, with the Atlantic trade as a catalyst.

## What Needs Correction

- **Captive origins:** The chapter should specify that jon traded to the Portuguese came from diverse sources, including raids on neighboring peoples, not only from internal Wolof conflicts.
- **Exchange ratio qualification:** The 7-15 captives per horse range is attested but varied significantly by period. In the earliest decades of contact (1440s-1460s), the Portuguese had less bargaining power and the ratios may have been lower. As Portuguese competition increased, the ratios generally shifted in favor of the Wolof sellers. The chapter could note this dynamic.

## Verdict: APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

Conditions: (1) Clarify the geographic and ethnic diversity of captive origins. (2) Note the dynamic nature of exchange ratios over the period.
