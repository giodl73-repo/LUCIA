# The Deep Time

## The Fire

The fire had been burning for hours, and the women were singing.

They sat in a circle, close enough that their shoulders touched, and the song moved between them the way water moves through sand. It was not melody in the way a listener from elsewhere might expect melody. It was a pulse, layered, the voices sliding over and around each other in intervals that belonged to this desert and no other place. The children sat behind the women. The old people sat behind the children. Beyond them, the Kalahari opened in every direction under a sky so thick with stars it seemed to have weight. The cold pressed in from the darkness. The men danced.

They danced in a circle around the fire, around the women, their feet striking the sand in a rhythm that answered the singing, and the singing answered the feet, and the whole thing held together the way a living body holds together. The n/um was rising. You could not see it, but the dancers could feel it. N/um is the energy that lives in the songs and in the fire and in the healer's body, coiled at the base of the spine like something waiting. When the dancing is right and the singing is right and the fire is right, the n/um rises. It climbs the spine. It reaches the skull. And then the healer is somewhere else.

/Twi!kxao was the one who went first tonight. He had been dancing for two hours, his feet wearing a trench in the cold sand, and his breathing had changed, short and sharp, the kind of breathing that comes when something is being pulled out of you or pushed through you. The women closest to him adjusted their singing. They knew the signs. They had been watching healers enter |kia since they were children, and their mothers had watched before them. They knew when to shift the song, when to hold steady, when to pull a dancer back from going too far.

/Twi!kxao, when he spoke of it later, said the fire became very large and then very small and then he was inside it. He said the people around the fire were visible but changed. He could see their sickness, the dark threads of it moving beneath their skin. He said his hands burned. He said he placed his hands on the old woman, /Gao's mother, who had been coughing for weeks, and he pulled the sickness out through his palms and screamed it into the night. He said the scream was not his. It belonged to the sickness, leaving.

The community held him. A healer who enters |kia alone can die there. The spirit world is not gentle, and the doorway between this world and that one does not care whether you come back. The singers hold the trance. The dancers who are not yet in |kia hold the dancers who are. The fire holds them all. No one leaves the circle until the healing is done, because the circle is not an arrangement of people. It is the instrument, and every voice in it is necessary.

When /Twi!kxao came back, he was shaking. They lowered him to the sand near the fire and the women shifted the song to the cooling melody, the one that brings the n/um back down, that closes the doorway gently. His sweat cooled in the night air. Someone put a skin blanket over his shoulders.

On the rock shelters of the Drakensberg, a thousand kilometers to the east, the walls hold this. Painted figures bent forward in the posture the healers call the "dying," arms back, legs trembling, the body emptying itself to make room for the spirit. Lines of energy rising from the dancers' heads. Blood from the nose, which comes in deep trance and which the painters recorded with an exactness that tells you they knew what they were painting. Therianthropes, figures half human and half eland, because the eland is the animal closest to the spirit world, the animal whose fat holds the most n/um, the animal whose death in the hunt most closely mirrors the healer's death in trance. These are not decorations. They are theology, recorded on stone in pigment mixed from ochre and eland fat and blood, and some of them have been on these walls for twenty-seven thousand years.

The oldest of the paintings are older than any other continuous artistic or spiritual practice whose record survives. How much older the trance dance itself is, nobody can say. The San's ancestors were in this land at least sixty-five thousand years ago. The paintings begin at twenty-seven thousand. Between those two numbers is a silence no one can fill. What survives is what persists: the dance, the songs, the knowledge of when to shift the song, and the people who carry it.


## The Tracker's Reading

In the morning, the Kalahari is a different country. The cold has burned off. The sand holds the light and throws it back, and the air shimmers above the ground by midmorning, but now, in the first hour after sunrise, the light is low and angled and everything casts a shadow.

This is when the tracker reads.

!Nai crouched where the spoor crossed a flat of open sand between two stands of mongongo trees. He had been walking since before dawn with his nephew /Ui, and they had not spoken for some time. The marks in the sand were faint. A person who had not spent a lifetime learning to see them would not have seen them at all: a slight compression here, a drag mark there, a pebble displaced by the width of a fingernail. !Nai saw an eland. A male, large, moving east. The left foreleg was favoring, a slight unevenness in the stride that shortened the print on that side by a fraction. The eland had passed in the night, perhaps four hours ago. It was not running. It was walking toward the pan where the tsamma melons grew thick this season, because an eland with a sore leg does not waste energy.

!Nai knew this the way he knew the weight of a digging stick in his hand. He had been reading spoor since he could walk. His father had walked him across sand and asked him what he saw, and when he saw nothing his father had pointed, and over years the nothing became something and the something became a language written in the earth that was as legible to him as any text.

But tracking is not simply reading. !Nai was reasoning. He had formed a picture of the eland's condition, its direction, its likely destination, and the time that had passed since it made these marks. Each of these was a hypothesis. The direction might change at the next water point. The injury might be worse than the spoor suggested, which would mean the eland was slower, which would mean it was closer. The time estimate depended on how much dew had collected in the prints overnight. !Nai tested each hypothesis against the next piece of evidence as he walked: a browsed bush confirmed direction, a deeper print at a soft patch confirmed weight, the absence of bedding-down marks meant the eland had not rested, which meant it was still moving when dawn came.

/Ui watched and learned. He was learning not to track, exactly, but to think the way a tracker thinks. His uncle did not explain every inference. He pointed sometimes, paused sometimes, changed direction sometimes, and /Ui was learning to reconstruct the reasoning from the decisions. In another ten years, he would be doing this alone. In another twenty, he would be teaching his own nephew.

The hunt would take two days. !Nai already knew approximately where it would end: near the pan, where the tsamma melons drew the eland and where the soft ground would slow a limping animal. But "approximately" is not "certainly," and the next two days would be a continuous conversation between !Nai's predictions and the eland's decisions, each new piece of evidence confirming or revising what he thought he knew.

They walked east. The sand was still cool enough that their bare feet left clear prints of their own. By noon those prints would be half-erased by the heat, the sand drying and crumbling at the edges.

As they walked, !Nai noted what the land was carrying. A stand of bi! bulbs near a termite mound, the leaves dry now but the tubers beneath the sand still holding water. Tsamma melons in a shallow depression, their green rinds paling toward yellow, which meant they were ripe, which meant water for the return journey. A grewia bush with berries the birds had not yet taken. The tracking and the gathering were not separate knowledges. They were one attention: what is the land doing, what is living on it, what does it offer, what does it demand. The women who gathered knew the eland's movements because they walked the same ground. The trackers who followed the eland knew where the bi! bulbs grew because their lives depended on water in every form it took.

In the deep Kalahari, there were seasons when the only water for a hundred kilometers was in the land itself: in tsamma melons, in bi! roots and dozens of other water-bearing tubers, in the sip-wells that the San dug in seasonal riverbeds and plugged with grass bundles, sucking water up through hollow reeds. Knowing where these sources lay and when they held water and how long they would last was the knowledge that separated living from dying in this landscape. It was carried in memory, and the memory was shared, and the sharing was life.

But !Nai's attention now was on the prints ahead of him, and they were telling him a story that had started in the dark and would not end until the eland fell or escaped, and either outcome would teach him something he would carry into the next hunt and the next and the one after that.


## /Kaggen Makes the Eland

At night, around a different fire, the old woman N!aisa told the children how the eland came to be.

/Kaggen, the Mantis, found the shoe of his son-in-law /Kwammang-a near the water. It was a piece of leather, that shoe, and /Kaggen took it and soaked it in the water and hid it among the reeds. He came back every day to feed it honey. And the leather grew. It swelled and it moved and it breathed, and one morning /Kaggen came to the water and found an eland calf standing in the reeds, wet and trembling. He had made it from a shoe.

The children laughed at this, because /Kaggen is the kind of creator who makes things from scraps and accidents, who steals and hides and lies to his own family, who gets things wrong more often than he gets them right and then fixes them by getting something else wrong in a way that accidentally works. He is not a dignified creator. He is not solemn. He is the Mantis, the trickster, the one who shaped the world not through wisdom but through persistent, improvisational, occasionally brilliant foolishness.

N!aisa told the story the way her mother had told it, with the pauses in the right places: the pause before /Kaggen discovers his family has found the eland and killed it, the pause before his grief, the pause where the children leaned forward because they knew what was coming and wanted to hear it anyway. /Kaggen's grief when the eland dies is real. He made it. He loved it. He fed it honey every day. And when /Kwammang-a kills it, /Kaggen fights with him, and the fight is messy and undignified, and the blood of the eland falls on the ground and becomes the first eland herd, and the world is changed because /Kaggen could not stop grieving for a thing he had made from a shoe.

It is the story of why the eland is sacred: because it was made by the creator, because it was the first animal, because its death was the first grief. And it is the story of why the eland hunt carries the weight it does: to kill an eland is to re-enter /Kaggen's grief, to participate in the first loss, and to receive from it the fat and the meat and the blood that carry n/um. The hunt, the trance, and the story are the same knowledge held in different forms.

N!aisa was not reciting. She was teaching. The children who sat at her fire would carry these stories for the next fifty years, and when they told them to their own grandchildren, the pauses would be in the same places, because the pauses are part of the knowledge.

The ||Xam man /Kabbo, who dictated his people's stories to Wilhelm Bleek and Lucy Lloyd in a Cape Town house in the 1870s, spoke of this carrying. He said he wanted to go home so he could sit by a fire again and listen to the stories coming from a distance, carried by the wind. He said the stories were in the wind. He meant it. The wind carried the voices of the people who told them, and the people who had told them before, and the land itself was made of stories that had been told across it for so long that the stories and the land had become the same thing.


## Insulting the Meat

When !Nai brought the eland home, the camp did not celebrate.

This was correct. This was how it was supposed to go. !Nai had tracked the eland for two days, killed it with a poisoned arrow and waited for the poison to do its work, and then butchered it in the field with /Ui's help, carrying the meat back in loads over the course of an afternoon. It was a large male. There was enough meat and fat to feed the camp for days. And when he arrived, people looked at the meat and said: is that all?

They said: this meat is thin. They said: is this what you call an eland? They said: we thought you went hunting, but it seems you brought us scraps.

!Nai said nothing, or he said yes, it was a poor animal, he was sorry.

This is what the Ju/'hoansi call "insulting the meat." When Richard Lee first witnessed it in the 1960s at Dobe, he asked why. The answer he received was precise.

"When a young man kills much meat," one man told him, "he comes to think of himself as a chief or a big man, and he thinks of the rest of us as his servants or inferiors. We can't accept this. We refuse one who boasts, for someday his pride will make him kill somebody. So we always speak of his meat as worthless. This way we cool his heart and make him gentle."

Cool his heart. The phrase is exact. In a community where survival depends on sharing and sharing depends on no one claiming more than their portion, the accumulation of prestige is dangerous. A man who is praised for his hunting might begin to feel that his hunting entitles him to more. Entitlement breaks the circle. So the community has a practice, learned and transmitted and consistent across Ju/'hoan groups separated by hundreds of kilometers, that prevents the accumulation of prestige at the moment when prestige is most likely to form. It is not cruelty. It is not modesty. It is a mechanism, as deliberate and as functional as any constitutional check on power.

The meat was divided. Not by !Nai. The owner of the first arrow that struck the eland is the owner of the meat, and because arrows are borrowed and lent through the hxaro exchange network, the "owner" of the kill might be someone who was not even present at the hunt. This meant that the distribution of meat followed the web of social obligation rather than the logic of individual achievement. You ate because you were connected, not because you had killed.

The hxaro network extended further than the meat. Gifts moved between Ju/'hoan communities across hundreds of kilometers: ostrich eggshell beads, worked leather, knives, blankets. These were not trade goods in the market sense. They were obligations made physical. When a family at Dobe gave beads to a family at /Xai/xai, they were investing in a relationship that might, in a drought year or a bad hunting season, mean the difference between starvation and survival. The beads would travel back eventually, or something of equivalent value would, and the obligation would renew itself.

This is what held a community together across a landscape where water was seasonal, game was mobile, and no single camp could guarantee its own survival from one year to the next. The sharing ethic was not a preference. It was the social structure that made desert life possible. It distributed risk across space and time. It made every person's success everyone's resource and every person's failure everyone's problem. And it did this without leaders, without laws, without enforcement of any kind beyond the steady social pressure of people who knew each other completely and would remember everything.


## The Eland's Body

The fat was the most valued part.

Eland fat is different from the fat of other animals the San hunt: it is firm, white, rendered easily, and when rubbed on the skin it has a faint sweet smell. The women rendered it over a slow fire and stored it in cleaned intestinal casings, and it served every purpose. It was food, the richest food available. It was medicine, rubbed on the sick, mixed with ground buchu leaves for healing. It was cosmetic, smoothed on skin cracked by sun and wind. And it was the medium for paint: ground ochre mixed with eland fat became the pigment that went on rock walls and stayed there for thousands of years.

The eland was the animal /Kaggen made from a shoe and fed with honey. It was the animal whose hunt required the deepest tracking knowledge, the longest endurance, the most precise understanding of landscape and season and animal behavior. It was the animal whose death carried spiritual weight because to kill an eland was to re-enter /Kaggen's story, to participate in the first grief. It was the animal whose meat, when brought to camp, was insulted to cool the hunter's heart. It was the animal whose fat became the paint on the rock walls, which recorded the trance visions of the healers who danced around the fire where the stories of /Kaggen were told. And the trance dance itself drew on the eland's power: the most potent n/um was eland n/um, and when a healer entered |kia at the deepest level, the healers said he was "dying" the way the eland dies, trembling, lowering, bleeding from the nose, the spirit leaving the body.

One animal. The story, the hunt, the sharing, the painting, the trance. Not five separate practices that happen to involve the same species. One knowledge, held in different forms, each form reinforcing the others, the whole system carried across thousands of years not because anyone decreed it but because every part of it needed every other part.

One knowledge. Sixty-five thousand years deep.


## The Painted Walls

The rock shelter at the upper Drakensberg was cool and dry and smelled of stone dust and the faintly sweet rot of bat droppings in the high crevices. Light came in at an angle from the shelter's mouth, falling across the rock face in a band that moved as the sun moved, and the paintings appeared gradually as your eyes adjusted: first the large eland panel, the animals rendered in polychrome with the careful shading that distinguishes the finest Drakensberg work, and then, around and between and overlapping the eland, the human figures. Dancers. Some upright, some bent forward in the "dying" posture, some transformed into therianthropes with eland heads on human bodies or human heads on eland bodies. Some were bleeding from the nose. Some had lines extending from their heads. Some were surrounded by smaller figures, the ones the scholars call "attendants" and the San call "the ones who hold."

The panels layered. Older paintings showed through newer ones. An eland from one century was partially covered by a dancer from another, who was in turn overlapped by a therianthrope from a third. The rock face was not a single composition but a palimpsest: generations of painters returning to the same surface, adding their own experience to the accumulated record. A single panel might contain images separated by thousands of years, and the later painters had painted around and among the earlier ones with the care of someone entering a conversation that had been going on for longer than they could remember.

The pigment itself carried information. The reds came from iron oxide, ground and heated to different temperatures to produce different shades: dark red from cooler processing, bright orange from hotter. The whites came from clay or bird droppings. The blacks came from manganese oxide or charcoal. The binder was eland fat, mixed with blood. The painter's knowledge was technical as well as spiritual: which ochre from which outcrop produced which shade, how to prepare the surface, how to apply the pigment so it would bond with the rock and survive the centuries of rain that sheeted across the shelter's face.

At the Tsodilo Hills in Botswana, three hundred kilometers northwest of the Kalahari camps, the rock faces hold what the San call the mountain of the gods. Paintings cover the surfaces of more than four thousand individual panels. Some are recognizable as the same tradition that produced the Drakensberg work: eland, dancers, therianthropes. Others are geometric: grids, dots, nested curves, the patterns that dancers see in the first stage of trance, before the visions sharpen into animals and spirits. The oldest securely dated rock art in southern Africa comes from Apollo 11 Cave in Namibia, perhaps twenty-seven thousand years ago; the Tsodilo dates are less precisely established but span a comparable range. The newest were painted in the nineteenth century.

Twenty-seven thousand years of continuous practice at a single site. What changed? The style changed. The species depicted shifted as the environment shifted. Techniques were refined, abandoned, recovered. New images appeared as new experiences entered the tradition. But the core practice held: people went to sacred rock faces, entered altered states of consciousness through the trance dance, and recorded what they saw. They did this in the same places, using the same materials, for longer than any other artistic or spiritual practice that has left a physical record. Not every scholar reads the paintings this way. Some see wider meanings, functions that the trance hypothesis alone may not capture. The panels may hold more than anyone has yet understood. But the trance connection is what the San themselves described when asked, and it is what the evidence most consistently supports.

The question of what we cannot know presses here. The paintings survive. The wood and leather and grass that made up most of San material life did not. We see the spiritual practice because it was recorded on stone. We do not see the ten thousand other dimensions of daily life that were carried in bodies and voices and decomposed with them. What the paintings represent is not the whole of San life, or even most of it. They are the part that stone preserves. The knowledge of tracking, of plants, of water, of weather, of social obligation, of child-rearing, of comedy, of grief, of how to be a person in this particular landscape under these particular stars, was carried in forms that do not fossilize. The paintings are the visible edge of something immeasurably larger, like the dorsal fin of a whale.


## What Persists

The fire was low now and the cold had deepened. The stars had shifted. The Milky Way, which the San call the backbone of the night, had turned on its axis the way it does every night, because the earth moves and the stars only seem to stay still, and the people who have watched this sky for longer than anyone else know its movements the way they know the movements of the eland.

The children who had watched /Twi!kxao heal /Gao's mother had fallen asleep, most of them, curled under karosses near the fire, though one girl was still awake, sitting with her chin on her knees and her eyes on the coals. She had watched the whole night. She had seen the n/um rise and the healer enter |kia and the community hold him and the sickness leave. She would not dance for years yet. But she had seen it done, and seeing it done was the first step in the knowledge that she would spend her life learning, if she chose to learn it, and that her daughters and her daughters' daughters would carry, in the way that the people of this desert have always carried it: in the body, in the voice, in the fire, in the sand where the tracks are written and erased and written again.

No one wrote it down. It did not need to be written down. It was danced and sung and tracked and told and painted on rock, and the rock held it, and the voices held it, and the bodies held it, and the land itself held it because the land and the knowledge were the same thing, made of each other, inseparable after sixty-five thousand years.

How long is that? Long enough that the question becomes its own answer. The San's ancestors were walking this ground before the last ice age. They painted the rock shelters before the last ice sheets melted. They carried the trance dance across millennia so vast that the word "millennia" becomes meaningless, a unit too small to measure the thing it describes. And they are here. The Ju/'hoansi of the Kalahari live in a different world now than the one the ethnographers documented in the 1960s. The economy has changed. The politics have changed. The pressures are new. But the dance continues. The songs continue. The trackers still read the sand in the morning light. The old women still tell the story of /Kaggen and the shoe and the eland calf in the reeds. The children still watch.

/Kabbo, the ||Xam man who spoke his people's stories into the Bleek and Lloyd notebooks in the 1870s because the colonial government had brought him to Cape Town in chains and he could not go home, said a thing that carries the weight of everything. He said he sat waiting for the moon to turn back, so that he could return to his place, and sit listening to the stories which come from a distance. He said the wind would bring them.

He never went home. The ||Xam land was taken. The communities were scattered. The language fell silent. But the notebooks survived, and /Kabbo's words survived in them, and the wind he was waiting for still blows across the Karoo, carrying whatever it carries, and the people who are still here, the Ju/'hoansi of the Kalahari and the San communities scattered across southern Africa, still dance around fires under the same stars, and the n/um still rises, and the children still watch.

The fire burned low. Somewhere beyond the circle, a jackal called, and then the desert was quiet again, the kind of quiet that is not empty but full, the silence that holds every sound the land is making for anyone patient enough to hear.

The girl rested her chin on her knees and waited.
