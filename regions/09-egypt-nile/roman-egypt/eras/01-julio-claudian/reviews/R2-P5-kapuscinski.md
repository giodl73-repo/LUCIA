## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — PANEL-2 Review of Notes

**Focus:** Sensory planning across sections

**What the notes resolve:**
Each section now has a planned sensory anchor: black soil and granary (S2), desert road and pepper warehouse (S3), temple interior and bull (S4), Alexandria streets (S5). This is responsive to my concern.

**What I want to feel in each section:**

**Section 2 (Granary):** The black soil between the farmer's fingers when the flood recedes. The smell of the granary: dry grain, dust, the particular warmth of stored wheat in an Egyptian summer. The sound of the nilometer reading: the priest descending the staircase to the water's edge, the marks on the stone. The weight of the grain in the baskets loaded onto the barges.

**Section 3 (India Trade):** The desert crossing from Coptos to Berenice or Myos Hormos: twelve days through heat that kills. The watering stations. The Red Sea port itself: the smell of pepper warehouses is overwhelming and specific (Sidebotham's excavations describe the pepper residue still present in storage jars). The sound of the harbor: multiple languages, the creak of rigging, the slap of water against hulls. The monsoon wind itself: the shift from northeast to southwest that opens the sea.

**Section 4 (Temple):** The interior of an Egyptian temple under Roman rule. The darkness: these temples were dark, deliberately, with light admitted through clerestory windows to fall on specific surfaces at specific times. The smell of incense. The sound of ritual. The bull itself: its physical presence, its size, the marks on its hide that the priests verify.

**Section 5 (Alexandria):** The Canopic Way, the main street, was said to be wide enough for multiple chariots. The harbor: the Pharos lighthouse. The Mouseion's gardens. The smell of a city of half a million people. The Jewish quarter on the eastern side. The noise.

**Verdict:** The sensory plan is adequate in outline. It needs to be executed with specificity drawn from the archaeological and literary sources (Strabo, Periplus, Sidebotham excavations, temple architectural studies). Do not invent sensory detail; derive it from what we know about these physical spaces.
