# Production Notes — The First Dynasty: Hammurabi's Babylon (Era 1)

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** Law as public theology. The publication of cosmic order in human speech.

**Mechanism:** The Babylonian king derives legitimacy not from military conquest alone but from the claim that he maintains the cosmic order (kittu and misharu, truth and justice) that the gods decreed. The law code is the physical proof of this claim. When the code is public, the king's legitimacy becomes verifiable: anyone who can read the stele can test the king's justice against the standard the king himself published. This creates a self-binding loop. The king publishes the standard by which he will be judged, and the standard is cosmic, not personal, so neither the king nor any successor can alter it without claiming to alter the order of the gods.

**Terminal condition:** The mechanism consumes itself when the political situation changes. The code is carved in stone; the world is not. When the Hittites sack Babylon in 1595 BCE, the dynasty ends, but the code survives because the stone survives and because the idea that law should be public has been absorbed into the intellectual tradition. The terminal condition is not the dynasty's fall but the moment when the code stops being the living law and becomes the inherited monument: still copied, still studied, still revered, but no longer the instrument of a ruling king.

## Arc Design

1. **The Stele** (~800w, opening, integrated) — The stele in the Esagila. Shamash and the rod and ring. The law cases quoted. The merchant reading. The central claim: law is public, law is cosmic order made visible. DONE as opening.md. Will revise per panel feedback: add sensory grounding (Esagila precinct, heat, mud brick, the transition from karum to temple).

2. **The Gate of the Gods** (~800w) — Bab-ilim before Hammurabi. The Amorite migration into the Sumerian-Akkadian cities. The theological problem: how do newcomers from the western steppe claim the authority of Marduk's city? The answer: they don't claim it. They submit to it. The Amorite kings take Akkadian names, worship Akkadian gods, write in Akkadian, and govern through the existing temple-and-canal system. The city absorbs its conquerors. Marduk's rise in the divine assembly parallels Babylon's rise among cities. The Enuma Elish as political theology.

3. **The Patient King** (~800w) — Hammurabi's first twenty-nine years. The year-names as a window into his priorities: canal maintenance, temple construction, wall building. The political situation: Shamshi-Adad of Assyria controls the north, Rim-Sin of Larsa controls the south, Eshnunna holds the Diyala, Elam stands behind the mountains. The diplomatic correspondence (the Mari letters): Hammurabi negotiating alliances, shifting allegiances, building the army the canals are paying for. One scene: the canal at the Sippar intake, workers reinforcing the levee in the spring, the year-name that records it.

4. **The Conquest and the Code** (~900w) — Year 30 to Year 42. The Elamite coalition breaks. Hammurabi's campaigns in quick succession: Larsa, Eshnunna, Mari. The destruction of Mari (Zimri-Lim's palace burned, the archive that survives the burning). Then the law. The prologue's theological argument. The epilogue's curse against anyone who alters the words. The specific cases: the merchant's contract, the physician's liability, the builder's responsibility, the woman's property rights. Law as the technology that holds a multi-city empire together.

5. **The Household of the God** (~700w) — Daily life under the code. The temple economy: Marduk's estate managed by the king's administration. The naditu women: priestesses of Shamash at Sippar who could own property, engage in commerce, but could not bear children. Their cloister, the gagum, is archaeologically attested. Their business records survive. The scribal schools: a student copying a contract, a teacher correcting the case form. The ration lists. The silver economy. The smell of the breweries, the weight of the grain measures.

6. **The Hittite Sack and the Stone That Stayed** (~600w) — The dynasty weakens after Hammurabi's son Samsu-iluna. The south rebels. The Sealand dynasty takes the marshes. The canal system frays. In 1595 BCE the Hittite king Mursili I raids down the Euphrates and sacks Babylon and carries off the statue of Marduk. The dynasty ends. But the code survives. The stele itself was carried to Susa by an Elamite king centuries later, where the French found it in 1901-02. The idea survives longer than the stone: Babylonian scribes copied the code for a thousand years after the dynasty fell. The law became curriculum. The publication became inheritance.

## Key Figures

- **Hammurabi** (r. c. 1792-1750 BCE) — Sixth king of the First Dynasty. The primary figure. His year-names, his letters (found at Mari), and the code itself provide unusually rich documentation for a ruler of this period. Present him through his own records: the patient canal-builder of the early years, the swift conqueror of the thirtieth year, the lawgiver of the epilogue.

- **The merchant in the karum** — Unnamed. Represents the commercial class attested in thousands of Old Babylonian contract tablets. Not a composite; a type. His presence at the stele is the test of the code's publicness: can the law reach the person it governs? Render through the material evidence (silver, tin, textiles, contracts, seal impressions).

- **Zimri-Lim of Mari** — King of Mari, Hammurabi's ally and then victim. His archive (over 20,000 tablets, one of the largest Old Babylonian archives) provides the diplomatic correspondence that illuminates the political world. Present through the letters: the alliances, the gifts, the intelligence reports, and then the silence when Hammurabi destroys the palace.

- **A naditu priestess at Sippar** — Unnamed but specific type. The naditu women are attested in hundreds of legal documents from Sippar's gagum (cloister). They owned houses, loaned silver, adopted heirs, and navigated the legal system the code describes. Render through her business records.

- **Marduk** — Not a figure in the human sense. The divine presence that organizes the theological world. His rise in the divine assembly parallels Babylon's political rise. Present as the Babylonians experienced him: real, present, owner of the city, source of the king's mandate.

- Sippar excavations (the gagum, the Shamash temple)
- Mari palace excavation (Parrot, 1930s-70s)

**Honest uncertainty:**
- The stele was found at Susa, not at Babylon. An Elamite king (probably Shutruk-Nahhunte, 12th century BCE) carried it off as a trophy. We do not know its original placement in Babylon with certainty. The chapter places it in the Esagila based on the prologue's language and scholarly consensus, but this is inference.
- Old Babylonian Babylon itself is largely unexcavated below the Neo-Babylonian levels because the water table is too high. Much of what we know about the First Dynasty comes from other sites (Mari, Sippar, Ur, Nippur).
- The code's relationship to actual legal practice is debated. Some scholars (Bottero, Westbrook) argue it is a monument to royal justice rather than a functioning law code. Others (Roth, Charpin) argue it had genuine legal force. The chapter presents the code as the culture presented it: the king's publication of cosmic justice. The debate about its practical application will be noted in the chapter body.

## Voice Notes

Babylon's voice is **legal-public with theological weight**. Distinct from Sumer's sedimentary voice (accumulative, layer-on-layer, administrative-yet-mythic). Babylon's voice pronounces. The sentences close like clauses in a contract. The rhythm is the rhythm of public declaration: measured, weighted, designed to be heard in a courtyard. Short declarative sentences carry theological certainty. Longer sentences build the cases, the contracts, the conditions, the way the code itself builds case upon case.

The colon is the voice's natural pause mechanism. "The stele said: this is the order of the cosmos." Not the em-dash (which interrupts) but the colon (which introduces, which opens the next clause the way a legal case opens the next condition).

Em-dash budget: 0. The voice does not interrupt itself. Declarations do not pause mid-pronouncement.

## Forbidden-Word Watch

No: primitive, advanced, civilization (as compliment), discovered by, tribe, Dark Ages.
Also: "code of laws" only if quoting scholarship; prefer "the cases" or "the law." Not "legal system" (modern). Not "jurisprudence" (modern). The law is the law. "Mesopotamia" only in orientation; default is "the plain" or "the land between the rivers" or "Bab-ilim." "Babylon" for the city; "Babylonian" for the people and the language.
