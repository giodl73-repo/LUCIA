# Fulɓe — "The Cattle Road and the Book"

The Fulɓe — called Peul in French, Fulani in Hausa, Fula in English — are one of the most widely distributed peoples in Africa, present from Senegal to Sudan, from the desert edge to the forest margin. What made them a coherent people across this vast dispersal was not territory but two things that traveled with them: cattle and knowledge. The pastoral Fulani moved their herds seasonally across the Sahel, negotiating access to water and pasture with the farming states they lived alongside. The scholarly Fulani — often called Torodbe, the "people of learning" — carried Islamic scholarship from city to city, serving as teachers, judges, advisors to rulers, and critics of those same rulers when their governance fell short of Islamic standards. The same people could be both: the lineage that owned cattle also produced scholars.

Usman dan Fodio, who launched the Fulani jihad in 1804, was the synthesis of both traditions. He came from a Torodbe scholarly family that had been settled in Gobir for generations. His critique of the Hausa ruling classes — their corruption, their syncretism, their mixing of Islamic practice with what he considered non-Islamic custom — was a scholar's critique, precise and textually grounded. But the movement that responded to his jihad call drew on the pastoral Fulani network's mobility and solidarity. The Sokoto Caliphate he built reorganized the entire western Sudan under Fulani-led emirates and sent ripples of reform east to Kanem-Bornu and west to the upper Niger. The Fulani transformation of the Sahel is among the most consequential political and religious events in nineteenth-century African history.

## What Defined Them

**Pulaaku — the Fulani code.** Pulaaku is the set of values that Fulɓe consider the definition of their identity: munyal (patience and self-control), semteende (modesty and shame), hakkiilo (care and forethought). These values shaped how the pastoral Fulani managed their relationships with the farming communities they lived alongside: dignified in adversity, restrained in abundance, careful in relationships. Pulaaku was not simply a personal ethic; it was the code that made coexistence possible in a world where herders and farmers competed for the same land and water. The scholar Fulani extended pulaaku into a standard for Islamic conduct: the good Muslim was also the good Fulani.

**The cattle economy as political network.** The pastoral Fulani's seasonal movements created a political network that crossed the boundaries of every settled state in the Sahel. A Fulani herder in Sokoto in December might be in Kano in March, in the Benue Valley in June, and back in Sokoto in October. The relationships formed along this circuit — with market towns, with farming villages, with fellow herders — constituted a solidarity network that the 1804 jihad could mobilize with extraordinary speed. When dan Fodio raised the jihad flag, Fulani flag-holders distributed across the Hausa states rose simultaneously in a coordinated action that the Hausa kings were not prepared for.

**The scholarly critique as revolutionary tool.** Dan Fodio's writings before the jihad were not private theological meditations; they were political documents circulated within the Fulani scholarly community. His *Ihya al-Sunna wa-Ikhmad al-Bid'a* (Reviving the Prophetic Practice and Extinguishing Innovation) detailed the specific practices of the Hausa rulers that he considered un-Islamic: the mixing of Islamic and non-Islamic law, the taxation that exceeded Islamic standards, the treatment of women, the complicity with the slave trade's violations of Islamic rules on enslaving free Muslims. This was a bill of indictment, and it had been building for twenty years before the jihad began.

**The emirate system as governance model.** The Sokoto Caliphate's administrative innovation was the emirate system: each conquered Hausa state became an emirate governed by a Fulani emir who held authority from the Sultan of Sokoto in exchange for loyalty and tribute. The emirs were not simply military commanders; they were expected to govern according to Islamic law, to support scholarship, and to maintain the reformed religious practice that the jihad had installed. The system was imperfect and many emirs governed in ways that reproduced the abuses dan Fodio had criticized. But the framework — Islamic law as the basis of governance, scholarly authority as the check on executive power — was a serious attempt to build a state on principles.

**The Fulani as a people without fixed territory.** The Fulani have never been a state-building people in the conventional sense of a people occupying a defined territory. Their identity is carried in the cattle, in the scholars, in the code of pulaaku — not in cities or walls. When the Sokoto Caliphate became a fixed state, it began to transform the Fulani from a mobile people into a governing class. The tension between the pastoral identity and the sedentary imperial identity was present from the beginning and has never been fully resolved.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Cattle Road:** A Fulani herder is moving his cattle south ahead of the dry season. He knows exactly where the water is, what the farming village at the junction will charge for access, and which route avoids the Hausa cattle tax collectors. He has been doing this his whole life. His father did it before him.

**2 — The Scholar in Gobir:** Usman dan Fodio is teaching a class in Gobir. The sarki's son is in the back row. Dan Fodio is teaching about the obligations of rulers according to Islamic law. The sarki's son is taking notes.

**3 — The Flag:** Dan Fodio has called the jihad. He has distributed flags to his followers, each one commissioned to raise it in their home territory. The flags go up across the Hausa states simultaneously. The Hausa kings are not prepared for a coordinated action.

**4 — Sokoto:** The caliphate has been founded. Dan Fodio is writing theology and leaving the governance to his son Muhammad Bello. He did not want a state; he wanted a community that lived according to the Prophet's example. Whether these are the same thing is a question he is still working out.

**5 — The Last Emir:** The British column has entered Sokoto. The Sultan has fled. The colonial officer is writing a report that will call this "the pacification of Northern Nigeria." The emir system will continue under British indirect rule. It will outlast the colonial period too.
