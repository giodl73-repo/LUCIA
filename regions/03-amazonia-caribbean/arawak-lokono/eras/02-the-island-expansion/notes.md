## Arawak / Lokono | c. 500 BCE–700 CE

**Status:** Notes stage (post-PANEL-1)

## 1. Sources

**Primary / Archaeological:**
- Rouse, Irving. *The Tainos: Rise and Decline of the People Who Greeted Columbus.* Yale UP, 1992. (Tier 2) — The foundational archaeological synthesis of Arawak island migration; documents the Saladoid ceramic series as the material signature of the island expansion.
- Siegel, Peter E. (ed.). *Ancient Borinquen: Archaeology and Ethnohistory of Native Puerto Rico.* Alabama UP, 2005. (Tier 2) — Excavation evidence for early Arawak settlement in Puerto Rico; dates of initial occupation c. 400–200 BCE.
- Boomert, Arie. *Trinidad, Tobago, and the Lower Orinoco Interaction Sphere.* Cairi Publications, 2000. (Tier 2) — The crucial archaeological study of the Orinoco-Caribbean interface; Saladoid sites on Trinidad as the gateway stage; argues for continuous population movement rather than single-wave migration.
- Curet, L. Antonio. "The Taíno: Patterns of Power, Hierarchy, and Complexity." In *A Companion to the Anthropology of the Caribbean*, ed. Garrido. Wiley, 2020. (Tier 2) — Social complexity of early Caribbean Arawak communities; chiefdom structures emerging in the Greater Antilles.
- Oliver, José R. *Caciques and Cemí Idols: The Web Spun by Taíno Rulers between Hispaniola and Puerto Rico.* Alabama UP, 2009. (Tier 2) — Trade network analysis; the cemí exchange system as the cultural infrastructure of the expanding network; relevant for Era 3 but background here.

**Linguistic:**
- Aikhenvald, Alexandra. "Arawak." In *The Amazonian Languages.* Cambridge UP, 1999. (Tier 2) — Documents the spread of Arawak-family languages through the Caribbean; analyzes lexical retention and divergence as evidence of migration routes and timing.
- Taylor, Douglas. *Languages of the West Indies.* Johns Hopkins UP, 1977. (Tier 2) — Classic study of Caribbean Arawak dialects; the island-language divergences from continental Lokono confirm the expansion's sequence.

**Manioc / Agricultural knowledge:**
- Olsen, Kenneth M. and Barbara A. Schaal. "Evidence on the Origin of Cassava: Phylogeography of Manihot esculenta." *PNAS* 96.10 (1999). (Tier 2) — Genetic study tracing cassava domestication to the southern Amazon; the Arawak expansion carried the crop northward.
- Rodríguez-Ramos, Reniel. *Rethinking Puerto Rican Precolonial History.* Alabama UP, 2010. (Tier 2) — Archaeological and botanical evidence for early Caribbean agriculture; the matapi (fiber press) as traded technology.

**Source situation:** The source base is primarily archaeological (Saladoid ceramics as the migration signature) and linguistic (dialect divergence as a clock). No written primary sources exist from the Lokono or proto-Taíno of this era. The source asymmetry should be used as a narrative technique: the ceramics and the language are the primary sources, and the chapter should render them as the voices they are.

## 2. Key Figures

**Kurima** (opening composite figure): The young man of the opening, based on the archaeological profile of the crossing-generation — Saladoid pottery-bearing communities who moved from the Orinoco delta through Trinidad and the Lesser Antilles. Not a named historical individual; a constructed figure honest to the archaeological evidence. The opening establishes him; the chapter will use him as the entry point but must then pull back to the network level.

**Unnamed elder/uncle**: Kurima's uncle represents the knowledge-transmission generation — the people who had already made the crossing and whose accumulated knowledge enabled the next generation's movement. The chapter should give this figure one specific scene: the moment of teaching.

**The island woman** (composite): For Achebe's concern about women's presence. Based on the evidence of women's roles in manioc processing and ceramic production, which are archaeologically gendered in the Saladoid tradition. A mainland woman who receives a returning trader, or an island woman who has established herself as a node in the network — both work. The manioc-processing scene should be hers.

**The Saladoid potter** (composite): The archaeological signature of the expansion is the Saladoid ceramic series — white-on-red painted ware with specific decorative conventions. Someone made these pots. For cultures where the archaeological record preserves objects but not names, the chapter's task is to embody the maker. This figure can be the chapter's most concentrated moment of knowledge-at-method-level.

## 3. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The canoe as social obligation made physical. The canoe is not transportation — it is the mechanism by which obligation networks are maintained, extended, and made real. Each crossing either honors an existing obligation or creates a new one. The network grows because the canoe crosses; the canoe crosses because the network requires maintenance.

**Mechanism:** Obligation → canoe → new node → new obligation. The engine is self-generating: every crossing creates new relationships that require future crossings. The expansion is not directed from above; it is the cumulative product of obligation maintenance at the level of individual households.

**Terminal condition:** The engine's terminal condition for this era is not collapse but transformation. By c. 700 CE, the island population has differentiated enough from the mainland population that the network's cultural coherence begins to strain. The island communities are not failing — they are becoming something new. The Lokono mainland and the proto-Taíno islands are still connected, but the connection is now between distinct peoples rather than between nodes of a single people. The era ends when the network produces cultural divergence sufficient to require a new category.

**Material object as structural principle:** The matapi — the fiber press used to squeeze cyanide-bearing liquid from grated bitter manioc — is the chapter's organizing object. Not because it is the most important Lokono artifact, but because it is the object that encodes the critical knowledge, travels with the expanding population, and represents the knowledge system that made expansion possible. The matapi is portable; it can be made in any new location from locally available fibers. Whoever knows how to make and use it can establish manioc agriculture in a new place within a single growing season. It is the migration technology most responsible for the expansion's success.

## 4. Narrative Arc — Six Sections

**Section 1 — The Crossing (c. 500 BCE, Orinoco delta)**
The opening scene (Kurima) integrated here. The crossing as a known, practiced event — not an adventure but a skilled labor that the community had been doing for generations. Establishes: the delta as the mainland node, the channel knowledge, the trade goods as social obligations made portable, the island-as-destination not as unknown but as the next node in a known network.

**Section 2 — The Matapi (manioc processing as migration technology)**
Shifts to a mainland woman's hands. The matapi: how it is made (woven from tirite palm), how it works (the cassava mash loaded, the basket twisted and stretched over a peg to press out the cyanide-bearing tucuma), how the knowledge is passed (mother to daughter, alongside the loom). The matapi as the reason the island expansion was possible: anyone who carried the knowledge could establish agriculture in a new place. Knowledge-at-method-level as the chapter's evidence claim.

**Section 3 — The Saladoid Signature (ceramics as network communication)**
The archaeologist's evidence: white-on-red painted ware appearing in the same stylistic tradition from the Orinoco delta to Puerto Rico. But the chapter does not tell this from the archaeologist's perspective — it tells it from the potter's: the conventions of the decorative tradition as a kind of network membership. To make the pots in the Saladoid style was to signal belonging. The chapter embodies the Saladoid potter making a vessel in the island style, knowing its decoration would be recognized by anyone in the network.

**Section 4 — The Island Node (c. 200 BCE–400 CE, Trinidad and the Lesser Antilles)**
The island communities as nodes in the network, not as isolated settlements. Evidence of trade objects moving in both directions: shell ornaments moving from island to mainland, manioc-processing equipment moving from mainland to island, ceramic styles evolving in parallel as the network maintained cultural connection across distance. The archaeology of exchange as the chapter's evidence base. Trinidad as the gateway node — the first island the delta population reached, close enough to maintain dense connection with the continent.

**Section 5 — The Divergence (c. 400–700 CE)**
The network's success produces its stress. As island communities become established — settled, agriculture-based, with their own reef-knowledge, their own fishing techniques, their own ceremonial traditions — the cultural distance from the mainland begins to grow. Language diverges. Pottery styles diverge. The network continues but is now connecting different peoples rather than the same people in different locations. This is not failure — it is successful cultural differentiation. The chapter presents it as the Lokono would have understood it: not as drift but as the network's own growth producing new kin-at-distance.

**Section 6 — The Mainland Holds (the Lokono as continental anchor)**
The continental Lokono community remains the network's root node. As the island population differentiates into what will become the Taíno, the mainland Lokono are not diminished — they are the source. The chapter closes on the delta, on the river mouth, on the next generation of young men loading canoes with the goods the island cousins have sent for, and the island cousins' goods coming back. The network still moves. The canoe crosses. The landing is not the island's emergence but the mainland's permanence — the root that sent out the branches and remained.

## 5. Panel Pressure Points (PANEL-1 addressed)

**Tuchman — narrative complication:** The chapter will give Kurima one moment of genuine difficulty: approaching the reef channel, where river-water knowledge fails and he must read the surf patterns without training. This is the "crisis" she wants. It also serves Davis's concern about the reef as epistemological threshold.

**Ibn Khaldun — governance of the network:** Section 4 will address the social structure of network expansion: the lonko (chief/leader) who validates new nodes, the mechanism by which reciprocity obligations are enforced (refusal of future exchange, exclusion from network), and the evidence that expansion was guided by established households rather than random individual initiative.

**Achebe — women's presence:** Section 2 is explicitly the woman's section. The matapi-processing scene is hers. The ceramic tradition (Section 3) is also predominantly female in the archaeological record. Women's knowledge — manioc processing, ceramic production — was the portable knowledge that made the expansion possible. The chapter will make this explicit through the figures, not through assertion.

**Davis — knowledge systems at method level:** Section 2 (matapi at method level) and the reef-approach passage (Kurima reading the surf patterns as a new knowledge domain he is entering) both respond to this. The matapi scene will show the technique in its specific steps.

**Kapuscinski — sensory anchor in the obligation section:** The obligation paragraph in the chapter will be grounded in a specific sensory memory — the smell of the cassava bread that came from the downstream community, the taste of it during the dry season when the garden produced nothing else.

## 6. Fixes Applied (PANEL-2)

**Ibn Khaldun — divergence driver:** The 400–700 CE divergence needs a specific mechanism. The chapter will use: the development of the batey (ball court) as an island-specific ceremonial institution that has no continental equivalent. When mainland Lokono receive island visitors who describe the batey, they recognize the game but not the institution's ceremonial weight. The ball court is the object of slight incomprehension that marks the network's cultural divergence — the island cousins still speak a recognizable language, but they are building a world the mainland did not grow.

**Davis — matapi at method level:** The processing scene will include the problem the technique solved (linamarin toxicity), the specific step that addresses it (liquid extraction under pressure, not just grating), and the knowledge required to know the liquid is the danger. The woman's hands know this in their bones; the chapter renders her as an expert performing an intellectual act, not a domestic chore.

**Davis — Saladoid ceramic detail:** The zone-incised crosshatch will be named and described as the network's identifying mark — the convention carried on trade-moving pots that signals membership. The potter's choice to apply it is a deliberate communicative act.

**Davis — reef-approach:** The scene will specify: color change over coral (turquoise over sand, dark over rock, purple-black over the living reef), surf-tone discrimination (the crash over the reef edge versus the softer break in the channel), and Kurima's decision to back water and wait for a local fisher to show him the channel. The gap between knowledge systems is honored rather than crossed by guesswork.

## 7. Open Questions (Residual)

- **Chronology precision:** The chapter will use "c. 500 BCE" as the conventional anchor and note scholarly debate in figures.md. Boomert's slightly later Trinidad dates do not change the narrative.
- **Batey institution timing:** The batey as a distinctly island institution may post-date 700 CE in its fully elaborated form; the chapter will present it as emerging rather than established at the era's close, which is appropriate to the divergence claim.
- **Saladoid zone-incised crosshatch:** The decorative convention is well-documented in Rouse and Boomert; the chapter will render it accurately.
