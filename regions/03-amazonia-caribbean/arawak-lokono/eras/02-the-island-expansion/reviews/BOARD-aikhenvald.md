## Overall Assessment

The linguistic framing is accurate and appropriate. The chapter uses language as evidence for the expansion without overclaiming what the linguistic data can tell us about dates or migration routes.

## Factual Review

**"Lokono" as the continental language name:**
- Used correctly throughout. "Lokono" is the Arawak name for themselves and for their continental language; "Arawak" is the family name applied by outsiders and adopted as a family designation. The chapter uses both without conflating them.

**"Same root of words but with sounds that had shifted, the way a river sounds different at its mouth than at its source":**
- This is an accurate and elegant characterization of what dialect divergence feels like from the inside. The phonological divergence between Lokono and the island varieties of Arawak (what became the Taíno language) is well documented: the basic vocabulary is shared, but the phonological shifts — especially in vowel quality and certain consonant clusters — are significant enough that a Lokono speaker and a Taíno speaker would have had mutual intelligibility at one remove, not full comprehension. The river metaphor is apt.

**"The Arawak language family... extended from Florida to the pampas of Argentina":**
- This appears in the overview context and is accurate. The Arawak family has one of the widest geographic distributions of any language family in the Americas, extending from the Caribbean coast of Florida (the Calusa had Arawak-family contacts) through the Caribbean to the Orinoco, Amazon, and into the southern cone. The pampas claim is technically accurate (Arawak-family languages are documented in northern Argentina) but the chapter does not make this claim in the chapter.md — it appears in the 00-OVERVIEW.md. No issue.

**Language-as-network-maintenance:**
- The chapter's implicit argument that the shared language (or recognizable linguistic family) was a network-maintenance technology is consistent with sociolinguistic research on contact languages and trade networks. Arawak-family languages show evidence of being used as contact languages in multi-ethnic trade zones — the "Arawak pidgin" hypothesis is debated but the evidence for Arawak as a trade-language is solid for later periods and is plausible for the expansion era.

**Divergence timing:**
- The chapter presents cultural divergence (batey emergence) as occurring in the 400–700 CE window. The linguistic divergence sufficient to require mediation (rather than mere tolerance of accent differences) would have occurred in this same timeframe or slightly later, based on the vocabulary retention rates. The chapter's cultural-divergence marker (batey) and the linguistic divergence are consistent.

## Corrections Required

**No corrections required.** The linguistic content is accurate within the limits of current scholarship.

**One observation (no revision required):** The chapter describes the island language as having "sounds that had shifted." The specific phonological shifts (vowel mergers, certain consonant changes) could in principle be named — but naming them would require technical linguistic vocabulary that would break the chapter's voice. The current level of specificity is the right choice for narrative prose. I note it as a source of potential depth for a scholar's edition, not as a criticism of the current chapter.

## Board Verdict

**ENDORSE.** The linguistic content is accurate, appropriately hedged, and serves the chapter's argument well. No revisions required.
