# Haebang — Production Bible

**Era:** Era 1: Haebang — Liberation and the Kim Il-sung State (1945–1950)  
**Culture:** Modern Korea (North) — DPRK  
**Region:** 26 — The Pacific Century  
**Date completed:** 2026-04-16

---

## The Story in One Sentence

A people emerging from colonial occupation accept a mythology about their founder because what that mythology offers — narrative sovereignty, the recovery of agency — matters more than the truth it obscures, and this bargain becomes the template for the state that follows.

---

## The Structural Engine

**Engine:** The **founding mythology** as political technology.

**Mechanism:** The Soviets and Americans partition Korea without consultation (external shock) → North Korean communists recognize that facts alone cannot generate legitimacy in a traumatized population (diagnostic moment) → they accept Soviet provision of a leader with a legendar name (bargain) → they commit to making that name true through institution-building (ritual and repetition) → the mythology becomes indistinguishable from truth because the state apparatus makes it so (transformation).

**Terminal Condition:** The state that begins by controlling the narrative of its own founding becomes dependent on that narrative. To question the founding is to threaten the foundation. The mythology is not a lie that can be abandoned once the state is stable. It is the state's permanent operating system. Any challenge to the mythology becomes, automatically, a threat to the state itself.

---

## Key Figures

1. **Kim Il-sung (or: the man the Soviets called Kim Il-sung)**
   - Age 33, arriving September 19, 1945
   - Identity: unclear, possibly not the original guerrilla fighter
   - Role: the vessel of the founding mythology, whether authentic or appointed
   - Interior: unavailable to us; will be rendered from outside perspective and through what others observe

2. **Im Hae-jo (schoolteacher)**
   - Observer of the ambiguity (the wrong cheekbones)
   - Represents the possibility of doubt
   - Later imprisoned for his doubt
   - Structural function: shows that the state will not permit questions

3. **Soviet Commander (unnamed)**
   - Presents the man and the name
   - Represents the external power that structures Korean choice
   - No interior; only functional

4. **The Crowd (collective)**
   - The moment when individual Koreans become a people
   - Their breath catching is the moment when mythology begins
   - Collective interior: hope, fear, willingness to believe

5. **Korean Mother (unnamed, background figure)**
   - Teaching Korean words in whispers during occupation
   - Represents what was lost and what is being recovered
   - Sensory focus: the risk of language

## Source Architecture

**Primary sources:**
- Soviet records of the Kim Il-sung installation (partial; heavily controlled)
- DPRK state media (Rodong Sinmun, official histories) — records the mythology as truth
- Defector testimonies (particularly early accounts of the 1945-1950 period)

**Academic sources:**
- Bruce Cumings, *North Korea: Another Country* — sympathetic account; the Korean perspective on liberation
- Brian Myers, *The Cleanest Race* — the internal ideology; how the mythology was constructed
- Andrei Lankov, *The Real North Korea* — systematic account of early state-building
- Barbara Demick, *Nothing to Envy* — generational transmission of belief

**Tier 3 (scholarly secondary):**
- Korean historical accounts from Seoul National University historians
- Contemporary accounts from UN observers

**Knowledge gap:** The subjective experience of ordinary Koreans during this period is thin. Most accounts are either from state archives (propaganda) or from defectors who left decades later (memory filtered through trauma). The chapter will need to work with source asymmetry: official narrative vs. fragmentary personal testimony.

## Narrative Arc (Five Sections)

### Section 1: The Arrival (revised opening ~800 words)
- The ship cutting through cold water
- The man at the rail
- The presentation of the name
- Im Hae-jo's doubt (the cheekbones)
- **Panel feedback incorporated:** Deepen sensory grounding throughout; add one moment from a Korean person in the crowd (interior observation)
- **Gate:** The ending of occupation, the beginning of something else

### Section 2: What Was Lost — The Japanese Occupation (1200 words)
- The reality of 35 years of total colonization
- Sensory rendering: what colonization did to the body (the schoolchild beaten for Korean), to language, to identity
- The moment of Japanese surrender (August 15, 1945)
- Koreans singing in the streets
- But the liberation is not complete — Soviet troops are already there, Americans are arriving
- **Function:** Establish what is being recovered and what is at stake in the mythology
- **Structural use:** "The colonial language suppressed" (from opening) becomes now: show a specific child, a specific school, a specific act of suppression

### Section 3: The Soviet Installation (1000 words)
- The Soviets occupy the north without asking permission
- The Communist Party cells: who are these people? Where did they come from?
- The decision to accept Soviet support and the man the Soviets provided
- The first state structures: land reform (specific plot, specific family), Party organization (specific meeting), the military (first soldiers)
- **Function:** Show the machinery of the mythology being constructed
- **Mechanism from Ibn Khaldun's feedback:** Give one concrete example of how the training begins. A party study session? A school lesson? Show the institutional lever.

### Section 4: The Name and the Legend (900 words)
- The historical Kim Il-sung: who was he? What was known about him?
- The legend that existed in Korean minds before the ship arrived
- The moment of merging: the man becomes the name because the need for the name is greater than the need for truth
- Voices from the period: what did people believe? What did they hope?
- **Function:** The transformation of ambiguity into mythology

### Section 5: The Bargain (700 words)
- Five years of state-building: 1945-1950
- The construction accelerates: more statues, more study sessions, more repetition
- The preparation for war (the Korean War will begin in 1950)
- The mythology is now complete enough that to question it is to threaten the state
- **Landing:** Return to Im Hae-jo's imprisonment, but now the reader understands it not as cruelty but as structural necessity. The state cannot permit doubt because it was built on mythology, and mythology is fragile. End with: a North Korean person in 1950, fully believing, not knowing the history of the doubt.

## Panel Pressure Points (anticipated from PANEL-1)

1. **Tuchman's point about the five-year summary:** Don't say "A military would be raised." Show raising it. Don't say "A mythology would be constructed." Show constructing it. Sharpen the middle section by moving toward scene and away from summary.

2. **Ibn Khaldun's point about concrete mechanisms:** One specific institutional example of how the belief was trained into the population. A school curriculum? A party rally? Show the machinery at method level.

3. **Achebe's point about Korean voice:** The culture is explained, not inhabited. Let at least one Korean person's interior be present in the scene. Not dialogue — just the experience of watching this mythmaking happen from inside.

4. **Davis's point about sensory depth:** The opening has good moments (cold water, breath, cheekbones) but retreats into abstraction (language suppressed, empire total). Render what these things felt like through bodies, not through concepts.

5. **Kapuscinski's point about immersion:** The landing is emotionally present but conceptual. "Mythology was what a traumatized people needed" — feel the trauma. What wound was the mythology trying to heal? Show that wound, not just the remedy.

## Historical Constraints and Opportunities

**What we know for certain:**
- September 19, 1945: a man presented as Kim Il-sung arrived in Wonsan
- October 14, 1945: the establishment of North Korea's Communist government
- 1945-1950: rapid state-building under Soviet sponsorship
- August 15, 1950: North Korea invaded the South, beginning the Korean War
- By 1950: the mythology was sufficiently established that the entire state would go to war in its name

**What we don't know:**
- Whether the man on the ship was actually the historical Kim Il-sung or a Soviet appointee
- The subjective experiences of most ordinary Koreans during this period
- The extent to which the early mythmaking was spontaneous vs. orchestrated
- What Soviet archives contain (heavily restricted)

**Source asymmetry strategy:** The uncertainty about Kim's identity is not a weakness to overcome. It is the story's center. The chapter will present this ambiguity as the founding condition, not as a problem to be solved. From inside the North Korean frame: whether he was authentic matters less than whether he could be believed.

---

## Voice and Register

**Confirmed voice type:** **Foundational / State-Building** — similar to legal-public and edict-inscriptional, but grounded in the moment of state emergence from colonial trauma.

- Short declarative openings (the arrival sequence)
- Longer accretions as the systemic complexity becomes clear
- Return to short declarative at moments of institutional decision
- The register should carry both narrative and analytical weight: the pacing of the prose DOES the work of showing how a traumatized people moves toward belief
- Zero or near-zero em-dashes (the rhythm creates its own pauses)
- Sensory ground on every page: what colonization felt like, what liberation felt like, what mythology feels like to live inside

## Open Questions for Chapter-Writing

1. What is the sensory experience of watching a legend materialize? How can the reader feel the displacement of the mythmaking moment?
2. How does the machinery of belief get installed at the institutional level? What does a study session look like? What does a child in school learn about Kim Il-sung?
3. What is the emotional substrate that makes mythology possible? What wound in the Korean soul is the mythology trying to heal?
4. How does the ambiguity about Kim's identity actually function in North Korean thinking? (Answer: it's not discussed. But what does that silence feel like to live in?)
5. By 1950, when the Korean War begins, how completely has the mythology taken hold? Is belief universal or performed? (Likely: indistinguishable.)

---

## Edge Cases and Sensitivities

- The chapter deals with trauma (colonization) and its remedy (mythology). The tone should be respectful without being reverential toward the mythology itself.
- Avoid: "The North Koreans were deceived" or "The mythology was propaganda." Instead: "The mythology was necessary, and the state made it true through repetition and institutional embedding."
- The imprisonment of Im Hae-jo should be presented not as cruelty but as structural logic: a state built on mythology cannot permit the question that would destroy it.
- The chapter is not arguing that the mythology was justified, only that it was intelligible given the circumstances.
