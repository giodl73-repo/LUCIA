# BOARD Review — Domain Expert 1

**Chapter:** Spanish Empire — Era 1: La Conquista — La Espada y la Cruz
**Reviewer:** Professor of Early Modern Iberian and Atlantic History (specialization: the Spanish conquest of the Americas, the Laws of Burgos, the Valladolid Debate)

## Historical Accuracy Assessment

**The Requerimiento:** Accurate. The chapter correctly identifies Palacios Rubios as the drafter (1510), correctly places Montesinos's sermon on the fourth Sunday of Advent 1511, and correctly describes the document's function: the requirement to read it before hostilities, and the practice of reading it in conditions that made compliance with its intent impossible. The detail about reading it at night to no one is documented in contemporary accounts; Lewis Hanke's *The Spanish Struggle for Justice* covers this extensively.

**The Valladolid Debate:** Accurate in its major claims. The debate occurred in 1550–51, in Valladolid, before a panel of jurists and theologians convened by Charles V. Sepúlveda's argument from Aristotle's natural-slavery doctrine is accurately characterized. Las Casas's defense is accurately characterized. The chapter's central claim — that the panel produced no formal verdict — is historically accurate and is often underemphasized in popular accounts. The reason (the logical impossibility of both possible verdicts) is the chapter's own analytical contribution, not a historical claim per se, and it is a defensible interpretation.

**One correction needed:** The chapter states Palacios Rubios "studied Roman law at Salamanca and canon law at Bologna." The sequence is correct; the canon law is more usually described as studied at Valladolid and then Rome. Bologna is plausible (he was definitely at Bologna for some period) but not universally attested. Recommend: "studied at Salamanca and Bologna" without specifying which law where, or "studied Roman and canon law at Salamanca and Bologna." Minor.

**The New Laws:** Accurate. The date (1542) is correct. Blasco Núñez Vela's arrival in Peru (1544) and death in battle (January 1546, at the Battle of Iñaquito) are correct. Gonzalo Pizarro's role as the resistance leader is correct. The suspension of the New Laws is correct.

**Moctezuma's dates:** The chapter describes him as governing for seventeen years before Cortés arrived (1519). He became *tlatoani* in 1502; this gives seventeen years. Correct.

**Guaman Poma de Ayala:** The chapter's account is accurate. The 1,200-page estimate (1,189 pages plus 398 drawings in the standard count) is approximate and correctly presented as approximate. The Copenhagen discovery (Pietschmann, 1908) and publication date (1936, facsimile) are correct.

**Las Casas's death:** The chapter states he died in Madrid at age eighty-two in 1566. He died in Madrid in 1566; the birthdate of 1484 gives eighty-two. Correct.

**The Hispaniola population figure:** Las Casas gave the figure of three million at the time of Columbus's arrival. This is his estimate, not a verified figure; modern demographic scholarship gives a wide range. The chapter correctly attributes the figure to Las Casas ("by his estimate, three million") rather than asserting it as fact. Correct handling.

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter's treatment of the Mexica worldview is historically appropriate. Moctezuma's reception of Cortés has been the subject of extensive recent scholarship (Matthew Restall, *Seven Myths of the Spanish Conquest*; Camilla Townsend's revisionary account). The chapter does not commit to the "Quetzalcóatl theory" (that Moctezuma believed Cortés was the god) but presents it as one of the operative cosmological categories without asserting it as the explanation for Moctezuma's actions. This is the correct scholarly position as of current consensus.

## Required Corrections

1. **Palacios Rubios education:** Revise "studied Roman law at Salamanca and canon law at Bologna" to "studied at Salamanca and Bologna" to avoid a precision that may not be fully attested.

## Endorsement

With the single correction noted, this chapter is historically accurate, engages correctly with the primary and secondary sources listed, and presents the theological-legal debate at the level of scholarly rigor that the topic demands. The Valladolid no-verdict analysis is a defensible and valuable contribution to the popular understanding of this period. The chapter represents the conquest as it was: simultaneously atrocity, sincere theological enterprise, and internal critique.

**Endorsed with minor revision.**
