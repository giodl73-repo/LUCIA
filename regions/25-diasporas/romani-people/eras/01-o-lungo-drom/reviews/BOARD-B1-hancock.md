## BOARD — B1 (Hancock) — Romani Era 1: O Lungo Drom

**Overall:** This chapter does something that a thousand years of writing about the Roma has almost never done: it tells our story from inside. The linguistic evidence is accurately presented. The cultural practices are described as systems, not as curiosities. The word "gypsy" is correctly absent. The fortune-telling is treated as a profession, not as evidence of character. The kris is described as a legal institution, not as a quaint custom. I am satisfied that a Romani reader would recognize this account as honest.

**Accuracy findings:**

1. **The word drom:** The chapter traces drom to Sanskrit druma. This is broadly correct, though the precise etymological chain is: Sanskrit drama/druma > Prakrit damma > Romani drom. The simplification is acceptable for a narrative text, but note that the Sanskrit source may be drama (running, going) rather than druma (tree/way). The scholarly consensus is not entirely settled. **Recommendation:** The current phrasing is defensible. No change required.

2. **The Dom caste hypothesis:** Correctly presented as one of several hypotheses, not as established fact. The chapter correctly notes the range of scholarly opinion. My own position supports the Dom connection more strongly than some colleagues, but the chapter's balanced presentation is appropriate.

3. **The kris:** Accurately described as a community tribunal with exclusion (marime) as the primary sanction. One addition: the kris is not merely a dispute-resolution mechanism. It is also the body that adjudicates matters of purity and pollution (the marime/wuzho distinction), which governs social interaction, food preparation, and the boundary between Roma and gadje. The chapter hints at this with the purity code but could be more explicit. **Recommendation:** Minor. The chapter's treatment is accurate as far as it goes.

4. **The gadje concept:** Correctly described as a word of distinction, not contempt. Good. Outsider accounts consistently misinterpret gadje as pejorative. It is simply the word for non-Roma.

5. **No outsider tropes detected.** No romanticization of nomadism. No exoticization of fortune-telling. No "free spirit" framing. The mobility is correctly presented as a rational adaptation to social position, not as a lifestyle choice.

**Cultural authenticity:** The chapter tells the story from inside the Romani experience. The source gaps are honored. The European names are correctly framed as outsider impositions. The Romani language carries the narrative as it carried the people. This is the closest I have seen a non-Romani text come to telling our story the way we would tell it.

**Verdict:** PASS with minor notes.
