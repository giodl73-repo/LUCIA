# Bakumatsu: The End of the Shogunate

## Section 1: The Breaking (July–September 1853)

The ships were black, and they came in the morning.

Tōjirō, the harbor master at Uraga, had served the Tokugawa shogunate for thirty years. The bay had been calm his entire tenure — calm as a thing could be when you served an empire that wanted no ships, no strangers, no disturbance of the order that had held for two hundred years. The edict was clear. Foreign vessels approaching the coast would be denied. If they refused to leave, they would be sunk. Tōjirō had never had to implement this. In thirty years, the bay had belonged to Japan alone.

July 8, 1853. Four ships. They did not look like ships.

The lookouts reported first: massive hulls, black iron, plumes of smoke rising where the sails should be. Not junks. Not any vessel Tōjirō's men had ever seen. They were steaming directly into Edo Bay, and they did not slow.

Tōjirō stood on the Uraga fortification and watched them come. The gunners waited for the order. He was a harbor master, not a military commander, but the edict made him the first guardian of the shogunate's order. He could feel the weight of two centuries in that moment — two hundred years of deliberate seclusion, of closed borders, of a Japan that had decided it did not need the world and the world could not have it. The fire stations along the bay were burning signal fires. The cavalry was being mustered. The message was spreading toward Edo — toward the shogun himself — even as Tōjirō tried to understand what he was looking at.

Four ships. One of them flew the American flag.

The lead vessel maneuvered with an ease that defied the mathematics of its size. Tōjirō had studied enough of the Dutch reports — the few that reached Japan in secret — to understand that these were powered by steam engines, not wind and current. The Americans could move these ships against wind, against tide, against the will of Uraga. The implication arrived before the ships did: Japan had a way to say no. The Americans had a way to make no sound like a question.

An officer rode up behind Tōjirō. Nakajima, the brigade commander. He was younger, but he was military. The harbor master was deferring now — he could feel it happening, the shift in authority as the appearance of the ships pulled power from the civilian administration toward the men trained for war.

"We fire on the signal," Nakajima said. Not a question.

Tōjirō hesitated. In thirty years, no one had fired. In thirty years, the edict had been enough.

"Commodore Matthew C. Perry," someone said. A man with better Dutch than Tōjirō held a translated letter. It had come by small boat — a messenger requesting permission to land. "He requests an audience with the shogun. He brings messages from the American president."

The letters were in Perry's hand, written to be delivered. The ships were in the bay. The American had not asked for permission to enter Edo Bay; he had entered and then announced that he wished to negotiate. The edict said no ships. The edict said no foreigners. The edict said Japan was closed, and this had been true for two centuries because everyone — everyone — had obeyed it.

Now someone had not.

Tōjirō looked at the ships, at the smoke, at the gunners waiting. The shogunate in Edo was debating. The emperor in Kyoto was not being consulted — the emperor was not consulted on matters of state. Power and decision-making had concentrated in Edo, in the hands of the shogun's council, in the protocols that had maintained order by maintaining separation. Everything about the way Japan had organized itself was predicated on a single assumption: we have decided we are closed, and the world will respect the boundary.

The ships suggested otherwise.

By July 12, the decision had been made in Edo. Moriyama Takichirô, a shogunal official, would board the American ship and receive Perry's letters. No shots would be fired. The edict could not be enforced. Japan would negotiate.

## Section 2: The Fracture (1854–1860)

Perry returned to America to gather supplies and reinforcements. The shogunate purchased time — not a solution, but a delay. In the spring of 1854, Perry came back with more ships and more men.

Tōkugawa Iesada, the sitting shogun, was dying. No one acknowledged this, but everyone understood it. He was sickly, diminished, unable to decide. His council debated endlessly — the same debate replayed every time an American ship appeared, every time a letter arrived, every time a new demand was made. Negotiate or resist. Open or close. The question could not be answered because both answers ended the shogunate.

The Treaty of Kanagawa (1854) was signed. Japan would accept American ships seeking supplies. Japan would open two ports: Shimoda and Hakodate. These were small concessions — trade was not yet permitted — but they were concessions. The boundary was no longer absolute.

In the domains, word spread differently than official proclamations intended. Satsuma, in the far south, began buying Dutch muskets and assembling artillery pieces. Choshū, on the western tip of Honshu, did the same. These were powerful domains, wealthy enough to afford military innovation, angry enough to act on the anger. Each opened port seemed to confirm what they already believed: the shogunate was weak. Each negotiation seemed to confirm: a strong power does not negotiate, it dictates.

Tōkugawa Iesada died in July 1853. No — he died again in November 1853, the historical record uncertain on the exact date. The shogunate was now in succession, which meant it was in crisis, which meant the council was paralyzed by internal competition for the next shogun's favor.

Tokugawa Yoshinobu, a minor lord with a reputation for intelligence, was already being discussed. Tokugawa Iemochi, a younger man with stronger claim through birth, would be chosen instead. But everyone understood: Yoshinobu was waiting. If Iemochi failed, Yoshinobu would be the alternative. If the shogunate itself failed, Yoshinobu had already prepared his answer.

The Harris Treaty (1858) was more consequential. Townsend Harris, the first American consul-general, demanded full diplomatic relations and trade. The shogunate's negotiators — Moriyama again, now working with Iwase Tadanari — understood that they had no leverage. The Americans had superior military force. The refusal to trade simply meant the Americans would take the trade by force.

The signature of the Harris Treaty split the domains irrevocably. The southwestern domains — Satsuma, Choshū, Tosa, Saga — began to see the imperial restoration, not the shogunate, as the center of authority worth supporting. Young samurai from these domains were sent secretly to Europe. They returned with knowledge of how Britain had industrialized, how France had organized its state, how military innovation required centralized authority and dedicated capital.

They understood something the shogunate seemed not to understand: the answer to Western power was not to resist the West but to adopt Western knowledge and transform Japan from the inside. The slogan had not yet been articulated, but the idea was already present: **bunmei kaika**, civilization and enlightenment. To survive, Japan would have to transform itself deliberately, before being forced to transform by conquest.

This is when the assassination began.

The Ansei Purge (1858–1860) was the shogunate's attempt to suppress this growing rebellion. Ii Naosuke, now the chief advisor, ordered the execution of anyone expressing doubt about the Harris Treaty or the shogunate's policies. Imperial loyalists were hunted and killed. Domain officials who had corresponded with the enemy — that is, with the emperor's supporters — were arrested or executed.

Sakumatsu Taizô, the moderate official who had negotiated with the Americans, was assassinated. Tokugawa Nariaki, the father of Tokugawa Yoshinobu and a symbol of domain power, was forced into retirement and died shortly after in humiliation. Each killing revealed what the shogunate was terrified of: that the domains might answer to the emperor instead of the shogun.

On March 3, 1860, in the fog of early morning, samurai of the Mito domain attacked Ii Naosuke's procession as he traveled to Edo Castle. He was killed, his body left in the street. The message was unambiguous: the shogunate's grip was broken.

The treaty ports opened anyway. Yokohama, in 1859, became the primary site of foreign commerce. Japanese merchants arrived to trade. Foreign merchants arrived to profit. The two centuries of isolation were ending in the ordinary transactions of market economics. The bakufu could not close the ports because closing them would invite military invasion. The bakufu could not keep the ports closed because foreign merchants demanded access and the shogunate lacked the military to deny them.

Each accommodation revealed weakness. Each accommodation accelerated the domains' confidence that they could seize power themselves.

## Section 3: The Rupture (1861–1866)

The sonnō jōi movement — "revere the emperor, expel the barbarians" — peaked in the early 1860s. Young samurai, mostly from Choshū and Satsuma, assassinated foreign diplomats and shogunal moderates indiscriminately. They were wrong about strategy. Expelling the barbarians was impossible — the barbarians had superior military force, and everyone knew it. But they were right about authority: the emperor was the legitimate center, and the shogunate's attempts to manage the crisis were making the emperor's restoration inevitable.

Tokugawa Iemochi died in 1853 — no, Iemochi lived until 1864, and by then the shogunate's authority was visibly failing. When he died, the question of succession became the question of whether the shogunate itself would survive.

Tokugawa Yoshinobu was made shogun in 1866. He was the right man at the wrong time. He was intelligent, pragmatic, capable of understanding that the only way forward was to modernize Japan's military and administrative structure. But he inherited a system that had already lost the capacity to command. The domains were arming themselves. Satsuma and Choshū, now secretly allied, were building their own armies. They had no intention of asking the shogunate's permission.

The Boshin War was the formal ending, but it was less a war than a confirmation. In 1868, the combined forces of Satsuma, Choshū, Tosa, and other allied domains marched on Edo. The shogunate's armies, if they could be called that, dissolved. Tokugawa Yoshinobu's surrender was not defeat in battle; it was recognition that the institution had already died.

Before the military campaign, Yoshinobu made the critical decision. In November 1867, he announced that he was returning power to the emperor. This was not surrender to military force. This was recognition that the shogunate's legitimacy was evaporated. The domains, particularly Satsuma and Choshū, had already decided that the path forward ran through the emperor, not through the shogun.

By returning power voluntarily, Yoshinobu preserved some measure of authority. He would be treated with respect after the restoration. His choice to step down rather than be overthrown became, in the historical memory, an act of nobility. It was also practical rationality: the institution had failed, and the only question was whether he would fall with it or step aside and preserve his status.

The last samurai to truly fight for the shogunate were holdouts in the northeast. By September 1868, the Meiji Restoration was established. The domains had transformed from servants of the shogun to architects of a new state, centered on the emperor, organized around the slogan bunmei kaika — deliberate, rapid, selective transformation.

The young samurai who had assassinated shogunal officials in the name of imperial restoration were now the oligarchs of the new regime: Iwakura Tomomi, Ōkubo Toshimichi, Kido Takayoshi, Saigō Takamori. The emperor Meiji, then fourteen years old, became the symbolic center of the new authority. And the men who had understood that the answer to Western power was not isolation but strategic adoption of Western knowledge became the architects of a new Japan.

## Section 4: The Transformation

Sakoku was dead. The boundary that had held for two hundred years was gone. But the question Perry's ships had opened — what is Japan? what should it become? what is worth preserving? — was not answered. It was inherited.

The Meiji government moved quickly. The han (domains) were abolished and converted into prefectures. The samurai class was dissolved — no longer would feudal lords command armed retainers. Instead, a national conscripted army would be built, led by men trained in European military technique. The government issued orders: learn English, study the West, import technology. Young Japanese were sent to Europe and America to attend universities. Foreign advisors were hired to teach the new methods.

But the selective adoption — the wakon yosai principle, "Japanese spirit, Western technique" — meant that certain things would not be Westernized. The imperial institution would be strengthened and sacralized, not abolished. Confucian values of loyalty, duty, and filial piety would be preserved and integrated into the new system. The family structure would remain hierarchical, not reformed along Western lines.

The Meiji Constitution (1889) attempted to freeze this balance: the emperor as sovereign, sacred head of state, the source of all authority. The military reporting directly to the emperor, not to civilian government. The old samurai code (bushidō) repackaged as the spiritual foundation of the new military. A modern state built on ancient authority, Western technique serving Japanese values.

It worked. Within a generation, Japan had conscripted armies, a functioning bureaucracy, telegraph lines spanning the islands, a modern navy. By 1905, the Russo-Japanese War confirmed what some had already understood: Japan was no longer an ancient civilization trying to survive modernity. It was a modern military power, built deliberately, from the inside, by men who had learned in the West and returned to transform their own nation.

## Section 5: The Landing

By 1868, the age of sakoku was gone. The two-hundred-year seclusion had lasted as long as it took Perry's ships to cross the Pacific. The question that the shogunate had tried to manage by negotiation — how to respond to external force without losing internal order — had been answered by destroying the institution that had tried to manage it.

But the deeper question persisted: what is Japan? The boundary that had separated Japan from the world for two centuries had been porous, permeable, finally indefensible. But the idea that Japan had a particular identity, a particular way of understanding power, society, and authority, did not evaporate. The Meiji oligarchs would spend the next fifty years trying to answer it.

The edict was gone. The question remained active. What would Japan become? And more pressingly, for men like Iwakura and Ōkubo and Kido, who had assassinated moderates in the name of imperial restoration and were now building a modern state: would Japan transform itself in order to survive, or would it be transformed by conquest?

They had already chosen. The Meiji government's single unified answer was bunmei kaika — civilization and enlightenment through deliberate adoption, through state-directed transformation, through the preservation of certain essences (the emperor, the family, loyalty) while adopting new forms (armies, industry, constitutionalism).

The transformation was deliberate. The cost was not yet visible. The samurai class would disappear within a generation, creating a crisis of identity for an entire social stratum that had believed itself eternal. Women would be subordinated to the needs of the new state more thoroughly than before. Peasants would be conscripted into the new armies, exposed to the world, and returned to villages where their families were being broken by the pace of economic change.

In the Bakumatsu period, the question opened. In the Meiji period, Japan answered. The answer was not "preserve yourself as you are." The answer was "transform yourself deliberately before you are transformed by force."

Behind the signal fires that burned on the night Perry's ships arrived, the transformation was already beginning. It would accelerate, consuming itself, until the very speed and intensity of the modernization produced a militarism that would eventually threaten the stability it was meant to preserve. But in 1868, that consequence was not yet visible.

In 1868, the Meiji government faced the future with confidence born of having survived the crisis that had destroyed the shogunate. The edict was gone. The question was active. Japan would answer it by becoming something new — deliberately, rapidly, selectively, never entirely surrendering the conviction that it had a particular character worth preserving while it learned everything the West could teach it.

The two-hundred-year boundary was broken. What emerged was not a Japan that had surrendered to the West. It was a Japan that had learned to compete with the West on the West's terms, while preserving the conviction that Japan was fundamentally Japanese — that some essence persisted, even as everything changed.

That conviction would drive the next era. That conviction would also eventually drive the empire, the war, and the catastrophe of Hiroshima. But in 1868, in the moment when the sakoku edict was finally dead and the transformation was just beginning, the belief was simple: if Japan does not change, it will be conquered. Therefore, Japan will change — but it will change itself, on its own terms, in pursuit of its own survival.

The signal fires burned on.
