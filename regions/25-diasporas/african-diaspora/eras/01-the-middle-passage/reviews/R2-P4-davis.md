## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of Notes (Panel-2)

**Overall:** The notes address my concerns about plurality and honest loss. The section plan is structurally sound. One issue requires attention.

**What works:**
- Section 4 (What Crossed) names five distinct knowledge systems (Yoruba, Fon, Igbo, Kongo, Mande) and commits to method-level depth. This is essential. The African diaspora was not a single people but many peoples whose distinct traditions merged, interacted, and transformed in the Americas.
- Section 5 (What Was Lost) is the section I asked for. The specific categories of loss (plant-dependent herbalism, landscape-dependent rituals, age-grade systems, languages, names, genealogies) are well-chosen. Each represents a different failure mode of the body-archive: knowledge that required material support the Americas could not provide, knowledge that required social structures the slave system deliberately destroyed.
- The treatment of the Kongo cosmology (the kalunga line, the cosmogram, the contribution to the ring shout) represents a knowledge system that is often overlooked in favor of the better-known Yoruba and Fon traditions. Including it at method level corrects an imbalance in the popular understanding of the African diaspora.

**What doesn't:**
- Ibn Khaldun is right that five knowledge systems in 1,000 words is too compressed. My suggestion: render Yoruba (orixa/spirit possession) and Kongo (kalunga line/cosmogram) at full method level as the two primary examples. The Fon and Igbo systems receive briefer treatment that names their distinctive contribution (Fon: the lwa and the syncretism strategy; Igbo: the chi and the decentralized social structure that produced different patterns of resistance). The Mande contribution (the griot, the akonting) is covered in the musical transmission section. This gives two systems at depth and three at recognition level, which is honest and sustainable.

**The question I'd push on:**
The agricultural knowledge that crossed. This is not in the notes and it should be. Enslaved Africans brought rice cultivation to South Carolina (the "Black rice" thesis of Judith Carney). They brought knowledge of indigo processing, of cattle herding in tropical environments, of medicinal plants. The planters who purchased them needed their agricultural expertise as much as their labor. This is a powerful dimension of the body-archive: the enslaved were not only laborers but knowledge workers whose expertise was extracted alongside their physical effort. The chapter should include this, even briefly.
