## BOARD — B-egypt-2 — James P. Allen — Review of The Fragmentation

**Scope:** Theological accuracy, treatment of ma'at, Pyramid Texts/Coffin Texts, inside-voice rendering of Egyptian religion.

**Assessment:** The chapter handles the theological dimension of the fragmentation with care. Ma'at is treated as operative reality throughout, not as a concept under analysis. The transition from the Pyramid Texts to the Coffin Texts is correctly situated.

**What is accurate:**
- Ma'at is described as the order that the king maintains, and the flood as its proof. This is theologically correct for the Old Kingdom framework that the First Intermediate Period inherits and questions.
- The Coffin Texts are correctly described as appearing on non-royal coffins, with provincial workshops developing their own selections and styles. The provincial coffin traditions at Asyut, Beni Hasan, and Deir el-Bersha are real and well-documented.
- The description of coffins including maps of the underworld painted on the floor is accurate (the "Book of Two Ways" at Deir el-Bersha).
- The characterization of the democratization of the afterlife as an economic barrier replacing a theological one is a fair summary of the scholarly consensus.

**What needs watching:**
- Line 75: "The journey through the Duat, the transformation into an akh, the reunion with the gods in the Field of Reeds." The ka/ba/akh terminology is used correctly here. The akh is the transformed, effective spirit. The Field of Reeds (Sekhet Aaru) is the correct afterlife destination. No correction needed.
- The Eloquent Peasant's invocation of ma'at (line 71) is rendered in a way that is theologically coherent: the peasant claims ma'at as a universal principle, not as a royal prerogative. This is consistent with the text's theological framework, and it correctly captures what is revolutionary about the claim.
- The chapter avoids treating the theological crisis as a loss of "belief." The fragmentation of ma'at is described structurally, not as a crisis of faith. This is correct: the Egyptians did not "stop believing" in ma'at. They redistributed the claim to maintain it.

**Factual errors found:** 0

**Recommendation:** The phrase "the afterlife left the pyramid and entered the coffin" (line 101) is a strong formulation and theologically defensible. The Pyramid Texts to Coffin Texts transition is not a simple migration of texts from one surface to another, but the chapter does not claim it is. The phrase captures the cultural movement without reducing it to a mechanical process.

**Verdict:** Theologically sound. Ma'at is handled with the precision and inside-voice treatment the subject requires.
