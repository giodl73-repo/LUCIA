# La Reconquesta Aragonesa — The Aragonese Reconquest
## 1137–1238 CE

---

The king was thirty years old and had been at war for most of them.

James stood at the edge of the Aragonese lines outside Valencia in the spring of 1238 and looked at what he had spent nine years approaching. The city rose from the Valencian plain in white stone and flat-roofed towers, crossed by the acequia channels that watered the orchards of orange and mulberry and rice stretching south toward the sea. The walls were long. The gates were closed. Inside, Zayyan ibn Mardanish — the last Almohad governor of Valencia — held a city of fifty thousand souls and knew there would be no relief from the south.

James had first taken the field in this campaign at thirteen. The choosing had been done for him, by a regency council and by circumstance and by the wars that preceded his birth. But now, at the walls, the choosing was his. He had driven Zayyan back from Burriana, back from Peñíscola, back from every position along the road south, and here was the reckoning.

What he knew, standing at the edge of his own lines, was this: he needed Zayyan's people to stay.

Not as prisoners. Not driven out. He needed the farmers in the orchards and the silk merchants in the souks and the engineers who maintained the ancient acequia system to remain and work the land, because a Valencia emptied of its people was a Valencia worth nothing — a city of walls and dry channels and dead orange groves. The Aragonese did not have enough people to replace fifty thousand Moors. His kingdom's economy ran on Moorish agricultural knowledge and Catalan commercial intelligence, and the deal he was about to offer was not charity. It was calculation.

---

Zayyan ibn Mardanish had been governor of Valencia for four years and had known from the beginning that the time was not on his side.

His family had roots in Valencia longer than the Almohads. His grandfather Muhammad ibn Mardanish — the Wolf King, as the Christian chronicles called him — had governed Valencia and Murcia against the Almohads for a generation, allied with Castile and Aragon against the Berber empire he distrusted. The Almohads eventually absorbed the Mardanish territories after the Wolf King's death, but the family's position gave Zayyan a different kind of legitimacy than pure Almohad appointment: he was Valencian before he was anything else.

But after Las Navas de Tolosa — the great Christian victory of 1212 that broke the Almohad military capacity in Iberia — the empire that had maintained the Valencian frontier was already finishing. The North African reinforcements did not come. The Almohad governors of the south managed their own fragmentation. And in the north, the Aragonese under James moved systematically down the coast: Burriana in 1233, Morella in 1232, Peñíscola in 1233, the northern territories one by one.

Abu Zayd — the previous Muslim governor of Valencia's northern territories, a man of Almohad appointment but Valencian family — had read the situation earlier than most. In the early 1220s he converted to Christianity and became an Aragonese ally, providing James's forces with intelligence about Valencia's water sources and defensive geography. He received his northern lands back as a Christian vassal and spent the rest of his life navigating between the two worlds he had chosen to straddle. He was not a traitor by his own account. He was a survivor making a calculation that the compact on offer from Aragon was more durable than the compact from a crumbling empire.

Zayyan watched all this. He chose differently. He held the city, fortified the walls, waited for relief that did not come, and in late summer of 1238, with the Aragonese siege complete and the orchards outside the walls under Aragonese control, he sent his emissaries to negotiate.

The emissaries came with a specific mandate: protect the people. Not the city — the city was lost. Protect the fifty thousand. Protect the mosques, the markets, the law courts, the acequia schedules that kept the orchards alive, the customary rights that governed every village from the plain to the mountains. Zayyan had enough tactical instinct to know that a city with an agricultural system intact was worth more to the Aragonese than a city without one. He had leverage precisely because James needed what the city held.

The *Llibre dels Fets* records the negotiations with James's characteristic mix of piety and precision. Terms were offered. Terms were countered. What emerged was *capitulació*: Zayyan and his household, and those who chose to follow, would depart with safe conduct to the sea. Those who chose to remain on their land would remain as *mudéjars* — Muslims living under Christian administration, protected in their religion, their mosques converted only where the population had emptied, their customary courts for internal disputes intact, their agricultural practices uninterrupted. They would pay rents to their new overlords. They would not fight. They would farm and trade and tend the acequia channels as they always had.

In September 1238, Zayyan opened the gates and led those who would follow him to the shore. The boats were there. He stood at the waterline and looked back at the city. The *Llibre dels Fets* does not record what he said. He still called himself king of Valencia until his death in North Africa, still made alliances, still tried to return. He never did. But on the shore, in September, he made the decision that fifty thousand people who stayed behind would live under a compact their governor had negotiated for them.

James rode in.

The *Llibre dels Fets* says he wept when he saw the city — that he dismounted at the great mosque and gave thanks, and that the tears came before he could stop them, not from piety alone but from the long weight of nine years in the field, nine years of fourteen-thousand-man armies encamped in the Valencian heat, of negotiating with his own nobles for supplies, of watching men die of fever and crossbow quarrel alike. He was thirty years old. He had been king since he was five.

The city that opened before him smelled of orange blossom and animal smoke and the particular dust of flat-roofed stone streets in late summer heat.

---

In the days after the entry, there was work to do that no soldier could do.

The acequia amin — the water judge of the eastern distribution district — went at first light to check the main channel above the Rascanya acequia before the city's morning began. His name does not survive in the records from this specific week. But the institution that held him does: the water judge's office, the schedule he maintained, the register in which he recorded allocations and disputes, the rounds he made each day to check the channel openings and the water levels, these things are documented by Robert Burns's scholarship across the archives that the Aragonese later inherited. The institution continued because it had to. The oranges needed water. The rice paddies needed water. The silk mulberry groves needed water. None of the Aragonese soldiers who had just entered the city knew how to manage the Rascanya distribution system. The amin did.

He checked the stone gate at the head of the channel. It was open the correct amount — three finger-widths, as the schedule for this week required. The water ran south through the channel, brown with the silt of the autumn rains, carrying toward the orchards that would produce in December as they did every year. He made his notation in the register: date, water level, distribution hours assigned to each downstream community. A Catalan notary had been assigned to witness the recording. They did not share a language. They managed, because the record had to be made and the water had to run.

The mudéjars who stayed — and most stayed — made a calculation that was not simple and not sentimental. They were betting that the Aragonese needed them enough to protect them. They had watched what happened at Majorca nine years earlier, when James's 1229 campaign had ended with a significant portion of the Muslim population enslaved or expelled. Majorca's Muslims had not been needed the same way. Majorca's economy was commercial and maritime, not dependent on a generation-deep agricultural knowledge system. Valencia's was. The orchards and the silk and the rice and the channels connecting them: these required expertise that could not be imported. The mudéjars who stayed understood their leverage, as Zayyan had understood it in the negotiation. They would continue. They would pay their rents. They would maintain their mosques and their law courts and their *falaha* agricultural tradition. And they would watch the compact hold or fail.

It held, for a century and more, at the cost of constant pressure.

---

The machine that held it was older than James.

The Crown of Aragon had begun in 1137 with a marriage contract. Petronilla of Aragon was an infant; her father Ramiro had been a monk before the dynasty's need pulled him from his monastery to produce an heir, and then returned him to it. The contract bound the County of Barcelona to the Kingdom of Aragon before Petronilla was old enough to understand what binding meant. A king whose function was contractual from before his birth: this was the compact machine's founding document, and it shaped everything that came after.

Two constitutional traditions joined in that contract and never fully merged. The Aragonese had been building since the early eleventh century around the principle that the king's power derived from covenant with his nobles. The Catalan ran through the *Usatges of Barcelona* and the commercial law of the Barcelona merchant class. The Aragonese *Corts* and the Catalan *Corts* met separately, legislated separately, and taxed separately. When James needed money for the Valencia campaign, he had to negotiate with each. When his father Peter II had died at Muret in 1213 — fighting against Simon de Montfort's crusade in defense of the Aragonese territories in southern France, where his Toulousain vassals had been drawn into a war against the Cathar heretics — the Aragonese nobility had used the regency crisis to extract new concessions from the crown. The compact ran in both directions. The nobles got things; the king got service.

The instrument that made the compact real was the *Justicia* of Aragon — the great judicial officer appointed by the king, confirmed by the *Corts*, removable only by the *Corts*. His authority included the *manifestación*: the power to protect any person, including a person accused of a crime, from royal violence until the courts had examined the case. In practice this meant the *Justicia* could interpose himself between the king's arrest order and its execution. The king could not simply seize whom he wished. The law moved first.

In the decade before Valencia, there had been a case. A frontier lord — one of the Aragonese noble families along the Valencian march — had been accused by the king of conspiring with the Moors against the Aragonese campaign. James ordered his arrest. Rodrigo invoked the *manifestación*. The *Justicia* accepted the invocation and issued the notice of restraint. James was furious. The correspondence — fragments preserved in the Aragonese royal archive — records the king's pressure and the *Justicia*'s refusal. Rodrigo was eventually tried, eventually convicted on reduced charges, eventually fined rather than imprisoned. The *Justicia* had held.

This was not, from within the Aragonese legal tradition, heroism. It was the normal operation of the system. But the system's normal operation required a *Justicia* willing to send a notice of restraint to a king who controlled the army. That took something. The Aragonese called it *fuer* — the law's claim on behavior, prior to the king's. James understood this. He had grown up inside a regency system where his own power was constrained, where the *Corts* had real leverage, where his own nobles could and did refuse supplies until their terms were met. The compact was not a principle he admired. It was a condition he lived in.

When he established the *Furs de València* in 1261 — the law code for the new Valencian kingdom — he gave Valencia its own separate legal system, distinct from both Aragonese and Catalan law. His own Aragonese nobles objected: they had received grants of Valencian land and wanted Aragonese law to govern it, which gave them more power over their tenants. James refused. The *Furs de València* were more favorable to the mudéjar tenant farmers than Aragonese feudal law would have been; this was deliberate. Valencia's agricultural value required its people to work the land without the excessive obligations that Aragonese feudal tenancy imposed. But James also had a structural motive: he already had two incompatible law codes in the Crown of Aragon. A third legal system for Valencia was cheaper than a war between the Aragonese and Catalan factions over which of theirs would govern the new prize.

The Valencian kingdom got its own *Corts*, its own parliament, its own judicial apparatus. The machine multiplied its compacts.

---

Nine years before Valencia, in November 1229, James had landed on Majorca.

The Balearic islands were in Almohad hands and constituted a piracy problem that the Barcelona merchant community had been pressing the king to solve for a generation. The Catalan commercial intelligence networks had long understood that whoever controlled the islands controlled the sea lanes between Italy, North Africa, and the Atlantic. Barcelona's banking families financed the expedition. The Catalan fleet carried fifteen thousand men across the December sea and landed on the island's northwestern coast.

Majorca fell in a campaign of several months. What followed was harder than Valencia's arrangement. The Majorcan Muslims, who had governed an island for generations, found no such compact waiting for them — no agricultural knowledge the Aragonese urgently needed, no irrigation system irreplaceable by settlers, no calculation that made protection cheaper than displacement. A significant portion was enslaved or expelled. The mosques were converted without the protections that Valencian mudéjars would later receive. The terms James offered Majorca were worse than the terms he offered Valencia — not from cruelty but from calculation: Majorca's value was strategic and commercial, not dependent on the labor knowledge of the existing population.

James understood islands differently from how most medieval rulers understood territory. He was not thinking about contiguous frontiers and feudal obligations and march territories. He was thinking about sea lanes — about the point in the western Mediterranean where you could see four coastlines and tax three trade routes. Majorca gave the Crown of Aragon a position in the sea that no amount of Pyrenean territory could provide. From Majorca, the next position was Menorca. From Menorca and Valencia together, the next position was Sicily.

The Catalan commercial mind and the Aragonese conquest logic were not separate systems. They ran in tandem: the merchants identified the trade route, the king provided the military campaign, the compact machine absorbed the acquisition, and the revenues flowed back through the Barcelona banking network that had financed the whole operation. The Barcelona *Consolat de Mar* — the commercial court that administered maritime disputes under the *Llibre del Consolat de Mar*, the earliest codification of Mediterranean maritime law — was not a separate institution from the Crown's military ambition. It was the same institution in commercial dress. The empire was a business, and the business had a constitution.

---

By the autumn of 1238, Valencia had been absorbed. The question that remained was whether the compact would hold under the pressure of what it had created.

The stress was visible almost immediately. The Aragonese nobles who had received land grants in the Valencian kingdom began pushing for Aragonese feudal law to govern their new estates. The mudéjar tenant farmers on those estates invoked the *Furs de València* against the demands. The *Furs* were more protective; the Aragonese law was not. James had to adjudicate, repeatedly, between his nobles' demands and his mudéjar tenants' protections. He mostly found for the *Furs* — because the agricultural system required the mudéjars to have enough security to work it — but the pressure did not stop. Each noble who received a Valencian grant arrived with an Aragonese expectation of what tenure meant, and the expectation was higher than what James's compact with the mudéjars allowed.

The mudéjar communities who stayed had made their bet: that James needed them enough to protect them. For decades the bet held. The acequia amin recorded his distributions. The silk merchants traded through the Valencian port, now under a Catalan harbor master who spoke no Arabic but needed the silk to reach Genoa. The law courts that heard mudéjar internal disputes continued in Arabic, under Islamic legal precedent, with a Valencian Christian judge available for appeals. The great mosque became a cathedral — the Seu, Valencia's first Christian church — but the smaller neighborhood mosques remained open for the communities that filled them.

What the compact system built in Valencia was not tolerance. It was arrangement. The word *pactisme* did not describe a moral preference. It described a mechanism: the structure by which competing interests were held in equilibrium by mutual obligation. The mudéjars stayed because they had calculated that the obligation ran both ways. James held the line because the obligation was structural — break it, and the agricultural foundation of his richest kingdom would collapse.

The fragility inside this arrangement was the same fragility that ran through the whole Crown of Aragon. The compact system worked while each constituency needed the others. It began to fail when a future king decided that one constituency's needs outweighed the others'. The machine could not centralize. Centralization required choosing one law code over the others, one Corts over the others, one compact over the others. That choice would come — not in James's lifetime, but in the lifetimes of kings who did not share his calculation.

---

In December of 1238, the oranges ripened as they did every year.

The acequia amin made his rounds above the Rascanya channel in the early morning, when the light came in low from the east and the frost had not quite left the stones of the channel walls. He checked the main gate. He checked the secondary distribution points. He made his entries in the register, the notary beside him completing the Catalan translation in a separate column. The two columns would not always agree; translation is interpretation, and the Arabic categories for water rights did not map cleanly onto Catalan legal concepts. The discrepancies would generate disputes. The disputes would generate rulings. The rulings would generate precedents. Over the next century, the accumulated precedents of the Tribunal de las Aguas would produce the institution that still operates in Valencia today — still meeting in the open air beside the cathedral, still hearing disputes in the old way, still maintaining the schedules that the acequia amin's predecessors established before James was born.

The water ran south through the channel. The oranges came in. The record was made.

James had built a kingdom from a calculation. The calculation was correct: Valencia was worth more intact than empty, the mudéjar knowledge system was irreplaceable, the compact machine could absorb the new territory without destroying what made it valuable. He was right about all of it, in the way that calculations are right — precisely, for a time, within conditions that eventually change.

The conditions changed. They always do.

But in the autumn and winter of 1238, the channels carried water, the orchards bore fruit, and the city the king had needed to take without emptying remained, on its own terms, full.
