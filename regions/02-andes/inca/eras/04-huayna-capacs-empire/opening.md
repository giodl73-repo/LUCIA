# Huayna Cápac's Empire

The quipucamayoc in Qusqu were counting, and the numbers had never been larger.

In the administrative quarter of the capital, where the stone buildings that housed the empire's record-keepers stood in rows along streets so straight that the morning sun entered each doorway at the same angle, the senior quipucamayoc held their accounting cords across their laps and read the state of the world. The pendant strings hung in clusters of color, brown and red and yellow and white, and each cluster represented a province, and each cord within the cluster represented a category, and each knot on each cord represented a quantity, and the quantities were at their peak. More stored maize than any previous generation had recorded. More textiles in the qollqa. More mit'a workers committed to the road crews, the terrace projects, the new constructions at Tomebamba in the north where Huayna Capac was building a second capital. The quipucamayoc read the knots and the knots said: the empire has never been larger. The empire has never held more.

Huayna Capac, eleventh Sapa Inca, son of Topa Inca, had inherited a system at its fullest extension and chose to push it further. His campaigns in the north, in the difficult highlands of Ecuador where the Canari and the Caranqui had resisted his father, consumed years and demanded the sustained mobilization of the mit'a labor system at a level the empire had not previously attempted. The road to Tomebamba carried soldiers north and chicha south, and the quipucamayoc at each tambo along the route recorded the passage and adjusted the accounts.

The Sapa Inca was in the north. He had been in the north for years. The court at Qusqu continued to function, the ceque rituals continued, the festivals of Inti Raymi and Capac Raymi were performed on schedule, the chicha was poured and the offerings made. But the center of gravity had shifted. Huayna Capac preferred Tomebamba to Qusqu, preferred the northern frontier to the capital his grandfather had rebuilt. He held court in Ecuador. He married northern women. He fathered sons in the north, and the sons he fathered there would, in a few years, tear the empire apart.

The quipucamayoc could not record what was coming. The epidemic moved south along the road faster than any chasqui, and it killed without categories the knots could capture, and when it reached Tomebamba and took Huayna Capac himself, the quipucamayoc in Qusqu untied nothing and retied nothing because the event was outside the system's frame. The Sapa Inca was dead. The succession was not settled. The knots held the numbers of the empire's greatest extent, and the numbers were already wrong, because the people the knots counted were dying in numbers the knots could not keep pace with.
