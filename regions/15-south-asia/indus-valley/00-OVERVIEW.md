# Sindhu-Sarasvati — The Ordered Cities

We were the people of the great rivers — the Sindhu and the now-vanished Sarasvati — and we made our cities by the logic of water. Our streets ran straight and met at right angles; our drains ran beneath our floors and our lanes; our granaries stood above the flood line. We did not build walls to keep enemies out. We built walls to keep the water where we wanted it. Our cities were instruments of order, and the order they embodied was shared across a thousand miles of river country: the same weights in Mohenjo-daro and in Dholavira, the same brick dimensions in Harappa and in Lothal. We spoke no single language that anyone has yet recovered, but we shared a grammar of measure.

We called no king by name. No monument proclaims a ruler's conquest; no palace overwhelms the city; the great bath at Mohenjo-daro serves the community, not a sovereign. What our seals show are animals — the humped bull, the unicorn, the tiger — rendered with extraordinary attention, and figures seated in postures that would echo across South Asian sacred life for four thousand years. We were not without power or without hierarchy, but the forms our authority took have left archaeologists arguing. What is undeniable is this: we were urban before most of the world had learned what a city was, and our cities worked.

Major places:
- **Mohenjo-daro** ("Mound of the Dead," later name) — largest city; the Great Bath; the Granary
- **Harappa** (Punjab) — northern anchor; first excavated; gave the civilization its modern name
- **Dholavira** (Rann of Kutch) — island city; extraordinary water management; inscribed signboard
- **Lothal** (Gujarat) — dockyard city; bead workshops; maritime trade with the Persian Gulf
- **Shortugai** (Afghanistan) — northwestern outpost; lapis lazuli trade depot

## What Defined Them

**The shared standard.** Across the entire Indus world — from Afghanistan to Gujarat, from 2600 to 1900 BCE — weights and measures were standardized to a common system. A merchant from Lothal could trade in Harappa without argument about what a unit meant. This was not achieved by conquest; it was achieved by convention. The standard was the civilization's constitution.

**The draining city.** Every major Indus city was built around its drainage system. Covered brick drains ran beneath the lanes. Household drains connected to the street system. The great bath at Mohenjo-daro was sealed with bitumen and fed by a well. This was civic infrastructure as civic identity — a people who believed that the management of water, both for purity and for overflow, was the first duty of a settled community.

**The unread script.** The Indus script — appearing on seals, pottery, and a few remarkable inscribed objects — remains undeciphered. We know it was used across the civilization's full extent. We know it was associated with trade and administrative activity. We do not know what language it encodes, what it says, or whether it was fully phonetic. The civilization introduced itself to the modern world and then locked its meaning.

**The animal seal.** The Indus seal — typically a small carved steatite block bearing an animal image and a line of script — was the civilization's most characteristic artifact. The humped Brahman bull, rendered with muscular precision; the composite "unicorn" animal approaching a ritual stand; the tiger. These animals were clearly significant beyond simple decoration. They appear to encode identity — of lineage, guild, or sacred affiliation — in a vocabulary we cannot yet read.

**The non-military city.** In an age when Mesopotamian cities were building defensive walls and Egyptian pharaohs were carving their military victories into stone, the Indus cities built no monuments to war and no clear structures of military power. This was not naivety — the civilization lasted for centuries and managed complex trade networks. It was a different answer to the question of how a city maintains its order.

## The Chapters

**1 — Early Settlements:** A potter in a village on the Sarasvati bank marks the base of a jar with the same sign her grandmother used. The village is becoming something more.

**2 — The Urban Flowering:** The great bath is filled for the first time. A priest — or a merchant — or a civic administrator — descends the steps into water that the entire city has agreed must be clean.

**3 — The Wide Network:** A merchant from Lothal boards a boat with a chest of carnelian beads. He will not return for two years. His weights are recognized in Ur.

**4 — The Drying:** The river that fed the eastern cities slows, silts, and stops. A water engineer stands at the head of the old channel and counts the days since the last flood. The number is too large.

**5 — The Village Return:** The last family leaves Mohenjo-daro. They carry the seals and the weights. Where they go, the patterns continue — smaller, quieter, still recognizably themselves.
