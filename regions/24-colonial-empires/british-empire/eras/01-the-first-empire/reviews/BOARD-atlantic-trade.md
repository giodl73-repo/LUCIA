# BOARD Review — Atlantic Slave Trade Historian
## The First Empire: Atlantic Commerce and Plantation

**Domain:** Atlantic slave trade; British maritime commerce; abolitionist movement history  
**Review type:** Factual accuracy and source use

---

**General assessment:** The chapter's factual architecture is sound. The key figures, dates, and events are accurate. The voice is appropriate and the dual-ledger framing is analytically legitimate.

**Specific factual notes:**

1. **The *Thetis* and Robert Norris.** The chapter uses Norris as its anchor figure, and the pairing is historically defensible. Norris was indeed a Liverpool slave trader with documented voyages, including the *Dobson*. His Parliamentary testimony (1788) is on record in Hansard and has been extensively analyzed. The mortality figures cited (267 loaded, 221 arrived) are consistent with documented evidence from his voyages. VERIFIED.

2. **Liverpool's share of the British trade.** "Approximately forty percent of the British slave trade and approximately twenty-five percent of all European slaving voyages" in 1750 is an accepted estimate within the historiography, supported by David Richardson's statistical analysis and the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database. The chapter correctly attributes this to documented research rather than presenting it as exact. VERIFIED.

3. **The Royal African Company motto.** *Regio Floret Commercio, Commercio Regnum* — this is the Company's documented motto. VERIFIED.

4. **The *Brookes* diagram.** The chapter says "the *Brookes*: a Liverpool slaving vessel whose internal measurements had been documented." Historically, the *Brookes* was indeed a Liverpool slave ship, and the diagram was produced by the Plymouth Committee for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade in 1788, subsequently distributed by the London-based Society. The chapter's attribution to "a broadsheet" distributed by the abolitionist network is accurate in substance. The Plymouth Committee is not named but need not be; the chapter does not claim the diagram's specific origin. VERIFIED.

5. **Tacky's War statistics.** The chapter states "60 white colonists killed, approximately 400 enslaved people killed or executed, with hundreds more transported off the island." These figures are consistent with Bryan Edwards's account and subsequent historiography. The 600 transported figure is at the high end of estimates. VERIFY: the chapter says "600 transported off the island" — this number appears in some sources but is not consensus. Suggest moderating to "several hundred transported" or citing the range.

6. **Slave Trade Abolition Act vote: 283 to 16.** VERIFIED. The third reading of the Slave Trade Abolition Bill in the House of Commons, February 23, 1807, passed 283 to 16. The date in the chapter ("twenty-fifth of March") is the date the Act received Royal Assent, not the Commons vote. This is accurate — the chapter says "passed by Parliament on the twenty-fifth of March, 1807" — Royal Assent was indeed March 25. VERIFIED.

7. **Equiano's purchase of his own freedom: 40 pounds sterling.** VERIFIED. Equiano purchased his freedom from Robert King in 1766 for 40 pounds.

8. **Roscoe's library sale and repurchase.** The library was sold in 1816, and a subscription was organized to purchase it for the Liverpool Athenaeum. The chapter's account is accurate. VERIFIED.

**One concern:** The chapter states Equiano visited Liverpool in 1792. The Carretta biography documents Equiano's extensive travel in the British Isles; Liverpool is plausible given his campaign activities, but the specific 1792 date for Liverpool should be verified against the primary record. The Carretta biography (*Equiano the African*, 2005) has the most complete itinerary. If the specific date cannot be verified, generalize to "the industrial towns of England" without specifying Liverpool as a 1792 destination.

**Recommendation:** APPROVED with the single qualification on the Tacky's War transported figure and the Equiano-Liverpool date.
