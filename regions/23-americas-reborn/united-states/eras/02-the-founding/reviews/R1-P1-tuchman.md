# Panel Review — R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman
## Opening: The Founding — The Experiment Begins

**Voice: Barbara Tuchman — Narrative craft, pacing, scene construction, arc**

The opening works. Madison at the front of the room, writing faster than he sleeps — that is a scene, not a description of a scene. The detail about expanding shorthand to full sentences each night at his lodgings is exactly right: it is specific, it is historically grounded, and it earns the later revelation that no one will see those notes for thirty years. The reader understands what is at stake before the stakes are named.

The pivot sentence — "Outside the State House, enslaved people serve the delegates their meals" — is a craft decision. It lands cleanly. What saves it from being a rhetorical device is what follows: "This is not a detail added later for correction. It was true while it was happening." The opening refuses to moralize from above, and that refusal is itself dramatic. The contradiction is stated at the level of fact, not at the level of indignation, which is a harder thing to do and which serves the reader better.

**What works:**
The horizontal rule between the opening scene and the Constitutional analysis is a formal risk that pays off. It creates a beat — the way a chapter might pause before moving from the particular to the general. The prose sustains its register through the transition. The Three-Fifths Compromise quotation is handled with discipline: the document's own evasive language is reproduced and then named as evasive ("They did not use the word 'enslaved.'"), which is more devastating than any editorial commentary could be.

The final three sentences are earned:
> Madison finished his notes. The windows stayed shut. Outside, the people who had served the delegates cleared the dishes and went home to whatever home meant for them. The Constitution went to the states for ratification. Nine were needed. It took nine months.

This is a close. It carries weight without overstating. It returns to the material world that opened the piece — the room, the windows, the people who served — and closes the loop precisely. "Whatever home meant for them" does not explain itself. Good.

**What I'd push on:**
The middle section — the Articles of Confederation, Shays' Rebellion, the mechanisms of constitutional design — is necessary but it is the weakest passage narratively. It carries too much information in too compressed a space. The shift from "what Madison wrote" to "what happened to the Articles" to "what the Convention produced" moves too quickly for the reader to settle into any one part of it. The opening had us in a room with a person. The middle section has us in a summary.

The specific detail of Shays' Rebellion is right — it is the event that made the Convention feel urgent — but it is handled in a single subordinate clause. Give it a sentence of its own. Let the reader feel the farmers closing the courts before moving on.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening introduces Madison and Jefferson but keeps them at a slight remove — Madison is an observer-writer, Jefferson is in Paris writing letters. Where is the human being who will carry the chapter's emotional and intellectual weight? Madison seems the natural choice, but the opening has not yet committed to him as a continuous presence. Is the chapter going to follow Madison, or is it going to use multiple figures? The panel should know before the notes are written, because the answer shapes everything about how the arc is constructed.
