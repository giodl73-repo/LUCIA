## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of "The Great Hajj" opening

**Overall:** This is a genuinely good opening — better than good in places. It has a specific person, a specific morning, a jeli singing, and gold loaded on camels. It earns its first two pages. But it runs out of narrative engine around the midpoint and coasts on eloquent summary where it should be building scenes.

**What works:**

- The first line is exactly right. "Bala Faseke Kouyate stood before the gate of Niani and sang the names." A named human being, doing a specific thing, in a specific place. No throat-clearing, no context-setting, no "In the early fourteenth century, the Mali Empire..." I could have written that line myself, and I mean it as a compliment.

- The genealogy passage in the first paragraph works because it moves. Each clause lands and pushes to the next: "He sang Khalifa, who shot arrows into the crowd and did not last. He sang Abu Bakr, who went to sea and did not return." Those are not just names on a list. Each one carries a small, sharp story. The reader is learning dynastic history without being taught it. That is the trick, and the author has it here.

- "Musa was going to Mecca." That single sentence after the weight of the gold inventory is a superb structural move. The paragraph before it is dense with numbers — five hundred men, six mithqals, a hundred camels, three hundred pounds of gold dust — and then the prose drops to five words. The effect is a kind of narrative exhale. The reader pauses. The stakes are restated at their simplest. This is confident writing.

- "So the gold was not extravagance. The gold was the point." The best two sentences in the piece. They do the work of an entire explanatory section on Mande political culture in eight words. The author trusts the reader to absorb the logic from what came before — the Kurukan Fuga, the obligation of generosity — and then delivers the thesis as a clean punch. This is how you make cultural context feel like narrative momentum instead of a detour.

- The sensory detail in the second paragraph earns its place: "Niani smelled of dust and shea butter and woodsmoke." Three specific things. Not "the smells of the city" but dust, shea butter, woodsmoke. That is the kind of concrete detail that makes a reader trust the author.

**What doesn't:**

- The piece loses its narrative engine after "Musa raised his hand, and the caravan began to move." Everything from that point forward is summary — and good summary, but summary nonetheless. We are told what will happen in Cairo, told about mapmakers in Majorca, told about gold prices falling for twelve years. The reader is being informed rather than carried. Compare the opening paragraphs, where the jeli is singing and the reader is standing at the gate, to the final paragraphs, where the reader is sitting in a lecture hall being told about consequences. The shift is palpable.

- The paragraph on Inari Kunate is a missed opportunity that borders on negligence. "Musa's senior wife, Inari Kunate, traveled with five hundred women of her own household." She appears, is named, is given a number, and is never heard from again. Five hundred women of her household — that is an extraordinary detail. What did that column look like? Who organized it? What did she carry? The author introduces what could be one of the most vivid human presences in the piece and then drops her like an item on a manifest. If you are going to name her, give her a moment. If you cannot give her a moment, do not name her in a way that makes the reader expect one.

- "The sources agree on this" — referring to Musa's height — is a clever aside, but it pulls the reader out of the scene and into the historian's study. In the opening paragraphs, the author is inside the morning at Niani. With this phrase, the author steps outside it. I have used this kind of move myself, and I know its cost. It signals to the reader that they are reading a reconstruction, not witnessing an event. At this point in the piece — the second paragraph — it is too early to break the spell. Save the historiographical asides for later, once the reader is committed.

- The route paragraph ("The route was north. North through the savanna...") is well-written but inert. It is a geographical itinerary dressed up as prose. Nothing happens in it. No one makes a decision. No obstacle appears. The anaphora of "North" is rhythmically pleasing but narratively empty — it moves the reader across a map, not through a story. Either cut it or put something human in it: a well that was dry, a guide who was lost, a camel that died on the third day.

**The question I'd push on:**

Where is the complication? This opening has setup — magnificent setup — but no turn. The caravan assembles, the jeli sings, the gold is loaded, Musa raises his hand, and they leave. Then we are told, from a distance, what will happen later. But a great opening needs a crack in the surface, a moment where the reader thinks "ah, this is not going to go the way I expected." The Cairo gold crash is that moment — it is the best story element here — but it is buried in a summary paragraph at the end. I would restructure: give us the morning at Niani, give us the departure, and then cut to Cairo. Put us in a souk where a merchant is watching the price of gold collapse in real time and does not understand why. Now you have two scenes, two perspectives, and a mystery. The reader will turn the page. Right now, the opening is a beautiful procession. A procession is something you watch. A story is something you cannot stop reading. This needs to become the latter.
