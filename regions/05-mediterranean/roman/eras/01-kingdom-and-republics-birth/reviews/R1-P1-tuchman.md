## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of Roman Era 1 Opening

**Overall:** This opening does something I rarely see in Roman history writing: it makes the constitutional machinery feel like a story rather than a civics lesson. The funeral procession is the right hook. A body moving through a city that does not yet know what it is becoming.

**What works:**
- The Brutus funeral as opening scene. Dead man as founding argument. This is narrative logic at its best.
- "The argument was circular, and the circularity was the point." This is the kind of sentence that explains a political system in twelve words.
- The transition from strategic geography (salt road, river crossing) to origin myth is handled as parallel explanations, not as myth-debunking. Good.
- The toga passage works because it gives the reader something physical to hold while the constitutional abstraction is being built.

**What doesn't:**
- The middle section listing the kings (Romulus, Numa, Servius) reads as a catalog. I lose the narrative thread between Brutus's body and the final paragraph. Can the kings' contributions be woven into the constitutional argument rather than listed?
- "The most influential political engineering of the ancient Mediterranean" feels like a claim the opening hasn't earned yet. The chapter will earn it. The opening shouldn't pre-announce it.

**The question I'd push on:**
Where are the non-senators in this opening? The women mourn, but they do not speak. The workers curing hides near the Tiber are a sensory detail but not a presence. The Republic was built on a sharp class distinction between patricians and plebeians. If the plebeians are absent from the opening, the chapter starts with only half the story.
