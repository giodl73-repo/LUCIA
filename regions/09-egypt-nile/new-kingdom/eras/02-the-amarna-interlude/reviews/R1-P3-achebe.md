## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of The Amarna Interlude (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening passes the test. The culture owns this story. The stonecutter is not an instrument of the author's commentary; he is a man in his own world, facing a crisis in terms his world provides.

**What works:**
- The theological reasoning is rendered from inside. The chain (stone holds names, names hold gods, gods hold order) is not explained to the reader. It is presented as the stonecutter's air, the certainty he breathes. This is the difference between describing a worldview and inhabiting it.
- "Unless the king was wrong. A stonecutter did not think that thought." This is the finest moment in the opening. It acknowledges the possibility of dissent by showing that the culture's own structure forbids it. The reader feels the weight of the prohibition without the author having to editorialize about authoritarianism or obedience.
- The Aten paragraph does not judge Akhenaten's revolution. It does not call it visionary or destructive. It states what happened. The reader is left to weigh it.
- The absence of the stonecutter's name is handled with dignity. He is not pitied for his anonymity. His chisel is his presence, and the chisel is specific, material, real.

**What doesn't:**
- I want to hear the stonecutter's Egypt. He is a man of Waset. What does his quarter smell like? What does he eat? What does his wife think about the decree? The opening gives us his hands and his chisel, but not his life beyond the temple wall. This is a choice, and it serves the theological focus, but it also means the opening risks making the stonecutter a figure of the crisis rather than a person who happens to be standing at its center.
- The catalogue of gods in the penultimate paragraph briefly shifts from the stonecutter's perspective to a narrator's overview of the Egyptian pantheon. A stonecutter would not have catalogued the gods in this analytical way. He would have known them by their functions in his daily life: the god you pray to when the flood is late, the god whose feast means a day without work.

**The question I'd push on:**
Whose story is this? If it is the stonecutter's story, the chapter must sustain his perspective or one like it: the crisis seen from below, from the hands that execute what the minds above decide. If it is Akhenaten's story told through the stonecutter's opening, the shift in perspective must be handled with care. A stonecutter who opens the story and then vanishes is a device. A stonecutter who opens the story and returns, even briefly, is a person.
