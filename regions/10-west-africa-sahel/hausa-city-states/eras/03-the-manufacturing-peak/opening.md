# The Manufacturing Peak

The cloth arrived in Katsina at the wrong weight.

Ibrahim al-Katsinawi could feel it before he had unrolled the first bale: the weave too loose, the beat-glaze missing, the kind of work a Gobir loom produced when the weavers were pressed for time or the cotton had come in wet from a poor harvest. He set it aside. He had been buying cloth in this market for twenty-three years. He knew what Kano cloth felt like, the way the strip locked against itself when folded, the way the surface held the dye without bleeding. This was not that. He turned to the Tuareg caravan master and named a price, and the caravan master made a face, and Ibrahim said the face was reasonable given the cloth, and the caravan master said it was cloth from Kano, and Ibrahim said he would not argue about where it was made, but he would argue about what it was worth. They settled on a number. The caravan master moved down the line of buyers with a better price in mind.

Ibrahim spread the cloth on the stone floor of his stall and bent to look. The indigo had taken unevenly, a sign that the vat had been too cold or the fermentation too brief. The Marina guild in Kano would not have shipped this. He had visited the Marina quarter once, years ago, watched the master dyers assessing their vats by smell with a certainty that left no room for the kind of compromise he was holding in his hands. Someone had let the apprentices run a vat. The cloth would sell, it would sell to buyers who were not Ibrahim al-Katsinawi, but it would not cross the Sahara, and it would not carry the price that Hausa cloth commanded in Tripoli and Tunis because the Tripoli merchants knew the difference too.

The Katsina market at mid-morning held that particular noise that said business was serious: no music, no idle conversation, only the sounds of assessment and negotiation, the shuffle of bales, the clink of brass weights, the murmur of Arabic and Hausa and the Tuareg tongue Tamasheq overlapping around the issue of price. The scholars of Katsina kept their counting houses near the eastern gate. Their legal opinions on caravan partnerships, on damaged goods, on the fair value of a load that had been rained on crossing the Sahara: these were the documents that made trade possible at a distance, that let a man in Katsina trust a cloth bale originating from a man in Kano he had never met.

Ibrahim understood the system. Katsina was where the Saharan caravans terminated. The salt came down, the cloth went up. He knew Kano cloth because Kano cloth was the thing the Saharan merchants wanted, and they wanted it because it was consistent and because the guild system meant it could be trusted. The cloth he had just refused at full price was proof of the system's logic: a single bad batch was individual failure; the guild's reputation was collective, and the reputation was held in place by men like Ibrahim, who had been buying cloth long enough to know the difference.

To the south, the routes that fed Kano's looms were being extended again.

He had heard in the market that Zazzau's cavalry had taken another town on the Benue road, that the walls were already going up. He had no particular feeling about this. The walls meant the route was more secure, which meant the cotton and the iron and the kola nuts would arrive with fewer delays, which meant the production in Kano would hold its quality, which meant the cloth he bought next month might be better than the cloth he was looking at now. He was a merchant. He understood what the walls were for.

The call to prayer rose from the mosque by the north gate, and the market paused in the way that markets in Katsina paused: not stopping entirely, but quieting, some buyers going to pray and some continuing their negotiations in lower voices and some doing what Ibrahim did, which was to sit down and wait in the shade of his awning with a cup of water that had been cool an hour ago, and think about what he was going to do with the twelve bales of second-quality Gobir cloth he had bought at a merchant's price and would have to sell at a buyer's price somewhere down the line.

He was sitting there, considering this problem, when he became aware of the drumming. It was coming from the compound behind the tanners' quarter, a rhythm that was nothing like the mosque's call, nothing like market noise. He had heard it before in Katsina, always from compounds he did not enter. The women who ran those ceremonies were not his business. His business was cloth, and the afternoon caravans would be arriving from the eastern road, and there might be better Kano cloth among them, cloth that had left the Marina quarter the previous week when the vats were right.

He picked up his brass weight, the one his father had given him, and held it. His father had told him that the weight of things was the beginning of trust. You could not trade at a distance in goods you could not see unless someone somewhere had held the weight and said: yes, this is what it is. The guild dyers in Kano held that weight. Ibrahim held it in Katsina. The scholars held it in their legal opinions. The sarki held it in the walls he maintained and the cavalry he funded and the market he taxed.

The drums continued. The prayers ended. The market resumed its serious noise.

Ibrahim rolled up the second-quality cloth, stacked the bales, and went to meet the eastern caravans.
