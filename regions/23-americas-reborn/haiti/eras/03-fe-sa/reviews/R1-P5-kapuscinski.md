# R1 — Panel Review: Ryszard Kapuściński
## Haiti, Era 3: Fè Sa — The Revolution (opening.md)

**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I can feel the rain. The opening sentence puts weather before anything else — before the man, before the gathering, before the purpose of the night. "The rain came down like a pronouncement." I have been in tropical downpours that felt like this, though never in a forest in Saint-Domingue in 1791. The sentence works because rain at that weight and that hour IS a pronouncement — it changes what a human body can do, it makes noise that covers other noises, it makes visibility into a choice. The scene is already physical before the people arrive.

The ceremony is embodied. The pig's throat is cut. The blood is drunk. The head is later placed on a pike in Cap-Français. These are physical facts, rendered without flinching, and they make the political facts legible in a way that abstraction cannot. The fires "could be seen from ships at sea" — that is a specific sensory measure of the scale of what has begun, and it arrives without editorial amplification.

**The question I'd push on:** The colonial context section (the planter's ledger, the seven-year calculation) is analytically excellent but relatively low in sensory texture compared to the first section. I know that the second section needs to be drier — the planters' world is a world of ledgers, not ceremonies — but the chapter might find one physical detail in the colonial machinery that grounds it the way the rain grounds the opening. The smell of the indigo vat. The sound of the mill. The specific weight of the manacles used for the passepasse punishment. Something that makes the body of that system as present as the body of Cécile Fatiman dancing.

I will also ask: what do the mountains look like at night, behind the forest? Ayiti means "land of mountains" in the Taíno language — the land's own name is a landscape. The revolution moved through those mountains for thirteen years. The full chapter will want to ground itself in the terrain: the north plain, the mountain passes, the distances between plantation and forest, the paths that runners used. The geography of the revolution is the biography of the revolution.

**The physical detail that will stay:** "Boukman was captured and killed within weeks. The French displayed his head on a pike at Cap-Français as a message. The message did not travel the way they intended." The head is physical. The message is abstract. The gap between the two is where the horror lives — and the dark satisfaction of "The message did not travel the way they intended" arrives with exactly the restraint the moment requires.

**A question for the full chapter:** What does Port-au-Prince smell like in August? What does cane smell like when it burns? What is the sound of a plantation bell at dawn compared to the sound of drums in a forest at night? The chapter has the bones of sensory grounding; the full chapter should put flesh on them.
