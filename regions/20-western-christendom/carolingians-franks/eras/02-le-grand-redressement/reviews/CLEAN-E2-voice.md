## Mandate

Hunt register drift. Textbook voice. Modern commentary. Any paragraph that
sounds like it was written for a Western audience rather than from inside the
culture. Voice-random-paragraph test: open to three paragraphs in isolation
and ask: does the voice identify the culture?

## Random-Paragraph Test

**Paragraph 1 (Section III, *Admonitio Generalis* paragraph):**
"The theory of the reform, stated plainly: incorrect books produce incorrect
prayer. Incorrect prayer is an offense before God. Therefore, the correction
of books is an act of piety, and the education of those who copy and use the
books is an obligation of governance."

Voice test result: This paragraph could only be about a culture that combines
literate administration with theological governance. The rhythm is declarative,
the logic is theological-administrative, the register is Capitulary. PASS.

**Paragraph 2 (Section II, Carolingian minuscule paragraph):**
"Alcuin's contribution here was specific and permanent. He oversaw the
development of the Carolingian minuscule, a script cleaner and more legible
than any the Frankish monasteries had been producing, smaller on the page,
portable in a saddlebag, readable across a room by a man who had not copied
it himself."

Voice test result: Clean. Technical description at the method level, rendered
through the properties that made the script matter to the people who used it
(portability, legibility). Not "the minuscule was a significant innovation"
but "readable across a room by a man who had not copied it himself." This is
inside knowledge. PASS.

**Paragraph 3 (Section VI, landing sequence):**
"The idea traveled. The project of a Christian empire, governed by an emperor
who was also the defender of the Church and who derived his authority from
God through both the military conquest that extended the faith and the
intellectual reform that deepened it, this was the Carolingian contribution
to the political theology of the Latin west, and it would haunt every
subsequent emperor who wanted to be Charles and every subsequent pope who
wanted to be Leo III."

Voice test result: This paragraph shifts briefly into retrospective-historical
register: "this was the Carolingian contribution to the political theology
of the Latin west." The phrase "Carolingian contribution" is slightly modern
scholarly in its framing. The rest of the paragraph earns it: "haunt every
subsequent emperor who wanted to be Charles" returns to inside-knowledge
weight. Flag but do not revise; the retrospective is deliberate here and
does not break the voice as much as it steps back slightly to name what is
being described. Acceptable.

## Full Register Audit

**Opening through Section I:** Capitulary voice is established and maintained.
The *correctio* logic drives the register. Short declarative sentences at
assessment moments ("Most of them could not."). CLEAN.

**Section II (Palace School):** The passage about the palace school's evening
debates is the chapter's most "court" moment: "Charles loved the company of
educated men and preferred their dinner conversation to entertainment." This
is Einhard's testimony rendered accurately; the voice holds because it is
attributed. The ironic coda ("probably also a product of the fact that the
educated men Charles preferred had been selected for their ability to entertain")
is noted by E-1. In the Capitulary register, this irony works: it is the
voice of a reform program that is also self-aware about its own methods.
Retained.

**Section III (Admonitio):** The direct quotation from the *Admonitio* is
correctly integrated as primary evidence that carries its own voice. The
surrounding analysis maintains the Capitulary register throughout. The
Theodulf passage ("He found judges who accepted bribes in sight of his
inspection") is from inside Theodulf's perspective and is correctly pitched.
CLEAN.

**Section IV (Subjects of Correctio):** The Saxon passage holds the reform's
inside logic correctly. "The Saxons who had been baptized at knifepoint and
remained, in their own understanding of the world, Saxons performing Christian
rituals would break back into revolt when the military pressure eased" is an
inside account of what Alcuin predicted: grounded in his documented letters.
CLEAN.

**Section V (Weight of What Was Being Built):** Slight shift at "the empire
had produced something it had not quite intended." This is retrospective
framing. It is accurate to the Carolingian situation (Alcuin's own letters
show his surprise at what the empire had become) and does not step outside
into modern judgment. Acceptable. The *imperium* passage is strong: the
word arrives through the characters' own recognition of it.

**Section VI (Landing):** The three-part structure (minuscule; texts; idea)
maintains the voice. The note about modern lowercase letters is the chapter's
one deliberate address to the reader. E-1 cleared it. Correct decision.

**Em-dash count in prose body:** 0. Voice discipline confirmed.
Colons and semicolons carry the pauses throughout. The Capitulary register's
own rhythm eliminates the need for em-dash interruptions.

**Verdict: VOICE SUSTAINED. One minor retrospective phrase in Section III
landing accepted. No register breaks requiring revision.**
