# Sources — Māori

## Primary Sources
- Te Tiriti o Waitangi (1840) — both the Māori and English texts; the gap between them is the central document of New Zealand history
- Māori oral traditions — whakapapa, whaikōrero (formal speeches), waiata (songs); held by iwi; accessible through Te Ara (the Encyclopedia of New Zealand)
- Sir Āpirana Ngata — politician and scholar; central to the Māori cultural revival of the early twentieth century

## Key Academic Works
- Anne Salmond, *Hui: A Study of Maori Ceremonial Gatherings* (1975) — the fundamental study of the meeting house and formal ceremony
- Anne Salmond, *Between Worlds: Early Exchanges Between Maori and Europeans 1773–1815* (1997, Viking)
- Judith Binney, *Redemption Songs: A Life of Te Kooti Arikirangi Te Turuki* (1995) — the prophet and resistance leader
- Ranginui Walker, *Ka Whawhai Tonu Matou: Struggle Without End* (1990, Penguin) — Māori history from a Māori perspective; essential
- Michael King, *The Penguin History of New Zealand* (2003) — comprehensive history with strong Māori content
- Te Ara — The Encyclopedia of New Zealand (online) — the most accessible reference

## Archaeological Sources
- Waitangi National Trust archaeological surveys
- South Island moa-hunting sites — faunal record of the initial settlement period
- Pā (fortified village) sites — the archaeology of the warfare period

## Needed (ACQUISITION)
- Waitangi Tribunal hearing records — the most comprehensive documentation of Māori land claims
- Iwi oral history archives (held by each iwi)
- Te Reo Māori (Māori language) recording archives — Victoria University of Wellington
