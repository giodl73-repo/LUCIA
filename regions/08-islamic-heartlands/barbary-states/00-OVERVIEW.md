# Dawlat al-Jazāʾir / Tūnis / Ṭarābulus — The Corsair States

We were the states of Algiers, Tunis, and Tripoli — *Dawlat al-Jazāʾir, Dawlat Tūnis, Dawlat Ṭarābulus* — and we organized the central Mediterranean around the practice that European powers called piracy and we called *ghaza*: the raiding of enemy ships and coastlines that was the continuation of *jihad* by maritime means. The European powers who sent their ambassadors to ransom their captives and buy our friendship called us the "Barbary States," a name derived from the Berber — Amazigh — population that formed our social base. We called ourselves by our cities. We were, in the Ottoman framework, provincial governors (*pashas* and then *deys*) — but in practice we were autonomous states that had gradually escaped Ottoman control while maintaining the Ottoman connection as a useful diplomatic cover.

We were not simply pirates. We were chartered privateers with a political theology: the Mediterranean was a frontier between the world of Islam and the world of Christendom, the sea was the space where that frontier was contested, and the *ghaza* — the maritime raid — was the mechanism of that contest. We took ships, we took cargo, we took captives. The captives could be ransomed, or they could be enslaved, or they could convert to Islam and join our crews. Our rulers had themselves often been captives who converted — the *renegados* that the European sources called them, Christians who crossed the frontier and became Muslim and became us. The most celebrated of them was Hayreddin Barbarossa, who began as a pirate captain from Lesbos, became an Ottoman admiral, and organized the Algerian corsair system that would define the central Mediterranean for three centuries.

## What Defined Them

**The *ghaza* — maritime raiding as religious obligation.** The corsair captains of the Barbary states operated within a legal and theological framework that distinguished their activities from mere piracy: they held letters of marque from their governments, they raided only ships from nations at war or at non-treaty status with their state, and they understood their activities as *ghaza* — the frontier warfare against the non-Muslim world that Islamic jurisprudence recognized as religiously valid. This was not simply rationalization. The *ghaza* framework shaped who could be raided, what happened to captives, and how the proceeds were distributed. The state took its share, the mosque received its portion, the captain and crew divided the rest. It was organized, institutionalized, and regulated.

**The *renegado* — the man who crossed.** The most distinctive feature of the Barbary corsair world was the *renegado* — the European captive or adventurer who converted to Islam and became part of the corsair system. Hayreddin Barbarossa himself was from the Aegean, originally Greek or part-Greek; many of the most effective corsair captains were of European origin. The choice to cross the frontier — to convert, to join, to become part of the Mediterranean frontier world from the other side — was made by thousands of people for reasons ranging from sincere conviction to pragmatic survival. The *renegados* brought European navigational knowledge, shipbuilding skills, and linguistic capacity to the Barbary fleets. They also embodied the porousness of the frontier they served: the Mediterranean frontier was not a wall but a zone, and people moved through it in both directions.

**The ransom economy and its European connections.** The Barbary states' captive-ransom system was not peripheral to the Mediterranean economy — it was embedded in it. The ransoming organizations — the Trinitarian and Mercedarian friars who specialized in redeeming captives, the consular networks that negotiated collective ransoms, the credit instruments that financed ransom payments — were as much part of the Mediterranean commercial world as the spice trade or the grain trade. The Barbary states maintained consuls from European powers, signed treaties, and managed the captive economy with commercial sophistication. They were embedded in the European diplomatic and commercial world even as they raided it.

**The *deys* and the pragmatic autonomy.** The formal structure of the Barbary states connected them to the Ottoman empire through the title of *pasha* or *bey* appointed by Istanbul. The practical structure evolved toward autonomous governance by the *deys* — the local leaders chosen by the Janissary corps or the corsair community — who negotiated with European powers independently, signed treaties in their own names, and managed the state's affairs without reference to Istanbul except on matters of formal protocol. This de facto autonomy within a nominal Ottoman framework gave the Barbary states the diplomatic cover of the Ottoman connection while allowing them the flexibility to pursue their own interests.

## The Chapters

**1 — Barbarossa Organizes Algiers:** Hayreddin Barbarossa — born on a Greek island, turned corsair captain, turned Ottoman admiral — enters Algiers and begins building the corsair state that will last three centuries. He offers Algiers and its fleet to the Ottoman empire in exchange for Ottoman military support against Spain. The Ottoman-Barbary partnership that will define the western Mediterranean for two centuries is being organized.

**2 — The Corsair Peak:** A *renegado* captain from Venice sails out of the Algiers harbor at dawn. His crew is mixed: Moroccans, Turks, a few Greeks, two Dutchmen who converted in captivity and stayed. He has a Spanish merchant convoy's location from an informant in Oran. The Mediterranean frontier world is at its most active. European captive narratives are being written in dozens of languages. Cervantes is among those who have experienced captivity and written about it. The Barbary states are the most written-about places in the early modern Mediterranean.

**3 — The American Navy:** The American consul in Algiers receives the *dey's* demands: tribute, ransom, treaty renewal. The new American republic has just sent its navy to the Mediterranean for the first time — two frigates and a schooner. The *dey* has seen navies before. But the American navy is not paying tribute. The Barbary Wars are the new republic's first foreign conflict. The corsair era has one more generation before French guns end it.
