## B-2 — David C. Conrad — Board Review of "The Songhai Succession"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Mande oral traditions, the Sundiata epic, the social and political role of the jeli in Mande society, and the relationship between oral and written historical sources in West African historiography.

**Oral tradition accuracy:**

- The chapter correctly presents the jelilu as institutional carriers of Mande historical memory whose commission is specific: they carry the Keita names, the Mande names, the names authorized by the Kurukan Fuga. The observation that "the jelilu in Niani could not sing the Songhai's genealogy" is exactly right — the jeli's authority is lineage-specific, and the Songhai genealogies belong to different traditions.

- The acknowledgment of the Songhai jesere is welcome and important. The chapter correctly notes that the jesere's accounts were not recorded in the same way the Timbuktu scholars' were, and that what survives in the written record is therefore biased toward the scholarly class's perspective on Sonni Ali. This is a significant point that much popular history misses.

- The Kamabolon ceremony at Kangaba is correctly described as a seven-year ceremony maintained by the Diabate lineage. The description of the ceremony as a renewal of the covenant through the physical act of rebuilding the sacred house's roof is accurate to my fieldwork and to Jansen's published work. The detail that the ceremony "continued through the Songhai period, through the Moroccan aftermath, through the colonial era" is correct — the ceremony's persistence across these political changes is one of the most remarkable features of Mande cultural continuity.

- The chapter's handling of the genealogy as an institution that "did not contract when the empire did" is a genuinely insightful observation about how the jeli tradition works. The genealogy is a social-political document, not a territorial one. It records lineage relationships and covenant obligations, not borders. The chapter grasps this distinction.

- The statement that the genealogy "grew" — absorbing the contraction, the Songhai succession, and subsequent events — is consistent with how the jeli tradition actually functions. The epic tradition is accumulative; it adds without subtracting. New events are incorporated into the narrative without displacing older material. This is a defining feature of the tradition that the chapter renders correctly.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The framing of the jelilu's unchanged performance in a changed political context as the chapter's central tension is both narratively effective and historically accurate.
- The sanankuya (joking relationships) are correctly identified as a persistence mechanism of Mande social structure independent of imperial scale.
- The distinction between the covenant as social (regulating clan relationships) and imperial (extending those relationships across a territorial state) is analytically precise.
- "A jeli does not choose which ancestors to remember. A jeli remembers them all." This captures the ethical obligation of the jeli's role exactly.

**What needs attention:**

- The chapter refers to "Bala Faseke's heirs" in the opening. This is acceptable as a literary device (the name Bala Faseke recurs across generations of the Kouyate lineage), but the reader should understand that this does not mean literal direct descendants of one historical individual. The name functions as a title as much as a personal name within the Kouyate tradition. The figures.md handles this distinction for Era 3 but it should be reinforced here.

- The chapter could note, even briefly, that the jelilu carried not just the genealogy but the terms of the Kurukan Fuga itself — the specific obligations, the marriage rules, the craft designations. The ceremony at Kangaba is where these terms are recited in full. The chapter mentions the ceremony but does not specify that its content is the covenant's terms, which is the whole point.

**Verdict:** APPROVED

The chapter handles the oral tradition with accuracy and respect. The jelilu are treated as an institution with specific operating rules and ethical obligations, not as colorful performers. The acknowledgment of the jesere is important for scholarly balance. The central conceit — the unchanging genealogy in a changing political landscape — is both narratively powerful and historically correct.
