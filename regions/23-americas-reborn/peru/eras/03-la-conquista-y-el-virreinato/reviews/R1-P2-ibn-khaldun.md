# R1-P2 — Ibn Khaldun
## Panel Review — Opening: La Conquista y El Virreinato

I am interested in the systemic argument embedded in this opening, and I find it more sophisticated than it first appears. The author is not describing an uprising of oppressed people against an oppressor — that is the surface. The deeper argument is about legitimacy: where it comes from, how it can be transferred, and why the colonial system had created a legitimacy deficit that could only be addressed by reaching backward two centuries to a prior source. This is a structural observation of the first order. Condorcanqui understood something about asabiyya — the group solidarity, the binding force — that the corregidores did not: that the Andean world had a legitimacy vocabulary the Spanish system could not produce.

The mita analysis is where the opening's systemic argument is clearest. The author renders the mita as an ayni-violation — a disruption of the reciprocal cosmos — and this is analytically precise. The Andean labor system before the Spanish had a logic of mutual obligation: the state (the Inca, through the mit'a) provided food, materials, and tools; the household provided labor; the state stored and redistributed. The colonial mita borrowed the mechanism while removing the reciprocal obligation. This produced not just poverty but a broken cosmological contract. The chapter should develop this distinction carefully — the Inca mit'a versus the colonial mita is not merely semantic. It is the structural engine of the uprising's legitimacy claim.

The reference to Micaela Bastidas's organizational work is welcome. A rebellion of this scale required logistical infrastructure that a single charismatic leader could not provide. The opening hints at this ("she had been corresponding with every sympathetic kuraka for months") but does not pursue it. The chapter will need to account for the network: who else joined, what their interests were, why the rebellion spread in some areas and not others. Asabiyya is made, not found; the chapter should show how it was made.

What troubles me slightly is the treatment of the rebellion's failure. The opening tells us, correctly, that Condorcanqui's forces surrounded Cusco and did not take it, and that he was captured by betrayal. But "betrayal" is not an explanation — it is a narrative convenience. The systemic question is: why did the Andean coalition fracture? Who betrayed him and why? What were the competing interests within the Andean world that the Spanish were able to exploit? No successful suppression of a legitimate uprising occurs without internal division. The chapter should account for this.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening establishes pachakuti as the conceptual frame — the world-turning, the cyclical return. This is correct for the Andean worldview. But the chapter needs to account for why this particular pachakuti failed while remaining cosmologically legitimate in Andean memory. The Andean world continued to produce pachakuti claims after 1781 (including, eventually, the independence movement). What is the structural relationship between a failed pachakuti and the persistence of its legitimacy? This is the historical-systems question the chapter must address.
