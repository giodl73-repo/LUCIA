## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of "Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad" opening

**Overall:** The voice is right. This story belongs to its people. The scholarly world of the Torodbe is presented as self-evident, not as exotica requiring explanation. The Sharia operates as a living legal system, not as a cultural curiosity. The Hausa sarakuna are critiqued from within the Islamic legal tradition, not from a modern moral position. I have few objections and one concern.

**What works:**

- The letter is the correct entry point. Not a battle, not a crowd scene, not a "In the year 1804, the Fulani scholar Usman dan Fodio launched..." The reader enters through a specific physical object in a specific scholar's hands. The revolution begins not with a sword but with ink and paper. This is how the Torodbe understood themselves, and the opening honors that self-understanding.

- The Islamic legal vocabulary is used without translation or apology. Fiqh, bid'a, the Sharia, the Sunna, Maliki jurisprudence, al-Maghili and al-Suyuti. These are not glossary items. They are the operating language of the world being depicted. The reader enters the vocabulary as they enter the world: by immersion. A Torodbe scholar reading this passage would recognize the intellectual furniture of his own tradition.

- Munyal is named as a Fulani virtue, not explained as an anthropological concept. "The Fulani virtue that had kept the Torodbe scholars in their compounds and their students at their books while the sarakuna grew fat on unlawful taxes." The patience is the culture's own word, and its exhaustion is the culture's own turning point. This is inside-voice at its best.

- The word "revolution" is used precisely once and immediately reframed: "a revolution distributed across a network of books." This is not a Western revolutionary framework imposed on an Islamic reform movement. It is the author finding an English word and then reshaping it to fit the Torodbe reality. The books are doing the revolutionary work, not the barricades.

- The sarakuna are not villains. They are rulers described through the lens of the Shehu's legal critique. The critique is specific: "taxes the Sharia did not sanction," "customs that had no basis in the Quran or the Sunna," "practices... that any student of fiqh could identify as bid'a." The condemnation comes from within the Islamic tradition, not from the author. This is honest.

**What doesn't:**

- "A territory larger than France" is a comparative frame that introduces a European measuring stick. The reader does not need France to understand the jihad's geographic scope. If the scope must be conveyed, use the culture's own markers: the distance from Gobir to Adamawa, the number of days a messenger walked between the farthest flag-holders, the names of the regions the flags reached. France has no business in this paragraph.

- The phrase "a student who had walked three days through the dry scrub of Gobir" is fine, but the student has no name. He is a functional figure, a delivery mechanism for the letter. If he cannot be named, at least give him a moment of interiority: was he afraid crossing into Katsina territory? Did he know what the bundle contained? A nameless figure is acceptable only if the namelessness is addressed honestly (the record does not preserve him) or if his function is genuinely structural. Here, he could be made slightly more human without inventing anything the record would contradict.

**The question I'd push on:**

Where are the women? The opening is populated entirely by men: the student, Umaru Dallaji, the flag-holders in Kano and Zaria, the Shehu himself. The Fulani reform movement was notable for its engagement of women. Dan Fodio's daughter, Nana Asma'u, was a scholar and poet in her own right. His wife, Maryam, was part of the movement from its earliest days. The Torodbe women participated in the intellectual life of the community and in the jihad itself. An opening that renders the scholarly network as exclusively male has already narrowed the story in a way the culture would not recognize. The chapter must correct this. The opening does not need to introduce Nana Asma'u yet, but it should not create a world where women are absent, because the Torodbe world was not such a world.
