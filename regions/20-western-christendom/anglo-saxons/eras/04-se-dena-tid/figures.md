# Figures — Se Dena Tíd (The Danish Time)
## Anglo-Saxons, Era 4 (c. 793–900 CE)

---

## Alfred, King of the West Saxons (c. 849–899)

He was not supposed to be king. He was the youngest of four brothers, and two of them had already ruled before him. He had been to Rome as a small child, sent on pilgrimage in the family's tradition, anointed by the pope with what may have been a gesture of honorary titles or may have been something the family took more literally. He came back speaking of it, apparently, for years.

He was literate — unusual for a young nobleman of his generation — and became more so as an adult, driven by a chronic frustration that he had not been taught Latin when a child. Asser, who knew him, says that Alfred was given to books and learning from his earliest years, but that his tutors had not given him Latin, and he regretted it. In his forties he taught himself, badly and then adequately and then well enough to translate and interpolate texts that had defeated better-equipped scholars.

The illness Asser describes was real and persistent. Its nature is unclear — modern readers have diagnosed it as Crohn's disease, haemorrhoids, or other conditions, none of which can be confirmed. What is certain is that it struck at public moments, at feasts and in halls, and that Alfred managed it, apparently without using it as an excuse. He governed in pain for most of his adult life. The pain is part of the formation: a man who cannot take physical ease for granted is a man who has had to think about what he actually needs to function.

In the marsh, stripped of everything external to kingship, Alfred did not break. What he was without the title was apparently sufficient to rebuild the title around. The thegns who came to Egbert's Stone came because he was worth following. That is not a structural fact. It is a human one.

## Guthrum (d. 890)

Guthrum arrives in the sources as a name attached to a series of decisions. He led the force that took Chippenham in January 878. He accepted terms at Wedmore after Edington. He was baptized. He took the name Æthelstan. He ruled East Anglia under that name until his death in 890.

His interior is invisible. We do not know what he believed, what he thought of the Christian ritual, whether the oath he swore at his baptism meant anything to him in the terms his own tradition would have recognized. What we know is that he kept it. The peace between Guthrum and Alfred held for the eleven years between Wedmore and Guthrum's death. Given that the previous peace had lasted approximately a season before the Danes broke it and rode for Exeter, eleven years is evidence of something. Whether it is evidence of Guthrum's faith, his pragmatism, his fear, or simply the exhaustion of his army after Edington, the record does not say.

He was Alfred's godson. In the kinship logic of the Christian sacramental system, this made Alfred his father. The two men who had fought each other across the marshes of Somerset were now, by the terms of the ritual, kin. Alfred gave Guthrum gifts at the baptism — as a godfather gives gifts, as a man of higher status gives to a man of lower. The social hierarchy was established in the moment of the spiritual one. That was the governance transaction. Both men knew what it was.

## Asser (c. 853–908/9)

Asser was a Welsh bishop, trained in the scholarly tradition of St David's, recruited to Alfred's court around 885. He came from a different tradition — the Celtic church of Wales, with its own practices and calendar — and he came to a king who was building something he had not seen before: a court that treated learning as a policy matter.

His *Life of King Alfred*, written around 893, is the most intimate source we have for Alfred's personality. It is also a document with interests. Asser was writing for a continental audience — probably for the Franks, who knew Alfred as a distant Christian king and might benefit from knowing him better. He was writing in the hagiographic tradition, which means his Alfred is shaped by conventions of saintly biography as well as observation. He is a witness with a bias. Read him knowing that, and he remains invaluable.

What Asser gives us that the Chronicle does not: Alfred's books. The *enchiridion* tucked in his tunic. The candle-clock. The sleepless nights of translation. The palace school where Alfred sat and listened to his scholars read back their work. The frustration of a king who could not read well enough and who would not accept that limitation. These details feel observed because they are not the kind of details a hagiographer invents — they are too specific, too small, too undignified. A man who had to design a lantern to keep his candles from blowing out in the draft is a man Asser actually watched.

## Werferth, Bishop of Worcester (d. 915)

Among the scholars Alfred recruited, Werferth of Worcester deserves naming. He was an Englishman — from Mercia — and he translated Gregory the Great's *Dialogues* from Latin into Old English, one of the first translations produced under Alfred's program. The *Dialogues* are stories of saints and miracles in Italy, and their relevance to a West Saxon administrator is not immediately obvious. But Alfred sent copies throughout his kingdom, and they were read, and they were copied.

Werferth is significant not for what he translated but for what his recruitment represents: Alfred went looking for literate Englishmen and found them in Mercia, not Wessex. Mercia had better schools. Alfred's own kingdom had fallen behind. He acknowledged this and imported the knowledge he needed. That is not a humble act for a king — it is a practical one. He needed the men more than he needed the pride.

## Æthelflæd of Mercia (c. 870–918)

Æthelflæd is Era 5, not Era 4 — her great years as Lady of the Mercians come after Alfred's death. But she is formed in the Danish Time: Alfred's daughter, raised in a court that treated learning as a governance tool, married to Æthelred of Mercia to cement the political alliance between Wessex and English Mercia. She learned what her father built. She would go on to extend it — fortifying towns, negotiating with the Welsh kingdoms, pushing north. She is the evidence that Alfred's program was transmissible. He built a system that did not require Alfred to run it. His daughter proved it.

## The Unnamed: The Thegn at Egbert's Stone

The Chronicle says Alfred's people were glad to see him when he came to Egbert's Stone.

Consider the man in that meeting. He held land from Alfred — land that carried with it the obligation of military service, of appearance at the shire court, of payment of gafol. He had watched Guthrum's army move through Wessex. He had, probably, given what cooperation was needed to survive. When Alfred disappeared into the Somerset marshes, his lord was gone. The structure of his life — land tenure, legal standing, the whole network of obligation that connected a thegn to his lord and to the king above both — hung in the air without its anchor.

When Alfred came to Egbert's Stone, the thegn came. He brought what levies he had. He was glad in the specific gladness of a man for whom the structure of existence has been restored. Not sentiment. Relief. The lord was alive. The obligations could resume. The war could be fought.

He does not have a name in the record. He is the thing the record does not capture: the ordinary working of the social structure that Alfred had built and that Alfred's survival made possible. The thegns who came to Egbert's Stone are the argument for everything Alfred built afterward. They came because the structure held them together. He spent the next twenty years making the structure firmer.
