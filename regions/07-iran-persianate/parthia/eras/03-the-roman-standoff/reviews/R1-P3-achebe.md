# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Review: PANEL-1 — Opening

**Chapter:** Parthia / Era 3: The Roman Standoff
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (P-3)
**Stage:** PANEL-1

---

I want to begin with what this opening does right, because it does something important right that I have not always seen done right in this project.

It does not use Rome to explain Parthia.

The most common failure in writing about a culture that survived by holding a border against a larger, better-documented power is to let the larger power become the narrative center. Parthia is defined against Rome in almost every Western source, because almost every Western source about Parthia was written by Romans or from Roman materials. The Arsacid empire left few native chronicles — the great Iranian literary tradition that the Samanids would restore was not fully present under the Arsacids, who wrote in Aramaic for administration and in Parthian for royal inscriptions but left no native narrative history of their own comparable to what survives of Rome. This means a writer about Parthia must choose: tell the story through Roman eyes (which makes Parthia the Other), or find the Parthian center of gravity and tell the story from there.

This opening has found the Parthian center of gravity.

The opening moves from Abgar (who acts within the Parthian world, for Parthian reasons, even though his name appears in a Roman source) to the Suren family (whose logic is the logic of the great noble houses, the Parthian structural reality) to the Arsacid king at Ctesiphon (whose court hears of the victory through a performance of Euripides, which is the Arsacid court's own hybrid nature — not an imposition from outside). The story is told from the inside of the Parthian world, not from the outside looking in.

**What I want to protect:**

The Suren passage. "The general whose name the Romans could not quite transcribe — Surena in their accounts, but the clan-name was what mattered, not the personal name, because the Suren family had provided Parthian armies for a century." This is exactly right. The Roman transcription is noted as a limitation of the Roman record. The Parthian logic of clan-name over personal name is presented as the world's actual logic, not as something quaint or unusual. The reader adopts the Parthian framework because the prose does not offer any other.

The "Parthian shot" paragraph. This is the opening's most important cultural-ownership moment. The phrase entered Latin as a Roman misreading. The opening names the misreading and corrects it from the inside. "The Parthian shot was not a parting blow. It was a statement about terrain." This is not the author explaining the culture TO the reader. This is the Parthian strategic logic speaking for itself.

**What I would watch in the chapter:**

The sources are almost entirely Roman — Plutarch, Cassius Dio, Justin. The chapter writer will be tempted to compensate by noting how sophisticated Parthia was, how developed their culture, how this-or-that exceeded Roman equivalents. Any such impulse should be arrested immediately. The sophistication does not need to be argued. It needs to be shown. What the coin looks like — Greek inscription on one side, Iranian image on the other — tells you more about Arsacid cultural brokerage than any assertion of their sophistication.

Watch also for the word "hybrid." It will appear. It should be interrogated: the Arsacid court was not a mixture of Greek and Iranian traditions. It was a court that maintained both simultaneously for different constituencies, which is a political act, not a cultural confusion. The word "hybrid" implies incompleteness. The Arsacid synthesis was a completed form.

**The question I'd push on:**

Does the chapter have a Parthian voice? Not a character speaking Parthian, but a prose register that carries Parthian weight? The opening has found something right — the lapidary, forward-moving-but-qualifying prose — but the chapter will be five thousand words, and the voice must sustain. If the chapter lapses into Roman-perspective summary when the Roman sources are thick, the voice breaks. The chapter must tell the Roman campaigns from the Parthian side of the Euphrates.

*Achebe*
