# Opening — Era 1: The Parima World

Aramo had been calling since midday, and the waterfall's hekura had not yet come.

He crouched at the edge of the shabono's open plaza, his back to the families settling in for the evening — fires being coaxed up, the smell of plantain soup thickening in the air, children chasing each other between the roof posts where the hammocks hung. The yakoana powder was still in his nose, the bright bitterness of the Virola resin that opened the way. He felt the familiar dissolution: the edges of his body going soft, his chest becoming a place where something else could live. He breathed and waited and breathed again, and then — a shimmer in the air just past the line of trees where the stream fell off the edge of the slope — the hekura of the waterfall was moving.

It came in small and quick, the size of a child's fist, luminous. Aramo had been learning the hekura since his teacher's teacher showed him how to build the internal landscape that they needed to settle in — a clean space, swept and tended, scented with the right plants. A shapori who neglected this upkeep would find the hekura restless, difficult to direct, prone to wandering away. Aramo had been diligent. His inner landscape was in order. The waterfall's hekura came in and settled.

He did not shout. He did not make theater of it. Behind him, an old woman looked up from her fire to note that he was kneeling in the plaza, and then looked back down. She knew what he was doing. Everyone knew what he was doing. The shapori's work was not a mystery to be observed from outside; it was a maintenance task, like mending a roof or smoking a fish — essential, regular, requiring skill, belonging to the community rather than to the man performing it.

The hekura of the waterfall was one of hundreds Aramo had learned to call. Each had its own song, its own appetite, its own relationship to the human ailments it could diagnose and the forest conditions it could read. He had spent two years eating almost nothing, the deliberate starvation of initiation, while his teacher sang the hekura into him one by one. He had been young then and the hunger had been a kind of burning. Now he was old enough to carry the weight without the burning, though the yakoana still opened the same door.

Tonight he was looking for something specific. One of the children of the family in the eastern section of the shabono had been feverish for three days. Aramo had already visited twice. He had called the hekura of the wind and the hekura of the forest spider, and they had shown him something persistent at the edges of the child's body — not a fever that would break but something holding on. The waterfall's hekura knew water-carried sickness. It was the one to ask.

The Parima highlands had their own weather. The streams that came off the slopes fed both the Orinoco moving north and the Amazon tributaries turning south; Aramo's people had been in this upland forest long enough that the distinction between the two river systems was a fact of geography the way the sun's path was a fact of geometry. The forest here was different from the lowland forest — cooler, denser, the tepui formations rising above the treeline in flat-topped columns that caught the morning clouds. The hekura of the tepuis were ancient and powerful and not easily called; Aramo had met only two other shapori who could manage them, and one had died in the previous rainy season.

He sang quietly, the hekura song that addressed the waterfall spirits, and felt the creature in his chest respond with information. Water coming from somewhere outside the usual — the child had drunk from a stream farther upstream than the community usually went, during the recent hunt. A different spirit territory. A different negotiation would be needed. Aramo filed this away and began the process of sending the hekura back, carefully, the way you would lower a small flame into a lamp — you did not simply stop, you managed the transition.

Behind him, the old woman had finished her fire preparation and was watching the soup. Her granddaughter was trying to get a puppy to chase a palm frond. Her son-in-law had returned from the forest with two birds, and the quiet discussion of who would eat what was underway in the eastern section. The shabono's sounds were the sounds of an ordinary evening — the particular ordinary of the Parima world, the world that Aramo's shapori work was keeping in condition. The child's fever and the family's meals and the hunt and the forest's condition and the hekura who lived in it were not separate things, and the man kneeling in the plaza was not separate from the woman watching the soup and the birds coming in and the puppy with its frond. It was all one system, and Aramo was attending to the part of it that no one else in the shabono could reach.

He rose slowly, his knees aching in the way of a man who had spent many years on the ground. Tomorrow he would go to the family and tell them what the hekura had shown him. There was a spirit at the upstream water source that needed addressing. He would need to find a shapori from the neighboring community who knew that stretch of forest, whose hekura held jurisdiction there. A visit would need to be arranged. There would be food to share, conversations to have. The healing of one child would require the careful tending of an alliance.

The waterfall's hekura had gone. The soup was ready. The puppy had caught the palm frond and was pulling it to pieces in the dust.

Aramo walked back under the thatched roof and hung himself in his hammock, and the Parima world continued its evening.
