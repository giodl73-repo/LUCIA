# Figures — El Porfiriato y La Revolución
## Mexico Era 4 — 1876–1920 CE

---

## Emiliano Zapata (1879–1919)

He trained horses. This is where he starts, before the revolution makes him into a symbol: a young man from Anenecuilco known throughout the villages of Morelos for his ability to work with animals that other men gave up on. He wore the embroidered jacket and the wide sombrero that the charro tradition called for when it mattered, which is to say when he was riding and being seen, because in the villages of Morelos a man's relationship to his horse said something precise about who he was and how he held himself.

He was not from the poorest stratum of Morelos society. He was a ranchero — a small landholder of middling status — which meant he had enough to lose to care about losing it, and enough social standing to be elected president of the Anenecuilco village council at twenty-nine. Village councils were not ceremonial offices. They were the governing institution of the Nahua community, responsible for managing communal resources, resolving disputes, maintaining the ejido boundaries, and defending the village's documented rights against the hacienda's encroachments. He accepted the responsibility knowing that the hacienda of Hospital was still expanding. He dug up the iron box. He went to the courts. When the courts failed, he went to the fields.

The Plan de Ayala is the clearest statement of his politics: he was not a Marxist, not a liberal in the Madero sense, not a nationalist who wanted to capture the state. He was an agrarian who wanted the ejido restored. The communal land — held collectively, managed by the village, inalienable as a category of tenure — was the social technology that made Nahua community life possible. It was not a romantic attachment. It was the practical basis of subsistence. When the Ley de Baldíos extinguished it legally, the community's capacity to sustain itself was extinguished with it. The Plan de Ayala was not a manifesto for a new social order; it was a demand for the restoration of an old one that had worked.

His tactical intelligence was real and recognized. He could not defeat the Constitutionalists in open field warfare by 1915; he never tried to. He held Morelos through guerrilla operations for eight years against forces much larger than his own. He was assassinated at Chinameca in April 1919 by a stratagem he was warned against. Whether he went knowing the risk, or because the offer of four hundred soldiers and a cache of ammunition was too valuable to refuse, or because after eight years of war a man may develop a certain fatalism — the record does not say with certainty, and the corrido tradition will not tell us: it says only that he rode in and that they fired.

He was forty years old.

## Porfirio Díaz (1830–1915)

He was from Oaxaca, Zapotec by origin, mestizo by the career he built. He fought at Puebla on May 5, 1862, and the battle made him a national hero: a Mexican general holding the French off a ridge, the numerically inferior force defeating the most professional army in the world at that moment. He spent the next fourteen years trading on that reputation while maneuvering toward the presidency he believed he deserved.

He was not an evil man in the manner of people who want evil. He was a man who had decided what Mexico needed — order, foreign investment, modernization — and built a system to deliver it, and the system was very good at delivering what it was designed to deliver and very thorough about suppressing what it was designed to suppress. The villages of Morelos were among the things it was designed to suppress. So were the textile workers of Orizaba, who went on strike in 1906 and were killed by federales on Díaz's order. So was Ricardo Flores Magón, whose anarchist Partido Liberal Mexicano published a newspaper that Díaz's government shut down before jailing its editors. The system was consistent.

He was seventy-four years old when he left on the Ypiranga. He may have believed, as some men of his generation believed, that a country required a father, and that a father who left his children's governance to others was failing them. He was wrong about this. He died in Paris in 1915 having outlived the regime he built but not the era he named. The Porfiriato is his name in Mexican history, which is a form of permanence, and not the form he chose.

## Francisco Madero (1873–1913)

He believed in things. This is not a minor quality in politics, but it was not sufficient for what Mexico required in 1910–1913. He believed in democracy — in effective suffrage and no re-election, in the constitutional process, in the possibility that a country could transition from dictatorship to electoral democracy without the transition requiring a social revolution. He was wrong about the last part. The social revolution was already under way when he took office, and it would not wait for the constitutional process to catch up.

He had been educated in Paris and at Berkeley. He practiced spiritualism, held seances, wrote pamphlets. His *La Sucesión Presidencial* (1908) was a careful analysis of the succession problem in Díaz's system; it showed real intelligence. His inability to understand Zapata was not stupidity — it was class. He came from a landowning family in Coahuila. His model of agrarian reform was litigation and legislation. Zapata's model was: take the land back. These models were not compatible, and Madero did not see this until it was too late to address it.

He was murdered in February 1913, two days after his arrest, in the garden of the Lecumberri prison by officers who reported him killed while attempting to escape. Nobody believed the report. His murder is the moment at which the revolution became a war without constitutional limit, because the constitutional order had demonstrated that it could be destroyed by men with guns in the service of the men with haciendas.

## Pancho Villa (Francisco "Pancho" Villa, born Doroteo Arango, 1878–1923)

He came out of the Chihuahuan desert and he was never fully explicable in terms that political theorists found comfortable, which was part of his power. He had been a bandit, a cattle rustler, a fugitive, before the revolution gave him a frame in which his particular skills — tactical improvisation, personal loyalty, the capacity to inspire men to follow him into situations where following him seemed insane — were valued rather than criminalized. The Division del Norte at its peak in 1914 was an army of sixty thousand men, self-financing through requisitioned cattle and captured silver, capable of taking cities. Its soldiers were not paid the way formal armies were paid; they were fed and armed and given a stake in the outcome, which was enough.

His politics were personal and redistributive in the specific sense that he rewarded loyalty and punished betrayal, and wanted his men to have land when the fighting was over, which is not the same as having a theory of communal land tenure. He and Zapata sat beside each other in the presidential palace in December 1914 and were photographed. The photograph shows two very different men who understood each other as fellow enemies of the same enemy and did not inquire too closely into their differences.

Celaya ended his military power. He was forty-five years old when he was assassinated in 1923, ambushed in his car in Hidalgo del Parral. He had eight bullets in him when they found him.

## Venustiano Carranza (1859–1920)

He was a senator from Coahuila, white-bearded and formal, who had served under Díaz and survived him. His revolutionary credentials were real — he had refused to recognize Huerta's government from the first day — but they did not extend to agrarian reform. He issued a decree in January 1915 promising to return ejido land to the villages, specifically to undercut Zapatista recruitment. He did not implement the decree. He convened the Constitutional Congress at Querétaro and accepted the final text, including Article 27, because he needed the legitimacy it provided. He did not implement Article 27.

He ordered Zapata's assassination.

He was killed in May 1920 when Obregón turned against him and his army dissolved. He fled north with a train full of gold from the national treasury, was caught, died in a village in Puebla. His role in the Revolution is to be the man who held the covenant in one hand and the gun in the other, and used both as he calculated.

## María de la O (c. 1880–1960s)

She was from Miacatlán, in Morelos. She fought with the Zapatista forces from approximately 1911 onward, eventually as a coronela — a rank she earned in the field, not by designation. She organized the movement of supplies through the mountain terrain that the Zapatistas depended on, and she fought when the situation required it. She gave testimony years later about the Morelos campaign to historians who recorded it.

The corrido gave the soldadera the name Adelita, made her beautiful and loyal and attached to a general. The actual soldaderas — the women who traveled with the revolutionary armies — were more various and more necessary than the corrido version. They cooked and nursed and carried intelligence and kept the social life of the army functioning, and some of them also fought. María de la O was one who did both.

She outlived Zapata by decades. She was interviewed by researchers who came to Morelos looking for the revolution's survivors. She told them what she remembered. The archive holds it.

## The Village Council of Anenecuilco

This is a collective figure that the historical record does not fully individualize, and the gap is honest. The council existed before Zapata was elected to it and after his death. Its members are sometimes named in the documents that passed through it; Zapata himself appears in the records as secretario (secretary) before he became presidente; the men who signed the Plan de Ayala can be named from the document itself. But the council's continuous institutional life — its meetings in the years before 1910, its survival through the worst fighting, its recovery of the archive after the Constitutionalist campaigns drove it into hiding — is more clearly documented than its individual members.

The council is the chapter's collective protagonist because the revolution it made was collective. Zapata was the council's instrument as much as its leader; he held the papers because the council elected him to hold them; the Plan de Ayala expressed the council's political position, which was the position of the Nahua villages of Morelos. When Zapata rode to Chinameca and did not come back, the council continued. The claim continued. The archive continued.

The iron box that held the village's title documents — the 1607 records, the colonial maps, the documented evidence of what had been taken — was moved out of Morelos during the worst fighting to keep it safe from burning. It was brought back. It is said to be in the archive of the ejido of Anenecuilco, which still exists, which is to say the claim exists, which is to say the revolution's promissory note is still in the file.
