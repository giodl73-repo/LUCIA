# BOARD Review — British Imperial Legal Historian
## The First Empire: Atlantic Commerce and Plantation

**Domain:** British constitutional history; colonial law; rule of law ideology in imperial context  
**Review type:** Legal accuracy and ideological representation

---

**General assessment:** The chapter handles the Somerset Case well, which is the most technically demanding piece of British legal history in the era. The rule-of-law ideology is correctly represented as sincere AND instrumental simultaneously. This is the right call — the scholarship on this question is clear that the British self-understanding cannot be reduced to cynical cover for economic interest.

**Specific legal notes:**

1. **Somerset v Stewart (1772).** The chapter's account of the ruling is accurate in its substance and appropriately careful in its scope: "He found that English common law contained no such authorization [to seize, detain, and forcibly remove to another jurisdiction]. He did not rule that slavery was illegal in England, or that the slave trade was illegal, or that the Barbados Slave Code... was invalid." This is the correct reading of Mansfield's limited ruling. Many popular accounts overstate Mansfield's holding; this chapter does not. VERIFIED.

2. **Lord Mansfield's ruling language.** The chapter paraphrases rather than quotes. The most famous formulation of Mansfield's ruling — "The state of slavery is of such a nature that it is incapable of being introduced on any reasons, moral or political, but only by positive law... It is so odious that nothing can be suffered to support it but positive law" — is not quoted directly, but the chapter's paraphrase captures the ruling's operational effect accurately. The direct quote might strengthen the chapter without overloading it.

3. **Bengal Regulation III (1818).** The chapter does not mention this — it is a Second Empire matter (1783 onward) and therefore outside this era's scope. Correct.

4. **The Royal African Company's legal structure.** The chapter correctly notes the monopoly was "broken in 1698, when Parliament opened the trade to all English merchants on payment of a ten-percent duty." The specific year and mechanism are accurate. VERIFIED.

5. **Dunmore's Proclamation (1775).** The chapter notes "Lord Dunmore's Proclamation of 1775 offered freedom to enslaved people belonging to rebel colonists who could reach the British lines." The qualification "belonging to rebel colonists" is important and the chapter includes it. VERIFIED. The chapter notes that Loyalist-owned enslaved people were excluded — also accurate. VERIFIED.

6. **The rule-of-law ideology.** The chapter's treatment is its strongest analytical passage: "The same Parliament that debated and endorsed the trade's regulation" coexisting with the Habeas Corpus Act and the Barbados Slave Code. The chapter does not need to name the Habeas Corpus Act; it captures the contradiction correctly through the structure of its argument. The Achebe Test applies here in its hardest form: a serious British constitutional scholar of 1800 would recognize this as an accurate account of how the system held itself in mind.

**No concerns requiring correction.** APPROVED.
