# Board Review — Domain Expert 2: Persian Literary History
## Samanids Era 2: The Peak

**Expertise:** Classical Persian poetry, Rudaki, the qasida tradition, Samanid literary culture
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Factual Accuracy Assessment

**Rudaki's dates and status:** The traditional dates (c. 858–940/941 CE) are correctly given. The title *Amir al-shu'ara'* is attested in later sources, though it may have been retrospectively applied rather than formally granted during his lifetime. The chapter's handling ("a title that implies both preeminence and administrative function") is appropriately hedged.

**The "Mother of Wine" poem:** The attribution of *Mader-e May* to Rudaki is contested in modern scholarship, and the chapter correctly notes "attributable to him with reasonable confidence." The quoted line — "the Mother of Wine rose from the deep sleep, took the cup, and came in the morning" — is a reasonable prose paraphrase of the fragment attributed to him in anthological sources. No objection.

**The qasida's structure:** The description of *nasib*, *rahil*, and *madih* is accurate and appropriately simplified for a narrative context. One minor note: not all qasidas contain all three sections in the sequence described — some open with the *madih* directly, and the *rahil* was increasingly optional by the Samanid period. The chapter's description represents the classical form correctly while the caveat that Rudaki was working within inherited conventions applies.

**The blindness tradition:** The chapter's handling is exemplary. "Whether the historical Rudaki was this man or not, the court he maintained made it possible for others to become so" — this is exactly the right epistemological stance: using the tradition's self-image as evidence about the tradition rather than about the literal fact.

**The "Bukhara ode" anecdote:** Correctly identified as "almost certainly embellished." The story is attested in several later Persian literary histories (*tazkiras*) but with varying details; no contemporary source confirms it. The chapter uses it correctly as evidence about how the court imagined the poet's social function.

---

## Literary History Accuracy

**One addition recommended:** The chapter would benefit from a brief mention of the *Shahnameh* tradition's precursors at the Samanid court — the earlier Persian epic poets (Abu Mansur Daqiqi, who began the *Shahnameh* before Ferdowsi) who were active under Samanid patronage. Daqiqi is killed around 980 CE, which falls in Era 3, but his work as Rudaki's literary heir within the Samanid court is relevant to the literary arc of Era 2. This is a suggestion for enrichment rather than a correction.

**The Arabic/Persian synthesis:** The chapter's characterization of the Persian literary tradition as "mythological-cosmological" in contrast to the Arabic "analytical" tradition is an oversimplification that works for narrative purposes but would require significant qualification in a scholarly context. The chapter uses this framing carefully enough (in a narrative rather than an analytical mode) that it is defensible; a footnote in the notes.md for scholarly readers would be appropriate.

---

## Verdict

Accurate in its literary historical claims. The treatment of Rudaki is the best narrative account I have encountered of what he was doing technically, at the level of how the form operated — the "Knowledge System at Method Level" principle is fully achieved here. The chapter should stand.
