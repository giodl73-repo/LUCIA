# PANEL-2 Review — P-3 Chinua Achebe

**Chapter:** Portuguese Empire, Era 2 — O Século de Ouro
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (cultural voice, dignity, authenticity)
**Stage:** R2 — Notes review

---

## Assessment of the Production Bible

The notes handle the Lusotropicalism problem correctly. Naming the myth explicitly, from inside the belief system that produced it, through the voice of Camões — who believed it — is the right approach. The instruction "Let Camões speak; let the evidence do the rest" is sound. Do not editorialize. The chapter's task is to let the reader understand what it felt like to believe this, not to correct it from outside.

The figure list is strong. Fernão Mendes Pinto is the right counter-voice to Camões: both are inside the empire, both are Portuguese, and they arrive at radically different assessments of the same enterprise. Pinto's ambivalence is not disillusionment imposed by a modern critic; it is the empire's own internal dissent, visible in the empire's own documents.

## What the Notes Handle Well

The casado as structural figure is correctly positioned. The chapter resists the temptation to make the casado either a symbol of Portuguese benevolence (the Lusotropicalism reading) or a symbol of colonial exploitation (the postcolonial reading). The casado in the production bible is a person in a structural position — economically dependent, culturally mixed, legally ambiguous — and the chapter proposes to use that position as the empire's lived reality. This is the correct approach.

The note on the Malay and Gujarati merchants displaced from Malacca (from Ibn Khaldun's Panel-2 pressure) is well-received. The chapter should show this not as a moral judgment but as an operational fact that the empire's own factors recorded: trade relocated when the transit cost of using Portuguese Malacca exceeded the cost of avoiding it.

## What Needs Attention Before Writing

**The Calicut harbor encounter in the opening needs a named or functionally named official.** The records of da Gama's first arrival are available in Portuguese chronicles. The Zamorin (Samutiri) is named; his officials who received da Gama are described in Gaspar Correia's accounts. Even if the specific official is unnamed in the record, Correia's descriptions give enough texture to render a specific encounter. Use this to ground the opening scene in the same specificity the rest of the chapter requires.

**The empire's treatment of converted peoples at the feitorias** — the Indian Christians at Goa, the Kongo nobles who were baptized and then found baptism did not change their legal status in practice — is a dimension the chapter should not omit. Not as a verdict on the empire's sincerity, but as part of what the empire actually was: a system that offered conversion as a legal category, and then applied the legal category inconsistently. The chapter can record this from inside the Portuguese legal framework without stepping outside it to judge.

## The Question I Would Push On

The section on Alcácer Quibir (1578) is the dynastic ending, which belongs to this era's close. But before writing that section, the chapter should decide: does King Sebastião understand himself as a Crusader or as a king? His Moroccan campaign was understood internally as the revival of the Crusader mission — Portugal as the sword of Christ, striking at the heart of Islam. He was twenty-four years old, childless, obsessed, and militarily inexperienced. His court tried to dissuade him. He went anyway. The empire's ending in this era is not an accident of fortune; it is the Crusader logic consuming itself. That is the chapter's final statement on what the empire understood itself to be.
