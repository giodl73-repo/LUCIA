## Pass: Outside Judgment

**Finding 1 — "of course" (Section V):**
"The people who had been living on that coast for generations had not, of course, consented to this theory."
The "of course" is a mild editorial intrusion, signaling the author's moral position rather than the culture's. It is a small violation but worth noting. The sentence is stronger without it: "The people who had been living on that coast for generations had not consented to this theory."

**Finding 2 — "reading the situation correctly" (Section IV):**
"The Wolof who ran from the shore were not ignorant of what strangers with ships could mean. They were reading the evidence correctly."
The phrase "reading the evidence correctly" is close to endorsing their behavior from a modern perspective. It holds: within the worldview being described, their response WAS rational given what they could observe. The framing is defensive rather than condescending. No change required, but flagged.

**Finding 3 — Zurara's "divided witness" framing:**
The chapter uses "divided witness" to describe Zurara's position. This is editorial framing applied by the author, not Zurara's own characterization of himself. However, it describes a real structural feature of the chronicle and is not a judgment; it is an observation. The chapter does not tell the reader what to conclude from Zurara's divided witness. It presents the division and stops. This is correct.

## Pass: Anachronistic Morality

The chapter does not apply modern moral standards retrospectively. The slave trade is presented as what it was: an economic and theological structure that the participants operated within without the conceptual framework to condemn. Zurara weeps and continues; the chapter shows this without explaining it. No anachronistic morality found.

## Pass: Romanticization / Demonization

Neither Henry nor the program is romanticized. Henry is specifically described as "responsible for the slave trade" in figures.md. The navigational achievement is not presented as redemptive; the chapter does not imply that the exploration compensates for the extraction. No demonization either: the chapter does not render the Portuguese as uniquely evil, only as participants in a structure that began here and would expand far beyond what they built.
