# BOARD-1 — Stuart B. Schwartz

**Domain:** Colonial Brazil, sugar plantation economy, Brazilian social history
**Key work:** *Sugar Plantations in the Formation of Brazilian Society* (1985); *Slaves, Peasants, and Rebels* (1992); *All Can Be Saved* (2008)
**Chapter reviewed:** Brazil, Era 2 — A Colônia — Açúcar, Ouro, e Escravos

---

## Factual Accuracy Assessment

**The engenho and safra:** The description of the engenho rollers, the three-cylinder press, the boiling house sequence, and the use of bagasse as fuel is accurate. The mallet kept beside the rollers for emergency amputation is historically documented in plantation records and in the accounts of travelers who visited the mills. The word safra (harvest season) is correctly used. The continuous operation of the mill during safra is accurate — the cane's fermentation timeline imposed exactly this rhythm on the labor.

**Palmares timeline:** The dates given are accurate within the scholarly debate. The expeditions of 1645 and 1677 are documented. The Dutch occupation of Pernambuco running 1630–1654 is correct. The Treaty of 1678 with Governor Aires de Souza de Castro: the chapter says Governor Aires de Souza de Castro — this is slightly imprecise. The treaty of 1678 was actually negotiated during the governorship of Aires de Souza e Castro. This is a minor name variant, not a factual error.

**Zumbi:** Born c. 1655 in Palmares. Captured as a child, raised by Father António Melo in Porto Calvo. Learned Portuguese and Latin. Escaped at approximately 15. These details are in the primary sources (notably the account of Fernão Carrilho). The chapter's handling of this biography is accurate and well-selected.

**Ganga Zumba:** The title discussion is defensible. The chapter says "nganga a nzumbi, something like 'great spirit-lord' or 'master of the dead'" — scholars debate the precise etymology; "master of the dead" or "great lord" are both in the literature. The uncertainty is appropriate to the state of the field.

**Domingos Jorge Velho:** Correctly identified as a São Paulo bandeirante. The siege of Cerca Real do Macaco lasting twenty-two days is from the colonial records (Fernão Carrilho's report). Accurate.

**Antônio Soares:** Correctly identified as the man who betrayed Zumbi. His name is in the colonial records. The chapter's statement that "the records do not say what Antônio Soares received for this" is honest — the records are silent on this.

**The Engenho Velho terreiro:** Founded "in the 1830s by three women — Iyá Detá, Iyá Kalá, Iyá Nassô." The dating is debated (some sources say 1830s, others say the founding traditions extend earlier), but the three founders are consistently named in Candomblé scholarly literature. Acceptable.

**The gold rush of Minas Gerais:** "c. 1690–1750" is a reasonable range for the main gold-rush period, though gold was still extracted into the late eighteenth century. For the chapter's purposes the dating is appropriate.

## Cultural Representation Assessment

The chapter is admirably careful with the distinction between Bantu and Yoruba/Nagô traditions. The composite woman is from the Mina coast (Yoruba/Fon), and carries Xangô knowledge — which is the Nagô tradition. The governance structure of Palmares uses Bantu titles. These are kept distinct throughout. This is more careful than most popular accounts and some scholarly ones.

The Freyre engagement is handled with the right combination of acknowledgment and critique. Freyre's work is unavoidable for colonial Brazil; dismissing it entirely would be historiographically naive. Acknowledging that he "was right that the relationship was intimate" while noting he "was not right enough about what intimacy conducted under conditions of absolute power costs the people without power" is precisely the right calibration.

## Corrections Required

One factual note: the chapter states the Movimento Negro Unificado established the Dia da Consciência Negra "in 1978." The MNU was founded in 1978; the official recognition of November 20 as a national holiday came later — 2003 at the federal level (though some states recognized it earlier). The chapter should say the MNU established the commemoration in 1978, and the national holiday was officially recognized in 2003.

No other corrections.

## Verdict

**Approved for final.** One factual clarification (MNU 1978 / national holiday 2003). Historically accurate, culturally respectful, engages the source base appropriately, handles the Freyre controversy correctly.
