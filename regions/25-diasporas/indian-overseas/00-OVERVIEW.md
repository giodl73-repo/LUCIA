# भारतीय प्रवासी — Bharatiya Pravasi: Indians Overseas

The Ramayana was performed in Trinidad. The Holi festival was celebrated in Fiji. The temple to Lord Shiva rose from the red earth of KwaZulu-Natal. The Indian overseas diaspora is the story of a civilization's extraordinary capacity to reconstitute itself in places its originators had never imagined — carrying not just genes and language and food but the complete architecture of a way of life: the religious traditions, the family structures, the educational values, the music, the social hierarchies (complicated by displacement), and the stories that gave it all meaning. From the Bihar and eastern UP villages that provided most of the indenture laborers to the Tamil Nadu fishing communities that sent their sons to Ceylon and Burma, to the Gujarati traders who built commercial networks from Durban to London — the Indian overseas experience is as diverse as India itself.

The mechanism of the diaspora's largest wave was indenture — the coolie system. After the abolition of African slavery in the British Empire (1834–1838), the plantation owners of Trinidad, British Guiana, Fiji, Mauritius, Natal, and East Africa needed labor. The British colonial system developed the indenture contract: an Indian laborer, typically from the rural poor of Bihar, eastern UP, or Tamil Nadu, signed a five-year contract to work on a plantation in exchange for passage, food, housing, and wages. The conditions were frequently miserable; the wages were barely enough to live; the "freedom" at the end of the indenture left workers stranded 10,000 kilometers from home with no means to return. Approximately 1.5 million Indians were indentured between 1838 and 1917, when the system was abolished. Their descendants became the founding populations of Indian communities in Trinidad, Guyana, Fiji, Natal, and elsewhere. They also built, in these places, some of the most remarkable examples of cultural persistence and community construction in the modern world.

## What Defined Them

**The Girmit — the indenture as founding experience.** The word girmit is the Hindi/Bhojpuri pronunciation of "agreement" — the indenture contract. Indo-Caribbean, Indo-Fijian, and Indo-South African communities use it to name the founding experience of their diaspora: the contract that brought their ancestors across the ocean, the conditions that tried to break them, and the culture they built in spite of and through those conditions. The girmitiya (the indentured laborer) is the founding figure of these communities' self-understanding — not a victim, but a survivor who built something. The communities that emerged from the girmit system maintained their Indian religious traditions, their Hindi dialects (Bhojpuri especially), their musical forms, and their family structures with a tenacity that repeatedly surprised the colonial administrators who expected assimilation.

**The Ramayana as portable homeland.** The Ramcharitmanas — Tulsidas's sixteenth-century Hindi retelling of the Ramayana — is the primary sacred text of the North Indian Hindu tradition, and it was the most important cultural object the girmitiya brought to the Caribbean and Fiji. In villages where the priest might be lost, where the Sanskrit of the official religious texts was inaccessible, the Ramcharitmanas — in Awadhi, understandable by the rural poor of Bihar and UP — could be read aloud and understood. The Ramlila (the dramatic performance of the Ramayana story) was performed in Trinidad and Guyana and Fiji and Mauritius, creating a shared sacred geography in places that had no other connection to the Gangetic plain except the text and the memory of the people who carried it.

**Satyagraha — the diaspora's political invention.** Mohandas Gandhi arrived in South Africa in 1893 to serve as a lawyer for an Indian merchant. He encountered the systematic legal discrimination against Indians in Natal: Indians could not travel first class; Indians needed passes; Indians were excluded from public spaces. His twenty years in South Africa were the laboratory in which he developed satyagraha — the method of nonviolent resistance through civil disobedience, truth-telling, and the willingness to accept suffering rather than cooperate with injustice. Satyagraha was not an Indian philosophical tradition transplanted to Africa; it was an invention made necessary by the specific conditions of diaspora existence — a community without political power, confronting a state with legal authority, and unable to fight back with violence. The Indian overseas diaspora produced the most influential political philosophy of the twentieth century.

**The caste question in diaspora.** The Indian diaspora is not caste-free. The hierarchies that organized social life in India were transported along with everything else. In some diaspora contexts — the Caribbean especially — the disruptions of the indenture system (which mixed castes on the same ships and plantations) reduced caste's practical significance, though not its symbolic one. In other contexts — the Indian professional diaspora in Britain, the United States, and Canada — caste discrimination has been documented in hiring, housing, and social networks. The 2023 California bill that would have prohibited caste discrimination (it was eventually vetoed) was the most visible expression of this tension. The diaspora is still working out what it carries from India and what it leaves behind.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Girmitiya:** Bihar, 1880: a recruiter (arkatti) approaches a family in debt after a failed harvest. He describes the work in Trinidad: five years, guaranteed wages, food, housing. He does not describe the distance, the loss, or the conditions. The family signs. The journey to the depot in Calcutta takes three weeks. The voyage to Trinidad takes three months. The five years become a lifetime.

**2 — The Ramayan Mandali:** In a Trinidadian village in 1910, a group of indentured workers and their children gathers in the evening to sing from the Ramcharitmanas. They have no priest; the nearest pandit is in the next estate. One man knows the text by memory. They sit on the ground. The Tulsidas verses that their grandparents sang in Bihar are being sung on the other side of the world. The Ramayana has come to Trinidad.

**3 — Gandhi in Natal:** September 1893: Mohandas Gandhi is thrown off a first-class train at Pietermaritzburg station. He has a first-class ticket, but he is not white, and the conductor insists he move to the third-class carriage. He refuses. He is thrown onto the platform. He sits in the station through the cold night, deciding what to do next. The non-violent resistance movement that will eventually topple the British Empire begins in this train station.

**4 — Fiji, 1987:** The Indian-descended community has been a plurality in Fiji since the 1940s. In 1987, the coalition government led by Timoci Bavadra, which includes Indo-Fijian representatives, wins the election. Two months later, Lieutenant Colonel Sitiveni Rabuka leads a military coup. He justifies it by the need to protect Fijian indigenous rights against Indian political power. It is the first of four coups in Fiji's post-independence history. The Indian community — whose grandparents built this country under indenture — discovers that citizenship is not secure.

**5 — The Silicon Valley Diaspora:** By 2020, Indian-Americans lead or co-founded 8% of Silicon Valley start-ups and hold a disproportionate share of H-1B visas and professional positions in the technology sector. Sundar Pichai leads Google; Satya Nadella leads Microsoft. This diaspora — English-educated, technically trained, migrating for professional opportunity rather than under labor contracts — is as different from the girmitiya as it is possible to be while remaining within the same cultural tradition. The question of what connects them — the language, the food, the religious practice, the family obligation — is a question about what Indian identity is at its core.

**6 — Pravasi Bharatiya Divas:** January 9: India celebrates its overseas diaspora with a national holiday established in 2003. The date commemorates Gandhi's return to India from South Africa in 1915. The government of India formally recognizes its diaspora as a national asset — 32 million people whose remittances ($125 billion in 2023) exceed India's foreign direct investment. The diaspora is both celebrated and instrumentalized. The people who left — some under duress, some for opportunity — are now positioned as India's global ambassadors. Whether they feel this way depends on which generation you ask, and where they are.
