# CLEAN Review — E-1: Judgment Auditor

**Editor:** E-1 — Judgment Auditor  
**Focus:** Forbidden words, anachronistic morality, outside-lens violations  
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Reading for Violations

I read the chapter for instances where the author steps outside the culture's worldview and judges from a modern or Western perspective. I looked for:

1. Forbidden words (primitive, advanced, civilization, tribe, discovered, New World, Dark Ages)
2. Anachronistic framings ("they should have understood earlier," "they failed to modernize," "they couldn't adapt")
3. Outside-lens judgments (pity, condescension, surprise at sophistication)
4. Romanticization or demonization

---

## Findings

**Forbidden words:** None found. The word "sophisticated" never appears. The word "advanced" never appears. No "discovered by." No "primitive." The language is precise and avoids the conceptual traps.

**Anachronistic framing:** None found. The chapter does not say "the Qing should have understood." It says "the Qing discovered." It does not say "they failed to modernize fast enough." It says "the system could not transform without destroying itself." This is correct framing. The distinction between "they should have" and "they could not without destabilizing" is the difference between outside judgment and inside understanding.

**Outside-lens judgments:** I found one moment that came close. In the self-strengthening section, the phrase "created a modern military without a modern political structure to command it" could be read as suggesting the Qing was foolish to try. But the chapter immediately contextualizes this: creating a modern political structure would have required diminishing imperial authority, which the system could not tolerate. So the judgment is not that they were foolish, but that they faced a genuine bind. Acceptable.

**Romanticization:** None found. The Boxers are not portrayed as noble resisters or as primitive mystics. They are shown as making sense within their own logic (rejection of foreign presence, return to traditional strength) while acknowledging that their approach failed militarily. This is neither romantic nor contemptuous. Good.

**Demonization:** The foreign powers are not demonized. They are shown as rational actors pursuing rational interests with asymmetrical power. No moral judgment is imposed. This is the correct frame.

---

## The Achebe Test

Could a thoughtful Chinese reader, a thoughtful British reader, and a thoughtful Japanese reader all feel their story was told with dignity? 

- **Chinese reader:** The Qing's perspective is respected throughout. Lin Zexu is intelligent and coherent. Cixi is a political operator, not a villain. The court's choices are shown as intelligent responses to constraints. The century is framed not as a failure but as a crisis that the system could not resolve without ceasing to be itself. Yes, dignity.

- **British reader:** The British are not demonized. They are shown as pursuing commercial interests and enforcing power asymmetry—that is what happened. They are not presented as evil or as villains in some moral sense. They are presented as actors in a system that favored their interests. Yes, dignity.

- **Japanese reader:** Japan's Meiji Restoration is treated as what it was—a more successful transformation than the Qing's self-strengthening movement. The victory in the Sino-Japanese War is presented as the outcome of better integration of military capacity into political structure. There is no demonization of Japan. Yes, dignity.

---

## Verdict

**PASS.** The chapter passes the Judgment Auditor's examination. No forbidden words, no anachronistic morality, no outside-lens violations. The frame is consistent throughout: we are inside the Qing's understanding of its own world, and the reader discovers what the Qing discovered—that the world had decided the rules were different.

The chapter can proceed to Voice Keeper and Compression Editor without revision on judgment grounds.
