# Figures — Sassanid Era 1: The Founding (c. 224–272 CE)

---

## Ardashir I (r. 224–242 CE)

*Shāhanshāh — King of Kings*

Ardashir comes to us through his own propaganda and very little else. The rock reliefs are his clearest self-statement: a man on a horse receiving a ring from a god, with the defeated enemy beneath. The coins show a face — a strong brow, a distinctive crown that evolves as his reign stabilizes, the fire altar always on the reverse. What the coins and reliefs cannot show is the quality of attention that made this possible: the decades of careful consolidation in Persis before the war with Artabanus, the patient construction of coalitions among Fars lords who had their own reasons to prefer a Sassanid to an Arsacid suzerain.

He was from the priesthood but he was not a priest in the administrative sense. He was the grandson of Sasan, the fire temple keeper at Istakhr, and the son of Papak, who parlayed that sacred office into local political control. The path from temple to throne was three generations long. Ardashir had watched his father manage the intersection of sacred authority and political power; he inherited both the technique and the specific legitimation the fire provided.

What distinguished him from the Arsacid lords he replaced was not military genius alone — he was a capable general but so were they. It was the comprehensiveness of his claim. The Arsacid kings had ruled a diverse empire through a light suzerainty that left local practices largely intact. Ardashir was building something different: a state whose divine authorization derived from a specific religious tradition, whose king's legitimacy was inseparable from Ahura Mazda's endorsement, and whose institutional infrastructure connected the throne to the sacred fires through an organized clergy. He did not merely win the war. He changed what winning the war meant.

He died in 242 CE, having associated his son Shapur in the kingship perhaps two years before. The transition was smooth. The institution held.

## Shapur I (r. 242–272 CE)

*Shapur the Great — Shāpur dhul-aktaf in the later Arabic tradition*

Shapur I is the most dramatically visible of the Sassanid founders because his achievements left the most stone. Three Roman emperors defeated — Gordian III, Philip the Arab, Valerian — is a record that no other ruler in antiquity matched, and the reliefs commemorating it at Naqsh-e Rostam, Bishapur, and Darabgird ensure it was not forgotten. But the stone emphasis can mislead: Shapur was more than a military king.

His inscription at Ka'ba-ye Zartosht is a primary document of unusual complexity. In it, Shapur presents himself as universal sovereign of a universal empire: the provinces are listed not as conquered territories but as the natural extent of a world properly ordered. The grants he records — to Zoroastrian fires, yes, but also to Buddhist, Brahmanical, and Christian institutions — reveal a king thinking in imperial rather than sectarian terms. He understood that ruling 67 provinces required more than Zoroastrian legitimacy; it required the cooperation of communities that the Zoroastrian framework alone could not accommodate.

His patronage of Mani is the most revealing episode. Mani's theology was intellectually remarkable — a systematic synthesis of Zoroastrian, Buddhist, and Christian elements that Shapur recognized, one suspects, not just as spiritually interesting but as politically useful. A religion that honored Zarathustra as a predecessor and offered itself as the completion of all truth was exactly the kind of universal claim that an empire stretching from the Euphrates to the Oxus might find valuable. Shapur was not confused about his own tradition. He was using his universal position to explore the limits of what a universal empire could contain.

He died in 272 CE, leaving the tension between his tolerance and his high priest's orthodoxy unresolved. Bahram I, who succeeded him, resolved it in Kartir's favor.

## Kartir (fl. c. 240–290 CE)

*Mobadan Mobad — High Priest of Priests*

Kartir is one of the most unusual figures in ancient history: a priest who inscribed his own autobiography onto the same cliffs where kings recorded their victories, in the same medium, in the same genre, claiming the same kind of permanent truth. That he could do this — that the Sassanid religious-political system had created a space in which a priest could accumulate enough authority to warrant such commemoration — is itself evidence of how thoroughly the founding era had transformed the relationship between religion and power.

His rise is documented in his own inscriptions: from *hrb* (priestly teacher) under Ardashir, through increasing titles and responsibilities under Shapur I, to *mobadan mobad* under Bahram II. Each step represents the formalization of an authority that had previously been informal. The clergy that Ardashir had organized still needed someone to organize it; Kartir provided that function across five reigns, outlasting and outmaneuvering every political change.

His inscriptions at Naqsh-e Rostam and Naqsh-e Rajab include, unusually, an account of a mystical journey — a vision of the afterlife in which Kartir himself travels to the divine realm and returns with knowledge of what awaits the righteous and the wicked. This is the tradition's own way of certifying priestly authority: not just administrative position but direct access to the cosmic truth the empire claimed to defend. Kartir is not merely the high priest in bureaucratic terms. He has seen what the fires point toward.

His suppression of Mani and Manichaeism after Shapur's death defines his legacy in one direction. His organization of the Zoroastrian clergy, his establishment of fire temples across the empire's territories, and his creation of a coherent institutional structure for the priestly estate define it in another. Both legacies are real. The stone preserves both.

## Mani (c. 216–274/277 CE)

*The Prophet of Light — al-Mani in Arabic; the Living Gospel, in his own terms*

Mani does not fit neatly into a chapter about the Sassanid founding because he was not Sassanid and not fully Zoroastrian and not, finally, contained by either. He was born near Ctesiphon into a Jewish-Christian baptismal community, grew up speaking Aramaic, received his revelation in his twenties, and spent the rest of his life constructing the most systematically ecumenical religious tradition the ancient world produced.

He belongs in this chapter because Shapur gave him the space to exist within the empire, and the space Shapur gave him reveals something about the founding era that the stone reliefs alone cannot show. The Sassanid empire at its founding was not a sealed Zoroastrian state; it was an empire that contained within it the full range of the ancient world's religious traditions, and its ruling dynasty was making decisions about what that meant.

Mani's theology identified light and darkness as the fundamental principles of existence — not as symbols but as real substances, in permanent cosmic conflict. Human beings were composed of both; the divine light imprisoned in matter could be liberated through specific practices of knowledge and asceticism. He presented Zarathustra, the Buddha, and Jesus as predecessors who had each given partial truth to specific peoples; he was the seal of the prophets, giving complete truth to all peoples simultaneously.

Shapur found this useful or interesting or both. Kartir found it intolerable. After Shapur's death, Kartir prevailed. Mani died in captivity. His religion spread along the Silk Road, west into North Africa (where Augustine would briefly convert to it), east into Central Asia and China and ultimately into the Uyghur Empire. The empire that tried to extinguish it had built the roads it traveled. History does not always cooperate with the suppression orders carved in stone.

## Artabanus IV / Ardawan V (d. 224 CE)

*Last Arsacid, King of Kings*

Artabanus IV appears at Naqsh-e Rostam beneath Ardashir's horse — the defeated enemy, the dynasty's end, the proof that the old order has been superseded. This is how the Sassanid record presents him. What the Sassanid record cannot present is his side.

The Arsacid dynasty had held the Iranian plateau for nearly five centuries. They had fought Rome to a strategic standoff that neither side could resolve. They had maintained trade across the Silk Road. They had preserved, in their eclectic way, enough of the Iranian cultural and religious tradition to give the Sassanids something to claim continuity with. Their eclecticism — the Greek gods on the coins, the tolerance of multiple religions — was not failure. It was the management strategy of an empire that stretched from the Euphrates to the Oxus and needed to accommodate the full range of what that territory contained.

What they could not provide was the kind of concentrated, exclusive divine legitimacy that the Zoroastrian reformers wanted. The fire temple at Istakhr burned under Arsacid suzerainty; but Ardashir's grandfather was a temple custodian, not a royal advisor. The gap between tolerated and institutionalized is the gap between the Arsacid and Sassanid periods. Artabanus died at Hormozdgan closing that gap from the wrong side.

The traditions differ on how he died. Some say single combat with Ardashir — the old Iranian form, the kings' duel, the empire's fate settled between two men and their horses. Others say the battle was more conventional and Artabanus fell in the general rout. The Sassanid relief at Naqsh-e Rostam shows him under the horse's hoof, which is the language of absolute defeat rather than honorable combat. Whichever account is accurate, the stone's version is the one that lasted.
