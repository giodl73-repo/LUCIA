# VaShona — "The Builders of the Plateau"

The Shona are the farmers, miners, and builders who have inhabited the Zimbabwe plateau for more than a thousand years. The stone-walling tradition — dzimba dza mabwe, houses of stone — that produced Great Zimbabwe, Khami, Naletale, and hundreds of smaller enclosures across the plateau is a Shona tradition: developed, refined, and maintained by Shona-speaking communities across ten centuries. The gold mining that drove the Indian Ocean trade connections of Great Zimbabwe and its successors was done by Shona miners. The political structures that organized the plateau — the Mutapa, the Rozwi, the smaller chieftaincies — were Shona political structures. The spiritual system of mhondoro (royal ancestral spirits) and svikiro (spirit mediums) that legitimized political authority on the plateau is a Shona spiritual system. "Shona" names the people whose world all of this was.

"Shona" is, however, a term with a complicated history. It was not in common use as a collective self-identifier before the colonial period; it was partly applied from outside by colonial administrators and linguists who grouped together the dialects of the plateau (Zezuru, Karanga, Korekore, Manyika, Ndau) as a single language family. The people of the plateau knew themselves by their specific dialect group, their specific clan, and their specific chieftaincy, not as "Shona" in general. The collective identity was partly a product of colonial categorization. This does not make it false — linguistic family and shared cultural traditions are real — but it requires acknowledging that the unity of "the Shona" as a single people is partly a modern construction built on genuine pre-existing cultural commonalities.

## What Defined Them

**The mhondoro system and ancestral governance.** The mhondoro — the royal ancestral spirits of Shona polities — were not simply religious figures; they were political institutions. Each major territorial chieftaincy had its own mhondoro, the spirit of the dynasty's founding ancestor, who communicated with the living through svikiro (possessed mediums). The medium was consulted on political decisions, on questions of war and peace, on the appropriate location of a new settlement. The mhondoro had territorial authority — it protected the land and its people, and its permission was required for certain activities on that land. This was a spiritual technology for legitimizing political authority that was simultaneously a system for incorporating diverse communities into a shared political framework.

**The stone-walling tradition as cultural continuity.** The dry-stone walling tradition that produced Great Zimbabwe's monuments continued for eight centuries and is one of the most distinctive cultural markers of the Zimbabwe plateau's Shona communities. From the small local-chief's enclosure to the great walls of Great Zimbabwe and Khami, the tradition maintained consistent technical principles — the selection and shaping of granite blocks, the coursing technique, the use of chevron and other decorative patterns in the later Rozwi period — that mark it as a continuous cultural practice rather than a series of independent inventions. This continuity is itself evidence for the depth of Shona cultural rootedness on the plateau.

**The gold mining tradition.** Shona miners developed the alluvial and reef gold extraction techniques that powered the Indian Ocean trade connections for five hundred years. The Shona knew where the gold was — in specific river valleys, in quartz reef formations — and they knew how to extract it: panning alluvial gold from river gravels, following the reef into the ground, breaking the ore with fire-setting (burning timber against the rock face to crack it) and iron tools. The scale of pre-colonial Shona gold mining is documented by the tens of thousands of ancient mine workings scattered across the Zimbabwe plateau — some of them hundreds of feet deep.

**The spirit medium tradition in liberation war.** The mhondoro mediums played a significant role in the Zimbabwe liberation war (Chimurenga) of the 1970s. ZANLA guerrillas (the military wing of ZANU) explicitly sought the support and blessing of local mhondoro mediums before establishing their operations in a new area. The mediums' endorsement gave the guerrillas legitimacy with the rural population and connected the liberation struggle to the deep cultural logic of mhondoro authority over territory. This was not simply tactical; it reflected a genuine continuity between the spiritual legitimation of political authority in the Shona tradition and the project of reclaiming the land from settler colonialism.

**Zimbabwe and the name.** When the country gained independence in 1980, it took the name Zimbabwe — from dzimba dza mabwe, the Shona words for "houses of stone" that named the Great Zimbabwe site. This was a deliberate assertion: we are the people of the stone houses; the magnificent ruins that colonial ideology claimed were built by Phoenicians or Arabs were built by our ancestors; this country belongs to the people who built it. The name is a political argument encoded in three syllables.

## The Chapters

**1 — The First Enclosure:** A Shona chief has ordered the first stone wall built around his cattle kraal. The walls are small, low, practical. The tradition begins here, in function. It will become art in eight centuries.

**2 — The Gold Valley:** A Shona miner is in a shaft thirty feet down, hammering at a quartz reef with an iron tool. Above him, the fire that cracked the rock is still smoking. The gold visible in the white quartz is real and the price it will fetch at the coast is real. He keeps hammering.

**3 — The Spirit Medium:** A svikiro has been entered by the mhondoro of the founding ancestor. The possession has been verified by the signs. The chief is asking about the drought. The mhondoro speaks: there has been a violation of territorial boundary. The answer is specific. The community will act on it.

**4 — The Guerrilla and the Medium:** 1972. A ZANLA operative has arrived in a village in the Chiweshe communal land. He is asking to speak with the spirit medium. He has brought the prescribed gifts. He is asking the mhondoro's permission to operate in this territory. The medium consults. The mhondoro agrees. The liberation war in this valley is now sanctioned by something older than the state.

**5 — Independence:** 1980. A new flag is raised. On it: the Zimbabwe Bird — the soapstone figure from the ruins, the ancestor who looked out over the plateau from the Hill Complex. The name of the country is the name of the ruins. The people of the ruins have come home.
