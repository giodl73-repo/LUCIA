# B-3 -- Jansen -- Board Review of "The Scholarly Peak"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Mande political culture; the Kurukan Fuga as social charter; social categories in Mande society; the relationship between oral performance and political legitimacy.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The Kurukan Fuga's establishment of women's right to speak in assembly: this is present in the oral tradition as recorded by multiple scholars, including Niane and in the UNESCO-recognized version. The chapter correctly references this in the context of Ibn Battuta's disapproval of Malian women's freedom. The framing is appropriate: the Kurukan Fuga provides the internal logic for practices that Ibn Battuta judges by external standards.
- The description of the court protocol -- the mansa speaking through an intermediary, the courtiers' gesture of drawing robes off shoulders -- is consistent with Ibn Battuta's account and with what we understand of Mande court practice.
- The chapter's treatment of Mansa Sulayman is appropriately restrained. The succession politics after Sulayman are complex and not fully documented; the chapter correctly limits itself to what the sources support.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The Kurukan Fuga appears in this chapter more lightly than in Era 3, which is appropriate. The covenant remains the political framework but the chapter's focus has shifted to the scholarly tradition. The covenant is referenced where relevant (women's rights at court) without being forced into the center.
- The social categories (horon, nyamakalaw, jon) are not elaborated in this chapter, which is acceptable given the era's focus. The scholarly families existed within the Mande social framework, but the chapter's interest is in their intellectual function rather than their social classification.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The treatment of women's freedom at court as Mande normalcy rather than as the scandal Ibn Battuta considered it. "The Malians were not measuring" is the right formulation -- it locates the judgment in Ibn Battuta, not in the text, without pretending the practices were identical across all Islamic societies.
- The acknowledgment that the Mande and Maliki traditions "shared in some respects and diverged from in others" is precisely right. Islam in the Mande world was not identical to Islam in the Maghreb, and the differences were not deficiencies.

**What needs correction:**
- The passage on Ibn Battuta's complaint about slave girls at court: "He wrote about slave girls who appeared naked at court." The term "slave girls" simplifies a complex social category. In the Mande framework, these were jon -- a category that is not reducible to "slave" in the way European-language readers understand the term. The Kurukan Fuga established specific roles and protections for jon. The chapter should either use the Mande term or avoid the phrase "slave girls," which imports a set of connotations that may distort the reader's understanding of the social reality. (I note that the chapter uses "enslaved men" for the Taghaza miners and "enslaved persons" in the trade-goods list, which suggests awareness of the terminological issue. The inconsistency at court should be resolved.)

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS. The correction above is important for the chapter's internal consistency and for the accurate rendering of Mande social categories.
