# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Panel Review, Round 1

**Text reviewed:** opening.md — The Lueji-Chibinda Foundation
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

I want to begin with what the opening gets right, because it is specific: the lukanu smelling of skin and the river, the red laterite soil of the Kasai watershed, the inner curve worn smooth from generations of wrists. This is sensory work that earns its place. I know where I am geographically — the Kasai, the savanna — and I know what one specific thing feels and smells like. That is a starting point.

But after those opening paragraphs, the sensory ground thins.

From the fourth paragraph onward, the opening becomes increasingly abstract. We hear about brothers who were absent, about temperament, about Lueji adjudicating cases and performing rituals. None of this is felt. What does the sound of a chief's court sound like in this part of the Congo savanna? What does the air smell like in the early morning when she holds the lukanu and someone comes to her for judgment? What does Chibinda Ilunga look like when he arrives — not his political significance but his body, moving through country, coming from the east?

"He had come west following animals. He had no political intention when he crossed into Ruund country. He was hunting." This is good — spare, concrete. But it stays on the surface. I want to feel him tracking. I want the sensation of the savanna from a hunter's body — the grass height, the heat shimmer, the way you move differently when you are following something large. The tradition preserved him as a great hunter. Let me feel what that means.

The moment of their meeting is the most important sensory moment in the opening, and it is stated in the most abstract language: "She saw in him something." I understand the sources are indirect. But the writer's job is to be specific where the sources are general. Where are they standing? Indoors, in a compound, in the open? Is there shade? Is it raining? What time of year? The Kasai watershed has its own seasonal rhythms — wet season, dry season, the rivers rising and falling. Place this meeting in the world.

The closing is correct. Short, weighty, right. But it earned its weight from what came before, and what came before is too thin in places.

**The question I'd push on:** Where exactly is Lueji when she sees Chibinda Ilunga for the first time? Can you put both of them in a physical place — a specific kind of light, a specific time of day or season — before you tell me what she recognized in him?
