# The War of Brothers

## I. The Victor

Atahualpa was bathing when the messengers arrived.

The hot springs at Cajamarca steamed in the afternoon air, the sulfurous water rising from the volcanic rock in clouds that the mountain wind carried across the valley. The Sapa Inca sat in the warm water with his attendants around him, the cumbi cloth that would cover his body folded on the stone rim, the chicha ready in ceramic vessels. He had earned this rest. The war against his brother was over. His generals had taken Qusqu. Huascar was a prisoner. The borla, the crimson fringe of the Sapa Inca, was his by right of victory, and the road that ran from Quito to the Bio-Bio was, for the first time since his father's death, under a single authority again.

The civil war had consumed perhaps five years, from roughly 1527 to 1532, and it had consumed the empire with it. The quipucamayoc at the provincial centers had recorded the demands: more workers for the army, more food from the qollqa, more textiles for the soldiers, more chicha for the marches. Both sides had drawn on the mit'a with an intensity the system had never been designed to sustain, because the system had been designed for construction and maintenance and redistribution, not for a war that mobilized both halves of the empire against each other simultaneously.

Atahualpa's side had prevailed through the quality of his generals. Chalcuchima and Quisquis were commanders who had learned their craft in Huayna Capac's northern campaigns, who knew the road and the terrain and the logistics of moving large forces through mountain country at speed. They drove south from Ecuador through the highlands, defeating Huascar's forces at engagement after engagement, the victories accumulating with a momentum that owed as much to the road Pachacuti had built as to any tactical brilliance. The road that was supposed to connect the provinces to the center was now the highway of civil war, carrying armies in both directions, the tambos stripped of stores to feed the soldiers who passed through them, the chasqui carrying battle orders instead of census reports.

Huascar fought from Qusqu with the loyalty of the heartland, the support of the traditional panaqas, the legitimacy of the capital, and the soldiers the central and southern provinces could mobilize. It was not enough. His generals were not Atahualpa's generals. His forces were weakened by the same epidemic that had weakened Atahualpa's, but the northern army, hardened by the Ecuadorian campaigns, had a cohesion the southern forces could not match. The final engagement occurred near Qusqu itself, and Huascar was captured, and his capture ended the war and left Atahualpa as the sole Sapa Inca, ruling from the north, his brother a prisoner in the capital of their ancestors.

The cost was visible in the knots. The quipucamayoc who survived the war recorded what the war had consumed. The qollqa at the administrative centers along the highland road were depleted. The mit'a labor that should have been maintaining the terraces, repairing the road, filling the storehouses, weaving the textiles, was returning from military service or not returning at all. The population, already reduced by the epidemic of Huayna Capac's last years, had been further reduced by the war, and the reductions were distributed unevenly across the territory, the provinces that had fielded the most soldiers suffering the deepest losses. The reciprocal system, the one Pachacuti had built as a circle of giving and receiving, had been forced to run in one direction, taking, for five years, and the reserves it had accumulated across a century of careful management were gone.

Atahualpa was at the hot springs, and the war was won, and the system was broken, and the messengers were arriving from the coast with reports of strangers.

## II. The Broken Circle

The damage the civil war had done to Tawantinsuyu was structural, not merely military.

The mit'a, the reciprocal labor that had built the empire, functioned because the exchange was perceived as balanced. The ayllu gave its workers, and the state gave food and chicha and tools and the assurance that the state's stores would be available when the ayllu needed them. The civil war had destroyed the balance. Both Huascar and Atahualpa had demanded mit'a labor for military purposes, and the demands had not been accompanied by the reciprocal feasts, the chicha ceremonies, the redistributions that legitimized the taking. Workers were conscripted for war without the ritual framing that distinguished the mit'a from forced labor. The form was maintained, the quipucamayoc recorded the obligations, but the substance, the reciprocity that made the obligations acceptable, was eroded.

The vertical archipelago was disrupted. The llama caravans that connected the ecological zones, carrying coastal products to the highlands and highland products to the coast, had been commandeered for military logistics. The herders who drove the caravans had been conscripted as porters. The llamas themselves had been consumed as food by armies that had exhausted the qollqa. The connections between zones that the ayllu had maintained for centuries, the delicate web of exchange that kept the vertical system functioning, were strained to the point of breaking in the provinces where the fighting had been heaviest.

And the qollqa were empty. The storehouses that had been the visible proof of the reciprocal contract, the stone buildings on the hillsides above the administrative centers whose contents the quipucamayoc had tracked with such precision, were drawn down to levels that could not sustain the system's normal operations, let alone the demands of recovery. When the war ended, the provinces needed the stores more than ever, because the workers who should have been planting were returning from the war or were dead, and the harvest would be short, and the normal mechanism for covering a shortfall, the lateral redistribution from surplus areas, could not function when no area had surplus.

The system Pachacuti had drawn as a circle was now a line, running in one direction, taking from the provinces and giving nothing back, and the quipucamayoc at the provincial centers recorded the imbalance with the same precision they had always applied, cord by cord, knot by knot, the arithmetic of a system consuming its own foundation.

## III. The Strangers

The men from the coast reached Cajamarca in November of 1532.

There were 168 of them, according to the most careful count, led by Francisco Pizarro, a man from Extremadura in Spain who had been in the Americas for nearly twenty years, working his way from the Caribbean islands to the Pacific coast of what the Spanish called Tierra Firme, accumulating experience and debt and the particular ruthlessness that the frontier bred. He had heard reports of a rich kingdom in the mountains, and he had secured a royal charter, and he had recruited men who wanted what he wanted: gold, land, authority, the chance to remake their fortunes in a world that did not know their poverty.

This is how the Spanish saw themselves. From Atahualpa's side, the strangers were something else entirely.

The messengers who reached the hot springs described the physical details: the beards, the metal clothing, the horses (animals the Inca had no name for, because no horse had set foot in the Americas since the Pleistocene extinctions ten thousand years before), the weapons that made noise and killed at a distance. The messengers had watched these strangers move along the road, the road the Inca had built, using the Inca's own infrastructure to approach the Inca's own capital. The strangers numbered fewer than two hundred. Atahualpa's army, camped in the hills around Cajamarca, numbered tens of thousands.

Atahualpa did not flee. He did not mobilize his army for immediate attack. He invited the strangers to come to the plaza at Cajamarca and meet him. The invitation was consistent with the way a Sapa Inca received subordinates: in the plaza, surrounded by his court, displaying the full ceremonial weight of his position, allowing the visitor to approach and to be overwhelmed by the scale of what they were approaching. The Sapa Inca had received delegations from a hundred peoples across the length of the empire. He had a protocol for this. The protocol assumed that the visitor, upon seeing the Sapa Inca in his full regalia, surrounded by thousands of attendants, carried on his litter by eighty lords of the highest rank, wearing the gold and the cumbi cloth and the borla that marked the son of the sun, would understand their position in the relationship.

## IV. The Plaza

The plaza at Cajamarca was surrounded by long stone buildings with multiple doorways, and Pizarro hid his men inside them.

On the afternoon of November 16, 1532, Atahualpa entered the plaza in a procession that the Spanish witnesses later described with a mixture of awe and calculation. The Sapa Inca came on his litter, borne by eighty men of the Inca nobility, wearing garments of the finest cumbi, his ears weighted with the gold spools that marked the Inca elite. Before him came attendants who swept the ground clean of every pebble and blade of grass, because the earth the Sapa Inca passed over must be prepared for his passage. Behind him came the ranks of his court, thousands of men in the regalia of their provinces, unarmed or lightly armed because this was a ceremonial occasion, a reception, not a battle.

The plaza was empty except for a single man. Fray Vicente de Valverde, a Dominican priest, stepped forward with an interpreter and a book. The book was a Bible, or a breviary, the sources disagree. What Valverde said, through the interpreter, was that Atahualpa should acknowledge the authority of the Christian God and the Spanish king and submit to baptism and vassalage. What Atahualpa heard, through the interpretation, was that a stranger was telling the Sapa Inca, the son of the sun, to submit to a god he had never heard of and a king he had never met, on the authority of a book.

Atahualpa took the book. He examined it. He held it to his ear, because in his world, objects that carried messages made sounds: the quipu was read aloud by the quipucamayoc, the pututu conch carried its voice across the mountain, the drum spoke in tones the listener could decode. The book was silent. He set it down, or threw it down, the sources differ. He said something the Spanish interpreted as contempt.

What happened next took less than an hour. Pizarro gave the signal. The Spanish cavalry charged from the buildings into the plaza, and the cannon that had been positioned in the doorways fired into the crowd, and the armed men who had been hidden in the buildings poured into the mass of Atahualpa's unarmed or lightly armed attendants, and the killing began. The Spanish had steel swords and armor and horses and the explosive shock of an ambush prepared against a ceremonial procession. The Inca in the plaza had cumbi cloth and gold earspools and the assumption that this was a reception.

The dead filled the plaza. The number the Spanish reported varies from several hundred to several thousand, and all of the dead were Inca. Not a single Spaniard was killed. Atahualpa was pulled from his litter and captured. His attendants died around him, some still trying to hold the litter upright after their arms had been cut, because to let the Sapa Inca touch the ground was an unbearable failure of their duty.

Atahualpa was a prisoner. The empire was not.

## V. The Ransom and After

From captivity, Atahualpa tried to hold the system together.

He offered a ransom. He would fill a room, the famous ransom room that visitors to Cajamarca can see today, with gold to the height of his raised hand, and fill it twice more with silver. The gold would come from the temples, from the Coricancha's plating, from the offerings at the huacas, from the metal that the Inca valued not for its economic function, which in a non-monetary economy was minimal, but for its sacred significance: gold was the sun's sweat, silver was the moon's tears. Atahualpa ordered the gold collected and brought to Cajamarca, and it came, from across the empire, along the road, the same road, the same chasqui routes, the same tambo stations, the system still functioning even as its center was held captive.

The ransom arrived. The Spanish melted it down. The objects that the Chimu smiths and the Inca goldsmiths had shaped with the skill of centuries, the ceremonial vessels and the figurines and the gold sheeting from the temple walls, became ingots, because the Spanish valued the metal for what it weighed, not for what it meant. The room was filled. The terms were met.

Pizarro had Atahualpa executed anyway. On July 26, 1533, the Sapa Inca was killed in the plaza at Cajamarca. The method was garrote, a Spanish device for strangulation. Atahualpa had accepted baptism before his death, taking the name Francisco, because baptism was the condition for avoiding death by burning, and burning would have meant the destruction of his body, and the destruction of his body would have meant the end of his afterlife, because in the Inca understanding, the dead whose bodies were properly maintained continued to participate in the world of the living, and a burned body could not be maintained. He chose the garrote over the fire. He chose to keep his body. The choice was rational within his cosmology, and the Spanish allowed it within theirs, and the transaction occurred at the intersection of two worlds that did not understand each other.

What followed was the world that followed. The Spanish marched on Qusqu. They found the capital already damaged by the civil war, Huascar's faction having been purged by Atahualpa's generals. They installed Manco Inca, another son of Huayna Capac, as a puppet Sapa Inca, because the system needed a Sapa Inca to function, and a Sapa Inca who served Spanish interests would keep the mit'a flowing and the tribute coming. Manco Inca eventually rebelled, retreating to Vilcabamba in the jungle, where the Inca resistance continued for forty years, the last Sapa Inca, Tupac Amaru, executed in the plaza of Cusco in 1572.

## VI. The Knots

The quipucamayoc did not stop.

In the decades after the conquest, colonial administrators found quipucamayoc still working, still maintaining their records, still able to recite from their cords the census data, the tribute records, the genealogies of the Sapa Incas. The Spanish used them. The early colonial administration, unable to impose a European record-keeping system on a continent it did not understand, relied on the quipucamayoc to provide the population counts, the tribute assessments, the labor records that the colonial state needed to extract the revenue it wanted. The quipucamayoc served new masters with the same precision they had served the old ones, because the skill was the skill and the cords were the cords and the knots held what the knots had always held.

But the tradition was dying. The Spanish did not train new quipucamayoc. The schools that had taught the art, the yachayhuasi of the Inca state, were closed or converted. The young men who would have learned to read the cords learned to read letters instead, or learned nothing, or died. Within two generations, the ability to read the complex quipus was nearly gone. The cords survived. The readers did not. The approximately 900 quipus that survive in museum collections today are the body without the voice, the book that no one living can fully read, the record of an empire that kept its memory in a medium that required human beings to interpret it, and when the human beings were taken, the memory went with them.

Not all of it. Not entirely. In the village of Tupicocha, in the Huarochiri highlands east of Lima, a community maintained knotted-cord records into the modern period, and the anthropologist Frank Salomon documented their practice: the cords brought out at community assemblies, the records read aloud, the obligations they encoded renewed in the reading. In other communities, fragments of the tradition survived in modified forms, the knowledge attenuated but not extinguished, carried in practices that the colonial and the modern world did not fully understand and therefore could not fully suppress.

The quipucamayoc held the empire's memory. They held it through the peak, when the knots recorded the largest numbers the system had ever known. They held it through the epidemic, when the numbers declined in ways the categories could not explain. They held it through the civil war, when the system consumed its own reserves. They held it through the conquest, when the Sapa Inca was captured and killed and the gold was melted and the temples were rebuilt as churches. They held it until they could not hold it anymore, and then the cords went silent, and the silence was not the absence of information. It was the presence of everything the cords had held, waiting in the wool and the knots for readers who have not yet come.

The lake where Manco Capac was said to have emerged still sits at 3,812 meters. The water is still cold and the sky is still that deeper blue, and the sun still strikes the surface with the unfiltered force that comes from having less air between itself and the earth. The road Pachacuti built still crosses the landscape, its stones worn by five centuries of weather and feet. The terraces still step down the mountainsides, their walls holding against the slope as they held when the mit'a laborers set them in place. The descendants of the ayllus still farm the valley of Cusco, and in some communities the ayni still functions, the reciprocal labor exchange still sealed with chicha, the obligation still honored.

The quipucamayoc are gone. The knots are silent. But the wool has not decayed, and the colors, though faded, are still legible, and in the vaults of museums in Lima and Berlin and Washington, the cords lie coiled in climate-controlled cases, holding what they held, waiting. The empire they recorded was built without writing and destroyed by people who could not imagine a world without it. The record survives. The readers will come, or they will not, and the knots will hold their meaning either way, because the thread does not forget. The thread was spun from the wool of animals that still graze the puna above the valley where the golden staff sank into the earth, and the thread was knotted by hands that understood what they were doing, and the knots are still tied, and what they hold, they hold.
