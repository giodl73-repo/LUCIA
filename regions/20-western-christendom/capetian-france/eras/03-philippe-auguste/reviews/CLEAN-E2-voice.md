## Voice Register Check: Sacred-Legal

The expected register: medium declaratives, colons for legal enumeration, the rhythm of a royal charter being read aloud. Zero em-dashes. No textbook passages. No modern commentary.

**Voice random-paragraph test (three paragraphs selected at random):**

**Paragraph 1 (baillis section):**
"The *baillis* were the mechanism of that bureaucracy. Philip had begun appointing them in the 1190s: royal officials, paid by the crown, empowered to collect royal revenues, administer royal justice, and enforce royal claims in the provinces. They bypassed the great lords. Where a baron had previously collected what he could and passed on what he pleased, the bailli arrived with the king's authority, with written instructions from the royal chancery, and with the power to hear appeals from barons' courts to the king's court. The bailli was the word made flesh: the feudal right of the French crown, converted into an administrative fact that the next bailli would find waiting for him when he arrived, and the bailli after that."

VOICE: Unmistakably Sacred-Legal. The colon enumerates. The declarative sentences state and advance. "The bailli was the word made flesh" functions as both legal-institutional analysis and deliberate echo of the Gospel of John, which is exactly the register's technique: sacred and legal in the same sentence.

**Paragraph 2 (Ingeborg section):**
"Philip spent the next twenty years trying to make her disappear from history while she was still living. He imprisoned her in varying degrees of comfort, married another woman (Agnes of Merania, whom the papacy did not recognize as his wife), and argued with a succession of popes that his first marriage had been invalid. Pope Innocent III, who became pope in 1198, refused every argument. He placed France under interdict in 1200: no sacraments, no church bells, no Christian burial for any Frenchman who died while the king of France kept his lawful wife in confinement."

VOICE: Consistent. The colon delivers the interdict's contents as a list of legal deprivations. The parenthetical on Agnes is handled with the register's characteristic compression. "Tried to make her disappear from history while she was still living" is the register's spare irony.

**Paragraph 3 (landing):**
"The story the monks told was Philip's story. The kingdom he left was Philip's kingdom. And the law that held it: the trésor, the baillis, the feudal right enforced into territorial fact, the sacred anointing that made every challenge to the crown an act against divine order: this was Philip's law, which would hold long after the man who built it had been laid beside his ancestors in the stone light of Saint-Denis, among the kings of France, in the ground of the kingdom he had made."

VOICE: Sacred-Legal at full register. The double colon structure enumerates the mechanisms in the same rhythm as a royal mandate. "The stone light of Saint-Denis" is the voice's only lyrical moment, and it is earned: the chapter has established Saint-Denis as the Capetian burial place and institutional memory.

## Register Drift Findings

**Finding 1 — Marc Bloch citation:**
"Marc Bloch, who studied these ceremonies with a scholar's care, concluded that people did recover from scrofula after the royal touch..."

This surfaces a modern scholar by name, briefly stepping outside the Capetian frame. The GATE-2 scorer flagged this. RECOMMENDED FIX: restructure to embed the observation without naming the scholar in the narrative voice.

Revised: "The ceremonies sustained themselves across four centuries of Capetian and Valois and Bourbon kingship, which is evidence enough that something in them worked. Not always, but often enough..."

**Finding 2 — "without precedent in Europe":**
"...becoming, in the last decades of the twelfth century, something without precedent in Europe: a community of scholars..."

"Without precedent" is comparative framing that briefly positions Paris against all of Europe as an evaluating frame. Within the French court's self-understanding this is defensible (they believed exactly this), but the phrase "in Europe" creates a slight Eurocentric centering effect. RECOMMENDED FIX: "...something the Capetian domain had not seen before" or simply remove the comparison and describe directly.
