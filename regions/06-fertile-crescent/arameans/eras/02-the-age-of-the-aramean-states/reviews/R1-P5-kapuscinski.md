# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Panel Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Arameans — Era 2: The Age of the Aramean States

## What Works

The opening puts me somewhere. Dust on sandals. Five days of travel. The dry hill country. The leather strip with terms rolled inside. Before I know what an Aramean state is, I know what the road feels like. This is correct.

The Ghouta paragraph is the strongest sensory passage. Apricot and almond orchards. The Barada spilling from the Anti-Lebanon mountains. Water channels cut from the flow. The brown steppe running east. I can see the transition from green to brown, from irrigated garden to dry steppe. This is the fundamental geography of the Aramean world: the oasis and the road, the water and the dust.

"Water in this country was a toll gate that geography itself collected." I feel this. The reader understands, physically, why Dammasq matters. It is the water that makes the steppe crossable.

The iron-fitted chariots are good. Heavy. Drawn by horses bred on grasslands. The physical presence of the Aramean military is established in weight and metal. The chariot is not an abstraction; it is iron and horse and ground.

## What I Would Push On

The negotiation scene has bodies but no voices. The messenger arrives at the gate, but I do not hear the gate. I do not hear the sounds of Dammasq: the market noise that would have been audible from the walls, the livestock, the hammering of the metalworkers who fitted the chariots with iron. The opening is visually grounded but aurally quiet. A city like Dammasq would have had a sound, and the sound would have told you what kind of city it was: a market city, a working city, a city where things were being made and sold.

The steppe road is mentioned but not traveled. I want the heat. The wind. The way the dry air desiccates the leather of the messenger's sandals and the parchment of his message. The steppe between the Jordan and Dammasq is specific country: basalt hills in the Hauran, then the long flat before the Ghouta. The opening mentions these features but does not put me on the road. I want to walk with the messenger.

The closing paragraph leaves my body entirely. It is all abstraction: "The states were mortal. The tongue was not." These are ideas. Where is the person speaking the tongue? Where is the scribe writing it? Give me the hand holding the reed, the ink on the leather, the sound of the Aramaic consonants in the mouth of a merchant counting his goods. End with a body, not a thesis.

## The Question I'd Push On

**Where is the sound of Dammasq?** A market city has a sound. An oasis city has a smell. The opening shows me the city from outside its walls. Take me inside.
