## Innovation Flags

**Flag 1 — Language-as-Inside-Voice in Source-Asymmetric Conditions:**
The Breton dictionary technique achieves something not previously named in the rubric. In source-asymmetric conditions (no written record from the culture), the culture's own language preserved in a colonial document serves as a primary inside-voice anchor. The dictionary words, rendered in the chapter without authorial interpretation, carry the inside perspective because they ARE inside — the Kalinago language is not the observer's language. The technique: present the word, present what the word names, let the naming do the analytical work. This is related to but distinct from the "chronicler-within-chronicle" technique (v1.5). The language itself is the chronicle. Consider logging.

**Flag 2 — Terminal Condition Through Engine-Substrate Exhaustion:**
The terminal condition in this chapter is the exhaustion of the social substrate that sustained the structural engine, rather than the engine's direct failure. The war canoe (engine) remained functional as a design and as a knowledge system; what failed was the community of builders who could produce it. This is distinct from the five terminal-condition types named in v1.5 (legitimacy gap, self-consumption, categorical failure, reversible feedback loop, ecological constraint). "Substrate exhaustion" — the engine's base conditions erode through attrition faster than they can be replenished — may be a sixth terminal-condition type. The Kalinago are not the only example: the Norse in Greenland (walrus-ivory trade route closes when Arctic exploration finds alternatives), possibly the Harappan (the builders of the great drains die without institutional continuity). Consider logging.
