## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of The Merchants of Ashur (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening achieves something important: it presents the Old Assyrian merchant network as a genuine intellectual and organizational achievement in its own right, not as a stepping stone to the military empire. The karum system is treated with the seriousness it deserves. But the knowledge system at work here, the merchant's epistemic practice, could be rendered more deeply.

**What works:**
- The karum assembly as a self-governing judicial institution with elected judges and recorded precedents is presented as a sophisticated organizational achievement without the word "sophisticated." The description does the work. This is how you honor a knowledge system: you show it operating.
- The letters are treated as primary evidence of a complete intellectual world, not as curiosities from a pre-literate people. (They were, in fact, among the most literate commercial communities in the ancient world.) The opening correctly positions the archive as a knowledge system, not an accident of preservation.
- The refusal to treat this commercial culture as less interesting than the later military culture is itself an act of intellectual respect. Many treatments of Assyria begin with the empire and treat the Old Assyrian period as prologue. This opening treats the merchants as the subject.

**What doesn't:**
- The merchant's knowledge system is described but not experienced. What did it take to run one of these trade operations? The calculation of profit margins across hundreds of miles. The assessment of risk on mountain passes. The evaluation of textile quality. The management of agents at a distance, using only letters carried by donkey. This is a knowledge practice of extraordinary sophistication, and the opening should let the reader feel its intellectual demands, not merely know they existed.
- The donkey caravan crossing the steppe and climbing into Anatolia is mentioned but not inhabited. What did that journey feel like? The terrain, the distances, the dangers. The merchant's knowledge of the route was earned knowledge, body knowledge, passed from father to son and guide to apprentice. The opening gives us the endpoints but not the road.

**The question I'd push on:**
Can the chapter render the merchant's knowledge practice at the level of method? Not "they calculated profits" but HOW they calculated, with what tools, against what risks, and with what precision? The karum archives contain enough detail to reconstruct the actual decision-making of these traders. That reconstruction would elevate this from a story about ancient merchants to a story about a way of knowing the world through commerce.
