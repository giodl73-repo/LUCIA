# PANEL-1 Review — P-5 Ryszard Kapuściński
## The Long Struggle — Qajar Era 2
**Round:** R1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I know this world. Not this palace, not this year, not this particular silence — but the silence itself. I have sat in rooms where power has shifted and the air has not caught up yet. The cold water-pipe is that room.

The opening puts me somewhere. That is the first thing. Tehran in autumn 1891, a palace, a table, a cold pipe, a shah who does not speak. I can feel the quality of that silence — not the silence of peace but the silence of an understanding that has just arrived and has not yet been spoken aloud. The opening has calibrated this exactly.

**What grounds me:**

The coffee-house passage is where the chapter's sensory life is richest: "the social lung of the city — where merchants met to argue prices, where students argued theology, where news traveled faster than any official messenger — sat quiet and empty." The phrase "social lung" is anatomical, physical. A lung that has stopped breathing. I can feel what Tehran sounded like without tobacco smoke and coffee-house argument — which means I can feel what it meant for both to stop.

The smell is there, almost: "The smell of tobacco, which had moved through every gathering in Iran the way incense moves through a mosque, was gone." Good. The absence of smell as presence. This is the technique — the ghost of a sensory world that proves by its disappearance what has happened. The fatwa is not visible; its effect on the atmosphere of the city is.

**What I want more of in the full chapter:**

The shah's body. We are inside his comprehension — his understanding of the situation is rendered clearly — but I do not know what he looks like, how he holds himself in the autumn of 1891, whether he paces or sits still, whether the cold pipe is at his left hand or his right. The chapter will need to put him in a body, not just in a mind.

The geography of the era. The opening mentions Russia and Britain as pressures but does not put me on the road between Tehran and Tabriz, or at the Persian Gulf coast where the British squadron turned back the Iranian army. The era covers sixty years and enormous distances. I want to feel the plateau — the distance between Tehran and Mashhad, the different quality of air and politics in Tabriz (closer to Russia, closer to Europe, speaking Azerbaijani Turkish) versus Isfahan (closer to the shrine cities, more embedded in the clerical networks). These cities are not interchangeable, and the chapter should not treat them as if they are.

**The question I'd push on:**

The opening is focused on 1891 — the endpoint of the era's central drama. The chapter will open back up to 1834 and Mohammad Shah's accession. The material between 1834 and 1891 must be given its own physical texture. Mohammad Shah's Herat campaign (1837–1838): what did the Iranian army look like? What was the Persian Gulf coast in 1838 when the British squadron appeared? What did the European observers who traveled through Qajar Iran in this period record about its physical appearance — the roads, the bazaars, the city walls, the quality of the light in Tehran versus the quality of the light in Mashhad?

Kapuściński's note: I wrote *Shah of Shahs* about a later revolution in this same country. The physical constants remain: the light of the plateau (harsh, clarifying), the smell of the bazaar (copper, saffron, hides, the particular damp of the caravanserai), the sound of Tehran at dawn (the call to prayer from a dozen minarets, slightly out of sync). The chapter should use what travelers and residents recorded. There is enough testimony. Use it.

**Verdict:** The sensory foundation is laid in the opening. The chapter must build a physical Iran across six decades, not just a conceptual one.
