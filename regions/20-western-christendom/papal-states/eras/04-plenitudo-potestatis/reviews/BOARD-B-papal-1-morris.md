## Overall Assessment

The chapter gets the most important thing right: it presents the papacy's authority as an intellectual achievement before a political one, and it anchors that achievement in specific people making specific decisions. The Gratian section is the chapter's strongest historical passage. The Anagni scene is historically accurate and well-executed. I have a small number of corrections and one significant clarification.

---

## Corrections

**1. Canon count at the Fourth Lateran Council**
The chapter states "seventy canons." Modern scholarly editions of the Fourth Lateran Council canon (Alberigo et al., Conciliorum Oecumenicorum Generaliumque Decreta; Garcia y Garcia for the critical edition) number the canons at 71. The discrepancy between 70 and 71 appears because one early manuscript tradition treats what are sometimes counted as canons 70 and 71 as a single text. The chapter should use "seventy-one" or "more than seventy" to avoid implying a precision not supported by consensus.

**Action required:** Change "seventy canons" to "seventy-one canons" or "the council's canons" in the opening section and Section III.

**2. England interdict dates**
The chapter states that England was placed under interdict and "For six years, the churches of England stood silent." The interdict was issued in 1208 and lifted in 1214 — six years is correct. John was personally excommunicated in 1209, also consistent with the chapter. John submitted in 1213 and was formally reconciled in 1213-1214. The chapter states "In 1213, John submitted" — accurate for the initiation of submission, though formal reconciliation took through 1214. No correction needed; the chapter's simplified account is accurate.

**3. Gratian as "probably a Camaldolese"**
The figures.md describes Gratian as "probably a monk, possibly a Camaldolese, working in Bologna." This is the current scholarly consensus — the Camaldolese identification comes from the medieval tradition that Gratian was a monk at the Camaldolese monastery of Saints Felix and Nabor in Bologna. This is uncertain but is the most historically defensible attribution. Acceptable.

## Clarification Request

**The Concordat of Worms analysis**
The chapter's analysis of the Concordat (Section I) is correct as far as it goes, but it slightly overstates how immediately the canonists perceived the papal advantage. In fact, the Concordat was understood at the time as a genuine compromise — Frederick Barbarossa, for example, attempted to interpret it as establishing rough parity well into the 1150s and 1160s. The canonists who systematized the papal advantage came later, working through Alexander III's pontificate. The chapter's phrase "The canonists who read the text immediately saw..." is slightly too confident about the speed of this recognition.

**Suggested revision:** Change "The canonists who read the text immediately saw what the settlement had actually done" to "The canonists who worked through its implications, over the following decades, clarified what the settlement had actually done." This preserves the analytical point while being historically accurate about the time frame.

## Confirmations

The following claims are accurate and require no revision:

- The chapter correctly describes the Investiture Controversy's Canossa incident (1077) and its significance.
- The characterization of Alexander III's reorganization of the Curia in the 1160s-1170s is accurate.
- The description of Innocent III's unanimous election on the first ballot at age 37 (actually closer to 37 or 38, depending on the uncertain birth date of c. 1160/61) is consistent with the sources.
- The characterization of the Fourth Crusade's diversion to Zara and then Constantinople, and Innocent's response, is historically accurate.
- The Anagni incident: September 7, 1303 date is correct; Guillaume de Nogaret and Sciarra Colonna as the principals is correct; the town rising to free Boniface on the following Sunday is correct; Boniface's death in Rome on October 11 or 12, 1303, approximately one month later (the chapter says "thirty-five days later" — the exact date of death is variously given, but "approximately one month" is accurate) is correct.
