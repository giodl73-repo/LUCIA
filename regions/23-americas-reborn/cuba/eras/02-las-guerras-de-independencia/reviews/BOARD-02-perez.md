# BOARD Review — Louis A. Pérez Jr.
**Domain:** Cuban political and social history, US-Cuba relations, independence wars
**Primary Works:** *Cuba: Between Reform and Revolution* (4th ed., 2011); *Cuba in the American Imagination* (2008)
**Chapter:** Cuba Era 2 — Las Guerras de Independencia — La Manigua
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Historical Accuracy Assessment

The structural analysis of the Platt Amendment and the premise non-recognition is historically correct and analytically important. The chapter's three-part explanation (no credit access, short war, strategic interest) is a valid analytical frame, and the claim that the Platt Amendment was "the logical expression of a strategic interest that the United States never concealed" is defensible and well-grounded. American policymakers were explicit about their strategic interest in Cuba from at least 1895 onward.

## Specific Concerns

The chapter says that Tomás Estrada Palma "had spent most of the previous years in the United States and was a naturalized American citizen." This is correct, and it is a significant point that Cuban historians have emphasized (Pérez himself has noted it as symbolic of the republic's compromised origins). No correction needed.

The chapter does not mention that Cuba's Liberation Army was demobilized before the American occupation formally ended, without the pension or land grants that many soldiers had expected. This omission is significant: the demobilized Afro-Cuban soldiers who had fought in the liberation army and returned to find no land, no pay, and a political system that would systematically exclude them from power, are the social consequence of the premise non-recognition that the chapter correctly identifies as the terminal condition. The chapter notes that "Some found land. Many did not" but does not explain why many did not — specifically, that the demobilization was conducted in a way that left Afro-Cuban veterans without the material foundation to participate in the new republic's political economy. This is a gap the chapter should address in the landing section.

## The Question of the Cuban Autonomous Movement

The chapter correctly focuses on the independence tradition rather than the autonomist current, and this is the right editorial choice for a chapter that is about the independence war. However, the chapter would be strengthened by one sentence acknowledging that the independence movement was not the only Cuban political current of the period: there were Cubans who argued for autonomy within Spain's imperial framework right up to 1897, and the independence movement's victory over that current is part of what it means to say the republic was Martí's republic rather than Montoro's. This is a suggestion, not a correction.

## The Diary

The chapter's characterization of Martí's diary is accurate: it is the primary record of his return, it stops on May 18, and the account of Dos Ríos is reconstructed from other sources. The chapter is honest about this. Well-handled.

## Verdict

Historically sound. The main substantive suggestion: add to the landing section something about the demobilized Afro-Cuban soldiers' specific material conditions after the republic was declared, which is the ground-level evidence of the premise non-recognition the chapter correctly identifies at the structural level. The chapter already states that "Some found land. Many did not." Expanding this by two sentences to explain the mechanism (the demobilization policy) would complete the argument.

**Recommended for FINAL with minor expansion in landing section.**
