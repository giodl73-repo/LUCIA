The notes promise to put me in multiple physical environments, and I am holding them to this promise. The road to Canossa section is the chapter's center of gravity, and the notes are right to allocate the most words there.

A specific note on the bells. Medieval life was organized by the liturgical hours — Matins, Lauds, Prime, Terce, Sext, None, Vespers, Compline — each marked by the ringing of the bells in the nearest church tower. A traveler on a road in January 1077 would not know the time by clock; he would know it by the sound of the bells, distant or close, from across the valley or from the tower above the village where he had stopped. The bells were the empire's timepiece and the Church's. When an excommunicate entered a town, the churches were supposed to close — the doors shut, the bells silent. But in practice, compliance was uneven: some priests obeyed, some did not, and the traveler could not always know whether the silence from a church tower was the proper exclusion of the banned king or simply a distance too great for the sound to carry.

This ambiguity — is the silence compliance or indifference? — is the sound of the Investiture Controversy at ground level. The chapter should find this sound.

The castle at Canossa: Matilda of Tuscany was a sophisticated ruler who maintained a real court. The castle would have had a chapel with regular liturgical observance. During the three days of Heinrich's penitence, the chapel bells would have rung at each canonical hour, audible in the outer courtyard where he stood. He would have heard the offices he was excluded from. This is not a small detail: it is the auditory experience of excommunication. The chapter should put this in the text, not in a note.

The road over the Alps in January: the pass Heinrich used was almost certainly the Mont Cenis or one of the other western Alpine crossings. In winter these passes are not impossible but they are severe — the snow compacted on the track, the wind off the high ridges, the particular quality of Alpine silence when the wind drops. A king with a small escort, an infant, a wife, moving as quietly as possible to avoid the German lords who would have been obligated to turn him away — this journey deserves a full paragraph. The body of the king, exposed on a mountain road in January, is the argument made flesh.

*Sensory plan: sound (bells), cold (mountain crossing), smell (castle), touch (hair shirt). All four must be present in the chapter. Don't let sensory variety collapse to sight only.*
