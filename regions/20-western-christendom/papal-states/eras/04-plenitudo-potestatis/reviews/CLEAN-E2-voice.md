## Voice-Random-Paragraph Test

Three paragraphs selected at random. Read each in isolation. Does the voice identify the culture without any context?

**Paragraph A (Section II, Gratian's method):**
"Gratian's method was the distinctio. Consider two canons that seemed to contradict each other. A canon from an early council permitted a slave to marry without his master's consent. A decretal from a later pope appeared to require consent for any marriage to be valid. Were these incompatible? Not if one distinguished between the consent required for validity, the consent of the parties themselves, and the consent required for licitness, the social permission the later decretal addressed in a different context. Both canons agreed on the first. They addressed different situations regarding the second. The apparent conflict dissolved when the question behind each canon was properly identified. They were answering different questions."

**Assessment:** This is unmistakably a juridical culture. The voice is analytical without being academic, procedural without being dry. The question-then-resolution structure (Were these incompatible? Not if...) is the canonist's argumentative rhythm. **PASS.**

**Paragraph B (Section III, excommunication mechanism):**
"The secular consequence followed directly. A king's subjects owed him loyalty because the Church recognized their oaths as binding. A king whose subjects had been freed from their oaths by papal excommunication had no subjects. He had a territory. He had soldiers who served for wages. He did not have a kingdom in the sense that gave him a right to rule."

**Assessment:** Short declarative sentences building a logical argument. The rhythm is that of a legal brief read aloud. The distinction between territory and kingdom, between soldiers-for-wages and legitimate subjects, is a canonical distinction being applied to political reality. The voice could not be a steppe culture or a maritime culture or a griot culture. It is specifically the voice of a culture that thinks in legal categories. **PASS.**

**Paragraph C (Section V, Anagni scene):**
"Boniface was at his summer palace in his birthplace. He was in his mid-seventies. He knew they were coming; there had been warnings; and he did not flee. He dressed in his full pontifical vestments and waited in the great hall. The soldiers came in. Sciarra Colonna, who led the Italian contingent, reportedly wanted to kill him on the spot. Nogaret, whose legal training told him that killing a pope would produce consequences no one could manage, restrained this. Boniface reportedly offered his neck. He told them to proceed with what they had come to do."

**Assessment:** The voice holds even in the chapter's most dramatic scene. Short sentences. The word "reportedly" used twice, which is the voice's honest-uncertainty mechanism — legally precise about what is documented versus what is chronicle tradition. Nogaret's restraint is explained through legal logic ("consequences no one could manage"), not through moral scruple. This is the legal-public register sustaining itself under pressure. **PASS.**

## Register Drift Inventory

Full chapter scan for any paragraph that sounds like it was written for a Western audience rather than from inside the culture.

**Potential drift — Closing paragraph (second paragraph):**
"Philip IV of France, who sent soldiers to Anagni, also engaged the services of theologians and canon lawyers who argued, in published treatises, that the pope's authority was limited to spiritual matters and that secular rulers were not subordinate to Rome in temporal affairs. The arguments were not novel; bishops and lay scholars had made versions of them before. But in 1302 and 1303, these arguments had political backing, and political backing is what makes an argument consequential in a dispute about jurisdiction. The papacy had won the jurisdictional disputes of the twelfth and thirteenth centuries partly because the opponents of papal claims had not mounted a sustained intellectual case alongside their political resistance. Philip IV's lawyers mounted both simultaneously."

**Assessment:** This paragraph contains the phrase "political backing is what makes an argument consequential in a dispute about jurisdiction." This is a political-science observation that could appear in a modern textbook. It is the one place in the chapter where the author's analytical distance is most visible. The sentence is not wrong and does not violate any editorial rule — but it reads slightly more modern than the rest of the chapter.

**Recommendation:** Minor revision. Replace "political backing is what makes an argument consequential in a dispute about jurisdiction" with language that maintains the legal-public register while making the same point from inside the medieval framework. Suggested: "The papacy had answered such arguments before by asserting that the authority of a general council could not override the authority of Peter's successor. In 1302, Philip IV's lawyers added an army to the argument."

This revision keeps the same analytical point but frames it in terms the curial world would use (the argument about conciliar vs. papal authority was a live medieval debate), and the phrase "added an army to the argument" lands in the legal-public register while making the power-dynamics point.
