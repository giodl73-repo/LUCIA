# Panel Review — R2-P3 (Chinua Achebe)
## La Conquête — Notes Review (PANEL-2)

**Reviewer:** P-3 Chinua Achebe
**Stage:** PANEL-2 (Notes)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The production bible has addressed my central concern: there is an English figure now, Æthelric, and he has been correctly placed — as a witness before Norman commissioners, rendered through the gap between his English testimony and the Latin document it produces. This is a sophisticated structural choice. The gap itself is the story: the Domesday Book contains English knowledge organized by Norman categories. Neither side disappears; the gap between them is where the chapter lives.

### What I Want the Chapter to Confirm

**The Harrying of the North must have English dead.** The notes acknowledge this: "the English dead are present as people, not as statistics." I want to hold the chapter to this. Orderic Vitalis — himself Anglo-Norman, born to an English mother — describes what he saw and heard: "I was careful not to be present in that winter. But I have often heard the survivors relate it." This is the right voice for the chapter's Harrying section. Not William's calculation, not Orderic's count of 100,000 — but "the survivors relate it." The survivors are the English voice that the chapter needs.

**The Bayeux Tapestry passage should honor the English embroiderers.** The notes mention the Tapestry as a knowledge artifact: "Norman story, English hands." This is important and should not be a footnote. The women who embroidered the Tapestry at Canterbury — we do not know their names, but we know they were English, working at the workshop associated with a Norman bishop, producing a document that told the story of their own country's conquest. This is not a minor irony. It is the Conquest's whole dynamic in miniature: the tools of the conquered, employed by the conquerors, producing the conquerors' version of the story. The chapter should honor the hands, not only the narrative.

**"Witness, not victim."** This is the right framing for Æthelric, and I want to extend it: the chapter should resist making the English figures passive recipients of Norman governance. Even in Domesday, the English testified. They answered questions. Their knowledge of the land — the customs of the manor, the names of the fields, the ancient boundaries — is what makes the survey possible. The Norman commissioners could not have conducted the Domesday survey without English informants. This is not a small thing. The knowledge of the landscape that the survey encodes is English knowledge, even if the form is Norman.

### One Risk to Name

The chapter is centered on Norman institutional achievement: the castle, the survey, the Church restructuring. There is a risk that this achievement is implicitly presented as value-neutral — as if the systematic replacement of the English ruling class were simply an administrative fact rather than a massive dispossession. The chapter should not moralize this; that would violate the No Verdicts discipline. But it should make the dispossession present. The Domesday Book shows us who held land in 1086. It also shows us who no longer held it. The gap between the two lists is the Conquest's human cost, without any authorial comment required.

---

**Verdict:** Ready to write, with close attention to the English voice in Section 3 and the Tapestry passage. The structural plan correctly keeps the English present as people — the chapter must execute this.
