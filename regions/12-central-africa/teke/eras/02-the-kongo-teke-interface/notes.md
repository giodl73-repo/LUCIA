# Notes — The Kongo-Teke Interface
## Production Bible

**Era:** Teke / Era 2 — c. 1500–1800 CE
**Date:** 2026-04-16
**Stage:** Post-PANEL-1 / Pre-WRITE

---

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** The broker's information asymmetry — knowing the price on both ends of the route while controlling access to the middle — as the Teke's primary mechanism of power and survival.

**Mechanism:** The broker's position depends on three interlocking structures:
1. **Geographic**: The Bateke Plateau physically sits between forest interior and coastal market. Goods cannot move from one to the other without crossing the plateau, and crossing the plateau requires Makoko's implicit or explicit permission.
2. **Informational**: The Teke broker knows the prices on both ends because they have been managing both relationships for generations. The forest trader does not know what the coastal market will pay. The Kongo agent does not know what the forest trader needs. The broker controls this gap.
3. **Spiritual-political**: The Makoko's onkani authority — jurisdiction over the land's spiritual resources, not merely its material ones — gives anyone trading on the plateau a sense that access to the plateau itself requires the land's permission, which the Makoko holds. Traders who bypass the Teke market risk, at minimum, bad fortune on the plateau roads; at maximum, active interference by Teke chiefs.

**Terminal condition**: The Atlantic trade's expansion (c. 1700–1800) massively enlarges the trade volume through the Pool but also reorders what the coastal end values. When enslaved people become the primary commodity the coastal end demands, the Teke position becomes deeply entangled in the slave trade as transporters and brokers of captives moving from the forest interior to the Pool. The broker's calculation does not change — it is still about the gap between what one end offers and the other end wants — but the commodity has changed in ways that the calculation alone cannot resolve. The chapter's terminal condition: the broker's logic, applied without modification to the slave trade, extends Teke commercial power but at a cost the chapter will not moralize about, and the era closes with the Teke firmly positioned but operating within an Atlantic system that is beginning to exceed the capacity of any interior broker to fully control.

## Sources

**Primary:**
- Stanley, H.M. *The Congo and the Founding of its Free State* (1885) — primarily useful for Era 4, but his descriptions of Pool market geography in the 1870s-80s inform the physical reality of the commercial junction.
- Brazza expedition reports — French colonial records of the Pool region; useful for era boundary material.

**Academic (Tier 2):**
- Vansina, Jan. *The Tio Kingdom of the Middle Congo 1880–1892* (1973, Oxford). Core source for Teke political culture, though focused on the colonial period; political theology of the onkani and Makoko authority is documented here and can be read backward into the earlier period.
- Vansina, Jan. *Paths in the Rainforests: Toward a History of Political Tradition in Equatorial Africa* (1990, Wisconsin). Contextual framework for the entire region's political traditions; the Teke broker position is discussed in the context of Central African commercial networks.
- Martin, Phyllis M. *The External Trade of the Loango Coast 1576–1870* (1972, Oxford). Loango coast trade system, which is the northern parallel to the Kongo-Pool axis; prices and goods lists are useful calibration material.
- Harms, Robert. *River of Wealth, River of Sorrow: The Central Zaire Basin in the Era of the Slave and Ivory Trade, 1500–1891* (1981, Yale). The Pool market system described in detail; the transformation of the trade from copper and iron to ivory and enslaved people is documented across the relevant period.

**Source situation:** The pre-colonial Teke record is thin in primary sources — no court chronicles, no Arabic travelers' accounts, no royal correspondence comparable to the Kongo or Mali records. Vansina is the primary scholarly authority, and his work is based partly on twentieth-century ethnography read backward against regional comparison. The chapter must acknowledge this situation through the techniques confirmed in v1.5 — the source gap becomes part of the story, not an apology external to it. The broker who does not record his transactions because recording them would give his counter-party information is a figure the chapter can use: Teke commercial culture's lack of a written record is structurally consistent with the information-asymmetry logic that underlies the broker position.

## Key Figures

**The broker (composite, unnamed):** The chapter's primary consciousness. Not a named historical individual — the Teke record does not preserve individual broker names from this period — but a composite drawn from Vansina's ethnographic reconstruction of the Teke commercial class. The broker is worldly, calculating, emotionally contained, and competent. He has no illusions about what the trade requires. He is the chapter's epistemological center.

**The Makoko (title, not name):** The Teke paramount chief. The Makoko's authority over the plateau's spiritual and territorial access is the political structure within which the brokers operate. The chapter will include one significant scene where the Makoko's authority is brought to bear — probably in the context of a Kongo attempt to impose tribute or redirect the trade routes. The Makoko's response will be rendered through the Teke understanding of what the Makoko's power consists of (onkani, not military force), not through a Western political analysis of how states exercise sovereignty.

**Kongo provincial governor's agents (unnamed, institutional):** The Kongo end of the trade relationship. They appear as the chapter's pressure from the south — the powerful state that the Teke must navigate without submitting to. At various periods, Kongo attempted to extend its political authority northward to the plateau; the Teke maintained their commercial independence not by defeating Kongo armies (which they could not have done) but by making the plateau's commercial value depend on Teke cooperation.

**Forest traders (composite, representative):** The chapter's counterpart to the opening trader — the eastern and northern end of the supply chain. Their role is primarily structural: they illustrate what the Teke broker knows about the forest interior that the coastal buyers do not.

**v1.4 archaeological figures note:** The Teke's primary figure does not have a name. Per the v1.1 Honest Limits amendment, this should be acknowledged in figures.md as the reason for the composite construction, not concealed.

## Narrative Arc / Section Plan

**Section 1 — The Plateau Market (integrated from opening, ~800 words)**
The opening broker scene. Establishes the engine, the voice, the world. The copper transaction is the chapter's seed crystal — everything that follows is an elaboration of this moment across three centuries.

**Section 2 — The Makoko's Ground (~900 words)**
The Bateke Plateau as physical and spiritual geography. The Makoko's onkani authority rendered at method level — not "the Makoko had spiritual authority over the plateau" but what that authority looks like when exercised. A specific scene: a Kongo agent attempts to establish a trading station on the plateau without Makoko permission, and the consequences. The scene shows the mechanism of exclusion (Vansina documents incidents of Teke route denial and market exclusion of non-compliant traders).

This section also establishes the plateau's physical reality — Kapuściński's pressure point. The high savanna, the drop-off into forest, the seasonal road patterns, the smoke of the trading compound fires visible from the plateau's edge.

**Section 3 — The Three-Way Calculation (~900 words)**
The Kongo relationship in depth. The Manikongo's provincial governors want interior goods — copper, ivory, forest products — and the Teke brokers supply them at Teke prices. The chapter shows a specific negotiation: a Kongo agent pushing to establish a direct relationship with the forest suppliers, and the Teke broker's response, which is not military confrontation but the quiet withdrawal of the logistical infrastructure that made the forest trade legible to outsiders. Roads that the broker knew and the Kongo agent did not. Porters who would not carry for anyone but their Teke employer. Prices that moved without explanation.

The three-way calculation (Kongo south, Pool southwest, Luba east) is dramatized here as competing simultaneous pressures, not just listed as destinations.

**Section 4 — The Pool (~700 words)**
The Malebo Pool as the chapter's most vivid set piece — Kapuściński's other pressure point. The Pool market's physical reality: the vast river widening, the fishing canoes, the market noise, the goods coming off the forest canoes and going onto the plateau roads. The broker at the Pool is not in the familiar territory of his compound; he is at the junction of two worlds, and the chapter should feel the difference.

The Pool is also where the Atlantic trade's effects first become visible on the interior system. Portuguese cloth arriving at the Pool in exchange for goods moving toward the coast. The broker registers this as a change in what the coastal end wants — more cloth, different metals, eventually guns — and adjusts accordingly. This section marks the era's midpoint and the first sign of what the Atlantic will eventually require.

**Section 5 — The New Commodity (~900 words)**
c. 1650–1750: the Atlantic slave trade reaches the Pool market in earnest. The broker's calculation extends to human beings moving east-to-west: forest interior captives, war captives from the conflicts that the interior trade's competition generates, people bound with the same vine as the copper ingots. The chapter renders this through the broker's calculus — the same transactional voice, the same habit of pricing, applied to a different kind of cargo. No verdict. The reader will feel what the chapter does not say.

The section also shows the Teke position strengthened by the Atlantic demand: they are now essential intermediaries in a much larger system, moving goods and people to a Pool that has become the most important commercial junction in Central Africa. Teke commercial power in this period is near its peak.

**Section 6 — Landing: What the Plateau Holds (~300 words)**
The chapter's close returns to the plateau, to the Makoko's seat at Mbe, to the broker's view of the road running in both directions. The Atlantic system is now enormous and the Teke are inside it, but the plateau is still the plateau. The road still runs where it has always run. The broker's skill is still what it always was. The chapter closes on the Makoko's authority as the one constant — not wealth, not military power, but the land's own permission, which no Atlantic company can purchase and no Kongo governor can command.

Landing mechanism: a quiet scene at the Makoko's compound, the Makoko sitting in the same receiving position his predecessors occupied for two centuries, the same onkani in the ground beneath him, the Atlantic trade loud somewhere below the plateau's edge and quiet up here. The broker's calculation still running. The pool not always the price.

## Panel Pressure Points Anticipated

**Tuchman:** The chapter needs a turn — a moment when the calculation fails or nearly fails. The mid-chapter scene of a Kongo agent trying to bypass the plateau market is that moment. It must be dramatized, not described.

**Ibn Khaldun:** The mechanism of exclusion must be shown, not assumed. How does the broker make the road "run through his market and not around it"? The chapter's section 3 addresses this through the withdrawal of logistical infrastructure. Must be specific.

**Achebe:** The Atlantic section must sustain the Teke voice. The temptation to let a European/Atlantic perspective intrude — to frame the slave trade as "what the Portuguese demanded" — must be resisted. It is what the Pool wanted. The broker's world is the frame.

**Davis:** The onkani rendered at method level, not description level. The Makoko's authority shown in operation, not catalogued.

**Kapuściński:** The Pool scene must put the reader in the physical space. The plateau's landscape must be felt. The opening's sensory register must not be abandoned in the analytical middle sections.

## Open Questions

1. Vansina's *Tio Kingdom* covers 1880-1892 — can the political theology documented there be reliably read back into the 1500-1800 period? The chapter should acknowledge this as a limitation (v1.5 source gap as cultural honesty technique) while using the material where it is well-supported by regional comparison.

2. The Luba buyer in the opening — is the east-facing trade (Teke to Luba) well-documented for this period? Harms covers the Pool basin; regional comparison with the Luba trade networks is needed. The chapter can treat this as a less-documented axis and use it accordingly.

3. The specific mechanism of Kongo-Teke political pressure in the 1500-1600s — Vansina documents Kongo expansion pressure but the specific interactions require careful handling. The chapter will keep the political pressure at the structural level (state vs. commercial broker position) rather than claiming specific named confrontations that the sources do not support.

## Voice Confirmation

**Register:** Transactional, calculating, world-weary. The broker's voice. Short declarative sentences for prices, decisions, and material facts. Longer clause-chains for the calculation process itself — the running total beneath everything, the habit of sight. No sentimentality. No surprise at sophistication. No mourning.

**Voice source:** Confirmed from within the Teke commercial tradition as Vansina documents it — a culture that valued commercial competence, neutral brokerage, and the appearance of having no strong preferences about who was buying or selling. The Teke broker voice is the voice of a person who has strong preferences but shows none of them, because showing preferences is a form of giving information away.

**Em-dash budget:** 0 target. The voice does not use em-dashes; it uses periods. A sentence ends when the thought ends. The pause is the period, not the interruption.
