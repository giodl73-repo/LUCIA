## Factual Verification

**Carrhae (53 BCE) — VERIFIED with one note:**
The chapter's account of the battle aligns with the primary sources (Plutarch, Cassius Dio). The camel-arrow resupply system is accurately described; this is confirmed in Plutarch's *Life of Crassus* (Plut. Crass. 24). The force size of ten thousand Parthian cavalry against seven legions is consistent with the scholarly consensus. The Suren's use of combined cataphract-and-horse-archer tactics is accurate.

One note: The chapter states Crassus lost "twenty thousand Romans." Plutarch gives approximately ten thousand dead at the battle itself, with ten thousand captured; the remainder retreated with the officers who survived Crassus. The chapter's figure combines dead-and-captured, which is a reasonable convention but should be internally consistent with "the rest were retreating north through the night" — if twenty thousand are dead, the math requires clarification. Recommend: "half the Roman force was dead or taken."

**The Suren's execution — VERIFIED:**
Orodes II ordering the execution of Surena (the Suren general) after Carrhae is confirmed in Plutarch (Plut. Crass. 33). The reason given in the sources is envy of his fame; the chapter's structural interpretation (the noble-house system's internal logic requiring it) is a valid scholarly reading and is more explanatory than the ancient sources' simpler account of royal envy.

**The "Surena" name question — VERIFIED and nuanced:**
The chapter's treatment of this is accurate. "Surena" in Plutarch is now understood by most scholars as the family/clan name (Suren), not a personal name. The Parthian naming convention privileging clan identification is historically documented. The chapter handles this correctly.

**Orodes II's background — VERIFIED:**
The fratricidal succession (Orodes II and Mithridates III) is historically confirmed and is an important detail the chapter includes appropriately.

**Standards recovery (20 BCE) — VERIFIED:**
Phraates IV's return of the standards is well-documented. The chapter's interpretation — that Phraates understood Augustus needed the ceremony more than Parthia needed the standards — is a reasonable scholarly reading consistent with the broader Arsacid diplomatic pattern. The chapter does not overstate Parthia's agency; it presents a bilateral negotiation where each side received what it required.

**Antony's campaign (36 BCE) — VERIFIED:**
The Armenian approach and the disaster of the siege train's loss is accurately represented. The number of dead (the chapter says "another thirty thousand") is within the range given in ancient sources (Plutarch gives twenty-two thousand in the retreat, not counting those lost with the siege train). The "thirty thousand" figure combines categories; recommend specifying "more than twenty thousand in the retreat alone."

**Trajan's campaigns (113-117 CE) — VERIFIED:**
Trajan's seizure of Ctesiphon in 116 CE and the subsequent withdrawal are accurately described. The quotation about being "too old to go further" is preserved in Cassius Dio (Dio 68.29) and is accurately flagged by the chapter as a reported statement ("said, reportedly"). Hadrian's immediate restoration of the Euphrates border is confirmed.

**The noble-house system — VERIFIED with elaboration note:**
The chapter correctly identifies the Suren, Karen, Mihran, and Gev as the major houses. The hereditary right of the Suren to crown kings is confirmed in Strabo and other sources. The chapter's description of the system as a coalition of hereditary obligations is consistent with current scholarship (see Dąbrowa, Olbrycht).

One elaboration to consider: the chapter describes the great houses as having "their own armies, their own revenues, their own courts." This is accurate but the degree of autonomy varied significantly across the empire's extent and across the era. The Mesopotamian satrapies were more tightly administered than the eastern plateau territories. The chapter might benefit from one phrase acknowledging this range rather than describing all great-house territories as uniformly autonomous. This is a minor nuance and not a factual error.

**Isidore of Charax — VERIFIED:**
Isidore's *Stathmoi Parthikoi* is a real document, dated approximately to the late 1st century BCE to early 1st century CE (the dating is uncertain and the chapter wisely does not pin it). The road-book describes exactly what the chapter says it describes. The ten-percent customs rate is not specified in Isidore's surviving text but is consistent with what is known from comparable ancient trade contexts and is a reasonable scholarly inference. The Board recommends noting this in the notes.md as an inferred figure rather than a directly documented one.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

**The Euripides scene — RECOMMENDED CAUTION:**
The Euripides scene (Crassus's head used in a theatrical performance of the *Bacchae*) is preserved in Plutarch (Plut. Crass. 33) and is the most contested detail in the Carrhae narrative. Several scholars consider it a later embellishment. The chapter handles this with "the story went," which is the correct epistemic qualification. The Board recommends adding a brief note in notes.md (or in a future source annotation) acknowledging the scholarly debate: "Preserved in Plutarch; doubted by Sherwin-White (1984) and others as too dramatically perfect. The chapter uses it with appropriate epistemic qualification."

**The Parthian shot as political theology — WELL HANDLED:**
The chapter's claim that the Parthian shot was "a statement about terrain" rather than a simple military maneuver is a defensible scholarly interpretation and is consistent with recent work on Parthian military doctrine (Overtoom, 2020; Olbrycht, 1998). The chapter does not overclaim — it presents this as the Arsacid doctrine as the Arsacids understood it, not as the chapter's own innovation.

**Zoroastrian-Hellenistic coexistence — ACCURATE:**
The chapter's treatment of the dual cultural registers (the coin, the theater, the fire temple) is historically accurate and reflects current scholarship on Arsacid cultural policy. The chapter avoids calling this "hybrid" (a word the notes.md explicitly flags) and instead presents it as deliberate maintenance of parallel registers. This is the more accurate characterization.

---

## Recommendation

The chapter is historically sound with three minor adjustments needed:

1. **Carrhae casualty figure:** Specify "dead or taken" or adjust to "half the Roman force."
2. **Antony casualty figure:** Specify "more than twenty thousand in the retreat" rather than a general "thirty thousand."
3. **Ten-percent customs rate:** Flag as inferred rather than directly documented.

No cultural authenticity concerns. The chapter treats Parthian history with respect for the scholarly record and from the Parthian perspective.

**Status: BOARD APPROVED with three minor factual adjustments.**
