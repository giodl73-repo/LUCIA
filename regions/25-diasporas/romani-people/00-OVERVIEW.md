# Roma — "O Lungo Drom": The Long Road

We come from India. This is not metaphor or myth — it is genetic and linguistic fact, established by both the structure of the Romani language (which belongs to the Indo-Aryan branch and is most closely related to Hindi and Punjabi) and by genetic studies that trace Romani ancestry to populations in northwestern India, likely the Punjab and Rajasthan regions, who left approximately 1,000 years ago. Why they left is not known. Some accounts suggest military service under Mahmud of Ghazni; others suggest low-caste origin and flight from persecution; others simply accept that the record is lost. What is known is that by the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, Romani people were present across Persia, Byzantium, and southeastern Europe, and that by the fifteenth century they had spread across all of Europe, carrying their language, their music, their crafts, and a social organization built around the extended family (familia) and the community council (kris).

The long road — o lungo drom — is the phrase that Romani people sometimes use for their collective experience of perpetual movement, perpetual exclusion, and perpetual survival. For a thousand years, the Romani people have been denied the right to settle permanently, expelled from country after country, enslaved in Romania for five centuries, murdered by the millions in the Nazi genocide (the Porajmos — "the devouring"), and subjected to forced sterilization, forced assimilation programs, and systematic discrimination across Europe into the present day. The Romani response has not been political power (the Romani people have never had a state) but cultural persistence: the maintenance of the Romani language, the music that has shaped European folk traditions from flamenco to Hungarian czárdás to Romanian manele, and a social and ethical code organized around the extended family and the traveling community's needs.

Key nodes:
- **Andalusia (Spain)** — the Gitano culture; flamenco as the synthesis of Romani, Moorish, and Spanish musical traditions; the deepest roots of popular Spanish music
- **Romania / Wallachia** — where Romani people were enslaved for five centuries (until 1855–1856); the largest Romani population in Europe today
- **Auschwitz-Birkenau (Block BIIe — the "Gypsy Camp")** — 23,000 Romani people were held here; almost all were murdered on the night of August 2–3, 1944
- **Budapest and Eastern Europe** — the contemporary center of Romani political organizing and cultural revival

## What Defined Them

**The road as home.** The Romani experience of movement — the wagon, the camp, the seasonal circuit of fairs and markets and agricultural labor — has produced a culture that is organized around mobility rather than settlement. The extended family traveling together, making camp, and moving on is not a failure to settle; it is a complete way of life with its own architecture, its own social rhythms, and its own values. The culture of the traveling people prioritizes flexibility, family loyalty, and the craft and musical skills that make you welcome wherever you stop, over the land ownership and civic participation that settled cultures value. For a thousand years, this culture has been described by outsiders as shiftlessness or criminality; from inside, it is the rational adaptation of a people who have been denied the right to stay.

**Music as the universal passport.** Romani musicians have shaped the folk music traditions of virtually every country in Europe. In Spain, the Gitano (Romani) contribution to flamenco is foundational: the deep song (cante jondo) with its Middle Eastern-influenced ornaments, its quarter-tone inflections, its emotional rawness — these are Gitano elements that entered flamenco through the great Gitano singing families. In Hungary, the Romani violinists and cymbalomists defined the Hungarian popular music tradition; Franz Liszt believed (incorrectly) that the Hungarian national music was fundamentally Romani. In Romania, the lăutari (Romani musicians) are the custodians of the Ottoman-Balkan musical tradition that defines Romanian folk and popular music. In the twentieth century, Romani musicians were central to jazz in Central Europe. Music has been both the Romani people's economic survival strategy and their most visible contribution to European civilization.

**The kris — community justice.** Romani communities have maintained their own system of dispute resolution and community governance — the kris (community tribunal) — in parallel with (and often in opposition to) state legal systems. The kris adjudicates disputes between Romani families, enforces community norms, and maintains the social cohesion of the traveling community without recourse to external authority. This is not merely a parallel legal system; it is an expression of the Romani refusal to be governed entirely by states that have historically been hostile. The kris is the institutional form of Romani sovereignty in the absence of a state.

**Porajmos and the denied genocide.** The Nazi genocide of the Romani people — the Porajmos, "the devouring" — killed between 500,000 and 1.5 million people (estimates vary widely due to the inadequacy of the record). The Romani genocide was carried out in concentration camps, in mobile killing units (Einsatzgruppen), and through systematic starvation and forced labor. Unlike the Jewish Holocaust, the Romani genocide was for decades denied the status of genocide by the German state and largely ignored by the wider world. The Romani survivors received no reparations. The official German acknowledgment of the Porajmos as genocide came only in 1982 (from President Herzog's predecessor Bundespräsident Karl Carstens — recognition was slow and contested). The memorial at the Tiergarten in Berlin was not inaugurated until 2012. The denial of the Romani genocide is not primarily a historical dispute; it is an expression of the same anti-Romani racism that made the genocide possible.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Departure:** In northwestern India, around 1000 CE, a group of people — what caste or castes, what precise origin, what specific reason — begins moving west. The Romani language will preserve the evidence of their origin: the words for family, for horse, for the road, for food — all Sanskrit derivatives. The memory of India is in the language even when it is not in the story.

**2 — The First Appearance:** 1417: a group of "Saracens" or "Egyptians" or "Bohemians" — the medieval European names were various and none were accurate — appears at the gates of a German city. They have a letter, claimed to be from the Holy Roman Emperor, identifying them as Christian pilgrims doing penance. Many cities receive them with some hospitality. The hospitality will not last.

**3 — The Romanian Slavery:** In the principalities of Wallachia and Moldavia (modern Romania), Romani people have been enslaved since the fourteenth century. They are bought and sold, whipped and branded, separated from their families. Slavery is not abolished until 1855–1856 — the last slave emancipation in Europe. The Romanian state's reckoning with this history is still incomplete.

**4 — Auschwitz-Birkenau, August 2–3, 1944:** In the part of Auschwitz-Birkenau designated as the Zigeunerlager (Gypsy Family Camp), 2,897 Romani men, women, and children — the last of the 23,000 who had been imprisoned there — are murdered in the gas chambers of Crematorium II and III. The Romani prisoners had resisted an earlier attempt to kill them in May 1944. The August killing is carried out without warning. The date — August 2 — is now the Romani Genocide Remembrance Day.

**5 — Flamenco:** In the juerga (private Romani gathering) in a tavern in Jerez de la Frontera, a Gitano cantaor (singer) performs a siguiriya — the deepest, oldest, most serious flamenco form. The siguiriya is described by its practitioners as the song of grief, the song that expresses what cannot otherwise be said. The room is small. The audience knows what they are hearing. Outside, the tourists are watching a flamenco show. They are not watching this.

**6 — The Living Present:** In Slovakia, Roma are systematically denied access to adequate housing, healthcare, and education. In France, Romani communities are periodically bulldozed from their camps. In the Czech Republic, there are documented cases of forced sterilization of Romani women as recently as 2012. The European Court of Human Rights has issued dozens of rulings against European states for anti-Romani discrimination. The rulings are not consistently enforced. The road continues.
