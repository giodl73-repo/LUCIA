**Voice-random-paragraph test (v1.4):**

Three paragraphs selected at random by opening the chapter to unmarked positions:

*Test paragraph 1 (mid-chapter, lead singer section):*
> "The lead singer's work is technical before it is expressive. She knows the genealogy in its entirety, hundreds of names in the order that is also an argument about who descends from whom and therefore who belongs to what. She knows which sections require which posture, which sections require which rhythm, which zemís are activated by which sequences of sound and motion. She teaches not by explanation but by performance: she sings it, the students mirror it, she refines it through the mirroring, the students absorb the refinement, and after enough repetitions the phrase lives in the body and is no longer hers but theirs."

Reading in isolation: the cumulative parallel construction ("She knows... She knows... She knows...") is recognizably the areíto-ceremonial register. The analytical content (knowledge transmission through embodied repetition) arrives in the same rhythm as the description. A reader encountering this paragraph in isolation would recognize it as a Caribbean oral-tradition culture with a ceremonial transmission system. PASSES voice-random-paragraph test.

*Test paragraph 2 (political architecture section):*
> "When a cacicazgo wanted to register a formal claim, over territory, over a marriage arrangement, over the resolution of a dispute about fishing grounds, the mechanism was not a court and not a written contract. It was the areíto. The lineage in question would commission a composition that embedded the claim in the genealogical record: your ancestor and my ancestor stood together at this moment, and from that standing together, the following understanding follows."

Reading in isolation: the contrast structure ("not a court and not a written contract... It was the areíto") carries the chapter's analytical argument in the chapter's own voice. The "your ancestor and my ancestor" construction moves into the culture's own first-person formulation. PASSES.

*Test paragraph 3 (Columbus section):*
> "Columbus took several zemís back to Spain. This is worth holding still for a moment. He picked up the carved wooden figures, the cotton figures, the stone faces, and he took them. To him they were curiosities, artifacts to show as evidence of where he had been. To the community he took them from, they were the ancestors, the presences that governed the local forces, the objects that the areíto was partly designed to maintain in proper relationship with the living. Taking them was not theft of decoration. Taking them was the removal of the nodes through which the living maintained contact with everything that governed the island's wellbeing."

Reading in isolation: "This is worth holding still for a moment" — this is the chapter's own voice stepping into instruction mode briefly. This is the one place in the chapter where the voice is not entirely the areíto-ceremonial register; it is closer to the author's direct address. The rest of the paragraph recovers immediately ("He picked up... To him they were... To the community he took them from"). Flag: "This is worth holding still for a moment" risks breaking the voice. However, it is a deliberate structural pause at the chapter's most critical evidence moment. Judgment call: it functions as the chapter's most important pivot and the momentary register shift is justified by its weight. PASSES with note.
