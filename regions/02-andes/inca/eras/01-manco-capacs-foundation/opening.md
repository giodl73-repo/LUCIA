# Manco Cápac's Foundation

They came out of the lake.

The water of Titicaca sits at 3,812 meters above the sea, higher than any lake of its size on earth, and at that altitude the light is different. Thinner. The sky is not blue in the way lowland skies are blue; it is a deeper color, closer to indigo, and the sun that strikes the water strikes harder because there is less air between the sun and the surface. The lake stretches to the horizon in every direction, and the cold of it reaches into the bones of anyone who wades in past the totora reeds that fringe its shores. The Uru people who lived on floating islands of bundled totora knew this cold. The Aymara herders who brought their llama flocks to drink at the lake's edge in the dry season knew it. The wind that came off the water in the afternoon carried the smell of algae and wet stone and the particular mineral edge of water that has been cold for a thousand years.

From this water, the first Inca emerged.

Manco Cápac and Mama Ocllo, brother and sister, husband and wife, children of Inti the sun, rose from the waters of Titicaca carrying a golden staff. Inti had given them the staff and the instruction: walk north. Where the staff sinks into the earth on a single thrust, build. They walked. The altiplano stretched before them, flat and brown and dry in the spaces between the wet seasons, the ichu grass bending under a wind that never fully stopped at that altitude. They passed through Aymara country, through communities of herders and farmers who had been living at this altitude for millennia, whose lungs had adapted across generations to pull oxygen from air that held forty percent less of it than the air at sea level. They walked through a landscape that was already ancient, already populated, already organized into ayllus, the kinship groups that held land collectively and understood their relationship to the earth beneath them as a relationship to the ancestors buried in it.

The staff sank at Qusqu.

The valley was narrow, the hills close, the two rivers, the Huatanay and the Tullumayo, converging through the valley floor between slopes that rose steeply to the east and west. At 3,400 meters it was lower than the lake but still high enough that maize, the crop that would become the marker of Inca agricultural ambition, grew only in the warmer microclimates of the valley bottom. Above the maize zone, potatoes. Above the potatoes, quinoa and kaniwa. Above those, the puna grasslands where the llamas and alpacas grazed, their wool the raw material of the textiles that would become Tawantinsuyu's currency of obligation. The valley was not empty. Other peoples lived here, other ayllus, with their own ancestors in the earth and their own claims on the water and the terraced slopes.

This is the origin story the Inca told about themselves, and it was told after the empire existed, to explain why it had to exist. The chroniclers who recorded it, Spanish priests and administrators writing in the decades after the conquest, heard it from quipucamayoc and Inca nobles who understood that the story's power lay not in whether it happened but in what it authorized. A people who came from the sun, through the water, carrying a mandate to build where the earth itself received them, did not need another justification. The golden staff was the argument. The earth that accepted it was the verdict.

What the archaeology shows is humbler and, in the way of real things, more interesting. The Inca were a small ethnic group in the Cusco valley, one among several, for most of the period between roughly 1200 and 1400 of the common era. They competed with their neighbors, the Chanka to the west, the Qolla and Lupaka to the south, the peoples of the Urubamba valley to the north. They raided. They were raided. They made alliances through marriage and broke them through war. Their leaders bore the title Sapa Inca, "the only Inca," and the title carried the weight of divine descent, but the territory the title governed was a valley, not a continent. The early Sapa Incas, the ones the later genealogies would list, existed in the space between myth and memory where the quipucamayoc placed them: Sinchi Roca, Lloque Yupanqui, Mayta Cápac, names carried on the knotted strings that recorded what the empire needed remembered.

The quipu held them. Not ink on paper, not chisel on stone, but thread on thread, knot against knot, the wool of the alpaca dyed in colors that carried meaning the quipucamayoc could read and that we, eight centuries later, mostly cannot. The information was not less for being stored in fiber rather than on a page. It was differently stored, and the difference meant that the keeper of the record was inseparable from the record itself. A manuscript can survive without its scribe. A quipu without its quipucamayoc is a bundle of colored string. The knowledge lived in the relationship between the object and the person trained to read it, and when the Spanish killed the quipucamayoc or let them die or simply stopped asking what the knots meant, the records did not burn. They went silent, which is a different kind of loss and in some ways a deeper one.

But in the valley of Qusqu, in the centuries before the great expansion, the quipucamayoc were alive and the knots were speaking, and what they recorded was a small polity learning the practices that would, in the hands of a ruler not yet born, become the machinery of the largest state the Americas had ever seen.
