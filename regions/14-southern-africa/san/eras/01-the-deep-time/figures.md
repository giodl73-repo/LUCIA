# Figures — The Deep Time

## /Twi!kxao, n/om-kxao

/Twi!kxao carried n/um. This is not a title that is given. It is a capacity that develops over years of training within the trance dance, years of learning to let the n/um rise without being destroyed by it, years of the community's singers and dancers holding you while you learn to cross the threshold into |kia and come back. Not everyone who dances becomes a n/om-kxao. Among the Ju/'hoansi, roughly half of adult men and a third of adult women carry n/um to the degree required for healing, but the strongest healers, the ones who can enter the deepest trance and pull sickness from the dying, are fewer.

/Twi!kxao was one of these. He was known in his camp for the depth of his trance. He could enter |kia more quickly than most healers, and he could stay longer, which frightened some of the singers, because a healer who goes too deep may not return. The community's role in managing his trance was as much a skill as the trance itself. His wife was one of the lead singers, and she could read the state of his trance from the rhythm of his breathing and the angle of his body, and she adjusted the song to hold him or to bring him back. The healing was never his alone. It belonged to the circle.

/Twi!kxao is a composite figure, drawn from multiple accounts of Ju/'hoan healers recorded by ethnographers and by the healers themselves. His experience is real. The details of his trance are drawn from first-person accounts. But he is not a single documented individual, and this chapter acknowledges that honestly: the trance dance has been witnessed thousands of times, and /Twi!kxao's dance is assembled from these witnesses to render the practice faithfully.


## /Kabbo, ||Xam narrator

/Kabbo's name means "Dream." He was a ||Xam man, arrested by the Cape colonial government for stock theft and imprisoned in Cape Town, where he became one of the primary informants for Wilhelm Bleek and Lucy Lloyd's great archive of ||Xam language and narrative. He dictated for months. He told the stories of /Kaggen and the eland, of the rain and the rain animal, of the moon and the hare, of the wind and the people it carried. He described the land he came from, the Karoo, with a precision that makes the transcriptions ache: the places where the springbok gathered, the water sources, the rocks where specific things had happened in specific seasons.

He wanted to go home. He said so repeatedly. He said he sat waiting for the moon to turn back so that he could return to his place. The colonial system that had brought him to Cape Town had also destroyed most of what he was waiting to return to. The ||Xam communities of the northern Cape had been decimated by two centuries of commando raids, land seizure, and forced labor. /Kabbo's stories were not antiquarian recollections. They were the living knowledge of a community that was, at the moment he spoke them, being destroyed.

What /Kabbo gave to the archive is irreplaceable. His narratives are the most extensive record of ||Xam cosmology, ecological knowledge, and social life. They are also the record of a man who knew that his world was ending and who chose to speak it into permanence. He left the Bleek household in 1873. What happened to him after that is not fully documented. He did not return to the Karoo that the colonial system had taken from his people. The notebooks that hold his voice are kept at the University of Cape Town.


## /Kaggen, the Mantis

/Kaggen is not a human being. /Kaggen is the Mantis, the creator-trickster of San cosmology, the being who made the eland from a shoe, who stole fire, who shaped the world through a combination of brilliance, stupidity, love, and accident. /Kaggen is sacred. /Kaggen is also ridiculous. The San tell his stories with laughter as often as with reverence, because /Kaggen is the kind of creator who gets his own plans wrong and then fixes them by making new mistakes that accidentally produce the world as it is.

In the ||Xam narratives, /Kaggen is a person with a family: his wife the Dassie (rock hyrax), his adopted daughter the Porcupine, his son-in-law /Kwammang-a. He argues with them. He lies to them. He steals from them. He grieves when things he has made are destroyed. His grief when the eland is killed is the foundational act of the San spiritual world: from the eland's blood comes the first herd, and from /Kaggen's grief comes the sacredness of the hunt.

/Kaggen does not command. He does not rule. He does not judge. He creates by tinkering, and his creations are imperfect, and the imperfections are what make the world livable. This is a theology without a throne. It is consistent with a people who insult the meat and cool the heart of anyone who claims to be more than their share. /Kaggen is the creator they deserve: brilliant, flawed, beloved, and incapable of keeping a secret from his own family.


## !Nai, tracker

!Nai is a composite figure drawn from multiple accounts of Ju/'hoan and /Gwi trackers documented by Louis Liebenberg, Richard Lee, and others. The tracking knowledge he demonstrates is real and specific: the identification of species, sex, age, gait, injury, and time-since-passage from marks in sand that untrained observers cannot detect.

What makes !Nai's knowledge a knowledge system rather than a skill is its structure. He does not simply recognize patterns. He reasons from evidence to hypothesis, tests the hypothesis against new evidence, revises, and follows the logic to its conclusion. Liebenberg has argued that this cognitive process is structurally identical to scientific hypothesis-testing, and that tracking may represent the oldest form of such reasoning in the human record. Whether or not this specific claim holds, the cognitive complexity of what !Nai does is undeniable to anyone who has watched a tracker work: the tracker is thinking, continuously, about what the evidence means and what it predicts, and the thinking is as rigorous as any empirical inquiry.

!Nai's knowledge was not his alone. It was learned from his father and uncles, refined through decades of practice, and was being transmitted to his nephew /Ui through the same method: walking together, watching, learning to see what the sand says. The knowledge persists because it is shared. It would die if it were hoarded.
