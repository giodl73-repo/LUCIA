# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński

**Chapter:** Papal States — Plenitudo Potestatis
**Stage:** PANEL-1
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I want to be in the room. This is my only demand.

The opening gets me into the room — "in the great hall of the Lateran, Lotario de Segni counted what he had made" — and then, almost immediately, the room disappears. The six hundred bishops and eight hundred abbots become a list of categories (Norse converts, Iberian borderlands, Levantine settlers) and then the room becomes the canons, and the canons become the argument, and by the time I reach the submission of John of England and Philip of France, I am reading about power in the abstract rather than feeling power in a place.

The legal-public voice is correct — I accept this, and I understand that it produces a different kind of immersion than sensory saturation. The Lateran is not the Haile Selassie court. The weight of a papal bull is different from the weight of incense. But weight IS still weight. And the opening does not yet give me the physical weight of this moment.

The Lateran Basilica in November 1215 was a specific, documented place. The delegates had been arriving for weeks. The hall would have been crowded past comfort — a thousand or more clerics, many of them representing the full spectrum of Latin Christendom, pressed into a space built for liturgy, not for council. The Latin being spoken was not all the same Latin: a Sicilian bishop's Latin sounded different from a German abbot's, different again from the Latin of the English delegates who had traveled for weeks to be there. The smell of a thousand bodies in November, the candles required to light the proceedings, the documents being passed from hand to hand — none of this is in the opening.

I am not asking for a sensory overload. I am asking for one detail. One thing that pins me to the room before the argument begins. "In the ninth year of his pontificate, in the great hall of the Lateran" — and then one sentence about what it looked like. What it sounded like when the hall fell silent before Innocent spoke.

The Perugia death scene at the end is precise and good. July. On his way to negotiate peace between Genoa and Pisa. The purposefulness of the journey, the mundane geography of the dying, the institutional continuation. This is Kapuściński's kind of ending: power does not pause to grieve its holders. The road from Rome to Perugia in July is hot. The text does not say this, but the month alone implies it, and the reader's body supplies the rest.

**The question I'd push on:** Where is Innocent's body in this opening? We have his count, his argument, his recognition of what he could and could not control. But we do not have his posture at the lectern, his voice in the hall, the specific gesture with which he delivered the sermon from Luke. Power in a body is more terrifying than power as an argument. Find the moment where the plenitudo potestatis is embodied — not in the canons but in the man who spoke them into the assembly.

**Verdict:** The voice is correct. The ending is right. The opening needs one physical anchor — the hall, the bodies, the silence before the sermon — to pin the argument to a place before the argument carries us away from it. This is a small addition that will make the whole opening more powerful.
