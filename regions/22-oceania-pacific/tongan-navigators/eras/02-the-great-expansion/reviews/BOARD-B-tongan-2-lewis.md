## B-tongan-2 — David Lewis — Board Review of The Great Expansion

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Firsthand documentation of traditional Pacific navigation techniques, including swell-reading, star navigation, and the moving-island reference frame.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The description of swell-reading (feeling interference patterns through the hull) is accurate. I documented this practice with navigators in the Carolines and across Polynesia. The technique of lying in the hull to feel deep swells is correctly described.
- The star compass description is accurate in its essentials. The training sequence (swells, then stars, then birds, then clouds, then water) is a reasonable ordering, though in practice these skills were taught in overlapping fashion rather than in strict sequence.
- The bird species mentioned (golden plover, fairy tern, booby) and their behavioral patterns as navigational indicators are correct.
- The cloud-reading description (cumulus buildup over high islands, green reflection over lagoons) is accurate.
- The etak attribution is handled honestly. The concept is best documented from the Caroline Islands, and the chapter correctly notes this while arguing for its broader presence in Polynesian navigation. This is a fair treatment.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The opening scene of Kautai reading the swells is the most authentic passage in the chapter. The physical sensation of reading the ocean through the hull is exactly what I experienced sailing with navigators. The chapter gets the FEEL right.
- The night sailing passage is strong. The density of stars visible from the open Pacific, the reflection on the water, the navigator tracking a specific star's rise — this is how it works.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The knowledge system is treated as exactly that: a system. Not a collection of folk techniques but an integrated practice. This is the correct treatment.
- The apprenticeship is shown as long and demanding. The reference to "years" of training is accurate (in some traditions, the training lasted from childhood to middle age).
- "The songs were the charts. The body was the instrument. The ocean was the world." This is correct in every particular.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter says navigators could "detect these patterns lying in the hull with his eyes closed." In my experience, the eyes-closed practice was specific to certain training exercises, not standard operating procedure during a voyage. Navigators read swells through the hull while also scanning the horizon, the sky, and the water. The chapter should not imply that eyes-closed swell-reading was the normal mode.
- The chapter does not mention the use of phosphorescence as a navigational indicator. On dark nights, the wake of the canoe and the disturbance of the water surface produce phosphorescent trails that an experienced navigator can read for information about current direction and speed. This is a known technique and would strengthen the night sailing passage.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (2 minor corrections above)
