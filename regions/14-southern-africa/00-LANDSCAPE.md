# Southern Africa

The San have been in southern Africa for at least 65,000 years. That number is not a curiosity — it is the central fact of the region's human story. The people who painted the rock faces of the Drakensberg and the Cederberg, who developed the tracking and foraging knowledge that is among the most sophisticated ecological intelligence ever recorded, who shaped the languages with the click consonants that have since migrated into Khoisan, Bantu, and neighboring tongues — these are the original authorities on this land. Everything that came after, including the Bantu-speaking farmers who began arriving from the north over two thousand years ago, including Great Zimbabwe's stone towers and Zulu's impis and the Dutch East India Company's fort at the Cape, happened on a landscape that the San had been reading for longer than any other continuous human tradition on Earth.

What makes southern Africa a region is the interplay between its ecological zones — the winter-rainfall Mediterranean corner of the Cape, the summer-rainfall highveld grasslands, the subtropical lowveld, the Kalahari basin, the coastal strip — and the peoples who organized their lives across those zones. The Khoikhoi herded cattle at the Cape long before European contact. The Zimbabwe plateau supported stone-walled agricultural states whose gold trade reached the Indian Ocean. The Zulu, Xhosa, Sotho, Tswana, and Shona states of the nineteenth century were the culmination of long processes of state-building and migration — not a static "traditional" world but a world in dynamic motion, interrupted and reshaped by the catastrophic arrival of settler colonialism.

**Key features:**
- **Drakensberg Escarpment:** the great mountain wall separating the highveld
  from the coastal lowlands; San rock art sites concentrate here
- **Limpopo River:** northern boundary of the highveld; the Great Zimbabwe
  state's southern trade frontier
- **Zimbabwe plateau:** 1,000–1,500 m elevation; good rainfall; gold-bearing
  geology; the location of Great Zimbabwe and its successors
- **Sofala coast:** the Indian Ocean port through which Zimbabwe plateau gold
  reached Kilwa and the wider trade world
- **Zambezi Valley:** the river highway between the interior and the coast;
  Mutapa territory; Portuguese penetration from the sixteenth century
- **Kalahari Basin:** the semi-arid interior; the San world; waterholes
  as the organizational nodes of a mobile economy
- **Cape of Good Hope:** where the Atlantic and Indian Oceans meet; Khoikhoi
  cattle country; the Dutch East India Company's 1652 fort begins the
  settler colonial story

## How They Connect

Great Zimbabwe and the Indian Ocean trade are a single story told across a thousand kilometers. The stone enclosures at Great Zimbabwe — the elliptical building, the hill complex, the valley ruins — were not mysterious to the people who built them. They were the palaces and cattle kraals of a gold-trading state whose rulers controlled access to the gold-bearing reefs of the plateau and to the trade routes running east to the port of Sofala. Shona-speaking farmers built Great Zimbabwe; Shona-speaking miners and traders maintained the gold supply; Swahili merchants at Sofala bought the gold and moved it north to Kilwa and into the Indian Ocean network. Chinese porcelain found in the ruins of Great Zimbabwe got there because someone carried it inland from a ship that had sailed from a Chinese port. The plateau was not isolated — it was a node.

The Zulu state under Shaka KaSenzangakhona is one of the most studied and most distorted episodes in southern African history. Shaka's military reorganization — the amabutho age-regiment system, the short stabbing spear used with a large cowhide shield at close range, the encircling "bull horn" formation — was a genuine military innovation that transformed the political landscape of southeastern Africa in the 1810s and 1820s. The Mfecane ("crushing" in Nguni) or Difaqane ("scattering" in Sotho) — the period of warfare, displacement, and state formation that followed — created new states (Moshoeshoe's Basotho, Mzilikazi's Ndebele, Zwangendaba's Ngoni) as it destroyed old ones. The scale of the upheaval has been exaggerated in colonial historiography, which used the Mfecane to claim that the interior was depopulated before settler arrival; the reality is that the region was densely inhabited and that the wars, while real and devastating, were also a creative political moment.

The San's relationship with Bantu-speaking farmers and Khoikhoi herders over two thousand years was not simply one of displacement. There were centuries of intermarriage, exchange of knowledge, shared territory, and mutual incorporation. San tracking knowledge was sought by Khoikhoi herders and Bantu farmers alike. Tswana chiefs employed San hunters. San women married into Khoikhoi and Bantu communities. The click consonants that appear in Zulu, Xhosa, and other Nguni languages are direct evidence of intimate linguistic contact over centuries. The Cape Colony's violence against the San — the commandos that killed or enslaved San families throughout the eighteenth century — was the end of a long period of coexistence, not simply the encounter of two unrelated worlds.
