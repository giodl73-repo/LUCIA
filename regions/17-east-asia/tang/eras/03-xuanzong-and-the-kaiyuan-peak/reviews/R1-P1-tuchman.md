## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of The High Tang (Opening)

**Overall:** This opening does what the best historical writing does — it makes you care about a person before you care about a period. Du Fu walking into Chang'an after failing his examination is a scene I would keep reading.

**What works:**
- The failed examination as the entry point is a masterstroke. The reader meets the Tang not at its most powerful but through a young man's defeat, and the defeat makes the city's splendor land harder. The contrast between personal failure and civilizational confidence is doing the work of an arc before the arc has even begun.
- The West Market passage is the opening's strongest section. The Sogdian cooks, the Persian glass, the Silla ginseng, the Uyghur horses — this is not a list of trade goods, it is a functioning economy rendered as a scene. Each merchant carries a world with them.
- The closing lines earn their weight. "The last years of something" is not a cheap trick — it is the reader's knowledge weaponized as dramatic irony. The pull is genuine.

**What doesn't:**
- The paragraph beginning "There were Nestorian Christians" risks catalog mode. It moves from religion to religion in a way that, while vivid, threatens to become a guided tour. The sentence about the Daqin monastery and the Zoroastrian temple saves it with specificity, but the paragraph walks a line.
- Yang Guifei appears only as a dancer. The full chapter will need to give her interiority, but even in the opening, a single detail beyond the Rainbow Skirt dance would signal that she is a person, not a spectacle.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening promises two arcs — Du Fu's personal arc (failure, witness, poet of loss) and the empire's arc (peak, rebellion, aftermath). The full chapter needs to braid these without making Du Fu merely a vehicle for the empire's story. How will you keep his human stakes alive once the chapter widens to court politics, An Lushan, and the rebellion itself?
