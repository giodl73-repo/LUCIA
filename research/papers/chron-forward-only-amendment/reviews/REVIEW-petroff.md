## Substantive Engagement

The paper transposes a specific doctrine from educational measurement — the fairness principle expressed in AERA/APA/NCME Standards — onto an AI-assisted creative production setting. As the person being asked to adjudicate whether the transposition is sound, I want to engage with it carefully, because the transposition *does* carry weight where the authors claim it does, but it also breaks in places the authors have not yet addressed.

The authors' use of Standard 5.16 and equating logic is correct on the surface: when a test form is revised, the test-taker who took the earlier form should be evaluated against that earlier form. Forward-only amendment is the direct analog — a chapter scored under v1.0 keeps its v1.0 score even after v1.7 lands. Good. But the Standards' equating framework rests on a specific technical condition that the paper acknowledges but does not resolve: *the construct being measured must be stable across test forms*. The items can change; the underlying construct (say, 8th-grade algebra proficiency) cannot. In LUCIA, the construct itself is evolving — Progression, as a dimension, meant something narrower at v1.0 than at v1.7, because the v1.7 anchors name techniques v1.0 did not. This is not a surface-form revision; it is a construct revision. Equating mathematics does not actually apply here, and I think the paper understates how much weight that concession bears.

What the paper does instead is inherit the fairness *principle* while acknowledging the equating *math* does not hold (§2.1 is explicit on this). That is a defensible move, but it means the test-taker fairness transposition is normative rather than technical. The authors should be clearer that they are borrowing the Standards' ethical argument, not its measurement apparatus. Under an evolving construct, "the standard at the time of testing" is a moving target in a way 8th-grade algebra is not, and the chapter-as-test-taker analogy starts to strain when the very definition of what is being tested shifts mid-corpus. A chapter scored under v1.0 Progression and a chapter scored under v1.7 Progression are not taking different versions of the same test; they are taking what is arguably a different test entirely. The fairness claim survives — neither chapter should be held against the other's standard — but the comparability claim that equating usually underwrites is gone, and the paper's Measurement B (forward-overlay, 99.6% preservation) should not be read as restoring comparability. It restores *textual continuity*, which is not the same thing.

This is not a reason to reject the paper. It is a reason to sharpen §2.1 and §6.2 so that readers from my field understand exactly what the paper is borrowing and what it is not. The risk otherwise is that the paper gets read as "forward-only is equating-sanctioned," which is stronger than what the paper actually argues.

## Major Issues

1. **The equating-vs-fairness distinction should be load-bearing in §2.1, not a parenthetical.** The paper acknowledges the construct-stability violation but does not reckon with how much of the Standards' authority rests on that stability. A two-paragraph treatment: "Here is what the Standards provide (fairness principle); here is what they do not provide in an evolving-construct setting (equating math); here is what forward-only therefore inherits and does not inherit." Without this, the transposition will read as overclaimed to any educational-measurement reader.
2. **Construct drift vs. rubric drift are conflated.** The reversal classifier operates on rubric text. Construct drift can occur even under high text preservation — if the anchors for Progression 9 now require techniques v1.0's anchors did not, the construct has drifted even though the dimension name has not. The paper's Measurement A does not capture this, and §7.1's borderline-case discussion does not either.
3. **The pilot's positive sign has a measurement-theory reading the paper does not engage.** In educational measurement, if the re-scored exam produces uniformly higher scores under a new rubric, that is evidence of *rubric leniency shift*, not of anchor consolidation. The paper's alternative reading (the rubric now names what the chapter did) is plausible but not distinguished from the leniency reading by the data shown. An independent-rater replication would be decisive; the same-author pilot is not.

## Minor Issues

1. [NEED-C] should cite Standard 5.16 specifically, and probably also Chapter 4 (Fairness in Testing) of the 2014 Standards, where the rationale is developed.
2. §3.4 phrases the fairness principle as "the chapter is not penalized for failing to meet criteria the rubric did not yet contain." This is correct but should acknowledge the Standards' converse claim — that the chapter is also not *rewarded* for meeting criteria the rubric did not yet contain. The positive-delta finding is exactly this converse, and should be named as such.
3. The phrase "test-takers are the chapters themselves" is striking but anthropomorphizes in a way that will irritate my field. "Artifacts scored against the rubric" reads cleaner.

## Quality-Improvement Framing

The transposition is defensible but presented with more confidence than it has earned. The paper should narrow what it claims to inherit from educational measurement — the ethical principle of not holding test-takers against later standards, full stop — and be explicit that the comparability guarantees equating provides do not transfer to an evolving-construct setting. Once that boundary is sharpened, the paper reads cleanly from my field; without it, the Standards-based framing will be read as overreach.
