# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel-1 Review: Opening — Aimsir na Manach

This culture owns this story.

That is not a small thing to say, and I do not say it lightly. The fili's calculation — why Áed is praised first, what éraic means, what the community cannot afford to leave unrepaired — is rendered entirely from inside the logic that makes it rational. There is no moment where the author steps back to explain this practice to a reader who might find it strange. The honor-price is simply the price. The feast is simply the legal procedure it is. The reader must enter the world on its own terms or stand outside — and the prose makes entering easier than standing out.

The treatment of the monastic arrival is what I will watch most carefully in the chapter. The opening handles it well: Tírechán is doing something different from Ferdomnach, but the difference is presented as a difference in technology, not a difference in sophistication. "A second technology of memory, not to replace the fili's trained voice but to run alongside it." This is the correct frame. Christianity arriving in Ireland is not a story of light replacing darkness, nor of barbarian custom yielding to Roman order. It is a story of a highly sophisticated oral culture deciding what to take from the new system and what to retain.

The danger in the chapter will be the temptation to make the monks the main event — to tell the story of Patrick and Columcille and Brendan as a Christian mission narrative, which is the narrative those figures have been given by their own biographers and by a millennium of church history. The chapter must resist this. Patrick is a figure in an Irish story, not an Irish figure in a Christian story. His biographers were Irish monks working within an Irish tradition of praise-poetry and genealogy. That is the inside perspective.

**The question I'd push on:** Who speaks for the people who did not convert easily, who held the old ways longer, who negotiated the synthesis on their own terms rather than the Church's? The brehons, the filid, the craftspeople who worked in enamel and interlace — they did not simply become monks. They remained. The chapter must give them full presence, not just the monasteries.
