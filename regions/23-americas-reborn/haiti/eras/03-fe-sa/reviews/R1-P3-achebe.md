# R1 — Panel Review: Chinua Achebe
## Haiti, Era 3: Fè Sa — The Revolution (opening.md)

**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The voice is right. This is the sentence I look for first, and I find it here. The lwa are not explained to a skeptic. They are not called "beliefs" or "traditions" or "what the enslaved people practiced." They answer. They come. This is the most important thing the chapter does, and it does it without announcement, which is how it should be done.

"This was not theater. This was not a motivational meeting. What was sworn at Bois Caïman was sworn in the language the ancestors understood, in front of witnesses who were not only human." I sat with these sentences. The author has understood that the problem with most treatments of Vodou is that they explain it to someone who doesn't believe in it. They translate it into secular political terms so that Western readers can process it. This opening refuses that translation. The ceremony is real in the ceremony's own terms. The secular terms (organizational structure, network, asabiyyah) arrive later, in the third section, and they arrive as a second lens, not as the primary one. This is exactly the right order.

The Achebe Test question: could a thoughtful Haitian read this and feel their story was told with dignity, from inside? My answer is yes, with one flag.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening describes what happened at Bois Caïman through a lens that is respectful and internally consistent. But the historical record around Bois Caïman is itself contested — not the fact of the uprising, but the nature of the ceremony, and particularly whether the ceremony that has been described in these pages is the ceremony as it was, or as French observers later reconstructed it (with embellishments that made it seem more savage, more scandalous). The chapter notes "whether it was a Vodou ceremony is debated" in the OVERVIEW but does not carry that uncertainty into the opening.

This is not a failure — an opening at 800 words cannot carry every scholarly dispute. But the NOTES.md and the full chapter should address this directly: what do Haitian historians and Vodou practitioners say about Bois Caïman? The ceremony should be rendered from inside Haitian memory, not from French colonial accounts of it. The pig, the blood, the dancing — these are in the colonial record, and they were used by French sources to discredit the uprising. The chapter should be aware of this and handle it with care.

**What should not be changed:** The ending. "The ancestors had been called. They came." This is the inside voice delivering the inside verdict. Not a metaphor. Not poetry as ornament. Truth as the culture holds it.

**Line that will stay with me:** "He was also something else, something the French would later call a *houngan*, a Vodou priest, though the categories they used for what happened at Bois Caïman were always their categories, not his." This sentence does in one breath what lesser writers take a paragraph to do: it names the category, attributes it to the wrong party, and opens a space for the right framing without filling it in. Excellent.
