# A Colônia — Açúcar, Ouro, e Escravos

---

Somewhere in the interior of Pernambuco, in the Serra da Barriga — the hill with the name of a belly, as though the land itself had swallowed something — there is a place the Portuguese could not hold. They tried, in 1645. They tried again in 1677. They sent militia columns and Dutch soldiers and armed bandeiras into the forest, and the forest gave back silence, and the people they were looking for were already gone, already watching from a ridge the Portuguese could not see, and the fight, when it came, came from the wrong direction.

The place was called Palmares. The name came from the palm trees. The people who lived there called it Angolan, sometimes — they came from Angola, from the Congo, from Dahomey, from the slave ships that docked at Recife with their holds full and their manifests signed — but the place itself was theirs now. Forty thousand people, some said. Thirty thousand. The Portuguese counted badly when they were afraid. What they knew was that for nearly a century, a state had existed in the interior of their colony that they could not reach, could not subdue, could not explain in terms their world allowed. Free people, in a colony built on the premise that certain people could not be free.

Zumbi was not born in Africa. He was born in Palmares, in 1655 or thereabouts, into the only world he would ever know — a world his people had made from nothing, from what they carried inside themselves when everything else was taken. He would have learned to walk on the same red soil his grandparents had reached by running. He would have heard, from the time he could hear, the rhythm that held the community together: the songs that came from Bantu ritual, reshaped in the forest, carrying names and invocations the slavers on the coast could not have recognized. The axé — the sacred force, the power that moves through everything — had traveled the Atlantic in the bodies of the people, not in any object the cargo manifests recorded. It could not be confiscated. It could not be inventoried. It arrived in Palmares intact.

Call: Who brought the orixás across the water?

Response: Those who had no hands free to carry them.

Call: Where is the àṣà now?

Response: Here. In the drum. In the feet. In the child who was born on this side and does not know the other.

This is how knowledge moved in Palmares. Not in written documents — the Portuguese controlled paper, ink, the official archive of the colony. Not in any object that could be seized. The knowledge that governed Palmares, that told its people who they were and where they came from and what they owed the living and the dead, moved in the bodies of the people themselves. In the batucada that sounded at dusk when the work was done. In the capoeira that looked, to a Portuguese observer, like dancing — which was the point.

The engenho — the sugar mill — was the machinery of the colony. On the coast of Pernambuco and Bahia, the engenhos ran day and night through the harvest season. The cane was cut, bundled, dragged to the mill, fed into the rollers while the rollers were still turning because stopping them cost money and time, and the people who fed the cane into the rollers could not afford to lose a hand to the mechanism because a hand cost the senhor de engenho an investment he preferred to protect. The great houses stood at a distance from the smell. The senzala — the slave quarters — stood between the house and the mill, close enough for the senhor's convenience, far enough for his comfort.

From the senzala, people ran.

## Açúcar — A Máquina

Her name, before the ship, was something that would not be written in any document that survived. The Portuguese gave her a saint's name at the port. She came from the Mina coast — what the Portuguese called the Costa da Mina, the stretch of shoreline from which Fon and Ewe and Yoruba peoples were taken in enormous numbers throughout the seventeenth century. She carried in her body the specific knowledge of Xangô's ceremonies: the rhythms of the batá, the colors he accepted, the offerings that opened his attention. This knowledge was not metaphorical. It was procedural. It had been passed to her through her grandmother's hands during initiation, through the particular way her grandmother's voice changed when the orixá was present. The knowledge was in her muscles, in her ears, in the part of her that knew things her mind could not yet name.

The engenho at Pernambuco ran on water and on her.

The harvest season was called safra — from Arabic, from the Arab traders who had carried sugar to Portugal before Portugal carried it to Brazil. During safra, the mill ran without stopping, because the cane began to ferment once cut and had to be processed before it was lost. The smell of the mill was sweet in a way that was not pleasant: the dense, fermenting sweetness of mass-produced sugar, underneath which was the smell of burning bagasse, the dried cane pressed dry of its juice and fed back into the furnace as fuel. The furnaces ran hot. The boiling house was where the juice was clarified and concentrated, moving from cauldron to cauldron in stages of increasing heat, and the people who worked the boiling house worked in heat that took people not born to it. She was born to nothing that was here. She adapted. This is not a virtue the colony granted her; it is a fact about her body's capacity that the colony could not prevent.

The rollers were the most dangerous part. Three vertical cylinders in tight contact, turned by water or animal power, through which the cane stalks were fed to press out the juice. The workers who fed the rollers could not let the rhythm break. If a hand was pulled in — and hands were pulled in — the only tool for preventing the roller from taking the arm as well was a wooden mallet kept beside the machine for exactly this purpose. The mallet was there to break the bones before the roller took the arm. This is what the senhor called prudent management of his investment.

At night, after safra work ended or between shifts, the senzala had its own sounds. Call and response in Yoruba, in Bantu languages that had begun to merge along the coastal zone as people from different origins lived together and negotiated a common tongue. Drums when the senhor permitted them and drums when he did not but could not hear from behind the great house walls. The sound of people remembering who they were by performing who they were. The orixás were given the names of Catholic saints — Xangô became São Jerônimo; Iemanjá became Nossa Senhora; Ogum became São Jorge — not because the enslaved people believed they were the same, but because the Portuguese believed they were the same, and in that misreading was protection. The ceremony continued. What the colonizer saw as syncretism was survival operating through camouflage.

She ran in the third year. Not on the night she decided to run — there was no single night, no declaration of will. She ran because one morning the cost of staying became, in her body before her mind had registered it, greater than the cost of going. The forest at night was dark in a way the plantation was not. The plantation was lit by the furnace's glow. The forest was dark and wet and full of sounds she did not know. But the sounds of the forest were not the sound of the mallet.

The path to Palmares, if you knew it, was five days through the mata. She did not know it. She knew the direction — north and west, toward the hills she could see on clear days from the cane fields. She knew the name. People in the senzala passed it without looking at each other when they spoke it: Palmares. The palm trees. She walked toward the name.

## A Contra-Polity — O Quilombo

Palmares at its height was not one place. It was a federation of ten mocambos — settlements — spread across forty leagues of the Serra da Barriga and the adjacent forest, linked by paths known to the people of Palmares and known to no one else. The largest settlement, Cerca Real do Macaco, held perhaps six or eight thousand people. The others ranged from a few hundred to several thousand. The total population estimates from the Portuguese records — which were based on fear, not census — ranged from ten thousand to thirty thousand to forty thousand, numbers whose escalation tracks the progress of Portuguese anxiety as much as actual population growth.

The governance of Palmares was not the governance of the Portuguese colony. The paramount leader held a Bantu title: gangazumba, sometimes rendered as "great lord" by the colonial sources. Below the paramount were the leaders of each mocambo, who governed their settlements with significant autonomy. Decisions of importance to the federation were made collectively — the sources are unclear about the exact form, but the longevity of Palmares's resistance to overwhelming military force suggests an internal cohesion that purely top-down authority cannot produce. The people of Palmares had reasons to be loyal that the plantation had not manufactured for them.

The agricultural foundation was Brazilian. They grew manioc, beans, sweet potato, corn, banana — the subsistence crops of the coastal indigenous peoples, learned from Tupi and Tapuia communities with whom Palmares maintained complex relationships of alliance and trade. The food that made the quilombo independent of the plantation economy was food that the indigenous people of the interior had been growing for centuries before the Portuguese arrived. Palmares was not African culture transplanted to Brazil. It was something new: African political organization, Bantu title structures, West African cosmological practice, Tupi agricultural knowledge, and the particular solidarity of people who had survived the Middle Passage and the engenho and the forest at night and arrived, against all probability, somewhere they were free to be what they were.

The Dutch occupation of Pernambuco (1630–1654) accelerated Palmares's growth in ways the colonial authorities did not fully understand until afterward. When the Portuguese and Dutch were fighting over Recife and Olinda, the surveillance of the interior collapsed. Enslaved people ran in larger numbers than usual, toward a destination that had now survived for a quarter century. By the time Portugal retook Pernambuco from the Dutch in 1654, Palmares was large enough, organized enough, and entrenched enough in terrain the Portuguese did not know that it would survive another forty years.

The ceremonies were at the center of everything.

At dusk, when the day's work ended — the farming, the fortification, the training of capoeiristas — the sounds of Palmares were not the sounds of the plantation. There was no furnace. There was no mallet. What there was: the drums, the agogô, the voices of people calling to the orixás in languages that the forest absorbed and held. The ceremonies ran through the night in the dry season, when the stars were clear over the Serra da Barriga and the air smelled of dry palm frond and red clay. Xangô's devotees wore the red and white of his colors. Ogum's people brought tools to his altar. The babalorixás — the fathers of the orixá, the keepers of the initiatory lineage — performed the ceremonies they had received from their teachers, who had received them from theirs, in an unbroken chain of transmission that had begun in Yorubaland and now continued in the Pernambuco interior, unchanged in its essentials, transformed in its forms.

Call: How do you know where you are?

Response: By the name of the god who answers.

## A Escolha de Ganga Zumba

The governor of Pernambuco sent a proposal in 1678. It arrived at Palmares through intermediaries, and Ganga Zumba — the gangazumba, the paramount lord — received it and considered it for longer than the colonial records suggest he should have.

The terms: Palmares would be recognized as a legitimate settlement. Those born in Palmares — those who had never been enslaved — could remain free. Those who had escaped from the engenhos would be returned. The quilombo would not accept new runaways. The quilombo would exist, but it would no longer be the destination toward which people ran.

Ganga Zumba signed.

Zumbi did not.

What the Portuguese records do not capture, because they were not watching for it, was the argument that happened inside Palmares before and after the signing. Ganga Zumba's position was not cowardice and it was not naive trust in Portuguese promises. It was a reading of what Palmares was for. If Palmares was a refuge for people who had escaped slavery, then Palmares would eventually be destroyed — the Portuguese would not indefinitely tolerate a state in their colony that demonstrated, by its existence, that the colony's premise could be refused. If Palmares was a transmission mechanism — a place where the axé could be preserved and the community could survive — then it might be possible to accept a form of recognition that preserved the community at the cost of its role as refuge. Better a smaller Palmares that survived than a larger one that was destroyed.

Zumbi's counter-argument, as best it can be reconstructed from the fracture line it caused: Palmares that returned escapees had already become what it was built against. The axé does not travel through a gate that is locked. The woman who walked five days through the forest toward the name — she was Palmares's purpose, not its problem. The quilombo that refused her was not Palmares anymore. It was a Portuguese-licensed settlement with African names.

Ganga Zumba signed the treaty. He moved to a new settlement — Cucaú, in the coastal zone — with those who accepted the terms. Zumbi led those who did not back into the Serra da Barriga, where the mocambos that had not signed continued as before. Ganga Zumba died within two years. The colonial records say he was poisoned. By whom is not recorded. The records were not interested in Palmares's internal politics, only in whether it was finished. It was not finished.

Neither man was wrong. They were right about different things, and the difference between them was not of courage but of calculation about what the thing they were protecting actually was.

## Axé — O Que Atravessa

While Palmares held the Serra da Barriga, something else was forming in the coastal cities.

In Salvador, in Recife, in the senzalas and urban free Black communities that existed on the margins of the plantation economy, the terreiro network was taking shape. Terreiro: the sacred house where the orixás were honored, where the initiatory transmission continued, where the axé moved from babalorixá to yawô — initiate — in ceremonies that had been carrying this knowledge since before the ships. The terreiros of the coastal cities were not Palmares. They did not resist the colonial order with arms. They resisted it by continuing, by existing, by performing the transmission every time the drums were permitted and by finding ways to perform it when they were not.

The babalorixá knew which deity claimed which person at birth. This was knowledge that required years of training to acquire and decades of practice to master. The initiatory process was long and deliberate: the yawô entering the reclusive period, receiving the markings and the consecrations, learning the songs in Yoruba (a language they might not otherwise speak), learning the dances that were not dances but were prayers conducted in the body. The knowledge passed hand to hand, mouth to ear, in a chain of specific transmission that was the only archive the tradition had.

The ialorixá — the mother of the orixá, the mae-de-santo — ran most of the terreiros, because the tradition understood that certain forms of sacred authority were held by women. The women who carried the initiatory lineages of the Nagô/Yoruba cabildo in the coastal cities carried the same tradition that had survived in Palmares. They were not the same people. The tradition was the same river, moving through different channels.

One channel: the Engenho Velho terreiro in Salvador, founded in the 1830s by three women — Iyá Detá, Iyá Kalá, Iyá Nassô — who had come from the Nagô nation and who established the house that would become the mother-house of Candomblé Nagô in Brazil. The dates say the 1830s. The tradition those three women were transmitting was already centuries old, already had been transmitting since before Palmares, since before the ships. The terreiro formalized what had been moving underground.

The Catholic saints were still present on the altar alongside the orixás. Xangô behind the face of São Jerônimo. Iemanjá behind the face of Nossa Senhora. This was not confusion. It was a method. The priest who came to inspect the altar saw what he expected to see. The babalorixá knew what was actually there. The double reading — what the colonizer saw and what was actually present — was not hypocrisy. It was survival operating as theology. The axé does not require the colonizer's recognition to be real. It requires only the drum and the body that knows how to answer.

Call: Where is the nation?

Response: In the house. In the songs the house remembers. In the hands that learned from the hands that learned from the hands.

## A Queda — 1694

Domingos Jorge Velho was a bandeirante from São Paulo — a raider, a hunter of indigenous peoples for the slave trade, a man whose biography was the colonial economy written in its most direct form. He had spent his career in the interior, in territory the government could not reach without him. He understood the forest differently than the militia commanders who had failed in 1645 and 1677. He did not try to penetrate the mocambos. He laid siege to the Cerca Real do Macaco — the main settlement — and cut it off.

The siege lasted twenty-two days. Palmares, at its height, had held against everything the Portuguese and the Dutch could bring. In 1694, after nearly ninety years, the walls of Cerca Real do Macaco fell.

Zumbi was not there. He had escaped into the forest with a small group, into terrain he knew the way he knew the songs — in his body, before his mind, in the specific knowledge of a man who had been born in this landscape and had spent his life learning it. He lasted another year. He was betrayed, in November 1695, by a member of his own community — a man named Antônio Soares, who told the colonial forces where to find him. The records do not say what Antônio Soares received for this. The records are interested in the ending.

Zumbi was killed on November 20, 1695. His head was displayed in Recife — proof, the colonial authorities said, that the rebellion was finished. The rebels saw the head. They understood what they were being shown. They did not understand it the way the Portuguese intended.

The head was not the axé.

## O Que Não Para

The drum did not stop.

November 20, 1695 — the date the colonial authorities displayed Zumbi's head in Recife as proof that Palmares was finished. November 20 is now the Dia da Consciência Negra — the National Day of Black Consciousness — in Brazil. The colonial record of a death became, three centuries later, a national day of reckoning. The Portuguese were not wrong about Palmares. They were wrong about what Palmares was.

In the decades after 1695, quilombos continued to form across Brazil. There were hundreds by 1800. None lasted as long as Palmares or grew as large, but they were never absent. The quilombo was not a strategy Palmares invented and bequeathed; it was a response that emerged wherever the conditions that produced it were present, which was everywhere the Atlantic slave trade reached in Brazil.

The gold rush of Minas Gerais (c. 1690–1750) transformed the colony's economy and brought a new wave of enslaved people from different origins — more Mina/Fon peoples, strengthening the Nagô/Yoruba tradition in the urban centers of the coast. The terreiro network accelerated. The ialorixás who maintained the initiatory lineages in Salvador and Recife carried a tradition that was now three or four or five generations deep in Brazil, transformed by the conditions it had survived, continuous in its essentials.

In 1791, in Saint-Domingue, the ceremony at Bois Caïman opened the only successful large-scale slave revolution the Atlantic world had ever seen. Every slaveholding society in the Americas heard the news from Haiti. Brazil doubled its surveillance, tripled its brutality, increased the punishments for running and for assembly and for drumming at unauthorized hours. The terreiros went deeper underground. The axé went with them.

Abolition did not come until 1888 — the last country in the Americas to end slavery, ninety-three years after Zumbi's head was displayed in Recife. By then the terreiro tradition was already a century deep in Brazil's coastal cities. By then the capoeira circles were already forming in the urban periphery, carrying the same epistemological camouflage — it looks like dancing, which is the point — into the republican period. By then the axé had passed through so many hands, so many initiations, so many moments of transmission in circumstances that tried to prevent it, that no colonial record could have documented where it had been or tracked where it was going.

The Serra da Barriga still stands in Pernambuco. The hill with the belly. What it swallowed, it is still holding.

In the terreiros of Salvador and Recife, the drums sound at dusk. The ialorixás perform the ceremonies they received from their teachers, who received them from theirs, in a chain of hands and ears and voices that runs back through the nineteenth century and the eighteenth century and the seventeenth century, back through the ships, back to the hands that learned on the other side of the water. The babalorixás know which orixá is present by the way the air in the room changes. The yawô in reclusion is learning the songs in Yoruba, the language that traveled in a body across the Atlantic and arrived in Palmares intact and never stopped moving.

Call: Is the axé still here?

Response: Ask the drum.

Response: Ask the child learning the song.

Response: Ask the Serra da Barriga, which held it in its belly for a century and never let it go.
