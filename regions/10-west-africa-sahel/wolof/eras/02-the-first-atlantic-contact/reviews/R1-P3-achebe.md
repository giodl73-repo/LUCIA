# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Wolof / Era 2: The First Atlantic Contact
**Round:** 1 (Opening review)

## What Works

The voice is right. The Wolof are the protagonists from the first sentence. The Portuguese arrive in the Wolof world, not the other way around. "The Wolof knew more" is the opening's thesis, and it is earned by what follows: the Wolof assess the horses, calculate their worth, send word through their own political channels, and negotiate on their own terms. At no point does the text step outside the Wolof worldview to validate their sophistication from an external position. This is correct.

The treatment of the jon is honest. "The people given in trade were jon, war captives whose status under Wolof law was defined and bounded." The text does not apologize for the trade. It does not condemn it. It presents it in the Wolof legal framework without modern commentary. This is the hardest sentence in the opening to write well, and it is written well.

The Portuguese chronicler Zurara appears as a character inside the Wolof story, not as an authority on it. His "puzzled respect" is his limitation, not the Wolof's achievement. This is the correct framing.

## What Needs Work

**"He did not know the name of the river he had entered. He did not know the name of the people watching from the shore."** This is technically written from the Portuguese captain's ignorance, but the prose lingers on him for too long. Three sentences about what Lancarote de Freitas knew and did not know positions him as a figure of interest in his own right. He is not. He is a visitor. Compress his presence. One sentence of identification, then back to the Wolof.

**"the people they encountered were neither the monsters the old maps promised nor the blank-eyed heathens the papal bulls imagined."** This sentence borrows the Portuguese framework (monsters, heathens) even to refute it. It lets the European imagination into the room. A Wolof would not define themselves against what the Portuguese expected to find. Cut this sentence entirely. The Wolof are traders with states and opinions about horses. That is sufficient. The European expectation is irrelevant to the Wolof story.

**"a sovereign entity with whom Portugal dealt as an equal."** The word "equal" invites a comparison that the Wolof would not make. The Burba Jolof did not think of himself as Portugal's equal. He thought of himself as the Burba Jolof, and the Portuguese were one more set of traders to be managed. Reframe: the Wolof expected to be treated according to their own understanding of their status, not according to a concept of equivalence with a foreign power they had just met.

**The gewel needs more presence.** The gewel is mentioned twice (as a historical reference: "the gewel had a name for" and "the gewel would remember") but never performs. In the Mali opening, the jeli sings. Here, the gewel is invoked but silent. Even a half-sentence of the gewel's voice, a phrase of what was remembered, would establish the oral tradition as a living authority in the narrative rather than a footnote about who keeps the records.

## The Question I'd Push On

Whose story is this, ultimately? The opening tells the story of a political calculation: the Burba Jolof assessing a new trade partner. This is sound, but it is a story of elites. Where are the people who are not the buur, the Burba Jolof, or the Portuguese captain? The women on the beach. The fishermen who first saw the ships. The jon who were traded. If the chapter is told only from the perspective of those who calculated, it risks becoming a diplomatic history rather than a Wolof story. The gewel tradition carries all of Wolof society, not just the rulers. The opening should signal that the chapter will find those other voices, even if it begins with the buur's calculation.
