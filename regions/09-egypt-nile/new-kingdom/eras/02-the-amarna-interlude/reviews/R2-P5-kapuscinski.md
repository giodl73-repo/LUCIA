## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Review of Notes (Panel-2)

**Focus:** Sensory plan for the full chapter.

**Assessment:** The section plan gives me the structure. I need to see the sensory plan beneath it.

**What needs attention:**

1. Section I (Karnak): The hypostyle hall needs heat, light, scale. 134 columns. Painted surfaces. The stonecutter's body working between them. The morning light falling through the clerestory windows. The sound of chisels echoing off stone that was carved a century ago.

2. Section III (Akhetaten): The desert site. The cliffs behind, white and sheer. The river to the west. The sun. The city was built on flat, clean desert between the cliffs and the cultivation strip. In summer, the stone surfaces would radiate heat well after sunset. The open temple: imagine performing ritual in full sun rather than in the cool darkness of an Amun sanctuary. The bodies of the priests would be drenched in sweat. The incense would burn differently in open air.

3. Section IV (The Letters): Even diplomatic evidence can carry sensory weight. Rib-Hadda's letters are desperate. The clay tablets themselves were carried by runners across the Sinai and the Negev in leather pouches. The physical act of correspondence is a sensory fact: a scribe in Byblos pressing a stylus into wet clay, the tablet drying in the Levantine sun, the journey to Egypt, the arrival at a court that may not read it.

4. Section VI (The Silence After): The landing needs the sound of chisels again, but this time carving names back into scarred stone. The scars would have been visible. The restoration carvings are rougher than the originals. The stone remembers what was taken.

**Verdict:** The sensory plan is implicit but must be made explicit in the writing. Each section needs at least one grounding sensory anchor. The chapter must not float above the ground when it moves from the stonecutter to the court.
