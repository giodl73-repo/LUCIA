## B-fulani-1 — Murray Last — Board Review of "Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** The Sokoto Caliphate's administrative and political history; the flag-holder system; the emirate structure; the relationship between central authority and provincial governance.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The flag-holder system is correctly represented as a distributed commission system rather than a military chain of command. The chapter's central metaphor (a revolution distributed across a network of books) aligns with the evidence.
- The founding of Sokoto in 1809 is correct. The characterization of Bello as the institution-builder who chose the site for practical reasons is consistent with the evidence.
- The fall of Kano in 1807 and Alkalawa in 1808 are correctly dated.
- The emirate system's structure (emirs holding authority from the Sultan, tribute flowing to Sokoto) is accurately described.
- The Gwandu-Sokoto tension is correctly identified. Abdullahi's *Tazyin al-Waraqat* is accurately characterized as more candid than Bello's accounts.
- The characterization of the slave economy tension is historically accurate. The Caliphate did become one of the largest slaveholding polities in Africa, and the legal debate over the status of enslaved persons was conducted within the Maliki framework the chapter describes.
- Minor concern: the chapter says "thirty emirates." The number is approximate and varies by how one counts dependent and semi-independent territories. "Approximately thirty" would be more precise.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The jihad is presented through its own theological and legal framework, which is correct. The *Kitab al-Farq* and *Ihya al-Sunna* are accurately characterized.
- The hijra is correctly presented as a deliberate political-theological act modeled on the Prophet's hijra, not merely a retreat.
- The Shehu's withdrawal from governance is consistent with the evidence. He did leave administration to Bello and Abdullahi.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The central insight that the scholarly network was the revolution's infrastructure is correct and well-supported by the evidence.
- The portrayal of the pen-and-herd alliance is accurate: the Torodbe provided legal authority, the pastoral Fulani provided military capacity.
- The transition from revolutionary movement to governing state is the central tension of the post-jihad period, and the chapter renders it accurately.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter should note that the flag-holders were formally commissioned by the Shehu with written documents (wathiqat al-jihad), not merely sent flags. The flags were symbols; the written commissions were the legal instruments. The chapter's emphasis on the letter alongside the flag is largely correct, but the formal term should appear.
- The statement that Sarki Bawa Jan Gwarzo granted the Torodbe "the right to free any enslaved person who came to them" requires qualification. The privilege was specifically about the community's jurisdiction over its own members, not a general emancipation authority. The description is close but could be tightened.
- The battle of Tabkin Kwotto description should note that the victory was not entirely a surprise; the community had been preparing for armed conflict, and some pastoral Fulani fighters had military experience from earlier conflicts. The chapter's characterization of "many had never held a weapon" may overstate the community's inexperience.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (3 minor corrections above)
