# Panel-2 Review — P-5 Ryszard Kapuściński
## Le Grand Redressement — Notes Review

**Reviewer:** Ryszard Kapuściński (P-5)
**Stage:** PANEL-2
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The production bible addresses my primary concern from PANEL-1: Theodulf on
circuit in southern Gaul is the right body in the right place. His *Contra
Iudices* gives the chapter a scene outside Aachen, in a specific physical
environment — a village court in Languedoc, late in the campaign season,
the air still warm in the south, a judge receiving a bribe in plain sight.
This is where the reform meets the world it is trying to correct, and that
encounter has a texture that the palace school does not.

**What I am confident will work:** The section plan moves from Aachen
(interior, lamp-lit, careful) to the provinces (exterior, road-worn, resistant)
and back to Aachen (winter 814, the manuscripts accumulating). This is the
right physical arc. The reader should feel the distance between the Aachen
scriptorium and the Languedoc village court — not just political distance
but physical distance, measured in roads and horses and mud.

**What I want the chapter to deliver that the plan gestures at:**
- The scriptorium at Tours. Alcuin moved there in 796. The scriptorium was
  his administrative domain — he directed the copying program, standardized
  the script, supervised the scholars. What did it look and feel like? The
  windows in a Carolingian scriptorium were placed specifically for natural
  light because artificial light ruined the eyes and the parchment. The
  scribes worked in silence. The only sounds were the stylus on wax for
  drafts and the pen on parchment for fair copies. Alcuin walked the room
  and corrected. Give me that room.
- The Roman road between Aachen and the south. Parts of the Via Agrippa
  were still usable; parts had degraded. Theodulf would have known which
  sections required detour. The physical experience of carrying a royal
  directive into the provinces — the weight of the document in a saddlebag,
  the quality of the road, the time it took — is part of what the reform
  project actually was.
- The arrival of a missus in a village. What did the community see? A clergyman
  and a layman, with the king's seal, dismounting at the church or the
  great house. The court assembled. Questions asked in Latin that the local
  priest may or may not understand. This is the reform made physical.

**One note on the landing (Section 6):** "What cannot travel is the man who
made the scribes believe the correction mattered." This is strong. Keep it.
It is the physical truth of the terminal condition: the manuscripts travel,
the scripts travel, the buildings remain — but the person who animated them
is gone, and the empire that depended on his person begins, silently, to
fragment. I can feel the weight of that sentence. The chapter should earn it
by making us feel Charles's presence throughout — not just Alcuin's —  so
that his absence, implied in the landing, has physical weight.
