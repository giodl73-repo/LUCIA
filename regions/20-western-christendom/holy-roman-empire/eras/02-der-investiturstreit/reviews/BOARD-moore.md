# BOARD-HRE-3 — R.I. Moore | Domain Review

**Chapter:** Holy Roman Empire, Era 2 — Der Investiturstreit (1024–1122 CE)
**Reviewer:** R.I. Moore (B-HRE-3) — Popular Reform, Social History
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## The Social Dimension

The chapter does something unusual for a chapter on the Investiture Controversy: it includes the Patarene movement and Ariald of Milan as a genuine structural element of the story, not as a footnote to the elite-level constitutional argument. The decision to open Section I through Ariald's eyes — watching the investiture ceremony and seeing simony — is exactly the right structural choice. It immediately establishes that the argument was not only between Gregory and Heinrich but ran through the entire society.

The accuracy of the Patarene account is good. The name "Pataria" as a term of abuse applied by cathedral clergy is correctly characterized. The description of the movement's base in "artisans, minor merchants, laypeople" is broadly accurate, though the Patarenes also had aristocratic supporters — specifically, a faction of the Milanese nobility that had its own reasons to oppose the archbishopric's power. The chapter simplifies slightly, but the simplification does not distort.

**One correction:** The chapter says the Patarenes "demanded clergy live chastely." This is accurate but incomplete — the Patarene program had three main elements: against simony (the purchase of offices), against nicolaitism (clerical marriage and concubinage), and against lay investiture. The chapter mentions simony and lay investiture but not nicolaitism, which was arguably the most viscerally controversial for ordinary people, because it was visible: a married priest celebrating mass was an offense that the reform theology made deeply problematic. The chapter could add one sentence acknowledging that married clergy (a common reality in the eleventh century) was a third target of the reform, to give a fuller picture of what the popular movement was actually arguing about.

## The Literate/Illiterate Divide

My central argument in *The First European Revolution* is that the Investiture Controversy accelerated the consolidation of a literate governing class — the clergy — and created new institutional structures that would define European society for centuries. The chapter touches this in Section V (the universities emerging from bishop's schools) but does not make the structural point explicit.

The ordinary faithful — the "farmer on an episcopal estate" who appears briefly in Section IV — are present in the chapter, which is more than most treatments manage. But the chapter could go slightly further in indicating what the Investiture Controversy meant for the relationship between the literate clergy and the people who depended on them. When the controversy made the bishop's authority uncertain, it also made the literate administration of the territory uncertain — the courts, the charters, the dispute resolution that ordinary people needed. This ground-level institutional disruption is real and historically documented.

This is a suggestion for enrichment, not a correction. The chapter is structurally sound without it.

---

## Ariald — Accuracy

The chapter's Ariald is accurate. The figure section correctly identifies him as a deacon (not a priest or bishop), notes the mob violence in which he was killed, and correctly states that his predecessor Alexander II began the canonization process.

**One minor correction:** The chapter says Ariald was "murdered in June 1066, on an island in Maggiore." The location is attested in the sources. "Maggiore" refers to Lake Maggiore — the island is thought to be one of the Borromean Islands. This is correct. No correction needed.

---

## The Transformation Argument

The chapter's Section V argues that the Investiture Controversy "made the world" — produced the institutional Church, the more autonomous principalities, the seeds of the secular state. This matches my own argument in *The First European Revolution*, though I would phrase the transformation more broadly: the controversy was both a symptom and an accelerant of a wider social transformation that included the growth of towns, the expansion of literacy, the emergence of new professional classes (lawyers, merchants, notaries) who operated outside the feudal hierarchy. The chapter focuses on the institutional political consequences and does not need to expand into the full social history — that is beyond its scope. But the claim that "the world that came out of the Concordat of Worms was not the world that went in" is correct.

---

## Recommendation

One factual enrichment recommended: add one sentence on nicolaitism (clerical marriage/concubinage) as a third Patarene target, alongside simony and lay investiture. All other historical content is accurate. The social dimension of the controversy is better represented here than in most treatments of this material.
