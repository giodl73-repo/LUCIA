The opening places the reader on the Uraga fortification; the middle sections move through Edo politics and domain councils; the closing acknowledges the transformation beginning. No modern framing intrudes. The analytic moments in the landing are minimal and serve the transition to new era. The score reflects excellence in maintaining inside perspective through complex material.

**Evidence (9/10):** The chapter is grounded in historical fact. One minor note: Tōjirō is a representative figure. This is disclosed in figures.md ("He is not a historical figure; he is a representative of the system"), which is appropriate. The opening's specificity (July 8, 1853, Edo Bay, Uraga fortification) is historically anchored. All dated events are documented (Treaty of Kanagawa 1854, Harris Treaty 1858, Ansei Purge 1858–1860, Ii's assassination 1860, Yoshinobu's restoration announcement November 1867, Boshin War 1868). 

**v1.3 Amendment – Knowledge System as Evidence:** The chapter shows sakoku not as a superstition but as a knowledge system and policy that functioned. The edict held not through blind obedience but through understanding: "The edict said Japan was closed, and this had been true for two centuries because everyone — everyone — had obeyed it." The chapter shows the system's own logic breaking when its assumptions are violated. This is evidence at the level of the system's own understanding, not modern reinterpretation. This technique merits notation.

**Progression (9/10):** The five-section arc is clean. Section 1 (Breaking): assumption violated. Section 2 (Fracture): adaptation accelerates failure. Section 3 (Rupture): internal rebellion becomes irreversible. Section 4 (Transformation): new institution forms. Section 5 (Landing): question persists at new level. This is not chronological narration; it follows the structural engine (legitimacy collapse). The Boshin War in Section 3 is mentioned rather than dramatized ("less a war than a confirmation"), which is intentional — the real battle is institutional, not military. Some readers might want more dramatization of the actual fighting, but the choice to treat the military dimension as secondary to the institutional dimension is supported by the story and the voice.

**Voice (9/10):** The saga-spare register sustains throughout. The rhythm belongs to this story, not borrowed from another culture's register. No generic literary prose. ## Innovations Detected

**Innovation 1 — Knowledge System as Structural Principle:**
The chapter shows sakoku not as a primitive isolation but as a sophisticated knowledge system. The system worked because everyone — shogunate, domains, world — understood and accepted the boundary. When one actor violates the assumption, the system breaks not through military defeat but through the revelation that the boundary was always conditional. This is a technique for showing how sophisticated systems can be undone by the violation of their foundational assumptions. **Location:** Section 1-2, repeated in closing. **Category:** Evidence / Honesty / Progression.

**Innovation 2 — Trapped Choice as Structural Engine:**
The chapter implies throughout that the shogunate faces a genuinely trapped choice: it can fight or negotiate, and both choices end it. Fighting risks military defeat and foreign war; negotiating reveals weakness and accelerates domain rebellion. The institution is broken by its own attempt to choose between two unacceptable options. This is distinct from external defeat — it is internal collapse triggered by impossible choice. **Location:** Section 1-2, implicit throughout. **Category:** Progression / Voice.
