# La Organización Nacional y la Generación del 80

## Opening — El Conventillo

Buenos Aires, 1895. The building at Calle Defensa 179 holds eleven families and one room that is nobody's room. Italian from Genoa, Spanish from Galicia, a Pole who doesn't speak to anyone, three Afro-Argentine women who have lived in the Barrio Sur since before any of the newcomers arrived and will be here when most of them have moved on. The courtyard is a shared problem: the laundry, the water pump, the smell from the communal latrine. At night, when the heat of the day has lifted slightly and the pump is not being fought over, someone brings a guitar.

This is where it starts. Not in a ballroom. Not in any place the city's newspapers would mention.

The music that comes out of that courtyard is nobody's music yet. It is not what the Italian brought from Naples — his songs had a different sweetness, a different backward pull. It is not what the Galician brought either, nor the African rhythm that surfaces in the bass line without announcing itself. It is the sound of people who came from somewhere else and have not yet become anything together, trying to make an evening bearable. The bandoneón player — a German instrument, originally designed for hymns in small churches — arrived in the port district sometime in the 1870s and nobody remembers exactly when it became the voice of this. But it did. The accordion breathes in and breathes out. The violin enters. Two people who barely know each other stand very close and move.

The name "tango" is already in use by the time these families are crowded into Defensa 179. Where the word comes from is contested: some say it is African, a word for the gathering place where enslaved people danced; others say it comes from the Portuguese; others trace it to the Latin tangere, to touch. Nobody in the conventillo is worrying about etymology. The music is being played. The dance is happening in three feet of shared space because there is no more space than that.

What the Argentine elite understands about this is simple: it is vulgar. The tango is a dance of the arrabal, the city's edge, the immigrant slums where languages pile up and propriety does not reach. The women who dance it are assumed to be of questionable virtue. The men who dance it are laborers, dockhands, people of color, recent arrivals who have not yet learned how Argentina works. The newspapers that serve the oligarchy — that thin upper crust of landowning families who run the country from their estancias and their clubs — do not cover the conventillo music except to condemn it. The country's government, dominated since 1880 by the Partido Autonomista Nacional and the Generación del 80, has very clear ideas about what Argentina should become: educated, European, modern. The oligarchy is building the Teatro Colón. It is inviting Italian opera companies. It is laying out Buenos Aires on a Haussmann grid — the great diagonals, the wide boulevards, the parks named after Libertad and Independencia. There is a vision here, and the tango is not in it.

In 1900, six million Europeans will arrive in Argentina over the next thirty years, the greatest sustained immigration in the country's history. The Generación del 80 planned this immigration, legislated it, paid for it. They needed workers. The Pampa needed to be settled. The railways needed to run. The wheat and cattle economy needed hands. What they did not plan was what six million people would do to the culture when they arrived — the languages they would bring, the music they would make in the courtyards, the way the city would change underneath the government's plans for it.

The tango is happening in the conventillos of La Boca and San Telmo, in the arrabales at the city's edge, in the milonga dance halls that the elite ignores. It is moving outward, slowly, into the middling neighborhoods where clerks and shopkeepers live. It is not yet respectable. It will not be respectable for another fifteen years, and the respectability will not come from Buenos Aires.

It will come from Paris.

Around 1910, the tango reaches the cabarets of Montparnasse. The exact route — which sailor, which musician, which ship — is not something the historians can fully reconstruct. But Paris receives it and Paris decides it is fashionable. Paris teaches it to the Russian aristocracy. Paris sends it back across the Atlantic as a craze. And when Buenos Aires learns that Paris has been dancing the tango, the Argentine upper class discovers that it has been a national art form all along.

The tango is a compression of the whole thing, three minutes long. A country that wanted to be Europe so badly that it needed Europe's permission to recognize what it was. The bandoneón breathes in. The violin enters. The dancer's heel finds the floor.
