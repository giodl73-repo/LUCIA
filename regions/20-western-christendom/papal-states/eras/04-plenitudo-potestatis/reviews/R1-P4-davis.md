# R1-P4 — Wade Davis

**Chapter:** Papal States — Plenitudo Potestatis
**Stage:** PANEL-1
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I come to this chapter from a different angle than my usual. Most of my interventions are about preventing the diminishment of cultures whose sophistication is invisible to those who equate intelligence with literacy or urbanization. The medieval papacy is not that kind of culture — it is a literate, juridical, politically complex institution whose sophistication has never been in question.

But there is a version of this chapter that would still fail my test, and it would fail it not by diminishing the papacy but by presenting it as the center of a world in which other cultures are satellites. The opening, as written, mostly avoids this — the crusade references are brief, the conversion of the Norse is framed (with the minor revision Achebe flagged) from the curial perspective rather than from a judgment about those cultures. The Jews and Muslims who must wear distinctive clothing appear as legal subjects of papal governance, which is what they were from inside the system — not as victims in a modern frame but also not as invisible.

What I want the chapter to hold onto as it expands: the plenitudo potestatis was a knowledge system. Gratian's Decretum, Innocent III's decretals, the whole apparatus of canon law — this is an intellectual achievement of staggering sophistication. The chapter should engage with canon law not merely as political power but as a way of knowing and organizing the world. The canonists who argued about the relationship between positive law and natural law, about whether a pope could err in a matter of faith, about the status of corporate bodies (the universitas) under law — they were doing philosophy of the highest order. The medieval papacy produced, in the corpus of canon law, one of the most complex intellectual systems any culture has ever created.

The opening does not engage with this yet — it lists the canons but does not show the system at the level of HOW IT WORKS. For the chapter, I want to see canon law shown as method: a specific case appealed to Rome, a specific question of interpretation, the juristic reasoning applied to it. Show the knowledge system operating, not just producing outputs.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter mentions Francis of Assisi and Dominic of Osma as orders Innocent recognized. This is where the chapter could open into the complexity the chapter needs: these were not simply papal instruments. Francis wanted voluntary poverty and got an institution; Dominic wanted preaching and got a mendicant order that became an apparatus of the Inquisition. The reform impulse that produced them was in tension with the institutional impulse that recognized them. The chapter should find this tension — it is where the papacy's own internal knowledge system (what is the right way to be Christian?) generates forces it cannot fully control, just as the crusade impulse generated the sack of Constantinople.

**Verdict:** The opening is intellectually sound. The chapter must show canon law as a living intellectual system, not just as a list of decrees. The papacy's knowledge system — its theory of legitimate authority, its jurisprudence, its theology of the sacraments — deserves the same depth of engagement that this project brings to navigational knowledge or shamanic pharmacology. It is, in its own terms, equally astonishing.
