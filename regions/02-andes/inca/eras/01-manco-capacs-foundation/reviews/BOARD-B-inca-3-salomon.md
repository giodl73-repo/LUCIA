# BOARD-B-inca-3 — Frank Salomon — Board Review of Manco Cápac's Foundation

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Andean ethnohistory, colonial filter navigation, Quechua terminology, living Andean traditions.

## Historical Accuracy

- **Correct:** Quechua terms are used correctly: ayllu, kuraka, quipucamayoc, qollqa, huaca, ceque, ayni, minka, cumbi, ahuasca, chuño, chicha.
- **Correct:** The colonial filter is explicitly acknowledged: "Spanish priests and administrators writing in the decades after the conquest, men who filtered what they heard through the categories of European Christianity and Roman law."
- **Correct:** Garcilaso is appropriately noted as someone who "carried Inca royal blood in his veins and wrote from exile in Spain with the particular ache of a man trying to preserve a world he could no longer touch" — this captures his unique position without uncritically accepting his account.
- **Correct:** The Pacaritambo variant of the origin myth is not mentioned (a reasonable omission for length), but the chapter notes that the origin story was told retrospectively, which is the key point.

## Cultural Authenticity

The inside voice is largely sustained. The Andean world is presented through its own categories (ayllu, huaca, ceque) rather than through European translations. The chapter does not call the ayllu a "clan" or the mit'a a "corvee" or the kuraka a "chief." This is essential.

The treatment of the origin myth is handled with appropriate nuance: it is told as story, not as "belief," and the chapter explicitly notes that the people who told it understood its political function without being cynical about it.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The ayllu is presented as an Andean category, not a European one. The chapter says "that phrase in English carries the wrong weight" when introducing it, which is exactly the right instinct.
- The ceque system is introduced at the right level of complexity for a foundation chapter — enough to establish it as a spatial-temporal-ritual framework without overwhelming the narrative.
- The colonial chroniclers (Cieza, Betanzos, Cobo, Garcilaso) are named and their limitations acknowledged without dismissing their evidence.

## What Needs Correction

1. **Huaca definition:** The chapter uses "huaca" as "sacred place" which is partially correct but too narrow. A huaca could be a spring, a rock, a mountain peak, a mummy, a carved stone, an oddly shaped potato — anything in the world that displayed unusual properties or held spiritual significance. The term's breadth is part of the Andean worldview's character. A brief expansion would prevent the reader from thinking "huaca = shrine."

2. **Mama Ocllo's weaving gift:** The attribution to "some versions told to Bernabe Cobo" is fine, but the chapter should note that the association of women with weaving and men with warfare/agriculture is an Andean complementarity principle (yanantin) that organizes the cosmos itself, not merely a social division of labor. This deepens the inside voice.

3. **"Common era" dating:** The chapter uses "roughly 1200 and 1400 of the common era" which is appropriate, but for the Inca chapters specifically, it might be worth noting that the Inca's own time-reckoning was organized around the ceque-calendar system and the agricultural cycle (planting to harvest), not around a linear year-count. A single sentence acknowledging their calendar would be consistent with Style Guide Principle #4.

## Verdict: APPROVED WITH CONDITIONS

Minor corrections: expand huaca definition, note the complementarity principle behind Mama Ocllo's role, add a gesture toward Inca time-reckoning. The chapter successfully navigates the colonial filter and sustains the inside voice. The Quechua terminology is correct and well-integrated.
