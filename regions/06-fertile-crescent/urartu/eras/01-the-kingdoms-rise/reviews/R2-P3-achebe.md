# R2 — P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Notes (The Kingdom's Rise)

**Focus:** Cultural voice and the highland people.

The notes promise to bring the highland population into section II. Good. But I want to push further. The kingdom of Biainili was not only its kings and their inscriptions. The highland had peoples before the kings organized them: herders who moved livestock between summer and winter pastures, metalworkers who smelted iron and copper from the volcanic deposits, farmers who terraced the slopes near the lake. When Sarduri built his citadel, he built it over and among these people. The kingdom's rise is also the story of a highland population being organized into a state, and the chapter must decide whose story it is telling: the kings' or the peoples'.

The inscriptions are the kings' voice. But the canals and the fortress storerooms are the peoples' labor. The deportation counts in Argishti's inscriptions are evidence that this kingdom, like Assyria, built its agricultural base partly through forced population transfer. The chapter must handle this honestly. The people who dug the canals and filled the storerooms were not all volunteers. Some were deportees from conquered territories. Biainili's story told from inside does not mean Biainili's story told uncritically. It means the deportees are part of the inside, not an embarrassment to be managed.

On the language question: the notes acknowledge uncertainty about the relationship between inscriptional Urartian and spoken varieties. This is sufficient at the notes stage. In the writing, a single honest sentence will serve: we know the language of the inscriptions, not the language of the fields.

**Verdict:** The chapter's voice must hold room for the labor as well as the inscription. The kingdom rose on both.
