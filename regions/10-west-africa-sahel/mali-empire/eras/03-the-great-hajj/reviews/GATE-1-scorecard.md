# GATE-1 Scorecard — "The Great Hajj" Opening

**Scorer:** LUCIA Rubric v1.0
**Date:** 2026-04-15

## Scores

| Dimension | Score | Evidence |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| Scene     | 5/5   | The opening lands in a wholly specific moment: "Bala Faseke Kouyate stood before the gate of Niani and sang the names." A named person, a named place, an action already underway. By the end of the first paragraph, we have the jeli, the genealogy, the morning light, and the column stretching "beyond where any eye could follow." No abstraction, no preamble — the reader is dropped into the scene. |
| World     | 4/5   | The culture's logic shapes the prose from within: "a jeli does not choose which ancestors to remember. A jeli remembers them all." The Kurukan Fuga's demand for generosity is presented as operating law, not anthropological observation: "A king who does not give is not a king." Social categories (horon, nyamakalaw, jelilu) appear without glossary. The hajj is framed through Mande-Islamic logic. Held back from a 5 by moments of soft explanation — "the hajj is the fifth pillar," "the dundun, which speaks in the tonal language of the Mande and says what words alone cannot" — where the prose pauses to teach the reader rather than trusting the world to carry itself. |
| Honesty   | 4/5   | No outside judgment anywhere. Khalifa "shot arrows into the crowd and did not last" — stated without moral editorializing. The gold is framed entirely within the culture's own value system: "So the gold was not extravagance. The gold was the point." No romanticization, no pity, no ranking against a modern baseline. Withheld a 5 because "The sources agree on this" is a brief historiographic intrusion — the writer's vantage as someone reading external accounts surfaces for a moment, creating a slight distance from the fully-inhabited perspective. |
| Texture   | 4/5   | Richly specific and grounded in material culture: "Niani smelled of dust and shea butter and woodsmoke." "The harmattan had come and mostly gone, leaving a haze over the Niger that the sun was burning through." Gold staffs weighed in mithqals. "Saddlebags that sagged with the weight." The kora's low notes, the dundun speaking in tonal language, the djembe setting the body moving. "Dust that hung in the haze and did not settle for hours." Sensory detail is woven into action rather than catalogued. Falls short of a 5 because the forward-looking passages (the route north, the consequences in Cairo) shift into summary where the sensory world recedes. |
| Voice     | 4/5   | The prose has a distinctive accumulative rhythm that echoes oral narration: "He sang Sundiata, the Lion, the hungering lion, who crawled and then walked and then ran and then conquered." Repetitions build like a jeli's performance: "North through the savanna... North to Walata... North across sand." Short declarative sentences land with weight: "Musa was going to Mecca." "The gold was the point." The voice is consistent and belongs to this story. Held from a 5 by occasional shifts toward a more writerly-historical register — "The Arab scholars who later recorded this could not agree on the number" — where the narrator steps slightly outside the world. |
| Pull      | 4/5   | The opening builds genuine momentum through accretion — the genealogy, the scale of the caravan, the weight of gold — then releases it: "Musa raised his hand, and the caravan began to move." The single-line paragraph "Musa was going to Mecca" works as a pivot that sharpens everything before it into purpose. The closing image — dust that "did not settle for hours after the last of them had passed" — points forward while completing the scene. The pull is earned and specific to this story. Not a 5 because the momentum is more a gathering grandeur than an unresolvable tension; it relies partly on the event's inherent scale rather than a question the reader cannot put down. |
| **Total** | **25/30** | |

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong opening that succeeds most fully in its scene-setting and its voice — the jeli's genealogy is a superb entry point that simultaneously establishes character, world, and narrative momentum. The prose rhythm is distinctive and mostly self-sustaining. The main pattern holding it back from the highest tier is a tendency toward brief explanatory asides ("the hajj is the fifth pillar," "The sources agree on this," "which speaks in the tonal language of the Mande") where the writer steps outside the world to ensure the reader follows — moments where trust in the reader, and in the world's own gravity, could be greater.
