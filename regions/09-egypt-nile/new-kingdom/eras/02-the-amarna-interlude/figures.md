# Figures — The Amarna Interlude

## Akhenaten (Amenhotep IV)

He changed his name, his capital, his god, his art, and the theological foundations of the oldest state in the world, and he did it in less than two decades. Whether this makes him a visionary or a fanatic depends on where you stand, and this chapter does not stand anywhere. It shows what he did.

Born as Amenhotep IV, probably the second son of Amenhotep III and Queen Tiye, he came to the throne around 1352 BCE. His early years show an escalating interest in the Aten cult that predated his reign: his father had named a royal barge "The Aten Gleams" and built a temple to the Aten at Karnak. The son took the inheritance and absolutized it. By regnal year 5, he had changed his name to Akhenaten, begun construction of a new capital, and set in motion the erasure of Amun's name from the monuments of the Two Lands.

The Amarna art depicts him with an elongated face, narrow eyes, a long jaw, thin chest, wide hips, and a swelling belly. Scholarly debate over whether this represents a medical condition, an artistic convention, or a theological statement has not reached consensus. What the art shows, read from inside the Atenist worldview, is the body of the sole mediator between the Aten and humanity rendered in a form that deliberately breaks with every convention of royal portraiture. The body is not human and not divine in the old sense. It is something else: the form that the Aten's agent takes, as understood by the new theology.

He reigned for approximately seventeen years. He died around 1336 BCE. The cause is not recorded. Within a generation, his name was hacked from monuments by the same methods he had used against Amun, and the city he built was abandoned, and the theology he proclaimed died with him because he had made himself its only point of contact with the divine.

## Nefertiti

She is one of the most represented and least understood figures in the ancient world. Her painted limestone bust, found in the workshop of the sculptor Thutmose at Akhetaten in 1912, is one of the most recognized objects in any museum. Her role in the Aten cult was, by the visual evidence, central: she is shown making offerings, driving chariots, performing ritual acts that no queen before her had performed. In the reliefs at the Aten temples, she appears nearly as often as the king.

The name Nefertiti means "The Beautiful One Has Come." She may have been the daughter of the courtier Ay, who would later become pharaoh himself after Tutankhamun's death. She bore Akhenaten six daughters, whose names are recorded and whose images appear in the family scenes that are among the most distinctive products of Amarna art.

Around regnal year 12, Nefertiti disappears from the record. The disappearance coincides roughly with the death of at least one of her daughters, Meketaten, whose burial is depicted in the royal tomb at Akhetaten. Whether Nefertiti died, was disgraced, assumed a co-regency under the name Neferneferuaten, or withdrew from public life is unknown. The silence is complete. The most visible woman in the Two Lands simply ceases to be visible, and no text explains why.

## Ay

A courtier of uncertain origin who rose to prominence at Akhetaten. His tomb at the new capital contains the most complete surviving text of the Great Hymn to the Aten. He bore the title "God's Father," which may indicate a familial relationship to the royal house, possibly as Nefertiti's father. After the Amarna period, he served as an advisor during Tutankhamun's reign and briefly assumed the throne himself after the boy king's death. He is a figure who moved through the revolution and the restoration with equal facility, which tells us something about the relationship between conviction and survival in the Egyptian court.

## Rib-Hadda of Byblos

He is not an Egyptian. He is a Levantine vassal ruler whose voice survives in the Amarna archive because he would not stop writing. Over sixty letters in Akkadian cuneiform, addressed to the pharaoh, document his city's progressive abandonment by the power that had guaranteed its security. He reports military threats, accuses neighboring rulers of treachery, pleads for troops, and receives, as far as the archive indicates, inadequate responses. His final letters describe his flight from Byblos and his appeal for asylum. Then the letters stop. Rib-Hadda disappears from the record, and what happened to the most prolific correspondent in the Amarna archive is not known.

His letters are evidence of what the Aten revolution cost at the edges of the Egyptian world: not a theological crisis but a security crisis, experienced by a loyal vassal who kept writing to a court that was looking at the sun.

## The Stonecutter

He is composite. No individual stonecutter at Karnak during the Amarna erasure campaign is named in the surviving record. But the chisel marks are real. The scars on the walls where Amun's names were hacked away are visible at Karnak today, and beside them, in many cases, the rougher restoration carvings where the names were cut back. Someone held those chisels. Someone struck those stones. The stonecutter in this chapter is a figure constructed from the archaeological evidence of the erasure campaign, from what is known about the organization of stonecutting crews in New Kingdom temple construction, and from the logic of the situation: a skilled worker whose entire professional life had been devoted to making permanent, ordered to unmake.

His thoughts are not recorded. His crisis is inferred from the evidence and from the theological logic that the chapter renders from inside. The text does not claim to know what he felt. It claims to know what he faced, because the chisel marks are on the stone.
