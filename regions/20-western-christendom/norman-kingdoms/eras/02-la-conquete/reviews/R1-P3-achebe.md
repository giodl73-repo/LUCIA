# Panel Review — R1-P3 (Chinua Achebe)
## La Conquête — Opening Review

**Reviewer:** P-3 Chinua Achebe
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

This is a story about power, told from inside power. That is, in itself, a legitimate choice — the conqueror's perspective is a perspective, and it can be rendered from within without becoming an endorsement. The question I bring to any such opening is whether the text knows it is inside one perspective, or whether it believes it is inside all perspectives simultaneously.

This opening knows. It stays with William. It renders his logic — his legitimacy obsession, his military calculation, his architectural grammar — from inside his worldview, without stepping back to offer a more authoritative or universal view. When the burning of Kent and Essex is described as "calculation," the text does not endorse or condemn it; it renders William's understanding of what he is doing. That is the right move.

### What the Opening Owns

**William's voice is his own.** The psychological portrait is built from inside Norman culture's own logic: a bastard duke who learned that legitimacy is manufactured, who arrived with papal blessing and a broken oath as his justification, who understands governance as architecture. This is not a modern psychological reading of a medieval figure. It is a medieval Norman reading of himself, rendered faithfully.

**The burning is rendered without verdict.** "The Normans understood the grammar of fire." This is presented as Norman knowledge, Norman method, not authorial approval or condemnation. The column of smoke as a sentence "every people in Europe could read" — this is how William saw it, and the text stays inside that seeing. Good.

**"England was still there. This was the problem he had not thought through."** These two sentences are the opening's best moment because they render the gap between military victory and political reality from entirely inside William's position. He has won. The thing he has won is complicated. No authorial comment is needed, and none is offered.

### What I Would Push On

**The English are absent from their own experience.** I understand this opening is William's. But by the end, England has been entirely rendered as a governance problem: two million people, institutions to be repurposed, territories to be subdued. The English do not appear as people here. They appear as the weight of a country that William must learn to carry.

This is, for an opening, acceptable — we are with William on the morning after, and his focus is what it is. But I want to name the risk clearly: the chapter cannot sustain this absence. The English who lost at Hastings were not a governance problem — they were people with their own logic of kingship, their own understanding of what they owed and were owed, their own name for what was happening to them. At least one English figure must appear in the full chapter as a person, not as a problem.

**The witan passage.** "England had a tradition of council and consent — the witan, the assembly of great men that had made and unmade kings." This sentence describes English institutions from outside English understanding. The witan is not "council and consent" — that is a modern or Norman-external description. The English understood the witan through their own categories: obligation, counsel, the right of great men to be heard. The chapter should be careful not to describe English institutions in terms that make them legible to modern readers at the cost of how the English themselves understood them.

**Whose loss?** The ravens and the bodies are registered as a military fact. There is no English grief visible. Again, this is William's morning, and William does not grieve his enemies. But the total absence of English subjectivity — even in passing, even in a detail — creates an opening that could be read as endorsing William's perspective rather than merely inhabiting it. A single detail that acknowledges the English dead as people (not just as bodies that make the horse uncomfortable) would anchor the opening's honesty without breaking William's perspective.

### The Question I Would Push On

Who tells the English side? The chapter is about Norman governance, and its protagonist is Norman. But the story of the Conquest is also the story of what it means to have your world reorganized by a ruling class that speaks a different language and builds a different architecture on top of your fields. The Domesday Book records English landholders who held their land in 1066 and no longer held it in 1086. Those men have names. The chapter should find at least one of them.

---

**Overall:** The voice is correctly placed inside William's worldview. The honesty discipline holds — no verdict, no romanticization of either side. The main task for the chapter is to find the English voice without abandoning the Norman center. A story told entirely from the perspective of those who won is history as the winners wrote it. LUCIA is better than that.
