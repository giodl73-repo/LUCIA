# Figures — Le Grand Redressement

---

## Alcuin of York (c. 735–804)

He arrived at Aachen in approximately 782 carrying two things that Charles
needed and could not find anywhere in the Frankish kingdom: the tradition of
Bede and the library of the school at York. The tradition of Bede meant a
particular habit of scholarly attention — the patience to read a text slowly,
to check it against other texts, to prefer the accurate and less elegant to
the beautiful and corrupt. The school at York meant access to books that had
survived in the north during the two centuries in which the continental
libraries had thinned to almost nothing.

Alcuin was fifty years old when he came south, past the age at which most
men of his era could expect to do new work. He did the most important work of
his life in his fifties and sixties. He was not a physical presence in the
way that Charles was — Einhard says nothing about his appearance, which
probably means he was ordinary. He was a presence through his voice, his
letters, and his corrections. He called himself "Flaccus," the nickname
Charles gave him after Horace — the poet who had also served in a court, who
had also found his vocation in making language precise and resonant
simultaneously.

The letters he wrote to Charles are some of the most revealing documents of
the age. They are not the letters of a courtier to a patron — they are the
letters of a friend who is also a critic, a scholar who is willing to tell
a king when the king is wrong. He was critical of the forced baptisms of
the Saxons, wrote that baptism without instruction was "washing a dog that
returns to its mud," argued for patience and persuasion over compulsion.
Charles did not always follow his advice. But he read the letters and he
kept them. That he kept them is evidence of something in the relationship
that was not merely administrative.

After leaving Aachen in 796 for the abbacy of Saint-Martin at Tours, he
directed the most productive scriptorium in the Frankish west. He died there
in 804, ten years before Charles. The manuscripts he had caused to be copied
outlasted them both.

*Source limitation note: Alcuin's interior life is accessible primarily
through his letters, which are numerous and carefully composed. They reveal
what he chose to reveal, which is considerable but shaped by rhetorical
purpose. The person behind the letters can be glimpsed but not fully known.*

## Charles / Charlemagne (742 or 747–814)

He is the hardest figure in the chapter to hold — not because the sources
are thin but because they are shaped by purposes that are not neutral. Einhard
wrote the *Vita Karoli Magni* twenty years after Charles's death, from the
perspective of a student who loved his teacher, a subject who had lived through
what came after and had reasons to make the reign look like a golden age. The
Royal Frankish Annals are the official court record, which means they tell
the story the court wanted told. What we have of Charles is real but shaped.

The physical Charles is recoverable through Einhard's details: tall (Einhard
says six feet, which was exceptional for the era), with a round but authoritative
face, a neck that was "short and rather thick," a voice that was "clear but
not quite what you would expect from his physique." He was an excellent
swimmer, which Aachen's thermal baths accommodated. He ate and drank
moderately for a Frankish king — Einhard notes this as a characteristic
because it was unusual. He hunted when he could. He loved the company of
educated men. He kept his books under his pillow and taught himself to write
late in life, practicing the letter-shapes that had been natural to Alcuin
since childhood, "but since he had begun these efforts so late in life, they
met with little success."

The detail about the books and the late learning is the most revealing thing
Einhard tells us. It is not presented as a failure. It is presented as a
characteristic of a man who understood that the reform program he was driving
required him to be, himself, reformed — who would not demand literacy of his
clergy without attempting it himself, however imperfectly. The attempt
mattered. The imperfection was acknowledged. This is not how a tyrant relates
to his own requirements.

He governed through presence. The annual assembly at which the Frankish
nobility gathered each spring — the *Campus Martius*, the Field of March —
was an exercise in personal authority: Charles meeting face to face with the
counts and bishops who administered the empire, hearing their reports and
complaints, issuing directives for the coming year. The capitularies went
out from these assemblies. The missi went out afterward to verify compliance.
The system was a machine for extending Charles's personal attention across
a territory too large for personal attention. When his personal attention ended,
the machine stopped.

*On the Verden massacre (782):* Charles ordered the execution of approximately
4,500 Saxon prisoners after the massacre at Süntel, where a Frankish force
was ambushed in what he read as a treacherous attack by subjects who had
sworn loyalty. The decision is documented in the Royal Frankish Annals and
in sources hostile to Charles (the Revised Annals quietly remove it). The
modern debate about what happened centers on whether the numbers are accurate
and on how to interpret the decision within the Carolingian theory of justice:
the Saxons had sworn loyalty, broken their oath, and killed Frankish soldiers
under conditions that the Frankish legal tradition treated as perfidy. Charles
applied the penalty that his tradition reserved for oath-breakers. Alcuin
argued this was excessive and counterproductive. Both men were reasoning from
within the same system of values; they disagreed about application. The chapter
does not add a modern verdict to this disagreement.*

## Theodulf of Orléans (c. 750–821)

He is the figure who carries the reform program outside the palace — the
bishop who went out as a missus and found the gap between the capitularies
and the actual world. His origins are disputed: he may have been born in
Visigothic Iberia or in Septimania, the southernmost Frankish territory. He
came to Aachen before 794 and became one of Charles's most trusted advisors.

He was a poet. His Latin is the most sophisticated produced in the Frankish
kingdom — technically accomplished in ways that required genuine familiarity
with Virgil and Ovid and the late Roman tradition he had encountered either
through Iberian manuscripts or through the palace school's collections. His
poem *Contra Iudices* is addressed directly to Charles: "King David, whose
care extends over everything, whose judgment is sought in all things, hear
what I observed in the regions committed to me." It then describes, with
precision and some controlled rage, what a Frankish bishop found when he
went into the provinces to inspect on the king's behalf.

He found corruption. He found it so embedded in local practice that it had
become invisible to those who practiced it — judges who accepted gifts (not
"bribes" by their own understanding, because gifts were the normal medium
of relationships) without feeling they were compromising their judgments,
abbots who managed their monasteries as estates without feeling they were
neglecting their monastic obligations. He was unable to correct most of it.
A bishop could report; he could not compel. The *missi* system required a
count's cooperation to function, and counts were not always cooperative.

Theodulf was also an architect. He designed and built the Oratory at Germigny-
des-Prés, which survives today as one of the few Carolingian buildings still
standing, its apse mosaic depicting the Ark of the Covenant in a Byzantine
style that shows how far the palace school's cultural horizons extended. The
building is a physical embodiment of the reform program: a bishop's private
chapel built with the theological precision of a formal doctrinal statement.
The iconography is careful and deliberate, chosen with reference to the
debates about image veneration that the palace school had been conducting.
Theodulf built his thoughts into the wall.

He was imprisoned after Charles's death, accused of supporting a rebellion
against Louis the Pious. He died in 821, in custody or shortly after his
release. The *Contra Iudices* survives. Germigny-des-Prés survives. What he
found in the provinces is what the chapter finds when it leaves Aachen.

## Einhard (c. 775–840)

He was not a major figure of Le Grand Redressement — he was too young for
that, arriving at the palace school sometime in the 790s as a boy from
Fulda. But he is the eye through which we see Charles most clearly, and
that gives him a function in the chapter's evidence base that is disproportionate
to his role in the events.

Einhard was small — contemporaries noted it, usually fondly, calling him
"Bezalel" after the craftsman who built the Ark of the Covenant, a reference
to his exceptional skill at mechanical arts as well as letters. He was trained
at the palace school in the tradition Alcuin had established. He was close
to Charles personally — close enough to be described by later sources as a
kind of adopted son, though the exact nature of the relationship is not
documentable beyond Einhard's own eventual marriage to Imma, who may have
been a member of the royal circle.

He wrote the *Vita Karoli Magni* in the 820s, after Charles's death, modeled
deliberately on Suetonius's *Lives of the Twelve Caesars* — a choice that was
itself a statement about the Carolingian reform: the Franks had absorbed the
Roman biographical tradition and were now using it to record their own history
in the same forms the Romans had used. The *Vita* is not neutral biography.
It is an argument that Charles was a great king who deserved to be remembered
as Rome remembered its greatest emperors. Einhard was right that Charles was
remarkable. His portrait should be read as the work of someone who believed
what he was writing, and whose belief was not uncritical.

*Source note: Einhard's portrait of Charles is vivid and internally consistent.
Where it can be checked against the capitularies, the annals, and the
archaeological record at Aachen, it holds up reasonably well. The details
that cannot be checked — the pillow-books, the private habits, the voice —
should be taken as plausible testimony from someone who was there, not as
certainty.*
