## B-2 — Hélène Sader — Board Review of Arameans Era 2

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Aramean archaeology, Iron Age Levantine material culture, comprehensive synthesis of Aramean state archaeology.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The description of Dammasq's geographic setting (Barada River, Ghouta oasis, Anti-Lebanon mountains) is accurate. The Ghouta's agricultural character (orchards, irrigated fields) is well-attested in both ancient sources and modern geography.
- The Tell es-Safi (Gath) destruction layer attributed to Hazael is correctly referenced. The excavations under Aren Maeir have confirmed a significant destruction event consistent with the late ninth century BCE, which correlates with the biblical account of Hazael's siege.
- The chariot force of Aram-Damascus is described in terms consistent with what we know of Iron Age chariot technology in the Levant. The iron fittings for chariots are archaeologically attested at Levantine sites.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's representation of the Aramean urban landscape is plausible but necessarily inferential for Dammasq itself, which has been continuously inhabited and has not been extensively excavated at Iron Age levels. The description should be understood as drawn from comparative evidence at other Aramean sites (Tell Halaf, Tell Afis, Gozan) rather than from excavation at Damascus.
- The camel caravan description is well-handled. The use of camels for long-distance transport in the late second and early first millennia BCE is increasingly supported by zooarchaeological evidence, though the dating of systematic camel caravanning remains debated. The chapter avoids overclaiming by stating the Arameans "had used the camel for caravan work since at least the late second millennium BCE," which is cautious.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The material culture descriptions (iron forges, chariot components, leather writing materials, carbon ink) are consistent with what we know of Iron Age Levantine technology.
- The description of the Tel Dan Stele as a physical object (basalt, carved letters, reused as building material) is accurate to the archaeological context.

**What needs correction:**
- The water-powered bellows at Dammasq's forges is speculative. Water-powered metallurgical equipment is not attested at this date in the Levant. Simple hand-bellows or foot-bellows are more likely. The forges could be located near the river for water access (quenching, cleaning) without requiring water power.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (one correction as noted above)
