# R2-P2 — Ibn Khaldun
## Panel Review — Notes, Round 2

**Chapter:** Yanomami — Era 1: The Parima World
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The production bible answers most of my questions from Round 1. The engine is correctly named: shamanic maintenance and diplomatic maintenance as a single system. The mechanism is clear: healing requires inter-community jurisdiction, which requires alliance, which requires feasting, which cycles back to the legitimacy that makes both healing and feasting possible. This is coherent and satisfying as a structural logic.

My remaining concern is about the terminal condition and what the notes call the "failure stakes." The notes say the chapter should include "at least one moment of alliance stress or shamanic failure." I want to make this more specific.

The Yanomami system, as I read it through the lens of patterns and cycles, contains a productive tension: the very decentralization that makes alliance necessary also makes it fragile. No central authority exists to enforce obligations. Every reahu is a voluntary act of demonstration. A community that cannot feast is a community that is becoming isolated — and isolation, in a world where healing requires inter-community shamanic cooperation, is a form of civilizational precarity.

The section plan's Section 4 (feast and alliance) should not simply show a feast working. It should show what happens when the feast's terms are contested — when the guest community receives what they consider insufficient generosity, when the host's demonstration falls short, when the obligation created by the feast is not honored. This does not require a dramatic rupture; a small tension, honestly rendered, will do more structural work than an explicit failure. The system is more vivid when its maintenance is shown to require constant effort, not when it is shown to simply function.

The non-chronological organizing principle (concentric circles, as the notes describe) is appropriate for this era and culture. But the notes should ensure that the concentric movement builds — that each circle out adds something that cannot be understood without the inner circle, and that the return to the community at the end carries the weight of what was learned in the outward journey. The circles are not just organizational structure; they are the chapter's argument.

I am satisfied with the source handling. Using Kopenawa as the interpretive frame while acknowledging his temporality is correct. The outsider-as-structural-evidence technique should be named explicitly in figures.md, not just in these notes.

One final observation: the notes describe a world "without significant external disruption" across 750 years. This framing risks making Era 1 feel static, a world preserved in amber before contact breaks it open. The chapter should convey that the Parima World was dynamic within its own terms — communities forming and dissolving, alliances shifting, shamanic knowledge growing and sometimes being lost to the deaths of individual shapori, territorial boundaries adjusting over centuries. The engine is continuous; the outputs are not fixed.

— IK
