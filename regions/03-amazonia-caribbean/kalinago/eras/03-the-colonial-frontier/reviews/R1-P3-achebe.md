# PANEL-1 Review — P-3 Chinua Achebe
## Kalinago, Era 3: The Colonial Frontier
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The opening passes the test I am most concerned with, which is: does this feel like someone else's story about the Kalinago, or does it feel like the Kalinago's story told from inside?

It feels from inside. And that is not easy to achieve for this culture, in this moment — because this is the contact moment, the moment where the two worlds meet, and the gravitational pull toward European-centric narration is enormous. Every historian who has written about 1493 has written about Columbus. This opening manages to make Columbus a peripheral figure — literally described through Kalinago perception, from a distance, from a canoe. That inversion is the most important craft decision in the piece, and it is the right one.

**What I will praise:**

The description of the Spanish strangers through Kalinago senses is honest and specific: "skin gone pale as gutted fish, hair on their faces where no man ought to have it." This is not exotic curiosity about Europeans. It is the precise observation of a people encountering something outside their category system — the same precise observation they would apply to any unusual arrival. It is inside-voice writing. The Kalinago are not amazed by the Europeans in the way that lesser writing would make them amazed; they are applying the same evaluative eye they apply to Taino raiders.

The passage describing the Spanish recorder — "he had a book in which he was recording what he saw, and what he saw, he had already decided, before he saw it" — is an example of what the rubric calls "Outsider's Bias as Inside Characterization." The bias reveals something about the recorder, not about the Kalinago. I want to see more of this in the chapter. Every Spanish, French, English, Dutch account of the Kalinago should be handled this way: the account tells us what kind of observer this person was. The Kalinago are not diminished by the account. The account is diminished by what it fails to see.

**What I would push on:**

One sentence approaches the edge: "This was not cruelty. This was how the calculus had always worked." This is the author explaining the Kalinago to the reader — stepping slightly outside the worldview to offer a clarification that the worldview itself would not need. Within the Kalinago frame, the calculus would not need to be defended or explained as "not cruelty." It would simply be what was done. The chapter should trust the scene to carry this without the editorial clarification. Show the calculus. Let the reader recognize it as rational. Do not explain that it was not cruel.

The name "Kalinago" versus the word "cannibal" — the opening handles this correctly by not handling it at all. The word does not appear. The chapter must maintain this. If the chapter ever needs to address the semantic appropriation of their name (which the OVERVIEW describes as one of the defining facts about this people), it should do so from inside — the Kalinago were aware they were being renamed, as the OVERVIEW notes. That awareness should be a Kalinago character's awareness, not an editorial note.

**The question I'd push on:** Who is doing the deciding? The opening refers to "the warriors," "the men of Karukera," "the women and children" — the community is present but unnamed. At this early stage, unnamed is fine. But the chapter needs at least one specific Kalinago figure who embodies a decision in this era. Not a symbolic figure. A person who was somewhere, who decided something, whose choice we can follow. The 00-OVERVIEW gives us no named figures for Era 3. The chapter will need to either find one in the historical record or construct a composite from what is known — and if composite, it must be disclosed honestly. This is the hardest problem the chapter faces.

---

*The voice is right. Protect it for the full 5,000 words. The chapter's greatest risk is not condescension but the creeping arrival of the explaining voice — the author's modern consciousness inserting itself in a paragraph that is otherwise inside. Watch every sentence where the text shifts to "this was" or "this meant" — those are the places where the outside lens arrives.*
