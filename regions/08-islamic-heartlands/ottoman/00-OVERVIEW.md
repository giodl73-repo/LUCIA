# Devlet-i ʿAliyye-yi ʿOsmāniyye — The Exalted Ottoman State

We began as a frontier principality — a Turkmen *beylik* on the northwestern edge of the former Seljuk world, organized by Osman Ghazi and his father Ertugrul to raid and then to govern the Byzantine borderlands of Bithynia. Within two centuries we held three continents. Within six centuries we had governed an empire that included Anatolia, the Balkans, Egypt, the Arab Levant, Iraq, North Africa from Egypt to Morocco's border, and the Caucasus. The *Devlet-i ʿAliyye* — the Exalted State — governed this diversity not by making everyone the same but by organizing difference: the *millet* system that allowed Christian and Jewish communities to govern their own religious affairs, the *devshirme* that recruited Christian boys for the Ottoman administration and army, the *kanun* (royal law) alongside the *sharia* (Islamic law), the pashas and viziers drawn from every corner of the empire's enormous ethnic mosaic.

The Sultan was the center. The Sultan was the shadow of God on earth, the caliph after 1517 CE when Selim I reportedly received the title from the last Abbasid shadow caliph in Cairo, the Khan of Khans to the Turkic tradition, the Sovereign of the Two Seas and the Two Continents. Around this center the Ottoman imperial system organized itself through an elaborate bureaucracy, a standing professional army (the Janissaries), a legal system that synthesized Islamic jurisprudence with customary Ottoman law, and a provincial administration that governed the empire's hundreds of sancaks with a regularity that impressed every observer who tried to understand how such a diverse empire maintained itself. The Ottoman empire endured for six centuries because it was genuinely well-organized, genuinely pluralistic within its hierarchy, and genuinely capable of adapting its forms to new realities — until the 19th century brought pressures it could adapt to only imperfectly.

## What Defined Them

**The Kanun alongside the Sharia.** Suleiman I was called "the Lawgiver" (*Kanuni*) by his own people — not for the Islamic *sharia* he upheld, but for the *kanun*, the royal legislative system through which the sultan organized the aspects of governance that Islamic jurisprudence did not cover or did not cover in sufficient detail. The *kanun* regulated taxation, criminal procedure, land tenure, the organization of the military and administrative classes, and the relations between different communities in the empire. It coexisted with the *sharia* — the two legal systems had different jurisdictions and different courts — in a synthesis that allowed the Ottoman empire to govern an enormous diversity of populations with a single coherent legal framework. Suleiman's *kanun* was compiled into a comprehensive code that served as the empire's constitutional foundation for centuries.

**The *devshirme* and the Ottoman meritocracy.** The *devshirme* — the periodic levy of Christian boys from the Balkan provinces — was the Ottoman empire's most characteristic institution of governance. The boys, typically chosen from the village populations of the European provinces, were taken to Istanbul, converted to Islam, educated in the palace schools, and assigned to the Janissary corps or the administrative service according to their abilities. The most capable could rise to any position in the empire, including grand vizier. The Ottoman grand viziers who governed the empire during its classical age were overwhelmingly of *devshirme* origin — Bosnians, Albanians, Greeks, Serbs — who had risen through the palace system and owed their entire identity and position to the empire rather than to any family or tribe. This deliberate meritocracy, combined with the prohibition on *devshirme* men founding dynasties, gave the Ottoman administrative system a professional quality that hereditary aristocracies rarely achieve.

**The *millet* and the organization of diversity.** The Ottoman *millet* system — by which non-Muslim communities (Greeks, Armenians, Jews) governed their own religious, legal, and educational affairs through their community institutions — was the Ottoman solution to the problem of governing a genuinely diverse empire. The Greek Orthodox patriarch in Istanbul was the head of the Greek *millet*, responsible for Greek community affairs throughout the empire. The Armenian patriarch governed his community. Jewish rabbinical courts governed Jewish affairs. Each community paid its taxes to the Ottoman state, served in non-military capacities, and governed itself in everything that did not touch the Ottoman state's core prerogatives. This was not equality — the *millet* communities were *dhimmis*, protected but subordinate — but it was a system of organized pluralism that allowed enormous diversity to coexist.

**Istanbul and the architecture of empire.** The Ottoman imperial building program — concentrated above all in Istanbul but extending to every province — was the most sustained architectural achievement of the Islamic world. Sinan, the master architect who served Suleiman and his successors, built or supervised over three hundred structures and culminated his career with the Selimiye Mosque at Edirne, which he considered his masterwork. The mosques, hans, madrasas, hammams, and palace complexes that filled Istanbul were not simply religious buildings. They were the material declaration of Ottoman power — that the empire which had taken Constantinople had rebuilt it as an Islamic city, that the dome of the Hagia Sophia (converted to a mosque) had been answered by the Blue Mosque's six minarets across the square, that the empire's capital was the center of a new world order.

**The long decline and the nationalist challenge.** The Ottoman empire's 19th-century crisis was not simply military or economic — it was a challenge to the imperial idea itself. The *millet* communities that had governed themselves under Ottoman suzerainty began to formulate nationalist identities that required their own states rather than their own communities within a larger empire. The Greek revolt of the 1820s, the Bulgarian agitation of the 1870s, the Armenian question, the Arab nationalist movements — all of these were the Ottoman *millet* system's communities choosing the national form over the imperial form. The empire responded with the Tanzimat reforms that offered equal Ottoman citizenship regardless of religion — essentially dissolving the *millet* system into a single Ottoman identity — but the reformist vision could not outrun the nationalist movements fast enough. The empire that had organized diversity for six centuries found that the 19th century had invented a new organizing principle for human community that it could not accommodate.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Founding:** Osman and his warriors raid the Byzantine borderlands and organize the territory they take. The *gazi* ideology — the frontier warrior fighting for Islam against the Christian world — gives the early Ottoman movement its legitimacy and its energy. A century after Osman, his great-great-grandson Mehmed II is planning the siege of Constantinople.

**2 — Constantinople 1453:** Mehmed II's great bronze cannon have been casting against the Theodosian Walls for fifty-three days. The last Byzantine emperor Constantine XI dies fighting in the breach. Mehmed rides to the Hagia Sophia and orders the adhan called from its gallery. The city that called itself the center of the Christian Roman world is now the center of the Ottoman Islamic world.

**3 — Suleiman the Lawgiver:** Suleiman's court in Topkapi is organizing the empire's legal system while his army is in Hungary. The *kanun* is being compiled. Sinan is building mosques. The Ottoman empire is, by any measure, the most powerful and best-organized state in the world. Vienna does not fall in 1529 — the supply lines are too long — but the failure is tactical, not fundamental.

**4 — The Long 19th Century:** The Tanzimat decree promises equality for all Ottoman subjects regardless of religion. The grand vizier who signs it understands that this is an attempt to forestall the nationalist movements by offering them citizenship rather than independence. Whether it can work depends on whether the European powers will allow it to work, and whether they have any interest in an Ottoman empire that survives on reformed terms.

**5 — The End:** The World War has been lost. Mustafa Kemal is organizing the resistance in Anatolia, repudiating the Ottoman government's armistice, and building a new political entity — not an empire, not a caliphate, but a nation. The last Ottoman sultan Mehmed VI boards a British vessel in November 1922. The sultanate is abolished. The caliphal title will be abolished two years later. The Exalted Ottoman State, which governed three continents for six centuries, ends at a harbor in Istanbul.
