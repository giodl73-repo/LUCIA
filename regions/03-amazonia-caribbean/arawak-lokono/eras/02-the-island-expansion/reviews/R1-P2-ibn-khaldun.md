# Panel Review — Round 1
## P-2 Ibn Khaldun | Opening Review

**Era:** The Island Expansion | **Culture:** Arawak / Lokono
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I have spent considerable time thinking about the mechanisms by which peoples expand — through conquest, through pastoralism following the grass, through the pressures of population on a fixed territory. What the Lokono present, as introduced in this opening, is a fourth mechanism that I find theoretically interesting: expansion through the voluntary extension of an obligation network. This is not random drift. It is structured. The network logic determines who goes, why they go, and how they remain connected to the place they left. The opening makes this mechanism visible in miniature through Kurima's crossing.

The critical insight — "obligation was not a burden. It was what made you real in other people's worlds" — is theoretically precise. It identifies the Lokono's social physics accurately. What holds the network together is not hierarchy or kinship in the narrowly genealogical sense, but the circulation of obligation: you owe labor to your mother's brother, river rights to the eastern family, cassava bread to the downstream processing community. These obligations require maintenance. Maintenance requires contact. Contact is the canoe. The canoe is therefore not merely a technology of transportation but of social reproduction. The author has understood this correctly.

**My concern:** The opening presents the network's mechanism without yet showing its limits or its internal tensions. Every social system I have studied carries within it the forces that will eventually stress it. For the Lokono network, the question I would push is: what happens when a node becomes too strong? When an island family grows prosperous enough that it can receive without sending? The opening frames the network as elegantly self-sustaining, but the chapter will need to show what maintains the reciprocity norm against free-riding. Obligation without enforcement is a social fiction. What is the Lokono enforcement mechanism?

**The question I'd push on:** The opening says the network grew through "accumulated decisions." But who decided which decisions were legitimate? The uncle who had made the crossing twice — is he a recognized authority? Is there a council? An elder who validates the network's new nodes? The opening leaves the governance mechanism of expansion entirely to individual initiative, which is either historically accurate (a genuinely leaderless network) or an oversimplification that the chapter needs to address. I want to know where the asabu — the group solidarity — is located in the Lokono system.
