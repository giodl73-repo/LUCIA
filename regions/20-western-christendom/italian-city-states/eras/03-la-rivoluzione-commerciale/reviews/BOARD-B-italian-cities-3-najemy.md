# BOARD Review — John M. Najemy
## La Rivoluzione Commerciale

**Date:** 2026-04-17
**Reviewer role:** Florentine political and institutional history

---

The Florentine sections are accurate and the analysis of the guild system as a political institution is one of the most concise correct statements of this I have read. The chapter understands what is often missed: that the guild in Florence was not merely a trade association but a political constituency, that commercial standing and political standing were inseparable, that the Arte di Calimala's quality regulations were enforced by the commune's courts, not merely by the guild's internal authority.

**Corrections required:**

1. **The *priori* term and the two-month rotation.** The chapter states the *priori* "served for sixty days and were replaced." The office of the Signoria (with six priors) was established in 1282, not at the beginning of the era. Before 1282, Florentine governance was organized differently, with *consoli* and a more fluid arrangement. The chapter's description of the sixty-day rotation is accurate for the period after 1282 but is presented as if it characterized the entire 1200-1350 era. **Recommendation:** Specify "from 1282, the six *priori*..." to avoid implying the rotation system was in place at the beginning of the era. P2 correction.

2. **The guild count.** The chapter describes "Seven *Arti Maggiori*" and "Fourteen *Arti Minori*." The seven major guilds are correctly listed. However, the number of minor guilds varied across the period — there were fourteen after the reorganization of 1266, but the count shifted several times in the 13th and early 14th centuries. The "fourteen" figure is conventional and not wrong, but it should be understood as approximate. **Recommendation:** Add "approximately" before "fourteen" or add a note that the count varied. P3.

3. **The *podestà* and factional violence.** The chapter states the podestà was imported "precisely because a local man would inevitably favor his own faction." This is the textbook explanation, and it is correct, but the chapter does not acknowledge that the podestà system was also chronically dysfunctional: the podestà's authority was limited by the commune's councils, the rotating magistracies created incentives for short-termism, and factional violence continued throughout the era despite the podestà's presence. The chapter's current framing implies the podestà solved the factional problem, when in fact it managed it imperfectly. **Recommendation:** Add one sentence acknowledging the system's limits: "The podestà managed factionalism more than it resolved it; the same families returned, in different configurations, to contest the same spaces." P3.

**What the chapter does well:**

The guild oath passage is excellent. The layered obligation structure, the guild to the member to the commune to the city, is a formulation I would use in teaching. It captures the commune's institutional logic more clearly than most academic prose.

The Arte di Calimala's regulations as "a manufacturing protocol" is the right analogy. The guild's quality enforcement was not advisory — it was backed by exclusion from the market, which was the same as exclusion from civic life. The chapter grasps this.

The "remarkable sophistication" pitfall is successfully avoided. The chapter describes the commune's impartiality invention without marveling at it. It presents the solution as the practitioners understood it: a practical response to a practical problem.

**Recommendation:** APPROVE with one P2 correction (priori chronology) and two P3 notes. The Florentine sections are historically accurate and analytically strong.
