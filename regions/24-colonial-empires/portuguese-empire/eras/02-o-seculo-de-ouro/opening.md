# O Século de Ouro — The Golden Century

## Opening

The smell of Calicut was wrong.

Vasco da Gama had expected spice — nutmeg, cinnamon, pepper, the goods that had made the Venetian and Genoese merchants rich for two centuries by controlling the overland routes his ships were now circumventing. He had not expected the smell of a living city: cooking fires, salt fish, the particular damp of monsoon air that had never touched the Tagus, the incense from temples his confessor refused to enter. He had not expected the crowd that gathered at the waterfront to look at his ships with the calm assessment of people who had been trading with strangers for longer than Portugal had existed as a kingdom.

Calicut, in the year 1498 of the Lord, was everything Lisbon was not. A city of perhaps 100,000. A harbor where Chinese junks, Arab dhows, Gujarati merchants, and Malay traders shared the same anchorage without incident, because the Zamorin — the king of Calicut — maintained the conditions of trade as a function of royal power. Every ship paid its customs. Every merchant knew the weights and measures. The system had worked for centuries without Portuguese ships, and it would continue to work after them, or around them, depending on what they chose to do.

Da Gama chose. He was not a diplomat. He had crossed 12,000 miles of ocean in ten months, arriving with letters from King Manuel of Portugal and gifts that the Zamorin's officials examined with polite incredulity: striped cloth, sugar, oil, honey. The Zamorin's court had been receiving gifts for generations from Arab merchants who came with gold and silver, with fine cloth from Egypt, with the accumulated wealth of a trade network that moved across the entire Indian Ocean world. Da Gama's gifts were not adequate. The Zamorin's chamberlain said so, directly. What da Gama carried was the letter of an obscure Atlantic king who did not yet understand the world he had entered.

But da Gama had the ships. That was the thing, in the end, the only thing that mattered in the logic of what Portugal was building. Not the letters. Not the gifts. Not the Crusader cross painted on the sails, which the Hindus of Calicut regarded with curiosity and the Muslims of Calicut regarded with the specific wariness of people who had heard what the cross meant in Spain and North Africa. The ships themselves: armed, maneuverable, capable of returning, capable of bringing more. The Portuguese could not conquer Calicut. They could not administer it. They were a handful of sailors in a port city of 100,000. What they could do — what they would spend the next eighty years doing — was station themselves at every point along the route that connected Calicut to the Mediterranean, and charge.

This was the feitoria. Not a territory. A point on a network.

The men who built it understood saudade before the word was carved into the culture. Saudade: the Portuguese feel for absence, for the thing loved that is not here, for the harbor that cannot be seen from the open water. The sailors who left the Tagus in 1497 with da Gama knew they would be gone for a year, perhaps two. They knew men who had not come back from the previous expeditions. They had wives, children, debts, olive trees, the familiar smell of the Alfama at low tide. They went anyway, because the Crown had commanded it and the Church had blessed it and the promise of fortune or death at sea was still more vivid than the grey certainty of poverty at home. Saudade is not only longing. It is the state of the one who remains and the one who goes, both feeling the pull of what they cannot have.

Da Gama returned to Lisbon in September 1499 with 55 of the 170 men who had sailed with him. The rest were dead: storm, scurvy, violence, the fevers of the equatorial coast. He returned with enough pepper and cinnamon to generate profit estimated at sixty times the cost of the expedition. King Manuel, who had called himself Lord of Guinea and of the Conquest of Navigation and Commerce of Ethiopia, Arabia, Persia, and India since the expedition departed, received the returning ships with processions. Da Gama received wealth, the title Admiral of the Indian Seas, and the instruction to go back.

He went back.

This was the logic of the Golden Century: the route, once established, required constant reinstatement. Each feitoria had to be defended. Each chokepoint — Hormuz at the Persian Gulf's mouth, Goa on the Malabar coast, Malacca at the South China Sea's gate — had to be held against the Arab and Gujarati and Malay merchants who had controlled those routes for generations and were not willing to pay Portuguese customs duties without resistance. The empire was a maintenance problem as much as a conquest. What had been taken could be lost. What had not yet been taken could still be taken by someone else.

Afonso de Albuquerque, who would take Goa in 1510 and Malacca in 1511, understood this clearly. He was not building a province. He was building a chain. A network of fortified trading posts, each within range of the next, each controlling a passage that the spice trade had to use. Cut the passage, take the toll. He told the King of Portugal exactly what he was doing: "Whoever is lord of Malacca has his hand on the throat of Venice." It was a commercial strategy described in the language of violence, which was an accurate description of what the Estado da Índia was.

From Sagres the ships had left. To Calicut they had come. And now the empire that had begun as Crusade and hypothesis was becoming something neither the Crusaders nor the hypotheses had anticipated: the first genuinely global commercial network, organized around control of transit points on a trade system that stretched from Lisbon to Macau, across forty-five degrees of latitude and half the circumference of the earth.

The sailor who left the Tagus in 1500 did not return for five years, or ever.

The empire was what remained.
