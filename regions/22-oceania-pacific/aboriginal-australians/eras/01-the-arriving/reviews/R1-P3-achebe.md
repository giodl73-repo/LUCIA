# P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of "When the Ancestors Walked" Opening

**Overall:** The voice is right. The country is the protagonist. The Dreaming is presented as reality, not as belief. This is the correct starting point.

**What works:**
- "The song was not about the country. The song was the country." This is the sentence that establishes the chapter's authority. It does not explain the Tjukurpa from outside. It states it as fact. The reader is inside.
- The Tjukurpa is not glossed as "mythology" or "spiritual belief" — it is presented as the operating system of reality. "The Tjukurpa was not the past. It was the ground beneath the present, holding everything up." This is how the people of this country understand it. The text matches their understanding.
- "He pointed with his lips, the way you point in this country." This is a small detail that does enormous work. It marks the opening as belonging to this world, not describing it from outside. The phrase "the way you point in this country" places the reader inside the convention.
- The absence of the word "believe" anywhere in the opening. People do not "believe" in the Tjukurpa. They live in it. The opening never suggests otherwise.

**What doesn't:**
- The penultimate paragraph introduces "we" — "what they built here, what they encoded in song and ceremony... we cannot know in full." The "we" creates an observer position. The San chapter's handling of this was more careful: it placed the uncertainty inside the tradition's own silence rather than in an external narrator's limitation. The Aboriginal chapter should find a way to acknowledge deep-time uncertainty from inside the culture's own epistemology. The Dreaming is not time-bound; perhaps the uncertainty can be framed in the Dreaming's own terms rather than in the archaeological record's terms.
- The word "continent" appears twice. Both times it risks pulling the reader to a map-view, which is an outside perspective. The people of this country do not think in terms of "a continent." They think in terms of Country, specific and named. Consider whether the chapter can avoid the cartographic gaze.

**The question I'd push on:**
Whose voice is telling this story? At its best — the first six paragraphs — the voice belongs to Country. At its weakest — the penultimate paragraph — the voice belongs to a narrator standing outside, looking back across deep time with archaeological uncertainty. The full chapter must resolve this tension. The San chapter resolved it through /Kabbo's own words. What is this chapter's equivalent?
