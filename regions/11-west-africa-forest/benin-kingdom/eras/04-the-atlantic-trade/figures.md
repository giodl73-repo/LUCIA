# Figures -- The Palace's Memory

## Osemwede (composite)

He is a bronze-caster on Igun Street, pressing wax into the form that will become a plaque for the Oba's palace. His name is Edo, conventional for the period, and his actions are drawn from the documented practices of the Igun Eronmwon guild as recorded by Paula Girshick Ben-Amos, Barbara Plankensteiner, and from the surviving plaques themselves, which are evidence of the hands that made them.

This figure is composite. No single caster from the sixteenth or seventeenth century can be identified by name in the surviving record, because the guild tradition emphasized the guild's continuity over the individual craftsman's reputation. The Oba commissioned; the guild cast. The name on the record, if there was one, was the guild head's, not the individual caster's. This anonymity is not a gap in the record. It is the guild's own understanding of its work: the tradition matters more than the individual hand. Osemwede is the tradition made visible.

What the chapter gives him is a body and a practice: hands that press wax, fingers blackened with residue, eyes that judge the detail of a plaque against the standard the guild demands. His physical actions are reconstructed from the lost-wax casting process as documented ethnographically in the Igun Eronmwon workshops that still operate on Igun Street today. The workshops use the same techniques. The wax is the same. The clay is the same. The fire is the same. What Osemwede does in the chapter, his successors do now.

## The Oba (the office, multiple holders)

The chapter treats the Oba primarily as an office rather than as individual rulers, because the plaque tradition served the dynasty, not individual kings. The era spans approximately 1550-1700, a period that includes Oba Orhogbua (who expanded Benin's territory southward and engaged extensively with Portuguese trade), Oba Ehengbuda (whose reign saw continued Portuguese contact), and their successors.

The decision not to center an individual Oba is deliberate. The plaques were commissioned by the office. The ancestor altars honored the line. The guild's relationship was with the throne, not with the man who sat on it. The individual Obas of this period are less fully documented in the surviving record than, say, Oba Ewuare of the preceding era, and to invent interiority for them would cross the line between composite reconstruction and fiction. What the chapter gives the Oba is the office's function: the Oba commissions, authorizes, receives, governs, and maintains the relationship with the ancestors that authorizes everything else.

Where specific Obas are documented performing specific acts (sending the 1514 embassy, managing the Portuguese trade, incorporating firearms), the chapter attributes those acts to the Oba but does not invent the private thoughts or motivations behind them. The public record is sufficient. The Oba's private life was, by the court's own logic, not public knowledge, and the chapter respects that boundary.

## Duarte Pacheco Pereira (historical)

A Portuguese captain and navigator who sailed the West African coast in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries. His Esmeraldo de Situ Orbis (c. 1506) is among the earliest European descriptions of the Benin Kingdom. He described the Oba's military strength, the city's organization, the trade in pepper and ivory, and the physical appearance of the capital.

Pereira is used in the chapter not as an authority on Benin but as evidence of Portuguese perception. His account is specific and observant. It is also framed entirely by commercial and military interest. He described what Portugal needed to know about Benin to trade with it effectively. The kingdom's own self-understanding is absent from his account, because he did not seek it.

His description of the Oba's ability to field twenty thousand warriors in a day is quoted directly. His description of the city's houses as "large and well-kept" and "washed and polished" is also quoted. In both cases, the quotation marks signal that these are his words, his frame, his surprise. The chapter does not share the surprise. It uses the surprise as evidence about Pereira.

## Acting Consul General James Phillips (historical)

The British official who led the unauthorized party toward Benin City in January 1897, resulting in the ambush that the British used as the justification for the punitive expedition. Phillips had proposed the expedition to the Foreign Office and been told to wait. He did not wait. His party was attacked on the road to Benin City, and Phillips was killed.

The chapter presents Phillips briefly and without characterization beyond what his actions reveal. He went where he was told not to go, by both his own government and the Oba's court. The consequences of his decision shaped the next century of Benin's relationship with Britain and the wider world. The chapter does not speculate on his motivations. It records what he did and what followed.

## Oba Ovonramwen (historical)

The Oba of Benin at the time of the 1897 expedition. He fled the city before the British force arrived and was later captured and exiled to Calabar, where he died in 1914. The chapter mentions him briefly in the context of the expedition. He is not a developed character because the chapter's focus in Section VI is on the archive's displacement, not on the Oba's personal story. His exile and death are facts the chapter records. The reader may judge what they mean.

## The Guild (Igun Eronmwon, collective)

The guild is a character in this chapter in the same way that the Kurukan Fuga is a character in the Mali chapters: an institution with its own logic, persistence, and structural role. The Igun Eronmwon appear in the opening (Osemwede's workshop), in Section IV (the guild system at its height), and in the closing (the guild's survival). Their persistence is the chapter's landing: the plaques were taken, but the hands that made them were not.

The guild's historical continuity is documented. The Igun Eronmwon still work on Igun Street in Benin City. The lost-wax technique is still practiced. The guild head still holds a recognized title. The chain of transmission from master to apprentice remains unbroken. This is not a metaphor for resilience. It is a fact about an institution that has outlasted every political change the kingdom has experienced, including the colonial period and Nigerian independence, by carrying its knowledge in human hands rather than in objects that can be removed.
