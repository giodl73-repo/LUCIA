# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Targeted Review of Notes

**Chapter:** Songhai Empire / Era 2 — The River War
**Round:** 2 (Notes)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

The notes have identified Section II (The River's People) as the place where the Songhai cosmological relationship with the river must live. Good. But I want to push on what "sacred" means here, because it is easy to say "the river was sacred" and leave it at that, which is exactly the kind of vague reverence that substitutes atmosphere for understanding.

The Sorko were not just fishermen who happened to be useful to Sunni Ali. They were ritual specialists of the river. In the Songhai spiritual system, the river had spirits (the harey, the water spirits), and the Sorko were intermediaries between the human community and the river's own life. The Sorko mastery of the river was simultaneously practical (they knew the currents, they built the boats) and spiritual (they understood the river as a being with moods, with preferences, with demands). This is not decoration. It is the foundation on which the navy's cultural legitimacy rested. Sunni Ali's ability to command the river was, in Songhai terms, also a spiritual capacity.

The notes identify the tension between Sunni Ali and the Islamic scholars. The notes correctly frame this as structural (two forms of legitimacy). But the notes should also recognize that Sunni Ali's legitimacy drew on the older Songhai religious tradition, not only on military conquest. The scholars dismissed this as paganism. From inside the Songhai worldview, it was the older authority, the authority of the people who had known the river before Islam arrived on the caravans. The chapter must not adjudicate between these claims. It must render both.

**Specific recommendation for Section II:** Show the Sorko's river knowledge as simultaneously practical and spiritual. The current-reading, the boat-building, the seasonal flood knowledge are not separate from the ritual practice. They are the same thing. A Sorko who reads the current is reading the river's mood. A Sorko who builds a canoe is negotiating with the wood and the water simultaneously. This integration is the culture's own epistemology. Do not separate the "practical" from the "spiritual" because the Songhai did not.
