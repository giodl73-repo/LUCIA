The notes have answered my central question: Section 2 identifies the structural conditions — finished Reconquista, Aviz dynasty's legitimacy needs, English alliance, Order of Christ revenue. This is the right analysis. Portugal's exploration program was structurally overdetermined: the conditions that made it possible and necessary were all present simultaneously, which is why it happened when it did and not before.

I am satisfied with the engine naming: caravel-as-material-mechanism, the volta do mar as the method, knowledge accumulation as the logic. The terminal condition — necessary incompleteness, the program outliving its founder but not his method — is both true to the history and structurally interesting. It is not the death of a leader that ends an era; it is the handoff of a self-sustaining system. That is a terminal condition I have not seen in the manuscript before.

**Remaining concern:** The notes propose that Section 4 engages the slave trade economically — "the economy of the program and how it sustained itself." I need to see this carried through with precision. The 1441–1460 period produced revenue from three sources: (1) enslaved Africans sold in Lisbon; (2) gold and ivory traded from the coast; (3) licenses issued by the Crown to merchants who wanted access to the Guinea trade. The chapter should quantify at least one of these — not as a modern economic analysis but as a contemporary Portuguese understanding of what the program was producing. If Henry or his chroniclers recorded revenues, those numbers should appear.

The reason this matters structurally: the slave trade was not a side effect of the exploration program. It was the program's economic foundation during this era. Without the Lagos market and the Guinea trade, the program does not have the revenue to sustain the further expeditions. The causal chain runs: exploration produces trade; trade produces revenue; revenue funds more exploration. The chapter must show this chain explicitly, not just note the moral dimension.

**Terminal condition — a refinement:** The notes say the terminal condition is "necessary incompleteness." I would characterize it more precisely: the terminal condition of this era is that the program has achieved financial self-sufficiency before it has achieved its navigational goal. Henry dies, but the program does not need Henry anymore — it has generated enough institutional momentum, trade revenue, and trained personnel to continue without him. This is not incompleteness; it is the graduation of the program from founder-dependence to institutional independence. That is a specific and interesting terminal condition.

One question: what is the program's relationship to the Crown under Afonso V (Henry's nephew, who reigned 1438–1481)? Henry operated with considerable autonomy. After his death, did the program come more directly under royal control? This would be worth a sentence in Section 6.

The notes are solid. Proceed.
