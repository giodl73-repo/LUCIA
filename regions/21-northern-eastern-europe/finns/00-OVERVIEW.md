# Suomalaiset — "The Finns"

We are the people of the forest and the lake. The interior of the Finnish peninsula is a landscape of water and birch and pine — ten thousand lakes connected by rivers and portages, the forest pressing close on every side, the winter long and dark and absolute. Our world was organized not around cities or courts but around the *kylä* — the village community — and the accumulated knowledge of how to live in a landscape that demands competence from every person it supports. The fur trade, the salmon runs, the iron-smelt in the forest clearing, the boat carved from a single tree trunk: these were the technologies of Finnish life, and the people who mastered them were the people who survived.

The cosmology we carried for thousands of years before Christianity arrived is preserved in the *Kalevala* tradition — not as a written text until Elias Lönnrot compiled it in the nineteenth century, but as a vast body of oral song, runo-poetry chanted in the alliterative meter that is the oldest form of Finnish verse. Väinämöinen, the aged sage who shaped the world with his singing; Louhi, the powerful mistress of the North Farm whose magic is darker and stronger than the heroes expect; the Sampo, the mysterious object of power that everyone wants and no one can fully possess: these figures carry a cosmological vision in which the world is made and unmade through words, through song, through the contest of different kinds of knowledge. This is not primitive mythology. It is a sophisticated intellectual tradition that sustained a people through long winters without a king.

## What Defined Them

**The runo-song and the ancient cosmology.** Finnish oral poetry — the *kalevalaic* runo-singing tradition preserved especially in Karelia — represents one of the most complete survivals of pre-Christian cosmology in northern Europe. The runo-singers (usually male in the Karelian tradition) chanted long poetic narratives in an alliterative eight-syllable meter, often with a response-singer who repeated each line. The cosmology they preserved was animistic: the world was full of beings with their own *väki* (power or spirit), and the skilled person could negotiate with these beings through the right words. The *tietäjä* (knower, wise person) was the practitioner who knew the right words — for healing, for protection, for finding game. This tradition survived alongside Christianity for centuries, not as an alternative to it but as a parallel knowledge system operating in different domains.

**The contested borderland — Sweden or Novgorod?** Finland in the medieval period was not simply a Swedish possession; it was a contested frontier between two expanding powers — the Swedish Catholic sphere from the west and the Novgorodian Orthodox sphere from the east. The Peace of Nöteborg in 1323 drew a formal boundary across the peninsula, but the line was always porous, and the question of which cultural-religious sphere a given community belonged to was answered differently in different generations and different locations. Karelians east of the line developed a hybrid culture — Christian Orthodox, Finnish-speaking, trading with both Novgorod and the west. The Karelian runo-singing tradition is partly a product of this border existence: a culture that was between worlds developed an extraordinarily rich interior world.

**The forest economy.** Finnish subsistence was organized around the forest in ways that the agricultural economies of the south could not easily understand or administer. The slash-and-burn (*kaskiviljely*) cultivation system — clearing forest, burning it, farming the ash-enriched soil for two or three years, then moving on — required access to large areas of forest and produced yields that were adequate for small mobile populations but resisted the fixed-field agriculture that Swedish and German administrators tried to impose. The fur trade — beaver, squirrel, marten, ermine — was Finland's primary export commodity and the reason Novgorod and Sweden both wanted access to Finnish territory. Finnish hunters were the most skilled fur trappers in the Baltic region.

**Agricola and the Finnish language.** Mikael Agricola (c. 1510–1557), a Finnish-born scholar educated in Wittenberg under Luther's influence, created the first substantial corpus of written Finnish. His *ABC-kirja* (primer), his translation of the New Testament (1548), and his prayer book were not simply religious texts — they were the founding acts of Finnish as a written language. Agricola worked from the spoken dialect of southwestern Finland, making choices about orthography and vocabulary that shaped Finnish writing for centuries. The creation of written Finnish was simultaneously a religious project (the Reformation principle that people should read Scripture in their own language) and a cultural one: it made Finnish a language that could be read, and therefore a language that survived.

**The Club War — the voice from below.** The Finnish peasant uprising of 1596–1597 — the *nuijasota* or Club War, named for the agricultural tools the peasants used as weapons — was the most significant social revolt in Finnish history before the twentieth century. It arose from the Finnish peasantry's refusal to continue provisioning the Swedish military during the long war with Russia, and it demonstrated that the peasants of the Finnish interior had a sense of their own rights and limits that they were willing to defend with their bodies. The uprising was crushed brutally by the Swedish noble commander Klaus Fleming. But its memory — of peasants who rose — survived in Finnish cultural consciousness.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Ancient Time:** A Finnish hunter in the birch forest of Häme sets a snare for a beaver. He knows the names of the forest beings who control the game, and he addresses the *metsänhaltija* (forest spirit) before he sets the snare, asking permission. The Christianity that is coming will not quite replace this courtesy. It will sit beside it.

**2 — The Crusade Age:** The Swedish bishop arrives at the Finnish coast in a ship with armed escorts. He has come to baptize. The Finnish communities who meet him are calculating: the Novgorodians from the east and the Swedes from the west both want them, and the side that can offer more protection from the other is the side worth baptizing for. The bishop does not know he is being evaluated.

**3 — The Early Swedish Period:** A Finnish farmer in Häme receives orders from the Swedish castle to contribute grain and labor to the garrison. He adds these to the list of obligations that were not here before the Swedes came. He fulfills them because the alternative is worse. This arithmetic of submission and survival runs through Finnish history for five hundred years.

**4 — The Flowering of the Runo Song:** Two runo-singers in a Karelian village sing the Väinämöinen cycle, one leading and one responding. They have been singing since evening; it is now well past midnight. The fire has burned low. They continue. The song carries a world — a cosmology, a legal order, a set of relationships between humans and the non-human — that the Church has been trying to replace for three hundred years. It is still here.

**5 — The Club War and the Reformation:** Mikael Agricola finishes his translation of the New Testament and considers the Finnish words he has chosen. Some of them are new — words he has had to invent because Finnish has no equivalent for the Greek *logos*, the Latin *sacramentum*. In making these choices, he is also making Finnish into a language that can carry Christian theology. He is making Finnish into something it was not before. He is not troubled by this. He is Finnish himself.
