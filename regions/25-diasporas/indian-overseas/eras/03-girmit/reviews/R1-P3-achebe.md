## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Indian Overseas Era 3 Opening

**Overall:** The story belongs to the girmitiya. The arkatti is the mechanism, the colonial system is the context, but the family is the center. This is correct. The question is whether the full chapter sustains this ownership or lets the system become the protagonist.

**What works:**
- Dulari singing the Sundarkand. This is the moment where the culture speaks for itself. She does not carry a "cultural artifact." She carries a knowledge held in her body, and the text says so: "not a bound book but a knowledge held in the body." This is the difference between describing a culture and letting a culture act. Dulari acts. She sings. The Sundarkand travels because she sings it.
- The brass lota, the Hanuman murti. These are not symbols. They are what a woman packs when she is leaving everything. The specificity is the respect.
- "The people of the girmit, the people of the agreement, the people bound by a word." This naming is from within. The girmitiya named themselves. The text uses their name.
- The indenture records' erasure of the children's names ("whose names the indenture records do not preserve") is handled with the right weight. It does not lament. It states. The gap is the indictment.

**What doesn't:**
- Ramnaresh is presented primarily through his poverty. He sits in a doorway of a house that is not his. He owns what he can carry. He cannot feed his children. This is accurate, but if the chapter does not show him as more than his poverty, the girmitiya will be defined by their suffering rather than their agency. The arkatti's offer is rational precisely because Ramnaresh is making a rational decision under duress. The chapter must honor the intelligence of that decision even as it shows the deception that shaped it.
- The phrase "the word the British used" for coolie is a careful signal, but the chapter must decide how to handle this term throughout. It is a slur in some contexts and a self-identification in others. The Indo-Caribbean community has a complex relationship with the word. The chapter needs to know this.

**The question I'd push on:**
Who tells this story? The opening is told by a narrator who knows what the arkatti did not say. This is necessary for the opening. But the chapter's voice must remain inside the girmitiya's experience. When the text describes the colonial system's architecture, whose eyes are we seeing through? The people who experienced the system, or the historian who studies it? The line between showing the system and analyzing the system is where the Achebe Test will be most severely tested.
