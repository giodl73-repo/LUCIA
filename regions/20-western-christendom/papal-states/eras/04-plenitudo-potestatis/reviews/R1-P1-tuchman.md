# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman

**Chapter:** Papal States — Plenitudo Potestatis
**Stage:** PANEL-1
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I read it straight through. I did not want to stop.

That is the correct test, and this opening passes it.

The opening sentence — "In the ninth year of his pontificate, in the great hall of the Lateran, Lotario de Segni counted what he had made" — is exactly right. It does three things at once: it places us (in the year, in the hall), it names the man with his actual name before his title (which is the right order — we meet the person before the institution), and it gives us a verb that is quietly devastating. "Counted what he had made." Not surveyed. Not received reports from. Counted. As if the bishops were a ledger entry. As if the man who was also the Vicar of Christ was doing arithmetic on his achievement.

That verb earns the whole opening.

The middle section — the seventy canons enumerated — is a formal risk that works. Most writers would make this a survey, a list, a textbook. This writer makes it a cascade. The structure is additive: each canon landing as a separate weight. Transubstantiation. Annual confession. Jews and Muslims in distinctive clothing. The Fifth Crusade. The heretic's legal category. The rhythm of the enumeration IS the point: this is a man who, in a single council, defined what Christians believed in their bodies, required what they did each year, legislated what others wore on theirs, and launched a war. The breadth of the list is the argument.

The turn at the Fourth Crusade is the chapter's most compelling moment. He had launched it. He had condemned its outcome. It had happened anyway. This is the crack in the structure — and the text presents it without explanation, which is exactly right. The reader feels the gap between "authority that was real and formal" and "could not prevent a Christian army from burning a Christian city" without being told to feel it. Tuchman's rule: show the evidence, trust the reader.

The landing is strong. "The council's work continued without him. As it was designed to do." This is the chapter's thesis in two sentences: the institution outlasts the man, which means the man's achievement and limitation are the same thing. The system he built was designed not to need him. The system worked. Innocent died.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening currently gives us the council's scope and Innocent's power in beautiful clarity. What it does not give us is the resistance. We see John of England submit and Philip of France submit, but the submission is described as an endpoint, not a struggle. The chapter should find the friction — the point where the authority met something it could not simply pronounce away. The Fourth Crusade sack is one instance. But what did it feel like inside the curial machinery when the legates' dispatches arrived from Constantinople and Innocent read them? That interior moment — the fullness of power receiving news that power could not control — is the scene the full chapter should be building toward.

**Verdict:** This opening earns the chapter. The legal-public voice is correct, sustained, and carries narrative weight without ever becoming a lecture. I would read on.
