## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — PANEL-2 Review of Notes

**Focus:** Sensory plan across all sections

The sensory palette in the notes is implicit but needs to be explicit. I will give you what each section needs.

**Section 1 (Opening revision):**
- Lin's body: salt crust on forearms, calluses from rope, squinting against the glare off the water. The way a man who has been at sea for days holds his balance differently than a man on land.
- Sound: the creak of the junk's hull (Chinese junks have a particular sound, the bulkhead structure making them stiffer than sewn dhows), the slap of the matting sail, the crew's Hokkien calls.
- Temperature: the equatorial heat pressing down, the shade under the sail awning, the difference between the deck in sun and the deck in shade.
- Taste: tea. A Hokkien captain drinks tea. It is the one constant. The tea brewed on a small stove in the galley, strong, slightly bitter, in a cup that has crossed the South China Sea more times than the man holding it.

**Section 2 (The Port):**
- The port hits you with smell first: rotting fruit, dried fish, the sweetness of tropical vegetation, incense from the temple, the sharp scent of fresh-cut timber from the boatyard.
- The warehouse must be dark and cool. The contrast with the street outside. The goods in the warehouse have their own smell: camphor, sandalwood, raw silk, dried pepper.
- The Malay market: different spices, different cooking smells, the sound of a different language.

**Section 3 (Song Boom):**
- Quanzhou harbor: the scale. Hundreds of ships. The sound of hammering from the shipyards. The customs house: paper, ink, the scratch of the brush on the manifest. The shibosi officer's office: a room with records, the smell of ink and old paper.
- The copper cash: the feel of the coins, the weight of a string of a thousand, the sound they make when counted.

**Section 4 (Settlement):**
- The temple: incense, the red and gold lacquer, the smoke-darkened ceiling, the sound of the wooden fish drum during prayers. The Mazu statue's face.
- The wedding: the food. This is the scene where taste dominates. The Hokkien dishes and the Malay dishes side by side. The laksa that is both. The sound of two languages mixing.
- The cemetery: the hilltop, the wind, the stone markers, the view of the harbor below. The dead can see the ships. This is why the cemetery is here.

**Section 5 (Landing):**
- The monsoon shifting. The physical sensation: the wind changing direction, the temperature dropping slightly, the clouds building to the southwest. The harbor preparing: sails unfurled, cargo loaded, the last-minute rush.

**Recommendation:** Every section must open with a sensory anchor and close with one. The argument lives between the sensory bookends. If a section opens with an idea, it has slipped.
