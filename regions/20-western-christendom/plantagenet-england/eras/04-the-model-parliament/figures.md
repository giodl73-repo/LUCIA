# Figures — The Model Parliament
## Era 4, Plantagenet England (1272–1327 CE)

---

## Edward I (1239–1307)

He was called Longshanks because of his height. He was taller than most of his knights, and his reign was conducted in that register: he occupied more space than the people around him expected.

Edward came to the throne in 1272 after a decade of managing his father Henry III's government through the chaos of the baronial wars — wars that de Montfort had won militarily before Edward destroyed him at Evesham. The lesson Edward took from his father's reign was architectural: a king who governed through the law's forms was harder to overthrow than a king who governed through personal favor. The law, properly used, was more durable than any magnate's loyalty.

He applied this lesson with considerable aggression. The *quo warranto* inquiries — by what warrant do you hold this right? — stripped jurisdictions from lords who could not produce charters and demonstrated that the crown's legal authority was superior to hereditary custom when the crown chose to press the question. The great statutes organized and extended royal justice. The common law courts multiplied. The chancery's output of writs — the forms through which subjects sought royal justice — expanded throughout his reign.

He was not, in his personal conduct, a man who believed he was subject to the law. He was brutal in warfare. He ordered the execution of Scots who had surrendered under terms. He expelled the Jews from England in 1290 — an act of confiscation and expulsion that his legal machinery made smooth by providing forms — and used the revenue to finance his Welsh campaigns. His relationship to the law was that of a man who understood it better than his enemies and used that understanding as a weapon.

The irony of Edward's reign is that the machinery he built to extend royal authority was the machinery through which royal authority would eventually be checked. He was not a hypocrite. He believed the law was the king's instrument. He was right. He did not live to see that instruments can be turned.

## Robert Burnell (c. 1239–1292)

Burnell appears in the legal history of England as a name attached to statutes. In life he was the man through whom Edward's legal vision was converted into legal form — the translator between the king's instinct for governance and the chancery's technical capacity to execute it.

He had been Edward's chancellor since before Edward was king, serving during the crusade, through the legal apprenticeship of the early reign. He was a Bishop — Bath and Wells — though his private conduct would not have supported anyone's definition of episcopal virtue. He fathered at least four illegitimate children. His financial dealings were irregular. He was twice proposed for the Archbishopric of Canterbury and twice rejected by the papacy. He served as chancellor anyway, because Edward needed him and because Burnell, whatever his personal failings, was the best administrative mind of the age.

The Statute of Westminster I bears his thinking most clearly: fifty-one chapters of systematized common law, organized and rationalized without being replaced. Burnell understood that the common law's power came from its continuity — from the fact that it grew through decided cases rather than royal decree. He made the statutes fit the common law's own method rather than overriding it. The law continued to grow through cases after Westminster I. But it grew in a more orderly landscape.

He died in 1292, three years before the Model Parliament he had helped make possible. The Parliament of 1295 used the administrative machinery he had built.

## The Knight of the Shire (composite, c. 1295)

The parliamentary returns for the Parliament of 1295 survive imperfectly. What can be reconstructed from multiple parliaments of the period is the kind of man who was elected to represent a county.

He was a knight — not necessarily a great lord, but a man of property, known in his county, elected by the county court that met regularly at the county town. He held land enough to qualify as a knight by Edward's standards, which had been raised recently to exclude the lesser gentry and focus representation on men of real standing. He had appeared in the county court before, had served as a juror, had administered a local commission. He knew the procedures.

He rode to Westminster with instructions from the county: specific complaints about specific officials, a position on the tax being requested, perhaps a petition about a local matter. He sat with the other knights of the shire — two from each of the forty English counties — in the commons section of the Parliament. He listened to the Latin formula being read. He conferred with his fellow representatives about the common position. He voted to grant the tax when the king's promises to address the petitions were deemed adequate.

He was not a democrat. He did not believe that the commons should govern. He believed that the king should govern justly, and that the mechanism through which the king heard what justice required in the shires was the Parliament. He was doing his duty to his county and to his king simultaneously.

When he rode home, he carried in his saddlebag the king's responses to the county's petitions. At the county court, those responses were read. People acted on them. The Parliament had reached into the shire. The circuit was complete.

## Simon de Montfort (c. 1208–1265)

De Montfort was a Frenchman who became England's most English rebel. Born in Normandy, he came to England to claim the earldom of Leicester through his grandmother's line, married Henry III's sister against the king's express wish, and spent the next three decades in the ambiguous position of the king's most intimate associate and most dangerous enemy.

The Parliament of 1265 was called in January of that year, when de Montfort controlled the government and the king was effectively his prisoner. The summons included knights of the shires and burgesses of the towns alongside the magnates and bishops. This was not a democratic gesture. It was a political calculation: the great lords were deserting, and de Montfort needed the moral weight of a broader assembly to legitimate his position.

He got the Parliament. He lost the war. At Evesham in August, the young Prince Edward caught him in a river bend and destroyed his army. De Montfort's body was mutilated on the field. His head was sent to the wife of a lord who had been his enemy. His sons fled to France.

What survived was the form. Edward I, who had looked at de Montfort's body on the field at Evesham, used de Montfort's parliamentary form thirty years later to finance his wars. The man who invented parliamentary democracy — if the term means anything in the context of 1265 — was destroyed by the man who institutionalized it. The common law's method: the case survives the litigant.

## Edward II (1284–1327)

Every institution needs the man who puts pressure on it to reveal its strength. Edward II was that man for the Parliament his father had built.

He was not stupid. He was not cowardly — he was physically brave when required. He was temperamentally unsuited to the particular demands of governing England in the years between 1307 and 1327: a realm that expected military leadership against Scotland, a magnate class that expected counsel and deference, and a legal tradition that expected the king to govern within the law's forms. Edward II preferred his favorites, governed through personal loyalty rather than institutional procedure, and repeatedly confronted the Parliament with demands it refused to meet on the terms he offered.

The Ordinances of 1311 demonstrated that Parliament could be used to constrain the king's choice of counsellors. The deposition of 1327 demonstrated that Parliament could be used to legitimize the removal of a king who had failed to govern within the expected forms. Neither event established parliamentary sovereignty in any theoretical sense. Both events established parliamentary practice: that this was the institution through which major political transitions would be given their legitimacy.

Edward II was murdered in Berkeley Castle in September 1327, a few months after his formal renunciation of the throne. The manner of his death was not debated at Parliament. Some things were not done through forms.

His son, Edward III, would use the Parliament his grandfather built to finance the Hundred Years War and preside over one of the more confident expressions of English royal power of the medieval period. The machine, having survived the test of Edward II, ran well.
