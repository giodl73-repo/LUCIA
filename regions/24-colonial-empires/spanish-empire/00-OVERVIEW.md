# El Imperio Español — La Cruz y la Espada

God, gold, and glory: the three objectives that the Spanish Crown and its agents pursued across the Americas, Africa, Asia, and the Pacific from 1492 to 1898. But the Spanish Empire's self-understanding was more complex than this shorthand. The theological debate about whether the indigenous peoples of the Americas possessed rational souls — and therefore whether the conquest required justification and the conquered deserved protection — produced the Laws of Burgos (1512), the Requerimiento, and the New Laws (1542): a body of legislation that attempted to regulate conquest in theological terms, even as the conquest proceeded in defiance of those terms. Bartolomé de las Casas, the Dominican friar who spent fifty years documenting and denouncing Spanish atrocities, wrote his *Brief Account of the Destruction of the Indies* within the theological framework of a Catholic priest arguing to the Spanish Crown that what was being done in the Crown's name was a sin. Las Casas and the conquest existed simultaneously. The empire generated both the atrocity and the critique of the atrocity, from within its own religious framework.

The scale of the Spanish Empire at its peak is difficult to comprehend. From Madrid, the Crown governed territory on four continents: New Spain (Mexico, Central America, the Caribbean), New Castile (Peru, Bolivia, Chile), New Granada (Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador), Río de la Plata (Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay), the Philippines, the Pacific islands, Florida, Louisiana, and a string of outposts across Africa and Asia. The silver mountain of Potosí in what is now Bolivia — the largest deposit of silver ever worked — funded the Habsburg wars in Europe and underwrote the global economy of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. The Potosí mint produced more coins than any other mint in the world. The eight-real piece (the "piece of eight") was the world's first global reserve currency.

Key places:
- **Mexico City (Tenochtitlan)** — the Aztec capital rebuilt as the viceregal capital; the Templo Mayor visible through the colonial foundations
- **Potosí (Cerro Rico)** — the silver mountain; "worth a Potosí" entered the Spanish language as a synonym for incomprehensible wealth; perhaps 8 million people died mining it
- **Manila** — the Pacific endpoint of the galleon trade; the bridge between the Americas and Asia; the city where Mexican silver met Chinese silk
- **Cartagena de Indias** — the fortified port through which the treasure fleet loaded; the slave market; the Inquisition

## What Defined Them

**The viceroyalty system — administering a continent from Madrid.** The Spanish Empire organized its American territories into viceroyalties: New Spain (Mexico) and Peru (later subdivided). Each was governed by a viceroy appointed by the Crown, served by an audiencia (a court with judicial and administrative functions), and supervised by visitadores (inspectors sent from Madrid). The system was elaborate, expensive, and effective at extracting wealth; it was less effective at the governance of territories where the distance from Madrid meant that royal orders arrived months after the situations they addressed had changed. The gap between the law (which attempted to protect indigenous people and regulate labor) and the practice (which did not) was built into the system by its geography.

**The encomienda and the mit'a — labor as the engine.** The Spanish Empire ran on indigenous labor organized through two systems. The encomienda granted a Spanish settler the right to the labor and tribute of a group of indigenous people, in exchange for the obligation to Christianize them. The mit'a was the Andean labor-draft system the Spanish inherited from the Inca and reorganized: communities were required to send a percentage of their men to work in the mines for periods of months. Both systems were theoretically regulated; both were in practice engines of forced labor and death. The Potosí mine alone may have consumed 8 million lives across the colonial period, through accident, mercury poisoning used in the refining process, and the general conditions of highland mining.

**The Inquisition as instrument of empire.** The Spanish Inquisition (1478–1834) functioned in the Americas as well as Spain: the first American tribunal was established in Mexico City in 1571. Its primary targets were conversos (Jewish and Muslim converts to Christianity) suspected of secret practice, Protestants, and, increasingly, various forms of heterodoxy. The Inquisition in the Americas also targeted indigenous practices classified as idolatry and the syncretism through which indigenous communities incorporated pre-Columbian elements into their Catholic practice. It was an instrument of religious uniformity that served simultaneously as an instrument of social control. Its use against indigenous cultural practice was uneven — some inquisitors were Las Casas-like defenders; others were not — but its institutional presence shaped the entire colonial religious landscape.

**The Lettered City.** The Spanish colonial world produced a remarkable literary and intellectual culture alongside its violence: the Universidad de México and the Universidad de San Marcos in Lima were founded in 1551 — the same year as the University of Salamanca's peak — as institutions for the education of the colonial elite. The "lettered city" (Angel Rama's concept) was the network of educated, literate colonial administrators, clergy, and criollos whose writing organized, legitimized, and contested colonial power. Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz — the seventeenth-century Mexican nun who was among the greatest poets in the Spanish language and one of the first feminist intellectuals in the Americas — emerged from this world. Her silencing by the Inquisition is inseparable from her achievement.

## The Chapters

**1 — Requerimiento:** 1513: a Spanish conquistador reads aloud a legal document (the Requerimiento) to a group of indigenous people who do not speak Spanish, informing them that they have been given to the Spanish Crown by the Pope and that if they resist this gift, any violence that follows is their own responsibility. The document was required to be read before hostilities could legally begin. Sometimes it was read at night, with no one present. The legal fiction of just war required its performance.

**2 — Potosí:** A Quechua miner descends 300 meters into Cerro Rico in 1590. He has been assigned for the mit'a — six months of labor. The mercury used to process the silver ore causes the tremors that will kill him within a decade. In the city below the mountain, 160,000 people live in what is, at that moment, one of the largest cities in the world. The silver is flowing to Seville. The Seville merchants are paying their debts to Antwerp bankers. The European economy runs on this.

**3 — Las Casas:** Bartolomé de las Casas has spent decades in the Americas, watching. His *Brevísima relación de la destruición de las Indias* (1542) is addressed to the Crown Prince of Spain. It is an attempt, by a Dominican priest using the language of Catholic moral theology, to stop something that his own civilization is doing. It will be used by Spain's enemies for the next three centuries as propaganda. It will also be the most honest account of the conquest written by a participant.

**4 — Las Independencias:** July 9, 1816: the Congress of Tucumán declares the independence of the United Provinces of the Río de la Plata. Within ten years, all of Spanish South America is independent. The creole elite that led these movements was the same elite that had benefited from the colonial system; independence, for most of the indigenous and enslaved populations, changed the rulers without changing the structure. This is the founding ambiguity of Latin American independence.

**5 — El 98:** August 12, 1898: Spain and the United States sign the Protocol of Peace. Spain cedes Cuba, Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines to the United States. The last remnant of the empire that Columbus began is gone. The Spanish writers known as the "Generation of 98" will spend the next decade trying to understand what Spain is without its empire. The answer — what remains is the culture, the language, the people — is both obvious and difficult.

**6 — La Herencia:** 500 million people speak Spanish as their first language. Twenty nations trace their legal systems, their political vocabulary, their religious practice, and their social structures to the Spanish colonial period. The empire is gone; its institutional forms persist. The question of what to celebrate and what to reckon with in this inheritance is the question that October 12 (Columbus Day / Día de la Raza / Día de la Hispanidad) provokes annually across the Spanish-speaking world.
