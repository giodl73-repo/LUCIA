# Inuit — "The People"

They call themselves Inuit — "the people" (singular: Inuk, "a person"). The name "Eskimo," applied by outsiders, is not accepted by most Inuit communities (though some Alaska Native peoples use it without objection). The Inuit are not one people but a family of related peoples distributed across the Arctic coast from Siberia to Greenland — Yupik and Inupiat in Alaska, Inuvialuit in the western Canadian Arctic, Inuit in Nunavut and northern Quebec, and Greenlandic Inuit (Kalaallit) in the world's largest island. The dialects differ; the ecological adaptations vary; the cultural traditions show regional divergence. What unites them is the language family (Inuktitut and its relatives), the technology of the cold, and the intellectual tradition of living in the world's most demanding human environment with a precision and elegance that no outside culture has equaled.

Inuktitut has no word for "wilderness." The Arctic, to the Inuit, is not empty, wild, or remote. It is home — intensely known, named in extraordinary detail, traveled by the knowledge embedded in the language. The famous observation that Inuit languages have many words for snow is a rough version of a real fact: the vocabulary for ice conditions, for snow types, for weather patterns, for the behavior of the sea in different seasons is not poetic elaboration but precision instrument. An Inuit hunter who could not distinguish between the ice conditions that the vocabulary distinguishes could not travel safely, could not find the seals, could not come home. The language was — and is — the technology of survival.

Key places:
- **Nunavut** — "our land" — established as a Canadian territory in 1999; the largest political unit in Canada; the homeland of approximately 36,000 Inuit; the first major indigenous self-government achievement in Canadian history
- **Baffin Island (Qikiqtaaluk)** — "the big island" — the fifth largest island on earth; the core of the eastern Canadian Inuit world; the location of Iqaluit, Nunavut's capital
- **Igloolik (Iglulik)** — "the place with houses" — a small island in Fury and Hecla Strait; one of the most archaeologically rich sites in the Arctic; human occupation for approximately 4,000 years; the Inuit Circumpolar Council was founded here
- **Thule** — the archaeological culture that spread across the Arctic around 1000 CE, from Alaska to Greenland; the direct ancestral culture of most modern Inuit peoples

## What Defined Them

**The technology of cold as civilization.** The qajaq (kayak) — the single-person skin boat designed for the hunter — was perhaps the most precisely engineered watercraft ever made for a specific purpose. It was designed to fit the hunter's body exactly (measured against the individual user), to roll back upright after capsizing, to be maneuvered in the swell and among ice floes where a European ship-boat would be helpless. The umiaq (the larger, open skin boat) carried families and gear across open water. The iglu (snow house) provided shelter that could be built in an hour from the landscape's most abundant material and that maintained interior temperatures thirty to forty degrees above the outside air. The dog sled (qamutiik) covered distances in winter that no other technology could match. These were not primitive tools; they were refined instruments, the product of thousands of years of engineering in response to the most demanding environment on earth.

**Sila — the world's awareness.** The Inuit concept of sila is often translated as "weather" or "air," but its full meaning is closer to "the wisdom of the world" — the awareness or consciousness that pervades the natural environment and that can be read, if you know how. A skilled hunter reads sila: the behavior of the ice, the movement of the clouds, the direction of the wind, the sound of the ice changing beneath his feet. This is not mysticism; it is the application of multi-generational observation to the reading of physical conditions. But it is embedded in a framework that understands the natural world as possessing intention and awareness, and the human being as a reader of that awareness rather than a master of the environment.

**Inua — the spirit in all things.** Every living being — every animal, every significant natural feature — had an inua, a "person" or "spirit" that was its animating intelligence. The relationship between the hunter and the animals he hunted was therefore not simply a transaction but a social relationship: the seal that offered itself to the hunter was making a choice, and the hunter's responsibility was to treat the seal's body with the respect and ceremony due to any person who had made a gift. The correct treatment of animals was not optional; a hunter who failed to observe the proper protocols would find that the animals no longer offered themselves. This was a governance framework for sustainable hunting, embedded in spiritual terms.

**The oral tradition as the Arctic encyclopedia.** In an environment where the difference between life and death could be a matter of knowing which direction the seal's air hole was, or which ice conditions were safe to cross, the oral tradition was the Inuit's survival technology. The knowledge encoded in stories — the routes across the tundra, the locations of food caches, the behaviors of animals in different seasons — was as precise and as essential as any written technical manual. The stories that seemed to outsiders like entertainment were actually dense information systems, teaching the geography, the ecology, and the survival protocols of the Arctic to every generation of children who listened to them.

**Nunavut as political achievement.** The creation of Nunavut as a Canadian territory in 1999 was the culmination of a thirty-year negotiation process that began with the Tungavik Federation of Nunavut's land claims. The Nunavut Land Claims Agreement (1993) gave the Inuit of the central and eastern Arctic title to 350,000 km² of land, rights to wildlife management, and financial compensation. The creation of the territory gave them self-government over a political jurisdiction the size of Western Europe. This was not a gift from Canada; it was the outcome of a political campaign conducted by Inuit leaders — including John Amagoalik, called "the father of Nunavut" — who understood that the legal and political tools of the Canadian state could be turned toward Inuit purposes if wielded with sufficient skill and persistence.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Pre-Dorset and Dorset Traditions:** The small carved ivory figure — a bear, perhaps a spirit helper — is being made in the shelter of the Dorset winter camp. The carver is working in a tradition that has been refined for a thousand years. The Thule people are still in Alaska. They do not know yet that they will come and that the Dorset tradition will not outlast their arrival.

**2 — The Thule Expansion:** From Alaska, a Thule family is moving east along the Arctic coast in a umiaq. They have a better harpoon than the people who came before them. They have a dog team that can pull a sled faster than any runner. They are following the bowhead whale. In two hundred years, their descendants will be in Greenland.

**3 — The Little Ice Age Adaptation:** The Norse settlement at the Western Settlement in Greenland has been abandoned. The cold that drove them out is the same cold that the Inuit navigate with a technology the Norse never acquired. Somewhere in Greenland, Inuit hunters are watching the abandoned Norse buildings from a distance and wondering what kind of people build in stone and then leave.

**4 — The European Encounter:** Martin Frobisher reaches Baffin Island in 1576 and captures five Inuit — taking three adults and a child from their kayaks as specimens. The Inuit of southern Baffin Island will encounter European ships regularly for the next century. They trade with the ships, they watch the European explorers struggle with the ice and die, and they develop a precise understanding of what these strangers are capable of and what they are not.

**5 — The Colonial Century:** A child at the residential school in Churchill, Manitoba, has not spoken Inuktitut for three months. The language the teachers permit is English. The knowledge of the ice conditions that her grandmother encoded in stories is not being transmitted. The school has assessed this as education. In 1953, her parents will be relocated to Resolute Bay — one of the most extreme climate environments in the Arctic, chosen partly because the government wants Inuit bodies to support Canadian sovereignty claims in the High Arctic. The relocation is not authorized by the relocated families. They are told it will be better there.

**6 — Nunavut and the Climate Crisis:** On April 1, 1999, Nunavut officially becomes a territory. The Inuit who fought for thirty years to make this happen are watching the ceremony in Iqaluit. The sea ice outside the town is three months thinner than it was twenty years ago. The polar bears are ranging further inland because the ice is not supporting their hunting season. The land is ours. The land is changing. Both things are true.
