# How to Write a Chapter

**A walkthrough for chapter authors.**

You have been assigned an era. You are going to write ~5,000 words that
put a reader inside a human civilization at a specific moment in time —
from inside that civilization's own worldview. This guide walks you through
every step from first assignment to locked chapter.

The process is longer than you might expect. That is the point. A chapter
that takes shortcuts produces the kind of historical writing we are
explicitly trying to avoid: well-intentioned, respectful-sounding,
and ultimately hollow.

---

## 1. You've Been Assigned an Era

Before you write a single word, read.

**First:** Open the people's `00-OVERVIEW.md`. Read it completely. Understand:
- What era came before yours and what it established
- What era comes after yours and what yours sets up
- What the key themes are for this people across all their eras
- What their own terms are for the period you're covering

Your era does not exist in isolation. It is one chapter in a longer arc.
A reader who has just finished the previous era will arrive at yours with
specific knowledge and expectations. A reader who will continue to the
next era needs yours to have laid certain foundations.

**Second:** Read the region's `00-LANDSCAPE.md`. Understand what world your
people inhabited — the geography, the neighbors, the trade routes, the
pressures from outside. History happens in physical places. Know the place.

**Third:** Check `SOURCES.md` at both the region level and the people level.
What is already in the source library? What gaps exist? What has the project
flagged as needed but not yet acquired? You need to know the source terrain
before you start researching — so you know what you're working with and what
you'll need to note as uncertain.

**Fourth:** Look at any completed chapters from adjacent eras for this people,
if they exist. Not to copy the voice — but to understand the precedents
already established. What named individuals have already appeared? What
events have already been described? You do not repeat what the previous
chapter showed.

---

## 2. Research

Your job is not to summarize what scholars say. Your job is to find the
specific people, moments, and physical details that let you put a reader
in a place and time.

### Source Tiers

Work in this order:

| Tier | Type | Use |
|------|------|-----|
| 1 | Primary sources | Original texts, inscriptions, oral traditions recorded by the culture. Gold standard. |
| 2 | Academic sources | Peer-reviewed journals, university press monographs, archaeological reports. |
| 3 | Scholarly secondary | Well-sourced books by recognized historians, museum publications, institutional pages. |
| 4 | Reference | Wikipedia for dates, coordinates, and basic facts only — not for interpretation. Always verify against the underlying sources. |
| 5 | Do not use | Pop history YouTube transcripts, unsourced blogs, unverified AI-generated content, Wikipedia mirrors. |

MAXIM and FELIX (sibling projects) are Tier 3 — useful for orientation,
not authoritative for historical claims. Verify independently.

### What to Look For

Research with a specific goal: find the material you need to write in scenes.

**Named individuals.** Who were the actual people of this era? A ruler, a
merchant, a priest, a soldier, a farmer — someone the sources name, place,
and describe. You cannot write a scene without a protagonist. If the record
names no one, you need to know that early, because you will have to handle
it honestly (see Principle 13 — Honest Uncertainty).

**Specific moments.** Not "the empire expanded" but the specific campaign,
the specific year, the specific decision that made the expansion happen.
Not "trade flourished" but the specific good, the specific route, the
specific city that demonstrates what flourishing looked like.

**Physical details.** What did this place look, sound, smell, and feel like?
What did people wear, eat, build, use? What was the texture of daily life
in this era? The sensory ground that makes a scene believable comes from
research, not imagination.

**Their own words.** If any primary sources survive — inscriptions, poems,
speeches, letters, oral traditions — these are gold. A culture's own words
carry a weight that no scholar's paraphrase can replicate.

**The record's limits.** Where is the evidence thin? Where do scholars
disagree? Where are you working from inference rather than documentation?
You need to know this before you write, because the chapter must handle
uncertainty honestly. False certainty is a form of dishonesty, and readers
sense it.

Track your sources as you go. Each source you use goes into `notes.md`.
Sources you need but don't have get flagged in `notes.md` as ACQUISITION
items, which feed into `sources/ACQUISITION.md`.

## 3. Writing the Opening

The opening is a gate. It has a 30-point rubric (The Gate) and a pass
threshold of 24/30. But the rubric is not the target — the reader is.

### Pick the Scene

You need: one person, one place, one moment.

Not the most famous event. Not the most dramatic battle. The scene that
best embodies what this era was — the moment that, if you can put the
reader in it, makes them understand this world. Sometimes that is the
moment of greatest power. Often it is a quieter moment: a merchant at a
market, a craftsperson at work, a child at a ceremony.

Ask yourself: if this culture could choose one moment to show a stranger
what their world was, what would it be?

Then find the best-documented version of such a moment. A named person,
a date, a place, something in the record that lets you build from
evidence rather than pure imagination.

### The 800-Word Constraint

800 words is a discipline, not a limit. It is enough to establish:
- A person (named, placed, with a decision or observation or action)
- A place (physically real — the reader can feel where they are)
- A moment (something is happening — not just "in those days," but now)
- Stakes (something matters — what is in play for this person)

If you cannot do these four things in 800 words, more words will not fix
it. The problem is not length — the problem is that you have not found
the right scene, or you have not found the right person, or you are
beginning with context instead of with the scene itself.

### What Makes a Good Hook

**Person + place + moment + stakes.** All four. The opening that has
three out of four will feel incomplete. The opening that has all four
will carry the reader forward.

**Sensory ground.** The reader needs to feel where they are. Not a
catalog of sensory details — one or two precise ones. The smell of
something specific. The sound of something specific. The weight or
texture of something in the character's hands. These are not decorations;
they are how the reader's body enters the scene.

**The culture's logic, not yours.** The character in your opening operates
inside their own worldview. Their cosmology is simply true. Their social
logic is simply real. Do not explain it. Do not flag it as different. Let
it be present without commentary.

**No throat-clearing.** The opening does not begin with historical context.
Not "In the fourteenth century, the X people were..." Not "Before we can
understand this era, we must first consider..." Those sentences push the
reader away. Start in the scene.

### The Pull

By the end of 800 words, the reader must want to keep reading. Not because
of a trick — a cliffhanger, a manufactured mystery — but because they are
invested. They are somewhere. Someone matters to them. Something is at
stake. The pull is earned by the quality of the scene, not by withholding
information artificially.

## 4. The Production Bible (notes.md)

Before you write the full chapter, write your production bible. This is
the plan the chapter will execute. It is not a draft. It is the thinking
that makes the draft possible.

The `notes.md` has six sections:

### Sources

List every source you are using: Primary, Academic, and Needed (ACQUISITION).
For each source, note what it contributes to this specific chapter —
not what the source is about, but what you are drawing from it.

This section does two things: it disciplines your research (if you
cannot say what a source contributes, you may not know the material
well enough), and it creates an audit trail for the BOARD stage, when
domain experts will check your accuracy.

### Key Figures

Who are the human beings who will carry the narrative? Table format:
name in their own language, their title or role in their own terms,
why they matter to this specific era.

2-5 figures is typical. If you have fewer than 2, the chapter may not
have enough human texture. If you have more than 5, you risk losing
the reader in a crowd of names.

### Narrative Arc

The spine of the chapter. 2-3 paragraphs answering: What is the story
of this era? What changes over the course of it? What is the central
tension? How does it resolve, or why doesn't it resolve?

This is the most important section of the production bible. If you
cannot write it clearly, you do not yet know what you are writing.
Do not proceed to the section plan until this is solid.

### Section Plan

A table: each section, its content, word target, key source, and panel
risk. The panel risk column is where you anticipate which reviewer will
push back on this section and why. If you cannot identify any risk for
a section, look harder — every section has potential weaknesses, and
anticipating them now prevents surprises later.

### Panel Pressure Points

One paragraph per panel voice, noting what they will likely push on:

- **Tuchman** will push on pacing and arc. Where is the chapter
  chronologically plodding? Where does it survey instead of narrate?
- **Ibn Khaldun** will push on explanatory depth. Where does the chapter
  describe without explaining? Where is causation thin?
- **Achebe** will push on voice. Where are the risks to inside-not-outside?
  Where might your own cultural position show?
- **Davis** will push on equal depth. Are there any sections where this
  culture gets shallower treatment than a literate empire would receive?
- **Kapuściński** will push on sensory ground. Which sections are most
  abstract? Where has he not been placed yet?

Answering these questions now means the feedback from PANEL-1 will be less
surprising, and FIXES will be faster.

### Open Questions

What does the research not resolve? Where are you working from inference?
Where do scholars disagree? Where is the record thin? These are the places
where Principle 13 (Honest Uncertainty) applies — the chapter must handle
them honestly, and you need to know where they are before you start writing.

## 5. Writing the Full Chapter

The chapter is ~5,000 words. Its arc — from opening scene to landing —
should feel like a complete experience: you were somewhere, something
happened, you understand something you did not before.

### Pacing

| Section | Target | Function |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Opening | ~800w | The hook scene from opening.md — integrated as the chapter's first section |
| Development 1 | ~600-800w | World widens; second perspective or the opening character moving through the era |
| Development 2 | ~600-800w | Third dimension; arc building; each section advances understanding |
| Deepening 1 | ~600-800w | Central tension or transformation arrives |
| Deepening 2 | ~600-800w | Human texture; figures from figures.md in full |
| Landing | ~600w | Close at a specific moment or image; reader sits with it |

These are targets, not a rigid template. If the chapter needs a different
structure to tell its story, use a different structure. The targets exist
to prevent the two failure modes: thinness (too little to immerse the reader)
and sprawl (so much that voice cannot be sustained).

### Section Transitions

Do not summarize what the previous section showed before moving to the
next one. If the previous section did its job, the reader understood it.
Trust that.

Do not announce what the next section will cover: "We now turn to..."
"Another important aspect of..." These are textbook transitions. They
break immersion.

Move between sections the way a good film cuts: the end of one scene
carries enough momentum to pull the reader into the next. Sometimes a
single sentence of bridge. Sometimes no transition at all — let the
new section begin in its own scene.

### Building the Arc

Every section must earn its place by doing something new — not adding more
information, but changing what the information means. The reader should
arrive at the end of each section knowing something different from what
they knew at the beginning.

A common failure: the chapter is full of interesting facts with no arc.
Each section is accurate and vivid, but nothing builds. The reader finishes
feeling informed but not moved. The narrative arc is what prevents this.
It is why the production bible matters — you need to know the arc before
you start writing, not discover it halfway through.

### The Landing

The chapter's final section does not summarize. It does not write
"And so, the era of X came to an end, having established..." That is a
textbook close.

The landing arrives at a specific moment: a final image, a final voice,
a final action that carries the weight of everything that came before.
It does not explain the weight. It places the reader in a moment from
which the weight can be felt.

Short sentences. Plain language. When the weight is heavy, the prose
should be plain. (See Principle 10 — Simple When It Matters.)

## 6. Writing figures.md

The figures file is not a list of important people. It is a gallery of
human beings through whom the era's story runs.

### Who Gets Included

2-5 figures, selected for: presence in the primary record (named, described,
with actions attributed to them), importance to the era's central story,
and diversity of perspective (not all leaders, not all the same type of role).

A figure who appears in the chapter should usually appear in figures.md.
But figures.md can go deeper — more background, more character, more of
the texture that the chapter's pacing doesn't allow.

### How to Write Them

Each figure gets 2-4 paragraphs. Not a biography. Not a list of
achievements. A person as they were understood within their own world.

- What did they do, in their own terms?
- What decisions did they face?
- What did contemporaries think of them (if the record says)?
- What is the one detail that makes them a human being rather than a title?

Avoid the two traps:

**The archetype trap:** "the wise ruler," "the ruthless general," "the
visionary reformer." These are categories, not people. Find the specific
thing about this specific person that goes beyond the category.

**The modern lens trap:** Describing their choices in terms of modern
ethical frameworks, or being surprised by their sophistication. They were
fully human, operating in a fully coherent world. Their intelligence and
their complexity need no comment.

If the record is thin, say so. "What we know of her comes from a single
inscription and two later sources that may have been embellished..." is
more compelling than invented certainty, and it models intellectual honesty.

## 7. Common Mistakes

These are the patterns that cause chapters to fail — at the Gate, at the
panel, or at the CLEAN stage. Know them before you write.

**Opening with context instead of a scene.**
"In the thirteenth century, the X people inhabited a territory stretching..."
This is the most common mistake and the most damaging. It tells the reader
they are about to read history, when they should already be in it. Start
with a person in a place, not with a paragraph that explains what they
are about to read.

**Textbook voice.**
"The economy was based on agricultural surplus, with trade networks
extending to neighboring regions." True sentences, no reader. This voice
treats the culture as a subject of study, not a world to inhabit. If you
can hear a professor delivering it as a lecture, rewrite it as a scene
or cut it.

**Surprise at sophistication.**
"Remarkably, the X people had developed..." or "What is striking is the
level of..." These phrases reveal that the author expected less. The
expectation is the problem. Remove the surprise. Describe the achievement
directly. The sophistication speaks for itself.

**Re-explaining what was already shown.**
"This illustrates the centrality of X in their culture." "This shows that
they valued Y above all else." If the scene showed it, the explanation is
redundant and condescending to the reader. Trust the reader to have
understood what the scene showed.

**Chronological plodding.**
Moving through time event by event without building toward anything.
"In year X, this happened. In year Y, that happened. In year Z, a third
thing happened." This is a timeline, not a narrative. The arc is not
the sequence of events — it is the meaning that emerges from the sequence.
If a decade adds nothing to the story, skip it and say you skipped it.

**The modern observer cameo.**
A sentence where the author appears as a modern person reacting to what
they're describing. "To a modern reader, this would seem..." "From our
perspective today..." These sentences break immersion completely. There
is no modern observer in the room. There is only the world of the chapter.

**Forbidden words in flight.**
"Primitive," "advanced," "tribe" (unless self-designation), "the New World,"
"discovered by Europeans," "Dark Ages." Each of these smuggles in a
worldview. See `guides/STYLE-GUIDE.md` for the full list and the reasoning.

**Forbidden framings.**
"While Europe was doing X, they were doing Y." "Before contact with the
West, they had already..." "They were remarkably sophisticated for..."
All three center another culture as the measuring stick. Every chapter's
protagonist is its own center.

## 8. What to Expect from the Panel

The panel is not there to praise your work. They are there to stress-test
it from five different directions simultaneously. Understanding what each
reviewer is looking for prevents the feedback from feeling personal or random.

### P-1 — Barbara Tuchman (Narrative Craft)

**Key question:** "Would a reader who picked this up at a bookstore keep reading?"

She reads for arc, scene construction, and pacing. She will praise bold
narrative choices and an opening that drops the reader into a moment.
She will challenge chapters that survey instead of narrate, characters who
appear as names and titles but never as human beings, and endings that
summarize instead of land.

She is generous with chapters that take risks. She is merciless with
chapters that are competent but dull. If she says the chapter reads like
a Wikipedia article, that is her highest expression of concern.

**What she will push on:** Any section where the chapter becomes a list
of events. Any character who exists as a historical fact rather than a
human being. Endings that explain rather than arrive.

### P-2 — Ibn Khaldun (Historical Systems)

**Key question:** "Does this chapter understand WHY, or does it only know WHAT?"

He reads for explanatory depth — causal mechanisms, structural forces,
the reasons things unfolded as they did. He will praise chapters that
identify the deep forces beneath events and explain why a civilization
organized itself as it did. He will challenge chapters that substitute
vivid anecdote for analytical depth, and "great man" narratives that
miss the system beneath the individual.

He has no patience for chronological narratives that move from event to
event without ever asking why this sequence.

**What he will push on:** Any section that describes without explaining.
Any passage where the chapter presents a cultural practice without
addressing its functional logic. Golden ages that don't account for
the conditions that made them possible — and ended them.

### P-3 — Chinua Achebe (Cultural Voice)

**Key question:** "Does this culture own its own story, or has someone borrowed it?"

He reads for voice — whether the chapter is written from inside the
culture or about it from the doorway. He catches the subtlest violations:
the admiring outsider voice ("their pottery was remarkably refined"),
the explaining guide ("to understand why they did this, we must consider"),
and the moral tourist who lingers on practices that provoke modern reaction
because they provoke modern reaction, not because the culture centered them.

His assessment is nearly binary: the voice is right or it isn't.

**What he will push on:** Any sentence that reveals the author's own
cultural position. Any moment where the text steps outside the worldview
to comment for a modern audience. Any character who exists to demonstrate
a cultural practice rather than to live.

### P-4 — Wade Davis (Anthropological Wonder)

**Key question:** "Does this chapter treat this culture's way of knowing as a genuine intellectual achievement?"

He reads for equal depth across cultures. He will catch any passage where
an oral or indigenous culture gets shallower treatment than a literate
empire would receive. He distinguishes between honest wonder (which sees
the culture as complex and real) and romanticism (which sees it as a
noble ideal without contradictions).

He is especially alert to descriptions of knowledge systems as "beliefs"
when they are empirically grounded practices, and to passages that
translate indigenous concepts into Western terms and then proceed in those
Western terms as if they are more real.

**What he will push on:** Any cultural knowledge system described as
"tradition" or "belief" when it is a sophisticated empirical practice.
Any implicit hierarchy that treats pastoral, nomadic, or oral cultures
as less sophisticated than urban or literate ones.

### P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński (Sensory Immersion)

**Key question:** "Can you feel where you are?"

He reads with his body. He asks: where am I? What is the temperature?
What can I hear? He will praise openings that put the reader in a
specific physical environment, embodied characters with hands and voices
and postures, and abstract ideas made physical. He will challenge chapters
that open with dates or context before establishing place, passages that
could be set anywhere, and over-reliance on sight at the expense of sound,
smell, and touch.

His principle: the sensory must be structural, not decorative. It cannot
be added after the fact.

**What he will push on:** Any section that floats — prose that could
describe any place in any era. Any character who is a name and title
but has no body. Any abstract passage about power, faith, or decline
that has not been grounded in what those things look, sound, and feel like.

### How the Five Work Together

The five voices create friction by design:

- Tuchman wants the narrative to pull; Ibn Khaldun wants it to explain.
- Kapuściński wants physical presence; Achebe wants cultural ownership.
- Davis wants intellectual depth; Tuchman wants it to still be readable.

A chapter that satisfies all five has survived the full range of tests.
A chapter that satisfies only one or two has found its audience but not
its rigor. The target is to satisfy all five — and the tension between
them is what makes that hard and worthwhile.

## 9. After the Panel — FIXES, CLEAN, and BOARD

### Reading the Feedback

When PANEL-1 reviews arrive, read all five before responding to any.
The five reviews interact. A critique from Achebe may point to the same
sentence that Tuchman is praising for a different reason — and understanding
that tension will tell you something about the sentence that neither review
could tell you alone.

For each critique, make a decision: apply it, adapt it, or reject it
with documented reasoning. "Ignoring" is not an option — if you are not
applying a critique, you need to know why, and that reasoning goes in
your revised `notes.md`.

### What FIXES Means

FIXES is not just revising the opening. It is updating the production
bible to reflect what you learned from the panel, and then — if the panel
feedback warrants it — revising the opening to address the most significant
findings.

Some panel feedback will be for the full chapter, not the opening. That
feedback shapes your section plan before you write, not after.

### The CLEAN Stage

After GATE-2, before BOARD, the chapter passes through editorial review
by three lenses:

- **E-1 (Judgment Auditor)** hunts outside-lens violations — forbidden
  words, anachronistic morality, any sentence that steps outside the culture.
- **E-2 (Voice Keeper)** hunts register drift — textbook voice, modern
  commentary, prose written for a Western audience rather than from inside.
- **E-3 (Compression Editor)** hunts re-explanation and filler — any
  passage that explains what a scene already showed, any redundancy
  between sections.

CLEAN is not optional and it is not a light pass. Chapters that have
passed GATE-2 still fail on individual sentences that violate the
editorial principles. CLEAN exists because the panel looks at the whole;
the editorial stage looks at every sentence.

Address CLEAN feedback before BOARD, so the domain experts are reading
a chapter that is editorially clean.

### The BOARD Stage

Domain experts for this specific culture — archaeologists, historians,
anthropologists whose scholarly work covers this ground — give the final
historical accuracy and cultural authenticity review.

They are not reviewing craft. They are asking: Are the facts right?
Are the sources used correctly? Does the chapter respect current scholarship?
Does it represent the culture as people who study it would recognize?

Board feedback is authoritative on factual and scholarly matters. Apply it.
If you disagree with a board member on interpretation, note the disagreement
and the reasoning in the chapter (per Principle 13 — Honest Uncertainty)
rather than overriding a scholarly consensus.

### Locking the Chapter

After BOARD, you apply the fixes, update TRACKER.md, update
`scoring/INNOVATIONS.md` if the scorer flagged anything exceptional,
and the chapter is FINAL.

FINAL means done. Not "done for now." Not "subject to revision."
Done, unless new scholarship requires documented amendment.

The chapter has been through every stage. It has been seen by five panel
voices, three editorial lenses, and 2-3 domain experts. It has passed
two rubric gates. It has earned its place.

## Quick Reference: Stage Checklist

| Stage | What You Produce | Key Standard |
|-------|-----------------|--------------|
| Research | Source notes, named individuals, physical details | Primary sources first; uncertainty documented |
| Opening | `opening.md` ~800w | Person + place + moment + stakes; sensory ground |
| GATE-1 | 24+/30 | Six dimensions, 0-5 each |
| Notes | `notes.md` | Arc clear; section plan; panel pressure points anticipated |
| FIXES | Revised opening + updated notes | Every critique examined; decisions documented |
| Chapter | `chapter.md` 4,500–5,500w | Arc builds; no textbook voice; ≤8 em-dashes |
| Figures | `figures.md` 500–1,500w | Human beings, not titles; inside voice |
| GATE-2 | 48+/60 | Six dimensions, 0-10 each |
| FINAL | Locked chapter | Board fixes applied; tracker updated |
