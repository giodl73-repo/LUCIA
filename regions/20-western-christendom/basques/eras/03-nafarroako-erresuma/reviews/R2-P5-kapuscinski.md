# R2-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Panel-2 Review: Notes (Production Bible)

The Camino de Santiago is the chapter's best sensory opportunity and the notes name it without yet planning its sensory texture. Let me be specific about what Section 4 should feel like, because this is where the chapter can earn its most vivid ground.

The Camino through Navarre in the eleventh century was not a paved road. It was a track through high country, through the Pyrenean foothills where the climate changes within a morning's walk. In August it smells of dried grass and dust; in October of rain on stone and the smoke of hospice fires. The pilgrims came in groups because the Pyrenees were dangerous alone — not just wolves and weather but bandits who found the pilgrimage road profitable. Sancho III's contribution was to make the road safer and more navigable, which meant hospices at regular intervals, bridges across the rivers, and the routing of the road through towns where the pilgrims could buy food and shelter. This is what his Camino investment looked like on the ground: specific bridges, specific hospices, specific market towns that grew up because the road passed through them. The chapter should show one of these — a hospice at Roncesvalles (irony noted: the same pass), or the bridge at Puente la Reina, or the market at Pamplona on the feast of San Fermín — as a physical location where the abstraction "Navarre as pilgrimage infrastructure" becomes a sensory reality.

**What the notes get right:**

The section plan for the landing — Theobald of Champagne swearing the *foru* in a language he does not speak — is the chapter's best ending. I would add one physical detail: the oak tree at Guernica. The tradition of swearing under the oak is ancient; the specific oak at Guernica, which the Basques called *Gernikako Arbola*, is not just a symbol but a physical presence. The chapter should give us the oak — its age, its size, the shade it throws, the sound of the assembly gathered beneath it, the specific quality of light under its canopy in whatever season Theobald arrived. Let the tree be a body in the scene, not just a convention.

**Where I would push harder:**

The *ferrería* — the ironworking site — that Davis recommends for the *foru*-at-method-level scene is also a sensory site. Iron smelting in a traditional forge is loud, hot, and smells of charcoal smoke and hot metal. If the chapter uses the *ferrería* dispute as the moment to show the assembly working, it should also put the reader in the physical space of the iron forge: the water wheel turning, the bellows, the glowing metal, the sound of hammering that you can hear from the valley below. The dispute over the *ferrería* is not an abstract legal matter — it is about who controls a hot, loud, smoke-filled operation that is the economic heart of the valley. Make it feel like what it is.

**The opening fix I want to see:**

The panel asked for sound (the approaching rearguard) and smell (August at altitude) in the opening. The notes acknowledge this. I want to confirm that the revision will deliver both as structural elements rather than decorative additions. The sound of the rearguard — armor, wagon wheels, animals — is not just atmosphere. It is information: the Basques on the ridge can hear how spread out the column is, can estimate when the end of the baggage train will clear the far end of the pass. The sound is tactical intelligence. Put it in those terms.

**Verdict:** Plan the Camino section with specific physical locations. Give the oak at Guernica a body. Turn the ferrería's sensory reality into evidence in the assembly scene. Confirm the sound-as-tactics in the opening revision.
