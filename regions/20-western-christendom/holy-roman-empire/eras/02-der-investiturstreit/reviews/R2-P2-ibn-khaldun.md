# R2-P2 — Ibn Khaldun | Panel Review, Round 2

**Chapter:** Holy Roman Empire, Era 2 — Der Investiturstreit
**Reviewer:** Ibn Khaldun (P-2)
**Stage:** PANEL-2 (notes review — targeted)

---

The structural engine in the notes is the correct one: the doubled authority, the irresolvable split, the vocabulary compromise that constitutionalizes rather than resolves. I am satisfied that the chapter has an analytical spine.

Two things require deeper treatment in the chapter itself:

**The prince factor.** The notes mention that the controversy strengthened the German princes, but the mechanism must be explicit. During the Investiture Controversy, the princes discovered something that would define the empire for the next four centuries: a divided emperor and a divided papacy meant that neither could effectively discipline the princes. When Heinrich and Gregory were fighting each other, neither had the resources — political, military, financial — to enforce compliance from the princes. The princes used this interval not just to support one side or the other (though they did that too) but to accumulate hereditary rights that had previously been held by imperial appointment. This is the structural consequence that matters. The German principalities become more autonomous during 1076–1122 not because anyone intended this but because the central power was distracted. The chapter should name this mechanism.

**The asabiyyah problem.** The empire's group solidarity was always conditional — the princes supported the emperor when he could reward them, withdrew support when he could not. The Investiture Controversy revealed that the emperor's most powerful reward — appointment to ecclesiastical offices, with their revenues and military capacity — was no longer clearly in his gift. When the *Dictatus Papae* was circulated, the princes who held imperial appointments must have asked: if the pope can remove bishops at will, what happens to my family's investment in the bishopric I helped appoint? The answer was: the investment was suddenly uncertain. This uncertainty drove some princes to support Gregory (hoping to replace imperial patronage with papal patronage) and others to support Heinrich (hoping to defend their existing investments). The settlement at Worms reduced the uncertainty — both ceremonies, both oaths — but did not eliminate it. The chapter should make this economic logic explicit in Section 4.

The terminal condition as formulated is correct: the Concordat of Worms produces precise irresolution, not peace. The German princes emerge with more power. The papacy has won the theological argument. The emperor has survived. But the system that produced the Ottonian Settlement — crown and Church as a unified administrative body, the king as the Church's protector and the Church as the crown's administrator — that system is permanently altered. It does not reconstitute. The Hohenstaufen emperors will fight Italy and the papacy for another century precisely because the institutional solution that made the Ottonian empire functional no longer exists.

*Structural analysis: sound. Must make the prince-accumulation mechanism explicit and the economic logic of asabiyyah disruption visible in Section 4.*
