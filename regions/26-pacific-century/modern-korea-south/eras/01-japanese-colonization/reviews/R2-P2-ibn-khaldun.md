# Panel Review — R2-P2: Ibn Khaldun (Historical Systems)

**Production Bible: Iljegangjeongi (Japanese Colonization, 1910–1945)**  
**Date: 2026-04-16**

---

## The Question

Does the production bible identify the *machinery* of the system? Will the chapter show how colonization actually operates, or will it just describe its effects?

---

## The Assessment

**THE MACHINERY IS IDENTIFIED. PROCEED WITH ONE CLARIFICATION.**

Ibn Khaldun reads the notes carefully and finds the structural engine well-defined: the haegeum as persistence mechanism. This is strong. The chapter will not be about "Korean suffering" — it will be about "what the system cannot fully control."

However, Ibn Khaldun presses on one point: **the soshi-kaimei policy as a *causal mechanism*, not just an event**.

The notes write:

> "The soshi-kaimei policy (1939–1945), the suppression of Korean language in schools, the requisition of rice and resources... were not random cruelties. They were *parts of a unified system designed to absorb Korea into Japan as a racial and cultural satellite*."

This is correct. But the chapter must show the *logic* of why names come first. Ibn Khaldun wants to see:

**Why does the colonial system target naming?**

In a Confucian society, names are genealogy. The soshi-kaimei was not arbitrary cruelty; it was the systematic destruction of Korean kinship structures. By replacing Korean family names with Japanese ones, the policy attempted to *sever the inheritance chain*. A person with a Japanese name is no longer legally a descendant of a Korean ancestor. The records show them as descendants of Japan.

This is bureaucratic genius. It uses the *same record-keeping system that Confucianism relies on* to erase Confucian continuity.

**Section 2 ("The System of Names") must show this logic.**

Not just: "The administrator comes and takes the name."

But: "The administrator is not taking a label. He is severing the genealogical chain. He is erasing the woman from the history of her own family."

When Kim-sa registers her name change, she is participating (under coercion) in the erasure of her own continuity. She knows this. The chapter should show her knowing it.

---

## The Question I'd Push On

**The March First Movement (Section 4).**

The notes give ~800 words to the March 1, 1919 uprising and its suppression. This is the *first major organized Korean resistance*. It fails. The chapter must show why it fails (Japanese military superiority) and what it costs (deaths, arrests, psychological devastation).

But what Ibn Khaldun wants to know: **does this failure change the system, or does it entrench it?**

The notes don't make this clear. If the March First just *happens* and then the oppression continues the same way, the chapter will feel like one damn thing after another.

But if the suppression of March First *causes* the system to become more systematic and brutal — if Park Min-jun's torture is directly a *response* to the uprising, if the comfort women system is implemented *because the Japanese fear Korean resistance* — then the causal chain becomes visible.

Make sure the chapter shows: **how does the Korean resistance change the colonial system itself?**

---

## The Question I'd Recommend

**The terminal condition is well-named: "liberation through external event."**

This is uncommon in historical narrative. Usually, colonized peoples liberate themselves. Here, Korea is freed because Japan loses a war that is not Korea's war.

But what is the *causal mechanism* that leads to this outcome?

The chapter should show:
1. Korea is subjugated (1910)
2. Korea resists (1919)
3. Japan intensifies (1920–1937)
4. Japan goes to war with China, then the US (1937–1941)
5. Japan's war effort requires more from Korea — food, labor, women (1941–1945)
6. Japan loses the war (August 1945)
7. Korea is liberated, but not by its own victory (August 15, 1945)

The causal logic is: **Japan's own imperialism led to a war that required even more brutal colonization of Korea, which led to Japan's defeat, which freed Korea.**

This is not a triumphant narrative. But it is a *systematic* narrative. Ibn Khaldun wants to see this logic made clear.

---

## Recommendation

**PASS WITH STRONG GUIDANCE.** The machinery is identified. The sections are well-planned. The sources are strong. But Section 2 ("The System of Names") must go deeper into the *logic* of why names are the first target. And the progression from Section 4 (March First) through Section 5 (War) must show how Korean resistance *provokes* the system to become more brutal, not less. The causal chain should be visible to the reader.

With these clarifications, the chapter will be exceptional historical work.
