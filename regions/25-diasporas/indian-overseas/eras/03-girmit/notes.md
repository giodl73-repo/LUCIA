# Notes — Girmit: The Indenture System (1838–1917)

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** The girmit (contract/agreement) as a binding mechanism that attempts to reduce a person to a labor unit, and the counter-force of cultural reconstitution that makes the girmitiya more than the contract intended.

**Mechanism:** The contract promises legibility (five years, fixed wages, defined terms) but delivers illegibility (terms changed at will, no return passage, no exit). The girmitiya respond by building an alternative structure of meaning: the jahaji-bhai (ship-brotherhood), the Ramayan mandali (reading circles), the reconstituted village. The system extracts labor; the people reconstruct world.

**Terminal condition:** The system collapses when its internal contradiction becomes politically visible. The indenture contract claims to be a free labor agreement, but it functions as coerced labor. When Indian nationalists (Gokhale, Gandhi) name this contradiction publicly, and when the girmitiya's own resistance (strikes, petitions, refusal) makes the cost of enforcement exceed the benefit of extraction, the system abolishes itself. 1917: the last indenture ship sails. The terminal condition is legitimacy failure: the contract cannot sustain the fiction that it is voluntary.

## Sources

### Primary
- Indenture records: Trinidad and Tobago National Archives, Fiji National Archives, National Archives of India (Calcutta depot registers)
- Gaiutra Bahadur, *Coolie Woman* (2013): personal/archival narrative of one woman's indenture journey; primary source embedded in scholarly account
- M.K. Gandhi, *The Story of My Experiments with Truth* (1925-29): for the South African connection and anti-indenture context
- Anti-Indenture Campaign papers (1910-1917): National Archives of India
- Tulsidas, *Ramcharitmanas* (c. 1574): the text that traveled with the girmitiya

### Academic
- Hugh Tinker, *A New System of Slavery* (1974): foundational account of the indenture system's structure and conditions
- Madhavi Kale, *Fragments of Empire* (1998): the political economy of indenture; how the system connected colonial capitalism to Indian rural distress
- Brij Lal, various works on the Fiji Indian experience, particularly indenture-period Fiji
- Marina Carter, *Voices from Indenture* (1996): compilation of girmitiya testimony, petitions, and complaints
- Lomarsh Roopnarine, *Indo-Caribbean Indenture* (2007): social history of indenture in the Caribbean

### Oral/Cultural
- Ramayan mandali traditions in Trinidad, Guyana, Fiji
- Bhojpuri folk songs of departure and sea-crossing (biraha, separation songs)
- Jahaji-bhai oral histories

## Key Figures

1. **Ramnaresh and Dulari** (composite, based on indenture records from Shahabad district, 1880s): the opening family. Ramnaresh is the contracted laborer; Dulari is the carrier of cultural knowledge. She sings the Sundarkand. She packs the brass lota and the Hanuman murti. In the chapter, she will be the figure through whom cultural reconstitution is shown. Disclosed as composite in figures.md.

2. **The Arkatti** (unnamed, composite): the recruiter. Not villain, not hero. A man doing work for commission. Some arkattis were former girmitiya themselves. The arkatti is the system's human interface, and his omissions are the system's method.

3. **Totaram Sanadhya** (historical): indentured in Fiji, 1893. Author (with Pandit Benarsidas Chaturvedi) of *Fiji Mein Mere Ikkis Varsh* ("My Twenty-One Years in Fiji," 1914), the first published firsthand account of indenture conditions. His testimony was instrumental in the campaign to abolish indenture. He is the girmitiya who becomes a witness.

4. **Gopal Krishna Gokhale** (historical): Indian nationalist leader who moved a resolution in the Imperial Legislative Council in 1910 calling for the abolition of indenture. He named the system "a monstrous system" in parliamentary language. His campaign, supported by Gandhi from South Africa, pressured the colonial government.

5. **Kunti** (from Bahadur's *Coolie Woman*): Bahadur's great-grandmother, indentured in British Guiana. A woman who navigated the system, survived it, and whose story was recovered a century later. She represents the women's experience, which was distinct: women were recruited separately, faced sexual exploitation on plantations, and were subject to different forms of coercion.

## Narrative Arc — Section Plan

### Section 1: The Word That Bound (opening integrated, ~800w)
Bihar, 1880. The arkatti. The failed rains. The girmit explained and not explained. Dulari packing. The walk out of Shahabad. The opening as written.

### Section 2: The Depot and the Ship (~900w)
Calcutta depot. The registration process: names recorded, bodies examined, numbers assigned. The kala pani. The ship as liminal space. Ninety days. Seasickness, death, birth at sea. The terror of ocean-crossing for a people whose caste purity depended on not crossing water. The jahaji-bhai: shipmates who become fictive kin, a new social unit forged in transit. Sensory focus: the hold, the salt, the horizon that never changes.

### Section 3: The Plantation (~1000w)
Arrival in Trinidad (or Fiji, or Natal, or Mauritius). The barracks. Sugarcane work: the cutting, the heat, the hours. The overseer and the system of penalties. The five rupees docked, docked again. Women's experience: Kunti's story from Bahadur. Sexual exploitation, the "wife-murder" crisis (men outnumbered women; colonial records documented violence). The girmit's promise versus its reality. The contract says five years; the indenture extends through re-indenture, debt, and the absence of return passage.

### Section 4: The Mandali (~800w)
Cultural reconstitution. The Ramayan mandali in Trinidad. Dulari sings the Sundarkand in the barracks. The mandali is not a formal institution; it is people gathering in the evening to hear the verses that make home real. The Holi festival adapted to Caribbean climate. The cooking: dal, roti, the mustard oil somehow obtained, the chutney that gives its name to a musical form. The temple built from what is available. The persistence of Bhojpuri as the community's language, distinct from the colony's English and the island's Creole.

### Section 5: The Witness (~700w)
Totaram Sanadhya's twenty-one years in Fiji. His testimony. The publication of *Fiji Mein Mere Ikkis Varsh* in 1914. The book travels to India and becomes evidence. Gokhale's parliamentary motion. Gandhi's anti-indenture campaign from South Africa. The system's legitimacy crisis: the contract claims freedom, the testimony proves coercion. The Colonial Office under pressure.

### Section 6: The Last Ship (~700w)
1917: the last indenture ship sails. The system ends not because it was evil (it was) but because the fiction it required could no longer be sustained. The girmitiya are now permanent populations in Trinidad, Guyana, Fiji, Natal, Mauritius. They are not going back. The contract bound them to a place, and the place became home not because the contract intended it but because the people inside the contract built something the contract never imagined. Landing: the girmitiya's children are born in these new countries. They speak Bhojpuri and English and Creole and Fijian. They sing the Ramcharitmanas and calypso. They are the girmit's unintended consequence: not labor units, but founders.

## Panel Pressure Points to Anticipate

1. **Tuchman** will push on Dulari's structural role. She must carry weight in the chapter, not just the opening. Section 4 (the mandali) is where she reappears. She must be present in the ship section too.

2. **Ibn Khaldun** will push on the system's institutional architecture. Section 5 (the witness) must show the structural contradiction, not just the individual testimony. The colonial government's role, the arkatti commission structure, the plantation economy's dependence on cheap labor.

3. **Achebe** will push on voice ownership. Sections 3 and 5 are the risk zones: the plantation conditions and the political campaign could slip into historian's analysis. These sections must stay inside the girmitiya's experience.

4. **Davis** will push on South Indian diversity and the knowledge systems operating at method level. Section 4 must show the mandali and the cooking and the medicine as knowledge systems, not customs.

5. **Kapuściński** will push on the ship. Section 2 is his territory. The kala pani must be rendered as physical experience. Smell, sound, the body in the hold.

## Open Questions

- The "coolie" question: how to handle the term throughout the chapter. Use it in the colonial context ("the word the British used"), acknowledge the community's complex relationship with it in figures.md.
- South Indian girmitiya: the opening is North Indian (Bihar, Bhojpuri). The chapter should acknowledge Tamil and Telugu indentured workers, particularly in Natal and Malaysia. Section 3 or Section 6 can carry this.
- The women's experience: Bahadur's *Coolie Woman* is the strongest source. Kunti's story must be handled with the same dignity as Ramnaresh/Dulari. The "wife-murder" statistics are colonial records and must be presented as such, not as characterization of girmitiya men.

## Voice

**Register:** Discover. Intimate domestic realism colliding with systematic extraction. Hindi/Bhojpuri texture. The prose should feel like a hand being forced open: the close, warm, domestic world pried apart by the system's cold logic, and the response (the mandali, the singing, the cooking) as the hand closing again around something smaller but still held.

**Em-dash benchmark:** 0. Pause mechanisms: the period (for the contract's blunt facts), the comma-chain (for the domestic and sensory passages), the colon (for the system's accounting).
