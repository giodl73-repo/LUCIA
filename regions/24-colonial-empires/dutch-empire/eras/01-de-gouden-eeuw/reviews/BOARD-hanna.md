## Banda Accuracy Assessment

The chapter's treatment of the Banda Islands and the 1621 campaign is accurate at the level of the available scholarship, with two notes.

**On population figures:** The chapter states "The Bandanese population at the time of the campaign was estimated at fifteen thousand. The population afterward was approximately one thousand, mostly enslaved." The pre-campaign population estimate of 15,000 is within the scholarly range (estimates run from 12,000 to 20,000; 15,000 is the most commonly cited median figure). The post-campaign population figure of "approximately one thousand" is accurate for the surviving Bandanese specifically; the total population of the islands after the campaign was somewhat higher once enslaved labor from other regions had been imported. The chapter's phrasing "approximately one thousand, mostly enslaved" implies these were Bandanese survivors, which is accurate — the enslaved laborers brought in afterward were not Bandanese.

**On the pacht system:** The chapter describes "the pacht, a lease arrangement under which Dutch perkeniers (nutmeg plantation managers) would be assigned plots of land and the enslaved labor to work them, and would sell their output exclusively to the VOC at prices set by the VOC." This is accurate. The parenthetical translation of perkeniers as "nutmeg plantation managers" is slightly loose; perkenier literally means "plantation holder" and implies more ownership-status than "manager" suggests. But in context, the chapter's description of the pacht as a lease rather than a freehold correctly establishes that the perkeniers were beholden to the VOC, which is the structural point.

**On agricultural knowledge loss:** The chapter states "yields declined in the first decade of the new system, then stabilized." This is consistent with what the scholarship shows: early productivity losses from the knowledge disruption followed by partial recovery as the new workforce learned the cultivation methods. The chapter correctly identifies this as both a commercial cost and a knowledge system destruction — the Davis board question is answered here.

**One factual note:** The chapter describes the Bandanese as having "trading relationships with Javanese merchants who had been coming to Banda for generations." This is accurate but understates the network: Bandanese trade relationships also extended to Malay and Javanese merchants from Gresik and Tuban, Gujarati traders, and Malay traders from the Malay peninsula. The Banda trade was a multilateral network of considerable complexity, not simply a Javanese-dominated one. This is a minor addition, not a correction.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

The chapter handles the Bandanese with appropriate presence and dignity within the constraints of a chapter told from inside the VOC's worldview. They are not romanticized, not erased, and not reduced to victims without prior existence. The passage on their agricultural knowledge ("their knowledge of the nutmeg tree was inseparable from their knowledge of their islands, their soil, their seasons") is accurate and appropriately specific.

**One framing note:** The chapter describes Coen's analysis of Bandanese trade as his "considering this a breach of contract." This is accurate to Coen's documented reasoning. The chapter does not editorialize about this framing, which is the correct approach. A sophisticated reader will understand the asymmetry without being told.
