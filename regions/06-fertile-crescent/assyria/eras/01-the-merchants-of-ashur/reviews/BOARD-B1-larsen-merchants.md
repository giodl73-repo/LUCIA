## B-1 — Larsen (model) — Board Review of The Merchants of Ashur

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Old Assyrian city-state political structure, karum system institutional organization, Kultepe archive interpretation.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The description of the rubā'um's coexistence with the city assembly (alum) and the limmu system is correct. The chapter accurately represents Ashur's political structure as a distributed system unlike the monarchies to the south.
- The karum assembly's judicial function, including elected judges, recorded verdicts, and citation of precedent, is accurately described and consistent with the published corpus.
- The description of the customs arrangement with the ruler of Kanesh correctly identifies the mutual benefit: Ashur merchants provided tin, the ruler collected duties and secured bronze supply.
- The tin-textile trade pattern and the approximate doubling of tin's value between Ashur and Kanesh are consistent with the tablet evidence as reconstructed by Veenhof.
- Shamshi-Adad's seizure of Ashur (c. 1808 BCE) and its impact on the karum system is correctly presented.

**What needs attention:**
- The chapter states the caravan carried "perhaps two hundred donkeys." This is at the upper end of estimates. Most individual caravan consignments in the tablets involve smaller numbers (30-50 donkeys per merchant). Larger caravans may have combined multiple merchants' goods. The chapter should qualify: "A large caravan might involve two hundred donkeys or more, combining the consignments of several merchant houses."
- The chapter does not mention the existence of subsidiary karum settlements beyond Kanesh (Wahshushana, Hattush, and others). While Kanesh was the largest, the network included approximately forty trading posts across Anatolia. A brief mention would strengthen the picture of the system's scale.
- "A quarter of clay tablets from the entire ancient Near East was found" at Kanesh. This is a frequently cited statistic but should be noted as approximate. The Kanesh archive is extraordinarily large (over 23,000 tablets and fragments) but "a quarter" depends on which corpus you are comparing it to.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter succeeds in presenting the Old Assyrian period on its own terms rather than as a precursor to the Neo-Assyrian empire. The merchant world is treated as a complete subject.
- The karum's self-governing institutions are described with appropriate respect for their sophistication without the word "sophisticated."

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS. The chapter is historically sound. Apply the corrections to the caravan size claim and add a mention of the wider network of subsidiary karum settlements.
