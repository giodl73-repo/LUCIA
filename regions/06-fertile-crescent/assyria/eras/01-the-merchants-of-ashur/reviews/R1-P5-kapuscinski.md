## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Review of The Merchants of Ashur (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening has ground beneath it. The clay, the reed, the mud brick house on a narrow lane. But it is a letter-opening, not a body-opening. I can read the letter. I cannot feel the city. I want the heat.

**What works:**
- "Fourteen minas, wrapped in leather, loaded on the lead donkey." This is material. The leather wrapping, the lead donkey. The tin has weight and texture. The reader can hold it.
- "Your daughter is well. She has begun learning the loom." This grounds the commercial transaction in domestic life. The sentence does double work: it tells you about the family structure and it tells you about textile production. The loom is both personal and economic.
- "Clay, unlike papyrus, does not burn." A single sentence that explains the entire archival miracle without lecturing. The material tells the story.

**What doesn't:**
- Ashur the city is not present as a physical place. There is a "mud brick house that backed onto a narrow lane near the temple." That is a description, not a sensation. What did the lane smell like? What was the sound of the city at the hour this letter was being dictated? Was it morning? Was the Tigris visible from the temple? I need the river. I need the light. I need the temperature.
- The donkey caravan is mentioned but not traveled. "Crossed the steppe through territory that was not always friendly, climbed into the Anatolian highlands." These are directions, not a journey. The steppe is flat and hot and smells of dust and dried scrub. The highlands are cold and the air thins and the donkeys labor on the inclines. The merchants knew every water source, every danger point, every night camp. The chapter must put the reader on the road, at least for a paragraph.
- Karum Kanesh is described architecturally (burned ruins, merchant houses, clay tablets) but not sensorily. What did the trading quarter sound like? The languages, the haggling, the loading and unloading of donkey packs? What did it look like at dawn versus dusk? The settlement is a place; it should feel like one.

**The question I'd push on:**
Where is the body of the merchant? I know what he wrote and what he traded and what institution he governed himself through. I do not know what his hands looked like, whether they were calloused from travel or soft from years at the writing table. I do not know the temperature of the room where Lamassatum dictated her letters. The opening has the voice right. Now it needs the body.
