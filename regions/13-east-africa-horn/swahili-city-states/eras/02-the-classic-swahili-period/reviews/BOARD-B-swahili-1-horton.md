## B-swahili-1 — Mark Horton — Board Review of "The Classic Swahili Period"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Swahili archaeology, coral-stone construction, Shanga excavations, Indian Ocean trade networks, material culture analysis.

**Historical accuracy:**

- The coral-rag construction technique is correctly described: blocks cut from the reef at low tide, set in morite (lime and red earth mix). The description of the building process aligns with the archaeological record at Kilwa, Shanga, and other sites.
- The sewn-hull dhow construction (coconut fiber coir) is accurate. The observation that sewn hulls flex with swells where nailed hulls cannot is a genuine engineering principle documented in maritime archaeological literature.
- The monsoon rhythm (kaskazi northeast October-March, kusi southwest April-September) is correct.
- Kilwa's copper coinage is well-attested in the archaeological record. Chittick's excavations recovered significant quantities of copper coins from the Kilwa site. The description of the mint and the coins circulating in the market aligns with the numismatic evidence.
- The trade goods inventory (Gujarat cloth, Chinese porcelain, glass beads from the Middle East, ivory, gold, mangrove poles) is accurate and reflects the assemblages found at Swahili archaeological sites.
- The porcelain in coral-stone house niches is a specific archaeological finding, documented at Kilwa, Songo Mnara, and other sites. The display function is accurately described.
- The Great Mosque of Kilwa is accurately represented. The mihrab, the coral-rag construction, the timber roof of mangrove poles, the lime plaster are all consistent with the archaeological record.
- The reference to earlier mosques at Shanga (eighth century, timber and mud-daub) is accurate. My own excavations at Shanga confirmed the sequence from timber to coral-stone mosque construction.

**Cultural authenticity:**

- The Bantu foundation of Swahili civilization is correctly established. The chapter consistently presents the coast as an African civilization participating in the Indian Ocean world, not as an Arabian outpost. This is the correct framing and reflects the current scholarly consensus.
- The waungwana social class is accurately described. The distinction between stone-town and thatch-town populations reflects the archaeological evidence of social differentiation at Kilwa and other sites.
- The Shirazi genealogy is handled well: presented as the dynasty's own understanding of its authority, with the analytical question raised but not answered at the expense of the inside voice.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The coral-as-building-material passage is the best representation of Swahili construction practice I have seen in non-specialist writing. The detail about fossils in the coral, the tide-dependent access to the reef, and the setting properties of morite are all specific and accurate.
- The transition from mangrove-and-thatch to coral-stone construction is correctly presented as a social statement, not merely a technological upgrade. Fleisher and LaViolette's work on house form as social power is well-reflected.
- The anonymous builder as entry figure is appropriate. The archaeological record preserves buildings, not builders' names. Giving the builder prominence is a way of honoring the labor the record silences.

**What needs correction:**

- The chapter states that the Great Mosque of Kilwa is being built during the narrative period. The dating of the Great Mosque's earliest phase is debated, with Chittick placing the earliest coral-stone construction in the eleventh or twelfth century. The chapter should be careful not to imply a specific date for the construction that the archaeological evidence does not support. The narrative device (the old man building a mosque) is effective, but a brief acknowledgment in figures.md that the mosque's construction phases span centuries would be appropriate.
- The phrase "calcium morite" in the opening should be simply "morite" — the term is already specific enough. "Calcium" is a modern chemical description that the builders would not have used.
- The chapter describes Kilwa's control of the Sofala gold trade but does not specify the mechanism by which this control was established. The Kilwa Chronicle suggests a dynastic connection (the Mahdali sultans placing a governor at Sofala), but the archaeological evidence is more ambiguous. The chapter correctly avoids false certainty here, but a sentence acknowledging the uncertainty would strengthen the passage.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

Conditions: (1) Correct "calcium morite" to "morite." (2) Add a brief note in figures.md about the mosque's construction phases. (3) Consider a sentence in Section IV acknowledging uncertainty about the mechanism of Kilwa's control over Sofala.
