# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe | Panel Review, Round 1

**Chapter:** Holy Roman Empire, Era 2 — Der Investiturstreit
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (P-3)
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (opening review)

---

This is an unusual position for me to be in: reviewing a chapter about Western Christendom, the civilization from which most of the historical writing I have critiqued in my own work originates. You might think I have no standing here, that this is "their" history and they have every right to tell it their own way.

But that is exactly wrong. The question is not whose civilization is being depicted. The question is always the same: does the text enter the world, or does it describe it from outside? Does it let the people be human beings, or does it let them be symbols of larger forces? And here I can see the risk more clearly, perhaps, because I am slightly outside.

**What works:**

The opening does not moralize. Gregory excommunicates; Heinrich submits. Neither act is framed as triumph or tragedy. "Gregory wrote later that he had acted 'with a heavy heart.'" That single line does something essential: it gives Gregory an interior life that his institutional power tends to erase. We know him as the architect of papal supremacy, the author of the *Dictatus Papae*, the man who made a king stand in snow. We do not always remember that he was also a man who found no pleasure in it.

Heinrich receives the same treatment. His winter crossing — "he had ridden south over the Alps in winter with his wife and his infant son and a small escort, because no German lord would shelter a man under the ban" — this is a king reduced to a traveler, a family man exposed on winter roads. The human being inside the political crisis. This is what the opening does right.

**What to watch:**

The voice is legal-public and deliberate. This is correct for the Holy Roman Empire — it is a civilization that governed by written law, formal argument, documented precedent. But I want to raise a caution for the full chapter: there is a risk that the legal-public voice speaks only for the literate class — the emperors, the popes, the bishops, the princes. This era also included the ordinary faithful, the serfs who worked episcopal lands, the monks who kept the monasteries where much of this argument was housed. These people are not invisible in the historical record. They appear in monastic chronicles, in miracle accounts, in the records of councils that debated their spiritual welfare. If the chapter never finds one of them — a farmer on a bishop's estate who could not know whether to pay his tithes to an excommunicate bishop or to hold them against the papal order — then the chapter will have told the story of the argument without telling the story of the world the argument was about.

The opening correctly frames the bishops as administrators. But bishops were also pastors. They were responsible not just for chanceries but for souls. The Investiture Controversy, at its ground level, was also a question about whether God's grace could be administered by bishops whose appointments were tainted by secular manipulation. This is the question that the reform movement — the Gregorian reform — was founded on. It matters that there were people, across the Latin West, who genuinely believed that an emperor-appointed bishop might be spiritually compromised, that the sacraments administered by such a bishop might be contaminated. The popular religious energy behind the Gregorian reform is as important as the institutional power politics, and the chapter should find a way to render it that is not merely background.

**The question I'd push on:**

*Who spoke for the reform movement from below?* The opening gives us Gregory and Heinrich — both at the top of their respective hierarchies. But the investiture argument was also carried by monks, by cathedral canons, by laypeople in the Italian communes who sometimes physically expelled simoniacs (bishops who had purchased their offices) from churches. There were riots. There were crowds gathering around figures like the Patarenes in Milan, who were not literate administrators but ordinary Christians demanding that their clergy not be owned by secular lords. The chapter should find one face from below. Not to romanticize popular religion, but because the argument was not only between two men at the top. It ran all the way down.

---

*Voice: inhabits the world well. Risk: may tell the story from inside the literate elite's world only. The full chapter needs a face from below.*
