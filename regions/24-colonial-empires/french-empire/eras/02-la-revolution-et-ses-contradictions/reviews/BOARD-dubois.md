# Board Review — Laurent Dubois
## Domain: Haitian Revolution, French Caribbean history, Atlantic slavery
## French Empire Era 2: La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

**Role:** Professor of History, Duke University. Author of *Avengers of the New World: The Story of the Haitian Revolution* (2004) and *A Colony of Citizens: Revolution and Slave Emancipation in the French Caribbean* (2004). The leading English-language historian of the Haitian Revolution and its French imperial context.

---

## Historical Accuracy Assessment

**The 128 categories:** Moreau de Saint-Méry's classification system is correctly characterized. The *Description* (published 1797 from his manuscript) is the standard primary source for the colonial period's racial taxonomy. The characterization of the system as administrative rather than merely prejudicial is historically sound.

**The Société des Amis des Noirs:** Membership and positions are accurately characterized. Condorcet's *Réflexions* (1781) is correctly dated. The account of the society's political failure is accurate: the colonial lobby was more powerful than the abolitionist coalition in the National Assembly, and the exclusion of Saint-Domingue from the Declaration's provisions is documented. One small correction: Mirabeau died in April 1791, before the Société's most active period of advocacy. He was a founding supporter but not present for the critical debates of 1791. Suggest: "and Mirabeau, until his death in April 1791" or remove Mirabeau from the active membership characterization.

**Vincent Ogé:** The account is accurate. Ogé was executed by being broken on the wheel in Le Cap-Français in February 1791, witnessed by a large crowd. The detail of approximately 20,000 witnesses is consistent with contemporary accounts.

**Bois Caïman:** The characterization of the ceremony as known "only through fragmentary and hostile witnesses" is exactly right — this is the scholarly consensus. The dating (August 14, 1791) is correct. The characterization of what followed as coordinated and incomprehensible to the French administration is accurate.

**The Convention's abolition:** February 4, 1794 is correct. The three deputies (Dufay, Belley, Mills) are correctly characterized by composition if not by name; the chapter correctly identifies Belley as the African-born Black man among them. One note: the standard account holds that Belley was born in Africa (likely Gorée), though some sources date his arrival in Saint-Domingue as an infant rather than as an older child; the distinction does not affect the chapter's characterization.

**Jean-Baptiste Belley and the Girodet portrait:** The portrait exists, is in Versailles, and is as described. Belley's service at Savannah (1779) is confirmed. His arrest in 1802 and death on Belle-Île in 1805 are confirmed. The detail about the tricolor cockade in the portrait is accurate: Girodet depicted him in the full dress of a Convention deputy.

**Toussaint Louverture and the Fort de Joux:** The characterization is accurate. He arrived August 1802, died April 7, 1803. The letters from the fort are primary sources and their characterization here is consistent with their content. The final statement attributed to him (the trunk/roots quotation) appears in several nineteenth-century accounts; its authenticity is debated by historians, and the chapter correctly attributes it to "what survivors report he said." The temperature detail is a creative claim that cannot be verified from available records but is plausible for the Jura in April.

**The Louisiana Purchase:** Date (April 1803) and amount (15 million dollars, though the precise allocation is complex — 11.25 million for the territory and 3.75 million for outstanding claims) are correct. The characterization of Napoleon's strategic reasoning is consistent with the historical consensus.

**The 1825 indemnity:** The *ordonnance royale* of April 17, 1825 is correct. The original demand was 150 million francs (reduced through negotiation to 90 million). The framing of this as delivered under the guns of fourteen warships is accurate and is the standard account. The claim that Haiti's final payment was made in 1947 is correct and has been confirmed by recent historical research (notably the 2022 New York Times investigation).

**Required correction:**
- Mirabeau's characterization: he died April 1791; revise to reflect his early role rather than ongoing membership.

**One clarification to consider:**
- The law of 20 floréal restoring slavery applied to Martinique and Guadeloupe; the application to Saint-Domingue was more ambiguous and is the subject of scholarly debate. The chapter's phrasing ("Saint-Domingue was the final item on the list") implies a clear sequence that the historical record is murkier on. Consider: "The restoration of slavery in Saint-Domingue was the expedition's unstated purpose" rather than implying it was formally on the list.

**Overall verdict:** Historically sound, with two recommended corrections. The chapter's central argument — the Declaration as the instrument of the system's own undoing — is consistent with the most important recent scholarship on this period, particularly my own work and Susan Buck-Morss's on Hegel and Haiti. The chapter is an honest account of the French imperial self-understanding and its contradictions.
