## Source Tier System

| Tier | Type | Use | Examples |
|------|------|-----|---------|
| 1 | Primary sources | Gold standard. Cite directly whenever available. | Original texts, inscriptions, papyri, oral traditions, contemporary accounts, excavation field reports |
| 2 | Academic | Authoritative for facts and interpretation. Cite freely. | Peer-reviewed journals (*Journal of World Prehistory*, *Antiquity*, *Journal of Archaeological Science*), university press monographs, formal archaeological site reports |
| 3 | Scholarly secondary | Good for orientation and synthesis. Verify key claims independently. | Well-sourced historian books (general audience but rigorously cited), museum publications, curated reference encyclopedias |
| 4 | Reference only | Dates, coordinates, names, basic facts — never interpretive claims. Prefer the underlying academic paper the article cites. | Wikipedia (as a pointer, not a source), major encyclopedias, standard atlases |
| 5 | Do not use | Unreliable, unverified, or unscholarly. Not citable under any circumstances. | Grokipedia, Wikipedia mirrors, pop history YouTube transcripts, unsourced blogs, unverified AI-generated content |

## Citation Style

**Inline:** Sources are named naturally in the text — author name, work title, or institution as fits the prose. Do not use footnote numbers or bracketed citations in the narrative.

> *Example: According to Thucydides' account in the History of the Peloponnesian War...*
> *Example: The 2019 Çatalhöyük Research Project report documents...*

**Full citations:** Every source named inline must have a full citation entry in the relevant `SOURCES.md` file (chapter-level or region-level). Format:

```
Author Last, First. *Title*. Publisher, Year. [Tier N]
```

For journal articles:
```
Author Last, First. "Article Title." *Journal Name* Vol. No. (Year): pp–pp. [Tier N]
```

For primary sources (where authorship is ancient or collective):
```
[Author/Tradition]. *Title / Description*. [Date / period]. [Medium if relevant]. [Tier 1]
```
