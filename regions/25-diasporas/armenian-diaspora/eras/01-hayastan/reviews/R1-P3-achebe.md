# R1-P3 -- Chinua Achebe

**Chapter:** Hayastan -- The Ancient Kingdom (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Whose Voice?

The voice belongs to the culture. I am satisfied of this. The opening does not explain Armenian civilization to an outside reader. It places the reader inside the problem as Mashtots understood it: a language without a script is a language at risk. The borrowed-shoes metaphor comes from within -- it is the insight of someone who has tried to pray in another tongue and felt the gap. The opening does not say "the Armenians were remarkable for inventing their own alphabet." It says: the alphabet was necessary because the borrowed scripts did not fit. The agency is Armenian. The logic is Armenian. The measuring stick is Armenian.

## What Works

**No outside vantage point is available to the reader.** This is the highest mark I give. The opening does not once step outside to comment, contextualize, or compare. "The first kingdom on earth to adopt the new religion as state faith" is a factual statement, not a boast. The conversion is presented as what happened, not as an achievement to be ranked. The Battle of Avarayr is presented through Armenian understanding: a military defeat that preserved the faith. No outside historian would frame it this way. An Armenian would.

**The people are human.** Mashtots is not an archetype of the wise monk. He was a soldier, then a courtier, then a preacher. His path to the alphabet came through pastoral experience: watching a priest struggle to translate the Gospel in a village near Taron. The alphabet was not born of abstract intellectual ambition. It was born of a practical problem witnessed by a man with his feet in the mud.

**The language metaphors are the culture's own.** "A borrowed script fits another language the way another man's shoes fit your feet." This is not an analogy imposed by the author. It comes from the world of the Highland Plateau, where shoes matter, where craft matters, where fit matters. The culture's material life generates the chapter's imagery.

## What I Would Push On

1. **"No one had written it down."** This sentence in the first paragraph is almost too clean. It risks positioning the absence of a script as a deficiency. The opening partially corrects this by explaining that Armenians used borrowed scripts -- they were not without literacy. But the sentence still carries a faint implication that a scriptless language is an incomplete language. I would suggest rephrasing to emphasize that Armenian was a complete spoken civilization; the absence was of a native script fitted to its sounds, not of sophistication.

2. **The final paragraph** -- "not with borders and armies, though the Armenians had both, but with an alphabet" -- is close to telling the reader the theme rather than letting the theme emerge. The opening has already shown this through narrative. The declaration is unnecessary and slightly didactic. Trust the reader. The borrowed-shoes metaphor, the priest at Taron, the first words in Proverbs, the Avarayr passage -- they have already made the argument. The final paragraph should open outward, not summarize.

3. **"The first kingdom on earth to adopt..."** -- I noted this phrase above as factual, but it is worth watching. In the chapter, this claim should be presented as the Armenian tradition understands it. Some scholars dispute the precedence (Osroene, Ethiopia). The Armenian tradition claims the honor, and the chapter should let the tradition claim it, with the dispute handled in notes or footnotes rather than in the narrative.

## The Question I Would Push On

Is the opening too focused on the alphabet as a technology and not enough on the alphabet as a living practice? The reader understands that the script was invented. But what did it FEEL like to write Armenian for the first time? What did it mean to a congregation to hear the Scriptures in their own language for the first time? The opening comes close in the priest-at-Taron scene but stays at the level of the inventor rather than the community. The chapter should include at least one moment where the alphabet arrives in the hands of ordinary Armenians and changes the texture of their daily lives.
