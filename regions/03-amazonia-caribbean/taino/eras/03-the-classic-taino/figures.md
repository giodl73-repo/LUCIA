# Figures — The Classic Taíno (c. 1200–1492 CE)

---

## Anacaona (fl. 1490s–1503)

What we know about Anacaona comes through Spanish accounts, primarily Peter Martyr d'Anghiera and Bartolomé de las Casas, and the limitation belongs to the story rather than outside it. The Spanish wrote about her because she was impossible to ignore: the cacica of Xaragua, the largest and wealthiest cacicazgo on Ayiti, and by all accounts the most respected political and ceremonial figure on the island at the time of contact. Las Casas called her "the most distinguished woman in the whole island." Peter Martyr described her as receiving Spanish visitors with a ceremony that impressed even those who did not understand it.

She was a composer. This is the detail that distinguishes her in the record and gives her her full particularity. In a tradition where the composition of areítos was the highest form of intellectual and ceremonial work, she was recognized across the island as the foremost practitioner. Other cacicazgos performed her compositions. This was not a figurative reputation. The areítos traveled as the community that carried them traveled, and that Xaragua's areítos traveled to Maguana and Marién and Higüey is a political fact: the communities that performed Anacaona's work were acknowledging a relationship with Xaragua, maintained through its most powerful medium.

She married Caonabó, the cacique of Maguana, a marriage that was itself a political statement: the western cacicazgo and the central mountain territory in formal alliance. When Alonso de Ojeda captured Caonabó through a deception — he offered Caonabó "the shackles of the King of Castile" as an impressive gift, put them on him as he offered them, and led him away in chains — Anacaona managed Xaragua alone. The record is not specific about how she did this, which is the record's failure rather than hers. She continued to lead. She continued to receive. When the Spanish governor Nicolás de Ovando arrived in 1503 with a large force, she organized a reception for him and the eighty or more Spanish soldiers who accompanied him. She was arrested at the reception, accused of plotting against the Spanish, and hanged.

The accusation almost certainly reflected the Spanish administration's desire to remove the most capable Taíno political figure rather than any genuine conspiracy. Las Casas, who was present in Hispaniola at this period, recorded his horror at the event. The detail worth holding: Ovando hanged her rather than burning her, as the original sentence stipulated, apparently as a courtesy. What she thought of this courtesy, we do not have.

What remains is the composition. The areítos she wrote were in the bodies of the people who survived. Some of those people's descendants are alive today.

## Bohechío (d. c. 1500)

Anacaona's brother and predecessor as paramount cacique of Xaragua. The record of his 1496 reception of Bartholomew Columbus is one of the most detailed eyewitness accounts of a Taíno ceremonial welcome in the primary sources. Peter Martyr describes thirty women in the canoe procession, carrying palm branches and singing, followed by the cacique's own procession and then the batey game and the feasting.

What Peter Martyr could not quite render was the ceremony's political architecture. The procession placed the Spanish visitors inside the Taíno network of relationship; the areíto section performed for them incorporated their arrival into Xaragua's ongoing historical record; the batey game demonstrated the cacicazgo's cosmological competence. Bohechío was not hosting a banquet. He was performing the ceremony that established the terms on which these strangers would relate to Xaragua. The Spanish did not accept those terms, because they did not know what terms were being offered.

Bohechío died around 1500, before the full weight of what the Spanish presence would mean became clear. Anacaona took Xaragua. She had three years.

## Guarionex (fl. 1490s–early 1500s)

Cacique of the central La Vega Real region, the most fertile agricultural territory on Hispaniola and the area the Spanish identified earliest as the site for forced gold extraction. Guarionex's situation shows the colonial pressure on the ceremonial system in its early phase: the Spanish demanded that he and his people perform Christian religious practices. He attempted to accommodate this while maintaining the zemí ceremonies in private — a Taíno political solution (incorporate the stranger into the existing network) applied to an entity that was not interested in the network's terms.

His zemís were seized and destroyed. He asked the Franciscan missionaries, in the record, to return them or to allow the community to continue their ceremonies. He was refused. He then organized a resistance movement among the interior cacicazgos, which failed when the Spanish discovered it. He fled to the mountains. He was eventually captured. Las Casas records that he was a gentle man, a careful politician, a figure trying to maintain his community's integrity against forces he could not have anticipated. He was not wrong in his analysis of the situation; he was right. He was simply outresourced.

## The Elder Singer (composite figure)

She has no recorded name. She appears across the primary sources in fragments: Las Casas mentions women who maintained the areíto ceremonies even after the Spanish forbade them. Pané records the names of some of the performers he observed. The archaeological record of the batey courts shows the space where the community gathered. From these fragments, a composite figure emerges: the senior woman within the areíto tradition who held the longest portions of the living library, who taught the children, who trained the next generation of lead singers.

She is composite and honestly disclosed as such. What she represents is not a single person but a function — the transmission mechanism that made the areíto's survival possible. The living library required people who had dedicated their lives to holding it. It required that those people teach others before they died. That this continued to happen, even in the degraded conditions of the post-contact decades, is why the tradition has descendants today.

## Notes on Sources and Method

The figures in this chapter are reconstructed from an asymmetric source base: all primary accounts are external (Spanish) and contact-period or later. Pané's *Relación* (c. 1498) is the closest thing to an inside source — a Spanish friar recording what the Taíno told him, through translation — and even it is mediated by his interpretive framework and his purposes. Peter Martyr d'Anghiera, Las Casas, and the later chronicles provide external descriptions of Taíno political figures and ceremonies.

The technique used throughout: **outsider-as-structural-evidence** (per v1.5 Source Asymmetry Amendment). The Spanish accounts of what they saw and what they destroyed tell us the structure of what existed. Las Casas's horror at the destruction tells us what the Spanish destroyed was substantial. Pané's recorded mythologies tell us the framework of the cosmological system. None of these sources are neutral; all of them are used as evidence of the system they observed rather than as authoritative descriptions of it.

The continuity claims in the chapter's landing rest on: Guitar, Ferbel-Azcarate, and Estevez's collaborative work on Taíno persistence; the 2018 Menéndez et al. ancient genome study (*Cell*, 2018) confirming genetic continuity between pre-contact Bahamian populations and contemporary Dominican and Puerto Rican populations; and the ongoing Taíno revival scholarship and community practice that has documented the maintenance of specific cultural elements through the centuries of disruption. The claim that the Taíno are not extinct is not sentimental or political. It is a statement of documented fact that the scholarship has established.
