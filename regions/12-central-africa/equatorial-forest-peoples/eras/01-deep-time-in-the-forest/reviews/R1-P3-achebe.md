# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Review, Round 1 — Opening

**Chapter:** Deep Time in the Forest — Era 1
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (P-3)
**Stage:** PANEL-1

---

I read this twice. I was looking for the moment where the author steps outside and tells the reader what to think about what they have seen. I did not find it. This is rarer than you might expect.

The most important thing the author gets right is the sequence. The opening does not tell us that the honeyguide relationship is remarkable, or complex, or a sign of ecological sophistication. It shows Mambu following the bird. It shows the call, the response, the portion left behind. It shows this working. By the time the author uses the word "relationship," the reader has already understood what that word means here, from the inside. This is the difference between cultural description and cultural immersion. The author has chosen immersion, and has earned it.

What I want to note: the passage comparing Mambu's knowledge to "a Western-trained botanist" is the one moment I paused. The comparison is structured carefully — the author says the botanist would take "years to approximate," not that the botanist would eventually match or surpass. And the intent is clearly to honor the depth of the knowledge. But the vantage point shifts, briefly, to a Western reference point. The reader is no longer inside Mambu's world; they are briefly invited to view it from outside, through the lens of what a Western scientist would make of it. In the chapter, I would prefer to lose this comparison entirely. Let the knowledge speak for itself in its own terms. "He knows which caterpillar species emerge in which months" — the reader understands the scale and precision. The botanist comparison is not needed and it briefly centers a different world.

Similarly: "This is not what people usually mean when they say knowledge." Who is "people"? This is the author speaking to a reader who uses knowledge in the ordinary Western academic sense. It is a small breach in the voice — a moment of explaining rather than inhabiting. In the chapter, the knowledge should simply be shown operating, in enough specificity that the reader understands its scope without being told that it is "other" than what they expect.

These are small corrections to what is, overall, a strong and honest opening. The voice is right. Mambu is a man, not a specimen. The forest is a world, not a setting.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter must maintain this discipline across five thousand words, including the sections on the relationship with farming communities and whatever structural analysis the chapter requires. The risk is that the analytical sections — which will need to do more explanatory work — will drift toward the outside voice. Watch for it especially when discussing the "exchange relationship" with farmers, where the temptation to frame the asymmetry in modern terms will be strong.
