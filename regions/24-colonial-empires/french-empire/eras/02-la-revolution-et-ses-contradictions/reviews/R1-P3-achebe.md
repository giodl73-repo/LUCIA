# Panel Review — R1 / P-3 / Chinua Achebe
## French Empire Era 2: La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

This is a difficult assignment — perhaps the most difficult in the Colonial Empires region — because the culture whose story is being told here is the oppressor, and the task is to inhabit its self-understanding without endorsing it, while ensuring that the oppressed do not become mere backdrop.

The opening navigates this with considerable skill.

**What I will praise:**

"Moreau de Saint-Méry was not a cruel man. He was, by the standards of his world, an educated one." This is exactly right. The refusal to villainize is not the same as exoneration — it is the more demanding act of rendering someone comprehensible within their own framework. The colonial administrator who reads Montesquieu and classifies human beings into 128 categories is a figure the Enlightenment produced, not an aberration from it. The opening does not flinch from this.

The passage about Toussaint reading Raynal "with more urgency, because the stakes for them were not theoretical" is the opening's most important sentence. It acknowledges the enslaved as readers, thinkers, agents — not objects of classification but readers of the same texts, drawing different conclusions from the same premises. This is the chapter's central human truth.

The close — "Someone had read it" — is both elegant and just. It restores agency to the people the system classified without sentimentalizing them.

**What concerns me:**

The 500,000 enslaved people are a presence in the opening but not a specific one. They appear as a category — "500,000 enslaved laborers," "500,000 bodies" — in numbers that are accurate and staggering but not embodied. This is appropriate for a French-Empire chapter (the Haiti chapter tells the other side), but I want to register the risk: 5,000 words written from inside the French imperial vantage will require at least one scene where a specific enslaved person is present as a subject, even briefly. Not as victim, not as symbol — as a person who makes a decision that the French administrative system cannot account for.

**The question I'd push on:**

The Société des Amis des Noirs — the French abolition society, founded 1788, whose members included Condorcet, Brissot, Robespierre — is the most interesting test case for the chapter's central argument. These were the men who genuinely believed the Declaration's universalism. They argued for abolition within the same Enlightenment framework that produced the 128 categories. Their failure — not through hypocrisy but through being outvoted, outmaneuvered, and eventually drowned in the noise of the revolutionary years — is the French Empire telling its own story. The chapter should find at least one of these men and render his position from inside.
