# B-wolof-3 — Mamadou Diouf — Board Review of The First Atlantic Contact

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Wolof political culture; the islamo-wolof synthesis; the ceddo identity; the gewel tradition.

## Historical Accuracy

- **The ceddo identity is correctly represented.** The chapter presents the ceddo as a martial aristocratic class defined by horse-riding, military service to the buur, and resistance to Islamic reform. The detail about millet beer and the maintenance of pre-Islamic practices is accurate.
- **The gewel tradition is correctly positioned as a political institution.** The chapter treats the gewel not as a performer but as the keeper of political memory and legitimacy. This is the correct understanding.
- **The serigu are mentioned appropriately.** The chapter correctly identifies the serigu as Muslim clerics at court and notes their pragmatic patience with the Wolof's religious flexibility. The Wolof practice of maintaining both Islamic and pre-Islamic religious obligations is well-attested.

## Cultural Authenticity

- The chapter's use of Wolof political vocabulary (buur, Burba Jolof, ceddo, gewel, serigu, jon) is correct and consistent.
- The representation of the Wolof as religiously flexible rather than as either Muslim or pagan is historically accurate for this period. The ceddo-Islam tension is correctly presented as nascent, not yet a full conflict.
- **The gewel as framing authority throughout the chapter is the correct structural choice.** The gewel tradition is the Wolof's own historical voice, and placing it as the chapter's narrative authority is both culturally authentic and editorially sound.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The ceddo's relationship to the horse is presented with genuine depth. The detail about a ceddo warrior being named for his horse, defining his status through the animal, reflects the real cultural significance of horse-ownership in Wolof martial culture.
- The coexistence of ceddo and serigu, framed as "uncomfortable but functional," is an accurate characterization of the period. The metaphor of two trade routes serving the same market is apt.
- The Wolof language's spread as a commercial lingua franca in Section V is an important and often overlooked detail. Wolof became the dominant language of Senegambia not through conquest but through commercial utility.
- The landing's characterization of the gewel carrying "all of it, the binding and the breaking, without declaring that one is better than the other" is an accurate representation of how the gewel tradition handles political transformation.

## What Needs Correction

- **The "Njaay Sall" reference.** The Sall lineage is associated with gewel functions, but the specific name Njaay Sall is not firmly attested for this period. The text already hedges ("they would say"), which is adequate, but the figures.md should note this uncertainty.
- **The ceddo drill scene.** The chapter describes ceddo warriors drilling "in the dry season, when the ground was hard and the dust rose in clouds behind the galloping line, their short swords at the hip and their lances level." The primary weapon of the ceddo was indeed the lance, and the short sword (takouba in Sahelian context, or the Wolof equivalent) was a secondary weapon. However, the ceddo's military practice also included archery and, later, firearms. For this period (pre-1550), the lance-and-sword description is defensible, but a note that archery was also practiced would add accuracy.
- **The gewel's instruments.** The chapter does not specify the gewel's instrument. The Wolof gewel tradition uses the xalam (a plucked string instrument), distinct from the Mande kora. If the gewel is heard performing in the chapter, the instrument should be the xalam, not the kora.

## Verdict: APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

Conditions: (1) Note uncertainty of the Njaay Sall identification. (2) If the gewel's instrument is specified in any revision, use xalam, not kora. (3) Consider adding archery to the ceddo's military practice if expanding the drill scene.
