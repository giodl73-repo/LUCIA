## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of Notes (Panel-2)

**Overall:** The structural engine is now clearly named and the terminal condition is specific. The notes address my primary concern. Two issues remain.

**What works:**
- The engine (body as archive) and the terminal condition (isolation that prevents community of practice) are clearly stated and well-reasoned. The tension between the system's drive toward isolation and the enslaved people's drive toward community is the right narrative arc. This gives the chapter a genuine progression, not merely a chronological survey.
- Section 2 (The Trade) now addresses the system's own logic: sugar economics driving a mortality-replacement cycle. This is the structural mechanism I wanted. The trade consumed human lives at a rate that required constant importation. This is a systemic dynamic, not a series of individual crimes.
- The section plan has analytical weight at every stage. Section 5 (What Was Lost) is essential and addresses Davis's concern about honest loss, but it also serves the structural argument: what was lost is what the terminal condition (isolation) managed to destroy. What survived is what community managed to preserve.

**What doesn't:**
- The notes mention the Bois Caiman ceremony but do not name the structural significance of Vodou as an organizing force. The Haitian Revolution is in Era 2, not Era 1. But the connection between Vodou as a spiritual technology of community formation and Vodou as a political organizing force should be planted in this chapter. The body-archive engine has a political implication: when the enslaved form community around shared spiritual practice, the community becomes capable of collective action. This is the bridge to Era 2.
- The word count estimate (5,500w) is tight for seven sections. The section on "What Crossed" (1,000w) attempts to cover Yoruba, Fon, Igbo, Kongo, and Mande knowledge systems at method level in a single section. This may require compression that weakens the analytical depth. Consider whether two of these five can serve as primary examples with the others receiving briefer treatment.

**The question I'd push on:**
The chapter must avoid making the body-archive sound like a metaphor. It is a structural claim about knowledge systems. The chapter should demonstrate, at least once, the specific mechanism by which embodied knowledge transmits: a specific rhythm taught by a specific method (physical demonstration, not verbal instruction), learned by the body before the mind, and therefore portable in a way that verbal instruction is not. Show the transmission mechanism, not just the transmission.
