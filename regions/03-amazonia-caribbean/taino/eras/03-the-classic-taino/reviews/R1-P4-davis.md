# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Review: The Classic Taíno, Opening

What this opening gets right is what most anthropological writing about the Taíno gets catastrophically wrong: it treats the zemí-inhabited cosmos not as a belief system to be explained but as a reality to be inhabited. The zemís are not in quotation marks here. They are not "spirits" that the Taíno "believed" inhabited objects. They are presences. They are listening at the end of the opening. This is the correct epistemological posture, and it is rarer than it should be.

The line that most precisely captures what I want from this chapter is: "The zemís are not present for information; they are present for living knowledge, which is knowledge in motion, knowledge with breath in it." This is not just a beautiful sentence. It is a distinction between two entirely different theories of knowledge — the Taíno epistemological tradition against which Western archival/inscriptional knowledge systems look partial rather than complete. The author has understood that the areíto is not a substitute for writing but an alternative to it that has its own advantages the written record cannot replicate.

**The deepest thing this opening does:**

It renders the Taíno world as one in which the cosmological, the political, and the material are not separate domains. The batey ceremony maintains cosmic balance. The cacique's authority is generated by his capacity to make the zemís present. The conuco's health is a sign of the world being "in its proper evening arrangement." These are not three different things (religion, politics, agriculture) that the author is connecting analytically. They are one thing, experienced as one thing, that the Taíno understanding held together without distinguishing. The author has not divided them and then reconnected them. They are never divided in the prose.

**The question I'd push on:**

Every culture's knowledge system has its most precise, most demanding expression. For the Tibetan translators, it was the syllable-by-syllable rendering of Sanskrit into Tibetan. For the Tongan navigators, it was the reading of the hull in the dark. For Anacaona's tradition, I want to know what the hardest thing is — the most technically demanding element of areíto composition or performance. Not what it means. What it requires. What a composer gets wrong who hasn't mastered it. The opening gives us the theory; the chapter must show us the method at work.

What specifically makes Xaragua's areítos better than others? Is it rhythm? Is it the quality of the zemí activation? Is it the genealogical depth of the ancestor-naming? This specificity will make the knowledge system real rather than admirable.

**Overall assessment:** The cosmological frame is correct and sustained throughout. The chapter must show the knowledge system at method level, not just claim it.
