## BOARD — B1 (Tinker) — Review of Girmit Chapter

**Domain:** Institutional architecture of the indenture system

**Overall assessment:** The chapter renders the institutional mechanics of the indenture system with a level of accuracy that reflects close reading of the archival record. The progression from recruitment through depot to ship to plantation to abolition is structurally sound.

**Verified as accurate:**
- The arkatti commission structure (per-head payment, incentive for volume over accuracy) matches the documentary record.
- The depot at Garden Reach, Calcutta, and the medical examination process are accurately described.
- The ship conditions (space allotment, voyage duration, mortality rates) are consistent with the shipping records. The "six feet by eighteen inches" figure is within the range documented in the regulations (the actual allotment varied by vessel class and period; 18 inches was the minimum under some regulations, more generous under others).
- The wage deduction system (docking for absence, sickness, refusal) is accurately rendered and matches the plantation labor codes of Trinidad and other colonies.
- The 1917 abolition is correctly attributed to the convergence of political pressure (Gokhale, Gandhi, the Indian National Congress), economic decline (falling sugar prices, rising system costs), and the girmitiya's own resistance.
- Gokhale's 1910 resolution and its language are accurately described.
- Totaram Sanadhya's publication date (1914) and its role in the anti-indenture campaign are correct.

**Corrections needed:**
1. The chapter states the ship was "a sailing vessel of approximately 1,200 tons." By the 1880s, most emigrant vessels on the Trinidad route were steam-sail hybrids or full steamships. Pure sailing vessels were more common in the 1840s-1860s. Recommend revising to "a vessel of approximately 1,200 tons" (removing the specification of sail).
2. The chapter states "five rupees a month for a man, four for a woman." These rates are approximately correct for the 1880s Trinidad contracts, though the actual rates varied by colony and period. A note acknowledging variation would be appropriate.

**Cultural authenticity concerns:**
- The term "coolie" is handled appropriately: attributed to the colonial system ("the word the British used") and used in the final section within the system's own language ("The system called them coolies").

**Verdict:** The institutional architecture is sound. Two minor corrections recommended. The chapter correctly identifies the structural parallel between indenture and slavery without collapsing the distinction.
