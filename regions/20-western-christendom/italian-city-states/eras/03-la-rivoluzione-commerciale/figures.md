# Figures — La Rivoluzione Commerciale

## The People of This Era

---

### Berto di Iacopo Frescobaldi (fl. 1290s–1300s)

The Frescobaldi are documented in English exchequer records from the 1270s through the early 14th century, serving simultaneously as papal bankers and commercial lenders to the English crown. Berto di Iacopo appears in the records as a representative of the firm in England — one of the senior agents managing the firm's English operations during the height of Frescobaldi influence at the English court.

What we know of Berto is what we know of his profession: a man trained from boyhood in the counting house, who spent years in a foreign city on behalf of his firm, managing relations with the English crown and the wool merchants who supplied the Florentine trade. He would have been fluent in enough English and French to conduct business; he would have known the exchange rates between London, Paris, and Florence by heart; he would have known which English wool merchants were reliable and which were not, which sheriffs could be negotiated with and which required a different approach.

What we cannot know is his inner life. The historical record does not preserve the thoughts of Florentine banking agents in London. We know what he signed, not what he felt about signing it. The chapter's portrait is drawn from what his position required of him — the skills, the knowledge, the daily practice — rather than from documented inner experience. He is a representative figure of a class, used honestly as such.

His family did not survive the crisis of the following century unscathed. The Frescobaldi were expelled from England in 1311, their assets seized by Edward II in a dispute over their financial operations. They recovered; they continued in trade; but the expulsion foreshadowed the larger crisis that the Bardi and Peruzzi would face thirty years later. The Frescobaldi are a lens through which the era's peak can be seen, and through which its structural vulnerability is already visible at the edges.

### Giovanni Villani (c. 1280–1348)

Villani was a Florentine wool merchant, banker, and city official who spent most of his adult life writing a chronicle of Florence and of the world as Florentines understood it. He traveled — to Bruges for the cloth trade, to Rome for the Jubilee of 1300, where he says he was inspired by seeing the classical ruins and deciding that his own city deserved a similar record. He returned to Florence and began writing.

The *Nuova Cronica* that he produced is the most detailed primary source for Florentine commercial and civic life of the era. His figures for the city's economic output — the number of firms, the volume of trade, the city's annual revenues — are the quantitative foundation for all subsequent scholarship on the commercial revolution. He counted things because he was a merchant and merchants counted things. His statistics are imprecise by modern standards; they are invaluable by any standard we have for the period.

He died of the Black Death in 1348, leaving his chronicle unfinished. His nephew Filippo and his brother Matteo continued it. The break in the chronicle is the break in the era — the chapter that cuts off mid-sentence, the historian consumed by the event he would have written about. Villani, more than any other figure, embodies the confidence of the commercial era and its catastrophic ending; he is the voice that was recording the world when the world changed.

He was not a philosopher or a theorist. He was a practical man who understood that what he was living through was worth recording. That understanding — the commercial civilization's habit of documentation, of treating the present as a record for the future — is itself the era's achievement. Villani kept a ledger of history the way his colleagues kept ledgers of trade.

### Pietro Gradenigo (Doge of Venice, 1289–1311)

Gradenigo is the figure most associated with the *serrata* of 1297 — the constitutional act that closed the Maggior Consiglio to new families and created the hereditary Venetian nobility. He proposed it; his council voted for it; it was his political achievement and his political gamble.

The gamble was that stability was worth exclusion. The Venetian noble class that the serrata created was not large — around 1,000 to 1,200 families — but it was stable in a way that the shifting merchant oligarchies of other Italian cities were not. A Florentine merchant family that rose to dominance in one generation might be destroyed by factional politics in the next. A Venetian noble family, whatever its internal vicissitudes, held a constitutionally guaranteed position in the republic's governing institutions. The stability came at the cost of the social mobility that had characterized earlier Venetian commercial expansion.

Gradenigo faced revolts. Bajamonte Tiepolo's conspiracy of 1310 — put down by the commune's defenders, including (according to tradition) an old woman who threw a mortar from her window onto the conspirators below — led directly to the creation of the Council of Ten, the security apparatus that would become one of the Serenissima's most characteristic institutions. The attempt to destabilize the serrata produced, as its institutional response, one of the serrata's most effective enforcement mechanisms.

He is not a sympathetic figure in the simple sense. He built oligarchy. He built it brilliantly, and the oligarchy he built lasted five centuries, which in political history is a form of proof. Whether this was good for the Venetians who were excluded is a different question, and the chronicle does not answer it, because the Venetians who were excluded did not leave the record that the noble families did.

### Benedetto Zaccaria (c. 1248–1307)

The Genoese model in one person. Zaccaria was simultaneously: admiral of Genoa in the naval war with Venice (battle of Meloria, 1284, where his tactics were decisive); admiral of Castile in a campaign to recapture the Strait of Gibraltar from the Marinids; holder of the alum monopoly at Phocaea on behalf of the Byzantine emperor Michael VIII Palaiologos; entrepreneur in the grain trade; negotiator in half a dozen separate political contexts.

He succeeded in all of these simultaneously because he understood something the Venetian state apparatus did not need to understand: individual commercial relationships are portable in a way that civic institutions are not. Zaccaria's network was personal — built on his own reputation, his demonstrated competence, his willingness to serve whoever had the asset or the need he could match. When the Byzantine emperor needed a trustworthy operator for the Phocaea alum deposits, Zaccaria was available because he had already demonstrated his value in other contexts. When Castile needed a naval commander who knew the Mediterranean and could adapt to the Atlantic approaches, the same qualities applied.

The alum monopoly was his most important commercial achievement. Alum was the mordant that fixed dye in cloth — without it, the colors in a finished bolt would wash out. Every cloth-producing city in Europe needed alum. The Phocaea deposits were among the best in the Mediterranean basin. Zaccaria negotiated exclusive rights from the Byzantine emperor, paid annual tribute, and charged the rest of Europe what the market would bear. This is the Genoese commercial theory applied: identify the strategic resource, organize the capital and political relationships, extract the monopoly rent.

What he built did not outlast him in the Venetian sense — he had no institutional structure to pass on, only a network of relationships that decayed when he died and his sons could not maintain them at the same intensity. The Genoese commercial adventurer produced brilliant individual achievements and left nothing permanent behind except the precedent. This too was the Genoese model: everything bet on the individual, which meant everything won or lost with the individual.

### Francesco Pegolotti (fl. 1310–1347)

An agent of the Bardi company in Florence, Pegolotti compiled the *Libro di mercatanzie et usanze de' paesi* around 1340 — a merchant's handbook that covered trade routes, exchange rates, weights and measures, customs duties, quality grades of goods, and practical advice for merchants in 50 markets from England to China. It was not published in his lifetime in the modern sense; it circulated in manuscript among merchant families and companies who found it useful.

Pegolotti is the era's most useful witness because he was not trying to explain the commercial revolution — he was trying to help merchants operate within it. His handbook takes for granted everything the era had built: the correspondent network, the bill of exchange, the standardized quality grades, the exchange rates. He records them as practical facts, not as achievements. This makes him invaluable: the commercial infrastructure is visible in his handbook the way a city is visible in a street directory — not theorized, just mapped.

His career ended with the Bardi company's failure in the late 1340s. What happened to him personally after that is not recorded. He is one of the era's anonymous professionals — a man who served the commercial network all his life, documented it in more detail than anyone else of his generation, and disappeared into the crisis that ended the era he had mapped.
