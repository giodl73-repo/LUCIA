## Dimension Notes

**Immersion (9/10):** The inside perspective is sustained from the opening through the landing. The reader is placed in Toledo — smells it, hears the market, sits in the workshop, understands the three-language chain from inside its method. One point withheld: the Las Navas de Tolosa section is slightly more chronicle-like than immersive — the battle itself is rendered in the third person of dispatch rather than embodied presence. Given the chapter's scope this is defensible (the battle is not the subject; its consequences are), but a reader who has been inside Toledo for five thousand words will feel the slight distance.

**Evidence (9/10):** The v1.3 Knowledge System at Method Level standard is met: Avicenna's *Qanun* is explained at the level of what problem it solved (Greek medicine's gaps) rather than just what it was. Gundissalinus's *De divisione philosophiae* is explained at the level of what intellectual work it did (finding Latin equivalents for Arabic philosophical categories). The v1.5 Source Asymmetry technique is applied: Abubacer's uncertain identity is used as evidence about the record's structure, not as an obstacle to narrate around. The *Sefer ha-Qabbalah* is used as primary evidence from inside the Jewish scholarly world. One point withheld: the *Cantar de mio Cid*, mentioned in sources, does not appear in the chapter. This is a deliberate scoping decision (the Cid is Era 2's central figure), not an omission, but it slightly reduces the chapter's Castilian cultural texture.

**Progression (9/10):** The structural engine — translation cycle as self-sustaining knowledge circuit — is identified, shown in operation, and traced to its terminal condition (engine's success transforming engine's conditions). Each section builds: the city's geography explains its importance; the manuscript tradition explains what was in the libraries; the workshop explains how translation actually worked; the funding structure explains how it survived military crisis; the Almohad structural pressure explains why it concentrated in Toledo; Las Navas explains the beginning of the transformation. The terminal condition is a potential new type (gradual premise erosion) that should be flagged for the innovation log.

**Figures (8/10):** Six named figures with varying depth of treatment. Gerard and Gundissalinus are fully rendered; Abubacer and Abraham ibn Daud are given their own intellectual projects (not just their function in Gerard's story); Alfonso VIII is present at the right register (a king in crisis, not a hero); Ibn Tumart is rendered without verdict. Two points withheld: the women of Toledo's scholarly world are absent (acknowledged as lacuna); the Mozarabic community, mentioned in the chapter, does not have a named representative.

**Voice (9/10):** The translation-diplomatic register is a new confirmed voice type for the v1.4 spectrum — the prose of a culture whose primary intellectual activity was moving ideas across linguistic borders. It is characterized by: precision about words and their meanings; patience in rendering process; attention to what is gained and lost in translation; refusal to rush toward conclusions. Zero em-dashes is the natural result of this register, which finds its pauses through periods and the occasional semicolon. One point withheld not for failure but for the slight register variation in the Las Navas section.

**Landing (10/10):** The final three paragraphs earn the highest score. The chapter closes on the engine's persistence mechanism (the workshops still running, Gerard's translations already in university curricula) and then on the terminal condition's beginning (the conditions transforming), and then on the city as a condition rather than a moment — which is the chapter's entire argument, compressed into two sentences. The final line — "Gerard of Cremona understood this by the time he died in 1187" — returns to the opening's anchor figure without summarizing; it closes a circle that the chapter opened and does not explain what the circle means.

## Potential Innovation

**New terminal-condition type: Gradual Premise Erosion**

The Toledo chapter demonstrates a terminal condition not yet in the v1.6 framework. The translation cycle's engine did not fail from external shock (Almohad military pressure was defeated), deliberate self-consumption (the engine was not designed to end), categorical failure (the system encountered events within its categories), or any of the ten confirmed types. Instead: the engine's success — the military victory that the translation era served — created the conditions (massive territorial expansion, fuero-driven Christian settler influx, reduction of military necessity for three-community coexistence) that slowly replaced the conditions on which the engine depended.

This is distinct from "legitimacy gap" (the engine produces the wrong kind of legitimacy for what it needs) and from "deliberate self-consumption" (the engine performs correctly and thereby ends). It is closest to "ecological constraint" (an external factor limits the engine's reproduction) but the constraint is internal — it is the engine's own output that creates the new conditions.

Proposed type: **Gradual premise erosion** — the engine's conditions are slowly replaced by the conditions of its own success. The engine does not fail; it transforms. The period between the engine functioning as designed and the new conditions completing the replacement can span generations. Applies to cultures whose foundational conditions were pragmatic (necessity-based) rather than ideological (principle-based): as the necessity shifts, the pragmatic tolerance shifts with it, and the engine continues in a different mode until the original mode is no longer recognizable.
