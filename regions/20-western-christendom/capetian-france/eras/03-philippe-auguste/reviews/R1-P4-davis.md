# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel-1 Review: Opening, Philippe Auguste et la Grande Expansion

**Round:** 1
**Reviewer:** Wade Davis (P-4)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

This opening is about a literate, urban, documentary culture — and it engages with that culture's knowledge systems with appropriate depth. The feudal law, the coronation theology, the royal archive: these are the Capetian knowledge systems, and the opening treats them as what they are — sophisticated technologies for organizing power across time and space, not colorful customs of a medieval court.

The sentence I find most revealing: "Philip had spent his reign learning the lesson his predecessors had not, or had not been able to act on: that words became land when you enforced them." This is the Capetian epistemology stated precisely. The French crown had developed, over three centuries, a theory of legitimate authority that was simultaneously legal, sacred, and administrative. Philip's achievement was to understand all three dimensions and use them in concert. The author grasps this without reducing it to a single cause.

The thaumaturgical dimension — the healing touch — is the knowledge system I'll be watching most carefully in the full chapter. The ability of the king to heal scrofula was not superstition. It was an empirically observed phenomenon (people presented themselves and reported cures), embedded in a theological framework that gave it meaning, and administered as a political practice that generated loyalty. To tell this story from within, the chapter needs to engage with the healing as the Capetians and their subjects engaged with it — not as magical thinking to be explained away, but as a fact of their world with its own internal logic and social function. How did the ceremony work? What did the sick experience? What did the king experience? The opening promises this. The chapter must deliver.

**The question I'd push on:** The university's emergence — the cathedral school of Notre-Dame growing into the greatest intellectual institution in Christendom — is mentioned at the close but deserves deeper treatment in the full chapter. This is not merely institutional history. The University of Paris was a knowledge-production system that attracted scholars from every Christian kingdom because it offered something none of them could produce at home: a secular-sacred curriculum, in Latin, recognized everywhere, that turned theological argument into a transferable credential. This is a technology of knowledge circulation as sophisticated as the trans-Saharan trade routes or Polynesian star navigation. The chapter should engage with it at that level — not as the "rise of universities" but as a specific answer to the question of how a kingdom preserves and transmits its intellectual capital across generations.
