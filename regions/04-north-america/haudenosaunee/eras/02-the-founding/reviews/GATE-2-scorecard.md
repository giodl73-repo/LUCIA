**Notes:** The chapter passes GATE-2 comfortably. Its greatest strength is voice — the deliberative, oral-tradition rhythm is sustained across 4,500 words without breaking. Its greatest achievement is treating the Great Law as a genuine political and intellectual system while never stepping outside the culture's own worldview. The condolence ceremony as the prerequisite for governance, the clan mothers as the source of political legitimacy, the wampum as a knowledge technology — all receive the depth that literate traditions' institutions would receive.

**Innovations flagged:**

1. **Grief as Structural Engine:** The chapter uses the Mourning War cycle's grief-violence feedback loop as the structural mechanism driving the narrative, and the condolence ceremony as its institutional resolution. Grief is not context or backdrop — it is the plot. The political philosophy emerges from the diagnosis of a psychological condition (unaddressed grief), making the chapter simultaneously a story about governance and a story about the prerequisites for rational deliberation. This technique parallels the Mali chapter's "Covenant as Structural Engine" innovation but applies it to a psychological rather than economic mechanism.

2. **The Present-Tense Close:** "Not the people who built it. The people who are building it. The present tense was the point." This technique uses the grammar of the culture's own name as the chapter's landing — the shift from past to present tense IS the argument about the Great Law's living quality, delivered through the Haudenosaunee language itself rather than through authorial commentary.
