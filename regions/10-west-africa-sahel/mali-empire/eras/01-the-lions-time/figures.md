# Figures — The Lion's Time (Era 1)

## Sundiata Keita (c. 1217–c. 1255)

The Lion of Mali. Son of Maghan Kon Fatta and Sogolon Kedju. Born unable to walk. Exiled as a child. Returned to defeat Sumanguru Kante at the Battle of Kirina (c. 1235). Proclaimed the Kurukan Fuga. First mansa of the Mali Empire.

**What we know:** Sundiata is attested in both the Arabic written tradition (Ibn Khaldun's genealogy of the Mali kings) and the Mande oral tradition (carried by multiple jeli families across eight centuries). The core narrative — disability, exile, return, victory, covenant — is consistent across all major oral versions, which gives it exceptional structural reliability. Specific details (the duration of his crawling, the exact events of exile, the manner of his death) vary between versions.

**What we cannot know:** No contemporary written source describes Sundiata's personality, appearance, or private thoughts. The jelilu carry his public story — the story of what he did and what it meant for the Manden. His interiority is not accessible through the historical record. The chapter renders him through his actions and through the jeli tradition's understanding of what those actions signified, not through invented psychological detail.

**Sources:** D.T. Niane, *Sundiata: An Epic of Old Mali* (1965, from Djeli Mamoudou Kouyate); Fa-Digi Sisoko's version (recorded by John William Johnson); Wa Kamissoko's version (recorded by Youssouf Tata Cisse); Ibn Khaldun, *Kitab al-Ibar* (c. 1375).

## Sogolon Kedju

Sundiata's mother. From the land of Do. Married to Maghan Kon Fatta on the word of a hunter-diviner. Made the decision to take her children into exile when Sassouma Berete's faction threatened them.

**What we know:** Sogolon appears in all major versions of the epic as a figure of agency. She is the one who decides to leave Niani. Her physical description (bent back, appearance mocked by the court) is consistent across versions and is narratively significant: like her son, she is marked by what others perceive as weakness, and like her son, the weakness is the qualification.

**What we cannot know:** The details of her life in exile beyond what the jeli tradition preserves. Her own perspective — as opposed to the tradition's perspective on her role in Sundiata's story — is not independently recoverable.

**Sources:** Niane (1965); Fa-Digi Sisoko; Conrad, *Sunjata* (2004).

## Sumanguru Kante

King of Sosso. Smith-king. Controlled the former territories of Wagadu (Ghana) and the Manden before Sundiata's return. Defeated at the Battle of Kirina (c. 1235).

**What we know:** Sumanguru is attested in both oral and written traditions. The oral tradition describes him as a man of extraordinary forge-based spiritual power — skin that turns to iron, a room of fetishes, mastery of the bala. The Arabic written tradition (Ibn Khaldun) confirms his political existence and his conflict with Sundiata. The jelilu's description of him is not demonization; it is a diagnosis of a specific kind of authority (sorcery/craft-power) that violated the Manden's covenant model of legitimate rule.

**What we cannot know:** Sumanguru's own understanding of his rule. The oral tradition is carried by families allied to Sundiata's lineage, and the Sosso perspective has not survived independently. The chapter presents Sumanguru through the Manden tradition while acknowledging that his power "was real and in its own terms coherent."

**Honest limit:** The chapter cannot fully recover Sumanguru's self-understanding. He is rendered through his opponents' tradition. This is acknowledged in the text and constitutes a genuine limit of the record.

**Sources:** Niane (1965); Fa-Digi Sisoko; Ibn Khaldun; Levtzion, *Ancient Ghana and Mali* (1973).

## Sassouma Berete

First wife of Maghan Kon Fatta. Mother of Dankaran Touman. Her mockery of Sundiata is structurally central to the epic — it is the voice that names the boy's weakness and thereby creates the condition for his vindication.

**What we know:** Present in all major versions. Her role as antagonist within the court is consistent.

**What we cannot know:** Her own motivations beyond what the tradition attributes. She is rendered through a tradition that positions her as obstacle, and her perspective is not independently recoverable.

**Sources:** Niane (1965); Fa-Digi Sisoko; Conrad (2004).

## Bala Faseke Kouyate

Sundiata's jeli. Captured by Sumanguru, who wanted the jeli's skill. The bala (xylophone) he played became the Sosso Bala, which the Kouyate family has held in the town of Niagassola for eight centuries. His capture by Sumanguru exemplifies the sorcery-king's approach to skill: possession rather than relationship.

**What we know:** The Kouyate jeli lineage is attested as carriers of the Sundiata tradition. The Sosso Bala exists and is held by the Kouyate family in Niagassola; it was recognized by UNESCO in 2001. The narrative of Bala Faseke's capture and return is consistent across versions.

**Sources:** Niane (1965); Conrad (2004); UNESCO Masterpieces of Oral and Intangible Heritage (2001, Sosso Bala).

## Fakoli Koroma

Sumanguru's nephew. Defected to Sundiata's alliance because Sumanguru had taken his wife, violating kinship obligations. His defection was one of the triggers for the gathering of the alliance that fought at Kirina.

**What we know:** Present in the oral tradition as a key ally. His defection is explained through the kinship-violation logic of the Manden — Sumanguru's seizure of a woman from within his own family network demonstrated that his power recognized no limits, not even those of blood.

**Sources:** Niane (1965); Fa-Digi Sisoko; Austen (ed.), *In Search of Sunjata* (1999).

## A Note on Sources and Composite Figures

No composite figures are used in this chapter. All named individuals appear in the oral tradition and, where applicable, in the Arabic written record. However, the chapter acknowledges throughout that the oral tradition is not a single text but a family of performances. The "Sundiata" of Djeli Mamoudou Kouyate is not identical to the "Sundiata" of Fa-Digi Sisoko. The chapter draws on multiple versions and notes key variations. This is not a flaw in the sourcing; it is the nature of oral-tradition historiography. The consistency of the core narrative across versions and across centuries constitutes the evidence base.
