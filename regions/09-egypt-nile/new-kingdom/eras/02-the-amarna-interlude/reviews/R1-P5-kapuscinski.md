## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Review of The Amarna Interlude (Opening)

**Overall:** I can feel the chisel. I can feel the copper meeting the limestone. I can feel the dust falling. What I cannot feel is the heat, the light, the weight of the air in the hypostyle hall, the sound of the other crews. The opening is strong in its tactile center and thin at its sensory edges.

**What works:**
- The chisel is magnificently rendered. "Re-hammered so many times the edge had thinned to a wafer where it met the stone." I know this chisel. I have held tools like this in my hands. The detail about the leather roll is cut but the re-hammered edge survives, and it is enough. The tool tells the story of its own use.
- "Copper on stone sounds the same whether you are carving a name or removing one." This is the sensory line that carries the entire analytical argument. Creation and destruction are acoustically identical. The reader hears this truth before understanding it. This is sensory-analytical fusion at its best.
- The dust. The pale limestone dust settling on the floor of the temple. This is specific and grounding. The dust of destruction is the same dust of creation. The material world does not distinguish between building and tearing down.

**What doesn't:**
- Upper Egypt at midday is one of the most punishing thermal environments in the inhabited world. The stonecutter has been working since before dawn. By the time he reaches the hypostyle hall, the stone columns would radiate stored heat. The air between them would be still and heavy. The sweat would sting his eyes. The opening gives us the chisel and the stone but not the body that holds them together.
- The hypostyle hall at Ipet-Isut is not a neutral space. It is a forest of columns, 134 of them in the great hall, painted in reds and blues and golds that would still have been vivid. The light enters through clerestory windows and falls in angled shafts through the stone forest. This is where the stonecutter works, and the opening does not put the reader in this space.
- I hear no voices. The stonecutter works alongside other crews. They would speak. They would call to each other. The stone chips would echo. The foreman would give orders. The silence of the opening is a narrative choice but it costs embodiment.

**The question I'd push on:**
Can the chapter sustain the sensory register when it moves from the stonecutter's chisel to the court politics of Akhenaten? The risk is that the opening's tactile specificity gives way to political narrative that floats above the ground. The bodies must remain present throughout. Akhenaten had a body. Nefertiti had a body. The courtiers at Akhetaten had bodies that felt the desert heat and the new city's dust. The chapter must keep its feet on the ground.
