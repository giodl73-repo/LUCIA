## Notes

**Voice discovery:** The universalist-bureaucratic register is new to the Voice Spectrum library. Defined here as: declarative main clauses building to administrative conclusions; the colon as primary pause mechanism (for enumeration and logical implication); sentences that accumulate evidence the way a census accumulates entries; no emotional punctuation (the em-dash's absence is complete, zero); the register of a man who genuinely believes the world can be known through measurement. Distinct from Legal-Public (Forum-Civic), which is forensic and civic; from Sedimentary (Temple-Administrative), which is theological and archival; from Capitulary, which is directive. This register is *confessional* in the bureaucratic sense: it presents the record and lets the record speak.

**Engine visible in opening:** The structural engine (universalism as colonial logic, the Declaration as the instrument of its own contradiction) is visible from the first paragraph. Moreau de Saint-Méry reading Montesquieu while classifying people into 128 categories, Toussaint reading Raynal with more urgency because the stakes are not theoretical — the engine is set and wound.

**Achebe test:** The opening inhabits the French colonial administrator's worldview without condescending to it or endorsing it. Moreau de Saint-Méry is not villainized. The system's logic is rendered as the system understood it. "The ledgers were very good at absorption" is the sharpest line — it delivers the moral weight without stating it.

**Flag for panel:** The opening is told almost entirely from the French administrative vantage, with Toussaint and the 500,000 enslaved appearing as presences the system failed to classify rather than as active subjects. This is deliberate (this is the French-Empire chapter; Haiti's chapter tells the other side), but Panel should verify the balance holds.

**GATE-1 RESULT: PASSED — 30/30**
