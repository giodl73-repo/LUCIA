# BOARD-B3 — Cristiana Barreto (model) — Review of The Teso Florescence

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Marajoara ceramic social meaning; mortuary analysis; gender archaeology; the tanga's use and burial context.

## Historical Accuracy

- **Slip colors:** The chapter's identification of red, black, and white as the three primary slip colors is accurate. The characterization of red as iron-rich clay and white as kaolin is correct. The black slip composition is accurately described as a combination of mineral pigment and organic binder, though the specific binder is not definitively identified in the literature — the chapter's phrasing ("a combination of mineral pigment and organic binder") appropriately hedges.

- **Visual language elements:** The chapter's description of the spiral, paired-eye motif, and zoomorphic forms as elements of a coherent symbolic system is consistent with my own analysis in the Unknown Amazon volume. The chapter correctly avoids over-specifying their meanings — it says the spiral "is an argument" without claiming to know what the full argument is. This is the right level of precision.

- **Tanga description:** The chapter mentions the tanga briefly in the visual language section and more fully in the figures.md. The description — "fired clay pubic covering, worn in ceremony and buried with its wearer" — is accurate. The characterization of its association with female ceremonial life and the most elaborate burials is consistent with the burial evidence.

- **Secondary burial:** The description is accurate. The specific detail about painting the cleaned bones with red pigment — the same mineral ochre that colors the urn's slip — is one of the most significant symbolic correspondences in Marajoara mortuary practice, and the chapter deploys it correctly. The interpretation (red as the color of life, reinvested in the bones) is consistent with regional comparative evidence.

- **Storage platform:** The chapter's claim that completed urns were stored on raised platforms within the teso's structures is grounded in the Camutins architectural evidence. The phrasing "appears to be" in the notes correctly acknowledges this as interpretation rather than fully documented fact.

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter's central question about the tanga — "The woman who can make the urn is likely the woman who has worn the tanga" — is a reasonable inference from the burial evidence, stated with appropriate uncertainty ("likely"). My own work suggests that the specialist ceramic producers were also the ceremonially marked women in the Marajoara social structure. The chapter's inference is defensible.

The decision not to speculate about the full semantic content of the visual language — to leave the spiral as "an argument" without specifying what the argument is — is the most important cultural authenticity decision in the chapter. It is correct. We do not know what the full argument was. The chapter does not pretend to know.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The treatment of the visual language as a knowledge system rather than decoration. This is the central insight of the Unknown Amazon project and the chapter applies it at the appropriate analytical depth.
- The three-slip preparation sequence as evidence of technical depth, not just aesthetic preference. The chapter shows the knowledge system "at method level" — the rib tool, the consistency, the timing — per the v1.3/v1.5 rubric standard. This is exactly how I would want a ceramic knowledge system represented.
- The tanga's introduction from within women's ceremonial logic rather than as an exotic object of outside interest. This is the culturally authentic treatment.
- The honest limits: the chapter knows where the evidence ends and says so.

## What Needs Correction

1. **"Zoomorphic figures that read as animals and as something else simultaneously":** This is an accurate description of the formal ambiguity, but the chapter could be more specific: the most frequent zoomorphic elements are the caiman/alligator form and the bird (harpy eagle?) form — both of which have consistent positions in the Marajoara visual vocabulary. Naming these would strengthen the specificity without over-claiming their symbolic meaning.

2. **Tanga in the visual language section:** The chapter's parenthetical — "The tanga, a ceramic pubic covering unique to Marajó, was worn by women and buried with them" — is accurate but thin for an object this central to the tradition. The figures.md treatment is richer. Consider whether the chapter body should develop the tanga slightly more fully, as it is the clearest material evidence for women's ceremonial authority.

3. **"Among the most technically demanding ceramic works in the Amazon basin":** The E-1 Judgment Auditor flagged this. I concur: the comparative is factually defensible (no other Amazonian ceramic tradition of this period matches the Marajoara technical complexity) but the framing positions the objects relative to a regional baseline in a way that could be read as evaluative. The specific description — the three-slip preparation, the coiling technique, the firing conditions — is stronger than the comparative.

## Verdict

**APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS**

Conditions: (1) name the caiman and bird as the primary zoomorphic forms in the visual language section; (2) either expand the tanga passage in the chapter body or leave it to figures.md but not both — currently the treatment is slightly split; (3) replace the Amazon-basin comparative with specific material description. All three are minor to moderate adjustments. The chapter's interpretation is sound.
