# The War of Brothers

Atahualpa was bathing when the messengers arrived.

The hot springs at Cajamarca steamed in the afternoon air, the sulfurous water rising from the volcanic rock in clouds that hung in the valley before the mountain wind dispersed them. The Sapa Inca sat in the warm water with his attendants around him, the cumbi cloth that would be draped on his body when he rose folded on the stone rim, the chicha ready in ceramic vessels, the women of his household attending. He had earned this rest. The war was over. He had won.

Huascar's armies were destroyed. The generals Chalcuchima and Quisquis, Atahualpa's commanders, had driven south through the highlands, defeating Huascar's forces in a series of engagements that had consumed the empire's military reserves on both sides, and they had taken Qusqu, and they had taken Huascar himself, and the brother who had claimed the borla from the legitimate capital was now a prisoner. The civil war that had torn Tawantinsuyu in half was finished. Atahualpa was the sole Sapa Inca. The road from Quito to the Bio-Bio was his. The quipucamayoc would retie the knots. The mit'a would flow to one center again. The system would heal.

The messengers came from the coast. They described strangers. Men with hair on their faces. Men who rode animals the size of large llamas but faster and louder, animals that struck the ground with a sound like stone on stone. Men who carried tubes that made a noise like thunder and threw something invisible that killed at a distance. Men who wore metal that covered their bodies and could not be pierced by the weapons the coastal peoples had thrown at them. The strangers had come from the sea in houses that floated, and they were moving inland, toward the highlands, along the road.

Atahualpa listened. He was in the hot springs, in the valley of Cajamarca, in the Andes, in the world his great-grandfather had built. The strangers were a curiosity. They numbered, the messengers said, perhaps 168. Atahualpa had an army of 40,000 to 80,000 men behind him, battle-tested from the civil war, their commanders the finest generals Tawantinsuyu had produced. The calculation was simple, and the calculation was wrong, and the calculation was wrong in ways the quipucamayoc could not have knotted, because the knots had no category for what was coming, and neither did Atahualpa, and neither did anyone in the world he knew.

He invited the strangers to come to Cajamarca. He would receive them in the plaza. He would show them what a Sapa Inca was.
