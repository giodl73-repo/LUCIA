# Manden Kurufaba — "The Mande Covenant"

The founding of the Mali Empire is the story of Sundiata Keita — the Lion of Mali, the "hungering lion" who was born unable to walk and rose to defeat the tyrant Sumanguru Kante at the Battle of Kirina in 1235 and build the greatest state West Africa had yet seen. The Sundiata Epic is the Iliad of the western Sudan — a narrative told by jelilu (griots, the praise-singers and historians) across eight centuries, in dozens of versions, carrying in its variations the living memory of how the Mande people understand their own origins. Sundiata is not simply a historical figure; he is the model of what the Mande covenant requires: a ruler who has suffered, who knows exile and dependence, who has been tested by the world before being given the authority to rule it.

But the Kurukan Fuga — the "Great Covenant" proclaimed at the assembly that followed Sundiata's victory — is the Mali Empire's most consequential creation. Assembled from the leaders of the allied clans who had defeated Sumanguru, the Kurukan Fuga established rights, responsibilities, and the structure of the new polity in terms that were not simply royal decree but covenant between free peoples. It regulated social relationships between the nyamakalaw (craft specialists), the horon (free nobles), the jon (servants), and the jatigui (hosts). It established the right of women to speak in public assemblies. It prohibited certain kinds of violence and required certain kinds of hospitality. The Kurukan Fuga was not a constitution in the European legal sense; it was a covenant in the Mande religious and social sense — an agreement between parties that was sacred, not merely political.

Key places:
- **Niani** — the traditional location of the Mali capital; on the upper Niger; archaeology limited
- **Timbuktu** — the great city at the Niger bend; scholars, trade, the three great mosques
- **Djenné** — the inland delta city; trade, Islamic scholarship, the great mud-brick mosque tradition
- **Kangaba** — the heartland of the Mande; where the Sundiata oral tradition is maintained

## What Defined Them

**The Kurukan Fuga as governing covenant.** The assembly that produced the Kurukan Fuga was not a moment of royal generosity; it was a negotiation between allied powers who had just won a war together and needed a framework for living together afterward. The covenant regulated who could marry whom across clan lines, who held which craft monopolies, how disputes were to be resolved, and what the rights of different social categories were. That the Kurukan Fuga has survived in oral tradition for nearly eight centuries and was recognized by UNESCO in 2009 as an intangible cultural heritage is testament to how deeply the Mande world valued it. The Mali Empire was not simply a state built on gold; it was a state built on a covenant whose terms were understood and enforced by the jelilu who carried it.

**The jelilu and historical memory.** The jeli — the griot, the praise-singer, the historian — is one of the most distinctive institutions of the Mande world. The jelilu are not simply entertainers; they are the keepers of genealogical memory, the recorders of political history, the performers who give legitimacy to rulers by placing them in the context of their ancestors. The Sundiata Epic is maintained by jelilu who have carried it for thirty generations. The variations between versions are not errors; they are the traces of different political moments and different clients. The jeli tradition gave the Mali Empire a form of institutional memory that did not require writing to be authoritative.

**Mansa Musa and the world's knowledge of West African wealth.** When Musa Keita, Mansa (king) of Mali, made his hajj to Mecca in 1324–25, he traveled with an entourage estimated in the tens of thousands, including five hundred enslaved men each carrying a gold staff. He spent gold so extravagantly in Cairo and along the route that he disrupted the Mediterranean gold market for a decade — the price of gold in Egypt and the Levant fell sharply and did not recover for twelve years. The Catalan Atlas of 1375, a European map, depicts Musa seated on a throne in the center of Africa, holding a gold nugget, labeled as the richest king in the world. This was not a European fantasy; it was accurate, and it placed West Africa permanently in the European geographic imagination.

**Timbuktu as intellectual city.** The city of Timbuktu at the Niger bend was, at the height of the Mali Empire and continuing through the Songhai period, one of the great intellectual centers of the Islamic world. The Sankore mosque was a university where scholars from across North Africa, the Middle East, and West Africa came to study Islamic jurisprudence, theology, mathematics, astronomy, and medicine. The private libraries of Timbuktu's scholars held hundreds of thousands of manuscripts. These are not remnants of borrowed culture; they are the products of a genuine West African intellectual tradition that engaged with, contributed to, and in some cases led the Islamic scholarly world.

**The gold of Wangara and the secret that sustained the empire.** The gold of Wangara — the goldfields of Bambuk and Buré — was the material foundation of the Mali Empire's power, as it had been for Ghana before it. The Mali state controlled the trade routes from the goldfields to the Saharan crossing points and taxed them. The exact locations of the gold deposits remained a closely guarded secret. When Ibn Battuta visited Mali in 1352–53, he noted that he could not discover where the gold came from, despite asking. The secret was institutional: if the buyers knew the source, they could bypass the intermediaries. The secret was worth a kingdom, and for three centuries it held.

## The Chapters

**1 — Kirina:** Sundiata has crawled, then walked, then run. He is now in exile, gathering allies. Sumanguru controls the old Ghana territories. The Battle of Kirina will be decided — the jelilu say — not by numbers but by the answer to a single riddle about what can kill a king whose skin turns iron.

**2 — The Kurukan Fuga:** The allied chiefs assemble after the victory. They are not simply celebrating. They are negotiating the terms under which they will live together. A jeli recites the lineages. The rights are spoken aloud before they are agreed.

**3 — The Great Hajj:** Mansa Musa leaves Niani for Mecca with an entourage that will stun the world. A merchant in Cairo is about to discover that the price of gold has just crashed. Musa does not yet know this. He is being generous; it is the covenant.

**4 — The Sankore University:** An astronomer in Timbuktu is writing a commentary on an astronomical text from Baghdad. His students include young men from Morocco, from Egypt, and from villages two days up the Niger. This is not unusual. This is what Timbuktu does.

**5 — The Songhai Rising:** At Gao, a Songhai king is reviewing his river fleet. Mali is still the nominal suzerain. But the tribute has stopped coming. The fleet goes upstream. The next chapter begins at the Niger bend.
