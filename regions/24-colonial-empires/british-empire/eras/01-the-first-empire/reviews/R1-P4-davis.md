# PANEL-1 — Wade Davis
## The First Empire: Atlantic Commerce and Plantation

The knowledge system here is the ledger, and the opening renders it correctly: the ledger is not neutral record-keeping but a specific way of knowing the world, a method of making certain facts visible and certain facts invisible. "The manifest did not say what the *Thetis* intended to carry on the return leg because the return leg was handled by a different ledger" — this sentence identifies the knowledge system's deliberate architecture. It is not that the merchants did not know what was in the hold on the return leg. It is that they kept that knowledge in a separate epistemological space. This is exactly what Davis is looking for: the hidden condescension is not in the text's attitude toward the enslaved people but in the ledger's.

What the opening does not yet do is render the knowledge systems of the other parties in the transaction. The African traders on the Gold Coast — the Fante merchants at Anomabo who sold captives to the British — had their own accounting, their own understanding of the exchange, their own knowledge of what they were participating in. The Akan concept of pawning and debt-slavery existed within a framework entirely different from Atlantic chattel slavery, and the British trade systematically exploited that difference without acknowledging it. The opening is, by design, from inside the British system. But the chapter will need to show what happened when the British ledger encountered knowledge systems it could not accommodate.

**What I would push on:** The opening identifies the triangular trade's participants but renders most of them as functions: the manufacturer, the merchant, the captain, the planter. Only Norris, Roscoe, and Equiano have names and particularity. The African traders at Anomabo were sophisticated commercial actors with their own institutions, their own credit networks, their own political calculations. They were not passive suppliers. The chapter need not collapse into an account of African trading systems — that belongs in other regions — but even a single sentence acknowledging that the "exchange on the coast" involved parties with their own frameworks would prevent the chapter from inadvertently presenting the entire system as a British creation with African victims.

**The question I'd push on:** Equiano is described as having dismantled "the system from inside the vocabulary it had given him." This is true, and the framing is careful. But Davis would note that Equiano also brought knowledge the British vocabulary did not have — cosmological, embodied, cultural knowledge that exceeded the *Interesting Narrative*'s formal English. What survived the crossing? The chapter's anchor text (the Middle Passage chapter in Region 25) answers this fully. This chapter does not need to repeat it. But a sentence that acknowledges the limit of the British frame — what the British ledger could not record — would be consistent with the chapter's own argument about "a different ledger."
