# BOARD Review — Javier Álvarez-Mon
## Elamite Art, Material Culture, and Cultural Identity
## Macquarie University

---

My concern with most treatments of proto-Elamite and Old Elamite culture is that they reduce Elam to an administrative function — the scribal system, the redistribution economy, the political structures — without engaging with the Elamite material and artistic world that made this civilization something more than a bookkeeping operation. This chapter avoids that reduction, and I want to note where it does so effectively before raising one concern.

**What the chapter does well:**

The treatment of Inshushinak and Napirisha is the best thing in the chapter. The theological grounding — the grain is the god's grain, the redistribution is theology made administrative, the sukkalmah is the god's representative to the political world — is correct and important. This is the Elamite understanding of their own system. The chapter has found the inside of it.

The physical differentiation between Susa and Anshan — "the stone and altitude of Anshan," the different light, the different smells — reflects a genuine geographic and cultural reality. The highland and lowland Elamite worlds were different in ways that went beyond administrative convenience. The chapter's insistence on this physicality is appropriate.

**The concern:**

The material culture of the proto-Elamite and Old Elamite periods is richer than the chapter presents. The proto-Elamite period produced a distinctive glyptic tradition — cylinder seals pressed into clay with images of animals in herding and control scenes, the iconography of the redistribution economy expressed in visual form rather than just scribal form. These seals traveled with the tablets along the same trade routes. A merchant's seal pressed onto a tablet was not merely authentication; it was the visual signature of an identity, an image of control and ownership expressed in the vocabulary of the Elamite animal world.

The chapter mentions the scribal system extensively but does not engage with the seal tradition that ran alongside it. For a chapter about a culture that expressed its administrative logic in both written and visual form, the absence of the visual register is a gap. I would not require a full treatment — the chapter is already well-balanced — but one sentence or image from the seal tradition would make the material world more complete.

**On Chogha Zanbil (Dur-Untash):**

The chapter correctly notes that Chogha Zanbil will be built in Era 2. The brief reference to the ziggurat precinct at Susa — Inshushinak's precinct — as the physical form of the theology is correct. What I would add for accuracy: the Susa acropolis in this period was a significant architectural complex, not just a temple. The scribal school, the administrative buildings, and the god's house were part of a single architectural ensemble at the top of the mound. When the scribe worked in the god's service, he was working in a complex that announced the god's presence architecturally before you reached the inner sanctuary. The weight of the architecture around him was part of the experience.

**Factual note on Puzur-Inshushinak:**

The Linear Elamite inscriptions of Puzur-Inshushinak include not only royal declarations but also dedications to Inshushinak that describe specific building activities at Susa — the construction of a monument, the dedication of objects. The chapter could, if it wished, specify one of these building dedications to make Puzur-Inshushinak's royal inscription more concrete. The Louvre holds the relevant pieces. This is a suggestion rather than a required correction.

**Verdict: APPROVED with one suggestion (seal tradition / visual culture). Factual content is accurate. Cultural representation is endorsed.**
