# Panel Review — R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Opening: The Founding — The Experiment Begins

**Voice: Ryszard Kapuściński — Sensory immersion, heat, smell, sound, texture**

The nailed windows are everything. That detail is doing more than physical work. It is sealing the room. The men inside are writing a document about the consent of the governed and they have nailed the windows shut against the ears of the people who will be governed. This is not stated. It does not need to be stated. A good detail explains itself.

Madison writing faster than he sleeps — yes. This is embodied. This is a person, not a position. He goes back to his lodgings and expands the shorthand. The lodgings are not described, but they do not need to be, because the action is specific enough to place him physically. He is somewhere with candlelight and a quill and the smell of ink, and he is writing. We can see him.

**What works:**
The sentence "Outside the State House, enslaved people serve the delegates their meals" — its brevity is correct. It should be a short sentence. The shortness is itself a refusal to elaborate what does not need elaborating. The long, qualified sentence would have been the wrong choice. The short declarative sentence lands like a fact, because it is a fact.

The closing return to the physical world is earned: "cleared the dishes and went home to whatever home meant for them." This is sensory and uncertain at once. The reader's imagination fills the gap. The phrase "whatever home meant for them" is the kind of honesty that good travel writing achieves when the writer knows they cannot enter a world they haven't lived in and chooses to mark that limit rather than pretend past it.

**What I'd push on:**
The Philadelphia of 1787 is almost entirely absent from this opening. We are in a room with nailed windows — which means, deliberately, that we are cut off from the city outside. But the city is present: Philadelphia in 1787 was the largest city in North America, a port city with a mixed-race population, where free Black Philadelphians had churches and mutual aid societies, where Quaker abolitionists were passing petitions, where the smell of the Delaware River wharves mixed with the smell of horse dung and baking bread and the tanneries along the waterfront. The choice to keep the windows nailed is coherent, but it costs the opening some of its physical world.

I want to smell September. The late summer heat of the mid-Atlantic coast, the thickness of the air in a room with the windows sealed. Madison is writing in that room. His coat is damp. His quill scratches. The room smells like bodies and candle wax and anxiety. This is the founding of the United States. Give me the physical experience of being there.

**The question I'd push on:**
The people who served the delegates are the opening's sharpest image and then they disappear. They clear the dishes and they go home to "whatever home meant for them" and they are not seen again in the opening. The chapter will presumably bring them back — it must, given the mandate. But the opening should give some indication that their world is not merely a mirror held up to the founders' contradictions but a world in its own right. What does the chapter know about what those people were thinking, doing, building, in Philadelphia in 1787? That knowledge, or the honest acknowledgment of its limits, should be visible somewhere in the opening.
