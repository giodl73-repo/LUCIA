# Haebang — Opening: The Return

The ship cut through the cold water of Wonsan harbor on September 19, 1945, and a man stood at the rail watching the Korean coast rise toward him. The war with Japan had ended four days before. Soviet troops had already moved into the northern districts, and now the Americans held the south across an arbitrary line drawn by two powers who had never set foot on this peninsula before the war ended. The man at the rail was thirty-three years old. He had spent the last ten years in Yan'an with the Chinese communists. No one in Korea had seen him since the 1930s. No one in Korea knew if he was still alive. No one in Korea knew him at all.

The dock at Wonsan smelled of salt and diesel fuel. Soviet uniforms moved among the gathering crowd — Korean communists, some who had fought in Yan'an, some who had survived the occupation, some who had simply appeared from the underground cells of Pyongyang and the mining towns of the north. They stood waiting, not knowing what they were waiting for, knowing only that the Soviets had promised something.

When the Soviet commander presented him to the waiting crowd, he gave a name: Kim Il-sung.

The crowd's breath caught. A man named Park Hon-yong, who had led the underground Communist Party through the occupation, felt something crystallize in his chest. Kim Il-sung was the name of a legendary guerrilla fighter — the man who had fought the Japanese occupation for years, who had become a symbol of resistance when the occupation seemed total, who had died or disappeared into China or into legend itself. Park knew this name. Everyone in the crowd knew it. They believed the story it carried. The Japanese had tried to erase Korean history, but this name had survived in whispers. This name meant No. This name meant they had not been erased entirely. This name meant that somewhere in the world, a Korean had stood against the occupiers. And now he was here.

But in the corner of the crowd, a schoolteacher named Im Hae-jo stood watching. He had grown up near the home village of the original Kim Il-sung, the real one, the one people remembered. He watched the man descend from the ship — the set of his shoulders, the way he held his hands, the line of the cheekbones. Im Hae-jo's mouth opened slightly, then closed. 

The cheekbones were wrong.

The movement was wrong too — trained rather than native, the walk of a man who had been taught to look a certain way. Or maybe Im Hae-jo was wrong. Maybe memory had shifted the original Kim into something clearer than the human being who had actually lived. Maybe this was the same man, aged by ten years of exile. Maybe Im Hae-jo's uncertainty was itself the machinery of doubt, and he should trust the name instead of his own eyes.

He said nothing. Around him, the crowd was crystallizing into belief. The name was doing its work. The story that the Soviets were offering was true because Korea needed it to be true, and what Korea needed was stronger than what Im Hae-jo could know from a schoolyard childhood. Later, when Im Hae-jo would speak these doubts aloud, he would be arrested for them. He would spend the next five years in prison for the crime of noticing that the emperor was wearing someone else's face. The founding of North Korea could not tolerate the question he had asked. And so it would erase him, and then erase the question, until everyone who might have asked it had learned not to see what Im Hae-jo had seen.

The man — whether he was the legendary guerrilla or a carefully chosen instrument — walked into a peninsula that had just broken free from thirty-five years of Japanese rule. The Japanese Empire in Korea had been total: the language suppressed, the colonial police everywhere, the comfort women system conscripting Korean women for military rape, the entire economy restructured to feed Japanese military production. For a generation of Koreans, occupation had been the only reality they had ever known. When it ended, the rejoicing was authentic and overwhelming. In the streets of Seoul and Pyongyang, Koreans sang. The colonizers were gone. Korea was free.

But freedom is not the same as sovereignty. The peninsula was divided by two powers that had not asked the Koreans where they wanted the line drawn. The Soviets held the north; the Americans held the south. Neither power had come to Korea to end colonization. They had come to end Japanese power, and they had stopped where it was convenient to stop. The man who called himself Kim Il-sung was not stepping onto the deck of freedom. He was stepping onto the deck of occupation wearing a different uniform.

Park Hon-yong understood what the Soviets were doing, and he understood it was necessary. Korea was split. Korea was occupied. Korea had no army, no government, no international recognition. Korea needed a name that meant something, a figure that could hold the hope of a people who had just learned to speak their own language again after thirty-five years of Japanese prohibition. The man stepping off the ship might or might not be the legendary Kim Il-sung. But the name was real. The story was real. The need was real. Whether this particular body matched the legend mattered less than whether the legend could do the work that legends do: teach a traumatized people that they were not erased, that resistance was possible, that they could survive.

Over the next five years, the mythology would be installed into the bones of the new state. A Soviet officer would stand in a meeting room and explain to Korean communists how land redistribution would work. The first peasant family would receive their plot. A mother would walk the boundaries of what was now theirs, understanding that this earth might keep her children alive. In schools, children would learn to write Kim Il-sung's name in the correct stroke order. In study groups, communists would gather each week to read his words and discuss his thought. In cities, statues would rise. In homes, photographs would appear. The repetition would be constant, unrelenting, structured into every institution. By 1950, when the Korean War would begin, the people of North Korea would have been trained so thoroughly in the grammar of belief that questioning it would feel like questioning whether they were Korean at all. The mythology would have become indistinguishable from truth.

The real Kim Il-sung — the guerrilla fighter of legend — may have been dead for years. Or he may have been the man who stood at the rail of the Soviet ship watching Korea rise toward him. History cannot tell us, because the state that was built on his name could not tolerate the question. What we know is that the story was true in the way that matters most to a people emerging from colonial rule: it gave them back their own narrative. They had been told for thirty-five years that Korea was not capable of governing itself, that Koreans were subjects of the Japanese emperor, that Korean language and culture were inferior. Now they had a name that said No. They had a figure who had resisted. Whether that figure was authentic was secondary to what authenticity could mean in a moment when everything — language, identity, sovereignty — had just been returned to them after a generation of deliberate erasure.

The boat had arrived. The man had disembarked. The state that would follow would be unlike any state Korea had known before — centralized, totalitarian, organized around the worship of a single figure, sustained by an ideology that demanded faith. It would build cities and schools, create a nuclear arsenal, and imprison hundreds of thousands of its own people. It would survive wars, famines, the withdrawal of its patron states, and the hostile encirclement of most of the world. And all of this would be done, from the very beginning, in the name of a man who may or may not have existed, stepping onto a dock in 1945 and accepting a legend that had been waiting for him.

This is the founding of North Korea — not as an accident of history, but as an act of deliberate mythology, made possible because mythology was what a traumatized people needed to survive.
