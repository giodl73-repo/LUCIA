# BOARD Review — David Edwards

**Chapter:** Kingdom of Kush, Era 1: Kerma
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Framing Assessment

The chapter succeeds in its primary task: presenting Kerma from within rather than as a satellite or precursor of Egypt. The structural decision to place "Egypt Watches" as Section V, rather than as the frame for the entire chapter, is correct. The first four sections establish Kerma on its own terms. Egypt enters the narrative only after the reader already understands what Kerma was.

**The chokepoint thesis is sound.** The Third Cataract as the geographic foundation of Kerma's power is well-established in the scholarship and correctly represented here. The chapter does not claim that Kerma was solely a trade state, but correctly identifies the bottleneck position as the factor that turned a pastoral settlement into a kingdom.

**The pastoral economy is given adequate weight.** This is often underplayed in treatments of Kerma that focus on the burial tradition. The chapter's decision to devote an entire section to the cattle economy and the herdsman's knowledge is the right choice. The description of pastoral knowledge at method level (rain-reading, breed selection, grazing management) goes beyond what most treatments offer and is ethnographically plausible for upper Nile pastoral societies.

## Corrections

1. **Minor: fortress count.** The chapter states "twelve fortresses in a stretch of sixty miles" for the Second Cataract. The exact count depends on how one defines "fortress" versus "fortified settlement." The number varies between eight and seventeen depending on the source. Twelve is within the range and defensible, but consider softening to "more than a dozen" or "roughly a dozen" to avoid false precision.

2. **Minor: Kerma expansion during Second Intermediate Period.** The chapter states that there is "evidence of Kerma occupation at Buhen." This is broadly correct: Kerma-period material culture appears at former Egyptian fortress sites. However, "occupation" may overstate the evidence. Consider "Kerma-period material culture appears at Buhen" to be more precise.

## Recommendation

**APPROVE with minor corrections.** The chapter's framing is sound. The factual base is accurate. The two corrections are minor precision issues that do not affect the chapter's argument or its cultural representation.
