# Production Bible — The Founding (Kaianere'kó:wa)

## Sources

### Primary
- **The Great Law of Peace (Kaianere'kó:wa)** — Oral tradition, preserved through wampum recitations and recorded in multiple versions. The most complete English renderings include Arthur C. Parker's 1916 transcription and the version maintained by the Onondaga Nation. Contributes: the structure of the Grand Council, the roles of clan mothers and sachems, the condolence ceremony, the founding narrative.
- **Wampum belts** — The Hiawatha Belt (founding document of the confederacy), the Two Row Wampum, condolence strings. Physical objects held by the Onondaga and in museum collections. Contributes: the mnemonic technology, the material culture of law.

### Academic
- **William N. Fenton, *The Great Law and the Longhouse* (1998)** — The most comprehensive scholarly treatment of the confederacy's political structure and founding traditions. Contributes: institutional analysis, dating debates, the condolence ceremony's role in governance.
- **Daniel K. Richter, *The Ordeal of the Longhouse* (1992)** — The Haudenosaunee from the perspective of inter-nation politics and European contact. Contributes: the Mourning War complex, demographic context, the pressures that shaped the confederacy.
- **Dean R. Snow, *The Iroquois* (1994)** — Archaeological and ethnohistorical overview. Contributes: material culture, settlement patterns, dating of the confederacy's founding.
- **Barbara A. Mann and Jerry L. Fields, "A Sign in the Sky" (1997)** — Argues for a 1142 CE founding date based on a solar eclipse. Contributes: the dating controversy and the honest-uncertainty treatment required.

### Needed (ACQUISITION)
- More detailed versions of the Jigonhsasee tradition from Haudenosaunee-authored sources
- Archaeological evidence for population stress / Mourning War intensification pre-founding

## Key Figures

| Name | Role | Why They Matter |
|------|------|----------------|
| Skennenrahawi (the Peacemaker) | The one who brings peace; founder of the Great Law | The figure whose message transforms five warring nations into a confederacy. His halting speech and reliance on Aiionwatha to carry the message gives him human texture within the tradition's reverence. |
| Aiionwatha (Hiawatha) | Mourner, orator, the Peacemaker's companion | The human heart of the founding. A man destroyed by personal grief who becomes the voice of a political philosophy. His journey from grief to condolence to advocacy is the emotional arc. |
| Atotarho | Onondaga sachem; the last holdout | The antagonist who becomes the keystone. His transformation from the most dangerous man among the five nations to the Keeper of the Central Fire is the founding's most dramatic moment. |
| Jigonhsasee (the Mother of Nations) | The first person to accept the Peacemaker's message; the model for clan mother authority | Absent from the opening (Achebe flagged this). Must be present in the full chapter. Her acceptance precedes the nations' acceptance, and her role establishes the principle that women's authority is foundational, not supplementary. |
| The clan mothers (collective) | Holders of the power to nominate and remove sachems | The institutional backbone. The chapter must show their authority as structural, not ceremonial. The dehorning power is the Great Law's accountability mechanism. |

## Narrative Arc

The founding of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy is a story about how grief becomes governance. The arc moves from crisis (the Mourning War cycle, a self-reinforcing system of violence with no internal exit) through intervention (the Peacemaker's insight that the cycle can only be broken by addressing grief itself) to institution (the Great Law as a living system of consensus governance, maintained by oral recitation and wampum).

The central tension is not military but psychological and political: can five nations that have been killing each other's children for generations agree to replace captive-taking with condolence, fragmented sovereignty with federated governance, and the authority of war chiefs with the authority of sachems chosen by clan mothers? The answer is yes, but only because the solution addresses the root cause (grief) rather than its symptoms (raids), and only because the political architecture distributes power in a way that gives every party a stake.

The arc should feel like consensus-building itself: deliberative, patient, building one agreement at a time, each agreement making the next one possible. The prose rhythm mirrors the political process.

## Panel Pressure Points

**Tuchman** will push on pacing in sections 4 and 5. The architecture of the Grand Council and the dehorning process risk becoming descriptive rather than narrative. Both sections must be anchored in scenes — the Council meeting as a specific event with specific people speaking, the dehorning as a specific exercise of power.

**Ibn Khaldun** will push on sections 2 and 4. Why did this particular form of governance emerge? What were the structural conditions (ecology, demography, the longhouse as economic unit) that made consensus governance viable? The chapter must explain the functional logic, not just describe the outcome.

**Achebe** will push everywhere the text might step outside the worldview. The greatest risk is in section 4, where the Grand Council structure could slip into textbook description. Also in section 6, where the dating controversy could introduce a modern scholarly voice. Both must remain inside the culture's own perspective.

**Davis** will push on sections 2, 4, and 5. The wampum as knowledge system, the condolence ceremony as political technology, and the dehorning as accountability mechanism all require the same depth that literate traditions' institutions would receive.

**Kapuscinski** will push on every section. The northeastern woodlands — the hemlock and birch, the lakes, the cold, the smell of elm bark, the sound of voices in a longhouse — must be present throughout. The longhouse is not just a metaphor; it is a building. The wampum is not just a concept; it is shell beads with a weight and a color and a sound when they are moved.

## Sensory Palette

- **Visual:** Smoke from the central fire rising through the longhouse roof. The purple and white of wampum beads. The great white pine. Snow on the lake. The antler headdress of a sachem.
- **Sound:** Voices echoing in a longhouse (bark walls, low roof, many fires). The quiet when a speaker pauses for consensus. The sound of wampum strings being handled. Birdsong in old-growth forest.
- **Smell:** Elm bark (sweet, slightly musty). Woodsmoke. Dried corn. The cold air off the lake.
- **Touch/Feel:** The weight of a wampum belt. The smoothness of shell beads under the thumb. The cold of water crossing the lake. Bark walls rough under the hand.

## Voice Notes

Deliberative. Patient. The rhythm of consensus-building: statement, pause, response, pause, agreement. Repetition as structure — because the Great Law was recited, not read, and repetition is how oral law maintains itself. Sentences that sound carved, like proverbs. The prose should move at the pace of a council meeting, not at the pace of a battle. When the weight is heavy (the combing of the serpents, the planting of the tree), the prose is plain.

## Open Questions

1. **Dating:** When was the confederacy founded? Estimates range from c. 1142 CE (Mann & Fields, based on a solar eclipse) to c. 1451 CE (Snow, based on archaeological evidence) to c. 1550 CE (other scholars). The chapter must present this honestly. The Haudenosaunee tradition does not date the founding in Common Era years — it dates it by the events themselves.
2. **The Peacemaker's identity:** Some traditions name him as Skennenrahawi from the Wendat (Huron) nation. Others emphasize that his origins are outside the five nations. The chapter should present what the traditions say without settling the question.
3. **Jigonhsasee's role:** The degree of her prominence in the founding narrative varies across traditions and scholarly sources. Some place her as the first convert; others give her a more structural role. The chapter should present the strongest version supported by the traditions while noting variation.
