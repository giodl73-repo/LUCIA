# Ñuu Dzaui — "People of the Rain"

They called themselves the Ñuu Dzaui — the People of the Rain — because they lived in the cloud-draped highlands of Oaxaca where the weather came from the mountains and the lords were sons of trees and rivers. The name the Nahuatl world gave them, Mixtec, comes from the same logic: Mixtlan, "the place of clouds." They lived above the clouds, in the rugged highlands that the Spanish would call the Mixteca, in a world of deep valleys and dramatic ridgelines where the rainfall was erratic and the corn grew on terraced hillsides carved from the mountain at tremendous labor. This landscape of effort produced lords who ruled by genealogy and scribes who recorded genealogy in the most elaborately beautiful books the pre-contact Americas produced.

The Mixtec screenfold book — the tōnalamatl, the painted deerskin accordion — was the central technology of Mixtec political life. Eight pre-contact Mixtec codices survive, and they record what no other Mesoamerican tradition preserves in such depth: the genealogies of ruling dynasties stretching back to the mythological age, the marriages and wars that connected one city-state's lineage to another, the sacred days on which particular rituals must be performed, and the faces — fully individualized — of lords and ladies who ruled specific valleys in the ninth and tenth centuries. The Mixtec did not simply record their history. They painted it in a visual language that any trained reader could follow across every screenfold panel like a story told in images.

Key places:
- **Tilantongo (Ñuu Tnoo)** — "Black Town"; the seat of the dynasty of Lord Eight Deer Jaguar Claw, the most famous Mixtec ruler; his story fills multiple codices
- **Tututepec (Yucu Dzaa)** — the Pacific coast power founded by Eight Deer; a port city that gave the Mixtec access to Pacific trade goods
- **Yucuñudahui** — "Hill of Rain"; an important early Mixtec center with strong connections to the codex tradition
- **Monte Albán (later period)** — the Mixtec occupied Zapotec tombs at Monte Albán and reused them for their own burials; Tomb 7 contained the richest treasure ever found in Mesoamerica

## What Defined Them

**The screenfold book as governance.** The Mixtec codex was not a luxury item or a religious object separate from political life. It was the political record — the proof of a lord's right to rule, stored in genealogical lines that stretched back through named ancestors to the moment the first lords descended from trees and rivers at the world's beginning. To contest a lineage was to unfold the book and point to the painted faces. To legitimate a marriage was to lay two opened books side by side and show how the bloodlines converged. The book was the state's memory, and the scribe who kept it was as powerful as any warrior.

**Genealogy as divine mandate.** Every Mixtec ruling house traced its ancestry to supernatural origins — lords who had descended from sacred trees, or from the Underworld, or from the union of a deity couple who first organized the highlands. This was not mythology kept separate from politics; it was the political system itself. A lord who could not demonstrate divine descent in the codex's painted panels could not govern. A lord who could show an unbroken line from the primordial ancestors to himself had the cosmos's endorsement for whatever he did. Eight Deer's career — his military campaigns, his three wives, his nose-piercing ceremony (the mark of full lordly status) — was understood as the actions of a man whose ancestors had been destined to unify the highlands since the world began.

**Gold as the medium of cosmological statement.** Mixtec goldsmiths were the finest in Mesoamerica, producing works that combined lost-wax casting with filigree and inlay in combinations of gold, turquoise, and shell that had no equivalent anywhere in the Americas. These objects were not wealth in an economic sense — they were the material form of the lord's divine connections. The gold pectoral showing the Sun God, the turquoise mosaic skull, the jaguar vessel from Tomb 7 at Monte Albán: each was a concentrated statement about the wearer's or owner's place in the cosmic order. The Aztec Triple Alliance coveted Mixtec goldwork and placed it high on their tribute lists. They understood what it meant.

**The lord's body as sacred text.** Mixtec political identity was inscribed on the body: the nose plug that marked full lordly status (a ceremony that required a senior lord's validation), the specific costume elements tied to particular sacred days, the facial paint and headdress combinations that identified rank and lineage to any observer. A lord moving through the marketplace was a walking codex page — his rank, his lineage connections, and his sacred associations visible in his dress to anyone who could read the visual language. The scribal tradition and the political tradition were not separate. They used the same language.

**The valley as the political unit.** The Mixteca was not unified until Eight Deer forced a brief consolidation. Its normal condition was a constellation of valley-based city-states, each governed by a royal house whose genealogy connected it to neighbors through strategic marriages. These marriages were not alliances in the modern sense — they were cosmological mergers, the combining of two sacred bloodlines to produce a successor whose divine mandate was doubled. The political map of the Mixteca was always a marriage map, and the scribes who kept the codices were tracking something that was simultaneously political history and sacred genealogy.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Age of the Codices' Origins:** A scribe in the highlands begins painting the first panel of a genealogical record. The events she is recording are already generations old. The visual language she is using will carry what she knows forward for five hundred years.

**2 — The World of Eight Deer:** Lord Eight Deer takes the nose plug that marks him as a full lord, under the patronage of a Toltec ally who performs the ceremony. He is not yet thirty. He has already fought more than a hundred battles. The codex records every one.

**3 — The Fragmented Lords:** After Eight Deer's assassination, the highlands fracture back into their valleys. In Tilantongo, the scribes are still painting. The genealogies continue. The fragmentation is political; the memory is not fragmented at all.

**4 — The Mixtec-Aztec World:** A Mixtec goldsmith in Tenochtitlan completes a commission for the Triple Alliance. The object he makes will travel to Spain, where it will be described as "the most beautiful thing" its European observers have ever seen. He does not know this. He knows what the object means in his own terms.
