# El Porfiriato y La Revolución
## Era 4 — 1876–1920 CE

---

The Plan de Ayala said what the villages had been saying for thirty years without anyone writing it down.

Emiliano Zapata carried it out of the sierra of Morelos in November 1911, signed by twenty-seven men at the village of Ayoxustla. It was not a declaration of war. It was a declaration of what had already happened: that the haciendas had swallowed the communal lands, that the courts had legitimized the swallowing, that the ejidos — the village commons held since before the Spaniards came — had been surveyed into private property by men with instruments who arrived with legal papers and left with land. The Plan said: restore what was taken. It named the hacendados. It named the lawyers. It named Porfirio Díaz, still clinging to power in Mexico City, as the source of the rot. And then it named Zapata as the commander who would take the land back.

He was thirty-one years old. He had watched the hacienda of Hospital absorb the fields of Anenecuilco, a Nahua village in the tierra caliente of Morelos where sugarcane grew tall enough to hide a man on horseback and the smell of the mill came down the valley when the wind turned. The village records — title documents going back to 1607, maps drawn in the colonial era showing which land belonged to which community — had been locked in an iron box and buried in the church floor when it became clear that anyone holding indigenous title deeds was in danger. Zapata knew where the box was buried. He had been elected president of the village council at twenty-nine, which meant he was responsible for those papers, which meant he was responsible for Anenecuilco itself, for the memory it carried and the argument it could still make.

He dug up the box. He took the documents to the state capital of Cuernavaca. The governor told him the courts were the proper venue. He took the documents to the courts. The courts told him the land had been properly transferred. He took the men of Anenecuilco to the fields and they planted the disputed land while the harvest season still permitted it and waited to see who would come to stop them.

No one came that season.

## I. La Tierra Caliente — The Porfiriato and the Land

The railroad arrived in Morelos in 1881.

Before the railroad, the sugar of Morelos traveled by mule train to Mexico City — a journey of days across the mountains, which made Morelos sugar expensive and kept the haciendas small enough that the village ejidos could hold their margins. After the railroad, Morelos sugar reached the capital in hours. The price fell; demand rose; the haciendas expanded to meet it. The hacienda of Hospital, which had grown alongside the village of Anenecuilco for decades in a condition of tension but not yet displacement, now needed more cane land. The village's milpa fields — cornfields and bean rows and squash vines growing together in the Nahua polyculture that had fed families here for a thousand years — were suddenly in the way.

The legal instrument was the Ley de Baldíos, passed in 1883 under Díaz's direction. The law declared that land without formal cadastral registration — individually titled, bounded by surveyed markers, showing evidence of private productive use recognizable under European property law — was baldío, vacant, available for transfer to private ownership. The surveying companies that performed the surveys were paid in kind: one-third of whatever they classified as vacant. Their incentive was to classify as much as possible. The Nahua villages of Morelos held their ejidos under a system of communal tenure that had no individual title documents, that was organized around seasonal use patterns and shared water rights and boundary markers known by the community but not recorded in any form the surveying companies were required to recognize. The land was not vacant. It was full — full of corn, full of memory, full of the particular knowledge of which plots drained and which held water and where the ceiba trees marked the old field edges. The law could not see that knowledge. The law saw: no title, no fence, no cadastral registration. Vacant.

The surveyor arrived. He set up his transit in the cornfield of Anenecuilco, measured the land, drew his maps, wrote his report. The hacienda of Hospital received the transfer documents. The village received notice that the land it had farmed since 1607 — since before the Díaz dictatorship, before the Reform War, before independence, since the Spanish colonial system had at least recorded the indigenous communities' rights in its own legal forms — now belonged to someone else.

This happened not only in Morelos. In Yucatán, the henequen boom of the 1880s and 1890s converted the peninsula's land into sisal plantations worked by Maya laborers bound by debt peonage. A Maya worker who fell into debt to a henequen planter could not leave; the debt passed to his children. The export figures for henequen rose through the 1890s and were reported in the newspapers as evidence of Mexico's modernization. The hacienda owners of Yucatán called their laborers gente de razón — people of reason — and meant by this that they were managed correctly, which was to say that they could not leave. Díaz called this progress. The Científicos who advised him calculated the revenues and agreed.

The Científicos were the technocratic inner circle of the Porfiriato: engineers, lawyers, economists trained in European positivism who believed that Mexico's problem was its insufficient modernity and that the solution was foreign investment, rational administration, and the suppression of the forms of communal life that obstructed rational administration. José Limantour, the Finance Minister, negotiated the railroad concessions with American and British capital that laid fourteen thousand miles of track between 1876 and 1910. The railroads brought investment, telegraph lines, manufactured goods, and market integration. They also restructured the economy in ways that made the hacienda system's expansion not merely possible but economically necessary from the perspective of anyone who had invested in it. Foreign capital needed land security. Land security required that communal tenure be extinguished. The surveying companies were the instrument; the Ley de Baldíos was the authority; the rurales — Díaz's rural police, riding in pairs along the roads — were the enforcement.

Porfirio Díaz himself was from Oaxaca, Zapotec by ancestry, mestizo by identification. He had fought heroically at the Battle of Puebla in 1862 against the French intervention — a general's general, celebrated for his courage. He had made himself into the servant of a system that dispossessed people whose origins were his own. He did not think of this as contradiction. He thought of it as necessity: Mexico needed order before it could have progress, and order required that the forces of disorder — land disputes, village claims, indigenous communal resistance — be managed into silence. The rurales managed it. In the state of Yucatán, Governor Olegario Molina managed it. In the state of Morelos, Governor Manuel Alarcón managed it. The system was national; its instruments were local; its revenues flowed to Mexico City and to the offices of the foreign companies that had financed the railroads.

In Anenecuilco, the village council met to discuss what to do about the loss of the cornfields. They had been meeting to discuss this since the 1870s. They had documents going back to 1607 proving the village's title to the disputed land. They had taken those documents to the courts and the governors and the federal authorities, and had been told, each time, that the transfer was legally complete and that no further review was available. They buried the documents in the church floor. They elected Emiliano Zapata to guard them.

## II. Order and Progress — The Díaz System

Díaz had been president, or the power behind the presidency, since 1876. He had seized power from Sebastián Lerdo de Tejada in a military coup, then legitimized himself through elections that he controlled. His slogan — Orden y Progreso, Order and Progress — was borrowed from Auguste Comte's positivism and applied to Mexico with the logic that the country's instability (fifty years of it since independence, military coups, the Reform War, the French intervention) required a strong hand to make development possible. The strong hand was his.

He was, in the early years, genuinely effective. He reduced banditry by incorporating bandits into the rurales or eliminating them. He balanced the federal budget, which had been in chronic deficit, through Limantour's fiscal management. He attracted foreign investment on a scale Mexico had never seen. American capital built the railroads; British capital extracted the oil from the Gulf Coast fields that the Díaz government opened to foreign exploitation under concession terms that kept royalties low and profits abroad; French capital built the textile mills of Puebla and Orizaba where workers put in twelve-hour days and were paid in company-issued scrip redeemable only at company stores. The foreign investment figures were cited in European financial press as evidence of Mexico's emergence into the modern economy. They were correct about the investment. What they did not report was the labor code in the textile mills, or the debt peonage in Yucatán, or the land transfers in Morelos.

The jefe político was the local instrument of Díaz's system — appointed, not elected, by the state governors who were themselves appointed or approved by Díaz. The jefe político collected taxes, managed elections (which meant delivering the required results), resolved disputes in favor of whoever had purchased his loyalty, and suppressed dissent at the level where it was small enough to suppress. Above him was the governor. Above the governor was Díaz. The patronage network ran from the village to the capital, and every link in it was a transaction.

Re-election was the system's political expression. Díaz had come to power with the slogan Sufragio efectivo, no reelección — effective suffrage, no re-election — which he proceeded to ignore. By 1887, he had amended the constitution to permit his own re-election; by 1904, he had extended presidential terms to six years. He was seventy-three years old in 1903 and showed no intention of leaving. The men who might have succeeded him — the cabinet ministers, the governors, the military commanders — were kept in competition with one another, each dependent on Díaz's favor, none permitted to build an independent base. When Díaz told an American journalist in 1908 that Mexico was ready for democracy and that he would welcome opposition in the next election, he may have believed it. He was wrong.

Francisco Madero read the interview in 1908 and took it seriously. He was thirty-five years old, heir to a landholding family in Coahuila, educated in Paris and at the University of California at Berkeley, believer in spiritualism and in the democratic promise of liberal constitutionalism. He was not poor. His family owned haciendas. He was fighting for democracy, not for land. He traveled Mexico in 1909 and 1910 making speeches for effective suffrage, drawing crowds that surprised everyone, including him. Díaz had him arrested before the 1910 election, held him in prison through the voting, released him after the results were certified. Madero fled to Texas.

From San Antonio, in October 1910, he issued the Plan de San Luis Potosí. It called the election fraudulent, declared Díaz's government illegitimate, called for Mexicans to rise in armed revolt on November 20, 1910. The revolt that followed was not what Madero had planned. He had imagined a civic movement, rallies, perhaps some armed pressure in the north. What happened was Pascual Orozco and his fighters in Chihuahua, Villa's men raiding federal garrisons, the Zapatistas moving through Morelos village by village, and Díaz's rurales discovering that the country they had policed for thirty years was no longer willing to be policed.

Díaz left on the Ypiranga in May 1911. The ship was German. The port was Veracruz, where Hernán Cortés had landed almost four hundred years earlier. Díaz is said to have told a companion that Mexico would come to regret his departure. He died in Paris in 1915, which is to say before he could know how much remained to be regretted, and by whom.

## III. The Grito Nuevo — Revolution's First Year (1910–1911)

In Morelos in the spring of 1911, Zapata rode. He was a horseman by reputation — he had trained horses for the hacienda owners who employed him before the revolution, and he trained them well, and the contradiction of serving the system he was now fighting against was one he had lived with quietly until it became impossible to live with any longer. His army was not an army in the formal sense: it was the men of the villages, armed with machetes and whatever rifles they could take from federal garrisons, riding under the banner of Tierra y Libertad — land and liberty — which was also the slogan of the anarchist Ricardo Flores Magón's Partido Liberal Mexicano, though Zapata had come to it independently from the specific conditions of Morelos.

When Zapata's men took a town, they returned the land records to the village authorities. They did not install a new jefe político. They did not establish a Zapatista administration. They returned the papers that proved the villages' claims and let the villages hold them. This was the revolution they were making. It was local, specific, documentary — the archive as the instrument of liberation, the village council as the legitimate authority.

Madero became president in October 1911. He was not yet comfortable. The revolutionary coalition that had overthrown Díaz included Zapata and Villa and Orozco and a dozen other regional commanders who had fought for different reasons and were now being asked to disarm. Madero's position: the Díaz regime was overthrown, democracy had been restored, it was time for the armed movements to stand down and participate in the constitutional order. The land question would be addressed through legislation, through the courts, through the proper channels.

Zapata had tried the proper channels. He had taken the documents to Cuernavaca and to the courts and to the federal authorities, and had been told each time that the transfer was legal. He wrote to Madero explaining the situation in Morelos. Madero replied that he understood, that the agrarian question would be addressed, that patience was required. Zapata's advisers and the village councils of Morelos held meetings. The conclusion of the meetings was the Plan de Ayala.

The Plan was read aloud at Ayoxustla in November 1911, in Nahuatl and in Spanish, to the men who signed it. It declared Madero a traitor. It declared the land transfers illegal. It declared that one-third of the lands held by the haciendas would be expropriated immediately, with compensation, and returned to the ejidos; that haciendas that opposed the revolution would be expropriated entirely. It named Zapata as commander in chief of the Liberating Army of the South. It was not a negotiating position. It was a statement of what the villages required before they would put down their rifles.

The corridos spread it. The ballad singers — the corridistas — composed their verses in the Morelos villages and carried them on the roads: Zapata was fighting for the people, Zapata would not surrender, the land was the people's land and the hacienda could not keep it. The corrido was Mexico's oral newspaper, faster than print and carried on the voice where print did not reach. Within weeks, the Plan de Ayala was known in every village in Morelos and beyond it.

## IV. La Decena Trágica — The Hydra (1913–1914)

In February 1913, generals loyal to the old Porfirista order rose against Madero in Mexico City. The rising was called the Decena Trágica — the Tragic Ten Days — because it lasted ten days of bombardment in the capital's center that killed civilians and left the Zócalo smelling of gunpowder and burning wood. The coup was planned partly with the knowledge of Henry Lane Wilson, the United States Ambassador, who wanted a stable government that could protect American investments and found Madero insufficiently stable. General Victoriano Huerta, whom Madero had appointed to suppress the rising, switched sides. Madero was arrested. Four days later he was shot while allegedly attempting to escape, which no one who knew him believed.

Huerta ruled for seventeen months. He was a drunk and a murderer and he made no pretense of constitutional legitimacy. He had the old Porfirista support and the backing, briefly, of the United States, until Woodrow Wilson replaced Taft and decided that backing a coup government was incompatible with the democratic principles he was committed to exporting. The Americans landed at Veracruz in April 1914 to prevent a German arms shipment from reaching Huerta. The landing united Mexican public opinion against the United States even among people who opposed Huerta.

Three revolutionary movements fought Huerta simultaneously: the Constitutionalists under Venustiano Carranza in the north, with Álvaro Obregón as the military commander in the northwest and Villa's Division del Norte in Chihuahua; and the Zapatistas in the south, who had been fighting Huerta's federales and Huerta's predecessors without interruption. They did not have the same politics. Carranza was a Coahuilan landowning senator who wanted to restore constitutional government and had no intention of redistributing land. Obregón was a methodical military modernizer from Sonora who organized his forces with tactical precision and read the war literature coming out of Europe. Villa was a caudillo from the Chihuahuan desert whose personal loyalty commanded an army of rancheros, miners, and railroad workers who had no interest in constitutional theory but a great deal of interest in defeating the men who had oppressed them.

Huerta fell in July 1914, fled to Europe, and died in a Texas prison in 1916. The revolutionary coalition had won the war against him. It then turned on itself.

The Convention of Aguascalientes, in October 1914, was the attempt to unite the victors. The Constitutionalists, the Villistas, and the Zapatistas sent delegates. The Zapatista delegation arrived with the Plan de Ayala and refused to negotiate it: the land provisions were not a bargaining position. The Convention accepted the Plan de Ayala in principle. Carranza rejected the Convention and withdrew his delegates. The revolution split into two.

Zapata and Villa entered Mexico City together in December 1914. The photographs show them side by side in the presidential palace: Villa seated in the presidential chair with a broad grin, Zapata beside him in his embroidered jacket and enormous hat, his face serious and watchful. It was the revolution's high-water mark and its last coalition moment. Villa did not understand what Zapata was fighting for; Zapata did not trust Villa's politics; they had in common only the enemy.

Obregón, commanding the Constitutionalist forces, had been reading the reports from the Western Front. At the Battle of Celaya in April 1915, he dug trenches, strung barbed wire, positioned machine guns on the flanks, and waited. Villa's cavalry charged. The Division del Norte, which had swept across Chihuahua and Durango in 1913 and 1914 like a force of nature, rode into artillery and machine gun fire at Celaya in the way the cavalry of Europe had been riding into it for a year. Villa charged three times. Each time the same. In April and June 1915, at Celaya and León, Obregón destroyed the Division del Norte as a military force. Villa was reduced to guerrilla raiding in Chihuahua, which he would conduct until his assassination in 1923. Zapata held Morelos.

## V. Querétaro — The Covenant Written (1916–1917)

Carranza controlled enough of Mexico by late 1916 to convene a constitutional congress. He chose Querétaro, the city where Maximilian had been executed in 1867, where Mexican sovereignty had been recovered from French imperial overreach. The symbolism was deliberate. The Constitution of 1857, which had been Mexico's fundamental law before Díaz undermined it, would be replaced.

Carranza's draft was moderate. He wanted municipal freedoms (the right of local governments to govern without appointed interference), effective suffrage, the separation of church and state reinforced. He did not want to touch the agrarian question beyond vague language about rural development. He did not want to antagonize the landholding elements of the Constitutionalist coalition — the Sonoran ranchers, the northern business class, the Coahuilan senators — who had supported the revolution against Díaz and Huerta because they wanted free elections, not because they wanted their haciendas redistributed.

The radical delegates at Querétaro were not his people. They were young military officers, schoolteachers, labor organizers, minor provincial politicians who had fought in the revolution at the level where the fighting was intimate — company commanders who had seen the villages, labor lawyers who had worked with the textile workers of Orizaba, teachers who had taught in the Morelos schools. They saw the draft constitution and amended it.

Article 27 as the radicals wrote it declared: the ownership of lands and waters within the national territory corresponds originally to the Nation. The ejido — the communal village land — was restored as a legal category that could not be alienated. Land held in violation of this principle was subject to expropriation with compensation. The subsoil — its oil, its metals, its minerals — belonged to the Nation, not to the foreign companies that had been extracting it under Porfiriato concession terms. Every foreign investment in Mexican oil, every British pound invested in the Tampico fields, every American dollar invested in the Sonoran mines, was now subject to national claim.

Article 123 declared: the length of the workday shall not exceed eight hours. Minimum wages shall be established. Workers shall have the right to organize. Companies shall not pay wages in scrip redeemable only at company stores. The textile mills of Puebla and Orizaba, where workers had been putting in twelve hours a day for company-issued tokens since the 1880s, would have to change.

Carranza accepted the final text because he needed to. He was still fighting Villa in the north and Zapata in the south. A constitution that acknowledged the agrarian and labor demands was politically useful. Implementing it was a different question.

## VI. Chinameca — The Covenant and the Bullet (1918–1919)

Zapata held Morelos. The Constitutionalist forces under Pablo González had been trying to pacify the state since 1916 with methods that the villages remembered: burned crops, killed livestock, destroyed irrigation works to prevent the Zapatistas from eating. The tactics drove families out of the villages into the mountains. The Zapatistas fought back. Morelos was ungovernable from Mexico City's perspective, which meant it was still the Zapatistas' state.

By 1919, Zapata's army was reduced. The war had gone on for eight years. The men were tired. The resources were thin. The Constitution of 1917 had been written, but no land had been returned to the villages. Carranza had appointed new hacienda administrators, not ejido commissioners. The promise of Article 27 was in the text; its implementation was not in the budget.

Colonel Jesús Guajardo was a Constitutionalist officer in Morelos. In late March 1919, he contacted Zapata's camp with a message: he was disgusted with Carranza's betrayal of the revolution, he wanted to bring his men and his arms over to the Zapatistas, he was ready to defect with four hundred soldiers and a quantity of ammunition. The offer was enormous. Zapata's advisers told him it was probably a trap. One of them, Palafox, told him explicitly: do not go. Zapata sent a letter asking for proof of Guajardo's sincerity. Guajardo executed a number of his own soldiers who had been disciplined — former Zapatistas who had surrendered — as proof. Zapata found this convincing. On April 10, 1919, he rode to the hacienda of Chinameca with a small escort.

The hacienda courtyard was quiet when he arrived. The soldiers of Guajardo's garrison stood at attention. A bugle played the march for honors. When Zapata rode in, the soldiers raised their rifles and fired. He died in the courtyard of the hacienda of Chinameca, in the state of Morelos, among the sugarcane fields that had been the whole argument.

The corridos said that he did not die. He rode off into the mountains; he would come back when Mexico needed him. The villages of Morelos knew better, and they kept fighting anyway.

Obregón overthrew Carranza in 1920. Carranza fled north, was killed in Tlaxcalantongo. Obregón became president. He negotiated peace with the remaining Zapatista forces under Gildardo Magaña. The Zapatistas surrendered their arms. In exchange, Obregón promised that the agrarian question would be addressed. The ejido lands were not returned in 1920. Some land redistribution began under Obregón and accelerated under Calles in the 1920s, but the systematic implementation of Article 27 waited for Lázaro Cárdenas in 1934, fifteen years after the revolution officially ended.

## VII. The Covenant Unpaid

Anenecuilco in 1920 was a village with fewer families than it had in 1910. The revolution had taken men into its armies and not returned all of them. The hacienda of Hospital was still there. The ejido lands that the Plan de Ayala had demanded returned had not been returned. The Constitution of 1917 said they should be. The government of 1920 said the process was under way.

The village council of Anenecuilco still met. The iron box that Zapata had dug up from the church floor — the title documents, the colonial-era maps, the 1607 records of the Nahua community's land — had been taken out of the state during the worst fighting to keep it safe. It came back. The council put it back in the archive. The claim was still in it.

The corridos kept Zapata alive in the way they kept everyone: Zapata no se rindió, Zapata no se murió — Zapata did not surrender, Zapata did not die. The corridistas sang it in the cantinas of Morelos and in the villages of Guerrero and Oaxaca and Puebla, where the same land question persisted. The corrido is not propaganda; it is memory. Memory of what was promised and what was not delivered is not the same as forgetting.

The Constitution of 1917 remained. Article 27 remained. The ejido as a legal category remained. They remained the way a promissory note remains when the creditor is dead and the debtor is claiming the debt has been paid. The villages of Morelos were the creditor. They held the note.

The revolution had made many things. It had ended the Díaz dictatorship, destroyed the old hacienda political system, produced the most progressive constitutional document of its era, established the legal frameworks for labor rights and agrarian reform and national ownership of the subsoil. It had killed — in fighting and in the famine and epidemic that followed — between one and two million Mexicans out of a population of fifteen million. The ratio belongs in any account of what the revolution cost.

What the revolution had not made, by 1920, was the ejido it had promised. That wait would continue.

The Plan de Ayala had named what was required. The Constitution of 1917 had written what was promised. The villages of Morelos counted the years between the writing and the delivery and found that the years multiplied without end.

They are still waiting.
