# Panel-1 Review — Ryszard Kapuscinski
## Samanids Era 2: The Peak — Opening

**Lens:** Sensory immersion — heat, smell, sound, texture
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The lamp. The lamp is the right detail. A secretary who brings a lamp into a sunlit afternoon room tells you everything about how he thinks and where his mind lives — in a space it has made for itself, independent of the day's conditions. I can smell that room. Paper and sesame oil and old correspondence. The letter from Baghdad that has been folded and refolded until it is separating along its creases — I can feel the texture of that paper, the softness of the worn fold lines. This is sensory writing that trusts the reader to inhabit the detail rather than being lectured at through it.

The city section is less successful in its sensory register. The description of Bukhara — the markets, the walls, the mausoleum under construction — is precise but does not quite put me there. The silk from Khotan and the lapis from Badakhshan are named but not felt. What does Samarkand paper smell like compared to papyrus? What sound does the Friday mosque make when the calls to prayer overlap in a city where the Arabic of worship and the Persian of the market coexist in the same street? The room scene is inhabited; the city scene is described.

The final image — Ismail Samani turning the word over in his mind, the city doing the same — is beautiful and earns its place. It is the right closing image for an opening about a word that carries a civilization's theory of itself. But I want the full chapter to give me more of the physical specificity of Bukhara: the feel of Transoxiana in the 880s, the heat and dust and the cold of the mountains behind the city, the sound of a world that speaks in two languages at once.

**The question I'd push on:** What does the Samanid court physically look like? The amir is present only in reported speech — "he asked me last month." I want to feel the room where that conversation happened. Was it a tent? A mud-brick audience hall? A garden pavilion? The full chapter should give me at least one scene in which Ismail Samani is physically present, and in which the texture of his court — what you would smell and hear standing in it — is rendered.
