# P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of "When the Empire Reached the Sea" Opening

**Overall:** The opening identifies the correct structural question — the resilience of institutions when individual rulers fail — and frames it through a concrete event. The Kurukan Fuga as a self-testing mechanism is the chapter's analytical spine, and I am satisfied it has been found early.

**What works:**
- The structural question is the right one. "What happens when the covenant holds and the king does not?" is not merely narrative; it is historiographical. It asks how a polity survives succession failure, which is the central problem of all dynastic systems and the one my Muqaddimah devotes the most attention to. The chapter has announced that it understands the difference between anecdote and mechanism.
- Abu Bakr's departure is presented as a test case, not as exotica. The reader understands that the mansa's disappearance is not the point — what the disappearance reveals about the system's resilience is the point. The deputy becomes the mansa. The covenant holds. This is institutional continuity under stress, and the opening treats it as such.
- The Dyula trade network and the goldfields are mentioned as structural elements, not decorative ones. The economy is present as a system, not as color.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening has not yet explained WHY the Kurukan Fuga proved resilient. To say the covenant "held" is to assert a conclusion without tracing the mechanism. The full chapter must show the specific institutional features that enabled the covenant to survive bad rulers: the role of the jelilu as independent carriers of the covenant's terms, the assembly's authority to depose a mansa (as happened with Khalifa), the Dyula network's economic decentralization that prevented any single ruler from monopolizing trade. Resilience must be analyzed, not merely claimed.
- Abu Bakr's Atlantic expedition is dramatic but needs analytical framing. What material conditions made such an expedition possible? The Manden's western expansion to the coast, the knowledge of Atlantic currents from Dyula coastal trading, the surplus wealth that could fund a speculative maritime venture — these are the structural preconditions, and the chapter must trace them.

**The question I'd push on:**
What is the precise mechanism of institutional survival during succession crisis? The Manden endured Khalifa (deposed), a period of freed-slave rule, and Abu Bakr's voluntary disappearance. Each of these is a different type of succession failure. The chapter must distinguish between them and explain what specific institutional features (the assembly's deposition power, the jelilu's covenant-keeping, the Dyula's economic independence from the court) allowed the system to absorb each shock. Pattern, not anecdote.
