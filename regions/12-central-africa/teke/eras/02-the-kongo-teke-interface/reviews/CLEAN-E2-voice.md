## Baseline Voice

Established in the opening paragraph: transactional, declarative, world-weary. The broker's voice. Short sentences for decisions and prices. Longer clause-chains for the running calculation. No sentimentality. No ornament. The pause mechanism is the period, not the em-dash.

Representative baseline passage: "He struck it against his ring. He turned it over. The casting seam ran where it should. He passed it back."

## Voice Audit — Full Chapter

**Section 1 (Plateau Market):** Voice consistent with baseline throughout. The calculation-chain sentences ("the same way a man with his eyes open makes calculations — not deliberately, but as a habit of sight") sustain the broker's register while doing the chapter's analytical work. Zero em-dashes.

**Section 2 (The Makoko's Ground):** Voice holds. The shift from the broker's compound to the plateau geography risks textbook drift ("The Bateke Plateau sits above the Congo Basin like a table edge..."). Assessment: acceptable — the landscape description is concrete and sensory, not encyclopaedic. "A man on the plateau can see who is coming before they arrive. A man in the forest cannot." Two declarative sentences that carry both geographic and strategic meaning in the broker's register. This is the voice doing analytical work, not departing from the voice.

The onkani passage holds its register well. "This is not metaphor for political influence. The onkani was what the plateau was." The declarative certainty here is the broker's certainty — he knows this, lives in it, does not explain it.

**Section 3 (Three-Way Calculation):** One minor finding.

*Finding 1 — P2 (single sentence departure):*
Paragraph: "The Kongo kingdom, to the south and west, understood this."

"Understood" is a summary-register word — it tells us what the Kongo kingdom knew rather than showing it through the transaction. The surrounding sentences show the Kongo's behavior (sending agents, paying Teke prices), so the understanding is demonstrated, not just asserted. Recommend cutting "The Kongo kingdom, to the south and west, understood this." The preceding paragraph establishes the mechanism; the following paragraph establishes the behavior. The summary sentence is not needed.

**Section 4 (The Pool):** Voice at its strongest in this section. The Pool scene is the chapter's most sustained sensory passage, and it does not drift into catalogue or textbook. "The sound of the Pool market was audible before you saw the Pool itself" — the shift to second person ("you") here is a single deliberate move that brings the reader bodily into the scene, consistent with the broker's voice, which occasionally speaks as "you" when generalizing from professional knowledge. This is the chapter's voice having absorbed the second-person construction naturally rather than borrowing it.

**Section 5 (New Commodity):** The P3 finding flagged by E-1 sits here. Voice assessment: the sentence ("The system that moved them operated by different physics...") represents the chapter's single clearest voice-risk. The surrounding paragraphs are firmly in the broker's register. The sentence itself has a slightly elevated analytical register — "different physics" is a metaphor the broker might not use literally, though it is consistent with a broker who has been thinking in material terms all his professional life. E-2 assessment: P3 — author decides. The voice risk is real but contained.

**Section 6 (Landing):** Voice consistent with baseline and with the opening. The circular return ("The pool was always the answer. But the pool was not always the price.") is the chapter's best moment — it completes the register, not just the content.

## Em-Dash Count

Zero. The voice found its own pause mechanisms (the period for decisions, the comma for calculation-chains, the semicolon for related consequences). This is the benchmark the rubric describes.
