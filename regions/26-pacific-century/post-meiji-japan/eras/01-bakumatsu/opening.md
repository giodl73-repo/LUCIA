# Bakumatsu: The End of the Shogunate
## Opening

The ships were black, and they came in the morning.

Tōjirō, the harbor master at Uraga, had served the Tokugawa shogunate for thirty years. The bay had been calm his entire tenure — calm as a thing could be when you served an empire that wanted no ships, no strangers, no disturbance of the order that had held for two hundred years. The edict was clear. Foreign vessels approaching the coast would be denied. If they refused to leave, they would be sunk. Tōjirō had never had to implement this. In thirty years, the bay had belonged to Japan alone.

July 8, 1853. Four ships. They did not look like ships.

The lookouts reported first: massive hulls, black iron, plumes of smoke rising where the sails should be. Not junks. Not any vessel Tōjirō's men had ever seen. They were steaming directly into Edo Bay, and they did not slow.

Tōjirō stood on the Uraga fortification and watched them come. The gunners waited for the order. He was a harbor master, not a military commander, but the edict made him the first guardian of the shogunate's order. He could feel the weight of two centuries in that moment — two hundred years of deliberate seclusion, of closed borders, of a Japan that had decided it did not need the world and the world could not have it. The fire stations along the bay were burning signal fires. The cavalry was being mustered. The message was spreading toward Edo — toward the shogun himself — even as Tōjirō tried to understand what he was looking at.

Four ships. One of them flew the American flag.

The lead vessel maneuvered with an ease that defied the mathematics of its size. Tōjirō had studied enough of the Dutch reports — the few that reached Japan in secret — to understand that these were powered by steam engines, not wind and current. The Americans could move these ships against wind, against tide, against the will of Uraga. The implication arrived before the ships did: Japan had a way to say no. The Americans had a way to make no sound like a question.

An officer rode up behind Tōjirō. Nakajima, the brigade commander. He was younger, but he was military. The harbor master was deferring now — he could feel it happening, the shift in authority as the appearance of the ships pulled power from the civilian administration toward the men trained for war.

"We fire on the signal," Nakajima said. Not a question.

Tōjirō hesitated. In thirty years, no one had fired. In thirty years, the edict had been enough. The world had respected the invisible boundary, or the world had not tried to cross it, or something in the geometry of time had protected Japan from having to choose.

"Commodore Matthew C. Perry," someone said. A man with better Dutch than Tōjirō held a translated letter. It had come by small boat — a messenger requesting permission to land. "He requests an audience with the shogun. He brings messages from the American president."

The letters were in Perry's hand, written to be delivered. The ships were in the bay. The American had not asked for permission to enter Edo Bay; he had entered and then announced that he wished to negotiate. The edict said no ships. The edict said no foreigners. The edict said Japan was closed, and this had been true for two centuries because everyone — everyone — had obeyed it.

Now someone had not.

Tōjirō looked at the ships, at the smoke, at the gunners waiting for an order he could not give alone. The shogunate in Edo was debating. The emperor in Kyoto had not been consulted — the emperor was not consulted on matters of state anymore, that was the arrangement, that had always been the arrangement. Power and decision-making had concentrated in Edo, in the hands of the shogun's council, in the protocols that had maintained order by maintaining separation. Everything about the way Japan had organized itself, the way power flowed, the way decisions were made — all of it was predicated on a single assumption: we have decided we are closed, and the world will respect the boundary.

The ships suggested otherwise.

The American did not fire. The Japanese did not fire. For four days, the ships remained in the bay, at anchor, and the Japanese could see what modern naval power looked like from inside their own harbor. Reports of their size, their firepower, their absolute indifference to the winds that governed the junks Japan had built in the previous two hundred years — these reports spread by horse toward Edo, toward Kyoto, into every domain and every castle of the empire. Word moved faster than the ships moved. By the time Perry left to sail south toward the port of Shimoda, everyone in Japan knew: the age of sakoku was ending. No one yet understood what would take its place.

The shogunate agreed to receive the American again. They purchased time by agreeing to negotiate. Perry left to trade in China and gather supplies. He would return in the spring with more ships, more men, more weight to add to the request. The letters would sit on the shogun's desk, demanding an answer. The world had entered Edo Bay, and Tōjirō, who had never in thirty years had to say yes or no to an order that fundamentally violated everything the domain served, would have to live with the fact that saying no had simply become impossible.

Behind him, the signal fires along the coast burned on. The message was still spreading. Every domain in Japan was now asking the question that no one had needed to ask for two centuries: what do we do when the boundary is broken?
