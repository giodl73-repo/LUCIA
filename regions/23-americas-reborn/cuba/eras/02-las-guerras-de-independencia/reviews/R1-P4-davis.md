# R1-P4 — Wade Davis — Panel Review of Opening

**Round:** 1
**Reviewer:** Wade Davis (P-4)
**Chapter:** Cuba Era 2 — Las Guerras de Independencia — La Manigua
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The manigua passage is the opening's best moment, and it is best because it takes seriously what the manigua actually was as a social and cosmological space. The statement that "the manigua was not a retreat. It was a republic" is not merely rhetorical. It identifies something real about how the mambises organized their world — the wilderness as legitimate political territory, governed by its own structure, sustained by its own knowledge systems including the Yoruba religious traditions the African fighters carried. This is the correct depth. The manigua as a Taíno word carrying the landscape's indigenous memory, as an Afro-Cuban spiritual geography, as a military-political republic — this layering is what I want to see more of in the chapter.

The Santería dimension is not developed here beyond one sentence, and the chapter will need to go further. The Yoruba religious system practiced by African-Cuban mambises (Lucumí, as the Cuban form was called) was not merely background to the independence war — it was a knowledge and solidarity system that operated in the manigua with its own logic, its own hierarchy, its own resources. The orixás were not invoked for comfort; they were invoked for protection, for guidance, for the specific forms of knowledge that the manigua required. Antonio Maceo's soldiers knew this. It is part of what the chapter needs to render at depth, not just gesture toward.

The exile knowledge system is well-rendered: the cigar factory readings, the tobacco workers' wages becoming revolutionary infrastructure. This is a knowledge transmission system of the same order as the Yoruba religious network in the manigua — a distributed system for sustaining community and purpose across physical separation. The parallel between the two systems (exile solidarity and manigua solidarity) is the chapter's richest potential vein. The writer should mine it.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening describes Martí's knowledge of the revolution's requirements — the logic of the proof, the credibility of presence — in ways that honor his intellectual sophistication. But it does not yet describe the knowledge systems of the mambises themselves: the tracking knowledge, the botanical knowledge (which plants could treat wounds, which could be eaten, which were poisonous), the communication systems (how messages moved through the manigua without being intercepted), the religious knowledge that governed who could fight, when, under what conditions. These are intellectual achievements as real as Martí's political analysis. The chapter should name them at the level of how they worked.

**What's working:** The discovery of the manigua as a layered concept — etymological, ecological, political, cosmological — is exactly the right analytical foundation. The chapter should not abandon this for a more conventional military history. The war is the context; the manigua is the culture.
