# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Review — Opening, Round 1

**Chapter:** Yanomami — Era 1: The Parima World
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The voice is right.

This is the first thing I look for, and I found it immediately. The prose does not explain the Yanomami world to me. It is the Yanomami world. The hekura arrive. The inner landscape must be maintained. The shapori is doing a maintenance task. None of this is presented as remarkable, exotic, or in need of contextualization for a reader who might not know what a shapori is. The reader learns by inhabiting, not by being taught.

The moment I would want to examine most carefully is the passage about what "outsiders would likely have misunderstood." This is the riskiest moment in the opening, because it is the moment where the author must be most careful not to become the enlightened outsider explaining a misunderstood people. I read it three times. My judgment: it passes, but narrowly. The passage is written from inside — the error is attributed to the hypothetical outsider, and the shapori's work is presented as self-evidently ordinary to everyone inside the shabono. The old woman looking up and looking back down is the right detail: she is not watching a ceremony, she is noting that the maintenance man is at work. This is inside-voice honesty operating correctly.

But I agree with what Tuchman will likely say: the passage announces itself slightly. The phrase "the thing that outsiders, if they had been present, would likely have misunderstood" — could the same move be made without naming the outsider at all? Could the chapter simply show the old woman's glance, the children playing, the ordinary sounds of evening, and trust the reader to understand that this is not separate from the life around it? The prose has earned that trust. Consider using it.

The Achebe Test: would a thoughtful Yanomami reader — or a reader who knows the Yanomami world as documented in Kopenawa's testimony — recognize this scene? Yes. The shapori's work as maintenance rather than ceremony is exactly the distinction that Kopenawa makes repeatedly in *The Falling Sky*. The chapter has grounded itself in the culture's own self-understanding, not in the anthropological archive's distortions.

One additional observation: the chapter should carry the reahu through the full chapter with the same dignity the opening carries the hekura. The overview notes that early anthropological accounts sensationalized the consumption of the dead's ashes. The chapter should address this practice — it is central to Yanomami cosmology — with the same inside-voice discipline. The chapter should not avoid it, and should not brace the reader for it. It should simply render it as what it is: the most intimate possible form of keeping the dead present.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening shows a world in which the Yanomami's own self-understanding is presented with full dignity and from inside. Can the chapter maintain this throughout, particularly when it addresses the practices that have been most misrepresented? Not whether the chapter can avoid those practices, but whether it can inhabit them from inside without flinching and without explaining.

The voice is right. Do not let it slip.

— CA
