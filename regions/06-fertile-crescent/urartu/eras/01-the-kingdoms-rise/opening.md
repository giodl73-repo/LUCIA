# The Kingdom's Rise

The first inscription is in their language, not his own.

Sarduri, son of Lutipri, inscribed his name on the rock face at the southern edge of the citadel of Tushpa, where the basalt drops sheer to the shore of the lake the people of Biainili called the Sea of Nairi. He wrote in the cuneiform script of Ashur. He wrote in the Akkadian tongue. He used the formula that Assyrian kings had used for centuries: "Sarduri, son of Lutipri, the great king, the mighty king, king of the universe, king of the land of Nairi, a king having none equal to him, a shepherd to be wondered at, fearing no battle, a king who humbled those who would not submit to his authority." The phrases were borrowed. The titulature was Assyrian down to the last epithet, and the stone that held it was cut with the same deep wedge-marks that the masons of Ashur and Kalhu used on the palace walls of Assyria's own kings.

He carved in their script because in the ninth century before the common era there was no other script in which to carve. The highland peoples of the region the Assyrians called Urartu had no writing system. The language they spoke was their own, unrelated to Assyrian or to any Semitic tongue, but it had no signs, no wedge-marks, no means of making words stay. The Assyrians had been recording their deeds for a thousand years. The kings of Biainili had been fighting them for nearly as long. Sarduri's choice was not submission. It was acquisition. He took the technology of the enemy and used it to announce that the enemy now had a peer.

The rock at Tushpa was basalt, dark, volcanic, the kind of stone the highland produced in abundance from the eruptions that had shaped the plateau over millennia. Basalt holds a chisel mark the way iron holds an edge: cleanly, deeply, and for a very long time. The masons who cut Sarduri's inscription worked in the open air, on the southern face of the citadel rock, above the water. The chisel rang on basalt, a sharp, clean sound that carried across the still surface of the lake and echoed off the cliffs. The wind off the Sea of Nairi carried cold even in summer, cold and the smell of the highland: juniper scrub on the slopes, dry volcanic dust, woodsmoke from the settlement below the walls where the herders and metalworkers and farmers of the highland lived under the protection of the king who was carving his name above them. In winter the lake surface froze along the margins and the snow covered the plateau and the rock face was a dark stripe against white ground, the letters visible from a distance because the fresh-cut stone was lighter than the weathered surface. The inscription faced south. South was the direction of Assyria.

Sarduri's father Lutipri is a shadow in the record. No inscription of his survives. He exists only in his son's text, a name in a patronymic, and what Sarduri thought of the man or what the man built before the inscription, the stone does not say. The Assyrian annals mention highland chieftains and raiding kings in the region they called Nairi and Uruatri, but the names shift and the borders are unclear, and the Assyrian scribes recorded these peoples as problems, not as kingdoms. The Assyrians had been coming north for generations, burning settlements, extracting tribute, carrying off cattle and horses and bronze vessels, and going home again because the highland was too cold, too steep, too far from the grain supply of the Tigris plain to hold permanently.

What changed, around the middle of the ninth century, was consolidation. The highland chieftains who had fought separately against Assyrian raids and against each other began to coalesce around the fortress at Tushpa and the authority of the king who held it. Sarduri built the citadel walls. The foundation stones were massive, cut from the volcanic rock and fitted without mortar, each block weighing several tons, laid in courses that followed the contour of the natural rock. The Assyrian king Shalmaneser III, who campaigned against Sarduri around 856 BCE, described the fortress as formidable. He claimed victory, as Assyrian kings always claimed victory. He did not claim to have taken Tushpa. The walls held. And the highland that the walls protected held something else: water, running in streams from the mountains to the lake, water that could be channeled and carried if a people knew how to cut a course through rock.

The fortress held. The inscription held. And within a generation, something shifted on the basalt face of the Van rock. The kings who followed Sarduri began to write in a different language. Ishpuini, Sarduri's son, and then Menua, Ishpuini's son, carved their inscriptions in the language the people of Biainili actually spoke: Urartian, rendered now in the same cuneiform signs that Sarduri had borrowed from Assyria, but arranged to capture a tongue that was not Assyrian, that was not Semitic, that belonged to the highland. The script was borrowed. The language was their own.

This is the sequence that matters. The first king writes in the conqueror's tongue to announce himself as a king. His son and grandson write in their own tongue, using the conqueror's signs, to announce themselves as a people. The shift from Akkadian to Urartian on the rock face at Tushpa is not a detail of epigraphy. It is the record of a kingdom finding its voice, carved into the same stone where it had first spoken in someone else's.
