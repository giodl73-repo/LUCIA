# R1 — Panel Review: Chinua Achebe
## Durrani / Era 1: The Founding

**Reviewer:** P-3 Chinua Achebe
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (Opening review)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Voice Check

The first question I ask is always: whose voice is this? Not whose story — voice. Who is speaking?

This opening belongs to the Pashtun world. The prose does not look at the Loya Jirga from outside; it sits in the circle. The detail about the assembly's nine days being conducted in Pashto — the language of the qawm — is exactly the right kind of information to offer, exactly in the right place. It tells the reader: this gathering had its own language, its own register, its own terms. The author has understood that language is not just communication but structure.

I am satisfied with the Pashtunwali section. I know the risk of listing cultural codes — it can become a catalogue, a museum exhibit: "here are the customs of these people." The author avoids this by embedding the list in an argument: these things required no throne. The code is presented as a complete system, sufficient in itself, which is exactly right. Pashtunwali is not a supplement to governance. It IS governance, in a form that requires no external enforcement.

## Where I Felt the Culture Most Clearly

"The qawm — the clan, the community, the blood-tie that made a man someone rather than no one." This is inside. Not "the Pashtun kinship system" — the qawm, with its function explained through what it GIVES a man, not what it is called by an anthropologist.

The Sabir Shah moment is handled well. The holy man's role in the assembly is presented not as superstition but as a legitimate political instrument — the religious sanction that allows the assembled khans to accept the decision without any individual khan having to bear the responsibility for proposing it. This is sophisticated political understanding rendered from within.

## Concerns

I want to raise one thing about the final paragraph: "the weight of a thousand years of Persianate civilization settling onto Pashtun shoulders." This sentence is doing a lot of work and I want to examine it.

It is written from inside the Durrani self-understanding — the Persianate tradition was a prestige inheritance, and the Durrani court knew it and cultivated it. So the image is not wrong. But the phrase "settling onto Pashtun shoulders" risks a slight external weight — as if the author stands above and watches civilization descend upon its new carriers. A small risk. The chapter could render this from Ahmad Shah's own awareness of the inheritance he was accepting, which is historically attested. He was a poet. He knew what Persian civilization meant.

## What I'd Push On

The Persian court culture must appear in the full chapter as something LIVED, not as a description of what was practiced. Ahmad Shah wrote poetry in Persian and Pashto. If there is a verse — even a fragment — it should appear in the chapter, doing what his poetry did: demonstrating that the Persian language was not a veneer on Pashtun military power but was itself a form of power, a way of making authority legible across the empire's vast linguistic variety.

## The Question I'd Push On

**Does the full chapter render the bilingual Durrani world — Pashto in the tent, Persian in the chancery — as Ahmad Shah himself would have moved between them?** Not as an analyst describing code-switching, but as a man for whom both languages were real, carrying different registers of authority, and who used each at the right moment for the right purpose.
