# Era 2: The Portuguese Encounter — Afonso I
## The Letter Written in Latin

---

The letter is written in Latin.

This is the first fact, and it is not a small one. It is the year 1526 of the Christian era — the sixteenth year of the reign of Manikongo Dom Afonso I, lord of the Kongo kingdom, master of Mbanza Kongo, ruler of lands whose full extent no map in Lisbon has yet accurately drawn. The letter goes to Dom João III, King of Portugal, and it is composed with care. The secretary who holds the quill has been trained in Lisbon. The Latin is correct. The arguments are precise. The king knows what he is saying, and he knows that the King of Portugal will be able to read it, because they share, Afonso believes, a faith that obligates them both.

He writes: *And we cannot reckon how great the damage is, since the mentioned merchants are taking every day our natives, sons of the land and the sons of our noblemen and vassals and our relatives, because the thieves and men of bad conscience grab them wishing to have the things and wares of this Kingdom which they are ambitious of...*

He is writing about the slave trade. He is writing to the man who profits from it.

There is nothing naive about this letter. Afonso I has been watching the Portuguese for forty years. He was born before they came. He became a Christian — not because a missionary pressed a cross into his hands and he yielded, but because he saw in Christianity something that mapped onto the Kongo world's deepest categories: the intersection of the living and the dead, the covenant made visible in sacred objects, the power that flowed between heaven and earth. The crucifix looked, to a Kongo eye, like the cosmogram — the crossed lines that the Bakongo drew on the ground to mark the place where the two worlds met. He chose baptism as a young man, took the name Afonso, fought a civil war to claim the throne from a brother who had not converted, and won. He took his victory as confirmation. God had ratified his kingship.

He then built. He built a cathedral in Mbanza Kongo, which the Portuguese called São Salvador — the Savior — and which was, when Afonso's masons finished it, among the finest stone churches in all of Africa, and one of the few built by African craftsmen to an African ruler's commission. He sent young nobles to Lisbon and to Rome to be educated — his own son, Henrique, went to Rome, studied theology, returned, and was consecrated by Pope Leo X as the Bishop of Utica and Auxiliary of Kongo, the first sub-Saharan African bishop in the Catholic church. He established schools. He corresponded with the Vatican. He maintained, in Mbanza Kongo, a court that could conduct diplomatic business in Latin and Portuguese and that knew the protocols of Christian sovereignty as well as any minor European principality.

He was using European legal and theological categories to bind the Europeans. If the covenant of Christianity was real — if Dom João was in fact a Christian king, bound by the same moral architecture that Afonso had staked his reign upon — then the covenant obligated something. It obligated the slave traders to stop seizing Kongo's free subjects. It obligated the King of Portugal to enforce the laws he had been petitioned to enforce. The letter of 1526 is not a supplication. It is an argument from first principles. Afonso is invoking the shared framework that, he believes, neither of them can repudiate without confessing that their faith is theater.

Outside the palace at Mbanza Kongo, in the year the letter is written, the trade is operating. At Mpinda, at the mouth of the Congo River, the Portuguese traders have established a presence, and the goods moving through that point are not only the copper and ivory and raffia cloth that the Kongo kingdom produces. They are people. They are Kongo subjects — people who should be, under the legal categories of both Kongo sovereignty and Christian ethics, free. They have been seized by raiders, sold by provincial governors who have learned that a captive body is worth more to a Portuguese slaver than a year of ordinary trade, passed down a chain of exchange that ends on a ship heading west.

The king knows this. He has tried to stop it. He has prohibited the traders from traveling inland without his permission. He has tried to impose a committee of inspection to regulate what the Portuguese take out of his kingdom. The Portuguese have ignored these measures, or worked around them, or simply continued as if the Manikongo's laws applied only to people who chose to obey them.

So he writes a letter. He writes in Latin so that it cannot be misunderstood. He writes in the formal register of a sovereign addressing a sovereign. He writes in the language of the Church because the Church is the institution that both of them, in theory, answer to. He is asking Dom João to enforce the covenant.

The letter exists. It sits today in the Portuguese national archives, the Arquivo Nacional da Torre do Tombo, in Lisbon. Its Latin is correct. Its arguments are intact. Its grievances are documented. The reply that came, when it came, was not adequate to what had been asked.

But the letter was written. And in writing it, Afonso I did something that no amount of slave raiding could undo: he put the argument on record, in the language of the people who held the archive, and the argument is still there.

## The Cosmogram and the Crucifix

In the Kongo world, the cross was older than the Portuguese.

The cosmogram — dibundu in some sources, the four-movement pattern in others — was drawn in the ground where covenants were made, where healers worked, where the living reached toward the dead. It was two lines: one horizontal, running between the world of the living and the world of the dead, marked at its midpoint by the kalunga line — the river, the threshold, the permeable boundary between existence and what lay beyond it. One vertical, running from the sky through the earth, tracing the path the soul made when it was born downward into the living world and when it died upward into the world of the ancestors. The intersection of these lines was where power gathered. Where a healer set their hands. Where a covenant was witnessed.

The kalunga was not metaphor. It was geography. The Congo River was the kalunga — the boundary between this world and the world of the dead was the river itself, running wide and deep below the plateau where Mbanza Kongo sat. The dead lived below the water. The ancestors rose from beneath the river's surface and descended back into it. A healer who worked the crossing between these worlds worked at the river's edge, or drew the cosmogram in the earth, or held the nkisi that marked the intersection point. The structure of the cosmos was visible in the landscape. Every Kongo child grew up knowing where the boundary was.

When the Portuguese missionaries arrived in 1491 at the court of the Manikongo, and showed the Kongo court the crucifix — a human figure mounted on a crossed structure — the court's recognition was not the recognition of something alien. It was the recognition of a familiar pattern: a being at the intersection of the horizontal and vertical lines, positioned exactly where power resided in Kongo cosmology. The Christian story — a man who died and crossed the kalunga line and came back — was, in Kongo terms, a story about a being who had made the crossing and returned with knowledge of what lay beyond. This was not the same as Christianity. But it was close enough to a Kongo category that it could be inhabited, revised, made meaningful. The missionaries had brought a cosmogram with a figure on it, and the figure stood where, in Kongo understanding, figures of power always stood.

Afonso did not convert because the Portuguese convinced him. He converted because the Christian framework, understood in Kongo terms, offered resources he could use: a covenant structure more durable than any he had access to within the Kongo system alone, a network of obligations that reached across the Atlantic, and a shared vocabulary with the most powerful maritime force his kingdom had yet encountered.

The nkisi in his court understood all of this.

A nkisi is an object charged with the power of a covenant. The most well known to European observers were the nkisi nkondi — iron-nail figures, human in form, their surfaces studded with iron nails and blades, each nail recording a covenant made in the nkisi's presence. The nkisi did not merely symbolize the covenant; it held it. When two parties agreed to something in front of a nkisi nkondi — a boundary, a debt, an obligation — a nail was driven into the figure's body. The nail was the record. The nkisi was the witness. If one party violated the covenant, the nkisi's power turned against them; the community of believers in the nkisi's authority ensured that violations had consequences.

The effectiveness of the nkisi depended on the certainty that every member of the community knew the covenant was recorded and would be held accountable for it. When missionaries arrived and burned the nkisi — calling them "fetishes," objects of idolatry — they were not destroying superstition. They were destroying a contract registry. They were erasing records.

Afonso built his Christian project on the understanding that the Christian God could be the nkisi of the covenant with Portugal. A covenant witnessed by God, in the presence of the Church, was a covenant that the whole community of Christendom would enforce. This was a theory of power. It was not wrong, exactly. But it required the Portuguese to be, in fact, a community that enforced its covenants. That requirement was the theory's vulnerability.

There was one more dimension to the theory. In the Kongo system, a person who violated a covenant witnessed by a powerful nkisi called the nkisi's force against themselves. The violation had automatic consequences, distributed through the community's shared acknowledgment that the covenant was binding. Afonso's letters to the Portuguese crown were, in this framework, the equivalent of the nail driven into the figure: he was making the record visible, making the covenant explicit, making the violation public and therefore accountable. If the Christian God worked the way the Kongo God did — if the covenant was genuinely witnessed and its violation genuinely consequential — then writing the letter was not a supplication. It was a legal act.

## The School and the Ship

The investment Afonso made in the Christian project was not rhetorical. It was material, generational, and expensive.

He sent young men to Lisbon. Not servants, not traders — the sons of Kongo nobles. They went to learn Latin and Portuguese and theology and the protocols of European diplomacy. Some were gone for years. Some died in Portugal, of diseases the Kongo plateau's body had no defense against. Some returned fluent in languages the elders did not speak, trained in liturgical practices the nkisi keepers did not share. The investment was a bet: that the generation trained in European languages and disciplines would be the generation that could hold the Portuguese to the covenant on equal terms.

His son Henrique was the highest expression of this bet. Born around 1495, sent to Portugal as a child of approximately eight, educated in the languages of the Church, Henrique traveled from Lisbon to Rome and studied theology at the Sacra Rota. In 1518, Pope Leo X consecrated him as Bishop of Utica and Auxiliary Bishop of the Kingdom of Kongo. He was the first sub-Saharan African to hold that office in the Catholic church. He returned to Kongo, took his place at his father's court, and died young — around 1531, the records say, though the date is uncertain. He was, at most, thirty-six years old.

His death was a blow that Afonso did not publicly name as such. The court records do not say what Afonso felt. The letters do not mention it. But Henrique had been the proof of the project: a Kongo man, educated in the Catholic tradition at its highest level, endorsed by the papacy, capable of corresponding with Rome as an equal. His death took away a generation of that argument. There was no second Kongo bishop for a very long time. The generation of nobles educated in Lisbon who would have filled that succession — the men who had gone as boys and returned as adults fluent in the theological languages of the Church — could not be replaced quickly. Training them had required years of investment. Their deaths, or their failures to produce successors, left the court dependent on Portuguese missionaries and Portuguese-trained secretaries in ways that the original project had been designed to overcome.

The cathedral at São Salvador — Mbanza Kongo in Kongo language, São Salvador in Portuguese — was built in stone, which was unusual in the Kongo world, where most significant buildings were built in organic materials. Afonso commissioned it. Kongo masons built it. When it was finished, it held the court of a Christian king who could read the Mass in Latin and who had, himself, attended the liturgy for decades. The building was a political statement: the Kongo kingdom is not a mission outpost. It is a sovereign Christian state.

Afonso wrote to the Pope in 1514 — two years into his reign — and the Pope replied. He wrote again in 1517. He maintained contact with the Vatican throughout his reign, treating the papacy as an authority whose recognition mattered and whose obligations he took seriously. He also wrote to the Portuguese crown, regularly, formally, in the register of a sovereign equal: requests, petitions, protests, reports. The Kongo craftsmen at his court were making Christian objects that were not European objects — crucifixes carved in local wood and ivory, saints rendered in forms that carried Kongo cosmological logic beneath their Christian surfaces — a material synthesis that expressed in objects what the letters expressed in Latin. The archive in Lisbon preserves dozens of formal communications from the Kongo court during Afonso's reign, many in his name and written in his voice.

He knew what he was doing. He was building a paper trail that the Portuguese could not later deny having received.

## The Governor's Calculation

The slave trade did not break the Kongo kingdom through Portuguese violence. It broke it through a calculation made by people inside the kingdom who were not, in any simple sense, wrong.

The calculation worked like this: the Kongo political system held together through tributary relationships. A provincial governor sent tribute to Mbanza Kongo — raffia cloth, copper, ivory, sometimes captives taken in legitimate warfare — and received in return recognition, protection, and access to the goods that the capital controlled. The relationship was reciprocal. It was also asymmetric: the Manikongo had things the provincial governor needed, and the provincial governor had things the Manikongo needed, and this mutual need was the ligament of the kingdom's unity.

The Atlantic trade offered an alternative source of goods. The Portuguese operated primarily through Soyo, the coastal province at the Congo River mouth — a semi-autonomous power with its own interests in the Atlantic commerce, interests that were not always aligned with what the Manikongo wanted. At Mpinda, the port at Soyo's edge, the Portuguese had textiles from Portugal and the Atlantic islands — fabric that did not require the long supply chains of the interior trade. They had copper. They had, later, firearms. These goods were not available through the tributary system. They were available through the Atlantic exchange, and the Atlantic exchange's primary currency was not ivory or raffia cloth. It was people.

A provincial governor who could provide captives to the Portuguese traders could acquire goods that made him more powerful locally: textiles to distribute as gifts to maintain loyalty, copper for tools and weapons, firearms that no interior alliance could match. The more he acquired, the less he needed the Manikongo's recognition. The tributory relationship became optional.

Each governor making the calculation was solving a local problem — how to maintain power in their territory — using the tools now available. The tool happened to be the systematic capture and sale of human beings, some of whom were free subjects of the kingdom they served. The governor did not experience this as a betrayal of those subjects. He experienced it as survival.

Afonso understood the mechanism. His 1526 letter named it precisely: *the thieves and men of bad conscience grab them wishing to have the things and wares of this Kingdom which they are ambitious of.* He was not describing strangers. He was describing his own provincial structure. He had tried to build a committee of inspection — Kongo officials who would review all goods leaving the kingdom through the Portuguese trading posts, turning back anyone who should not be enslaved. The committee existed on paper. It could not exist in practice because the provincial governors who were supposed to cooperate with it were the same governors who were profiting from the trade it was meant to regulate.

His own position was not uncomplicated. Earlier in his reign, Afonso had himself traded captives — taken in the wars through which he consolidated power, sold to the Portuguese for the goods that allowed him to build the cathedral and fund the schools and send his son to Rome. The slave trade was not, in its origins, something he rejected entirely. What he rejected was its expansion into the seizure of the free subjects of the kingdom — people who were not captives of war but citizens of Kongo. By 1526, that line had been crossed so many times that it was no longer a line. The complaint of his letter is not against the trade in principle but against this specific violation: his own people, his own subjects, taken.

The Portuguese Crown issued regulations. João III responded to Afonso's letters with formal concern. In 1529, the Crown sent a new mission to Kongo with instructions to limit the trading abuses. The instructions had no enforcement mechanism in Kongo territory. The Portuguese Crown's jurisdiction extended to its own traders at the coast; it did not extend to the interior networks through which captives moved, and the Kongo officials who controlled those networks were not subject to Lisbon's law. The regulations announced a commitment the Portuguese legal architecture could not fulfill.

## The Reply

When the reply came from Lisbon, it was read aloud by the secretary in the same room where the letter had been composed. The same stone walls. The same quality of morning light through the cathedral windows — the Kongo plateau's dry-season sun, coming in flat and golden through the stone arches, falling across the floor in long rectangles that Afonso had walked across hundreds of times. The same smell of incense and red laterite dust.

Afonso listened. He sat in the chair where he sat when he dictated his letters. His hands were on his knees. The secretary's voice was correct and clear.

The reply acknowledged. It expressed concern. It promised to address the matter. It did not enforce.

*Know that we, the King of Portugal, have received your letter...* the formula began, in the correct Latin, with the correct salutations. The Portuguese ambassadors at the Kongo court delivered it with the correct protocols. The covenant had been invoked; the covenant had been formally received; the covenant was not being kept.

Afonso wrote again. He wrote in 1529. He wrote in 1533. He wrote in 1540, by which point he was an old man and the trade had expanded, not contracted, in the years since his first protest. The letter of 1540, dictated when his eyesight was failing, noted that the Kongo kingdom no longer had enough people to cultivate its own fields: *There are many of our subjects both free and slave who want to go to the Kingdom of Portugal and sell themselves, causing great ruin and harm to our kingdom.*

The kingdom was selling its own people now. Not only enemies captured in war. Not only people seized by raiders from outside. The economic pressure had reached deep enough that free Kongo subjects were entering the trade voluntarily, selling themselves into Atlantic slavery to acquire the goods that the tributary system could no longer provide. The structure Afonso had spent his reign building was digesting itself.

He kept writing. He had no other instrument. The military power of the Kongo kingdom could not match the Atlantic commercial system's capacity to reach into the kingdom's interior through its own political structure. The nkisi could witness the covenant; they could not enforce it across the Atlantic. The Cathedral of São Salvador stood on the plateau above the Kongo valley, its stone walls visible for miles, the largest Christian building in this part of Africa, built by a king who believed in the covenant it represented. The ships left Mpinda below.

The theory of power was coherent. It required the Portuguese to be a community that enforced its covenants through something more than words. They were not that community — not in relation to the Atlantic trade, where the money flowed in the wrong direction for enforcement. Dom João's successors would be no different. The commercial logic of the Atlantic system had its own momentum, and the momentum did not bend toward theological obligation when the two came into conflict.

The gap between what the covenant required and what the commercial system could deliver was not a gap that any amount of correspondence could close. Afonso understood this, eventually. The letters become shorter as his reign advanced, less elaborately argued, more direct in their grievances. The early letters spent paragraphs establishing the theological and legal framework. The later letters arrived at the complaint in the first lines. Not because his argument had changed. Because the elaboration of the argument had proved unnecessary: the Portuguese Crown knew the argument. They had received it dozens of times. The inadequacy was not in the persuasiveness of Afonso's case. The inadequacy was structural, and it did not yield to persuasion. He kept making the case because he had no other case to make.

## The Archive

The letter of October 18, 1526 is in the Arquivo Nacional da Torre do Tombo in Lisbon. It smells of old parchment and the particular damp of a Portuguese winter. The Latin is correct. The handwriting is a secretary's — trained in Lisbon, formed in the Kongo court — and the phrases carry the weight of a man who chose them knowing that this was the last clear instrument available to him.

Afonso I died around 1543, the date uncertain — the Kongo court did not keep the same records the Portuguese did, and the Portuguese records are patchy on this point. He had reigned for approximately thirty-four years. In that time he built a cathedral, founded a bishopric, sent multiple generations of Kongo nobles to European universities, maintained a diplomatic representation at the Portuguese court, corresponded with the papacy and at least three European monarchs, issued internal laws governing trade, established schools that would survive him by several decades, and wrote — by the estimates of scholars who have counted them — dozens of formal letters to the Portuguese crown, the Vatican, and other European powers.

None of it stopped the trade. The trade would continue. It would expand. In the century after Afonso's death, the Kongo kingdom would be reorganized around it in ways that corroded the political relationships the kingdom needed for unity. The governors would deepen their involvement with the Atlantic system. The competition for access to Portuguese goods would generate the internal violence that would eventually become outright civil war. The cities would empty. The cathedral would fall into disrepair and then ruin. The Portuguese would eventually put a garrison at São Salvador and call it their own.

The project Afonso built was dismantled not by external conquest but by the internal logic of the trade that he had spent his reign trying to contain. This is what the letters understood and could not stop: that the commercial system was reorganizing the political system from inside, using the political system's own actors as its agents. The letter's argument was correct. The argument's correctness was irrelevant to the mechanism it was trying to interrupt.

But the letters exist.

The argument Afonso made — that the Christian covenant was binding, that it applied to the behavior of traders and governors and kings, that its violation was not merely a political failure but a theological one — is intact in the archive. It was made in the language of the people who would later claim that Africa had no written history worth preserving, and it was preserved by them, in their archives, in their capital city, where it waited four centuries for scholars to find it and read it and understand what had been asked and what had been answered and what the gap between those two things meant.

The archive did not preserve Afonso's project. It preserved his argument. There is a difference. The project — the Christian Kongo kingdom, the college of bishops, the diplomatic parity with the Portuguese crown — was not preserved. It was dismantled. The argument — that sovereignty was real, that the covenant obligated, that the Christian faith meant something about how you treated the citizens of a Christian kingdom — survived in the only form that could survive: in the words, in the correct Latin, on the correct parchment, in the archive that the Portuguese maintained because the Portuguese also, for their own reasons, believed in the value of keeping records.

He wrote the letter in Latin. He knew it would be understood. He knew the argument was correct. He wrote it anyway, because that was the instrument he had, and he used it with the full weight of his learning and his faith and his thirty-odd years of watching the covenant fail in practice while remaining, on paper, intact.

In Mbanza Kongo, in his cathedral, under his mpu — the raffia hat of legitimate authority that no amount of slave trading could legally remove from his head — the Manikongo waited, and wrote, and waited again.

The argument is still in Lisbon. It has outlasted everything else.
