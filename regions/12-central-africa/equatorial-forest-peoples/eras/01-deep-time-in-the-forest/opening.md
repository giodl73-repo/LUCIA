# Era 1: Deep Time in the Forest
## Opening

Three trees ahead.

That is what the bird is telling him — not in words, but in the specific chattering call that means "here, come here, close now." Mambu has heard this bird since before he could walk. His father heard it. His father's father heard it, and back before memory has names for it, someone heard it and answered it for the first time, and began the conversation that has lasted longer than any kingdom in Africa.

The greater honeyguide is small, unremarkable, the color of dry bark. It does not point with its wing. It gives a call, then moves. It gives another call, from a new position, and waits for the answer. Mambu's answer is specific — not any human sound, but the particular rolling trill that the bird has learned, across generations, to associate with a human who will follow and leave something behind. He gives the call and the bird moves again. Three trees ahead now, the chattering more urgent.

It is morning in the Ituri, and the forest is doing what it always does: everything at once. Light comes through the canopy in shafts that move as the leaves move. A hornbill is calling somewhere to the north. The smell of loam and decay and new growth is so dense it is almost a thing you could hold. The forest does not distinguish between the living and the dying — they are the same process, and the soil is thick with the proof of it. Mambu does not think of the forest as a place he moves through. He moves through it the way a thought moves through a mind that already knows it.

He follows.

There is a practice among the Mbuti men of calling the forest by a name that is also the name of their most sacred ritual: molimo. The forest is not a backdrop. It is the entity itself — the one that provides, that calls you to account when you behave badly, that falls silent when it grieves, that can be woken when it sleeps. This is not poetry. When Mambu says the forest is generous today, he is making an empirical observation, not a figure of speech. The bee nest ahead of him is evidence.

The honeyguide leads him to a dead muvuli tree at the edge of a small clearing. The chattering stops. The bird lands near the top of the trunk and is still. That stillness is the signal: here.

Mambu can hear the hum now. He begins collecting dry leaves and fibrous wood — the right materials, which he has known since childhood, which smoke in a particular way, dense and thick, the kind of smoke that gentles but does not kill. The killing of bees is not what this is. Killing the bees would end this. The understanding between the forest peoples and the bees is the same as the understanding between the forest peoples and the honeyguide: it is a relationship, and relationships require the right behavior from both sides.

He smokes the hive slowly. He takes the honeycomb. He leaves a portion — not a token portion, not the fraction of an afterthought, but enough that the bird has been paid. The bird knows this system. It has learned, across as many generations as Mambu has, that the human partner delivers. When a human partner takes everything, that partnership ends. The honeyguide does not lead cheaters back to honey.

What Mambu carries back to camp is not just food. It is the proof of a relationship that works, refreshed this morning like any relationship that requires practice to survive.

The ecological knowledge Mambu holds would take a Western-trained botanist years to approximate. He knows which caterpillar species emerge in which months, and precisely where in the Ituri their food trees concentrate. He knows the fruiting schedules of forty-three tree species by sight, smell, and the behavior of animals who depend on them. He knows which bark reduces fever, which sap treats infected cuts, which root acts on the gut and in what quantities. This knowledge is not catalogued. It lives in practice, in story, in the specific ways of moving through specific terrain — in the knowledge that the tree near the stream where the ground dips is always the first to fruit, and that when it fruits, the elephants will be nearby, and when the elephants are nearby you move differently. The knowledge is ecological and positional and relational and embodied all at once, in a way no list can capture.

This is not what people usually mean when they say knowledge. It is something older, more intricate, and more durable.

The Ituri Forest has been continuously inhabited for at least forty thousand years. The forest peoples who live in it now are not the same as those who arrived forty thousand years ago — no people remain unchanged across that span — but the conversation with the forest has not stopped. The knowledge has been transmitted, generation by generation, refined by the million small adjustments that happen when a way of living is tested against reality every day. When a Mbuti child learns to track, they are learning a body of accumulated intelligence that dwarfs in complexity and depth any single science.

The greater honeyguide circles back. It gives one last call from the muvuli tree, then is gone, into the canopy and the light.

Three trees ahead. It has always been three trees ahead.
