# Figures -- Hayastan: The Ancient Kingdom

## Mesrop Mashtots (c. 362-440 CE)

He was a soldier before he was a monk, and a monk before he was a linguist, and the path matters because it explains the alphabet. A man who had spent years in the Armenian countryside, preaching to farmers in villages where the stone churches were cold and the congregations could not read the Gospel in any language, understood the problem of borrowed scripts not as an intellectual exercise but as a pastoral failure. The faith could not take root in a language that had no written form. Mashtots carried this understanding from the villages back to Vagharshapat, where the Catholicos Sahak Partev gave him the institutional support to act on it.

Koriwn, his student, wrote the biography, and it is hagiographic in the way that the biographies of founders always are: the master is wise, his motives are pure, his creation is divinely inspired. The human Mashtots, the man who surely struggled with the problem for years before solving it, who must have produced failed attempts and discarded drafts, who must have argued with colleagues and second-guessed his own choices, is visible only in the gaps of Koriwn's reverent account. What Koriwn does preserve is the method: Mashtots began with the sounds, not with existing scripts. He listened to Armenian and built the letters from what he heard. This is the act of a man whose ear was trained by years of speaking the language aloud to people who understood it only when they heard it.

He died in 440 and was buried at Oshakan. His tomb became a place of pilgrimage. The alphabet survived him by sixteen centuries.

## Trdat III / Tiridates III (c. 250-330 CE)

The king who made Armenia Christian is presented by the tradition as a man who was broken and remade. Agathangelos's account gives him the arc of a convert: powerful, cruel, humbled by illness, healed by the saint he had imprisoned, transformed by the God he had persecuted. How much of this is historical and how much is the conversion narrative's standard structure is impossible to separate, and the tradition does not try. Trdat is the tradition's example of what the faith does to power: it does not destroy the king. It rebuilds him.

The political Trdat, visible beneath the hagiography, was an Arsacid king who ruled a buffer state and needed a distinguishing institution. The old gods, Aramazd and Anahit and Vahagn, were shared in various forms with neighboring cultures and offered no unique identity. Christianity, adopted before any neighboring kingdom, gave Armenia a spiritual distinctiveness that doubled as a political boundary. Whether Trdat calculated this or simply experienced conversion and the geopolitics followed is a question the sources do not answer and the tradition does not ask.

## Grigor Lusavorich / Gregory the Illuminator (c. 257-331 CE)

The man who spent thirteen years in a pit and emerged to baptize a king is, in the Armenian tradition, the beginning of everything that the Church became. He is the Illuminator, Lusavorich, the one who brought light. The tradition does not treat his suffering in Khor Virap as an injustice to be protested. It treats it as the preparation that made the illumination possible. The light came from the darkness. The pit was not the opposite of the mission. It was its foundation.

Gregory founded the cathedral at Etchmiadzin, where the tradition says Christ himself descended and struck the ground with a golden hammer to mark the spot. Etchmiadzin, which means "the only-begotten descended," has been the spiritual center of the Armenian Church for seventeen centuries. Gregory's descendants held the position of Catholicos for generations, creating a line of spiritual authority that paralleled and eventually outlasted the royal line.

The historical Gregory is almost entirely inaccessible behind the tradition's account. He is a figure of faith, not of biography. The chapter uses him as the tradition uses him: as the vessel through which the faith arrived.

## Vardan Mamikonian (d. 451 CE)

The commander who led the Armenian army to defeat at Avarayr and became a saint for it. Yeghishe's account gives Vardan the speech of a martyr, not a general: he tells his soldiers that the death of the body is not the end if the soul is preserved, that the faith is worth more than survival, that the act of fighting is itself the victory regardless of the battle's outcome. Whether Vardan spoke these words or Yeghishe composed them is a question the tradition does not raise, because in either case the words carry the meaning that the tradition intends.

Vardan's significance is not in his military skill. It is in what his death was used to mean. The Armenians chose Avarayr as the central narrative of their relationship to suffering: a defeat accepted knowingly, for the sake of something that the victors could not take. This pattern would recur, with terrible variation, across fifteen centuries of Armenian history. Vardan set the template.

## Sahak Partev / Isaac the Great (c. 338-439 CE)

The Catholicos who partnered with Mashtots was both the institutional architect and the political enabler of the alphabet project. Sahak was a descendant of Gregory the Illuminator, which gave him both spiritual authority and dynastic legitimacy. He understood that the alphabet was not a scholarly luxury but a survival tool: a Church that operated in Syriac and Greek was a Church that depended on the languages of neighboring empires, and a dependent Church could not serve as the anchor of national identity.

Sahak's contribution was organizational. He provided the resources for the translation of the Bible, established the framework of schools that would teach the script, and used the authority of the Catholicosate to make the alphabet the language of the Church's administration. When Sahak was briefly deposed by the Sasanians for political reasons, he continued the translation work in exile. The project was larger than any one institution's support.

## Movses Khorenatsi (5th century CE)

Called the father of Armenian history, Khorenatsi wrote the *Patmutiun Hayots* as an act of national construction. His history traced the Armenians from their legendary ancestor Hayk, whose rebellion against the Babylonian tyrant Bel gave the nation its origin story, through the Artaxiad and Arsacid dynasties. The history is not neutral. It is the work of a writer who understood that a people without a written past is a people whose past belongs to others. Khorenatsi used the new alphabet to claim the Armenian past for the Armenians, gathering oral traditions, reading Greek and Syriac sources about Armenia, and weaving them into a single narrative told from inside.

The modern scholarly debate about Khorenatsi's dating, his use of sources, and his reliability is real and ongoing. But within the tradition, these questions are secondary to the fact of the history itself: it exists, it is in Armenian, and it gave the Armenians their first comprehensive account of themselves in their own language.
