# The Middle Kingdom

## The Edict

The scribe pressed the reed into wet clay and wrote what the king had spoken. The room smelled of damp earth and lamp oil. Outside, the wind off the Anatolian plateau pushed against the walls of Hattusa, and beyond those walls the kingdom was smaller than it had been a generation ago. Smaller and bloodier. What the king dictated was not a boast. Not a list of conquered cities. It was a confession.

"Formerly, Labarna was Great King," the text begins, and then it tells the blood. Hattusili, the first Great King whose name the tablet preserves, had named his nephew as heir. The nephew conspired. Hattusili stripped him of the succession and named his grandson Mursili instead. Mursili marched to Babylon and sacked it, and when he returned to Hattusa his own brother-in-law murdered him. Hantili took the throne. Hantili's son-in-law Zidanta murdered Hantili's sons and grandsons. Zidanta's own son Ammuna killed Zidanta. Ammuna's officials killed Ammuna's sons. Huzziya killed his brothers and attempted to kill Telipinu, who was married to Huzziya's sister.

Telipinu survived. Telipinu took the throne. And then Telipinu did something that no Hittite king before him had done. He sat down and dictated, into the wet clay, the full record of the killing. Every murder, every usurpation, every broken succession from the founding of the kingdom to his own reign. He did not flinch from the record. He laid the corpses out in order.

Then he laid down the law.

"In the future," the edict declared, "whoever becomes king after me and plans evil against his brother or his sister, you are his court assembly, and you shall say to him plainly: Look at this tablet. Formerly, blood was common in Hattusa. And the gods took it out on the royal house."

The gods took it out on the royal house. That was the lever. Not philosophy, not abstract principle, not an appeal to the good nature of princes. The argument was forensic and theological at once: the kingdom has been eating itself, and the gods have noticed, and the proof is in the record. The harvests failed. The borders shrank. Enemies pressed in. The connection between royal blood spilled and divine punishment visited was, for Telipinu, not metaphor. It was cause and effect, as certain as seed and harvest, as observable as the shrinking of the kingdom's lands under king after king who gained power through murder and lost it the same way.

The edict was carved and copies distributed to the provinces. Not hidden in the archive. Not sealed in a storeroom where only scribes would touch it. Placed where officials could read it, where the court assembly could point to it, where any prince contemplating murder would know that the tablet existed and that it said, in the voice of a king who had survived precisely such a plot: this is what happens. The clay would outlast the men. That was the wager.

The clay was the mechanism. The Hittite kings had bound foreign vassals with sworn covenants for generations: the gods of both kingdoms summoned as witnesses, the obligations carved into tablets, the curses for violation spoken aloud so that heaven and earth would remember. Now Telipinu turned the covenant inward. The gods who heard the oath between Hatti and Kizzuwatna could hear the oath within the royal house. The tablet that bound a foreign king bound the king's own sons. The curses that fell on the oath-breaker abroad would fall on the oath-breaker at home.

Telipinu was not a conqueror. The sources give him no great campaigns, no sacked cities, no plundered temples brought back through the Lion Gate to heap before the Storm God's temple. He reorganized the judiciary. He regulated the provinces. He wrote the edict. These are not the acts that make a king famous in the ancient Near East, where fame was measured in the cities you burned and the kings you dragged back in chains. But the edict survived. It was copied and recopied. Later kings referenced it. The principle it established, that succession must follow rules and that the court assembly had the authority to enforce those rules, shaped the Hittite kingdom for the next century and a half.

Whether it worked is the question the edict itself could not answer. Telipinu could write the law. He could invoke the gods. He could distribute the tablets and command the assembly to enforce them. What he could not do was swear the oath on behalf of men not yet born. The tablet would wait in the provinces, in the archive rooms, in the places where the assembly gathered. The words would hold. Whether the men who read them would also hold was beyond the reach of any covenant, any god, any king who had learned from blood that blood was not the answer.

## The Blood Before

The murders that Telipinu recorded in his edict were not distant history. The oldest killing was three generations back. The most recent was his own accession.

The trouble began with Hattusili I, the king who built Hattusa into a power that could project force south across the Taurus Mountains and into the wealthy lands of northern Syria. Hattusili was a builder and a campaigner. He sacked Alalakh, took Aleppo's tribute, fought his way through Hurrian territory, and returned to Hattusa with plunder enough to fill the temples. The Great Temple on the lower city's plateau received offerings from his campaigns: bronze vessels, silver, cattle. The storerooms filled. The kingdom grew.

But Hattusili could not control his own family. He had designated his nephew Labarna as heir. Labarna conspired against him. The sources do not record the details of the conspiracy, only its discovery and its consequences: Hattusili stripped Labarna of the succession in a public address to the assembly, recorded in what scholars call the Bilingual Edict because it survives in both Hittite and Akkadian. The language of the address is raw. "He is no longer my son," Hattusili declared. "He has turned cold to me." The king then named his grandson Mursili as heir, charging the assembly to protect the boy and the boy to obey the assembly. The old king's voice in the text is that of a man who has watched trust fail and is trying, with what authority remains to him, to prevent the failure from repeating.

It repeated.

Mursili I inherited the throne and did what his grandfather had trained him to do: he campaigned. In a military operation that stunned the ancient Near East, Mursili's army marched south through the Euphrates valley and sacked Babylon, bringing down the dynasty of Hammurabi. The distance was extraordinary. From Hattusa to Babylon is over a thousand kilometers of mountain, steppe, and river valley, and the Hittite army crossed it, looted the city, and returned. The plunder from Babylon entered the temples of Hattusa. Later tradition held that the statue of Marduk, Babylon's city god, was carried north through the Taurus passes.

Mursili returned to Hattusa a conqueror. His brother-in-law Hantili and Hantili's son-in-law Zidanta murdered him. The edict records it plainly: "Hantili and Zidanta committed an evil deed. They shed blood." No elaboration. No motive given. The plain language of the edict is itself a judgment: what they did needs no explanation because the act speaks for itself.

What followed was a cascade. Hantili took the throne. Under Hantili, the kingdom's borders contracted. The Hurrians pressed from the east, their chariots probing the frontier where the Euphrates headwaters cut through the foothills. The Kaska peoples, mountain raiders from the Pontic ranges to the north of Hattusa, began their raids in force: burning farms on the plateau's edge, driving livestock into the mountain valleys, cutting the roads that linked Hattusa to the northern provinces. The smoke from a Kaska raid would have been visible from the walls of the capital on a clear day. The granaries that Hattusili had filled with campaign plunder now depended on harvests from shrinking territory. The grain wagons came through the gates less frequently. The storerooms beside the Great Temple, built to hold the surplus of a conquering kingdom, held less each year.

Zidanta killed Hantili's sons and grandsons and took the throne. Ammuna, Zidanta's own son, killed Zidanta. Under Ammuna the kingdom shrank further. The edict records that "the lands of Hatti became hostile" and that the surrounding peoples "began to attack the frontier." The specific losses are listed: Arzawa in the west, Adaniya in the south, cities and territories that had submitted to Hattusili's campaigns now breaking away under kings who could not hold what their predecessors had taken. The kingdom was consuming its own strength. Each new king came to power through blood and ruled over less than the king he had killed.

Ammuna's officials killed Ammuna's sons. The edict does not name the officials. It does not explain their motives. The bare statement is sufficient: in a kingdom where kings killed to rule, officials learned that they too could kill. The violence had seeped downward from the royal house into the ranks below it.

Huzziya, who emerged from this killing, murdered his own brothers to secure his position. When Huzziya then turned on Telipinu, who was married to Huzziya's sister Ištapariya, the pattern seemed destined to continue. Telipinu survived. The edict does not say how. It does not describe a battle, a conspiracy, or a palace coup. It says only that Telipinu drove Huzziya from the throne and then, in a decision that the edict presents as its founding act of mercy, did not kill him. "I did not do evil to Huzziya. I did not do evil to his house." Exile, not execution. The edict begins its prescription with the author's own example.

## The Covenant Turned Inward

The oath was the foundation of the Hittite world. Every relationship of obligation between the Great King and his vassals was formalized in a sworn covenant. The form was specific and invariable: first, the historical preamble, recounting the relationship between the two parties and the benefits the Great King had conferred; then the obligations, specifying what the vassal owed in tribute, military service, and loyalty; then the divine witnesses, naming the gods of both kingdoms who would observe the oath; and finally the curses, enumerating the punishments that would fall on the oath-breaker. The gods did not merely witness. They enforced. A violated oath was a personal offense against every deity named in the tablet.

The treaty with Kizzuwatna, which Telipinu concluded during his reign, shows the form in its mature state. Kizzuwatna, the kingdom that controlled the Cilician plain and the passes through the Taurus, had been contested between Hatti and the Hurrian kingdoms for generations. Telipinu's treaty brought Kizzuwatna into the Hittite sphere as an ally, not a vassal: the terms were relatively equal, the obligations mutual, and the divine witnesses included gods of both kingdoms. The tablet was sealed and copies made for both parties. The words were spoken, the gods named, the curses recited. The covenant was now a fact in the world, as real as stone, as binding as the gods who heard it.

Telipinu's innovation was to take this form, tested and proven in the management of foreign relations, and apply it to the problem that no foreign treaty could solve: the violence within the royal house.

The edict follows the treaty structure. The historical preamble becomes the confession: the litany of murders from Hattusili I through Huzziya. The obligations become the succession rules: the eldest son of the first-ranking wife inherits; if there is no son of the first wife, the son of the second wife inherits; if there is no son at all, a husband is found for a daughter and he becomes king. The divine witnesses are the gods who have already punished the kingdom for the blood it spilled. The curse clause is not hypothetical but demonstrated: "Look at this tablet. Formerly, blood was common in Hattusa. And the gods took it out on the royal house." The punishment for violating the edict has already happened. The edict presents past suffering as proof of future consequence.

The difference between the edict and a foreign treaty was the enforcement body. Foreign treaties were enforced by the gods and by the practical consequences of breaking faith with a Great King who commanded armies. The edict was enforced by the panku, the assembly of the Hittite nobility. The edict addresses the assembly directly: "You are his court. You shall say to him plainly." The assembly was given not merely the right but the obligation to confront a king who violated the succession rules. The words were carved. The assembly could point to them. The gods could see them. The covenant was public, permanent, and witnessed.

This was new. Not the assembly itself, which had existed as a body of the Hittite nobility for generations, advising the king and participating in legal proceedings. Not the treaty form, which had been refined over decades of diplomatic practice. What was new was the assignment of constitutional authority: the assembly was charged with enforcing rules that bound the king himself. The king who wrote the edict was limiting the power of kings who came after him by empowering an institution that existed independently of any single reign. The tablet was the instrument. The assembly was the hand. The gods were the sanction. Together, the three formed a mechanism that was meant to outlast any individual ambition.

Whether this was idealistic or pragmatic is not a question the edict addresses. The edict's voice is not that of a philosopher debating governance. It is the voice of a man who has counted the bodies and drawn a conclusion: the killing must stop, and the only tool available to stop it is the same tool that holds the kingdom's foreign allies in place. The covenant. The oath. The clay.

## The Assembly and the Law

The panku that the edict charged with enforcement was the body of the Hittite Great Family: the high officials, the military commanders, the royal kinsmen who together constituted the ruling class of the kingdom. They gathered in the great hall at Hattusa, a space large enough for the court to assemble beneath stone walls that held the cold of the plateau even in summer, where the king's seat was raised on a platform and the officials stood or sat according to rank.

What authority the assembly held before the edict is not entirely clear. The panku had advised kings and participated in judicial proceedings, but whether its counsel could be refused or its verdicts overruled depended on the king who sat on the platform. What the edict did was commit to writing the assembly's role as enforcer of the succession rules, removing the ambiguity. Whatever the panku had been before, the edict declared what it must be after: the body that confronts the king, that holds up the tablet, that speaks the words "Look at this" when blood threatens to flow again.

Telipinu's judicial reforms extended the same logic into the legal system. The Hittite Laws, preserved in multiple copies from the archive at Hattusa, were reorganized and clarified during the Middle Kingdom period. The laws addressed specific cases: theft, assault, homicide, property disputes, agricultural damage, sexual offenses, ritual pollution. They specified compensations rather than punishments for most offenses. A free man who killed another free man owed four persons to the dead man's household. If a plow ox was killed, the responsible party replaced it. If a vineyard was damaged, the offender worked the damaged vineyard until it recovered.

The connection between the legal reforms and the edict is not coincidental. Both are exercises in the same principle: replacing private violence with institutionalized procedure. The blood feud, which the laws explicitly address, is the domestic equivalent of the succession crisis: a cycle of killing driven by obligation and honor that, unchecked, consumes the community. The edict addresses the royal blood feud. The laws address the common one. Both say: there is a procedure. Follow it. The alternative has been tried, and the gods have shown what they think of it.

Capital cases were reserved for the king. This is significant: the concentration of lethal authority in the crown meant that private execution, whether by a wronged family or by an ambitious prince, was not merely illegal but sacrilegious. Only the king, in consultation with the assembly, could impose death. The law drew a circle around the act of killing and placed it inside the institution that the edict had just empowered. To kill outside that circle was to break the covenant. The gods would see.

The physical record of these proceedings has survived in fragments. Clay tablets from the archive at Hattusa preserve court cases, property disputes, and ritual procedures. The tablets were stored in rooms within the royal citadel, organized by subject, some shelved on wooden racks that have long since rotted, the clay tablets falling to the floor and lying where they fell for three thousand years. The scribes who wrote them used a reed stylus pressed into damp clay, the same instrument and the same gesture that recorded the edict itself. Every legal case inscribed in the archive was, in a sense, an act of the same faith: that the written word, witnessed and stored, would hold the society together more reliably than memory, custom, or force.

## The Kingdom Rebuilds

The Hittite kingdom that Telipinu inherited was wounded. The territory that Hattusili I and Mursili I had conquered in their campaigns south through Syria and east toward Mesopotamia had been lost under the succession of murderous kings. The Hurrian kingdoms to the southeast, which would soon coalesce into the Mitanni confederation, pressed against the Hittite frontier. The Kaska peoples to the north raided the plateau's northern edge with increasing regularity: burning villages, stealing livestock, disrupting the roads that connected Hattusa to the Black Sea coast. The western regions were restless. Arzawa, the loose confederation of Luwian-speaking kingdoms in western Anatolia, had slipped from Hittite control entirely.

Telipinu did not attempt to reconquer what had been lost. The sources record no major military campaigns during his reign, no expeditions to recover Syria or push back the Hurrians. Instead, he stabilized what remained. The treaty with Kizzuwatna secured the southeastern frontier. Kizzuwatna controlled the passes through the Taurus Mountains and the fertile Cilician plain where the rivers ran down from the highlands to the Mediterranean. With Kizzuwatna allied rather than hostile, the southern approach to the plateau was no longer a vulnerability. The treaty was not an act of conquest. It was an act of diplomacy: two kingdoms recognizing mutual interest and binding themselves to mutual obligation.

The roads that connected Hattusa to the provinces were the skeleton of the kingdom. The Anatolian plateau is a harsh landscape: high, windswept, dry in summer and bitter in winter, the snow lying on the upper ridges from November to March. River gorges and mountain ridges channel movement along specific routes, and a kingdom that lost control of those routes lost contact with its own territories. The roads followed these routes, linking the capital to garrison towns, temple cities, and provincial administrative centers. Telipinu's provincial reorganization strengthened the network. Officials in the provinces were given clearer jurisdictions. Judicial authority was defined. The chain of command that ran from the Great King in Hattusa down to the provincial governors and from there to the local officials was formalized and recorded.

The religious festivals continued. The KI.LAM festival, one of the great annual ceremonies at Hattusa, brought the king out of the citadel and through the lower city in a procession that passed the Great Temple, the storehouses, and the workshops where the artisans who served the temple economy worked bronze, wove textiles, and carved stone. The festival was not decoration. It was the mechanism by which the king renewed his relationship with the gods, the city, and the land. The offerings were counted, the rituals performed in the prescribed order, the priests and priestesses moving through spaces that smelled of burnt animal fat and incense. The Storm God of Hatti received his offerings. The Sun Goddess of Arinna received hers. The divine order was maintained. The gods were fed, and because the gods were fed, the land would be fed.

The puruli festival, celebrated when the snow melted and the rivers ran full again, marked the defeat of the serpent Illuyanka by the Storm God: a mythological narrative performed as ritual, the victory of divine order over chaos reenacted each year so that the year could begin clean. The theological logic was consistent: the king served the gods, the gods sustained the land, the rituals maintained the connection, and the edict ensured that the king who served would be the rightful king, not a usurper whose offerings the gods might refuse. The entire system held together, each element supporting the others. Telipinu's contribution was to articulate the political link in the chain that had been breaking: the succession. The festivals could not save a kingdom whose king was illegitimate. The edict was meant to ensure that the king was never illegitimate.

## After Telipinu

The kings who followed Telipinu are shadows. The record thins. Alluwamna, who may have been Telipinu's son-in-law, appears in a handful of texts. Hantili II, Zidanta II, Huzziya II: names in king lists and fragments of documents, the details of their reigns largely lost. This thinning of the record is itself significant. The great archives at Hattusa preserve thousands of tablets, but the Middle Kingdom period after Telipinu is the least documented stretch of Hittite history. Whether this reflects genuine quiet (a stable period that generated fewer dramatic texts) or archival accident (the tablets existed but did not survive) or deliberate suppression by later kings who preferred to trace their legitimacy to the Old Kingdom or to their own New Kingdom achievements is not known.

What the fragments show is this: the kingdom did not collapse. The succession after Telipinu appears to have been orderly for at least one or two reigns. The provincial system functioned. Tax records and administrative correspondence, fragmentary but present, show a bureaucracy operating in the expected patterns. The festivals were performed. The treaties with Kizzuwatna held. The Kaska continued to raid, and the kingdom continued to endure their raiding without losing its core territory. The Hurrian pressure continued, and the Mitanni confederation grew into a great power to the southeast, but the Hittite kingdom was not absorbed. It survived.

It survived, but it did not escape the pattern entirely. The later Middle Kingdom kings include figures whose accessions are uncertain. Muwatalli I may have been killed. Tudhaliya I, who is sometimes placed at the end of the Middle Kingdom and sometimes at the beginning of the New Kingdom, may have come to power through irregular means. The sources disagree, and the fragments do not resolve the disagreement. The edict did not create a world without political violence. It may have created a world where political violence required justification, where the assembly's authority could not be entirely ignored, where the covenant's weight made usurpation more costly even when it did not make it impossible.

The honest assessment is partial success. The kingdom that Telipinu left behind was stable enough to survive the obscure Middle Kingdom period and emerge, under Tudhaliya I and his successors, as the great power of the New Kingdom: the empire that would contest Egypt for the Levant, fight the Battle of Kadesh, and sign the first international peace treaty whose text survives from both sides. The edict did not prevent all violence, but the kingdom it was written to save survived long enough to become something greater than Telipinu could have foreseen.

Whether Telipinu would have claimed the credit is a question the sources do not allow us to answer. The edict's voice is not that of a man predicting triumph. It is the voice of a man stating a problem, proposing a solution, invoking the gods, and committing the solution to clay because clay does not forget and men do.

## The Clay Holds

The tablet survived. That is the first thing to say about the Telipinu Edict: the clay held.

When Hugo Winckler's excavations at Bogazkoy, the site of ancient Hattusa, broke through into the archive rooms of the Hittite capital in the summer of 1906, they found thousands of clay tablets. Some were intact. Some were fragmentary. Some had fallen from shelves when the wooden racks rotted beneath them, lying on the floor of the archive rooms in the positions where they had landed centuries or millennia earlier. Among them was the text of the Telipinu Edict, preserved in multiple copies, the words still legible in the cuneiform script that the Hittite scribes had pressed into wet clay in a room that smelled of damp earth, in a kingdom that no one in the modern world had known existed until the tablets were read.

The New Kingdom kings knew the edict. Tudhaliya IV, ruling two centuries after Telipinu, referenced the succession principles the edict had established. The treaty form that the edict had turned inward was turned outward again on a grander scale: the Egyptian-Hittite treaty after Kadesh, the vassal treaties that bound a dozen kingdoms to the Great King, all followed the same structure that the edict had adapted for a different purpose. The historical preamble. The obligations. The divine witnesses. The curses. The form proved versatile enough to hold together an empire and durable enough to outlast the empire's fall.

The gods who witnessed the edict did not prevent the kingdom's eventual destruction. The forces that ended the Bronze Age were beyond any covenant's reach. Around 1180 BCE, Hattusa burned. The archives were buried. The name "Hittite" disappeared from living memory until the nineteenth century. But the clay did not burn. Clay, fired or sun-dried, survives fire. It survives water. It survives neglect. The tablets that Telipinu ordered his scribes to write lay in the rubble of Hattusa for three thousand years, and when they were found, they said what they had always said: formerly, blood was common. The gods noticed. Here is the law.

The edict could not save the kingdom from the forces that destroyed it. No document could. But within its own terms, for the problem it was written to solve, the clay held. The words held. The covenant between a king and his successors, witnessed by gods whose names the modern world had forgotten, remained legible long after every party to the covenant had turned to dust. Telipinu had wagered that the written word, carved and distributed and placed where the assembly could see it, would outlast the ambitions of men. The men are gone. The tablet remains. It says what it has always said.
