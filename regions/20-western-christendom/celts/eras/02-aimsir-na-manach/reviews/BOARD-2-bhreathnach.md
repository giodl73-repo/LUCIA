# BOARD-2 — Celtic Christian Monasticism Specialist
## Modeled on: Edel Bhreathnach, *Ireland in the Medieval World, AD 400–1000* (Four Courts Press, 2014)

**Domain:** Early Irish Christianity, the monastic movement, Patrick and Columcille, the interface between church and secular society
**Assignment:** Accuracy of the Patrick and Columcille material; the monastery's structure and social function; Brigid's treatment

---

## Assessment

The chapter handles the most contested figure in early Irish history — Patrick — with appropriate scholarly caution. The distinction between the historical Patrick (accessible through the *Confessio* and the *Epistola*) and the hagiographic tradition (Muirchú, Tírechán) is maintained implicitly rather than belabored, and the Patrick who emerges is the one in his own voice: compelled, uncertain, working through the Irish social framework rather than above it.

**On Patrick's Latin:** The characterization of Patrick's Latin as "rough" is a judgment that the primary sources support. Patrick himself apologizes for his language in the *Confessio*, and later scholars including Bieler and Hanson have noted the unusual syntactical constructions and the rusticus style. The chapter's use of "rough" reflects this tradition without over-stating it.

**On Columcille and the manuscript dispute:** The *Cathach* dispute (the manuscript Columcille copied, believed to be a psalter) and the Battle of Cúl Dreimhne (561 CE, the consequence) are part of the historical tradition recorded in later Irish sources. The chapter's formulation ("Whether this specific story is historical...") no longer appears in the final text, which is correct: the story's structural significance is what matters. The chapter handles the episode accurately.

**On the currach crossing:** The physical description of the currach (ox-hide over hazel rods, sealed with tallow) is accurate. The time estimate (twelve hours in fair weather) is reasonable for the North Channel crossing from northeast Ireland to the Scottish Dal Riata coast. The description of singing the Divine Office on water is speculative but consistent with what we know of the peregrini's practice — the offices were marked by time, not by location, and the monks would have continued them wherever they were.

**On Brigid:** The chapter's handling of the dual nature of Brigid (Christian saint and pre-Christian goddess) is the best treatment I have seen in a narrative history. The refusal to rationalize or resolve the overlap, and the framing ("They were the same fire") as the community's own theological position rather than as syncretism or confusion, is both accurate and appropriately inside the tradition's own logic. The acknowledgment that Kildare's abbess held jurisdiction over a bishop is accurate and important: this was a recognized anomaly in the Irish system and not unique to Brigid's foundation.

**One addition to consider (non-essential):** The chapter does not mention the *Navigatio Sancti Brendani*, the voyage of Brendan, which is the era's most famous narrative of the peregrini. Its absence is defensible (it is a complex text with a debated relationship to historical practice), and the currach crossing in the Columcille section covers the physical experience of the wandering mission more authentically. No correction required.

**Overall verdict:** Accurate. No corrections required.
