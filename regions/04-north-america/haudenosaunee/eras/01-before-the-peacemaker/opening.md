# Before the Peacemaker — The Mourning War Cycle

The mother's name is not remembered. What is remembered is that she had three sons, and that two of them did not come back from the last raid, and that when the news reached her she tore her hair and cut her arms with a mussel shell and sat in the smoke of the longhouse fire for four days without eating.

This was in the time before the Great Law. The longhouses then were set in palisaded villages on the hills above the streams, and the palisades had been built higher every generation for as long as the old people could remember. The corn grew in the bottoms. The fish were in the rivers. The deer were in the woods. The land gave what the land had always given, and it was not the land that was failing.

It was the seats at the fire.

A longhouse is a structure of kinship. The posts are relatives. The bark walls are relatives. The families along each side of the central aisle are the clan, and when a son dies the seat he would have occupied at the fire does not close. It remains open. It waits. An empty seat is a wound in the structure, and the structure cannot hold if the wounds are not closed.

The closing happens in two ways. The first is that the dead one is mourned, properly, with the songs and the ceremonies. The second is that the clan replaces him. A captive is taken in a raid. The clan mothers examine the captive. If they approve, the captive is given the name of the one who died, and becomes that person, takes his seat, speaks in his voice at the council, husbands his wife, raises his children. The seat closes. The longhouse holds.

This is the way the people had learned to carry the weight of loss. It had worked for generations. It worked for generations more.

But the mother with the two dead sons called for a raid, and when the warriors went out, they crossed the territory of another nation, and in crossing they were seen, and when they returned with two captives, the captives' clan sent warriors to avenge the taking, and three men of the first nation died in the counter-raid, and the clans of those three men sent their own warriors, and the clans of the men killed in that raid sent theirs.

The old people had seen this begin. They were beginning to see it not end.

An elder at Onondaga, sitting outside the longhouse in the late afternoon with his pipe, watched his grandson sharpen a flint-tipped arrow and thought about counting. The elder had been a warrior. His father had been a warrior. In his grandfather's time, a raid every two summers. In his father's time, every summer. In his own time, twice a summer, three times, and now his grandson was sharpening arrows at the age of ten.

The counting did not add up. A raid to fill one empty seat created, on average, more than one new empty seat. The clan that lost the captive lost him also, and would raid in turn, and the raid would create new losses in the raiding clan. The ledger of grief had become a compounding ledger. The longhouses on both sides of every valley were filling with empty seats faster than the seats could be filled. The corn still grew. The fish still ran. But the men were gone more than they were home, and the children were learning the songs of lamentation before they learned the songs of the Strawberry Festival.

Inside the longhouse at Onondaga that night, a different man was feeding his fire. Those who came to speak with him could not look at him directly. His hair was said to move. His body was bent by a crookedness that had come into him with the killing of his own family, or by sorcery, or by a grief so absolute it had curdled into something else. He did not mourn his dead by replacing them. He mourned them by multiplying the dead of others. His name was Atotarho, and his fire at Onondaga was the hottest fire in the country between the lakes, and his mind was a fire that burned without giving warmth.

The Peacemaker had not yet crossed the beautiful lake. The Peacemaker had not yet been born, or he had been born but had not yet spoken, or he had spoken but had not yet been heard. The grief-struck mother cut her arms with the mussel shell and called for another raid. The elder watched his grandson sharpen the arrow. Atotarho fed his fire.

The cycle turned. It was turning faster. Somewhere in the north, on the other side of the beautiful lake, a woman was carrying a child who would be born in a way the stories would later describe, and the child would grow, and the child would walk south, and what he carried would meet what the land already could not hold.

This is the time before that meeting. This is what the Peacemaker came to answer.
