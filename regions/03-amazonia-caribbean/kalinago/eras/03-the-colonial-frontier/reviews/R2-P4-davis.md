The notes have identified the right knowledge systems: navigation, canoe construction, mountain-interior knowledge, botanical/agricultural knowledge. **Navigation at method level:**

The Kalinago navigated the Lesser Antilles without instruments. This was not guesswork. The Lesser Antilles chain sits within the trade wind belt, which blows reliably from the east-northeast. The inter-island channels between the islands are each distinct in their current patterns, wave directions, and landmark profiles. A navigator who grew up on Karukera knew: the channel between Karukera and Madinina (Guadeloupe and Martinique) runs approximately 100 km, with a specific current that runs southwestward at 0.5–1 knot in normal conditions; the best departure time for a northward raid was before dawn, to arrive with the early daylight; the reefs off the northern point of Madinina are visible by the color change in the water even at night if the moon is right. This specific knowledge was transmitted orally, probably through the age-grade system. The chapter should pick ONE crossing — one specific route — and render what a Kalinago navigator knew about it. Not in general. In specific.

**Canoe construction at method level:**

The Kalinago war canoe (pirague) was built from a single large tree — typically Ceiba or similar — hollowed and shaped. The construction process required: selection of the right tree (straightness of grain, absence of defects, appropriate size), felling, transport to the shore, hollowing by fire and adze work, shaping of the hull, addition of outriggers or planked sides (accounts vary), rigging for sail use. Each of these steps encoded knowledge: which trees were suitable, where the best trees grew, the specific adze techniques for thinning the hull to the right flexibility without breaking it. The chapter should find one moment in the construction process that reveals the depth of the knowledge. The thinning of the hull, perhaps: too thick and the canoe is slow; too thin and it splits. The man doing the thinning knows by touch when it is right. That is knowledge at method level.

**Botanical knowledge:**

The Breton dictionary preserves Kalinago plant names. Some of these are medicinal, some dietary, some ceremonial, some toxic. The chapter's women's-house figure should operate with specific botanical knowledge — not "she knew the healing plants" but: she knew that the bark of X, steeped for three days, reduced fever (and what the Kalinago word for this preparation was, if Breton records it); she knew that the sap of Y, applied to a wound, prevented the infection that the Spanish sometimes called "the island fever"; she knew which plants the European settlers had brought that did not belong in this forest and what the arrival of those plants meant for the plants that did. The invasion of European plants was a detectable phenomenon to a people who knew this forest foot by foot. Let that detection appear in the chapter.

**The question I'd push on:** The notes describe the Breton dictionary as revealing "what the Kalinago thought was worth naming." This is true and important. But I want to push further: the Breton dictionary also reveals what Breton could not understand, which is equally revealing. The words he could not translate, the practices he recorded but labeled as "superstition" or "ceremony" — these are the places where the Kalinago knowledge system was operating beyond Breton's categories. The chapter should find at least one of these moments: a practice Breton recorded without understanding, and read it from inside as a functional knowledge system rather than as an exotic ritual.
