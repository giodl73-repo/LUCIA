# Brasil — O País do Futuro e do Presente

Brazil contains multitudes. It is the fifth-largest country in the world by area, the sixth by population, and the largest Portuguese-speaking nation on Earth. It received more enslaved Africans than any other country — approximately five million, nearly half the total Atlantic slave trade — and it was the last country in the Americas to abolish slavery (1888). Its national identity is organized around the concept of miscegenação — racial mixing — celebrated in the official ideology as a distinctive Brazilian achievement, a synthesis of indigenous, African, and European that produced a uniquely tolerant and harmonious society. The racial democracy myth was one of the twentieth century's most seductive national fictions. Samba, Carnival, the tropics, the ginga of Brazilian football — the cultural power of Brazil's African and mixed-heritage majority is global and real. So is the persistent racial hierarchy that correlates skin color with income, education, and incarceration rates with a statistical regularity that the racial democracy ideology was specifically designed to obscure.

Brazil's complexity is not reducible to any single story. It is the story of the quilombos — the escaped slave communities whose descendants still live in the interior, whose land rights are still contested, whose existence is the living proof that enslaved people resisted and built. It is the story of Amazônia — the largest river system on Earth, home to perhaps 100 uncontacted indigenous peoples, and the frontier zone where national development policy and ecological survival are in direct, ongoing, violent conflict. It is the story of a country that produced one of the twentieth century's great architects (Niemeyer), one of its great social philosophers (Freyre, contested), one of its great political movements (the PT and Lula), and one of its great ecological crises (deforestation, currently accelerating). Stefan Zweig, the Austrian novelist who spent his last years in exile there, called it "the land of the future." The future, Brazilians sometimes say, is always arriving and never quite here.

Key places:
- **Salvador (Bahia)** — the first capital; the heart of Afro-Brazilian culture; Candomblé as a living West African religious tradition; the highest percentage of African heritage outside Africa
- **Rio de Janeiro** — Carnival; the favelas; the Christ the Redeemer; the Zona Sul beaches; the most visible face of Brazil internationally
- **Brasília** — built from nothing in four years (1956–1960) in the interior; Niemeyer and Costa's modernist capital; the physical form of developmentalism
- **Palmares** — the quilombo community that lasted nearly a century (c. 1605–1694) and held perhaps 30,000 people; led by Zumbi, the national symbol of Black resistance

## What Defined Them

**Candomblé and the living African tradition.** In the terreiros (ritual houses) of Bahia, the orixás — the Yoruba deities carried across the Atlantic in the bodies and memories of enslaved Africans — are still honored. Candomblé maintained the Yoruba religious system under conditions of extreme suppression: the orixás were given the names of Catholic saints for protective purposes, but the ceremonies, the music, the possessions, and the theology remained distinctively West African. It is one of the most successful acts of cultural preservation under duress in the human record. The axé (sacred power) that flows through the terreiro is, in the understanding of its practitioners, the same axé that flows from West Africa — the Middle Passage did not sever it.

**Samba and the politics of joy.** Samba emerged from the Afro-Brazilian communities of Rio de Janeiro in the early twentieth century — specifically from the communities of recently freed enslaved people and their descendants who settled in the urban periphery. The escolas de samba (samba schools) that parade at Carnival are among the most elaborate collective artistic creations in the world: thousands of performers, elaborate floats, original music composed for each year's parade, and a theme (enredo) that is usually a political, historical, or social statement encoded in spectacle. Samba is not an escape from politics; it is politics conducted through other means, in a context where direct political speech was frequently suppressed.

**O Cerrado, a Amazônia, and the developmental frontier.** Brazil has spent its entire modern history in argument about what to do with the interior — the Amazon, the cerrado savanna, the Pantanal wetlands. The developmentalist answer (roads, dams, agriculture, mining) has consistently prevailed over the environmental and indigenous rights answer, with consequences that are now globally visible: Brazil holds approximately 30% of the world's remaining tropical rainforest, and it is being cleared at rates that have accelerated in every period of political instability. The indigenous peoples of the Amazon — including an estimated 100 peoples who have had no contact with the outside world — are the first affected by the frontier's advance.

**Cordialidade and its limits.** The Brazilian social anthropologist Sérgio Buarque de Holanda introduced the concept of the "cordial man" (*homem cordial*) in his 1936 *Raízes do Brasil* — the Brazilian tendency to manage social relations through personal warmth and flexibility rather than formal rules, to resolve conflicts through relationship rather than law. The concept has been both celebrated (as the foundation of Brazil's social creativity) and criticized (as the ideological cover for a system of personal connections that excludes those without the right relationships). The *jeitinho brasileiro* — the Brazilian way of finding a workaround — is the cultural practice that embodies this tendency. It can be a form of creativity. It can also be a form of corruption.

## The Chapters

**1 — A Terra dos Índios:** Before 1500, the coast of what will be called Brazil is densely populated by Tupi-speaking peoples. In the Amazon interior, archaeological evidence now reveals cities of tens of thousands connected by roads, managing the forest in ways that left the terra preta (black earth) that still enriches the soil today. The "empty forest" that Europeans described was not empty. It had been emptied — by the epidemic diseases that traveled ahead of the colonizers.

**2 — A Colônia:** A sugar mill (engenho) in Pernambuco in 1600: the machinery runs on water; the labor runs on terror. The enslaved people who cut, carry, and press the cane work in heat that kills those not born to it. Behind the great house (casa-grande), the slave quarters (senzala). Gilberto Freyre will one day write that the relationship between these two structures was characterized by intimacy and paternalism. He will not be entirely wrong, and not nearly right enough.

**3 — Zumbi:** In the interior of Pernambuco, the quilombo of Palmares has survived for decades by combining African political organization, indigenous forest knowledge, and the labor of people who will not be enslaved. Zumbi, its last great leader, is captured and killed in 1694. He becomes the symbol of Black Brazilian resistance. November 20, his death date, is now the National Day of Black Consciousness.

**4 — Brasília:** President Kubitschek arrives at the red dust construction site where Brazil's new capital is being built. Three years left until the inauguration he has promised. The workers (candangos) are living in construction camps in the cerrado. Niemeyer's buildings exist so far only on paper. Fifty years ahead of schedule — that is the motto. Brazil will invent its future by building it, literally, in the empty center of the country.

**5 — The Coup:** April 1, 1964: the Brazilian military, with the support of the US Embassy, removes the democratically elected President Goulart. The dictatorship that follows will last twenty-one years, torture thousands of political prisoners, and carry the country through an economic miracle and an economic catastrophe simultaneously. The AI-5 of 1968 — the decree that abolished all political rights — is the darkest point.

**6 — Lula:** A metalworker from São Paulo's industrial ABC region, who taught himself to read as a teenager and lost a finger in a factory accident, becomes president of Brazil in 2003. His Bolsa Família program lifts 20 million people out of extreme poverty. He is later imprisoned on corruption charges whose validity remains disputed. He returns to the presidency in 2023. Brazil, as usual, is working out its own logic.
