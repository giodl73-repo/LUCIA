## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — PANEL-2 Review (Notes)

**Focus:** Voice risk across the section plan. The notes identify the right problem — the "we" pronoun and the scholarly narrator. But the proposed solutions need sharpening.

**What works in the plan:**
- Using /Kabbo's own words to anchor scholarly passages. This is the right instinct. When the chapter needs to discuss the Bleek & Lloyd archive, /Kabbo speaks. The scholar's frame disappears behind the narrator's voice.
- The naming problem is acknowledged. Good. Handle it once, early, then use specific names throughout.

**What needs work:**
- Section 6 (The Painted Record) is the highest voice-risk section. Dating, interpretation, scholarly debate — all of this pulls toward academic register. The plan says "must be inside the rock shelter, not looking at photographs" but does not yet say HOW. Suggestion: enter the shelter through a specific person. A painter. A person returning to a panel their grandmother painted. Someone for whom the rock face is not a gallery but a living surface.
- Section 2 (The Tracker's Reading) risks becoming a demonstration of tracking's sophistication FOR the reader rather than a scene in which tracking simply happens. The tracker does not know he is demonstrating anything. He is reading spoor because he is hungry and his family is hungry. The scene must begin there.
- The /Kaggen section must tell a story, not describe a mythology. Pick one /Kaggen narrative — the eland's creation, or the theft of fire — and tell it the way a San storyteller would tell it. The reader should laugh. If the reader does not laugh, the section has failed, because /Kaggen is funny.

**The remaining risk:**
The honest uncertainty passages (the "we do not know" moments) are necessary and cannot be cut. But each one is a voice-break. The chapter needs a consistent technique for handling these — perhaps grounding each uncertainty passage in a specific physical moment (standing in a rock shelter, looking at a painting whose date is uncertain) rather than stepping into a narrator's omniscience.
