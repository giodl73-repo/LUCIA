# Figures — The Middle Kingdom

## Telipinu

Everything we know about Telipinu comes from what he chose to write down. The edict is his voice, his argument, his version of events. He tells us he survived Huzziya's purge. He tells us he drove Huzziya from the throne. He tells us he spared Huzziya's life. He does not tell us what he looked like, how old he was when he took the throne, or what he thought about in the hours that were not recorded. He is a king who exists almost entirely in his own proclamation.

What the proclamation reveals is a mind drawn to procedure. The edict does not rage against the murders it catalogs. It lists them. It draws a causal connection between the bloodshed and the kingdom's decline. It proposes a structural remedy. This is not the voice of a warrior-king boasting of conquest. It is the voice of a man who has examined a problem and arrived at a solution, and who is careful enough to commit the solution to writing so that it cannot be denied or forgotten.

His administrative record confirms this disposition. The treaty with Kizzuwatna, the judicial reorganization, the provincial reforms: each is an exercise in institutional design. Telipinu built systems. That his systems were expressed in the language of divine covenant rather than political theory does not make them less systematic. The gods were the guarantors, but the mechanism was human: rules, institutions, written records.

Whether Telipinu was personally merciful or merely strategic in sparing Huzziya is unknowable. The edict presents the mercy as exemplary: "I did not do evil to them." The statement serves the edict's argument, which is that the cycle of blood must end and that the author has demonstrated how to end it. The mercy may have been genuine. It may have been calculated. The edict does not distinguish, and we cannot.

## The Panku

The court assembly is not a person, but in the edict it is given a person's agency. "You are his court assembly," the edict says, addressing the panku directly. "You shall say to him plainly." The assembly is charged with confronting the king, with holding up the tablet, with speaking the words that remind the ruler of what happened when rulers shed blood.

Who sat in the panku is not fully known. The term appears to encompass the highest ranks of the Hittite administration: the chief of the royal guard, the chief of the wine-pourers, the chief of the palace staff, the military commanders, the members of the extended royal family. These were not elected representatives. They were the powerful men of the kingdom, the men whose support any king needed and whose opposition any king feared.

The edict transformed the panku from an advisory body into an enforcement body. Before Telipinu, the assembly advised. After Telipinu, the assembly was commanded to act. The distinction is significant. An advisor offers counsel that may be ignored. An enforcer holds a mandate that carries divine sanction. Whether the panku in practice ever confronted a king is not recorded. But the edict assigned the role, and the role was carved in clay, and the clay was distributed to the provinces where the assembly's members served.

## Huzziya I

Huzziya is the edict's villain, though the edict does not use that word. He is the last in the chain of usurpers, the king who killed his brothers and attempted to kill his brother-in-law. The edict records his acts without elaboration: the facts are sufficient.

What the edict does not record is Huzziya's perspective. He came to power in a kingdom where every recent accession had involved murder. He may have killed his brothers because he expected them to kill him, and the expectation would not have been unreasonable. The edict does not enter Huzziya's reasoning because the edict is Telipinu's document, written to justify Telipinu's accession and to establish Telipinu's constitutional solution. Huzziya is the problem the edict was written to solve. He does not get to explain himself.

That Telipinu spared Huzziya is the edict's most carefully placed detail. Telipinu drove Huzziya from the throne and sent him into exile with his family. The sparing is presented as proof that the cycle can end: a king can remove a rival without killing him. Whether Huzziya survived long in exile, or whether the exile was itself a form of erasure, the sources do not say.

## Hattusili I

The first Great King appears in the edict as the beginning of both the kingdom's greatness and its curse. He built the power. He could not secure the succession. His own public address to the assembly, preserved in the Bilingual Edict, shows a king in his last years, bitter and disappointed, disinheriting one heir and naming another, charging the assembly to protect a grandson he knows he will not live to protect himself.

"He is no longer my son," Hattusili declared of the nephew who betrayed him. The language is personal in a way the Telipinu Edict is not. Hattusili speaks as a father wounded. Telipinu speaks as a legislator diagnosing. The contrast between the two voices, separated by three generations of blood, measures the distance between a man trying to solve a personal crisis and a man trying to solve a structural one.

## Mursili I

The conqueror. The man who sacked Babylon. The edict gives Mursili one sentence of glory and one sentence of death. "He went to the city of Babylon and destroyed Babylon and fought the Hurrians." Then: "Hantili and Zidanta committed an evil deed." The compression is deliberate. In the edict's accounting, the sack of Babylon matters less than its consequence: the murder of the conqueror by his own kin. Military triumph is background. Succession failure is the foreground. The edict has its priorities, and they are not the priorities of a king list or a campaign record. They are the priorities of a document written to prevent what happened to Mursili from happening again.
