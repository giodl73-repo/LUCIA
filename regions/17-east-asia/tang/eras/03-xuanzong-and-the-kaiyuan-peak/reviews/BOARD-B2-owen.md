## B-2 — Stephen Owen — Board Review of The High Tang

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Tang poetry, the social function of verse, the examination culture, Du Fu and Li Bai scholarship.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The meeting of Du Fu and Li Bai in Luoyang in 744 is documented in Du Fu's poems and is scholarly consensus, though the exact circumstances are reconstructed from the poetry rather than from independent historical records.
- Du Fu's "Ballad of the Army Carts" (兵車行) is correctly placed before the An Lushan rebellion. It is generally dated to c. 750-751, associated with campaigns against the Tibetans and Nanzhao. The poem's quotation is a reasonable English rendering.
- "Spring Prospect" (春望) is correctly dated to spring 757 and correctly described as written during the rebel occupation of Chang'an. The translation provided is faithful to the original.
- Li Bai's quotations ("I raise my cup to invite the moon / with my shadow, we are three" from 月下獨酌; "Heaven gave me talents that must have their use" from 將進酒) are accurately attributed and reasonably translated.
- Wang Wei's "Deer Enclosure" (鹿柴) is accurately quoted and attributed.
- The characterization of Li Bai as dismissed from the Hanlin Academy is slightly imprecise. He was given a post in the Hanlin Academy (翰林供奉, Hanlin Attendant-in-Waiting) but it was not the later Hanlin Academician (翰林學士) position. The distinction matters for scholars but may be beyond the chapter's scope.
- The story about Gao Lishi and the boots is from later anecdotal sources and may be apocryphal, but the chapter handles this by noting "some said," which is appropriate.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's treatment of poetry as political culture rather than aesthetic decoration is exactly right. The keju system's literary component is correctly described: regulated verse (lushi) composition was indeed tested, with tonal patterns and parallel structure required.
- The three-voice framework (Li Bai/Daoist, Du Fu/Confucian, Wang Wei/Buddhist) is a standard scholarly shorthand that captures the range of High Tang poetry. It is useful but should be understood as a simplification; all three poets drew on multiple traditions.
- The description of Du Fu as the "poet-sage" (shisheng) in the figures file is historically accurate — this is the traditional Chinese literary epithet for Du Fu.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The use of actual poem quotations is the chapter's strongest literary decision. "Spring Prospect" is allowed to do its own work without excessive paraphrase, and the brief commentary after it is restrained and appropriate.
- Du Fu's examination failures are used as characterization, not pathos. The chapter does not sentimentalize his rejection.
- The Chengdu cottage poem ("My Thatched Roof is Torn by the Autumn Wind") is rendered accurately and its social dimension (the wish for shelter for all poor scholars) is correctly identified.

**What needs correction:**
- Du Fu's arrival in Chang'an in 735 is approximate. He traveled extensively in the 730s and his first documented examination attempt is usually placed at 736. The chapter should acknowledge this is approximate rather than stating it with certainty.
- The statement that "candidates who sat for the keju knew what the poems could hold. They had memorized Du Fu and Li Bai and Wang Wei before they wrote a line of their own" is chronologically problematic. Du Fu, Li Bai, and Wang Wei were contemporaries of the Kaiyuan/Tianbao examination candidates. Candidates in that period had memorized earlier poets (the early Tang masters, the Six Dynasties canon). Du Fu and Li Bai became canonical only in later generations.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (Du Fu arrival date should be softened; the keju/poetry chronology error must be corrected)
