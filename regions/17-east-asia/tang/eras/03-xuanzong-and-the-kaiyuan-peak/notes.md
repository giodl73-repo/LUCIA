# Production Bible — The High Tang (Xuanzong and the Kaiyuan Peak, c. 712–756 CE)

## Sources

### Primary
- **Du Fu's collected poems (杜工部集)** — Poems from Chang'an period (pre-rebellion) and the war poems of 755–759. "Spring Prospect" (春望), "Ballad of the Army Carts" (兵車行), "Lament of the Crescent Moon" (月夜). Contributes: firsthand witness to the peak and the destruction; specific imagery of Chang'an; the conscription crisis; the court's flight.
- **Li Bai's collected poems (李太白集)** — "Drinking Alone Under the Moon" (月下獨酌), "Bring the Wine" (將進酒), "A Quiet Night Thought" (靜夜思). Contributes: the Daoist counter-voice to Du Fu's Confucian witness; the wine-and-wandering ethos; the Tang at its most confident.
- **Jiu Tang Shu (舊唐書) and Xin Tang Shu (新唐書)** — The Old and New Tang Histories. Official dynastic histories. Contributes: court events, An Lushan's career, Yang Guifei, the Mawei post-station incident, Xuanzong's flight.
- **Zizhi Tongjian (資治通鑑)** — Sima Guang's comprehensive mirror. Contributes: the most detailed chronological narrative of the rebellion and the court's decisions.
- **"Song of Everlasting Sorrow" (長恨歌) by Bai Juyi** — Later poem (written c. 806) but the definitive literary account of Xuanzong and Yang Guifei. Contributes: how the Tang remembered itself; the love story as political parable.

### Academic
- **Mark Edward Lewis, *China's Cosmopolitan Empire: The Tang Dynasty* (2009)** — Contributes: structural analysis of the equal-field system, fubing military, jiedushi transition, Chang'an's urban design.
- **Stephen Owen, *The Great Age of Chinese Poetry: The High Tang* (1981)** — Contributes: close readings of Du Fu and Li Bai; the social function of poetry; the examination culture.
- **Edwin Pulleyblank, *The Background of the Rebellion of An Lu-shan* (1955)** — Contributes: the structural causes of the rebellion; the jiedushi system; An Lushan's career and the court's failure to control frontier military power.
- **Victor Cunrui Xiong, *Sui-Tang Chang'an: A Study in the Urban History of Late Medieval China* (2000)** — Contributes: physical layout of Chang'an; the ward system; market regulations; population estimates.
- **Valerie Hansen, *The Silk Road: A New History* (2012)** — Contributes: Sogdian merchants in Chang'an; the Hu restaurants; foreign religions; the economic basis of cosmopolitanism.

### Needed (ACQUISITION)
- Tang-era archaeological reports on the West Market excavations (Xi'an)
- Dunhuang manuscript corpus — specific cave documents on merchant life
- Japanese embassy records (kentōshi) for foreign perspectives on Chang'an

## Key Figures

| Name | Role | Why They Matter |
|------|------|----------------|
| **Du Fu (杜甫)** | Poet, failed examination candidate, Confucian witness | The through-line character. Enters the opening as a young failure; becomes the poet who documents the fall. His poems are the primary evidence for the human experience of the rebellion. |
| **Li Bai (李白)** | Poet, Daoist wanderer, wine-drinker | The counterpoint to Du Fu. Where Du Fu is rooted in duty and suffering, Li Bai is unmoored, brilliant, and indifferent to systems. Their meeting in Luoyang in 744 is the literary event of the era. |
| **Emperor Xuanzong (唐玄宗, Li Longji)** | Son of Heaven, patron of arts, Daoist devotee | The emperor who presided over both the peak and the catastrophe. His love for Yang Guifei is inseparable from his neglect of governance. His flight from Chang'an and his forced consent to her death are the era's defining political moments. |
| **Yang Guifei (楊貴妃, Yang Yuhuan)** | Imperial Consort, dancer, political figure | Not a femme fatale. A person in a court whose beauty and talent placed her at the center of power, and whose family's rise provoked the resentment that An Lushan exploited. She was killed at Mawei by soldiers who needed someone to blame. |
| **An Lushan (安祿山)** | Frontier general, rebel emperor | Half-Sogdian, half-Turkic. Rose through the jiedushi system to command three northeastern frontier armies. His rebellion was the product of the system that made the Tang great — frontier generals with independent military power. |

## Narrative Arc

The High Tang is a story of confidence and its cost. The Kaiyuan reign (713–741) represents the moment when the Tang system — the examination culture, the Silk Road economy, the cosmopolitan court, the frontier military — was working at full capacity. Chang'an was the largest and most international city on earth. Poetry was the medium through which political life was conducted. The court was brilliant, wealthy, and sure of itself.

The confidence had structural foundations: the equal-field land system distributed agricultural wealth; the fubing militia system provided defense without professional armies; the Grand Canal moved southern grain to the northern capital; the Silk Road generated commercial revenue. But by the Kaiyuan period, these foundations were already shifting. The equal-field system was breaking down as aristocratic estates consolidated land. The fubing was being replaced by professional frontier armies under jiedushi commanders who recruited non-Chinese soldiers and operated with increasing independence. The court's wealth came increasingly from the Silk Road and the south, making it dependent on trade routes it could not fully control.

An Lushan's rebellion (755–763) was not an external shock but a product of these structural shifts. The system that made the Tang cosmopolitan — the employment of non-Chinese talent, the delegation of military power to frontier commanders, the concentration of cultural and economic life in Chang'an — was the same system that produced a frontier general with the power and the motive to rebel. The rebellion destroyed Chang'an, killed perhaps thirty-six million people (two-thirds of the registered population), and left the Tang permanently weakened. What survived was the poetry — Du Fu's war poems, the memory of the Kaiyuan peak, and the idea of the Tang as China's golden age, an idea that has shaped Chinese self-understanding for thirteen centuries.

## Panel Pressure Points

**Tuchman** will push on pacing through the middle sections. The chapter risks becoming a survey of the Kaiyuan period's features (cosmopolitanism, poetry, court, rebellion) without a single arc pulling through. The solution is to keep Du Fu as the through-line — his personal experience braided with the empire's trajectory.

**Ibn Khaldun** will push on structural causation. The opening is vivid but has not yet explained WHY the Tang were cosmopolitan or WHY the rebellion happened. The full chapter must trace the causal chain: equal-field system → wealth → frontier military expansion → jiedushi independence → rebellion. The confidence and the catastrophe must emerge from the same structural conditions.

**Achebe** will push on the closing register and on the rebellion section. "The last years of something" is authorial voice. The rebellion section will tempt the writer toward the voice of nostalgia — a golden age lost. The voice must stay inside: Du Fu's loss is specific, not civilizational. Yang Guifei's death is specific, not symbolic.

**Davis** will push on poetry as a knowledge system and on the agricultural base. The chapter must treat the keju and the poetic tradition as genuine intellectual achievements, not cultural decoration. It must also acknowledge that the peak rested on the labor of farmers and conscripts who experienced the Kaiyuan differently from Du Fu.

**Kapuściński** will push on sensory immersion in the court and rebellion sections. The market is easy to ground physically. The court — the Daming Palace, the music, Yang Guifei's dancing — needs the same physical specificity. The rebellion needs the sounds and smells of war and flight, not just the politics of it.

## Sensory Palette

- **Sight:** Chang'an's grid — rammed earth walls, the twelve-carriage avenue, the Daming Palace's vermilion columns. The West Market's awnings. Silk in court — Yang Guifei's garments. Fire during the sack of Chang'an.
- **Sound:** Market drum at midday. Pipa music at court. Buddhist temple bells at dawn. Syriac liturgy in the Daqin monastery. The djembe — no, the jie gu (war drum) on the frontier. The silence of an emptied Chang'an.
- **Smell:** Sesame oil, lamb, foreign spices in the Hu restaurants. Incense in the Buddhist temples. The Zoroastrian sacred fire. Dust of the Wei River plain. Smoke from a burning city.
- **Touch/taste:** The weight of the kaiyuan tongbao copper coins. Rice wine. The cold of the northwest wind in autumn. Paper of the examination scripts. Silk against skin in the court.

## Voice Notes

Court culture voice per the Style Guide: layered, observational, unfolding slowly. Think silk — smooth, with pattern visible when you look closely. Accumulative sentences that carry political weight in their details. The prose should linger on fabric, on light, on the angle of a glance. But the rebellion sections shift — when violence arrives, the prose should shorten. Principle 10: when the weight is heavy, the prose should be plain. The chapter has two registers: silk for the peak, plain cloth for the war.

## Open Questions

- Du Fu's exact movements in 735 are uncertain. He arrived in Chang'an, failed the examination, and traveled extensively. The opening places him at the Chunming Gate, which is plausible but not documented to that specificity. The chapter should handle this with appropriate uncertainty.
- An Lushan's exact motives are debated. Pulleyblank argues structural causes; others emphasize personal ambition and the Yang family's provocations. Present the scholarly disagreement.
- The casualty figures for the rebellion (36 million by census decline) are debated — some scholars argue the census decline reflects administrative collapse, not actual deaths. Note the uncertainty.
- Yang Guifei's death at Mawei: the accounts differ on whether soldiers strangled her or whether she hanged herself. The chapter should present what the sources say and note the disagreement.
