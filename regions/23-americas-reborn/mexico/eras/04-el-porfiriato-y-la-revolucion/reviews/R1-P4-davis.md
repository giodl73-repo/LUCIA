# PANEL-1 Review — P4: Wade Davis
## Mexico Era 4 — El Porfiriato y La Revolución

**Lens:** Anthropological wonder — hidden condescension, worldview depth

---

The opening does something that most accounts of the Mexican Revolution do not: it grounds the politics in the agrarian knowledge system. The milpa — maize, beans, squash in intercropped rows — is not merely a romantic image of peasant farming. It is a sophisticated polyculture that maintains soil fertility, manages rainfall, and produces a nutritionally complete diet from a small plot. The people fighting for this land were not fighting for sentiment. They were fighting for a system of production and knowledge that sustained communities across generations, a system that the hacienda's monoculture sugarcane would erase.

The opening gestures at this but the chapter should go further. What did the Morelos villages know about their land that the hacienda operators did not? What agricultural knowledge was embedded in the ejido's management practices — the rotation, the water rights, the communal labor patterns for maintenance and harvest? The Ley de Baldíos classified communally-held land as unused precisely because it did not show the visible marks of ownership that European property law required: individual cadastral parcels, fenced boundaries, market production. But the land was not unused. It was held in a different system of knowledge and use, and the legal attack on it was also an epistemological attack — a refusal to recognize a different way of knowing and managing land.

The Plan de Ayala is not just a legal claim. It is a statement that the villages' knowledge system — their understanding of how land should be held, managed, and inherited — is legitimate and prior. The chapter should carry that epistemological dimension, not just the political one.

Also: the Maya workers in the Yucatán sisal plantations appear in the opening in a single sentence. Their situation was structurally different from Morelos — the henequen monoculture, the debt peonage, the political control by the Yucatecan elite — and deserves a moment in the chapter. The Revolution reached Yucatán differently than it reached Morelos.

**The question I'd push on:** What was the knowledge embedded in the iron box? The colonial-era maps showing village lands — how were they made? What understanding of the landscape, the water sources, the boundary markers did they encode? If the chapter can show what the box contained at the level of the knowledge it carried, not just the legal claim it documented, the epistemological dimension of the revolution comes alive.
