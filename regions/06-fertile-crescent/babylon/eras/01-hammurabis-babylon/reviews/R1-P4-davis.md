## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of Hammurabi's Babylon Opening

**Overall:** The opening treats the law code as a knowledge system deserving the same respect as any other culture's way of organizing the world. No hidden condescension. But the worldview could go deeper.

**What works:**
- The law as public theology is presented without translating it into modern categories. The stele is not "an early legal system" or "a precursor to constitutional government." It is what it is: cosmic order made readable. This is the correct approach.
- The year-names are mentioned as the Babylonian system for marking time. This is a genuine cultural particular: the idea that each year is defined by an act, not by a number. The chapter body should develop this further.
- The physical stele is described with material specificity: black diorite, carved columns, the warmth of stone under a hand. The object carries the culture's knowledge.

**What doesn't:**
- The cosmological depth is thin. Marduk and Shamash are named but not inhabited. The opening tells us Shamash holds the rod and ring; it does not tell us what the rod and ring mean inside the Babylonian understanding. The instruments of just measure are cosmic forces, not symbols. The chapter body needs to develop the divine assembly, the Enuma Elish, and the annual Akitu renewal as the theological framework within which the law code makes sense.
- The canal system is mentioned as infrastructure. In the Babylonian understanding, the canals are not infrastructure. They are the maintained order of the created world. The gods separated the waters; the king maintains the separation. Canal-digging is a cosmic act. This needs to come through.

**The question I'd push on:**
What is it like to stand before the stele? Not just warm stone under the hand, but: the scale of the Esagila complex, the height of the ziggurat behind the courtyard, the sound of the temple rituals, the smell of the offerings. The stele existed inside a sacred precinct, not in a public square. Reading the law in the Esagila was an act performed in the presence of the gods. The sensory and theological context of that act should be inseparable.
