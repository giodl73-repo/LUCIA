# Panel Review — R1-P1: Barbara Tuchman (Narrative Craft)

**Opening: The Haegeum and the Name**  
**Date: 2026-04-16**  
**Scorer: Barbara Tuchman**

---

## The Question

Would a reader keep reading? Does the scene earn attention? Does the craft sustain momentum?

---

## The Assessment

**YES. DECISIVELY.**

This opening does something exceptional in narrative craft: it begins in stillness (a woman and an instrument in darkness) and ends in silence (she does not play). The entire arc is *withdrawal*, not escalation. And yet the reader is pulled forward with genuine urgency.

How?

The administrator's arrival *compresses the stakes*. He is not threatening — he is administrative. This makes him more chilling. He has come to tell her that her name will be taken, not by violence but by bureaucracy. The haegeum is still in her lap. She is still free to play. But the administrator's visit has already stolen something: the future.

The final image — "By morning, the city had already begun its forgetting" — is a landing that works because it leaves the reader inside the woman's knowing. She understands what has just happened in a way that goes beyond this single interaction. The annexation of 1910 is two years old. She knows this is not an isolated warning. She knows the system. The silence at the end is not defeat; it is recognition.

**Line-level observations:**

- "The strings came first, waiting for the voice to know what they meant" — This sentence establishes the haegeum's primacy before language. When the administrator arrives, he represents language (the document, the name, the policy). The haegeum is what persists before and after language fails. Exceptional structural intuition.
- The administrator's dialogue is spare. He does not explain. He does not justify. He simply tells her what will happen. This restraint is the craft. A lesser writer would have had him lecture on policy. Tuchman would recognize that showing is earned here, not just prettified.
- "She waited until his footsteps were fully outside" — This single sentence contains the woman's entire agency. She is not paralyzed. She is choosing when to play. This is not defeatism; it is dignity through timing.

**The pull:**

The opening creates two questions that pull the reader forward:
1. What is the haegeum, and why does it matter so much to her?
2. What does it mean for an entire people when the administration begins taking their names?

Both questions are *implied*, not stated. The reader arrives at them through the scene, not through exposition. This is the Tuchman test: the momentum is earned.

---

## The Question I'd Push On

Here: the final passage shifts slightly. "By morning, the city had already begun its forgetting" — this feels like it might be *authorial reflection* rather than the woman's own understanding. It is a beautiful line, but does it belong in her voice?

Consider: could the opening end instead at "She set the haegeum down in the darkness and did not play again that night"? This keeps the reader entirely in her specificity. The forgetting of the city is something the reader will understand through the *chapter*, not through the opening's narration.

It is a small note. The opening is already exceptional. But if Tuchman has to push, it is there: the line between the woman's knowledge and the author's knowledge gets slightly blurred in that final reflection.

---

## Recommendation

**PASS WITH DISTINCTION.** This opening has the narrative DNA of the era: a people whose resistance is not loud but persistent, whose agency is quiet and embodied, who understand that they are part of a system larger than any single moment. The haegeum is both a musical instrument and a symbol of persistence. But it is not *symbolism imposed by the author* — it is *lived reality*. The woman plays. That is what people who have been colonized do: they continue.

The reader will continue too.
