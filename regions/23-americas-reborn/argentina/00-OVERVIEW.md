# Argentina — El País que Soñó con Europa

Argentina wanted to be Europe. Not metaphorically — literally. Between 1880 and 1930, more than six million Europeans immigrated to the country: Italians above all, then Spaniards, then smaller but significant numbers of Germans, Poles, Jews from the Pale of Settlement, Welsh farmers who settled Patagonia and maintained their language for generations, Lebanese and Syrians who became the backbone of the northern merchant class. Buenos Aires aspired to be Paris on the Río de la Plata — and for a period in the early twentieth century, when Argentina's per capita income rivaled that of France and Germany, the aspiration seemed almost realized. The boulevards, the café culture, the operatic grandeur, the conviction that culture emanated from Europe and that Argentina's destiny was to receive and refine it — all of this was real, and all of it collided, repeatedly and spectacularly, with the Americas' own logic.

Argentina's history since 1930 is the story of that collision: between the oligarchic liberalism that had grown rich on cattle, wheat, and European capital, and the working-class Peronism that organized the factory workers and the internal migrants who had never been part of the European dream. Between the military that defined itself as the guardian of civilization and the civilian governments it overthrew six times between 1930 and 1983. Between the country that kept imagining its next fresh start and the structural inequalities that made fresh starts available to some and not to others. The tango — that most Argentine of art forms, invented in the brothels and conventillos (tenements) of the Buenos Aires immigrant poor, rejected as vulgar by the Argentine elite until Paris pronounced it sophisticated — is the compressed history of the entire story.

Key places:
- **Buenos Aires** — the city that dreams of Paris; the capital; the port through which six million Europeans arrived; the stage on which Peronism played its drama
- **La Pampa** — the flat, fertile grassland that made Argentina rich; the gaucho country; the foundation of the landed oligarchy
- **Rosario** — Argentina's second city; Che Guevara's birthplace; the industrial heartland
- **ESMA (Escuela de Mecánica de la Armada)** — the former Navy School of Mechanics that served as the largest clandestine detention center during the 1976–1983 military dictatorship; now a memory site and human rights museum

## What Defined Them

**Peronism as the permanent fault line.** There is no neutral position on Perón in Argentina. The Peronist movement — which Juan Domingo Perón built around organized labor, nationalist economics, and his wife Eva Duarte de Perón's charismatic presence among the poor — divided Argentine society in ways that have not healed in eighty years. For the working class (the descamisados, "the shirtless ones"), Perón and Evita delivered real material benefits: the eight-hour day, paid vacations, social security, healthcare, housing. For the middle and upper classes and the military, Peronism was authoritarianism, corruption, and the mobilization of the masses against civilization. Both of these experiences were real. They describe the same government from different positions.

**The disappeared — los desaparecidos.** The military dictatorship of 1976–1983 (the Proceso de Reorganización Nacional) systematically kidnapped, tortured, and murdered an estimated 30,000 people — trade unionists, students, journalists, lawyers, priests, and anyone suspected of connection to the political left. The Madres de Plaza de Mayo — mothers who marched every Thursday in the plaza in front of the Casa Rosada, wearing white headscarves and carrying photographs of their missing children — became the symbol of resistance to a regime that had abolished all other forms of public opposition. The Juicio a las Juntas (Trial of the Juntas, 1985) was the first trial of a military junta for crimes against humanity in Latin American history. The wounds it addressed have not fully healed.

**The tango as social philosophy.** The tango emerged from the Buenos Aires docks and tenements in the late nineteenth century — a music and dance born from the encounter of Italian, Spanish, African, and gaucho traditions in the immigrant poor's social spaces. It is characterized by close embrace, precise footwork, and a musical vocabulary that alternates between melancholy (the nostalgic longing for origins that are already lost before they are left) and defiance. The Argentine elite rejected it as obscene. Paris discovered it around 1910 and pronounced it sophisticated. The Argentine elite then adopted it. The tango's history is the history of Argentine class dynamics and the role of European validation in Argentine self-perception, compressed into three minutes of music.

**The crisis as perpetual condition.** Argentina has defaulted on its sovereign debt more times than any other country in the world. It has had hyperinflation (14,000% in 1990), a frozen bank account crisis (the corralito of 2001–2002), and economic contractions of extraordinary severity followed by extraordinary recoveries. The resilience this has produced in Argentine civil society — the informality, the creativity, the barter economies and social clubs that emerged during the worst crises — coexists with a political culture that has never found a stable institutional formula for managing the country's structural economic contradictions. The abundance of the Pampa and the structural inequality of how that abundance is distributed are the paradox at the heart of Argentine economic history.

## The Chapters

**1 — El Puerto:** Buenos Aires in 1900 is receiving 200,000 European immigrants a year. Italian in the conventillos of La Boca; Spanish in the neighborhoods of San Telmo; Jewish in the Once district where the community is building its synagogues and social clubs. Everyone is building something. The city is enormous and ambitious and chaotic. The tango is being invented somewhere in the noise.

**2 — Evita:** Eva Perón — the illegitimate daughter of a rural landowner, a radio actress from the provinces who became the wife of the president and then a political force in her own right — is dying of cancer at 33. The country is watching. The descamisados who line the streets outside the Eva Perón Foundation know what she is losing and what they are about to lose with her. She dies on July 26, 1952. In Buenos Aires, the grief is public and total.

**3 — La Noche de los Lápices:** September 16, 1976: six students are kidnapped from their homes in La Plata. They had been organizing for a reduced student bus fare. Four of them will not be seen again. Their ages range from 14 to 18. The Night of the Pencils is not the worst single act of the Proceso — far worse happened. It is the most symbolically clear statement of what the Proceso was doing: disappearing the children of the working class who had the presumption to organize.

**4 — Las Madres:** Every Thursday at 3:30 in the afternoon, a group of women walks in a circle around the Pirámide de Mayo in the Plaza de Mayo. They wear white headscarves made from their children's diapers. Each woman carries a photograph. The police cannot arrest them all. The plaza is too public. The witness is too visible. This is how resistance works when every other form has been prohibited.

**5 — La Crisis:** December 2001: Buenos Aires is burning. The government has frozen all bank accounts — the corralito — to prevent capital flight. People who had savings denominated in dollars find them converted to pesos at a fraction of their value. The middle class that had imagined itself secure discovers it was not. Five presidents in two weeks. The banging of pots (cacerolazos) is audible across the city. Argentina defaults on its debt — the largest sovereign default in history at that point. It survives.

**6 — El Presente:** Javier Milei, a libertarian economist who carried a chainsaw to campaign rallies as a symbol of cutting the state, is elected president in November 2023. He immediately abolishes half the government ministries, cuts social spending, and dollarizes the economy. Whether this is the next crisis or the escape from the cycle of crises is the question Argentina is currently living.
