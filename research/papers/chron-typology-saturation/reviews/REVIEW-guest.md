## Substantive review

I was pleased to see our 2006 paper cited here, and cited as a quantitative bridge rather than as a throwaway comparator. The authors' reading — that our 12/60 ratio (most themes identified within the first 20% of the corpus) is methodologically analogous to their Voice Spectrum saturation — is fair. The Voice Spectrum rate-drop between Cohort 3 and Cohort 4 falls at roughly the 78%-of-corpus mark (Cohort 4 chapters 87–115 of 115), which is later than our 20%, but that is a difference of degree rather than kind. Voice registers may require more diverse cultural material to exhaust than the themes we coded from a relatively homogeneous interview sample. I accept the comparison.

What I want to push on is the stratified finding set against our finding. Our 12/60 ratio was an *aggregate* claim — we did not stratify themes by conceptual level, and when I look at the paper's aggregate ratio (15% final-to-initial rate by Cohort 4, saturation knee somewhere in Cohort 2–3), the LUCIA number is broadly consistent with ours. Where the paper makes a new claim is in the per-typology stratification, and here the comparison to our work is less clean than §2.1 implies. Our 2006 finding would predict that a LUCIA-style stratified analysis of interview themes might show analogous scale-dependent saturation — but we did not do that analysis, and the paper should not lean on our 12/60 ratio as if it validated the stratified result. It validates the aggregate result. The stratified result is the paper's own contribution and should be presented as such.

The place where the stratified finding most clearly *extends* our work is the Terminal Condition non-saturation. Our 2006 paper had no analog — no theme class that accrued indefinitely. If the authors are right that Terminal Condition reflects closure-level cultural specificity, this would suggest that in *our* data, some theme class (perhaps those most tied to individual life circumstances) might also have shown non-saturation if we had looked at it separately. That is a contribution to the qualitative-methods literature worth foregrounding. §6.3's transportability claim is too cautious; this is a finding with direct implications for how qualitative researchers plan sample sizes. I would welcome a short subsection in §6 explicitly spelling out what the LUCIA stratified finding implies for saturation-in-qualitative-interviewing.

Third, and more critically: the paper's 80% threshold is more lenient than anything our 2006 work would have tolerated. In qualitative practice, saturation is typically claimed when the new-code rate drops below ~5% of initial — i.e., a 95% threshold rather than an 80% threshold. The paper's 80% rule is therefore *easier to satisfy* than the implicit qualitative standard, which means a paper claiming saturation by the 80% rule might not be claiming saturation by the Guest/Bunce/Johnson rule. I do not think this invalidates the paper's claim (the observed ratios are 15%, 9%, 0% — all well below even a 95% threshold) but the threshold choice should be defended against tougher implicit thresholds in the qualitative literature, not just presented as a pre-registration.

## Major concerns

1. **Aggregate saturation claim is well-supported by comparison to our 12/60; stratified claim is not.** Distinguish these clearly in §2.1 and §5.6. Our work validates your aggregate; your stratification is novel and should be owned as such.
2. **The 80% threshold is lenient by qualitative-research standards.** Defend the choice against implicit 95% thresholds in Guest/Bunce/Johnson-style analyses. The observed ratios are far below either threshold, so this is a matter of framing rather than of substance.
3. **Terminal Condition non-saturation has direct implications for qualitative sample-size planning.** §6.3 should say so. The finding generalizes beyond AI rubrics.

## Minor concerns

1. §2.1's characterization of our 2006 finding is accurate but compresses some nuance. We found 12/60 for *most* themes but also noted that rarer themes required the full 60 — a point the paper's Terminal Condition finding parallels and could leverage.
2. §5.2's claim that Voice Spectrum's saturation knee falls "between chapters 60 and 90" is consistent with our data scaled up. A direct numerical comparison would strengthen §2.1.
3. I would welcome a brief sentence noting that "saturation" is a normative rather than purely empirical claim — this is a theme we wrestled with in the 2006 paper and the LUCIA work inherits it.

**Verdict**: Minor revision. Well-executed, properly cites our work, and extends it in a way I find genuinely interesting. The stratified finding deserves to be owned more boldly and the threshold defended more carefully.
