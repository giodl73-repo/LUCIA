# BOARD Review — Irving Rouse
## The Classic Taíno — Domain Accuracy

**Reviewer position:** Caribbean archaeology, Taíno material culture and chronology
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

**Chronology:** The era spanning c. 1200–1492 CE as "The Classic Taíno" is consistent with the archaeological periodization. The Taíno ceramic tradition (Chicoid series in the Greater Antilles) reaches its most elaborate development in the centuries before contact, which is consistent with the chapter's framing of this era as the period of peak cacicazgo complexity. No chronological errors detected.

**Material culture:**

- **Batey courts:** The description of the batey as flat, rectangular, stone-lined is accurate. The rubber-ball game using hips, knees, and shoulders is accurately described. The ceremonial function is consistent with the scholarly record. ACCURATE.

- **Zemí typology:** The chapter distinguishes between wooden carved figures, stone faces, and cotton figures stuffed with dry herbs. This is consistent with the material record: Taíno zemís appear in wood, stone, cotton, and shell. The distinction between zemís of the ancestors, zemís of natural forces, and the cacique's lineage zemís is implicit in the chapter's treatment and consistent with Pané's recordings. ACCURATE.

- **Conuco system:** The mound-based manioc cultivation system is accurately described. The earthen mounds that retain moisture and improve drainage are archaeologically attested. ACCURATE.

- **Tipití press:** The tipití (cylindrical woven press for extracting prussic acid from bitter manioc) is accurately described. The budare (clay griddle) for baking casabi is accurate. The detail about prussic acid in bitter manioc is accurate. ACCURATE.

- **Duho (carved wooden seat):** The backward tilt of the duho placing the sitter in a specific posture for zemí consultation is consistent with the archaeological and ethnographic record. ACCURATE.

**Population estimates:** The chapter gives a range of 300,000 to 2,000,000 and acknowledges the uncertainty ("the estimates vary enormously, because the Spanish did not count carefully what they intended to use as labor"). This is the honest presentation of what is genuinely an unresolved question in the scholarship. The range is consistent with current scholarly debate. ACCURATE.

**La Navidad garrison:** The chapter states the garrison was thirty-nine men. Historical records suggest Columbus left between thirty-nine and forty men at La Navidad. ACCURATE.

**One factual note:** The chapter attributes the 1496 reception to Bartholomew Columbus. The historical record supports this: Bartholomew (Bartolomé) Columbus, Christopher's brother, was in charge of Hispaniola during Christopher's absence and did travel to Xaragua in 1496. ACCURATE.

**Overall verdict:** The chapter's material and historical claims are accurate. No corrections required.
