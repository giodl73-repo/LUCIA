# Toledo y la Traducción — Toledo and the Translation
## 1085–1212 CE

The first thing Gerard of Cremona understood about Toledo was that it smelled of two cities at once.

From the south, the river. The Tagus wrapped the city's rock on three sides, cold even in August, carrying the particular mineral sharpness of water that had come down through limestone country. From the north and east, the city itself: baked stone and roasting mutton, the resinous smoke of cedar being worked in the Jewish quarter's workshops, the bright sting of acid from the tanneries. And underneath all of it, in the narrow streets that wound from the old mosque toward the cathedral, something older — the smell of manuscripts. Dust and goatskin and the faint sweetness of the oils used to cure the vellum against the dry Castilian air. It was this smell that had brought him from Lombardy, in the year 1145, by mule and by boat and by exhausted walking.

He had come for Ptolemy's *Almagest*.

The *Almagest* — the mathematical treatise that fixed the movements of the stars, that made the sky comprehensible — existed in Toledo in Arabic, translated centuries before from the original Greek by scholars whose names were now lost. In all of Latin Christendom, Gerard knew, there was no copy he could read. The mathematics of the sky belonged to a language that most Christian scholars could not approach. He had heard, from a Lombard merchant who traded cloth for silver in Burgos, that in Toledo — the city Alfonso VI had taken from the Moors sixty years before, in 1085 — the libraries were open. That Jewish scholars could read Arabic and Latin and their own Hebrew. That Christian canons and Muslim jurists still walked the same streets, used the same bathhouses, argued about the same texts.

He did not believe it entirely. He came anyway.

The canon who met him was a man named Dominicus Gundissalinus, an Archdeacon of Segovia who divided his time between Toledo's cathedral and the manuscript collections the new Christian administration had, by some governance miracle or simple pragmatism, left intact. Gundissalinus spoke Latin; his Arabic competence, if he had any, left no evidence — he worked through interlocutors whose bilingual range made the chain possible. He introduced Gerard to a Jewish scholar whose name Latin sources record as Abubacer — a Latinization of Abu Bakr, common enough in Arabic to identify nearly anyone — who spoke Arabic and Hebrew and enough Mozarabic to make the chain work.

This is how it went: Abubacer read the Arabic aloud. He rendered it into the spoken vernacular, stumbling sometimes, searching for the word that would carry the meaning across. Gundissalinus listened and wrote in Latin. Gerard stood behind both of them and watched and asked questions about the mathematics, about the values Ptolemy had assigned to the orbital radii, about the method for computing an eclipse. Sometimes the question required all three languages to answer. Sometimes all three men stopped and argued about what a word meant — not what it meant in Arabic or Latin, but what it meant for the thing the text was describing, for the actual motion of a planet against the fixed stars.

Gerard stayed forty years. He translated eighty-seven texts. His students, writing his biography after his death in 1187, noted that he had come to Toledo for the *Almagest* and had found, instead, that the *Almagest* was the least of what Toledo contained.

---

The city sat on a peninsula of granite above the Tagus, three sides of it cliff and river, the fourth guarded by walls the Romans had begun and everyone since had improved. From the highest point you could see thirty miles south on a clear day — the flat scrubland of the meseta rolling toward the Sierra Morena, toward al-Andalus, toward the world the city had been taken from and was now required to face. The geography was not accidental. Alfonso VI had not taken Toledo because it was conveniently located. He had taken it because it was the cultural capital of the Iberian peninsula — because the taifa kingdoms that had succeeded the Caliphate of Córdoba had concentrated their libraries, their mosques, their bathhouses, their textile workshops, and their mathematical instruments in this city above this river, and a king who held it held the argument about who was civilized in Iberia.

Alfonso VI was not shy about making the argument. He called himself *rex trium religionum* — king of the three religions. His son Alfonso VII went further: *Imperator totius Hispaniae*, emperor of all Hispania, a title that required a great city, because an emperor without a cultural capital was merely a warlord with pretension. Toledo, city of thirty thousand people in a kingdom whose largest Christian cities counted five thousand, was the only city in Castile that could begin to carry the title.

What this meant in practice: the libraries stayed open. The Mozarabic Christian communities who had lived under Muslim rule for three centuries — who spoke Arabic, read Arabic, prayed in Latin, and maintained a liturgical tradition older than the Reconquista — kept their rights under Alfonso VI's charter. The Jewish aljama in its quarter below the cathedral received its own charter confirming the internal governance it had maintained for generations. The Mudéjar craftsmen — Muslim artisans who remained after the Christian conquest — continued building in the style they had always used, which is why the churches of this era in Toledo have horseshoe arches and geometric tilework and ceilings of interlaced carved wood, the technique called *artesonado*, that no Christian craftsmen in Castile could yet match.

The Wednesday market in the Plaza del Zocodover — al-suq al-dawabb in Arabic, the market of the beasts, a name the Christian city kept unchanged because names for markets are made by the people who use them — drew traders from both sides of the frontier. A Castilian wool merchant who arrived at the Zocodover on a September morning in 1150 would have navigated his negotiations in Mozarabic, the spoken Romance dialect that served as the working tongue of commerce the way Latin served the Church. He might have bought spices from a Mudéjar trader who got them from a Granadan intermediary who got them from a North African merchant. The wool he sold would travel south. The spice would travel north. The money moved in both directions and did not care about theology.

This is what a city is for. Not for ideology. For the productive meeting of what one person has and another needs.

---

What the libraries held was not merely the preservation of Greek thought in Arabic translation. This is the misunderstanding that a later century would project backward, reading Toledo as a library where Greek wisdom waited in Arabic amber for a Latin scholar to release it. What Avicenna had written was not Aristotle. What al-Kindi had written was not Euclid. What al-Farabi had written was not Plato.

They were something more interesting: thinkers who had read Aristotle in Arabic translation, disagreed with him, extended him, corrected him, and built systems of thought that the Greek originals did not contain. Avicenna's *Canon of Medicine* — the *Kitab al-Qanun fi al-Tibb* — organized the entire medical knowledge of his world into a systematic framework that Galen and Hippocrates together had not achieved. Avicenna was not a librarian of Greek medicine. He found that Greek medicine's categories were insufficient for the diagnostic precision he required, and he built a new system of classification that reorganized the entire medical tradition around principles Galen had not articulated. The *Canon* was the result of building a more rigorous structure on Galen's foundations, using Galen's tools and discarding Galen's categories when a better arrangement was available.

Al-Kindi had done the same thing in optics. He did not merely transmit Greek geometric optics; he developed a theory of visual rays that extended and modified Euclid and Ptolemy in directions the Greeks had not pursued. Al-Farabi had read Plato and Aristotle and built a political philosophy that accounted for the specific conditions of an Islamic polity — a theory of the virtuous city that had no Greek equivalent because no Greek had lived in a city organized around revealed law.

When Gerard translated these texts, he was not releasing Greek wisdom from Arabic storage. He was bringing into Latin a living intellectual tradition that had spent four centuries building a philosophical infrastructure Latin Europe had not yet constructed, and the difference showed.

This was what the Arabic-speaking scholars of Toledo's aljamas and cathedral knew when they sat down with the manuscripts. They were not mediating between the ancient past and the European future. They were working with the intellectual output of a tradition that had not stopped producing.

---

Dominicus Gundissalinus did not think of himself as a transcriptionist.

He translated because he was a philosopher, and the philosophy he needed to do his work was in Arabic. His *De divisione philosophiae* — "On the Division of Philosophy," written around 1150 — is the first systematic attempt in Latin to integrate the Arabic Aristotelian tradition into Christian philosophical categories. It is not a translation. It is an original work that could only have been written by someone who had spent years with Arabic texts, who understood not just what al-Farabi and Avicenna argued but why they argued it, what problems they were solving, what their assumptions were. Gundissalinus used translation as a mode of original thought. The act of finding Latin equivalents for Arabic philosophical terms forced him to clarify what the Latin terms meant, which forced him to think more carefully than he could have thought from Latin sources alone.

This is what the translation school produced that its patrons did not quite intend: not just texts in a new language but thinkers who had been transformed by the encounter with a different intellectual tradition. Gerard became a mathematician of a different order because he had worked through Arabic astronomy. Gundissalinus became a philosopher he could not have been without Arabic philosophy. The transformation went in both directions — the Arabic tradition did not simply flow into Latin vessels; the encounter produced something new in both.

The work was funded by the cathedral chapter. This is not a minor logistical point. The royal treasury of Castile in the twelfth century was occupied primarily with maintaining fortifications, paying armies, and garrisoning frontier towns. The translation school was not a royal project. It was an ecclesiastical one, sustained by the income of the Archbishop's see and the patronage of individual canons who understood that the Church needed Aristotle in Latin if it was going to have the philosophical tools to address what educated clerics were starting to ask about the nature of God, the structure of creation, and the reliability of reason as a guide to faith. The church had its own reasons for wanting these texts, and those reasons persisted regardless of what was happening on the frontier.

This mattered in the years after 1195. When the Almohad armies crossed the Sierra Morena and met the Castilian knights at Alarcos in July of that year, Alfonso VIII's army was destroyed. The king survived; the battle did not. For the better part of a decade, Castile could not be certain it would survive. Fortifications were manned, taxes raised, alliances negotiated. The royal treasury had no room for intellectual projects. But the cathedral chapter still collected its rents. The workshops off the cathedral cloister still burned candles. The manuscripts were still there.

---

The Almohad movement had come out of the Atlas Mountains of Morocco a generation earlier, built on a religious reform that Ibn Tumart preached with the force of someone who had studied in Baghdad and Mecca and returned home convinced that Islam in the Maghreb had grown soft and syncretic. The Almoravids who had previously controlled Andalusia were, by Ibn Tumart's judgment, insufficiently rigorous about theology and insufficiently severe about the coexistence practices they tolerated in the cities of the south. The Almohads replaced them, took Seville and Córdoba and the fortified towns of Andalusia, and by the 1140s were pressing the northern frontier.

What this meant for Toledo was not immediate military threat — Toledo was too well-sited, too well-garrisoned, too far north — but cultural pressure of a different kind. The Almohads in Andalusia were not hospitable to the three-community arrangements that had sustained Toledo's particular character. Jewish and Christian communities under Almohad governance faced conversion or departure. The scholars who might have done this work in Seville or Córdoba came north, toward Toledo and the tolerant arrangements that still held there. The crisis in the south, paradoxically, concentrated Toledo's intellectual resources by pushing toward it the people most likely to do the work.

This is the compression that the translation era's first generation lived with: the Almohad pressure that produced the military catastrophe at Alarcos also produced the scholarly migration that made Toledo's workshop more productive in the 1150s and 1160s than it might otherwise have been. History does not make simple trades.

Alfonso VIII spent the years after Alarcos rebuilding what the battle had destroyed. He appealed to Pope Innocent III. He negotiated with Aragon and Navarre. He offered concessions and threatened consequences and waited for the political calendar of the peninsula to align in his favor. By 1212 he had assembled a coalition — Castile, Aragon, Navarre, Portugal, and the military orders — that crossed the Sierra Morena by way of a pass through the mountains.

The pass is the detail that history has debated. The coalition's advance required crossing the Sierra Morena, whose standard passes were defended. Contemporary sources mention a shepherd who showed the Castilian advance guard an alternative route — a narrow way through the hills, passable for an army if the army was willing to move in column. Some sources name him Martín Halaja. Some say his name was different. Some say there was no shepherd and the pass was known to the military orders, who had operated in this terrain for decades. What is not disputed is that the army crossed, and that on the morning of July 16, 1212, at a place called Las Navas de Tolosa near the modern town of Santa Elena, the combined Christian armies met the Almohad forces under the Caliph Muhammad al-Nasir and broke them.

The battle lasted most of the morning. The chronicles on both sides agree that the Christian cavalry broke through the Almohad center. After that, the Almohad force fragmented and the Christian pursuit continued south for days. The Caliph escaped. His treasury and his camp did not. Al-Nasir died two years later, in 1214, and the Almohad caliphate began to fracture from within. The battle did not end Islamic presence in Iberia — Granada would last until 1492 — but it ended the Almohad threat. Córdoba fell to Castile in 1236. Seville in 1248.

From Toledo's cathedral tower, on the morning of July 17, 1212, you could see nothing of what had happened forty miles to the south. The Tagus went where it always went. The market would open on Wednesday. The manuscripts were where they had been.

---

What changed, after Las Navas de Tolosa, was not visible in a morning. It moved the way the frontier had always moved: slowly, generation by generation, the line shifting south in increments that no one alive could see clearly but that their grandchildren would measure in conquered cities.

The territory that Castile absorbed after 1212 needed settlers. The fuero system — the charter model that had peopled the frontier towns north of Toledo with free settlers promised land and local governance — now extended south into Andalusia. Tens of thousands of Castilian settlers moved into reconquered territory over the following decades. The population balance of the expanded kingdom shifted. Toledo remained the cultural and ecclesiastical capital, but it was no longer the only city of consequence. Córdoba had its own cathedral now, built into the body of the great mosque. Seville had its own bishop.

The Jewish aljamas and the Mudéjar communities that had sustained Toledo's particular intellectual character were now subject to a different political climate. The Reconquista as an ideology had always required a logic of Christian supremacy — the fueros were Christian legal instruments, the military orders were Christian institutions, the crown's legitimacy rested on the completion of a project that was, at its ideological foundation, religious. Toledo's pragmatic coexistence had worked because the crown needed what three communities together could provide. As the military crisis receded and the kingdom expanded, the need shifted. The Almohad pressure that had concentrated scholars in Toledo was gone. The frontier that had made Jewish and Muslim expertise indispensable to Castilian governance had moved south. The conditions that had made the translation era possible began, slowly, to change into something else.

This is not a story with a villain. The pragmatic tolerance of Toledo's three-community world was built on mutual necessity, not on principle, and when the necessity shifted, the tolerance shifted with it. This is how most tolerance works.

The workshop continued. Gerard's translations were in the university curricula of Paris and Bologna and Oxford. Gundissalinus's students carried the Arabic-Aristotelian synthesis into the classrooms of the emerging universities. In Toledo, the cathedral chapter continued its intellectual projects. A generation later, Alfonso X — called "el Sabio," the Wise — would convene the greatest translation project the medieval world had seen, assembling Jewish and Muslim scholars under royal patronage to translate astronomical tables, legal codes, scientific texts, histories, and poetry into Castilian Spanish rather than Latin, making the work available not only to clerics but to anyone who could read the vernacular. The engine continued, under different conditions, in a different mode.

But the specific thing that Toledo had been — the city where Christian and Jewish and Muslim scholars worked in the same rooms because they needed each other, because the manuscripts were there and the chain was assembled and the question about what Ptolemy meant required all three languages to answer — that specific thing was already beginning to transform, even as the candles burned and Abubacer's successors continued their unhurried reading of texts that would outlast the world in which they were spoken.

The Tagus goes where it goes. The rock does not move. The city that sits on the rock continues, different in each century, carrying in its libraries and its arched doorways and its street names the accumulated residue of everyone who has needed it. Toledo was not a moment. It was a condition — the condition of the frontier city, where necessity makes neighbors of people whose theology insists on enemies, and where the neighbors produce, in the space of that necessity, something that neither could have made alone.

Gerard of Cremona understood this by the time he died in 1187, forty years after he had come for one book and found a library, and a city, and a way of working that the world he had left behind had not yet invented.
