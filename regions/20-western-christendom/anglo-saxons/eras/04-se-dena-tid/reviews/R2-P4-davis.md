# R2-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Review — PANEL-2 (notes review)
**Chapter:** Se Dena Tíd — The Danish Time (Anglo-Saxons, Era 4)

---

The notes have registered my main concern and committed to showing Alfred's translation program at method level — quoting the Preface to the *Pastoral Care*, showing the interpolations in Boethius. This is the right call.

I want to add one pressure point that the notes don't fully develop: the choice of which books to translate is itself an intellectual position.

Alfred chose: Gregory the Great's *Pastoral Care* (how to govern a Christian people), Bede's *Historia Ecclesiastica* (the history of the English Church and people), Orosius's *Histories Against the Pagans* (the whole history of the world, placed in Christian theological time), Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy* (the nature of fortune, providence, and the good life), and Augustine's *Soliloquies* (the nature of the soul and the afterlife). This is not a random list. It is a theory of what an English king and his administrators need to know: how to lead, where they come from, where they stand in history, how to think about suffering and loss, and what death means.

In other words, Alfred is not merely making Latin texts available. He is selecting and curating a knowledge system for a specific audience with specific needs. He is asking: what does a thegn in Dorset need to understand about the world? And he is answering that question. This is pedagogy as statecraft, and it is as intellectually rigorous as any knowledge system the project has documented.

The chapter should make this selection visible — not as a list, but as an argument Alfred is making. Why Boethius in particular, at this moment? Because Boethius was a man of high office who lost everything and had to find meaning in extremity. Alfred had just lived that. The *Consolation* is the book for a man in the marshes.

That is the method-level insight the chapter should carry.
