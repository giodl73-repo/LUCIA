# Timurids — Samarkand's Second Flowering

We are the Timurids — the dynasty that Timur (Tamerlane) founded and his descendants made magnificent. The two things are difficult to hold together, but they are inseparable: Timur's conquests between 1370 and 1405 CE were among the most devastating military campaigns in history, destroying the economy of Iran, sacking Delhi, slaughtering the populations of cities from Baghdad to Moscow's suburbs. And then his descendants — his sons and grandsons and great-grandsons — took the wealth extracted by those campaigns and built Samarkand into the most beautiful city in the world, produced the finest miniature painting in the Islamic tradition, wrote and patronized poetry in Persian and Chaghatai Türkic, founded astronomical observatories, and created the cultural synthesis that would shape the Mughal Empire of India, the Safavid dynasty of Persia, and the Ottoman court simultaneously. The Timurid Renaissance emerged from the ruins of the Timurid conquest. We did not apologize for the contradiction; we inhabited it.

The name we most often give ourselves is Timurids, but Timur himself claimed a different identity: he was a Mongol successor, legitimated by the Chinggisid lineage of the puppet khans he kept at his court while exercising real power himself. He could not be the Khan — he was not of Chinggis Khan's direct descent — so he was always the Amir, the military commander. But in practice he was the empire. After him, his successors ruled as sultans and princes in the Persian-Islamic tradition, surrounded by the Persian literary and artistic culture that was the medium through which steppe rulers expressed their cultivated sovereignty.

## What Defined Them

**Timur as the last great conqueror.** Timur's campaigns were the last successful attempt to reconstitute the Mongol Empire by military force. He defeated the Golden Horde, the Chagatai Khanate, the Mamluk Sultanate, the Ottoman Empire, and the Delhi Sultanate in succession — a record of military achievement unmatched in the medieval era. His victories were systematic: he studied the strengths of each opponent and found their structural weakness. The Ottoman defeat at Ankara (1402) was so complete that Timur captured the Ottoman sultan Bayezid in person and used him as a footstool (the historical reality behind the legend). But Timur died in 1405 on campaign against Ming China, and without his personal authority the empire began to fragment immediately.

**The great building program.** Timur's construction projects in Samarkand — the Bibi-Khanym mosque, the Shah-i-Zinda necropolis, the Gur-e-Amir mausoleum, the Registan square — were executed by craftsmen forcibly transported from every conquered land. The blue-tiled domes of Samarkand's buildings, the geometric muqarnas vaulting, the calligraphic programs in faience tilework, are the architectural expression of the entire Islamic world's artistic tradition concentrated in a single city. The buildings were finished by the artisans' children; Timur did not survive to see most of them completed. The construction was both aesthetic program and political statement: Samarkand as the center of the world.

**The Timurid miniature tradition.** The great achievement of Timurid court culture was the development of the Persian miniature painting tradition to its classical height. The painter Kamal ud-Din Bihzad, working at the Herat court of Husayn Bayqara in the late 15th century, is considered the finest miniaturist in the Islamic tradition. The Timurid miniatures — illustrated manuscripts of the Persian epics (Shahnameh, Khamseh), historical chronicles, and poetry collections — combine a mastery of color, composition, and detail that makes them visually overwhelming even without the literary context. The tradition passed directly to the Safavid and Mughal courts.

**Babur and the written self.** Zahir ud-Din Muhammad Babur — Timurid prince, driven from Samarkand and Fergana by the Uzbeks, eventual founder of the Mughal dynasty — wrote the *Baburnama*, the first true autobiography in any Islamic language. In it, he describes with extraordinary candor his military failures, his love for his homeland of Fergana, his appreciation of melons and flowers, his wrestling with grief, and his eventual conquest of Hindustan. The *Baburnama* is not only a historical source; it is a literary achievement that reveals a fully individual voice — curious, melancholy, precise, and self-aware — at the intersection of the steppe, Persian, and Indian worlds.

## The Chapters

**1 — Samarkand's New Dome:** The architects that Timur captured in Isfahan and Damascus are completing the dome of the Bibi-Khanym mosque. They are working under duress — they were brought here in chains — but the work they are doing is among the finest of their careers, and they know it. The dome rises. Timur is already on campaign again. He may never see the finished building.

**2 — Shah Rukh's Court:** Timur's son has made peace with the neighbors. The campaigns are over. The poets are arriving at Herat. Shah Rukh is a devout, careful man — nothing like his father — and his patronage is real. The libraries are growing. The observatories are being built. The Timurid Renaissance begins not with triumph but with the exhausted relief of surviving Timur.

**3 — Bihzad Paints:** The painter is applying the final highlights to the garden scene. The figures are arranged in the complex overlapping planes he developed: multiple perspectives, brilliant colors, the action distributed across the entire composition. No Islamic painter before him has achieved this integration of drama and decoration. His work will influence every court in the Islamic world within a generation.

**4 — Babur Loses Samarkand:** For the third time, Babur has been driven out of Samarkand by the Uzbek forces of Muhammad Shaybani Khan. He is twenty-two years old. He has had the city twice and lost it twice. He will never get it back. He writes about this in his memoir with a precision of grief that makes the loss vivid five hundred years later. He goes south. India is a consolation prize that becomes an empire.

**5 — The Mughal Inheritance:** Babur is sitting in a garden in Kabul — he has built it himself, in the Persian style — missing the melons of Fergana. He is planning the invasion of Hindustan. When he wins at Panipat in 1526 and establishes the Mughal Empire, he carries to India the Persian literary tradition, the Timurid miniature tradition, the garden design tradition, and the political culture of a dynasty that measured itself against the Mongol inheritance. The Taj Mahal is, among many other things, a Timurid building.
