## Domain Assessment: The Indian Ocean World

The chapter's framing of the Indian Ocean as a functioning trade system before Portuguese arrival is historically accurate and reflects current scholarship on the pre-Portuguese Indian Ocean.

### The Zamorin's Trade System

The chapter correctly portrays Calicut (Kozhikode) as the largest entrepôt on the Malabar coast, with an established multi-ethnic trading community. The characterization of the Zamorin's administration as maintaining trade conditions as a function of royal power is accurate: the Zamorin (Samutiri) collected customs duties, maintained order in the harbor, and provided security for merchants of all origins. This was not mere tolerance — it was the economic foundation of Calicut's power.

The chapter's statement that Chinese junks, Arab dhows, Gujarati merchants, and Malay traders shared the Calicut harbor is accurate for the period. The Zamorin's court included both Hindu Nair warriors who formed the military, and Muslim Mappila merchants who handled much of the maritime trade and served as essential intermediaries. This mixed commercial-military structure is not mentioned in the chapter, which focuses on the encounter's drama rather than the structural detail. No revision required, but the Mappila merchants' role as intermediaries is worth noting for any future cross-reference with Region 15 (South Asia).

### Da Gama's Gifts and the Zamorin's Assessment

The chapter correctly identifies the inadequacy of da Gama's gifts. The primary sources (Gaspar Correia, the Roteiro of the first voyage) confirm that the Zamorin's officials found the gifts inappropriate for a king — this was the accurate assessment. The Zamorin received Chinese, Arab, and Gujarati merchants regularly who brought appropriate gifts: horses, gold, fine cloth, porcelain. Da Gama's striped cloth and honey were merchant gifts, not royal gifts.

One factual note: the chapter states that "the Zamorin's chamberlain" assessed the gifts. The primary sources use slightly different terminology. The official who received da Gama's gifts is described in Portuguese sources as a "catual" (an official responsible for the port and merchants). Whether this figure would be accurately described as a "chamberlain" is debatable. The Portuguese sources use their own rendering of Indian official titles. The chapter's formulation is acceptable given its approach from the empire's perspective.

### The Malacca Account

The chapter's account of Malacca's fall (1511) is accurate. The note about the sultan fleeing "into the jungle with his court and his treasury and his grudge, which lasted for generations in the form of Johor" correctly identifies the Johor Sultanate as the successor state. Johor's sustained opposition to Portuguese Malacca throughout the sixteenth century is historically accurate and well-summarized.

The chapter's statement that "the Gujarati and Javanese merchants who had organized their trade through Malacca relocated" is accurate. Aceh and Johor both became major entrepôts for trade redirected away from Portuguese Malacca, and this is documented in Portuguese factory records from the period.

### Verdict

**APPROVED.** No factual corrections required. Minor note on the "catual" vs. "chamberlain" terminology logged above — the chapter's framing is defensible from its perspective but a footnote in figures.md might acknowledge the Portuguese rendering of Indian official titles.
