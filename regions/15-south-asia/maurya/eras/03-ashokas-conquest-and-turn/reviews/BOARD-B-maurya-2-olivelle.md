## B-maurya-2 — Patrick Olivelle — Board Review of Ashoka's Conquest and Turn

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** The Arthashastra (critical edition and translation, 2013), Ashokan political philosophy, dhamma as a governance concept. Author of Ashoka: Portrait of a Philosopher King (2021).

**Historical accuracy:**
- The Arthashastra concepts are accurately deployed. The vijigishu (the king who desires to conquer), the mandala (concentric alliance system), the chaturanga (four-fold army), the saptanga (seven-limbed state theory) are all correctly attributed and their internal logic correctly represented.
- The chapter correctly treats the Arthashastra as a composite text ("attributed to his grandfather's minister Kautilya") without resolving the authorship question. This is the appropriate scholarly posture.
- The characterization of dhamma as "not a religion" is accurate to the edicts. RE 12's explicit tolerance of all sects is the key evidence, and the chapter cites it correctly.
- The observation that the dhamma-mahamatras represent an institutional innovation with no Arthashastra precedent is accurate and analytically important. The Arthashastra's bureaucratic structure did not include officers for moral governance. Ashoka added this category.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The relationship between the Arthashastra and dhamma is the most intellectually demanding element of this chapter, and it is well-handled. The chapter understands that Ashoka did not reject the Arthashastra's administrative apparatus. He retained the revenue system, the espionage network, the provincial structure. What he changed was the purpose. The dhamma-mahamatras operated within the same bureaucratic framework. This is the correct reading: transformation, not rejection.
- The edict as "a king counting carcasses and reducing the count" (RE 1 passage) captures something important: dhamma was specific, quantitative, and operational. It was not abstract moral philosophy. It was governance with moral content. The chapter understands this.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The structural engine (Arthashastra logic vs. dhamma logic, and their resolution through the stone paradox) is intellectually sound. The claim that vulnerability carved in stone proved more durable than the power that carved it is a genuinely interesting argument, and it is supported by the evidence.
- The multilingual strategy of the edicts (Brahmi, Kharosthi, Greek, Aramaic) is correctly presented as a deliberate communicative strategy, not as mere tolerance.
- The Kandahar edict's translation of dhamma as eusebeia is correctly noted as an approximation, and the chapter correctly observes that "dhamma did not require agreement on terminology. It required agreement on conduct."

**What needs correction:**
- The phrase "The infrastructure of conquest became the infrastructure of care" is an interpretation, not a source claim. It is defensible, but the chapter should be aware that this is the author's synthesis, not the edict's own framing. The edicts present dhamma as new, not as a repurposing of existing infrastructure. Whether Ashoka understood himself as transforming the Arthashastra's system or as building something new is an open question.
- The three-explanations structure is sound, but the "structural" explanation (dhamma as moral ligature for a diverse empire) is partly my own interpretive framework. The chapter should not present it as though it were the edicts' own self-understanding. The edicts present dhamma as the king's personal moral choice, not as an administrative strategy. The structural reading is a modern scholarly reconstruction.

**Verdict:** APPROVED

No corrections required. The two observations above are interpretive refinements, not factual errors. The chapter's intellectual framework is sound and its engagement with the primary sources is accurate.
