# Figures — Era 1: The Parima World

**On the record:** The Parima World of c. 1000–1750 CE produced no written records. No individual Yanomami from this era is known by name. The shapori who carried the knowledge, the headmen who held the communities in balance, the women who ground the ash of their dead — they are present in the ethnographic record as types and practices, not as named persons. This is the nature of oral transmission in a world without a writing tradition: the knowledge survived, the names did not.

The figures below are composite, drawn from the ethnographic literature — primarily Davi Kopenawa's testimony in *The Falling Sky*, Jacques Lizot's daily-life ethnography, and the corrective scholarship that has resituated the Yanomami away from Napoleon Chagnon's contested "fierce people" frame. They are historical in the sense that they are grounded in documented practice; they are fictional in the sense that no specific person exactly matching any description below has been confirmed. The chapter acknowledges this not as a limitation but as the epistemological condition of this specific archive.

---

## Aramo — The Shapori

Aramo is the chapter's primary figure — a shapori of long experience, old enough that his knees ache from years on the ground, carrying a knowledge of the hekura that took two years of initiatory starvation and decades of practice to accumulate.

What makes Aramo a figure rather than a function is the chapter's insistence on his particularity: his diligent maintenance of the inner landscape (he is distinguished from a shapori he knew who took shortcuts), his awareness that the tepui knowledge is being thinly held across the generation (only two other shapori carry it), his practical decision-making about the child's fever (calling three hekura in sequence, narrowing toward the diagnosis), his concern about his apprentices and what survives after him.

He does not dominate the chapter. He is the chapter's center of attention in the opening and returns in the section on the shapori's method, but the feast section, the ash section, and the landing belong to the community rather than to any individual. This is appropriate: the shapori's significance is precisely that the work he does belongs to everyone.

**Composite basis:** The phenomenology of Aramo's calling draws directly from Kopenawa's first-person account of his own shapori experience — the dissolution of bodily edges, the chest as the place where hekura settle, the specific quality of older versus younger hekura. The diagnostic sequence (general survey, narrowing, specific hekura for specific conditions) is documented across multiple Yanomami ethnographers. The concern about knowledge transmission reflects Kopenawa's recurring theme in *The Falling Sky*: the epidemic deaths of the twentieth century killed shapori faster than they could be replaced, and the loss of knowledge this represented was a form of civilizational damage that Kopenawa frames as his primary grief.

---

## Towaho — The Visiting Shapori

Towaho appears in the feast section as Aramo's counterpart from the neighboring community — younger, with a particular reputation for the wind's hekura, present for two days of collaborative shamanic work. He represents the inter-community knowledge network that the chapter's engine requires.

His particularity is slight but deliberate: he has a reputation specifically for the wind's hekura (not general shamanic skill), he is younger than Aramo (the difference in experience creates a texture to their working relationship, the older shapori's knowledge wider, the younger's fresher in certain areas), and he is described through the diagnostic session in action rather than in profile — the reader understands Towaho through what he does, not through what is said about him.

The collaboration between Aramo and Towaho is the chapter's most explicit demonstration of the structural engine: two shapori whose different knowledge-territories complement each other, brought together by an alliance that was itself sustained through feasting, producing a diagnostic result that neither could reach alone.

**Composite basis:** The inter-community shamanic collaboration is documented across Venezuelan and Brazilian Yanomami communities. Lizot's accounts include several descriptions of shapori from different communities working together at feasts. The specific hekura attributed to Towaho (wind, long-distance travel, large-area survey) are confirmed types in the ethnographic literature.

## The Old Woman

She appears twice in the chapter — looking up from her fire to note Aramo at work, then looking down again, and later watching the soup while her granddaughter plays with the puppy. She is unnamed and undescribed beyond her actions. She is the chapter's most important figure.

The old woman's looking-up-and-looking-down is the chapter's central claim about the shapori's work: it is not a ceremony separate from daily life, observed with reverence or awe, but a maintenance task noted and returned from without interruption. The old woman is the reader's guide to the correct relationship between the shapori at work and the community around him. She tells us, without telling us, what this work means and how it sits within the ordinary.

Her granddaughter and the puppy are the chapter's lightest presence — the ordinary details of an ordinary evening, placed against the shapori's cosmic negotiation to show that neither the cosmic nor the ordinary is more real than the other. The puppy with the palm frond is, in its own way, the chapter's most important sensory detail.

**Composite basis:** The figure of the experienced community member for whom the shapori's work is simply part of the community's functioning is pervasive in ethnographic accounts. Kopenawa's account includes many moments where the xapiri's work is described as unremarkable to those around the shapori — ordinary maintenance, not theater.

## The Child

The feverish child is never characterized — never given a gender, a specific age, a name. This is deliberate. The child is the occasion for the chapter's engine to operate, not a person in the chapter's primary sense. The child's fever is the presenting problem that makes visible the system of diagnosis, alliance, and cooperative healing.

The child recovers. The recovery is mentioned briefly — "over the following week" — and without fanfare. The understatement is calibrated: a great recovery would make the chapter about a successful healing. A quiet recovery keeps the chapter about the system that made healing possible.

## Davi Kopenawa (interpretive authority, not a character)

Kopenawa is the chapter's epistemological anchor, not its character. He does not appear in the narrative of Era 1, which predates him by centuries. But his testimony in *The Falling Sky* — his account of learning the yakoana and the hekura from his father-in-law and other shapori, his description of the inner landscape's maintenance, his articulation of the shamanic cosmos as continuous ecological knowledge — structures the chapter's understanding of what Aramo is doing and why it matters.

The chapter uses Kopenawa as the inside interpretive frame rather than any anthropological account precisely because his is the only inside account we have. The anthropological literature (Chagnon, Lizot, Good) provides ethnographic detail; Kopenawa provides epistemological authority. He tells us what the practice means from within. The chapter honors this by never stepping outside the frame he provides.

**Source note:** Kopenawa, Davi, and Bruce Albert. *The Falling Sky: Words of a Yanomami Shaman*. Cambridge: Harvard University Press, 2013. This is the chapter's primary interpretive source. Albert's extensive scholarly apparatus does not override Kopenawa's voice; it contextualizes it. Where Albert and Kopenawa's framings differ, the chapter follows Kopenawa.

## Technical Notes (Board Corrections Applied)

**Regional variation:** The chapter is set primarily in the Parima highlands — Venezuelan Yanomami territory. Practices described (yakoana preparation, hekura associations, reahu protocol) are documented specifically for Venezuelan highland communities. Brazilian Yanomami communities (Demini, Catrimani, Toototobi watersheds) share the broad framework but differ in some specifics. The chapter should not be read as describing universal Yanomami practice.

**Yakoana preparation:** The primary additive to Virola theiodora resin in documented Venezuelan highland Yanomami practice is ash from Elizabetha princeps bark (documented by Schultes, 1985, and consistent with Lizot's accounts). The chapter's narrative-level reference to "the ash of a specific tree bark" is accurate. The species is not named in the chapter to avoid false precision at the level of narrative — practice varies by shapori and community.

**Reahu timing:** The chapter has been revised (per board correction) to reflect the documented protocol: cremation is immediate; bone-ash preparation and storage follows over days; the reahu ceremony is held months after death, following the formal invitation of allied communities and the preparation of food stores. The ash is stored in a decorated gourd in the family's section of the shabono during the interval.

**Tepui hekura and collaborative shamanism:** The chapter has been revised (per board correction) to reflect that tepui shamanism typically involves experienced shapori working together rather than individual calling. The weight of the ancient hekura is distributed between shapori in collaborative session. Individual shapori may have greater or lesser access to tepui hekura, but the full calling is typically a joint practice.

**Hekura specificity:** The chapter's naming convention ("hekura of the waterfall," "hekura of the wind") is a narrative shorthand. In documented practice, hekura/xapiri are more specifically identified — often named for the animal or spirit entity associated with a particular place or force, with the location being one attribute among several. The narrative shorthand is accurate at the level of jurisdiction (this hekura is associated with this kind of territory) without claiming the specificity of naming that varies across communities and shapori.
