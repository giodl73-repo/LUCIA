# Haudenosaunee — "The People of the Longhouse"

They call themselves Haudenosaunee — "the people of the longhouse," or more precisely, "they who are building the extended house." The longhouse was not merely their dwelling; it was the organizing metaphor of their political philosophy. The five nations of the original confederacy — Kanien'kehá:ka (Mohawk), Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, and Seneca (with Tuscarora joining as the sixth in the early eighteenth century) — were understood as the extended families living in a single great longhouse. The eastern door, guarded by the Kanien'kehá:ka, faced the Atlantic coast. The western door, guarded by the Seneca, faced the Ohio Valley. The central fire was kept at the Onondaga nation's territory. The confederacy was not a metaphor dressed up as politics. It was a political philosophy given architectural form.

The Great Law of Peace — Kaianere'kó:wa, "the great law" or "the good word" — was given to the confederacy by the Peacemaker (Skennenrahawi, "he who brings peace") and his companion Aiionwatha (Hiawatha), who together persuaded the five nations to lay down their weapons and govern through the Grand Council. The Great Law was not simply a set of rules. It was a philosophy: that the purpose of governance was to extend the peace of the household to ever wider circles, that decisions must be made by unanimous consent, that the voices of the women (clan mothers, who held the authority to nominate and remove chiefs) were as essential as the voices of the men who sat in council. The confederacy's political structures — representation by clan, decision by consensus, the separation of war powers from council powers — were the most sophisticated system of confederate governance in the northeastern woodlands.

## What Defined Them

**Kaianere'kó:wa — the Great Law as a living philosophy.** The Great Law was not a document in the Western legal sense. It was a set of principles, transmitted orally and encoded in wampum belts, that governed the conduct of the Grand Council and the relationships between the nations. Its central principles included: unanimous consent required for any decision affecting all nations; the authority of clan mothers to nominate, confirm, and remove sachems (chiefs); the separation of war power (not governed by the Great Law) from council power (fully governed by it); and the obligation of every nation to consider the welfare of the seventh generation to come. This last principle — that today's decisions must be evaluated for their effect on people seven generations in the future — is among the most demanding and specific political obligations in any governance tradition anywhere in the world.

**The clan mother and matrilineal authority.** In the Haudenosaunee system, political authority was held by men (the sachems who sat in the Grand Council), but the authority to select and remove those men was held by women — specifically, the clan mothers, who were the senior women of the clan lineages. A sachem who governed poorly could be warned, warned again, and then removed ("dehorned" — having the antler headdress of his office taken away) by the clan mother who had appointed him. This was not a system of women's subordination with a formal exception. It was a system in which the political and reproductive authority of women was the foundation on which men's governance was built.

**Wampum as the law's record.** Wampum — the shell beads, white and purple, strung in patterns on belts — was the Haudenosaunee writing system for political agreements. Not all wampum was political (shell beads had a ceremonial and exchange function throughout the northeast), but the great wampum belts — the Two Row Wampum (the coexistence agreement with the Dutch), the Hiawatha Belt (the confederacy's founding document), the strings presented at condolence ceremonies — were the confederacy's archives, kept by the Onondaga and read aloud by trained keepers at council meetings. The keeper of the wampum was the keeper of the law, and the reading of the wampum was the recitation of the Great Law — a living document that was physically present in the room every time the Grand Council met.

**The condolence ceremony — grief as governance.** The founding insight of the Great Law was that the Mourning Wars — the raids conducted to capture replacements for community members killed in previous raids — were a cycle of grief that had no political resolution unless grief itself was addressed. The condolence ceremony (the ohén:ton karihwatéhkwen — the words that come before all else) was the formal ritual of acknowledging grief, clearing the mind of the bereaved, and restoring the capacity for deliberation. Every Grand Council meeting began with the condolence ceremony. The Peacemaker's insight was that governance requires clear minds, and clear minds require the acknowledgment of grief. Politics begins not with power but with mourning.

**The two-path diplomacy.** The Haudenosaunee maintained a consistent and sophisticated diplomatic posture toward European colonial powers: the Two Row Wampum model, in which the Haudenosaunee and European canoes traveled the same river in parallel without crossing into each other's — a model of coexistence that required neither side to assimilate to the other. This was not a naive diplomatic formula; it was a carefully maintained position that the confederacy held, with varying success, for two centuries. When the American Revolution forced the nations to choose sides, the two-path model became impossible to sustain. But the diplomatic sophistication that had made it work for so long was itself a political achievement of the first order.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Time Before the Law:** The Onondaga sachem Atotarho is the confederacy's last holdout — a man so twisted by grief and anger that his hair is said to be made of serpents. The Peacemaker and Hiawatha must comb the serpents from his hair before the confederacy can be founded. This is not metaphor. It is a description of what grief does to a person's capacity for political thought, and what healing that grief requires.

**2 — The Founding:** The Grand Council meets for the first time under the Great Tree of Peace. The Peacemaker plants the tree and buries the weapons of war beneath its roots. The five nations take their seats around the council fire. The Great Law is recited. The confederacy exists.

**3 — The Longhouse at Its Height:** A clan mother at Onondaga has decided that the sachem she appointed three years ago is governing badly. She has warned him twice. The third warning is the removal. She sends the message. He is dehorned. His replacement is already being considered. The Great Law is working as intended.

**4 — The Dutch and English Encounter:** Kanien'kehá:ka diplomats at the Dutch trading post are negotiating the terms of the Two Row Wampum. They present a white wampum belt with two parallel purple rows. This is the agreement: two peoples traveling the same river in their own vessels, without interference, without assimilation. The Dutch accept the terms. They will not always honor them. The belt still exists.

**5 — The Great Peace and Its Fracture:** At the Grand Council in the winter of 1776, the Haudenosaunee nations are trying to maintain neutrality in a war between European colonial factions that has been framed by each side as requiring Haudenosaunee support. The neutrality holds for one year. Then it fractures. The confederacy that the Peacemaker founded to end the cycle of war is about to fight the most destructive war in its history — against itself.
