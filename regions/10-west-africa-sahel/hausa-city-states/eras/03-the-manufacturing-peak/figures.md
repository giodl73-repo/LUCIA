# Figures — The Manufacturing Peak (v2)

## Ibrahim al-Katsinawi (composite, named)

He is not in the Kano Chronicle, because the Kano Chronicle records sarkis and their building projects, not cloth merchants and their brass weights. But the Chronicle records the expansion of the Katsina market under successive rulers, and Leo Africanus, passing through around 1526, described a market where leather and cloth from Kano were assessed and traded in volume. The cloth merchant who knew the difference between a Marina guild vat and an apprentice's vat was not a rare figure — he was the system's quality-control mechanism at the downstream end.

Ibrahim al-Katsinawi is composite in the strict sense: the name is invented, the individual is not historical. The category — a long-distance cloth merchant based in Katsina, buying Kano goods for Saharan resale, holding a calibrated brass weight, with twenty-plus years of experience reading cloth quality — is well-documented as a social type in this period. The brass weight calibrated against the sarki's official standard is historically attested as the trust mechanism that made interurban cloth trade possible.

His father's principle — "the weight of things is the beginning of trust" — is invented dialogue. But it describes accurately the epistemological foundation of the Hausa commercial system: quality consistency as the precondition for trust, trust as the precondition for distance trade, distance trade as the precondition for the manufacturing economy's scale. The invention is honest in that it names, through a plausible voice, something that the historical record confirms as structurally true.

## Queen Amina of Zazzau

The dates are disputed. The Kano Chronicle places a powerful ruler of Zazzau in the sixteenth century. Oral tradition commonly gives her a reign of approximately 1588 to 1610, though some scholars have argued for an earlier period. What the sources share is the outline: a woman who ruled Zazzau, who led military campaigns, who expanded the city-state's territory to its farthest reach, and whose soldiers built the earthwork walls that still bear her name.

The wet-season campaign scene in Section III is constructed from what the geography and military logistics establish as true for campaigns south of the Sahelian belt. Cavalry operations in the transitional zone between the Sahel and the Benue valley forest — the terrain Amina's campaigns entered — were genuinely constrained by the laterite clay underfoot, the shortened sight lines, and the mosquitoes that arrived at dusk. The specific adaptation (narrower walls, better drained, soldiers returning to pack previous work) is inferred from what we know about earthwork construction in wet-ground conditions. It is constructed from method, not from sources that describe Amina's specific orders.

The tradition about the consort — taken in each conquered town, left at dawn — is recorded in the oral sources and in Hogben and Kirk-Greene. The chapter does not adjudicate whether this is biographical fact or the tradition's way of expressing her authority and autonomy. It treats it as what the tradition chose to preserve, and that choice is itself historical evidence about what the Hausa world found worth remembering.

The earthworks are real. They are visible between modern Zaria and the Benue, and their attribution to Amina, whatever the precise history of their construction, reflects the tradition's assignment of the southern expansion to a single transformative figure.

## Muhammad Rumfa, Sarki of Kano (r. c. 1463–1499)

Rumfa is the Kano Chronicle's transformational sarki, and the chapter treats him as historical background rather than dramatic figure. His key achievements — the Kurmi market, the expanded walls, the Tara-ta-Kano (council of nine titled officials), the invitation to al-Maghili — are all documented in the Chronicle. Al-Maghili's treatise for Rumfa, the *Taj al-Din fi ma Yajib ala al-Muluk* (The Crown of Religion Concerning the Obligations of Rulers), is a real text, and its central concern (how to govern justly in a city that is not entirely Muslim) is accurately characterized.

Rumfa does not appear in scenes in this chapter. He appears as the institutional founder, the sarki whose reforms created the infrastructure that the Manufacturing Peak rests on. This is the appropriate role for a figure who lived fifty years before the chapter's period opens.

Note on the Tara-ta-Kano: the chapter describes the nine titled officials as collectively administering guilds, markets, military, and treasury. This is a simplification of a more differentiated structure: the Galadima administered civil affairs, the Wombai had distinct responsibilities, and so on (Smith, *Government in Kano*). The chapter's summary is accurate at the level of the institutional relationship (a council of titled officials advising and serving the sarki) but does not represent the full internal differentiation.

## The Senior Bori Woman (unnamed, composite)

She is not named because the Bori practitioners of this period are not individually named in the historical record. The social category — a senior woman with decades of experience in the possession practice, trained in the drum rhythms associated with specific iskoki, capable of reading the onset of a possession and adjusting the ceremony accordingly — is documented by Paden's fieldwork in Kano, which, while conducted in the twentieth century, describes practices with deep historical roots confirmed in earlier sources.

Her knowledge is rendered in Section IV at the level of method rather than description: which drums call which spirits, how the possession's onset is recognized through the possessed person's posture and voice, how the offering is adjusted when the spirit's identity is confirmed. Paden documents that specific drum rhythms are associated with specific spirits in Hausa Bori practice. The detail that a misidentified spirit or an incorrect offering has consequences is consistent with how the practice understands itself.

The comparison to the guild dyer (years of embodied knowledge, no text to transmit it, the transmission through practice and apprenticeship) is analytical — it is the chapter's argument about parallel knowledge systems — but it is an honest one. Both disciplines require years to acquire. Both are transmitted through doing rather than through reading. Both serve functions that the city's other institutions cannot serve. The comparison does not flatten either discipline into the other. It identifies the structural similarity.

## Al-Maghili (Muhammad ibn Abd al-Karim al-Maghili, c. 1425–1503)

Al-Maghili came from Tlemcen in the Maghreb and traveled through the western Sudan in the late fifteenth century, visiting the courts of Timbuktu, Gao, and Kano and offering counsel on Islamic governance. His treatise for Rumfa is a real text. His visit is documented. His central concern — the gap between Islamic legal ideals and the pragmatic accommodations the Hausa sarkis had made — is accurately characterized.

He appears in this chapter only as historical reference (Section IV), cited as the man who pointed at the gap two centuries before the Fulani scholars would turn the gap into a revolution. His role is to establish that the gap was visible and named from within the Islamic scholarly tradition from an early stage, not a belated discovery.

## The Fulani Scholar (unnamed, composite)

By the seventeenth century, Fulani scholars were an established presence in the Hausa city-states, particularly in Katsina and Kano. The composite figure in Section V represents this scholarly presence before it became a revolutionary movement. He is teaching, not agitating. His students are writing down what he says. The manuscripts will not stay in Katsina.

This figure is deliberately not named because the specific individuals who became historically significant — Jibril ibn Umar and then Usman dan Fodio — belong to Era 4. In this era, the Fulani scholarly presence is a current, not yet a storm. Naming him would imply a specificity the historical record cannot support for this period's individual teachers.

The detail that the students are writing down what the scholar says, and that the manuscripts will not stay in Katsina, is the chapter's closing image rather than a documented fact about a specific event. It describes the mechanism by which the critique traveled and accumulated: copied, carried, read in other cities, building a textual tradition that would eventually become Dan Fodio's arsenal. The chapter closes on this image rather than naming it as conclusion.
