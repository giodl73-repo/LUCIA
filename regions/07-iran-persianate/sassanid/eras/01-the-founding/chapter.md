# The Founding — Ardashir and Shapur I (c. 224–272 CE)

The mason does not begin at the bottom of the cliff. He begins at the summit, where the Achaemenid tombs already stand — Darius, Xerxes, Artaxerxes — their facades carved into the vertical limestone face of Naqsh-e Rostam four centuries before Ardashir was born. The mason studies the height of those doorways, the proportions of the columns, the angle of light in the late afternoon when the shadows lengthen and the relief carvings speak most clearly to anyone on the road below. Then he descends. Ardashir's investiture scene will be carved at the base of that cliff, where a man on horseback is the natural scale, where the composition can be read by any rider passing on the road from Persepolis.

This is not modesty. It is a statement.

Above: the Achaemenid kings, their tombs cut into the mountain, their bodies sealed in silence.

Below: Ardashir, king of kings — *shāhanshāh* — receiving the ring of kingship from the hand of Ahura Mazda himself. The god rides a horse to the left. Ardashir rides a horse to the right. Between them, on the ground, crushed beneath the hooves of Ardashir's mount, lies Artabanus IV, the last Arsacid, the last king of the Parthian line. Beneath Ahura Mazda's horse lies Ahriman — the Lie, the dark twin, the cosmic enemy of truth. The two pairs mirror each other exactly: the divine defeating evil above, the king defeating the old order below. The message does not require translation. The world has been restored to its proper alignment.

On the road below, a merchant from Shiraz reins his mule and tilts his head back. The cliff face catches the afternoon light. He does not need to read the Avestan inscription to understand what he is seeing. He has seen Parthian coins — the small silver drachms that passed through his hands every market day, some bearing the face of a Hellenistic god alongside the Arsacid king's portrait, the Greek lettering worn smooth from handling. Those coins are gone now, melted or buried. The new coinage shows Ardashir on one face and a fire altar on the other. No Greek gods. The altar burns. The merchant knows something about this: Ardashir was putting the fire altar on his coins before Hormozdgan, before the battle was won, before the Arsacid king was dead. The claim came before the victory. Now the victory has come, and the cliff confirms what the coins already said. The merchant understands the change in the metal he carries. He sees it carved, permanent, into the mountain above him.

What the mason carves in stone, Ardashir has already carved in history. Two years earlier, at the Battle of Hormozdgan in 224 CE, the last Arsacid king died. Ardashir was not a great lord overthrowing a great lord. He was a local temple custodian from Istakhr in Fars, from the heartland province where the Achaemenid kings had built Persepolis, where the sacred fire of Adur Farnbag had burned without interruption for longer than anyone could remember. He was the grandson of Sasan, who had been the keeper of that fire. He understood what the fire meant.

## The Fire at Istakhr

In Fars, the fire temple stands on a raised platform visible from the market road. The walls are thick dressed stone — limestone again, the same pale material that accepts a chisel so cleanly. Inside, no statue, no image, no representation of the divine. Only the fire, contained in a stone vessel, fed with specific woods that the priests have carried for generations in exactly prescribed quantities: white sandalwood, which burns clean; and when sandalwood is scarce, the approved aromatic substitutes, ranked in a hierarchy as precise as any administrative roster. The *magi* who tend the fire move through the temple precincts in a sequence that has not varied for centuries: the prayer at dawn, the prayer at noon, the prayers that mark each of the five watches of the Zoroastrian day. They cover their mouths with a cloth when they approach the fire so that their breath — which carries the pollution of the world — does not contaminate the flame that belongs to Ahura Mazda alone.

This is not ceremony. This is maintenance. The fire at Istakhr was assigned to the priestly estate — Adur Farnbag, one of the three great sacred fires of Ērānshahr, the fire of the priests. The other two were Adur Gushnasp at Shiz in Azerbaijan, the warriors' fire, burning above a volcanic crater lake whose water never freezes even in the harshest Caucasian winter — its permanence was geological as well as spiritual. And Adur Burzen-Mihr in Khorasan, the farmers' fire, in the eastern provinces where the Silk Road caravans moved and the soil was worked differently from the plateau's core. Three fires for three estates of Zoroastrian society. The arrangement was not administrative convenience; it was cosmology made institutional. The empire's three sustaining classes — priests, warriors, farmers — each had a divine fire burning for them, endowed by royal revenues, tended by dedicated priests, and connected by the logic of *asha* to the divine order that made the world cohere.

When the news arrived at Istakhr that Artabanus was dead and Ardashir was king of kings, the priests did not stop their prayers. They did not pause the maintenance. The fire burned as it had burned before, but something changed in the prayers' meaning: where they had called on Ahura Mazda's blessing for the Arsacid king who tolerated them, they now called it down for the king who came from among them. Ardashir's grandfather Sasan had tended this fire. His father Papak had held the temple's authority as political office, governing Istakhr through the fire's institutional weight. The priests knew this man. They knew what he owed the fire, and what the fire owed him.

Ardashir was not creating religion. He was completing a circuit that the Arsacids had left open.

The Parthians had held the plateau for nearly five centuries. They had done it without the old religious clarity. The Arsacid court had been eclectic — Greek gods on their coins, Zoroastrian fires in their territories, local practices tolerated where repressing them would cost more than the tolerance. This was practical. It was also, from inside the tradition at Istakhr, a kind of abdication. The sacred fires needed not merely tolerance but active defense. The *druj* was always pressing at the edges of the ordered world. A king who did not actively fight it was not a neutral party. The druj does not wait for kings who will not fight it.

The formalization that Ardashir begins and his successors complete is not a break from tradition; it is the tradition finally given state machinery. A defined hierarchy of priests. A canonical form of the sacred texts. Revenue from royal endowment flowing to specific fire temples in specific amounts. The *magi*'s authority within the state — as judges, as advisors, as certifiers of the king's divine legitimacy — written into the fabric of governance rather than left to personal royal preference. When Ardashir formalizes the clergy, he is building what the Arsacids had left as a potential and never made into an institution.

## Kartir and the Architecture of Heaven

The man who actually builds the Zoroastrian clerical institution is not a king. He is a priest. His name is Kartir, and he is the most important Zoroastrian figure of the third century CE — a man whose inscriptions survive at four sites, including Naqsh-e Rostam itself, carved into the same cliff face as the royal investiture scenes, in the same medium, claiming the same kind of permanence.

Kartir begins his career under Ardashir as an *hrb* — a priest-teacher, a specialist in religious law, one of thousands of magi working in fire temples across the plateau. By the end of his career, under Bahram II, he holds the title *mobadan mobad* — high priest of priests — an office that did not exist at the empire's founding. He created it. Or rather: the institutional logic of what Ardashir started created it, and Kartir was there to inhabit the form as it took shape.

His inscriptions tell his story in the first person: how he organized fire temples across the empire's territories, established their revenues, defined their hierarchy, and suppressed or restricted religious communities that the Zoroastrian tradition regarded as competitors. Jews, Christians, Buddhists, Brahmanicals, Nazareans, Baptists — Kartir's inscription at Ka'ba-ye Zartosht lists the communities whose practices he curbed. The list is remarkable both for its specificity and for what it implies about the religious landscape of third-century Ērānshahr: these communities were present, active, and significant enough to require official attention.

But the picture is more complicated than Kartir's inscriptions alone suggest. Because Shapur I's own inscription at Ka'ba-ye Zartosht — carved in the same stone, in the same trilingual format (Middle Persian, Parthian, Greek, because the empire had audiences in all three worlds) — describes royal endowments not just to Zoroastrian fires but to Zoroastrian, Buddhist, Brahmanical, and Christian institutions alike. The king and the high priest, in the same carved medium, at the same site, are telling different stories about the same empire's religious policy.

The stone preserved both. The structural tension of the founding: the Sassanid empire had two theories about what it was. Shapur's theory was imperial and pragmatic: Ērānshahr contained many peoples, the king of kings was responsible for all of them, and legitimate authority over a diverse empire required a degree of religious accommodation. Kartir's theory was confessional and exclusive: Ērānshahr was the Zoroastrian empire, and its king's divine legitimacy derived from Ahura Mazda's specific endorsement, which required the active defense of *asha* against *druj* in all its forms, including the false teachings of competing religious traditions. Both theories found expression in stone. The stone preserved both.

The man who made the tension visible was named Mani.

Mani arrived in the Sassanid world in 240 CE. He was a prophet, born in the kingdom of Characene near Ctesiphon, raised in a baptismal Jewish-Christian community, who had received, he said, a divine revelation of the complete truth that incorporated and superseded all previous revelations — Zarathustra's, the Buddha's, Jesus's. His theology was elaborate: a cosmic drama of light and darkness, spirit and matter, in which sparks of divine light were imprisoned in the material world and could be liberated through the practice of a specific kind of knowledge and asceticism. He had traveled east to India and back. He was extraordinarily learned. He was also, in Kartir's terms, the druj given human form.

Shapur received him. More than received: Shapur invited him to court, patronized him, permitted him to preach throughout the empire, and may have taken him along on military campaigns. The Manichaean sources preserve this, and there is no strong reason to doubt it — Mani's ecumenical theology, which positioned Zarathustra as a predecessor rather than a rival, had obvious political utility for an empire whose king needed to present himself as universal sovereign. Mani at the Sassanid court was another way of saying: this empire is large enough to contain all truth.

Kartir disagreed. After Shapur's death in 272 CE, Mani was arrested under Bahram I. He died in prison in 274 or 277 CE. The Manichaean tradition called his death a martyrdom; it spread anyway, west into the Roman Empire and east along the Silk Road into Central Asia and China. The empire that tried to stop it had built the roads it traveled.

## The Stone Account of Rome

At some point during the 260s CE, a sculptor at Naqsh-e Rostam received new instructions. The king had done something unprecedented. He had defeated and captured a Roman emperor in the field. Not driven him off, not forced a negotiated peace — captured him. Taken him prisoner. The only Roman emperor, in the entire history of the empire, to be taken alive by a foreign power.

The scene the sculptor carved is larger than Ardashir's investiture relief. Shapur I sits on his horse in the center of the composition. To his left, the Roman emperor Philip the Arab kneels in submission, his arms extended, his posture that of a petitioner before a king. To Shapur's right, the emperor Gordian III lies beneath the horse's hoof — dead at the Battle of Misiche in 244 CE, though the Sassanid and Roman accounts disagree on whether he died in battle or was executed after capture. And kneeling before Shapur's horse, grasping the king's hand, is Valerian. Captured. Alive. The emperor of Rome.

Valerian had invaded in 260 CE to relieve the Roman city of Edessa, under siege by Sassanid forces. The battle went wrong. The army broke. Valerian was taken. He spent the rest of his life in Sassanid captivity, dying in Persian lands; there is a tradition, possibly invented, that Shapur used him as a mounting block when getting on his horse.

Shapur had his victory commemorated at five sites. Naqsh-e Rostam. Bishapur. Darabgird. Salmas. Taq-e Bostan nearby. The multiplication of sites was not vanity; it was the Sassanid method of making history. Truth, in this tradition, was carved rather than written because stone endures where papyrus rots. Shapur was creating the permanent record of what cosmic alignment had produced: Ahura Mazda's champion, defeating Rome's emperor, confirming the divine order of Ērānshahr.

The Ka'ba-ye Zartosht inscription records what the victory meant in material terms: 70,000 Roman prisoners taken in the three Roman wars of Shapur's reign. Entire communities of Roman craftsmen, engineers, soldiers, and their families, relocated to cities Shapur was building across his empire. At Bishapur, in Fars, Roman architects laid out the street plan in Roman style — grid streets, a forum, colonnaded avenues — and built a triumphal arch for the king who had beaten their empire. The mosaic floors at Bishapur use Roman tesserae and Roman techniques; the craftsmen who laid them had learned their trade in Antioch or Caesarea before the war changed where they lived. At the Shapur River, Roman engineers built a bridge using Roman arch construction: the Band-e Qaysar, still standing in its essential form after seventeen centuries.

The engineer who built the bridge stood at the river's bank in a landscape that was not his. The Shapur River runs through a narrow gorge in the Zagros foothills — limestone walls, the sound of water amplified by rock, the scrub oak on the slopes thin and dry in summer. He had learned his craft in a different landscape. But the arch he knew — how to calculate the curve, how to distribute the weight through the keystone, how to build the centering that held the stones until the arch locked itself into place. The arch doesn't need to know whose river it crosses. He built the best bridge he knew how to build. Shapur rode across it. The bridge outlasted them both.

## The Inscription Speaks

Carved into the base of the Cube of Zoroaster at Naqsh-e Rostam, in three languages because the empire has three primary audiences:

*I, the Mazda-worshipping lord Shapur, king of kings of Iran and non-Iran, whose origin is from the gods, son of the Mazda-worshipping lord Ardashir, king of kings of Iran, whose origin is from the gods, grandson of king Papak...*

The formula is precise. The invocation of divine origin is not metaphor. Shapur is not saying he was born of supernatural parents; he is saying that the legitimacy of his kingship derives from the divine order, from Ahura Mazda's endorsement, from the sacred fires that connect the earthly throne to the cosmic truth. Every word of the formula has been tested against the Avestan precedents. The priests approved the language before it was committed to stone.

The inscription continues for 70 lines in Persian, with parallel texts in Parthian and Greek. It lists the provinces of the empire — from Mesopotamia to Khorasan, from the Caucasus to the Persian Gulf. It records the Roman wars and the emperors defeated. And then, in a passage that surprised later scholars who expected only Zoroastrian self-promotion, it records the grants:

*And for the welfare and for the benefit of our soul, we have established at each fire temple which is in Iran, and at each fire temple which is in non-Iran... And the souls of Shapur, King of Kings, and of Ardashir, King of Kings, and of our family and our relatives, shall be thereby benefited and increased.*

And then: grants to Zoroastrian fire temples, yes. But also to Buddhist sanctuaries. To Brahmanical temples. To Christian churches. The list is there in the stone, in Shapur's own words, in a text commissioned by the king and approved by the priests. The empire that is organizing Zoroastrian orthodoxy through Kartir is simultaneously endowing the institutions of other faiths.

What does the text reveal? That the Sassanid empire in its founding generation had a more complex understanding of its own religious project than later accounts — Zoroastrian, Islamic, Western — have tended to suggest. Shapur knew what he was building. He was building a universal empire whose legitimacy derived from Ahura Mazda's endorsement but whose practical exercise of power required the cooperation of communities that worshipped Ahura Mazda's name in different forms, or did not worship him at all. The fire was real. The pragmatism was real. Both appear in the inscription.

Kartir's inscriptions, carved after Shapur's death, tell a different story: the story of correction, of suppression, of the clergy finally given the authority to impose what the king had tolerated. Both stories are true. The stone preserves both. The tension between them is the founding era's deepest legacy: a state whose divine legitimacy required exclusivity and whose imperial reach required diversity, holding both at once, committing both to the permanent record in the same medium at the same site.

## The Three Fires, the One Claim

Shapur dies in 272 CE. Bahram I follows, then Bahram II, then a sequence of short reigns. Kartir accumulates titles. Mani is arrested and dies in captivity. The succession wars of the 270s and 280s are brief and violent. But the fires burn through all of it. They burned through the Arsacid period. They will burn through the Arab conquest. They will burn through the Islamic era. Some of them will burn in this form for centuries more.

The fire at Adur Gushnasp at Shiz — the warriors' fire, above the volcanic lake in Azerbaijan — does not require wood to burn. It feeds on the natural gas seeping from the earth beneath it. This was understood in the Zoroastrian tradition as a sign of particular divine favor: a fire that sustains itself, that does not depend on human hands to keep it lit, that will not go out when the priests are too few or the wood too scarce. The Sassanid kings rode to Shiz after their coronations. The pilgrimage was not metaphorical. The road from Ctesiphon to Shiz was hundreds of kilometers through mountains; the king made the journey on foot for part of it, because approach to the eternal fire required the humility of the body in motion. He arrived at the lake's edge. The fire burned on the water. The smoke rose into the Azerbaijan sky.

This is what Ardashir claimed at Naqsh-e Rostam. Not merely a kingdom. A position in the cosmos. The right to approach the eternal fire and have the approach mean something. The right to receive the ring from Ahura Mazda's hand and have the stone record it truthfully.

The mason who carved Ardashir's investiture scene has been dead for decades by the time Shapur's carvers add the Roman victory relief to the same cliff. The cliff does not notice. The limestone accepts the chisel of each generation with equal patience. Ardashir receiving the ring. Shapur's horse above Valerian's kneeling form. Kartir's inscription recording the clergy's rise. Three stories in the same stone, in the same pale limestone, in the same afternoon light that falls across the plain of Fars each day and then holds for an hour past sunset.

The fires of Ērānshahr burn. The stone at Naqsh-e Rostam holds.

They carved it where it could be read from the road. They made it large enough to be legible from horseback. They made it permanent.

The cliff face holds the light for another hour after sunset.
