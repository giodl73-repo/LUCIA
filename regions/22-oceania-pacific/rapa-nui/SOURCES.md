# Sources — Rapa Nui

## Key Academic Works
- Jo Anne Van Tilburg, *Easter Island: Archaeology, Ecology and Culture* (1994, Smithsonian) — the standard comprehensive treatment
- Terry Hunt & Carl Lipo, *The Statues That Walked: Unraveling the Mystery of Easter Island* (2011, Free Press) — the "walking moai" hypothesis; the revisionist account of the collapse
- Jared Diamond, *Collapse: How Societies Choose to Fail or Succeed* (2005) — the "ecocide" thesis; influential but contested
- Thor Heyerdahl, *Aku-Aku: The Secret of Easter Island* (1958) — the popular account; inaccurate in key respects but historically important
- Alfred Métraux, *Ethnology of Easter Island* (1940, Bishop Museum) — the foundational ethnographic record
- Georgia Lee — rongorongo and rock art studies

## Primary Sources
- Oral traditions of Rapa Nui (collected by Métraux and others in the 1930s; some still held by families)
- Rongorongo tablets (surviving examples held by Bishop Museum, Vatican Museum, and others)
- Jacob Roggeveen's 1722 journal — the first European account

## Archaeological Sources
- Rano Raraku quarry surveys — Van Tilburg moai database
- Ahu Tongariki excavations (restoration project, 1990s)
- Orongo ceremonial village excavations
- Anakena beach excavations — earliest settlement levels

## Needed (ACQUISITION)
- Rapa Nui community oral history archive (Mata Tu'u Hotu Iti cultural center)
- Chilean state archive records of the annexation and wool company period
- Peruvian government records of the 1862–63 slave raids
- Rongorongo decipherment research — current state of scholarship
