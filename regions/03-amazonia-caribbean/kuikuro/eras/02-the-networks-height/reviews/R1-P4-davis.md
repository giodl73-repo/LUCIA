# Panel Review — R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## The Network's Height | Kuikuro Era 2

---

The Amazon has been one of the most systematically misunderstood landscapes on Earth. For most of the period I have spent working in this region, the dominant assumption among those who observed it from the outside was that the forest was pristine, the population sparse, and the peoples who lived there evidence of some earlier, simpler stage of human organization. This opening understands that this assumption was not neutral observation — it was a claim, and a false one.

What the opening does right: it shows the managed landscape not as a remarkable achievement that requires explaining, but as the ordinary infrastructure of a working society. The road needs clearing because roads need clearing. The fish weirs in the forest, the terra preta soils — these are not exotic achievements. They are the ordinary working of a landscape that has been in continuous human management for centuries. This is the correct anthropological stance: sufficiency, not wonder at the unexpected.

The knowledge system embedded in the road-clearing scene is operating at method level, as the rubric requires. The work is specific: hacking vine stems, rolling the branch, cutting back new growth to re-expose the straight line. The straightness of the road in a forest that does not want to be straight — this is engineering as knowledge: a decision about direction maintained against the forest's own logic through the repeated exercise of human intention. This is not "they were good at building things." This is a specific knowledge practice shown in motion.

**The question I would push on:**

The chapter needs to go deeper into the terra preta. This is one of the great knowledge system achievements of the pre-contact Americas, and it is invisible to anyone who does not know to look for it. The upper Xingu peoples built productive agricultural soil over centuries through the deliberate mixing of charcoal, bone, and organic waste — a practice that created soils still more fertile than the surrounding forest floor today. This is not "environmental management" in the way we use the term. It is a relationship with the landscape based on knowledge of what the landscape can become if you attend to it.

The chapter should show this knowledge operating — not explained, but demonstrated. What does a Kuikuro person working a terra preta manioc garden know about the soil? How does that knowledge manifest in practice? The opening gives us the road. The chapter should give us the garden. Together, they are the two infrastructures of the network — the road connecting communities, the garden feeding them.

— Davis
