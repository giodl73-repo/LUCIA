# R1 — Panel Review: Ryszard Kapuściński
## Durrani / Era 1: The Founding

**Reviewer:** P-5 Ryszard Kapuściński
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (Opening review)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Body in a Place

I covered Iran. I was in Kandahar once, briefly, from the Pakistani side, in a car that smelled of diesel and dried rosewater. The author of this opening has been to Kandahar in their imagination, and the journey was not entirely wasted.

Where I feel most present: the opening image. The green wheat that someone walked out to cut, the moisture still in the stalks, the assembly returning with something that was not yet a crown but could become one. I felt the stalks. I believed the decision. That is good writing.

Where I lost the place: in the middle, during the Pashtunwali passage. The author knows what the code requires — hospitality, asylum, blood-debt, honor — but the code is delivered in something close to a list. I cannot feel a list. I need to be in the tent when melmastia is honored. I need to smell the lamb being prepared for a guest who arrived with enemies at his heels, who invoked nanawatai and had to be protected regardless of what it would cost. The code needs a body wearing it.

## What the Opening Grounds Well

The autumn air of Kandahar. The dust of the high desert. The mulberry trees and the silk trade they carry. The weight of the nine-day assembly. These are present and physical and right.

I particularly value the sound of Pashto in the circle. Not just "debate" — Pashto debate. Language is sensory: its sounds, its rhythms, the way it hits differently in a mountain assembly than in a bazaar. The author correctly names it; the chapter should render it.

## What Needs Grounding in the Full Chapter

The campaigns. Every chapter on a military empire must put the reader in the army, on the road, at the moment before battle. I do not care about battle statistics. I care about what the road to the Punjab smells like in winter, what an Afghan army camp sounds like at dawn, the weight of a Pashtun tribesman's equipment as he walks through the Khyber Pass. I need to feel the empire being built, not just understand it was built.

The Persian court culture. If there is a garden, I want to be in it. If there is a fountain, I want to hear water. If Ahmad Shah sat in his chancery while his Persian secretary wrote his diplomatic correspondence, I want to know whether the ink smelled of iron or vegetable, whether the lamp burned oil or tallow, whether the document was read aloud before it was sent. The Persian court is sensory before it is administrative.

## The Question I'd Push On

**When Ahmad Shah rides east toward the Punjab — which the chapter must describe — does the chapter put me on the road with him?** Not as historical narration ("the army crossed the Indus in the spring of 1748") but as physical experience: the crossing itself, the sound of the river, the horses' reluctance, the opposite bank gradually becoming visible. If the chapter cannot put me on that road, it has made the empire an idea rather than a thing that happened to real bodies in real terrain.

## Minor Note

Em-dash count in the opening: zero. This is correct. The prose earns its pauses through sentence structure rather than punctuation interruption. The voice has discipline. Maintain it.
