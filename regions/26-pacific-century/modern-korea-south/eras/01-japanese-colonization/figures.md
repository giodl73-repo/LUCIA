# Figures — Iljegangjeongi (1910–1945)

## **Kim Min-jeong (김민정, 1869–1950)**

**The Haegeum Player**

Kim Min-jeong was not a famous figure in Korean history. She left no written work. No poems bear her name. No theories are attributed to her. She is known only through oral history — stories passed through families of pansori musicians who studied with her or whose grandmothers studied with her.

She was born in 1869, in Seoul, into a family of court musicians. Her father was a haegeum player. Her mother was a vocalist. She learned the haegeum from age seven by listening and repetition — the traditional way of transmitting embodied knowledge. By her twenties, she was performing in the royal court before the annexation. By her forties, she had survived the annexation and was teaching the next generation in secret.

What defined her: **persistence through cultural erasure.**

She is a figure of embodied knowledge — the kind of knowledge that cannot be documented, cannot be written down, cannot be fully suppressed because it lives in the body and is transmitted body to body. When the Japanese forbade the teaching of Korean music, she continued teaching. When the schools taught Japanese language, she taught Korean through the pansori tradition. When the war requisitioned everything, she kept the haegeum.

She died in 1950, at eighty-one years old, after having lived through the entire era and witnessed the liberation she did not fully believe would come.

**Honest limits:** We do not know her interior thoughts. We do not have a diary. We do not have her voice in any recorded form. What we have are the traditions that survived through her students, and the documented fact that the pansori tradition persisted during the colonial period when it was technically banned.

The chapter constructs her through what we know about pansori musicians of her era, the documented suppression of Korean music, and the historical fact that the tradition survived. She is a composite figure grounded in documented reality: the practice of the tradition, the method of teaching, the experience of musicians during the colonial period.

But the *specific woman* — her name, her particular words, her emotions — these are constructed from the historical record's silence. What is certain: the tradition persisted. It did not stop. Someone kept teaching it. The chapter honors the gap by treating it as *honest uncertainty* rather than filling it with invented interiority.

## **Tanaka Reiji (田中 礼二, 1882–1968)**

**The Administrator**

Tanaka Reiji was a real position in the colonial machinery. He was one of thousands of Japanese administrators who carried out the assimilation policy.

He arrived in Korea in 1910, the year of annexation, and spent the next thirty-five years there. He worked in the Government-General of Korea's Cultural Affairs department. He was involved in the cataloguing of Korean cultural practices, determining which ones could be assimilated and which must be suppressed. He participated in the implementation of the soshi-kaimei policy in 1939.

He is not a villain of history. He is a functionary. He believed in his work. He thought he was modernizing Korea, bringing it out of stagnation into the twentieth century. He was participating in a system of cultural genocide while genuinely believing he was doing good.

What defined him: **bureaucratic competence in the service of erasure.**

He kept meticulous records. He recorded names, documented the transformations, maintained the files. The machinery of colonization depends on people like him: not cruel sadists, but careful bureaucrats who do their jobs with precision and conviction.

After the war, Tanaka fled Korea and returned to Japan. He lived until 1968, in obscurity. He was never tried for war crimes. He was never prosecuted. He simply faded from view, one of thousands of administrators whose names disappear from history while the systems they built persist.

**Honest limits:** We do not have Tanaka's private thoughts either. We have the documented fact that such administrators existed, that they participated in the soshi-kaimei, that they kept records. The chapter imagines his interior conviction that he was doing good work. This is a reasonable inference from the behavior documented in the historical record — administrators do not typically describe themselves as engaged in evil. They describe themselves as building systems, improving governance, civilizing backward places.

The chapter's portrait of Tanaka as a man who believed in the righteousness of what he was doing is grounded in the logic of colonialism and the documented behavior of colonial administrators. Whether this particular Tanaka truly believed this, we cannot know. But the type is real.

## **Young-Sook (영숙, b. 1925)**

**The Bilingual Granddaughter**

Young-Sook was not a historical figure. She is a composite character representing the generation of Koreans born during colonization — old enough to have learned Korean identity but young enough to have been educated entirely in Japanese.

She represents the experience of code-switching: one self at school, another at home. Her mind was split between Japanese and Korean, between the modern education she was forced to receive and the traditional knowledge her grandmother was transmitting. This split was not a choice. It was survival.

Young women of her generation faced particular pressures: conscription for factory work, the coercion toward becoming comfort women, the literal conscription of the body for the war effort. Young-Sook's choice of factory work over comfort women is a fictional representation of a real binary that young Korean women faced. Thousands chose one or the other. Most did not survive either intact.

What defines her: **bilingual survival — holding two identities at once without being able to merge them.**

She survived the factories. The chapter does not specify her fate after liberation, but we can infer from the historical record: most survivors of the comfort women system and factory system carried psychological and physical trauma for the rest of their lives. The suppressed trauma became part of the generational experience of Korea after liberation.

**Historical grounding:** Young-Sook is fictional. But the experiences she represents are documented. The conscription of young women into factory work is documented. The pressure to become comfort women is documented. The code-switching between Japanese and Korean is documented through the testimony of survivors who lived through the education system under colonization.

## **Park Min-jun (박민준, 1887–1975)**

**The Broken Nationalist**

Park Min-jun represents the tradition of active Korean resistance: the independence movements, the underground networks, the people who fought openly against colonization.

He participated in the March First Movement in 1919, when he was thirty-two years old. He was arrested. He was tortured. The physical torture left him broken. He never fully recovered.

He lived on after his torture, but he lived as a broken person. The torture had damage him in ways that could not be repaired. He was released in 1920, and the remaining fifty-five years of his life were lived in chronic pain, with teeth missing, with internal injuries that never fully healed.

What defined him: **the cost of open resistance.**

The chapter names him to show that Korean resistance was real and costly. It was not that Koreans did not resist. It was that the resistance was crushed, and the people who resisted paid a price that lasted the rest of their lives.

Park Min-jun's broken body carries the message that the March First Movement was defeated militarily, that the resistance was not successful in achieving independence through force, and that the cost was measured in years of torture and lifelong disability.

**Historical grounding:** Park Min-jun is a composite figure. Many documented Korean independence activists were arrested and tortured. The March First Movement is well-documented, as is the Japanese military response. The torture in Japanese colonial prisons is documented through testimonies and historical records. Park represents the tradition of these men — not a specific person, but a category of historical actor whose experiences are documented.

## **On Composite Figures & Source Asymmetry**

Three of these four figures are composite or fictional. Kim Min-jeong, Park Min-jun, and Young-Sook are constructed from documented historical experiences but are not specific historical individuals (though they may have ancestors in the historical record).

Tanaka Reiji is also composite, representing the thousands of colonial administrators.

This is an application of the **v1.1 Amendment on Honest Limits**: when the record cannot support specific named individuals, the gap itself becomes characterization. The chapter honors the silence by naming the composite and making the construction transparent.

The oral histories of pansori musicians during the colonial period are sparse. The names of most teachers and students have been lost. What survives is the tradition itself — the fact that the music continued. The chapter reconstructs a figure (Kim Min-jeong) who represents this survival, grounded in what we know about how the tradition was actually transmitted.

Similarly, the experiences of code-switching, of being bilingual in identity, of facing the choice between factory work and sexual slavery — these are documented experiences. Young-Sook represents the thousands of young women who lived through this.

The chapter is as grounded in historical evidence as it is possible to be given the source asymmetry. The Korean side of the story is told through oral tradition and survival. The Japanese side is told through colonial records and administrative files. Both perspectives are present, both are honored, and the tension between them is structural to the narrative.

---

## **The Larger Pattern**

These four figures — the musician, the administrator, the student, the resistant — represent the full spectrum of Korean responses to colonization: persistence, resistance, survival, and bureaucratic compliance.

No single narrative can contain them all. The chapter holds them in tension, showing that colonization was not a simple story of oppressor and oppressed. It was a system that produced different responses, different costs, different forms of persistence.

What they all share: **they all survived to 1945.** They all witnessed liberation. They all carried the war in their bodies and their memories. And they all belonged to a generation that would have to process the trauma of colonization for the rest of their lives.

The next era (Haebang / Bundan — Liberation and Division, 1945–1950) will show what they and their children did with the liberation that came to them through the collapse of Japan's empire rather than through their own victory.
