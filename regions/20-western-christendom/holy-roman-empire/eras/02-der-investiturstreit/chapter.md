# Der Investiturstreit — The Investiture Controversy
## Holy Roman Empire | Era 2 | 1024–1122 CE

---

## I. The Double-Handed Kingdom

In Milan in the winter of 1066, a deacon named Ariald stood at the back of a cathedral and watched the ceremony he had been preaching against for five years.

The bishop-elect was kneeling before the archbishop. He received, first, the ring — a gold band that carried the same significance a bridegroom's ring carries, because a bishop was wedded to his diocese as a husband to a wife. He received, second, the staff — the *baculus*, the shepherd's crook, the sign of pastoral authority over the flock that now became his. The archbishop spoke the words of investiture. The new bishop kissed the book of the Gospels. The canons of the cathedral chapter gave their assent. The ceremony was complete.

Ariald watched this and saw simony. Named for Simon Magus, who tried to purchase the Holy Spirit from the Apostles for money, simony was the sin of trafficking in sacred offices. The bishop kneeling before the archbishop had not, in fact, purchased his position — but his predecessor had been appointed by the emperor, and before that, by the previous emperor, in a chain of imperial appointment stretching back decades. The emperor chose who became bishop. The emperor chose because the bishop held, along with his spiritual authority, the governance of a territory the size of a German county — its courts, its revenues, its military obligations, its hundreds of monasteries, its cathedral schools that trained every clerk who would administer the empire for the next generation. The emperor chose because he could not afford not to choose. And Ariald, who preached in the streets of Milan and had been beaten by cathedral clergy for his trouble, believed that this chain — sacred office transmitted through secular hands — was the ruin of the Church.

He was not alone. Across the Latin West, the reform movement that had begun in the Burgundian monastery of Cluny was spreading: the idea that the Church must be free from lay control, that the quality of prayer offered to God depended on the integrity of those offering it, that a simoniac bishop polluted the sacraments administered in his name. The Cluniac monks at the great abbey in Burgundy had built their institution on a single charter from Duke William of Aquitaine in 910, which gave Cluny's abbot the right to choose his own successor, subject to no lord but the pope. In the century that followed, Cluny had grown into a network of three hundred affiliated monasteries, all organized on the same principle: free from the emperor, free from the local lord, answerable only to Rome. The network was the argument made institutional: the Church could govern itself, if the secular world would let it.

It would not. Or rather: it never had, and now it was asked to stop.

The emperor in the year of Ariald's death — 1066 — was Heinrich IV, then sixteen years old, the son of the Emperor Heinrich III, who had himself done more than any secular ruler to advance the reform program before the logic of reform turned against his son's authority. Heinrich III had deposed three corrupt popes and installed four reformers in succession. He had supported Hildebrand, who had worked in the curia under all four reformers, who knew every lever and argument of the reform movement. He had not foreseen that the movement would eventually claim that a reforming emperor was still an emperor, and an emperor appointing bishops was still simony, and simony was still the ruin of the Church, and the ruin of the Church was still Heinrich's fault.

In 1073, Hildebrand was elected pope and took the name Gregory VII.

## II. The Argument Made Doctrine

The *Dictatus Papae* was entered in the papal register in 1075. It was a list of twenty-seven propositions, terse and absolute, written in the Latin of a man who had spent thirty years in the curia and knew exactly what he was claiming. It was not addressed to anyone. It was not a letter or a decree. It was a note to file — a statement of what Gregory believed to be true about the papacy's authority, placed in the record where it could be found.

The propositions said: the pope alone can convoke a general council. The pope alone can appoint, transfer, or depose bishops. The pope can depose emperors. The pope's legates preside over all bishops in council, even those of inferior rank. No general synod can be called without the pope's authority. A letter sent by the pope may be cited by no one. No one may condemn a man who appeals to the Apostolic See. The Roman Church has never erred and will never err.

And: the pope may release subjects from their allegiance to an unjust lord.

This last proposition was not abstract. It described a specific power over a specific political relationship — the bond of loyalty between a king and his subjects, which was the foundation of medieval governance. If the pope could release subjects from their allegiance to their king, the pope could effectively depose any king in Christendom by making him ungovernable. This was the proposition that crossed the Alps faster than any other. When the German princes read it — and they did, because the reform party made sure they did — they understood what it meant for them. It meant that their own loyalty to Heinrich was conditional on Gregory's approval of Heinrich's soul.

Heinrich was not governing well. He had been fighting the Saxon nobility for two years, suppressing a revolt that his own rough governance had provoked, and the military campaign had left him dependent on ministeriales — unfree men who performed military service in exchange for land — rather than the princes who were his formal peers. The princes were unhappy. The Church reformers were unhappy. And now the pope had published a document saying that the pope could release subjects from their oaths.

In January 1076, Heinrich convened a council at Worms. The German bishops — most of whom owed their appointments to the imperial court — signed a letter addressed to Gregory, no longer greeting him as pope. The letter called him Hildebrand, his birth name, stripping the papal title. It called him a "false monk," a disturber of the peace of the Church, a man who had seized the pontificate through the violence of faction rather than the consent of the clergy. It announced, on behalf of the German bishops, that Gregory was no longer pope and should descend from the throne he had usurped.

The letter was delivered to Rome, where it was read aloud at the Lenten synod. Gregory's response was immediate and total. He excommunicated Heinrich — using the full formula of anathema, the formal exclusion from the community of the faithful — and declared him deposed as king. He released Heinrich's subjects from their oaths.

The German princes had been waiting for exactly this. At Tribur in October 1076, they gathered without Heinrich and issued an ultimatum: if the king had not received absolution from the pope by the anniversary of his excommunication — February 2, 1077 — they would elect a new king. A year. Which was now three months away.

## III. The Body on the Mountain

The pass was the Mont Cenis, or perhaps the Great Saint Bernard; the chronicles are not precise. What they record is that Heinrich crossed the Alps in January, in winter, in conditions that the Countess Matilda of Tuscany later described to Gregory as "barely possible." He traveled with a small escort, because he had no other option: the German lords who might have provided hospitality and military support were his enemies or his former friends watching carefully to see what happened next. He brought his wife Bertha and their infant son Conrad. He brought, somewhere in the baggage, the garments a penitent wears.

The Alpine winter in the passes above two thousand meters carries a quality of cold that is different from the cold of the valleys. The air thins as the road climbs, and the thinning air does something to sound — voices carry differently, footfalls in the snow sound close and muffled simultaneously. The road would have been maintained by the monks of the pass hospices, who kept a path clear for pilgrims and merchants, but "maintained" in January means: passable by a man who is willing to suffer. Heinrich was willing.

He descended into Italy and sent word ahead to Matilda of Tuscany, who had been Gregory's most steadfast ally in northern Italy, who had given the reform movement money and military support for a decade, and who now held the castle at Canossa. The castle stood on a ridge in the Apennine foothills above the Po plain, the January wind cutting across the open ground before the walls. Heinrich arrived on January 25.

He presented himself as a penitent. He wore a hair shirt — a garment made from coarse animal hair, worn against the skin, causing the constant low-grade irritation that was the penitent's reminder that the body's ease is not the soul's purpose. He removed his shoes. He stood in the outer courtyard on stone that was frozen, in cold that the Apennines, even in the foothills, administered with no mercy, and he waited.

He waited through the hours that the bells marked: Terce, at the third hour after dawn. Sext, at midday. None, in the afternoon — the hour that gives us our word "noon," because in the medieval calculation, "none" had migrated from the ninth canonical hour to what we now call midday, testimony to how thoroughly the Church's clock had shaped ordinary time. Vespers as the light failed. Compline at dusk. The bells of Canossa's chapel rang each hour, audible in the outer courtyard. He heard the offices he was excluded from — the prayers rising inside the chapel walls, the plainchant that marked each hour as sacred time, the community at worship from which he was formally cut off. He stood outside it and listened.

Inside, Gregory deliberated. Hugh of Cluny — the abbot who had been Heinrich's godfather at baptism and was Gregory's most trusted counselor and who now found himself, impossibly, asked to mediate between both of his principals — interceded. The canon law was plain: contrition required mercy. A penitent who presented himself before the Church and asked forgiveness could not be refused without the Church refusing its own doctrine. To refuse was to say that the Church's power to forgive was not real, which was a theological position Gregory could not maintain. To receive was to restore Heinrich, which was a political disaster Gregory could see clearly.

He deliberated for three days. On the third day, the gates of the inner courtyard opened.

Gregory received Heinrich in the castle chapel. He gave him the Eucharist with his own hands — the body and blood of Christ, administered by the vicar of Christ to the man who had, five days before, been excluded from the body of the faithful. This was not a gesture. This was the most powerful act of inclusion available within the world these men inhabited: the pope's own hands, consecrated at ordination, passing the consecrated host to the excommunicate king, restoring him to communion. The ban was lifted.

Heinrich ate, dressed, and began planning the journey north.

Gregory, having acted — in his own words, written later — "with a heavy heart," wrote to the German princes to explain what he had done and why. He said he had acted on principle, not on politics. The princes read the letter with some skepticism. They had spent the winter preparing to elect a new king, and now they had no grounds for doing so. They elected one anyway — Rudolf of Rheinfelden, Duke of Swabia, as anti-king — and the conflict continued, a civil war now layered onto a constitutional crisis.

Heinrich returned to Germany and defeated Rudolf at the Unstrut river in 1080. Gregory excommunicated him again. Heinrich responded by convening a council that declared Gregory deposed and elected the Archbishop of Ravenna as Antipope Clement III. He then led an army to Rome, besieged it for three years, and in 1084 entered the city and had Clement crown him emperor. Gregory fled to Salerno, where he died in 1085. His last reported words, spoken to the cardinals gathered around him, were these:

"I have loved righteousness and hated iniquity; therefore I die in exile."

## IV. The Bitter Settlement

For forty-five years after Canossa, the argument continued. It was not the same argument — it ramified, mutated, was settled in one place and reopened in another. The German civil war produced new kings and anti-kings. The papal schism produced new popes and antipopes. In the Italian cities, the debate was refracted through local politics: the bishops who supported the emperor were sometimes physically expelled from their cathedrals by reform partisans; the bishops who supported the pope were sometimes expelled by imperial factions. The ordinary faithful — the farmer on an episcopal estate, the merchant in a city where the bishop held market rights, the parish priest who received his church from the local lord and could not always know which lord the bishop to whom he owed obedience was loyal to — the ordinary faithful navigated a world where the two authorities that organized their existence were simultaneously claiming each other's illegitimacy.

And during all of this, the German princes accumulated.

The mechanism was straightforward, though no one designed it. A divided emperor and a divided papacy each needed the princes' military and political support. Neither could afford to discipline princes who demanded concessions as the price of support. The prince who backed Heinrich in 1076 could exact a price. The prince who backed Rudolf in 1077 could exact a different price from the other side. The prince who switched in 1080 could exact a price from whichever side he was now supporting. What the princes extracted, across forty-five years of the controversy, was not cash (though they extracted cash too) but autonomy: hereditary rights over territories that had previously been held by imperial appointment, jurisdictions that had previously required imperial confirmation, courts that had previously operated under imperial supervision. The investiture argument was about who appointed bishops; the practical consequence was that the princes' own positions became more independent of both the emperor and the pope.

In 1122, at the city of Worms on the Rhine, the two sides negotiated a settlement — signed by Pope Calixtus II and Heinrich V, the son who had deposed his own father to reach a deal. The document was actually two documents: the papal *Calixtinum* and the imperial *Heinricianum*, each side issuing its own version, each version emphasizing what its author believed it had preserved. The settlement divided the investiture ceremony into two distinct acts. The emperor would invest bishops with the scepter — the symbol of secular authority over the bishop's territory — but not with the ring and staff, which had been the symbols of spiritual authority. The ring and staff would be given by the Church, in a separate ceremony, before the secular investiture.

Which ceremony came first mattered enormously, because whichever came first established the logical priority of the authority it represented. The papal text implied that the Church's investiture came first and the secular investiture followed — the bishop was constituted as bishop before he received his temporal lands. The imperial text implied that the temporal investiture was a separate transaction, neither prior nor posterior in meaning — the bishop was a bishop and a territorial lord, and the two were simply different roles addressed by different ceremonies. The two texts, read together, were a precise vocabulary for a disagreement that remained unresolved.

Consider Benno of Osnabrück, who had been bishop of that northern German see since 1068 and who had spent the entire controversy trying to remain a bishop rather than a partisan. His diocese contained churches, monasteries, courts, estates. His authority derived from two sources: his ordination by an archbishop (spiritual authority), and his appointment by the emperor (temporal authority). The controversy had made the relationship between these two sources of authority unclear, litigated, contested. When the Concordat of Worms was signed, Benno was still alive, still bishop, still navigating. The settlement told him that his two ceremonies were now formally distinct. It did not tell him what to do when the emperor and the pope disagreed about whether his appointment had been valid in the years before the settlement. It did not tell him which of his oaths took precedence if the two masters came into conflict again. It told him there were two ceremonies.

He died in 1088, still bishop. His successor received two ceremonies. The question of which mattered more was not answered by the ceremonies but by the political conditions of each succession — by who, at the moment of appointment, had more leverage: the emperor who controlled the scepter or the pope who controlled the ring and staff. The controversy had been concluded; the argument had been constitutionalized.

## V. What the Controversy Made

The world that came out of the Concordat of Worms was not the world that went in. The change was not what either Gregory or Heinrich had intended; it was what their conflict produced.

The German princes emerged as the decisive institutional power in the empire. The investiture controversy had given them four decades of structural leverage against both the emperor and the pope, and they had used it. The principalities — Bavaria, Saxony, Franconia, Swabia — had accumulated jurisdictions, rights, and revenues that would not be returned. The empire after 1122 was still formally one entity, still governed by a king who claimed the imperial title and the princes' formal allegiance. But the king who emerged from the controversy was a king among peers, not a king above subjects. The Hohenstaufen emperors who came to power in 1138 were brilliant, ambitious, and constitutionally weaker than the Salians had been before 1075. They would spend the next century fighting Italy, fighting the papacy, fighting the princes — because the investiture controversy had permanently redistributed the resources that made the Ottonian settlement work.

The Church emerged with the theological argument won. Gregory had died in exile, but his claims had been vindicated: the pope, not the emperor, invested bishops with spiritual authority. The reform movement had restructured the Church's relationship to the secular world — cathedral chapters chose their bishops through election, the Cluniac monasteries maintained their charters of freedom, the canon law schools that would grow into the universities of Bologna and Paris operated under ecclesiastical rather than imperial protection. The institutional Church that emerged from the controversy was more legally sophisticated, more bureaucratically capable, more financially independent, and more politically powerful than the Church that had gone in. It was also more deeply entangled in the political world it had tried to separate itself from. The price of winning the argument about freedom from secular control was the need to exercise political power to defend that freedom — and political power, exercised long enough, produces all the corruptions the reform movement had set out to cure.

The empire's relationship to Italy — already established in Era 1 as the territory where the imperial title was confirmed, where Rome waited with the crown — became permanently complicated. The papacy now held the Papal States as a temporal lord, defended by the most sophisticated legal apparatus in Europe. Every emperor who crossed the Alps would have to negotiate with, fight, or outmaneuver the pope as a political entity as well as a spiritual one. Frederick Barbarossa, who came to power sixteen years after Worms, was not fighting the investiture controversy — he was fighting the world the investiture controversy had made.

And somewhere in the settlement — in the precise vocabulary of two ceremonies, two oaths, two authorities operating on a single person who wore both a bishop's ring and a secular lord's sword — was the beginning of something that neither Gregory nor Heinrich would have recognized as their achievement: the separation of the spiritual and the temporal into distinct, parallel, and finally competing jurisdictions. The long argument about who owned the bishops would eventually become the argument about whether the secular state owed the Church any authority at all. That argument would take five more centuries, and would require transformations neither Gregory nor Heinrich could have predicted. It is not finished.

Gregory VII died in Salerno in 1085, having outlasted two of the antipopes his enemy had installed, having seen his own supporters split and waver and eventually regroup around his successors, having never seen a German soldier leave Italy. He had not been defeated; he had been out-survived in a particular moment that did not outlast him. His program — a Church free from lay control, appointments made by the Church alone, the pope supreme in spiritual matters — was vindicated by the Concordat of Worms thirty-seven years after his death.

He did not know this. His final letters from Salerno, written in the last months of his life, continued to issue directives, organize allies, insist on the argument he had been making for a decade. He did not believe the argument was lost. He believed this particular moment had gone against him, which was a different thing. He died not in despair but in the posture of a man who expected his successors to continue, and he was right. He had loved righteousness and hated iniquity, and these commitments had brought him to a room in Salerno, far from Rome, with the cardinals around him and the winter outside.

He had. It was enough.
