## Assessment of the Notes

The notes correctly identify the three sensory gaps from PANEL-1: the writing room, the dock at Mpinda, the body on the plateau. The section plan assigns these to specific sections, which is correct. My concern is that the "diplomatic register" note — while accurate — might make the writer hesitant to put full sensory weight on the chapter. Let me be clear: the diplomatic register does not require sensory restraint. It requires sensory precision. The formal letter writer is not unaware of their body. They are precisely aware of it.

## Specific Sensory Plan

**Section 1 (opening integrated):** The writing room. The light through the cathedral's windows — what kind of light? The Kongo plateau at dawn in the dry season has a particular quality: red laterite dust in the air, the sun coming flat and golden across the savanna. The secretary's hand on the quill — left hand steadying the parchment, right hand forming the Latin characters. The smell of the room: stone and incense and something organic, the palm oil in the lamps.

**Section 2 (covenant's architecture):** The nkisi nkondi in its corner of the room. Not described as an object to be explained — experienced as a presence. Its iron nails catching the light. The weight of the covenant it embodies. The cosmogram scratched in the threshold. The feel of the kalunga line as lived geography — the Kongo river below is the border between the worlds; the plateau above is the land of the living; the crossing from one to the other is managed, not chaotic.

**Section 3 (school and ship):** The ships in the harbor at Mpinda. Not the slave ships yet — the diplomatic ships, the ships that take Henrique to Lisbon. What does it smell like to watch your son's ship disappear into the Atlantic? The salt air. The sound of the Atlantic breaking on the coast below the plateau.

**Section 4 (governor's calculation):** The moment of exchange. The captive. The goods. This section must be physically present without being a spectacle. One specific exchange: a captive man, a bolt of Portuguese cloth, the transaction that takes less time than the walk between two villages. The chapter should not dwell on this but it must make it physical.

**Section 5 (the reply):** The inadequate reply arrives. What is the physical experience of receiving it? A letter carried by hand from the coast. The secretary reading it aloud. The quality of the silence in the room afterward.

**Section 6 (the archive):** The letter in Lisbon. What does the archive smell like — old parchment, the particular damp of a Portuguese winter. The chapter can end on this physical fact: the letter is there. The argument survived.

## What I'm Satisfied On

The notes have correctly identified the sensory gaps and the sections where they will be addressed. The voice note ("the Kongo register is the prose equivalent of a formal theological disputation") is correct and will prevent over-immersion. The chapter knows what it needs to do.
