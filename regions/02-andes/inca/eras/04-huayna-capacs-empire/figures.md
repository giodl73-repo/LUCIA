# Figures — Huayna Cápac's Empire (Era 4)

## Huayna Cápac, r. c. 1493–c. 1527 CE

Eleventh Sapa Inca. Son of Topa Inca. Last ruler of a unified Tawantinsuyu. Conducted prolonged campaigns in Ecuador, built Tomebamba as a second capital, died of epidemic disease (probably smallpox) before the succession was resolved.

**What we know:** Huayna Capac is well attested across the colonial chronicles. His northern campaigns, the construction of Tomebamba, and his death from epidemic disease are consistent across sources. The succession crisis that followed his death is described in all major chronicles, though the details of his final wishes regarding the succession vary.

**What we cannot know:** His reasoning for the prolonged stay in the north. The chronicles suggest personal preference, military necessity, and political calculation, but his interiority is not accessible. The chapter renders him through his decisions and their structural consequences.

**Honest limit:** The epidemic that killed Huayna Capac is the hardest event to pin down chronologically. Some scholars place it as early as 1524 (Noble David Cook), others closer to 1528. The disease itself is debated: smallpox is the leading candidate, but measles or a combination of pathogens cannot be ruled out. The chapter uses "probably smallpox" and notes the uncertainty.

**Sources:** Cieza de Leon (1553); Betanzos (1557); Sarmiento (1572); Cook, *Born to Die* (1998); D'Altroy (2002).

## Huascar

Son of Huayna Capac. Claimed the succession from Qusqu. Supported by the traditional panaqas and the ayllus of the heartland. Would fight and lose the civil war against Atahualpa (Era 5).

**What we know:** Huascar is attested as the Qusqu-based claimant. His mother's lineage gave him the traditional legitimacy. His conflict with Atahualpa is the central event of Era 5.

**Sources:** Cieza de Leon (1553); Betanzos (1557); Julien (2000).

## Atahualpa

Son of Huayna Capac. Born in the north. Commanded the loyalty of the northern army. Would fight and win the civil war against Huascar, only to be captured by Pizarro at Cajamarca (Era 5).

**What we know:** Atahualpa's birthplace, exact parentage, and the degree of Huayna Capac's preference for him are all debated in the chronicles. What is consistent: he had the northern army's loyalty and his father's battlefield-tested generals.

**Sources:** Betanzos (1557); Cieza de Leon (1553); Hemming, *The Conquest of the Incas* (1970).
