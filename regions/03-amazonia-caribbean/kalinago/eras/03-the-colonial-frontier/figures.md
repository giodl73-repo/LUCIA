# Figures: The Colonial Frontier
## Kalinago, Era 3: c. 1492–1650 CE

---

## A Note on This Era's Figures

The Kalinago of this era exist in the historical record only as seen by Europeans — and the Europeans of this period were not preserving Kalinago names or Kalinago perspectives. Columbus's journals, Peter Martyr's *Decades*, the French colonial records of the early 17th century — all record the Kalinago primarily as a military and administrative problem. Individual names appear occasionally (a chief's name transliterated in a way that no longer maps to any Kalinago linguistic form we can recognize) but cannot be matched to specific people with recoverable interior lives.

The figures below are constructed from the intersection of what the historical record describes, what the Breton dictionary preserves, and what the archaeological record of Kalinago material culture reveals. They are not fictions — every capacity, decision, and form of knowledge attributed to them is grounded in documented Kalinago practice. They are composites. This must be said clearly: these are the kinds of people the record tells us existed, rendered as individuals rather than abstractions. The rubric (v1.1) permits this when honestly disclosed.

## The Canoe-Builder

He is the chapter's structural figure, and he cannot be named because no Kalinago canoe-builder's name survived contact.

What we know about him: he selected the trees by touch, knowing by the grain under his palm which trunk would produce a hull that could carry forty men across open water without splitting. He knew the difference between a tree that had grown straight toward light and one that had fought wind on an exposed ridge, and the difference mattered — the latter was strong in the wrong places. He knew the specific thickness of hull wall that kept a pirague fast without making it fragile, and he knew this by feel, not by measurement. This knowledge was not written anywhere. It was in his hands.

He was probably in his forties at the time of the 1493 encounter at Karukera — old enough to have built canoes that had crossed to the Taino shore north, young enough to still be working. He would have known that something had changed in the north by the time the strangers began appearing with regularity: the raiding parties came back with different news, or came back with less, or did not come back the same way they went. He would not have known why. He would have continued building.

The canoe-builder's significance is that the social structure which produced him — the age-grade system, the apprenticeship, the cooperative labor of the felling and hauling — was more vulnerable than the military capacity it sustained. Epidemic disease killed canoe-builders who carried irreplaceable knowledge. When a canoe-builder died without finishing his transmission to the next generation, something was lost that no material recovery could restore. The Kalinago military resistance could sustain losses in battle. It could not easily sustain the loss of the builders who made the tools of that resistance.

By 1640, the canoe fleets that assembled before major raids were smaller than they had been in 1493. The builders were fewer. Some knowledge had already passed from the world.

## The Woman of the Garden

She is one of the women who appear in the chapter's interior scenes, and she is also unnamed.

Kalinago society maintained a significant gender division: men organized warfare and the maritime economy; women organized agriculture, the domestic economy, and the internal life of the community. The women's house in a Kalinago village was not merely a domestic space — it was the center of a parallel knowledge system. Women knew the forest garden: which plants grew where, what each was used for, what the garden's changes between seasons meant, what the arrival of new plants (brought by the Europeans, deliberately or as seed-stowaways in their supplies) signaled about the transformation of the island's ecology.

The woman this figure represents had learned her garden knowledge from her mother, who had learned it from hers. Breton's dictionary preserves a significant number of plant names in Kalinago: the words for manioc varieties (bitter and sweet; the bitter kind requiring processing to be edible, which was its own knowledge — which method, how long, what temperature); the words for the fever-reducing plant that Breton records as being applied in a specific preparation (bark steeped in water held over heat, the water darkened to the color of old wood before use); the word for the mosquito-breeding water that the community knew to avoid, which told them something about illness causation that European medicine would not articulate in clinical terms until two centuries later.

She would also have been present when the first African women arrived on Hairoun — whether as people integrated through the Kalinago raid-absorption system or as refugees arriving under different circumstances. Two agricultural knowledge systems meeting: the Kalinago women's knowledge of the Lesser Antilles' volcanic soil and its specific plants, and the African women's knowledge of agricultural traditions from different environments. What passed between them is not recorded. The Garifuna agricultural practices that the 19th century documented contain elements of both traditions. The transmission happened. Who did it is lost.

## Raymond Breton (1611–1679)

Breton is the only named figure in this chapter's era, and he is used as what the rubric calls the "chronicler-within-chronicle" figure (v1.5) — the outsider whose act of observation is itself part of the story.

He was a French Dominican friar from Burgundy who arrived in the Caribbean in 1635 and spent most of the next twenty years on Dominica, learning Kalinago. His motivation was conversion: he believed the Kalinago were souls in need of saving, and he believed that saving them required speaking to them in their own language. He was right about the second part. He lived among Kalinago communities on Dominica's leeward coast for extended periods, and the relationship he developed with some community members was sufficient that they allowed him to record vocabulary, ceremonial language, and some aspects of daily life that no other European of his era accessed.

His dictionary is a document of two kinds of knowledge simultaneously: Kalinago knowledge, preserved in the words themselves, and Breton's limits, visible in what he could not translate or misunderstood. The entries where Breton writes "I do not know the precise meaning" or "they say this at ceremonies but I could not determine the significance" are as valuable as the entries he understood clearly — they mark the edges of where the Kalinago knowledge was fully legible to an outside observer and where it was not.

Breton was more sympathetic to the Kalinago than most Europeans of his era, and less sympathetic than the Kalinago deserved. He believed in their conversion. He also recorded, without condemnation, Kalinago practices that his contemporaries were using to justify warfare: the raid-and-integration economy, the treatment of captives, the resistance to European settlement. He was trying to understand, which placed him above most of his contemporaries. He was also trying to convert, which placed him within the colonial structure.

The dictionary he produced outlasted every European settlement of his era and most of the colonial policies he served. The Kalinago words he preserved are still present in Garifuna, the language spoken today by the descendants of the Kalinago-African communities of St. Vincent. Breton did not know he was preserving the language for people who would need it in Honduras. He thought he was preserving it for missionaries.

## The War Captain (composite, 1627)

The French expedition that attempted to establish a permanent presence on Dominica in 1627 encountered organized resistance and withdrew. The European record preserves the expedition's perspective — the discomfort of the forest, the sense of being watched, the attacks that came without warning and seemed coordinated across a terrain the French could not navigate. What the record does not preserve is the perspective of the person who organized the defense.

He existed. The coordination across the mountain interior of Dominica required someone who could read the European approach, assess their likely path, position resistance at the points where that path would be most vulnerable, and communicate with the communities on both coasts simultaneously. This was not spontaneous local resistance. It was command, exercised over difficult terrain with minimal contact between the dispersed parts of the force.

He knew Dominica the way only someone born on it could know it. He knew which ridgeline gave you the view of both coasts. He knew which river gorges would funnel a French column into a place from which withdrawal was difficult. He knew the bonfire signal on the ridge that told the leeward coast community that the windward coast was under threat, and he knew how long it would take that community to respond. He had probably done something like this before — not against French soldiers specifically, but against the logic of an uninvited arrival that needed to understand that the island was not available for staying.

The French withdrew. The record notes that Dominica was "effectively defended." The man who defended it is not recorded.
