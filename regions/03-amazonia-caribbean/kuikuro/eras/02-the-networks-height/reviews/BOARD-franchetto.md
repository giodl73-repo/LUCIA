# Board Review — Bruna Franchetto
## Kuikuro Linguistics and Ethnography | The Network's Height | Kuikuro Era 2

---

The chapter's representation of the egitsü and the hüge role is broadly accurate and shows genuine familiarity with the published ethnographic record.

**Specific verifications:**

**The egitsü/Quarup terminology:** The chapter correctly uses "egitsü" as the Kuikuro term and "Quarup" as the pan-Xingu term from the name of the sacred log. This is accurate. The term's introduction ("the proper name for what the other peoples call the Quarup after the sacred log") is slightly imprecise — the Quarup name is used across multiple Xingu peoples and refers to the ceremonial event as a whole, named for the log. The distinction between the log and the ceremony is fine, but the sentence could be read to suggest other peoples use a different term from the Kuikuro, when in fact the same ceremony is known by slightly different local names across different communities. Suggest: "what the other peoples call the Quarup — after the sacred log that stands at the ceremony's center."

**The hüge role:** The description is accurate. The hüge is a recognized specialist role in Kuikuro ceremonial life. The transmission method (teacher-student, ear-training) is consistent with the documented oral transmission of Kuikuro ceremonial knowledge. The consequence of incorrect performance (egitsü does not close the grief) is consistent with the ethnographic account of the ceremony's efficacy requirements.

**The anetü's obligations:** Accurately characterized. The hereditary basis of the position combined with the continuous legitimacy requirement (he who cannot give, cannot lead) is a well-documented feature of Kuikuro chiefly organization.

**Gift exchange specifics:** The chapter names harpy eagle feathers, woven cotton goods, and pottery as exchange goods. These are consistent with the documented inter-community exchange goods in the upper Xingu ethnographic record. The macaw feathers (blue-and-yellow) are also consistent — macaw feathers are prestige goods in upper Xingu exchange.

**Language ecology:** "At least four language groups" is accurate — the upper Xingu in the pre-contact period housed speakers of Carib (Kuikuro, Kalapalo), Arawak (Mehinako, Wauja), Tupian (Kamayurá, Aweti), and additional small groups. The characterization of a "trading language" that most adults know is consistent with the documented multi-lingual competence in the upper Xingu, though the specific form of this inter-community communication is not fully documented for the pre-contact period.

**Corrections required:**

1. Minor: The egitsü/Quarup terminology sentence could be slightly clarified (see above). Not a factual error — a clarity issue.

2. Note: The chapter names the ceremony's "sacred log" as the quarup log. This is correct — the quarup is a hardwood log (often from the Bixa orellana tree) carved, painted, and feathered to represent the deceased. The chapter does not describe it being carved and decorated, which would strengthen the section. Not a required correction but a suggestion for enrichment.

**Endorsement:** The chapter correctly represents the Kuikuro ceremonial and social record. The hüge section is particularly good — the teacher-student correction scene is accurate in its depiction of the knowledge transmission method and does not romanticize it.

— B. Franchetto (board)
