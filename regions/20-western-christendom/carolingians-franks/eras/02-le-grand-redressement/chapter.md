# Le Grand Redressement — The Great Correction
## Carolingian Franks, Era 2: c. 751–814 CE

---

The manuscript arrived from a monastery in the south of Gaul, carried north
by a monk who had walked three weeks to deliver it. Alcuin of York took it
in both hands the way a physician receives an ill child: with care, with
concern, with the knowledge that what he was holding was serious. He sat
down with it under the lamp and began to read.

He could not.

The Latin had been copied so many times, by scribes who did not know what
they were copying, that it had decayed into something past meaning. A word
that should have been *spiritus* had become *spritus*, then *sprtus*, then
a shape that was no longer a word in any tongue. The margins were cluttered
with corrections that had themselves been copied as if they were the text.
A gloss explaining a difficult phrase had been absorbed into the sentence it
was meant to explain, doubling the confusion. The scribe who had made the
last copy had given up halfway through a chapter and written nothing at all;
Alcuin could feel his panic in the ink, the pressure heavier where the letters
made no sense, as if pushing harder would force the meaning through.

This was a liturgical text. Priests across the Frankish kingdom were reciting
it in churches every week. They were reciting nonsense. And because they could
not read, they did not know.

Alcuin set the manuscript down and did not move for a long time. Outside the
palace complex at Aachen, Aquisgranum to those who remembered its Roman name,
the winter of 782 pressed against the shutters. The palace school was quiet
at this hour. Somewhere in the complex, Charles was working; the king rarely
stopped before midnight, moving from his secretaries to his dinner companions
to his books, which he kept under his pillow and reached for in the moments
between sleep and waking. He was teaching himself to read, this king who had
remade more of the world than anyone since the Caesars. His letters were still
uncertain, his hand still that of a man who had learned to grip a sword before
he learned to grip a pen. But he was learning.

Alcuin understood what the king had asked him to come here and do. The request
had been simple on its surface: come from Northumbria, come from the tradition
of Bede and the great school at York, come and help us think. The king wanted
scholars the way he wanted good iron, not for display but for use. He wanted
the clergy reformed, the liturgy corrected, the monasteries turned from houses
of prayer into schools. He wanted *correctio*: correction. The word carried
everything the era would become. To correct a text was to restore what it
was meant to say. To correct a priest was to restore what he was meant to do.
To correct the Church was to restore what it was meant to be. And to restore
the Church was, in the understanding that Alcuin and Charles shared without
needing to argue it, to restore the order that God had placed in the world
and that ignorance had allowed to fall into ruin.

The manuscript in front of him was everything wrong, made legible. Every
generation of error preserved and transmitted as if it were truth.

He picked up his stylus and began.

## I. The World Before Correctio

When Pepin, son of Charles Martel, deposed the last Merovingian king in 751
and assumed the Frankish crown, the kingdom he received was vast, conquered,
and ungovernable by the methods that had built it. The territory ran from the
Breton coast to the eastern marches, from the Channel to the Pyrenees; it had
been won through three generations of warfare and held through a web of
personal loyalties that bound count to bishop, bishop to king, with the loyalty
of each node dependent entirely on the quality of the man who occupied it.

The clergy were the empire's administrative backbone. A bishop did not only
perform liturgy. He ran a diocese that was also a territory, heard court cases,
managed estates, collected obligations, maintained roads and bridges where the
system required it. An abbot governed a monastery that might be the largest
institution of learning and production in a region of ten thousand people.
A priest was often the only literate person in a village, the keeper of records,
the reader of royal decrees, the interpreter of a written world to a community
that experienced most of its life in speech. All of this depended on these
men being able to read.

Most of them could not.

The clergy that Pepin inherited, and that Charles would inherit from Pepin
in 768, had been shaped by two centuries of fragmentation. Monasteries had
become family estates, their abbacies passed from father to son or traded
as political favors, their libraries reduced to the handful of texts that
monks who could not read the whole collection preserved by copying without
comprehension. The great schools of the late Roman world, the grammar schools
that had produced Ambrose and Jerome and Augustine, had closed one by one
as the western cities that housed them shrank and the tax revenues that funded
them evaporated. What replaced them were local arrangements, each monastery
and cathedral chapter teaching what its community happened to know, which was
rarely very much.

A royal inspector arriving in a provincial diocese in 770 would find a bishop
who could recite the liturgy he had memorized as a child, who could manage
his estates with competence learned from his father, who could hear a court
case by the customs of the region. He would struggle to find one who could
explain the Creed in Latin, the formal statement of Christian belief that was
supposed to anchor every diocese's intellectual life. He would struggle harder
to find one who had read Augustine, or who understood the theological disputes
that had convulsed the Church in the previous century and whose resolutions
still shaped the doctrine the bishop was supposed to uphold. The liturgical
texts these men chanted had passed through so many hands, copied by so many
scribes who understood only enough to reproduce the marks on the page, that
they had drifted from any original and were slowly becoming, diocese by diocese,
a different religion.

Charles understood this as a problem of administration before he understood
it as a problem of theology. A kingdom administered by men who cannot read
the directives sent from the center is not a kingdom; it is a collection of
territories that happen to acknowledge the same king, for now, for as long
as the king's personal authority reaches them. He had spent twenty years
learning that conquest without administration dissolves back into anarchy:
the Lombard kingdom he took in 774, the Saxon marches he was still fighting
to hold, the Avar territories he would break in the 790s. Each campaign
produced territory. Territory required governance. Governance required men
who could read, write, and transmit the king's will accurately down a chain
of administrators who had never met the king.

He did not know, in 768 or in 772, exactly what he needed. He knew that his
grandfather's victory at Tours had given the Franks a sense of providential
mission: God's people, holding the line of the Christian west. He knew that
the Church was the institution through which that mission was to be performed.
And he knew, with the clarity of a man who governed through personal encounter
and found his government becoming too large for personal encounter alone, that
the Church was not capable of performing it.

*Correctio* was the answer he arrived at, slowly, through the campaigns of
the 770s and the conversations of the 780s, by the time he brought Alcuin
south from York.

## II. The Palace School and Its Peoples

What Charles built at Aachen was not, on first appearance, a school. It was
a court, the kind of court that every Frankish king had maintained, full of
warriors, clerics, royal children, and the constant movement of envoys and
petitioners. But underneath the court's surface, and increasingly above it,
was something that had not existed in the Latin west for three centuries: a
gathering of the most capable minds available, brought together not for
ceremony but for work.

Alcuin arrived from York around 782, fifty years old, carrying the tradition
of Bede and the library of the school where he had spent his intellectual
life. He was not the only scholar Charles had summoned. Theodulf had come
from the Visigothic world of Iberia, bringing a Latin tradition sharpened by
centuries of proximity to Islamic learning. Paul the Deacon arrived from the
Lombard court, where he had been the keeper of a historical memory that ran
back to the Roman world the Lombards had overrun and, gradually, become.
Peter of Pisa had come from Italy. Angilbert, a Frank by birth, had been
educated in the palace, and remained: he would become Abbot of Saint-Riquier
and Charles's son-in-law, the liaison between the court's intellectual life
and its political one.

Charles called himself "David" in the circle of the palace school. He was
the king of Israel who had established learning before Solomon built the
Temple, the patron whose role was to make the conditions for wisdom rather
than to possess it. He gave his scholars Roman and classical names: Alcuin
was "Flaccus" after Horace, Theodulf was "Pindar," Angilbert was "Homer."
The naming was not merely playful. It was a statement about what the palace
school was doing: the Frankish court was the new Rome, the new Jerusalem,
the place where the traditions of classical learning and Christian theology
were not merely preserved but actively remade. Charles was David who would
make possible a Solomon. The scholars were the classical authors whose work
would be revived and corrected into a new civilizational foundation.

The room where they debated was in the palace complex that Charles had spent
the 790s assembling at Aachen around the thermal baths the Romans had valued
for a millennium. The Palatine Chapel,
modeled on the octagonal church of San Vitale at Ravenna (the Franks knew
what Rome looked like and rebuilt it in the north), was the center of the
complex. Around it spread the halls, the residences, the scriptorium. The
scholars met in the evenings, after the administration of the day was done,
to debate Scripture and grammar and the proper form of the Creed. Charles
participated. He asked questions, pursued arguments, pushed back on answers
he found insufficient. Einhard, who was a young student in those years, would
later write that Charles loved the company of educated men and preferred their
dinner conversation to entertainment. This was probably true, and probably
also a product of the fact that the educated men Charles preferred had been
selected for their ability to entertain.

The debates were not merely academic. They produced decisions. The *Libri
Carolini*, the response to the Byzantine council that had authorized the
veneration of images, emerged from the palace school's deliberations: a
theological position that Charles sent to the Pope and that shaped the western
Church's relationship to art and doctrine for generations. The question of
whether the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone (as the eastern Church
held) or from the Father and the Son, the *filioque* that would eventually
split Christendom, was debated in the hall at Aachen before it became a matter
of official theology. The scholars were not ornaments. They were the
instruments through which the king governed the doctrinal life of the empire.

And they corrected manuscripts. That is where the work was done, where the
reform program had its daily practice: in the scriptorium, in the copying and
correcting and recopying of the texts that would then travel to monasteries,
to cathedral schools, to the dioceses where the program needed to reach.
Alcuin's contribution here was specific and permanent. He oversaw the
standardization of the Carolingian minuscule, a script already developing
in Frankish scriptoria but now made cleaner and more legible than any the
monasteries had been producing, smaller on the page, portable in a saddlebag,
readable across a room by a man who had not copied it himself. The old scripts of the Frankish tradition were regional, varied,
often beautiful, and frequently illegible to anyone trained in a different
region's conventions. The Carolingian minuscule was standard. A text copied
in Tours could be read in Saxony. The scriptural and liturgical uniformity
the reform program demanded required, as its physical precondition, a script
that made uniformity possible.

When Alcuin moved to Tours in 796 as Abbot of Saint-Martin, the richest and
most important monastery in the Frankish kingdom, he brought the scriptorium
with him in the sense of bringing the method. The monastery's copying program
under his direction became the most productive in the west. The windows of
the scriptorium were positioned for north light, which was steadier and cooler
than south light and easier on the eyes and the parchment. The scribes worked
in silence. The only sounds were the scratch of stylus on wax for the draft,
the quieter sound of quill on parchment for the fair copy, and occasionally
Alcuin's voice, still carrying York's cadences after twenty years in Francia,
pointing to a word and asking what it was supposed to mean. A great part of what we
have of classical Latin literature, Virgil and Cicero and Horace and Livy,
survives in Carolingian copies made in rooms like this one.

## III. The Admonitio Generalis and What It Required

In the spring of 789, Charles issued the *Admonitio Generalis*, the General
Admonition and the most comprehensive reform directive the Frankish kingdom
had yet produced. It ran to eighty-two chapters. It covered theology, liturgy,
law, the conduct of judges, the behavior of monks, the treatment of the poor,
the standardization of weights and measures, and, repeatedly, the requirement
that clergy be educated.

"In every monastery and episcopal church," it stated, "let there be schools
for the teaching of boys, for the singing of psalms, reckoning and grammar,
and let there be carefully corrected books of the Catholic books, psalms,
notation, chant, arithmetic, grammar, and let there be Catholic books. For
often some desire to pray to God properly, but they pray badly because of
incorrect books."

The theory of the reform, stated plainly: incorrect books produce incorrect
prayer. Incorrect prayer is an offense before God. Therefore, the correction
of books is an act of piety, and the education of those who copy and use the
books is an obligation of governance. Charles's theory of kingship was that
the king stood before God as the guardian of his people's souls as well as
their bodies. A population that prayed in corrupt Latin was a population whose
king had failed in his primary obligation. The *Admonitio* was his attempt
to meet it.

The mechanism for delivering the reform was the *missi dominici*, the lord's
sent men. Pairs of a layman and a clergyman, authorized by royal seal, traveled
in circuits through the counties each spring. They heard complaints about local
officials. They inspected monasteries. They tested bishops on their knowledge
of the Creed. They confirmed, or discovered the absence of, the schools the
*Admonitio* had required. They collected information about what the reform
program had and had not reached, and brought it back to Aachen. The following
year, another pair went out.

Theodulf of Orléans went out as a missus in the circuit that covered southern
Gaul. The Visigoth who had become one of Charles's most trusted bishops, the
poet who could write in the tradition of Virgil and debate theology with Alcuin
and administer a diocese with the precision of a Frankish count, he found in
the southern provinces what the *Admonitio* had not yet corrected. He put it
in a poem, *Contra Iudices*, which is one of the most remarkable documents
the Carolingian age produced: a bishop's first-person account of the corruption
he encountered and his inability to fully correct it.

He found judges who accepted bribes in sight of his inspection, knowing that
a bishop's authority could not compel a count's cooperation without the count's
consent. He found courts rendering inconsistent verdicts, not from malice but
from the genuine absence of any uniform law, any shared standard by which a
judgment could be measured. He found villages where the priest could chant
the liturgy and nothing else, where the missionary work of the previous century
had produced Christian forms without Christian understanding, where baptism was
understood as membership in the Frankish political community, partly correct,
more than as transformation of the soul, which was what it was supposed to be.
He found monasteries that had forgotten what monasteries were for, where the
abbot was the third generation of his family to hold the position and had
inherited it the way a count inherits a county, where the monks performed their
offices on schedule and had not opened a book in a decade.

He wrote the poem with precision and some despair. He was not condemning the
people he found; they were doing what they had been given conditions to do.
He was reporting the distance between what the *Admonitio Generalis* had
required and what the actual world, in the actual villages of the actual south,
had produced.

Charles read reports like this. He adjusted, issued further capitularies, sent
the missi again the following year. The system learned, slowly. Some monasteries
did establish schools. Some bishops did learn to explain the Creed. The
corrected texts did travel, carried in saddlebags along the Roman roads, some
sections still maintained and running straight through the hills, other sections
overgrown at the margins and requiring detour into the mud alongside. The
reform moved at the speed of a horse and a royal seal.

## IV. What Correctio Did to Its Subjects

The reform was not a request. Charles sent the *missi* with his seal, which
was the weight of his personal authority, and his personal authority in the
790s was not something that a count or an abbot chose to resist without
consequence. The capitularies went out. The missi went out. The requirement
to establish schools, correct liturgy, and produce a literate clergy was a
royal command, backed by a king who had demonstrated, repeatedly, that he
was willing to apply military force to achieve religious and political
uniformity.

The Saxons had learned this most directly. The Saxon campaigns that ran from
772 to 804 were simultaneously a military conquest and a forced *correctio*:
the Saxon gods were to be abandoned, the Saxon customary law replaced by
Frankish law and Christian doctrine, Saxon children brought to Aachen as
hostages and educated in the palace school. Some of those children grew up
to be Frankish administrators, bishops, loyal servants of the empire. Some
returned to their people and led further revolts. The campaign of 782 ended
with the massacre at Verden. The Royal Frankish Annals record 4,500 Saxon
prisoners executed; later revisions to the official record quietly omitted
the passage. It was a decision that shocked some of his own advisors and
that Alcuin, though he expressed himself carefully, found troubling.

Alcuin's concern was not that the Saxons were being corrected. It was that
they were being corrected too fast, by force, without the prior labor of
persuasion that he believed genuine conversion required. He wrote to Charles
that a person could not be made Christian by the sword, that baptism preceded
by compulsion without instruction was a form without substance, that the
Saxons who had been baptized at knifepoint and remained, in their own
understanding of the world, Saxons performing Christian rituals would break
back into revolt when the military pressure eased. They did. He was right
about the mechanism. He did not argue that *correctio* was wrong. He argued
that it needed more time, more teachers, more of the patient labor that the
palace school represented. His proposed alternative to forced baptism was
the palace school model applied to the Saxons: bring the children to Aachen,
educate them in Christian doctrine and Frankish governance, return them to
their communities as the corrected generation.

Charles and Alcuin agreed on what the Saxons needed to become. They argued
about the pace and method. This was the reform program's internal debate,
and it was a real one, conducted between two men who respected each other's
intelligence and disagreed in writing across the letters that survive as some
of the most intellectually alive documents of the age.

The Saxon children who came to Aachen, hostages technically though educated
as students, sat in the palace school alongside the children of Frankish nobles
and, in at least some cases, alongside the children of palace servants who
showed promise. What did that school sound like? Boys reciting Latin declensions
in accents shaped by three different vernaculars: the Frankish *theotisca
lingua* and the Latin of the curriculum and whatever Saxon or Lombard or
Visigothic tongue had shaped a child's ear before the school. Charles's own
voice, asking questions that Alcuin would answer and then turn back on the
king: what does this word mean? Why does Paul say this here and something
different there? The room was not a lecture hall. It was a conversation, held
in a building that smelled of wax and new parchment and the oil of the lamps
that burned when the winter afternoons ended too soon.

## V. The Weight of What Was Being Built

By the time Charles left for Rome in the autumn of 800, the reform program
was seventeen years into its work. The *Admonitio Generalis* had been issued,
revised, supplemented by further capitularies. The palace school had trained
a generation of scholars who were now dispersed across the empire: Theodulf
as bishop of Orléans, Angilbert as abbot of Saint-Riquier, the alumni of
Tours carrying the Carolingian minuscule and the corrected texts to their
new positions. Alcuin was at Tours, still writing letters, still correcting
manuscripts, still asking Charles difficult questions from a safe distance.

The empire had produced something it had not quite intended. It was now an
entity too large and too uniform to be governed as a territory had previously
been governed, too administratively ambitious to be described by the vocabulary
available to the Franks. The word *regnum*, kingdom, was technically accurate
but felt insufficient for a polity that ran from the Atlantic to the Danube,
that had absorbed the Lombard kingdom, broken the Avars, and was enforcing
a doctrinal uniformity on peoples speaking half a dozen vernaculars. There
was another word. The Franks knew it from the Roman roads they traveled and
the Roman buildings they occupied and the Roman legal texts they had been
copying and correcting for two decades in the palace scriptoria. The word
was *imperium*.

Alcuin, writing from Tours, had already begun to use it. He wrote to Charles
that the empire of the Franks now exceeded the Roman empire in the integrity
of its Christian faith, if not in territory. He meant this as praise. He also
meant it as a statement about what the reform program had been building toward:
not merely a better-administered kingdom but the restored Christian empire
that the western Church had lacked since the last Roman emperor in the west
had been deposed in 476. Charles was not only a king. He was, in Alcuin's
reading of the moment, the man who had made Christendom governable again.

The Pope in Rome had his own uses for this understanding. Leo III, driven
from Rome by a faction of the Roman nobility that had attacked him in the
street, had appealed to Charles and been restored to his see by Frankish
troops. He owed Charles his position, his safety, and the future of his
papacy. On Christmas Day, 800, in the basilica of Saint Peter, he placed
a crown on Charles's head and the assembled Franks acclaimed the new emperor.

Charles would later tell Einhard that he had not known it was coming, that
he would not have entered the church that day if he had. Einhard sets this
down without comment, which is itself a comment: a man who had spent twenty
years building the administrative and intellectual infrastructure of an empire
was apparently surprised to be crowned emperor. Whatever Charles's intentions,
the coronation opened a question that would take centuries to resolve and
that the Carolingian reform program had made, in some sense, inevitable: if
the emperor and the pope were the two heads of a single Christian order, who
held the higher authority? Who could crown whom, and under what conditions,
and with what consequences if they disagreed?

Alcuin, in Tours, wrote that he was glad to have been absent from Rome. He
had his reasons.

## VI. What Remained

Charles died at Aachen in January 814, in the palace complex he had built
and spent thirty years filling with scholars, capitularies, missi, and the
corrected texts of a civilization he had decided to restore. He was
approximately seventy-two years old; the exact date of his birth is uncertain,
which is itself a small irony in a reign devoted to the precision of the
written record.

He left behind an empire that his son Louis would struggle to hold together
and that Louis's sons would divide at Verdun in 843 into three territories
that became the rough outlines of France, Germany, and a middle kingdom that
neither would ever stop fighting over. The personal loyalty chains that had
held the Carolingian empire together, the counts and bishops and abbots whose
competence and fidelity had sustained the reform program, proved unable to
transfer their allegiance without degradation from a father to a son, or from
a son to three grandsons. The empire that Charles had governed through a
combination of military authority, intellectual engagement, and personal
presence was simply too large and too dependent on those personal qualities
to survive their absence intact.

But some things traveled.

The Carolingian minuscule traveled. The script that Alcuin and his scriptorium
at Tours had standardized and distributed became the basis for the letterforms
that medieval Europe used for the next six centuries, and that the Renaissance
humanists would revive from the Carolingian manuscripts they admired, believing
they were recovering ancient Roman writing, which in a sense they were, since
the Carolingian minuscule had been designed to reproduce the clarity of late
Roman scribal practice. The lowercase letters you are reading now are the
descendants of the script that Alcuin corrected into existence in a scriptorium
at Tours in the years around 800.

The texts traveled. A great part of what we possess of classical Latin
literature survives in Carolingian copies: the monks of the ninth century, trained in
the palace school tradition, copied Virgil and Cicero and Livy and Horace
because Alcuin had told them these were the texts that prepared a mind for
the understanding of Scripture. The original Roman manuscripts are mostly
gone. The Carolingian copies are what we have.

The idea traveled. The project of a Christian empire, governed by an emperor
who was also the defender of the Church and who derived his authority from
God through both the military conquest that extended the faith and the
intellectual reform that deepened it, this was the Carolingian contribution
to the political theology of the Latin west, and it would haunt every
subsequent emperor who wanted to be Charles and every subsequent pope who
wanted to be Leo III. The argument about who held the higher authority in
Christendom would run from 800 to the Reformation and beyond, through the
Investiture Controversy, through Canossa, through Gregory VII and Henry IV
and Innocent III and Frederick II and every confrontation between the sacred
and secular claims of the western world.

What could not travel was the man who had made the scribes believe the
correction mattered. Charles's personal authority, his ability to hold by
force of personality and by his physical presence at the annual assembly and
by the fear and affection he inspired in equal measure in his counts and
bishops, was the mechanism that made the missi function, the palace school
cohere, the capitularies reach their destinations and be obeyed. The
manuscripts accumulated. The scripts dispersed. The corrected texts spread
through the monasteries of Francia and Saxony and Lombardy and the Spanish
March. And the man who had driven all of it, who had summoned Alcuin from
York and Theodulf from Iberia and who had asked his dinner companions
difficult questions until midnight and kept his books under his pillow for
the moments between sleep and waking, that man died in January, and the
empire that needed him to be eternal began, quietly, to become something else.

The lamp that had burned in the scriptorium at Tours still burned. Alcuin
had been dead for ten years by the time Charles went. The manuscripts he had
corrected still sat on their shelves. The script he had standardized still
shaped every letter the next generation of monks put to parchment.

*Correctio* had done what it could do. It had corrected the texts, and the
texts had survived.

The empire was another matter.
