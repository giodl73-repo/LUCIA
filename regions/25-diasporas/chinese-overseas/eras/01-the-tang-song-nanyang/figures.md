# Figures — The Tang-Song Nanyang

## Captain Lin (composite)

Lin is not a named historical figure. The Tang-Song maritime trade record preserves the names of officials, of ports, of goods in extraordinary detail, but the captains who carried the goods across the South China Sea and through the strait are, with rare exceptions, anonymous in the sources. Zhao Rugua, writing in the 1220s from his post as superintendent of maritime trade at Quanzhou, described the junks, their routes, their cargoes, and the commercial practices of the Hokkien traders who operated them. He described the system. He did not name the men who made it work.

Lin is built from the structural evidence: Wang Gungwu's analysis of the Hokkien trading community's organization, Zhao Rugua's descriptions of cargo and routes, and the archaeological record of Song-era Quanzhou (ceramics, coins, ship remains). He is a third-generation Hokkien sailor, a composite of the class of men who built the Nanyang trade network through repeated crossings, accumulated knowledge, and the maintenance of guanxi relationships across the monsoon cycle. His navigation by water color, his knowledge of cargo handling, and his reliance on the list of names rather than on maps are all drawn from the scholarly consensus about how the Hokkien maritime trade operated.

What is specific to Lin is his body: the cracked hands, the bare feet reading the current through the deck, the lukewarm tea in a chipped cup. These details are the chapter's way of giving embodied presence to a figure the record did not name. They are plausible, grounded in the material conditions of Song-era maritime life, but they are the author's construction, not the archive's testimony. The chapter acknowledges this by never claiming to know Lin's thoughts, only his actions and his knowledge.

## Chen (composite)

The newly arrived trader, young and carrying his father's letter of introduction, represents the mechanism by which the guanxi network reproduced itself across generations. His experience in the chapter, arriving at a port where he has never been and being taken in by the surname association on the strength of his father's reputation, is drawn from Wang Gungwu's description of how the huiguan system functioned as the primary institution for integrating new arrivals into the overseas community. The detail of the father's advice ("do not eat anyone else's food until you know whose salt you are eating") reflects the practical wisdom that the sources attribute to the Hokkien merchant tradition.

Chen's decision to stay through the monsoon rather than return to Quanzhou is the structural pivot of the chapter: the moment when a sojourner begins to become a settler. This decision, multiplied across hundreds of individuals and dozens of ports, is what transformed the Nanyang trade network from a seasonal commerce into a permanent diaspora. The sources do not preserve individual decisions of this kind, but the demographic evidence (the growth of permanent Chinese communities in Nanyang ports from the tenth century onward) confirms that such decisions were made.

## Tan (composite)

The warehouse keeper who has lived in the Nanyang port for twenty years and speaks Malay fluently represents the settled Chinese merchant class, the men who had made the transition from sojourner to resident and who served as the institutional backbone of the community. Tan's role in the chapter, as the man who reads the letter, assesses the cargo, opens the warehouse, and records the transaction, shows the huiguan system operating at ground level. His bilingualism (Hokkien and Malay) is characteristic of the long-term Chinese residents of the Nanyang, who maintained their dialect for intra-community commerce while acquiring the local language for dealings with the Malay and Javanese merchants and authorities.

## Ismail (composite)

The Malay camphor trader represents the Nanyang's own commercial world: the local knowledge systems, trade networks, and economic relationships that existed before the Chinese arrived and that the Chinese traders depended on but did not control. Ismail knows which trees produce the finest camphor, which upriver collectors to trust, and what the Chinese will pay. His knowledge is as specific and as commercially valuable as Lin's knowledge of the sea routes, and the chapter presents the two men's negotiation as a meeting between equals, each bringing what the other needs.

The inclusion of Ismail is the chapter's response to a necessary question: whose Nanyang is this? The Chinese overseas story is told from inside the Chinese experience, but the Nanyang is not a Chinese space. It is a Malay, Javanese, Sumatran, and Cham space through which Chinese traders move and in which they eventually settle. Ismail's presence reminds the reader that the Chinese community exists within someone else's world and that the commerce that sustains it depends on local knowledge and local relationships that the Chinese network alone cannot provide.

## The Shibosi Pu (composite, based on the Pu family)

The superintendent of maritime trade in Quanzhou is based on the historical Pu family, Arab Muslim merchants who settled in Quanzhou and rose to positions of administrative authority in the Song-era customs system. The most famous member of the family, Pu Shougeng, served as shibosi in the late Southern Song and played a controversial role in the Song-Yuan transition. The chapter's Pu is set earlier, during the height of the Southern Song maritime expansion, and represents the cosmopolitan character of Quanzhou's administration: a port where an Arab Muslim family could administer the Chinese customs house because revenue mattered more than origin.

The shibosi's function in the chapter is to show the Song state's relationship to the maritime trade: taxing it, regulating it, needing it, but not controlling it. The shibosi counts the junks and reports the numbers to Lin'an, and the numbers are always supposed to grow, because the Southern Song court, having lost the northern territories and their agricultural revenue, depended on the customs income from the southern ports. The shibosi is a bureaucrat, and the chapter presents his bureaucratic precision (the manifest, the stamp, the report) as the state's way of participating in a system that the state did not build and could not fully command.

## Huang and his Malay wife (composite)

The intermarriage that produces the Peranakan pattern is shown through Huang's wedding, a scene that the chapter uses to demonstrate the cultural synthesis that occurred when Chinese settlers married local women. The wedding food, described in detail (Hokkien noodles, Malay rendang, the laksa that belongs to both traditions and neither), is the evidence of a transformation that no one planned and that the people living through it understood without needing to name. The Peranakan culture that would eventually produce its own literature, cuisine, and identity in the Straits Settlements of the nineteenth century has its roots in marriages like this one, made in Nanyang ports during the Tang-Song period, when staying was more practical than leaving and the children who resulted spoke two languages and ate from two tables.

The archaeological and textual evidence for intermarriage in the Tang-Song period is thinner than for later centuries. Chinese tombstones in Southeast Asia from this period are rare, and the textual sources (Zhao Rugua, Zhou Qufei) describe the trade but not the domestic lives of the traders. The intermarriage pattern is better attested for the Ming period and later. The chapter treats the pattern as beginning in this era, consistent with the scholarly consensus (Wang Gungwu, Reid), without overclaiming specific evidence for the Tang-Song period.

## The Malay Pilot (unnamed)

The pilot who boards Lin's junk at Temasek and guides it through the shoals of the strait is the chapter's acknowledgment that the strait belongs to the people who live on it. His knowledge of the shoals, read through ripple patterns in the current, is presented as equal in depth and specificity to Lin's knowledge of the open-sea routes. The pilot is not named because the chapter does not enter his perspective fully; he appears at the edge of Lin's world, a figure whose skill Lin respects and whose waters Lin is crossing with permission, not with right. The orang laut (sea people) of the strait passages were the most skilled maritime pilots of the region, and the pilot's presence on Lin's junk reflects the historical practice of Chinese captains taking local pilots aboard for the navigation of unfamiliar coastal waters.
