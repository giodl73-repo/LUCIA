# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Review: The Classic Taíno, Opening

I will say what needs to be said first: the voice is right.

I have read too many openings in this Chronicle where a thoughtful author describes a culture's interior life from just far enough outside to let the reader feel secure — to let the reader observe rather than inhabit. This opening does not do that. The reader is with Anacaona in the composition, not watching her compose. The distinction is everything.

The sentence that concerns me most in many chapters about Indigenous Caribbean peoples is the one that says something like "They had no writing, but..." — the but that turns absence into a concession before celebrating what substitutes for it. This opening does not do that either. The areíto is presented as a technology of knowing superior to inscription in certain crucial respects: the body remembers by repeating, and repetition makes the knowledge alive, and the zemís attend when the knowledge is alive. There is no apology here for the absence of an alphabet. There is instead a cosmological account of why embodied knowledge is alive in a way that written knowledge is not. This is the only acceptable way to handle this material. I will not pretend otherwise.

**What works without reservation:**

"This is not irony." Those four words do more cultural protection work than any number of editorial policies. The author saw the trap — the foreknowledge-as-tragedy frame that would have made Anacaona a figure of pathos for a modern reader rather than a figure of authority in her own world — and refused it explicitly. The refusal is clean, undefensive, stated once, and then the text moves on. This is how you protect a culture from being turned into a symbol of its own destruction.

The daughter learning the areíto from an elder singer while Anacaona works inside: that is a detail that renders the tradition as living, continuous, not yet broken. It does not sentimentalize continuity; it shows it at work in the ordinary afternoon.

**The question I'd push on:**

The chapter will encounter the question of Taíno survival. The commission notes that the Taíno were called "extinct" for centuries and are not. This is the deepest Achebe Test challenge in the whole chapter. The chapter must not treat 1492 as a death sentence. It must not perform grief for a culture that did not end. But it also must not pretend the catastrophe was anything other than catastrophic.

The way I would approach it: let the areíto's own logic answer this. The areíto was composed for a world that would outlast the composer. The areíto is how knowledge survives when the conditions of its performance are disrupted. The chapter's landing must find where the areíto goes when the batey is gone and the yucayeque is scattered. Not as a consolation. As a structural fact.

**Overall assessment:** The voice is the Chronicle's standard. Do not dilute it. The chapter's hardest work is ahead.
