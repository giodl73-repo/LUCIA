# Panel Review — R2-P1 — Barbara Tuchman (PANEL-2)
## Notes review: The Founding — The Experiment Begins

**Focus: Narrative craft, structural engine, arc viability**

The production bible is strong. The commitment to Madison as primary figure is correct and finally explicit. The section plan has a shape: it opens in the room, moves to the argument before the argument, describes the machine, introduces the counter-voice (Allen and the Free African Society), traces the first decade, and closes with Banneker's letter to Jefferson. This is a viable arc. It does not merely list events.

**What I'm satisfied with:**
The Banneker-Jefferson exchange is the right closing image. It performs the same function as Madison's notes — a document, a writer, an argument — but from the other side of the paradox. The ring closes. Madison's private anguish and public complicity at the opening; Banneker's public insistence and Jefferson's private noncommittal at the close. The chapter will have a structure.

The "Other Philadelphia" section (Allen, Jones, the Free African Society) is well-placed and well-reasoned. It is the counter-argument not added as correction but as completion: the same principles, applied consistently, by people the Convention was not yet prepared to include. This is the right framing.

**What I'd tighten:**
The section on the Constitutional mechanism (Section III: The Machine) risks becoming an explanation of constitutional theory rather than a scene of constitutional reality. The Federalist Papers are mentioned but not yet dramatized. Hamilton writing Federalist No. 78 (on judicial review) in three days during the New York ratification campaign — that is a scene. Madison at the Virginia ratification convention, arguing against Patrick Henry, who predicted that the Constitution would produce tyranny — that is a scene. The chapter needs at least one of these as a grounding moment. Abstract constitutional argument is the thing the style guide most warns against.

The election of 1800 is mentioned in the section plan as a closing event but is not yet developed. The tie between Jefferson and Burr, resolved in the House of Representatives after thirty-six ballots, with Hamilton finally supporting Jefferson over Burr (the man Hamilton distrusted most over the man Hamilton opposed most politically) — this is a scene, it is historically documented, and it is the chapter's best evidence that the machine actually worked. It should appear.

**One remaining question:**
The notes name Sally Hemings and handle her correctly — acknowledging the absence of her voice while honoring her presence. But the section plan does not yet place her. Is she in the Jefferson section (Section II)? In the closing (Section VI)? She should appear in the place that makes her presence felt as structural rather than added. My suggestion: she enters in Section II, when Jefferson is introduced from Paris, and she is with him there — legally free, choosing to return. That choice, and what it implies, is itself a founding-era event.
