# Figures — The Fragmentation

## Khety son of Tefibi, Nomarch of Asyut

Khety governed the Thirteenth Nome of Upper Egypt from the city of Asyut during the later First Intermediate Period. His father Tefibi and grandfather had held the same position before him, making the nomarchy hereditary in fact if not always in official title. His tomb, carved into the western cliffs above the Nile at Asyut, contains a biographical inscription in which he claims to have fed the hungry, maintained the irrigation canal west of the city, and defended the southern border against Theban military incursions.

Khety was a Herakleopolitan ally. His nome sat on the contested frontier between the northern Herakleopolitan kingdom and the expanding Theban domain to the south. His military preparations, described in his inscription with the specificity of an administrative report, reflect a man who understood his position as both strategic and precarious. He did not rebel against any authority. He governed the space that no single authority could hold.

The inscriptions at Asyut are among the most detailed provincial autobiographies from the period. They describe not abstract virtues but specific acts of governance: grain distribution in named years, canal construction, troop levies, boundary defense. Khety emerges from his text not as a hero or a philosopher but as a competent provincial administrator who happened to live in a time when competent provincial administration was the difference between a functioning nome and a starving one.

**Source note:** The Asyut tomb inscriptions were recorded by Griffith (1889) and more recently studied by Kahl (2007). The specific claims about grain distribution and canal maintenance are consistent across multiple inscriptions at the site, suggesting a genuine administrative tradition rather than formulaic borrowing. The granary scene in the chapter's opening is a narrative reconstruction grounded in the inscription's claims about grain storage and distribution; the specific moment depicted (counting grain after receiving news of Theban advance) is an authorial construction, not a documented event.

## Ankhtifi, Nomarch of Moalla

Ankhtifi governed the Second and Third Nomes of Upper Egypt from a base at Moalla, south of Thebes. His autobiography, inscribed on the walls of his tomb above the floodplain, is the longest and most vivid personal text from the First Intermediate Period. He claimed to have been invited by the god Horus of Edfu to take control of the neighboring nome, to have fought the Thebans, to have distributed grain to populations from other nomes who came to his territory during famine, and to have been, in his own estimation, "the beginning and end of mankind."

The language of Ankhtifi's inscription is distinctive. It combines the formal vocabulary of the Old Kingdom biographical tradition with a self-confidence that borders on the regal. He describes his military campaigns, his grain distributions, his canal works, and his alliances in language that attributes to himself the functions that earlier inscriptions had attributed to the king. Whether this represents genuine megalomania, a provincial literary convention, or simply the pragmatic rhetoric of a man who needed to justify his authority in the absence of royal endorsement is a question the text does not answer.

Ankhtifi's tomb paintings depict fishing, fowling, agriculture, and craftwork in the provincial style of Upper Egypt. The artistic quality is competent but distinct from the metropolitan tradition: stiffer proportions, a narrower color palette, local subjects. The paintings document a self-sufficient provincial economy operating without reference to a central authority.

**Source note:** The Ankhtifi inscription was published by Vandier (1950) and has been analyzed extensively by Moreno Garcia (2013). The famine passage ("the whole of Upper Egypt was dying of hunger, to such a degree that every man ate his children") is recognized as formulaic hyperbole standard to the crisis-description genre, but the underlying pattern of regional food insecurity during low-flood years is supported by other evidence.

## Khunanup, the Eloquent Peasant

Khunanup is a literary figure, the protagonist of the text known as the Eloquent Peasant (Papyrus Berlin 3023, 3025; Papyrus Amherst; Papyrus Butler). He is presented as a smallholder from the Wadi Natrun oasis in the western desert who travels to the Nile valley to trade salt, natron, reeds, herbs, and animal skins. After being robbed by a minor official, he petitions the chief steward Rensi in nine increasingly eloquent speeches that demand justice and invoke ma'at as a universal principle accessible to every person, regardless of rank.

The text's date of composition is debated. Some scholars place it in the First Intermediate Period; others argue it was composed in the early Middle Kingdom, looking back at FIP conditions. The distinction matters for historical dating but not for cultural placement: the text's concerns, its assumption that eloquence could be a commoner's weapon and that ma'at was not the king's exclusive domain, belong to the intellectual world that the Fragmentation created.

Khunanup is not a historical person in the sense that Khety or Ankhtifi are historical persons. He is a literary construction. But his construction reveals what the culture valued: the idea that speech could compel justice, that a man's words could outweigh his station, that the cosmic order was answerable to anyone who could articulate a claim against it.

**Source note:** Standard translations include Parkinson (1991, 2012) and Quirke (2004). The dating debate is summarized in Parkinson, Poetry and Culture in Middle Kingdom Egypt (2002), pp. 125-138.

## Merikare, Addressee of a Royal Instruction

Merikare was a prince of the Herakleopolitan royal house, the addressee of the Instruction of Merikare (Papyrus Hermitage 1116A), a wisdom text composed in the voice of his father, probably Wahkare Khety. The text advises Merikare on governance: be just, maintain the borders, treat the nomarchs with care, do not trust those closest to you blindly. It includes a remarkable admission of wrongdoing, the destruction of the sacred cemeteries at Thinis during the war against Thebes, and counsels Merikare not to repeat the error.

The Instruction of Merikare is the only text from the Herakleopolitan dynasty that gives extended access to the royal perspective. It reveals a king who is not confident but cautious, not commanding but advising, not claiming divine certainty but working through practical problems with the awareness that the old order has failed and something more careful must replace it.

**Source note:** Principal edition: Quack (1992). The text's composition in the Herakleopolitan period is generally accepted, though some scholars argue for early Twelfth Dynasty composition. The admission regarding Thinis has been analyzed by Parkinson (2002) as unprecedented in the royal literary tradition.

## The Intef Rulers of Thebes

Intef I (Sehertawy), Intef II (Wahankh), and Intef III (Nakhtnebtepnefer) were the first three rulers of the Eleventh Dynasty at Thebes. They are known primarily from their mortuary complex at el-Tarif on the west bank of the Nile at Thebes and from scattered inscriptions and stelae. Intef II, in particular, expanded Theban control northward through a series of campaigns that brought the nomes between Thebes and Abydos under his authority.

The Intef rulers represent the alternative model of post-Old Kingdom authority: not continuity with the old forms (the Herakleopolitan strategy) but new construction from a southern base. Their saff tombs at el-Tarif are architecturally distinct from anything in the north: broad, open courtyards cut into the desert edge, with pillared facades looking out over the valley. The architecture rejects the pyramid, the mastaba, and the cliff-tomb tradition of the nomarchs. It is a new form for a new claim.

**Source note:** The el-Tarif tombs are documented in Arnold (1976). The military campaigns of Intef II are reconstructed from stelae at Abydos and Dendera; see Grajetzki (2006) for a synthesis.
