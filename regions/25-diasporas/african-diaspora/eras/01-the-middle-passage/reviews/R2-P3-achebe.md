## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Notes (Panel-2)

**Overall:** The notes demonstrate awareness of the inside-voice problem at scale. The solutions are sound but must be tested in execution.

**What works:**
- The decision to describe the economics of the trade as experienced by the captives ("you are currency, not person") rather than from the traders' perspective is the right choice. It maintains the inside voice even during structural exposition.
- The section plan keeps a named or composite figure anchoring each section. This prevents the retreat to survey voice that I warned about. As long as the chapter executes this discipline, the inside voice should hold.
- The treatment of Equiano's birthplace debate is honest and generative. If his account is constructed from community testimony rather than personal experience, that itself demonstrates the body-archive: the community transmitted the memory of the crossing to someone who may not have experienced it, and the transmission was so effective that the account reads as personal testimony. This is the engine operating.

**What doesn't:**
- The "purchasing a library" metaphor from the opening has not been addressed. The notes should specify that this phrase will be revised in the FIXES stage. My suggestion from Panel-1 stands: find a formulation that stays inside the man's experience rather than stepping outside to characterize his knowledge for the reader.
- Section 6 (What Was Built) risks becoming a catalogue of cultural survivals. Candomblé, Vodou, Maroons, the ring shout, the invisible church. Each is essential, but listing them is not inhabiting them. The chapter must choose one or two to render from inside (the Candomblé ceremony at method level, the ring shout at sensory level) and let the others receive briefer treatment. Depth over breadth.

**The question I'd push on:**
Who is the "we" of this chapter? The Jewish diaspora chapter found its answer: the "we" is the community of argument, the people of the page. What is the African diaspora's "we"? It cannot be a single ethnic group (Yoruba, Fon, Igbo are distinct). It is the "we" that was created by the Middle Passage itself: a people forged from many peoples by the shared experience of the crossing and the shared condition of enslavement. The chapter needs to name this "we" in the culture's own terms, not in academic terms.
