# Daner — "The Danes"

We are the people of the hinge. Denmark sits where the North Sea meets the Baltic, where the Scandinavian peninsula meets the European mainland, where the Norse world meets the German world. Every ship that sails from the North Sea to the Baltic must pass through the Sound or the Belt — Danish water. Every land route from Jutland to the Norse north passes through Danish territory. This geographic position made us simultaneously the most connected and the most contested people of northern Europe. We were the first Scandinavian kingdom to adopt Christianity, the first to develop an organized royal state, and the first to project sustained military and political power outward — into England, into the Baltic, into Normandy. The Danish king Cnut held England, Norway, and Denmark simultaneously in the early eleventh century and was for a decade the most powerful ruler in northern Europe.

The Danes are also the Norse people who stayed closest to home — closest, that is, to the Frankish and Saxon world that pressed against our southern border. This proximity shaped everything: we needed royal organization and Christian institutions earlier than our Norwegian and Swedish cousins because the Carolingian and Saxon empires demanded a single, accountable sovereign. Harold Bluetooth's baptism in the 960s was not merely a religious event. It was a diplomatic calculation: that a Christian Denmark could resist German pressure more effectively than a pagan one, because the pope could be played as a counterweight to the emperor, and because Christian kingship carried administrative capacities — literate clergy, regularized taxation, canon law — that pagan kingship could not match.

Key places:
- **Jelling** — the royal estate; the great runestones of Gorm the Old and Harald Bluetooth; the burial mounds of the first Danish royal dynasty
- **Hedeby (Haithabu)** — the great trading emporium at the base of Jutland; the largest town in Scandinavia before 1000 CE

- **Danevirke** — the earthwork fortification across the base of Jutland; the definitive statement of where Denmark ended and Germany began

## What Defined Them

**The Danevirke — defining the border.** The great earthwork across the neck of Jutland was not simply a military fortification — it was a political statement. By building and maintaining the Danevirke, the Danish kings were saying: this is where Denmark begins. The line was real, it required organized labor to build and maintain, and it presupposed a political authority capable of mobilizing that labor. The Danevirke is evidence that Danish kingship was organizing the landscape centuries before the first written Danish records. The border made the state, and the state made the border.

**Jelling and the making of Christian Denmark.** The Jelling runestone complex — Gorm the Old's stone commemorating his wife Thyra, and Harald Bluetooth's larger stone commemorating his own baptism and conversion of the Danes — is the most important monument of early Danish history. Harald's stone explicitly states that he "made the Danes Christian." The political meaning was as important as the religious: a Christian king could present himself to the Carolingian and Saxon neighbors not as a barbarian raider but as a legitimate sovereign, entitled to diplomatic correspondence, treaty rights, and the protection of his kingdom's borders under the same international conventions that governed Christian Europe.

**Cnut — the Viking king as European monarch.** Cnut's conquest of England in 1016 produced something unexpected: a Norse king who governed England through English institutions, respected English law, attended to English Church affairs, and went on pilgrimage to Rome. His letter to the English people from Rome in 1027 is remarkable: it promises just government, fair taxation, and respect for Church rights in the language of an English king addressing his people, not a conqueror addressing subjects. Cnut understood that he needed English administrative culture to hold England, and that holding England gave him resources no Scandinavian king had previously commanded.

**The Baltic empire and the Wendish question.** Valdemar I and Valdemar II of Denmark, in the twelfth and early thirteenth centuries, built a Baltic empire that controlled much of the southern Baltic coast, crusaded against the pagan Wends, and briefly made Denmark the dominant power in the region. The *Jyske Lov* (Jutish Law) of 1241, issued under Valdemar II, is one of the most important medieval Scandinavian law codes. This Baltic expansion represented the mature phase of Danish statehood: a kingdom that could project power across a sea, hold conquered territory, and issue legislation that governed free peasants as well as nobles.

**The road to the Kalmar Union.** The Kalmar Union of 1397, which united Denmark, Norway, and Sweden under a single monarch (effectively always Danish), was the culmination of a century of dynastic maneuvering and the expression of a genuine pan-Scandinavian political vision. It was also a deeply contested arrangement that the Swedish nobility resisted constantly — the Stockholm Bloodbath of 1520 being the violent endpoint of that resistance. But the Union lasted for 126 years and shaped the political map of northern Europe. Its failure produced the modern separate Scandinavian states; its existence defined what those states were reacting against.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Ancient Days:** A Danish king surveys the Danevirke his ancestors have been building for a century. The earthwork stretches from the North Sea to the Baltic, thirty kilometers across the neck of Jutland. He rides along the top and thinks: this is what a kingdom looks like. Not a palace. A line.

**2 — The Viking Time:** The Great Heathen Army beaches its ships in East Anglia in 865 and begins a conquest that will take most of England within four years. This is not a raid. This is an organized military campaign with strategic objectives. The English do not have a word for what is happening to them.

**3 — The Christianizing of the Kings:** Harald Bluetooth stands at the font and allows himself to be baptized. His warriors watch. Some of them will convert because he has. Some of them will resist for another generation. Harald does not care about the religious question — he cares about the German emperor's army on his southern border, and the pope who can tell that army to stop. Christianity is the price of protection.

**4 — Cnut's Kingdom:** Cnut at the seashore, ordering the tide to stop, allowing his courtiers to get their feet wet. The story is told to illustrate his humility — he knows he cannot stop the sea. What the story actually illustrates is a king secure enough in his authority to demonstrate its limits publicly. That security is the product of a lifetime of successful governance.

**5 — Wendish Crusades and Kalmar:** Three Scandinavian queens meet at Kalmar in 1397 to ratify a union their parents had been negotiating for twenty years. Margaret I of Denmark has been the effective ruler of all three kingdoms for a decade. The union she ratifies will last until 1523. The idea of Scandinavian unity she embodies will last, in its complicated way, considerably longer.
