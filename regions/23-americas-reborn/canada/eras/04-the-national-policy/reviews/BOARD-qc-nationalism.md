# BOARD REVIEW — B-3: Quebec History / French Canadian Nationalism
## Canada Era 4: The National Policy and Its Discontents

**Domain expertise:** Quebec political history, French Canadian nationalism, Bourassa and *Le Devoir*, conscription crises from a Quebec perspective, the two solitudes.

---

### Factual Accuracy Assessment

**Bourassa's nationalism:** The chapter characterizes Bourassa's position as: French Canadians had no obligation to die for British imperial wars; if Canada was a sovereign nation it should have its own foreign policy; if it was a colony, it should admit it. This is an accurate summary of Bourassa's position as expressed in his *Le Devoir* editorials and his 1910 pamphlets. Pass.

**"Two solitudes" reference:** The chapter does not use the phrase "two solitudes" explicitly, but the two-track structure embodies the concept. The overview file credits Hugh MacLennan's 1945 novel for naming it. The chapter's implicit engagement with this tension is appropriate for an era chapter that must hold both tracks.

**Riel as Quebec political event:** The chapter states "In Quebec, the execution was a catastrophe" and quotes Laurier. This is accurate. Riel's execution produced the Liberal Party's breakthrough in Quebec in 1896 and the beginning of the English-French split within the Conservative Party that Macdonald had built. The chapter does not develop this political consequence, but it is a legitimate scope decision. Pass.

**Conscription 1917 from Quebec perspective:** The characterization of Bourassa's argument is accurate: this was a British imperial war that French Canadians had no obligation to fight. The chapter does not mention the Easter 1918 riots in Quebec City, when anti-conscription protests turned violent and federal troops killed four civilians. This event is within the chapter's scope and its absence is noted as a gap.

**The 1942 plebiscite numbers:** "Quebec voted seventy percent against; the rest of Canada voted seventy percent for." These are the standard figures and are correct within rounding. Pass.

### Cultural Representation Assessment

The chapter handles the Quebec dimension well within its scope constraints. The two-track structure (Indigenous experience / nation-building narrative) necessarily gives less space to the English-French dimension than a chapter focused specifically on that dimension would. Within the chapter's frame, the Quebec content is accurate and non-reductive.

**One concern:** The chapter presents Bourassa's nationalism entirely through the lens of the conscription crisis. His broader intellectual project — the defense of French Canadian culture, the critique of British imperialism, the Catholic social thought that informed his political theory — is not present. This is acceptable given the chapter's scope but should be noted for the Era 5 chapter (The Quiet Revolution and the Charter), where the Bourassa intellectual tradition becomes directly relevant again.

### Recommended Observation

The chapter mentions the Quiet Revolution as a future era but does not connect the 1867–1945 period to the social and institutional conditions that made the Quiet Revolution possible (the Catholic Church's dominance of Quebec social institutions, the Duplessis era's conservative nationalism). This is appropriate scope discipline for the current chapter. The connection should be developed in Era 5.

**Overall assessment:** The Quebec content is accurately handled within the chapter's scope constraints. The chapter correctly positions the conscription crises as revelatory of the English-French fault line without reducing French Canadian nationalism to mere anti-English sentiment. Board endorsement recommended.
