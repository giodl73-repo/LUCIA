# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe

**Chapter:** Papal States — Plenitudo Potestatis
**Stage:** PANEL-1
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

This opening does something unusual, and I want to name it precisely before noting where the work remains.

The unusual thing is this: the papacy is a Western institution, and the culture writing about it is also a Western culture — or at least, the culture that produced most of the historical writing about it. The risk in this chapter is not the usual risk of an outsider appropriating someone else's story. The risk is the reverse: a culture so deeply identified with this institution that the writer assumes its legitimacy without examining it, or presents its internal logic as self-evidently correct without making the reader EARN that acceptance.

This opening mostly avoids that risk. Innocent is presented from inside his own worldview — the plenitudo potestatis is not explained to us as a claim the papacy made; it is rendered as the structure of reality as Innocent understood it. "That was sufficient." These three words do everything: they give us the authority as Innocent experienced it, as fact rather than assertion. This is correct. This is inside voice.

The enumeration of the canons is where I watch most carefully. The canon requiring Jews and Muslims to wear distinctive clothing appears in the list without commentary, between the Fifth Crusade and the formal legal category of heresy. The text does not editorialize. It presents this as one item among many, which is how Innocent's council would have understood it — as an administrative measure in a regime of Christian governance. This is the right approach. The modern reader may feel discomfort. The text does not instruct them to. Good.

One passage requires attention. "From the cold northern seas where the Norse had barely finished converting" — this carries an implicit framing of conversion as progress, a ladder the Norse were ascending. The papacy itself would have used exactly this framing: the unconverted were outside the fold, the converting were entering it. So the framing IS inside the papal worldview. But the word "barely" is the author's word, not Innocent's. Innocent would have said the Norse were entering the fold. "Barely finished converting" measures their position on a timeline from pagan to Christian, which is an outside measurement applied to them, not a self-description of the Norse or the Roman perspective on them. A small revision: "from the northern seas where the conversion of the Norse was still recent" — which is how the curial mind would have categorized it — resolves the issue.

The presentation of the Jews-and-Muslims canon deserves thought at the chapter stage. The canon should appear in the chapter in the same tone as here: one legislative act among many, unremarkable from within the council's own logic. The modern reader's potential shock is not the chapter's concern. What IS the chapter's concern: does the text, at any point, linger on this in a way that uses it for dramatic effect at the expense of the council's own priorities? If the Fourth Lateran chapter centers this canon more than the council did, that is a distortion. If it gives it proportional space, it is honest. Watch for this.

**The question I'd push on:** Whose voices are absent from the Lateran? The bishops and abbots are present. The people whose lives the canons will govern — the parish priests who will now hear annual confessions, the Jewish communities who will now sew badges on their clothing, the Cathars in the south whose persecution is now formalized — are not yet present. The chapter does not have to give them equal space, but it should find at least one person whose life is shaped by what this council decrees. The plenitudo potestatis is most visible not in Innocent's perspective but in what it reaches.

**Verdict:** The voice is right. The culture owns its story. The specific revision (Norse framing) is minor. The chapter should hold this voice throughout and be careful not to let the Jewish and Muslim canons become either spectacular or invisible — they should be what they were: one aspect of a total system.
