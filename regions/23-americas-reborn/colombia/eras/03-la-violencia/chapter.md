# La Violencia
## Colombia, Era 3: 1930–1958

---

The bullet entered Jorge Eliécer Gaitán's body at 1:05 in the afternoon of April 9, 1948.

He was walking out of his law office on the Carrera Séptima — the main artery of Bogotá, the wide boulevard where government ministries stood alongside bookshops and shoeshine stands and vendors of chicha, the fermented corn drink that the city's respectable classes had spent a decade trying to ban. The city had that midday smell: diesel from the buses, grilled meat from the street carts, rain coming off the mountains. Gaitán had just finished a meeting. He was going to lunch.

He never arrived.

The shooter, a young man named Juan Roa Sierra, fired three times. Gaitán fell. The crowd on the Carrera Séptima — the vendors, the office workers, the passersby who had gathered the way passersby gather in any Latin American city at midday, slowly, warmly, with no particular urgency — turned. They saw what had happened. Someone grabbed Roa Sierra. The crowd grew. By the time anyone thought to take Gaitán to a hospital, the crowd had already beaten Roa Sierra to death on the sidewalk.

That took less than ten minutes.

What happened next took ten years.

The news spread through Bogotá the way news spread then: mouth to mouth, down the block, into the cafés and government offices, up the Carrera Séptima to the Plaza de Bolívar and out from there in every direction. In a room at the Hotel Granada, a twenty-one-year-old Cuban student named Fidel Castro was preparing for a youth congress. He walked downstairs and stepped into a history that was not his own. He watched the city come apart and later called it "spontaneous revolution" — meaning it as praise. Colombians would not have used that word.

Gaitán had been the one Colombian politician who spoke to the poor in language they recognized as their own. Not because he was poor — he had a law degree from Rome, a suit, an office on the Carrera Séptima — but because he said the thing everyone in the cafés and the market stalls and the barrio houses already knew: that Colombia was divided between the país político and the país nacional, the political country and the real country, and that only one of them actually governed. He said it from the stages of public plazas, in a baritone that people described as physical, something you felt in your chest. Two months earlier, in February 1948, he had led 100,000 people in silence through the Plaza de Bolívar. Not silence as absence — silence as statement. A hundred thousand people, no chants, no weapons, not a single word, to say that they had come to demonstrate that the violence was coming from the other side. The silence lasted twenty minutes. When it broke, the people dispersed in the same wordless order. The Conservative newspapers the next morning said nothing about it. The Liberal newspapers printed photographs of the square, 100,000 faces, all looking at the same sky.

After the shot, those faces came into the streets and screamed.

By nightfall, the center of Bogotá was burning. Rioters had torn apart the trolleys and used the iron rails as weapons. They had broken into the armories and distributed the rifles inside. They had set fire to the offices of the Conservative Party newspapers. They had stormed the prisons and freed whoever was inside. The fires were visible from the hills that ring Bogotá, and people in the barrios climbed to their rooftops and watched the city center glow orange and thought, some of them, that the revolution had finally arrived.

It had not. Or rather: what arrived was something for which "revolution" was the wrong word. Revolutions have programs. What spread from Bogotá into the valleys and mountains of the Colombian interior over the following years was older and stranger than any program — it was grief converted into habit, personal enmity raised to the level of policy, local scores settled under the cover of national conflict.

A young man from Aracataca, on the Caribbean coast, watched from a boarding house window and spent twenty years trying to find a language for what he had seen.

## La Máquina

The town of Líbano, in the department of Tolima, sits at 1,700 meters in the Andes, surrounded by coffee plantations. In 1948, its streets were unpaved above the central plaza. The church faced the alcaldía — the mayor's office — across the plaza the way churches always faced the seat of civil authority in Colombian towns, as if the two needed to keep each other in sight. The alcalde of Líbano in 1949 was a Conservative. So was the police chief. So was the priest. The largest landowners were Conservatives. The schoolteacher had been replaced — a Liberal, dismissed under administrative pretexts — with a Conservative. The credit institution that lent money to the smallholding coffee farmers required, by no written rule but by absolute understood practice, that applicants demonstrate their Conservative affiliation before their application was processed.

This is not to say the Liberals were powerless. They owned some of the coffee farms. They had their own cantinas and their own social clubs and their own mutual-aid societies. They knew which families were which. They had known for generations.

The Liberals and Conservatives had been fighting in Colombia for a hundred years. They had fought nine major civil wars since independence. The parties meant different things in theory — the Church, federalism, the role of the state — and in practice meant almost nothing at all, since every village had its color the way every village has its local saints, inherited rather than chosen, defended because ancestors had defended it, written into the landscape so deep that a farmer might not be able to say what the party believed but could tell you which families were Liberal and which were Conservative and what each owed the other. The political was personal. The personal was lethal.

After Gaitán's assassination, the Conservative government of Mariano Ospina Pérez responded to the Liberal riots not with conciliation but with consolidation. Police units in the departments were placed under Conservative political control. Armed groups, called chulavitas after the town in Boyacá where the model was developed, were organized under police cover to systematically kill Liberal leaders in specific municipalities. Later these became the pájaros, the "birds": hired killers who moved through the coffee zones picking off Liberal caciques, schoolteachers, union organizers, local notables. They were not random. They had lists.

In Líbano, the machinery worked like this: the alcalde identified Liberal families who owned property. The police conducted searches of their homes for weapons. The searches were always fruitful, because weapons could be planted, and everyone understood this. A Liberal farmer arrested had two options: sign over his land as a condition of release, or remain in jail while his family was left without income and his coffee harvest rotted in the field. Some signed. Some went to jail. Some — the ones who had brothers or cousins with access to a weapon — went into the mountains.

By 1950, the mountains above Líbano contained armed Liberal groups that had started as self-defense and become, by the logic of their situation, something harder to name.

## La Tierra

The particular intensity of La Violencia in the coffee zones — in Tolima, in parts of Caldas, in the Quindío — had to do with land.

A coffee farm in the mountains above Salento might be fifteen or twenty cuadras, cleared by one family's hands over a generation. The "colonización antioqueña" — the nineteenth-century movement of Antioquian peasant settlers south and east into the coffee frontier — had produced smallholder farms across Tolima, Caldas, and the Quindío rather than the large estates that organized most of the Colombian interior. A campesino family planted coffee trees from seed and waited five years for the first harvest. They had a document, perhaps notarized, perhaps not. The land was theirs. This was different from the hacienda country to the north and east, where the labor belonged to the landlord and the political color followed accordingly. In the coffee zones, small property-holders had something to lose — and the party through which they lost or kept it.

Conservative armed groups that displaced Liberal families from their coffee land were not acting irrationally; they were acting in perfect accordance with the logic of a system in which political power determined property rights in the absence of a state willing to enforce neutral law.

A woman named Rosa — from a family that had farmed coffee in the mountains above Salento for two generations — remembered, decades later, the morning the men came. This is the composite account of many women's testimony in the CNMH archive, assembled from multiple voices. She said: they came before the sun was up, which meant they had traveled from somewhere else and knew what they were doing. They were six men, with rifles. Her husband was in the field. Her two sons were still sleeping. The men told her to pack what she could carry. She asked where they were going. They told her. She asked about the house. They told her the house was already accounted for. Her husband came back from the field and saw the men and did not argue. They left before the sun cleared the mountains. They carried her grandmother's saint, a cast iron pot, and a cotton blanket. Her husband did not look back. She did.

The road she looked back at would have shown her the coffee plants in flower — white blossoms, a smell like jasmine mixed with something earthier — and the house with its mud walls and the roosters beginning to noise in the yard. She carried the image for fifty years. It did not leave.

The CNMH estimates that between one and two million people were displaced from their land during La Violencia. Most did not return.

## El Bandolero

From the mountains, violence answered violence.

The Liberal guerrilla groups that formed in Tolima and the other coffee departments in the early 1950s began as something coherent: men who had been displaced from their farms, or whose brothers had been killed, or whose political leaders had been murdered, taking up arms to protect what remained of their communities and to exact retribution. The leader of one of the most significant of these groups in the Quindío and northern Tolima went by the name Sangre Negra — Black Blood — though his given name, to those who knew him, was quite ordinary. He was twenty-three years old when he took command of a group of forty men. He had been a coffee picker and then a muleteer and then a Liberal party activist and then, when the machinery of violence was turned against his municipality, a man with a rifle in the mountains who understood that the government would not protect him and that no one else would either.

The groups that emerged from this period did not remain coherent for long. The original cause — partisan defense — was real, but the conditions of armed life in the mountains created new imperatives. A group of forty men in the Andes needed food, shelter, ammunition, and information about enemy movements. Communities that could provide these things were potential allies or potential victims, depending on their political color and the group's current situation. The boundary between armed political action and predatory banditry was not a line that could be maintained indefinitely in the conditions of La Violencia.

By the mid-1950s, the men in the mountains had moved beyond the partisan logic that had produced them. They had communities who needed them and communities who feared them, and the distinction between protection and predation was not always clear, even to them. The most documented of these figures was Efraín González, known as El Cóndor, who operated in the coffee zones of Quindío and Risaralda from the 1950s into the 1960s. González was Conservative by origin but became, in the late phase of his career, something that transcended partisan categorization: a regional power with codes of conduct, a network of informants, and a kind of folk legend that people in the coffee towns kept alive in half-told stories for decades after his death. He reportedly refused to kill children or priests, codes that other groups did not observe. He was killed in 1965 by an army patrol.

González was killed in 1965 by an army patrol in the mountains of Quindío. By the time of his death, the official phase of La Violencia had ended seven years earlier. He had continued because the mountains were where he lived and the rifles were what he had, and no letter had come from the government to tell him the arrangement had changed. The men with rifles were still in the mountains. The mountains did not change.

## La Gaitanada

In February 1948, two months before his death, Jorge Eliécer Gaitán led his people to the Plaza de Bolívar in complete silence.

The Plaza de Bolívar is the center of Bogotá, the center of Colombia: the cathedral on one side, the Congress on another, the Palacio de Justicia on a third, the alcaldía on the fourth. It is a civic space that has been used, across Colombian history, for proclamations, for celebrations, for executions, for riots. On the afternoon of February 7, 1948, it filled with 100,000 people who made no sound.

Gaitán had called this march specifically to demonstrate that the violence was coming from the Conservative side. There had been months of Conservative attacks on Liberal villages, Liberal schoolteachers, Liberal journalists. The response within his own party was to consider armed response. Gaitán said no. He said the response should be to show that the Liberal people were peaceful and that the killing was political.

One hundred thousand people came to show this. They wore black. They carried no weapons. They spoke to no one. Gaitán spoke once, a short address to the president, and then the crowd walked from the plaza in the same silence it had arrived in. The president, Ospina Pérez, received the address but did not appear.

Six weeks later Gaitán was shot on the Carrera Séptima.

What was destroyed by the bullet was not only a man but an argument: that the país nacional could make its claim through the democratic order, through the forms of civic peace, through 100,000 people in black standing in silence in the Plaza de Bolívar until the president understood what he was looking at. The bullet that entered Gaitán at 1:05 PM on April 9 was an answer to that argument, and the answer was: no.

The Liberals who went into the mountains with rifles had, in this sense, already received their answer. The silence of February 7 had been met with six weeks more of Conservative attacks and then, at the end, with the shot. The mountain answer was the only answer that remained.

## El General y el Acuerdo

On June 13, 1953, General Gustavo Rojas Pinilla arrived in power.

He did not arrive through election. He arrived through a coup so widely anticipated and so quietly welcomed by both Liberal and Conservative elites that it was less a rupture than an institutional exhalation: the political class, exhausted and implicated in five years of unprecedented killing, handed the state to the one institution that was not a party and could therefore stop what the parties had started.

Rojas Pinilla was a soldier and not subtle about it. He thought in terms of order and disorder, and La Violencia was disorder of a kind that embarrassed the country internationally and threatened the coffee economy — the economic foundation that both parties depended on. His solution was pragmatic: amnesty.

He offered the Liberal guerrillas in the mountains a choice. Come down, surrender your weapons, and no prosecutions. The government would provide land — not the land that had been taken from them, but land, somewhere. Many accepted. The armed groups in Tolima and the Quindío and elsewhere descended from the mountains in 1953 and 1954, in a movement the government photographed for the newspapers, guerrilleros emerging from the treeline with their families and their rifles.

It worked, for a while. The killing dropped dramatically. Some displaced families returned to their regions, not to their land but to the vicinity of their land. The coffee harvest of 1954 was good. Rojas Pinilla built public works — roads, schools, a television station. He cultivated the support of the popular classes that Gaitán had organized, positioning himself as the continuation of gaitanismo under military management.

Then he overreached. He attempted to extend his term, to build a political movement around himself, to convert the borrowed legitimacy of the army into the personal legitimacy of a caudillo. The parties, which had handed him the state in 1953 to stop the killing, moved against him in 1957. He was removed, peacefully, and went into exile.

The parties then sat down together in Sitges, Spain, in July 1957, and negotiated the agreement that would govern Colombia for the next sixteen years. The National Front — el Frente Nacional — was an extraordinary document of institutional exhaustion: Liberal and Conservative leaders agreeing to alternate the presidency every four years, to split all government appointments equally between the two parties, to make partisan competition impossible for a generation. The goal was to remove from the equation the incentive that had produced the killing — if both parties always govern, neither party has reason to eliminate the other.

It was signed. It was implemented. The killing stopped, mostly.

What was not signed away: the land question. The coffee farms that Rosa's family and hundreds of thousands of other families had lost were not returned. The displaced were not compensated. The men who had led the armed groups in the mountains were not integrated into a political settlement that gave them representation; they were amnestied into private life in regions that still had no adequate state presence, no courts, no protection.

The coffee crop of 1958 was harvested by people who were, in the main, on the correct coffee land, now correctly owned, because the violence had resolved the property question in the only way available to it when the courts were captured and the state was absent. The resolution was durable. The injustice was durable too.

## El Coronel y la Espera

In 1961, a writer from Aracataca published a short novel called *El Coronel No Tiene Quien Le Escriba* — No One Writes to the Colonel. The colonel is an old Liberal veteran of the Thousand Days War who has been waiting, every Friday, for fifteen years, for a letter from the government confirming his pension. The letter never comes. He waits anyway, because waiting is what he knows how to do, because the war that produced his pension claim happened forty years ago and the world has not yet finished with it, because Colombia is a country that does not finish with things.

The novel says everything the white papers and the commissions and the truth reports had not yet been able to say. Not because it explains more — it explains nothing, it is not a document of explanation — but because the colonel's Friday walk to the post office contains the whole of La Violencia's aftermath in miniature: the patience, the poverty, the dignity intact despite everything, the bureaucratic non-response that is both specific (no letter) and total (no recognition that the war happened at all), and the rooster the colonel refuses to sell because the rooster is all he has left of something he cannot quite name.

La Violencia ended, officially, with the National Front agreement of 1958. What did not end: the displaced who did not return. The land that had changed hands through violence and stayed changed. The regions of the interior — Tolima, the Meta, the Caquetá — where the state had never fully arrived and where the remnants of the armed groups remained, transformed now into something with a different name and a different ideology but the same roots: men in the mountains, in the coffee and jungle country, for whom the forms of democratic politics had never been available.

The FARC was founded in 1964, in the municipality of Marquetalia, in the department of Tolima. The founders included men who had been Liberal guerrillas in La Violencia and had never come down from the mountains when Rojas Pinilla offered the amnesty, because they did not trust it, or because they had nowhere to go, or because the mountain was the only world they had ever known how to govern.

They were still waiting. They had been waiting since 1948.

The colonel walked to the post office every Friday. No letter came. The rooster grew fat on borrowed corn. Outside the window, in the courtyard, October was arriving — the rainy season in the Colombian interior, when the Andean rivers swell and the coffee flowers close against the rain and the land that was taken and the land that was held and the land that was burned all look, from altitude, identical: green, steep, gorgeous, and belonging to whoever can hold it.

García Márquez published *Cien Años de Soledad* in 1967. The first printing sold out in Buenos Aires in a single day.

The Buendía family fought seventeen civil wars in the novel, all Liberal, all lost. Colonel Aureliano Buendía lost them all and survived them all and died old in his workshop, making little gold fish and then melting them down and making them again, because there was nothing else to do with time once the wars were over.

He had thirty-two sons, all named Aureliano, all killed before he died.

He did not finish with the wars. The wars finished with him. That was the only way they finished in Colombia — not through victory, not through resolution, but through the exhaustion of the men who had fought them. And then, before the exhaustion was complete, new men picked up the rifles and new names appeared in the coffee mountains, and the waiting continued, as it always continued, in the specific Colombian fashion: patiently, without hope, with dignity intact, for a letter that was never going to come.
