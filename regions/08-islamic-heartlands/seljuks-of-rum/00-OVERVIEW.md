# Salājiqa al-Rūm — The Turkish Sultanate in Byzantine Lands

We called ourselves the Seljuks of Rum — *Salājiqa al-Rūm* — where *Rum* meant Rome, the Byzantine world we had entered and were reshaping. The name declared our position: we were the Turkish branch of the Seljuk dynasty that had established itself in what was once Byzantine Anatolia, and we governed our sultanate from Konya — ancient Iconium — in the heart of the Anatolian plateau. The great Seljuk empire had fractured into regional dynasties after Malik Shah's death, and we were one of the survivors: smaller than our eastern kin in Khorasan and Persia, but positioned in a territory of enormous strategic and cultural importance.

Anatolia was the bridge — between the Islamic east and the Christian west, between the steppe peoples who continued to press through the Caucasus and the Mediterranean world that bordered it to the south. We governed this bridge for two and a half centuries, and in governing it we transformed it: the Turkish language took permanent hold in Anatolia during our period; the landscape filled with mosques, caravanserais, and madrasas that still stand; the Persian courtly tradition was transplanted into a land that had been Byzantine and Greek for a thousand years. Rumi — Jalal al-Din Muhammad Balkhi — wrote his Masnavi at our court in Konya, in Persian, for a Turkish sultanate, addressing a universal human condition. The paradox is the point: the Sultanate of Rum was the place where Turkish military power, Persian cultural tradition, and the transforming encounter with the Christian world created something new.

## What Defined Them

**The caravanserai network and the trade highway.** The Rum Seljuks built one of the densest caravanserai networks in the medieval world across the Anatolian plateau: stone rest-houses built at every day's journey along the major roads, providing free lodging, food, and stabling to travelers and their animals for three days. This was not charity — it was commercial policy. The caravanserai network made Anatolia the safe, reliable overland route between the Mediterranean and Central Asia, generating customs revenue and commercial activity that enriched the sultanate's cities. The Sultanhani caravanserai, with its grand portal and its great hall, is the physical expression of this policy in its most developed form.

**The Persian court in Turkish Anatolia.** The Rum Seljuk sultans governed in a tradition that was structurally Turkish but culturally Persian: their administrative language was Persian, their poetry was Persian, their court etiquette followed the Persianate model that every successor state to the Abbasid caliphate had adopted. The sultans patronized Persian poets and scholars, some of whom came from the east — from Khorasan and Central Asia, displaced by Mongol pressure — and found welcome at the Konya court. This was the mechanism by which Rumi came to Konya: a Khorasani family fleeing the Mongol advance, settling at the westernmost edge of the Persianate world, and producing the greatest Persian poet of the medieval period in a city that had been Greek-speaking fifty years before.

**Rumi and the Mevlevi order.** Jalal al-Din Rumi — Mevlana to his followers — spent most of his life in Konya, and his teaching there founded the Mevlevi Sufi order whose ritual practice — the *sema*, the whirling ceremony — became one of the most widely recognized spiritual practices of the Islamic world. Rumi's Masnavi, composed at Konya in the 13th century, is the longest Sufi poem in any language and one of the most widely read works in the Persian literary tradition. The fact that it was composed in a Turkish-ruled Anatolian city is the perfect emblem of the Sultanate of Rum's cultural position: the synthesis point where the Persian literary tradition met Turkish political power and produced something that belonged to both and transcended both.

**The Mongol vassalage and the Ottoman beginning.** After the Battle of Köse Dağ (1243 CE), the Sultanate of Rum became a Mongol vassal state. The sultans remained, but the real power was the Mongol *ilkhan*'s representative. This political fragmentation allowed the Turkmen *beyliks* — the small principalities of former Seljuk military commanders — to become effectively independent along the Anatolian frontiers. One of these frontier *beyliks*, organized by a Turkish chief named Osman in the northwestern corner of Anatolia facing Byzantine territory, would eventually become the Ottoman Empire. The Sultanate of Rum's dissolution was the Ottoman Empire's precondition.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Entry into Anatolia:** The Byzantine emperor Romanos IV faces the Seljuk sultan Alp Arslan at Manzikert in 1071 CE. The Byzantine army is defeated and the emperor captured — an event without precedent in Byzantine history. The Anatolian plateau, which had been Byzantine for a thousand years, begins its transformation into Turkish-speaking Muslim territory. The process will take two centuries.

**2 — The Golden Age of Rum:** The caravanserai builders are working on the road from Sivas to Konya. The Persian poet has arrived from Khorasan, displaced by the Mongol advance, and the sultan's court has welcomed him. Rumi has not yet met Shams of Tabriz; the Masnavi has not yet been written. The sultan is organizing the trade routes that make Anatolia the safest road between east and west. This is the sultanate at its height.

**3 — The Mongol Dependency:** The Mongol army has passed through at Köse Dağ. The sultan has agreed to the terms. The *ilkhan's* representative sits in the capital and reviews the fiscal accounts. Along the western frontier, Osman the Turkmen chief is organizing his warriors against the Byzantine towns. The sultanate is dissolving from the center while its margin is building something new. The Ottoman Empire is beginning.
