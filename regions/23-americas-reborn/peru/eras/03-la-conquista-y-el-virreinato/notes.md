# Notes — La Conquista y El Virreinato
## Production Bible — Peru Era 3

---

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** Ayni — Andean reciprocity — as the era's organizing cosmological claim. The Andean universe runs on return: you give to Pachamama, Pachamama gives back; you contribute labor to the community, the community sustains you; the Inca state takes mit'a labor and provides food, tools, and ritual protection in return. The colonial system borrowed the mechanism (the mita) while severing the return obligation. The engine is the structural breaking of ayni across two and a half centuries, building until the rupture at 1780.

**Mechanism:** Each colonial institution (the mita, the reparto de mercancias, the corregidor system, the tribute) is a one-directional extraction that violates the reciprocal cosmological contract. Each violation compounds the tension. The taki onqoy (1560s) is an early eruption — the Andean world registering the break. The Túpac Amaru II uprising is the era's terminal expression of the compounded breach.

**Terminal condition:** Premise non-recognition (v1.6 type 4). Condorcanqui's uprising operates from within the legitimacy framework of the viceroyalty — he petitions courts, claims legal status as a kuraka, invokes the laws of Burgos — and simultaneously from within the Andean cosmological framework. The Spanish do not recognize the Andean premises as binding. The uprising fails not because it was militarily weak (it surrounded Cusco and held the southern Andes for months) but because the colonial party does not recognize the cosmological claim as obligating. A pachakuti cannot compel a party that does not share the physics.

**Landing — culture's own persistence:** The taki. The quipu. The name itself. The chapter closes with Túpac Amaru II's name already moving through the Quechua-speaking world before the executions are complete. The culture's own transmission mechanisms — song, knotted string, oral genealogy — carry what the plaza was designed to erase. The landing stays inside the era (1781) and lets the persistence mechanism speak without importing later movements.

## Era Scope

**Temporal range:** 1533–1821 CE (the full viceroyalty period)
**Focal event:** Túpac Amaru II uprising, 1780–1781
**Structural anchor:** The uprising is the era's focal point; the prior two and a half centuries are depth charges that explain why the breach finally erupted there.

**Key dates within the chapter:**
- 1532 — Battle of Cajamarca; capture of Atahualpa
- 1533 — Execution of Atahualpa (garroted, July 26)
- 1545 — Potosí silver discovered
- 1570 — Viceroy Toledo's reforms institutionalize the colonial mita
- 1572 — Execution of Túpac Amaru I (beheaded, Cusco plaza)
- 1560s — Taki Onqoy movement: Andean religious resistance, songs as political weapon
- 1780 (November) — Corregidor Arriaga hanged; Condorcanqui becomes Túpac Amaru II
- 1781 (March) — Siege of Cusco reaches its height
- 1781 (May 18) — Public execution of Túpac Amaru II, Micaela Bastidas, family members

## Key Figures

**José Gabriel Condorcanqui / Túpac Amaru II (c. 1738–1781)**
Kuraka of Surimana, Tungasuca, and Pampamarca. Well-educated — he had read Spanish law, knew Inca history through the chronicles, had studied at the Colegio San Francisco de Borja for caciques in Cusco. Spent years petitioning the colonial courts to abolish the mita and the repartos. When the courts failed, he created his own tribunal. The name he chose was not impulsive — it was a studied claim about where legitimate authority resided.
Characterization challenge: He is described in Spanish sources as charismatic, literate, and strategically patient. His hesitation (which Micaela rebuked) suggests a man aware of the weight of what he was initiating. He is not a simple rebel; he is a man who tried every legal instrument available before moving to armed action, and understood the cosmological stakes of what he was claiming.

**Micaela Bastidas Puyucahua (1745–1781)**
Of Quechua and African descent (her grandfather may have been enslaved). Married Condorcanqui. Organized the uprising's logistics: correspondence with kurakas, timing, supply. Her letters to Condorcanqui are primary sources — urgent, impatient, strategically precise. When he delayed the march on Cusco, she wrote: "I am at the end of my patience. I will put myself at the head of the troops if you will not." She was executed the same day as her husband, before him — the Spanish forced him to watch.
Characterization goal: She must not be reduced to a supporting figure. In the operational reality of the uprising, she was its logistical commander. Her characterization must carry the chapter's full weight alongside his.

**Túpac Amaru I (Thupa Amaru, d. 1572)**
The last Inca of the Neo-Inca state at Vilcabamba. Executed by Viceroy Toledo in the plaza of Cusco. His beheading was designed to end the Inca lineage symbolically. The name Condorcanqui took in 1780 is a direct response to that execution. The chapter opens with this connection and must show how the 1572 execution created the 1780 uprising's legitimacy claim — the name as return, as pachakuti.

**Viceroy Francisco de Toledo (1515–1582)**
The architect of the colonial mita system. Arrived in Peru in 1569, spent five years touring the viceroyalty, reorganized the entire colonial economy around the Potosí silver mines and forced labor. The mita he institutionalized is the structural mechanism the chapter tracks. His reforms also executed Túpac Amaru I. He created the conditions the uprising addressed two hundred years later.

**Diego Cristóbal Túpac Amaru (cousin, d. 1783)**
Continued the rebellion after Condorcanqui's capture. Eventually negotiated a peace, was promised amnesty, was arrested and executed anyway. His story is the terminal condition's clearest expression: the colonial party does not honor the covenant when negotiated from Andean premises.

**Secondary figures to include:**
- Tomasa Titu Condemayta (kuraka of Acos, fought with the rebellion, executed with the family)
- Andrés Túpac Amaru (nephew, teenage commander of the southern theater)
- Bishop Moscoso (Catholic hierarchy opposed to the uprising, tried to excommunicate Condorcanqui)

## Narrative Arc

**Section 1 — Opening integration (from opening.md):** The corregidor's body, the name taken, pachakuti declared. Micaela's organizing. Potosí as ayni-violation at cosmological scale. (~800 words, the opening already written)

**Section 2 — The world the Inca built, and what replaced it:** The vertical archipelago. The mit'a before the Spanish — what it was, what it obligated. The quipu as information system. The Cajamarca encounter and Atahualpa's execution — rendered from the Andean side: the Bible put to the ear, the ransom room filled with gold, the garrote. Túpac Amaru I's execution. The slow installation of the colonial system: the reduccion (forced village consolidation), the reparto de mercancias (forced purchase of goods from the corregidor), the mita's transformation into forced mine labor. (~900 words)

**Section 3 — The songs that carried the rupture:** The Taki Onqoy — "dancing sickness" — the 1560s millenarian movement where Andean people began shaking, speaking in tongues, channeling the wak'as (sacred places and beings) that the Spanish had tried to destroy. The taki (song) as the political weapon the Spanish could not confiscate. The relationship between the Taki Onqoy and the later uprising: the same theological physics, 220 years apart. The quipu as counter-archive. The harawi as lament and political record. Show a specific taki at method level — how it traveled, who sang it, what it carried. (~700 words)

**Section 4 — The petitions and their failure:** Condorcanqui's years of legal activism before the uprising. He presented evidence of mita abuse to the Audiencia of Lima and was told to wait. He applied to have his Inca lineage legally recognized and was refused. The repartos — the forced purchase system where corregidores made fortunes forcing Andean communities to buy goods they did not need at prices they could not pay — had been complained about for decades. The system was not ignorant of its violations; it was structured around them. The threshold point: when does a man who believes in law decide that the law will not answer? (~600 words)

**Section 5 — The uprising and its geography:** November 1780 to April 1781. The rebellion spread faster than the Spanish expected because the network of sympathetic kurakas Micaela had been building was real. Mapping the southern Andes: Cusco, Puno, Arequipa, the altiplano down to what is now Bolivia. The kuraka network's unevenness — some joined, some defected, some waited to see which way the wind blew. The siege of Cusco: Condorcanqui's armies reaching the city's outskirts but not taking it. Why? The split within the Andean world itself: some kurakas and creoles sided with the Spanish; the urban indigenous population of Cusco did not uniformly support the uprising. The military situation is not the only explanation. The cosmological claim was not universally shared. (~700 words)

**Section 6 — The plaza of Cusco, May 18, 1781:** The execution was designed as spectacle. Condorcanqui, Micaela, their sons, their allies — brought to the plaza where Túpac Amaru I had been killed. Micaela was executed first: her tongue cut out, then garroted. The Spanish tried to quarter Condorcanqui by tying his limbs to four horses and driving them apart. The horses could not do it — his body held. He was beheaded instead. The body parts were sent to the towns that had supported the uprising, displayed at their entrances as warning. The chapter's task: render this scene from the Andean crowd's perspective. What the Andean witnesses saw was not the Spanish message. They saw a man the horses could not break. They saw what the colonial system required — total, spectacular, public annihilation — which told them everything about what it feared. The taki would carry this. (~600 words)

**Section 7 — Landing:** The name moves. Diego Cristóbal's continuation. The colonial authorities' panic response (banning Inca dress, Inca portraits, the quipu in some regions, even the name Túpac Amaru). The things they banned are the things they feared the most — and the things you cannot ban from a culture are its embodied knowledge systems. The harawi still sang. The quipu still knotted. The name, already traveling, cannot be quartered and sent to the four corners. The world is still turning. (~300 words)

**Total target:** ~4,900 words

## Voice Plan

**Register:** Discover (Linguistic-Nomadic primary) with Vertical/Mountain secondary.

The discover register accumulates — each paragraph adds what the language has been carrying. Quechua words arrive as revelation, not as footnotes. The vertical register ensures the prose moves through altitude zones (coast, highlands, Potosí underground) the way the culture moved.

**Pause mechanism:** The period. No em-dashes.

**Analytical delivery:** All analysis delivered in the same sensory register as narrative. The ayni-violation argument lives in the mercury patios, not in a separate analytical section. The cosmological claim lives in what the crowd felt in the plaza, not in a parenthetical explanation of pachakuti.

**Quechua integration:** Key Quechua terms arrive once, defined through context, and then used as they would be used — naturally. The reader learns the vocabulary by living in it. No glossary intrusions.

## Panel Pressure Points (from PANEL-1)

**Tuchman:** The closing of the opening ("two centuries of echoes") is slightly abstract. Fix: the chapter's landing should name at least one concrete resonance — the harawi still traveling, the name reaching specific valleys — rather than gesturing at echoes. Also: sustain the pachakuti frame as structural engine, not just opening device.

**Ibn Khaldun:** The internal division within the Andean world (why some kurakas sided with Spain) must be addressed. "Betrayal" is not an explanation. The systemic question: what competing interests made the coalition fracture? Address in Section 5. Also: distinguish Inca mit'a from colonial mita structurally — not just as different but as cosmologically different.

**Achebe:** The Taki Onqoy must be engaged as continuous with the uprising — same theological physics, not just historical background. The chapter must stay inside-voice even for the conquest sections (Atahualpa's execution must be rendered from the Andean side). The landing must stay within the era — no importing of MRTA or twentieth-century movements.

**Davis:** Taki must be shown at method level (specific song, how it traveled, who sang it). Quipu as counter-archive should be operational, not metaphorical. The kuraka network's epistemological unevenness — how ayni was felt differently across different communities — should be addressed.

**Kapuściński:** The opening's crowd is described but not felt. Fix: in Section 6 (the plaza execution), the crowd must be rendered sensorially — temperature, sound, what moved through bodies. The execution scene must be the chapter's most sensory passage. Also: the name-as-cosmological-act needs to land in the body, not just the mind.

## Source Plan

**Primary:**
- Micaela Bastidas's letters to Condorcanqui (multiple, late 1780–early 1781) — available in Spanish colonial archive, translated in academic works. Use for her direct voice.
- Felipe Guaman Poma de Ayala, *Nueva Corónica y Buen Gobierno* (c. 1615) — for the preceding colonial condition from an Andean insider's perspective. Guaman Poma is an Andean nobleman writing to the King of Spain in a 1,200-page illustrated letter the King never read. The letter is both a primary source and a structural metaphor for the chapter's premise-non-recognition engine.
- Pedro de Cieza de León, *Chronicle of Peru* (1553) — for the early colonial and conquest period.
- Colonial court records of Condorcanqui's petitions (cited in secondary literature).

**Secondary:**
- Charles Walker, *The Tupac Amaru Rebellion* (2014, Harvard University Press) — the definitive recent scholarly account; primary source for the uprising's military and political detail.
- Alberto Flores Galindo, *In Search of an Inca* (2010, Cambridge) — the long history of Andean utopianism and the Inkarri myth.
- Steve Stern, *Peru's Indian Peoples and the Challenge of Spanish Conquest* (1982) — kuraka networks, colonial labor systems.
- Jan Szeminski, on the Andean messianic tradition and the uprising's cosmological claims.
- Manuel Burga, *Nacimiento de una utopia* (1988) — the Andean utopian tradition.

**On the Taki Onqoy:**
- Luis Millones, *Historia y poder en los Andes centrales* — the most detailed treatment.
- Wachtel, *The Vision of the Vanquished* (1977) — excellent on the Andean internal experience of the conquest.

**Source gap:** The quipu's narrative function (as opposed to census/accounting function) remains undeciphered. The chapter should acknowledge this gap as the culture's own honesty: what the quipu carried cannot be fully read by outside systems. This is itself a statement about the asymmetry of archives.

## Open Questions

1. Did Condorcanqui intend to restore the Inca state, or to create something new? His correspondence is ambiguous. He addressed himself to creoles as well as Andeans; he did not always invoke Inca legitimacy explicitly. The chapter should hold this ambiguity rather than resolve it.

2. The role of the Church. Bishop Moscoso's opposition was decisive — he excommunicated Condorcanqui, which cost the uprising support among Christianized Andeans. But many local clergy supported the uprising. The Church was not monolithic. How should the chapter handle this?

3. The Andean cosmological claim vs. the legal-political claim. Condorcanqui argued both: he had legal standing under colonial law AND cosmological legitimacy as a descendant of the Inca. These were not the same argument, and the chapter should track where each was more effective and why.

4. The voices of the Andean women in the uprising. Micaela is central. Tomasa Titu Condemayta commanded troops. The uprising had female leadership that the Spanish sources undercount. Academic feminist Andean studies (Sinclair Thomson, Ward Stavig) have recovered this. The chapter should integrate it.
