# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Opening Review

**Chapter:** Norse/Vikings — Era 2: Vikingalond — The Viking Age Opens
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Whose Voice Is Telling This Story?

The Norse voice is telling this story, and it holds. The bonde's world is rendered from inside: his land, his cows, his standing at the thing, his freedom. "He owed no lord his service." This is not a description of Norse society. This is how a Norse farmer understood his own position. The culture's categories are the only categories present.

The Lindisfarne passage is the critical test, and it passes. Alcuin's voice appears, but it is clearly marked as an outside perspective ("writing afterward from the court of Charlemagne, called it a judgment from God"). Then the Norse perspective follows: "From the fjords, the story was simpler." Neither voice is privileged. The monks' horror is real. The Norse practicality is also real. The text holds both without choosing.

## What Works

"The reasons were the reasons that had sent his father west and his father's father before that." This is inside voice. It assumes the reader understands that expansion is a normal activity for these people, not an eruption of violence requiring special explanation. No apology. No exoticization.

The ship-building passage works because it renders Norse craftsmanship as knowledge: "split along the grain so the wood kept its strength." This is not admiration from outside. It is the shipwright's own understanding of what makes a hull live or die.

"A ship that fights the sea breaks. A ship that bends with the sea lives." This sentence belongs to these people. It carries the weight of a proverb formed by experience. I trust this voice.

## What I Would Challenge

**One moment of distance.** "The Nordmenn had been moving along the coasts and across the open water for generations before the monks at Lindisfarne learned to fear the word viking." The phrase "learned to fear" subtly adopts the monks' perspective in a sentence that should be entirely Norse. From the Norse side, the monks' fear is irrelevant. The sentence should stay in the bonde's world: the Nordmenn had been moving for generations. Period. What the monks learned or feared is their story, not this one.

**"The technology is not the explanation, but without it there is no story."** This is the author's voice, not the culture's voice. A Norse shipwright would not pause to comment on the relationship between technology and explanation. This is a moment of editorial self-awareness that breaks the inside perspective. Cut it or embed the idea in the narrative.

## The Question I'd Push On

The voice discovered here is strong. But it is working hardest in the ship-building and the bonde's world. When the opening moves to Lindisfarne, it briefly becomes a two-perspective narrative, which is honest but slightly diffuses the inside commitment. The full chapter must decide: is this entirely the Norse story, with the victims' perspective appearing only as the Norse would have encountered it? Or is it a dual narrative? I would argue for the former. The Norse tell the Norse story. The English tell the English story elsewhere.
