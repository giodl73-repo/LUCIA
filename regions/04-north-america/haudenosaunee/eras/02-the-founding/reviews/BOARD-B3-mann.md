## B-3 — Barbara Alice Mann — Board Review of The Founding (Kaianere'kó:wa)

**Domain:** Haudenosaunee women's authority, cultural authenticity, the dating controversy

**Cultural authenticity assessment:**

The chapter is one of the better treatments of the founding that I have seen from a non-Haudenosaunee source. The voice is largely correct — the Great Law is presented from inside its own logic, not as a curiosity or a relic.

**Specific findings:**

1. **Jigonhsasee's representation:** The chapter includes Jigonhsasee and correctly identifies her as the first person to accept the Peacemaker's message. This is important and often omitted. However, the chapter gives Jigonhsasee one section while Atotarho's transformation appears in two (the opening and the final section). The Mother of Nations deserves at least as much narrative weight as the man whose resistance the tradition highlights. The balance is acceptable but not ideal. In the figures.md, the treatment of Jigonhsasee is good — particularly the acknowledgment that "the tradition gives her a title rather than a biography" and that "the principle outlasted the person."

2. **Clan mother authority:** The chapter handles this well. The three-warning system, the dehorning, the matrilineal ownership of the longhouse, the women's control of food stores — all are accurately represented. The formulation "the power to create and destroy political authority was held by women, and the power to exercise political authority was held by men" is a correct and clear statement. The chapter avoids the common error of treating women's authority as a secondary feature of the system. It treats it as foundational. This is correct.

3. **Dating:** The chapter presents the dating controversy fairly, naming the 1142 CE hypothesis (Mann & Fields) and the later dates (Snow, Fenton) without dismissing either. The framing — "the traditions do not count in Common Era years" and "the traditions and the archaeological record answer different questions" — is respectful and accurate. I would note that the 1142 date is supported by the eclipse tradition and by the internal logic of the tradition itself, which describes the founding as occurring significantly before European contact. The chapter does not need to adjudicate, but it should not give the impression that the later dates are more probable simply because they are supported by a different kind of evidence.

4. **The Great Law as living law:** The chapter's closing emphasis on the present tense — "the people who are building" — captures something essential. The Great Law is not a historical artifact. It continues to be practiced by the Haudenosaunee. The chapter could be strengthened by one sentence noting that the Grand Council still meets at Onondaga, that the clan mothers still exercise their authority, and that the Great Law remains the governance framework of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy today. The present tense is not merely grammatical. It is factual.

5. **Terminology:** The chapter uses "Haudenosaunee" consistently rather than "Iroquois," which is correct. It uses the Kanien'kehá:ka spelling for the Mohawk and the traditional names for the key figures. This is respectful and accurate.

**Verdict:** APPROVE with 1 recommended addition (the Great Law as living, present-tense governance) and notes on balance (Jigonhsasee vs. Atotarho weight). No factual errors found. The cultural representation is respectful and substantially accurate.
