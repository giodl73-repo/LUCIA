# The Early Kingdom — The First Plaques

The wax came from the forest, and the copper came from the north, and the hands that joined them belonged to the guild.

Ihama was working at the long bench in his father's workshop on Igun Street, the street the Oba had set aside for the bronze-casters, which his family had held for as long as the family kept its count. His father held the master's place. His father's father before that. The count went back to a grant made by an Oba whose name was now spoken only in the ancestor ceremonies, whose face survived in a small bronze head on the altar of the inner room, cast by an ancestor of Ihama's whose skill had already, in that generation, been complete.

The plaque Ihama was modeling would show the Oba seated. Legs crossed in the posture of command. The coral-bead collar covering his chest in concentric rings. Two attendants flanking him, smaller because their importance was smaller, each holding the ceremonial sword pointed upward in the gesture that meant the king was present and being defended. Around the border, the river leaf motif, and in the upper corners the mudfish, because the mudfish moves between water and land as the Oba moves between the living and the ancestors.

The wax was warm. Ihama had brought it to temperature by holding the block between his palms for the time it took him to say, inwardly, the words his father had taught him when he was six. The words were not a prayer. They were the sequence. Core, wax, detail, channels, shell, drying, firing, pour, cooling, breaking, finishing. To say the sequence before beginning was to remind the hands what the hands already knew, and to remind the ancestors that the sequence was being followed with the care it required.

The core had been built the previous week. River clay mixed with charcoal dust and dung, shaped rough to the Oba's seated form, dried slow in the shaded yard. The wax would go on the core. The clay shell would go on the wax. The fire would drive the wax out of the shell, leaving the space the metal would fill. The metal would go in. The shell would cool and be broken. The bronze would emerge with every mark the wax had held.

Every mark. The guild did not permit a careless mark in the wax, because a careless mark in the wax became a careless mark in the bronze, and the bronze would stand on a pillar of the Oba's palace for the length of the dynasty, and the dynasty would look back at its own history through what the bronze held. The bronze was the palace's memory. A bronze that lied, or forgot, or was sloppy in its recording, was a failure of the guild, which was a failure of the ancestors who had granted the guild its place.

Ihama's father sat on a stool in the corner and did not look at Ihama directly. This was the teaching. A master did not stand over the apprentice. Ihama was in his fifteenth year at the bench. He had been permitted to model the figure of an Oba, which was not permitted until the hands had been judged ready by the guild head. But a man modeling his first Oba was still being watched, and the watching was done from the corner, without comment.

Ihama pressed the coral collar into the wax with a bronze stylus his grandfather had used. The tip was worn in a particular way. His grandfather's hand was in the worn spot. He set the first row of beads. Then the second. Then the third. Each bead a small press of the stylus, held for one breath, lifted cleanly. The collar had seven rows. A collar with six rows was the collar of a chief. A collar with eight rows was the collar of the Oba at the festival of Igue. Seven was the collar of the Oba in audience. The plaque was showing the Oba in audience. The count had to be exact.

Outside, the city held itself in the shape the earthworks had cut for it. The great walls ran in concentric rings around Benin City, raised by generations of corvee labor under the Oba's authority, and held inside them the streets laid out by the chiefs whose titles were as old as the dynasty. The markets opened at the first call. The palace drums marked the hours. The smell of the forge came from the blacksmith's quarter, which was not the bronze-casters' quarter, because bronze and iron did not share a guild. The Oba had separated them in the time of the founding because a kingdom that mixed its metals mixed its authorities.

In the inner room of the compound, the small bronze head of the founding-era ancestor stood on the altar. A drop of palm wine had been poured to it at dawn. Ihama had poured it himself. The head was looking, as it always looked, toward the door of the workshop, where a young man was pressing the seventh row of beads into the wax collar of an Oba who would stand on a pillar in the palace for as long as the palace stood.

The bronze is the memory. The memory is the kingdom. The kingdom begins here.
