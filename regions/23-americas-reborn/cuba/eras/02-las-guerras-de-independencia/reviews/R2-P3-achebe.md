# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Panel Review of Notes (PANEL-2)

**Round:** 2 (targeted)
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (P-3)
**Chapter:** Cuba Era 2 — Las Guerras de Independencia — La Manigua
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The production bible has taken the racial complexity seriously. Antonio Maceo is named as a central figure, not a secondary one. Ada Ferrer's *Insurgent Cuba* is the right source — it is the most rigorous account of the independence wars from inside the Afro-Cuban experience, and it will give the chapter the specific texture it needs to hold Maceo's story with the same weight as Martí's.

I want to add one more specific thing the chapter should get right about Maceo.

Maceo's military genius is well-documented and the chapter will not struggle to render it. What is harder to render, and what the chapter must attempt, is the specific nature of his relationship to the revolution's racial promise. Maceo did not fight for a Cuba that would remain racially stratified with the racial hierarchy merely rearranged at the top. He fought for what Martí called but Maceo embodied: a Cuba in which racial categories would not determine political standing. He was suspicious of any peace that did not deliver this — which is why he refused the Pact of Zanjón in 1878 with the Protest of Baraguá. He refused it not because the terms were militarily inadequate but because they did not address the question of abolition and racial equality. He knew what a peace without those conditions would look like, because he had lived inside Cuban society.

The chapter must show Maceo understanding his own position in the war's politics. He was necessary to the revolution's credibility (the mulato general's presence disproved the argument that independence was a white creole project) and he was also threatening to the post-revolution settlement that the Cuban elite and the American observers were already imagining. His death in December 1896 was mourned as a military loss. It was also — and the chapter should be honest about this without verdict — convenient for certain versions of what the post-war republic would look like.

**What to avoid:** The chapter should not resolve this into a conspiracy claim (Maceo was deliberately sacrificed). That is not the evidence. What the evidence supports is a structural observation: his death removed the figure most insistent on the racial terms of independence, eighteen months before the United States arrived to determine the peace. The chapter can hold this without attributing motive.

**The Protest of Baraguá** (mentioned in the notes' open questions) should appear in the chapter, not just as a historical note but as a character-forming moment: the young Maceo refusing the compromise that the older, exhausted commanders accepted. This is who he was. The chapter should show who he was.
