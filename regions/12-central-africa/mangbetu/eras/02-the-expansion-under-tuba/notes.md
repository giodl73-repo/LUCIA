## 1. Sources

**Primary:**
- Schweinfurth (1873) — essential, but describes 1870, not 1830-1860. Gap: approximately 10-40 years after Tuba's reign. Use for physical description of the capital, court structure, music, architecture. Apply source-gap technique: treat the gap honestly, use Schweinfurth's account as archaeological evidence of what existed, not as direct testimony of Tuba's era.
- Casati (1891) — later, additional detail on court culture.
- Belgian colonial records — for destruction end-point; illuminate what was ending.

**Academic:**
- Schildkrout & Keim (1990) — comprehensive. Primary reference for art, material culture, head-binding, music, social structure. Contains photographs and descriptions from 19th-century explorers including Schweinfurth.
- Keim (1983) — women and slavery; provides structural information about labor and status in the Mangbetu system. Critical for Achebe's note about subject populations.
- Vansina (1966) — broader Central African context; oral tradition methodology.

**Source Strategy:**
Apply source-asymmetry-as-technique (v1.5): almost all primary documentation is European, post-1860. The technique: Schweinfurth and others describe what they saw; their accounts reveal their own expectations and categories as much as they reveal the Mangbetu. The chapter should make the reader feel the source situation — European accounts providing physical detail while interpretive framework comes from within the Mangbetu aesthetic logic. The gap between Tuba's era and Schweinfurth's visit (c. 10-15 years) is navigable: courts change slowly; the physical forms (head-binding, coiffure, pluriarc, architectural tradition, ceremonial structure) are documented as long-established traditions, not innovations of the 1860s.

## 2. Key Figures

**Ne Tuba** (r. c. 1830-1850s CE)
The second major Mangbetu king. Under Tuba the state reached its greatest territorial extent. Schweinfurth's account of the later king Mbunza gives us the best physical description of what a Mangbetu Ne looked like and how his court functioned — Tuba would have presided over similar ceremonial forms, because the court's forms predated and outlasted any individual ruler.
- Role in narrative: the source of territorial and political authority; present in the chapter more as organizing force than as individuated figure (the record does not support full interiority)
- Honest limits technique: acknowledge that what we know of Tuba comes partly from inference backward from Schweinfurth's Mbunza account, forward from Nabiembali's foundation

**Nasakara** (composite figure)
Named in the opening as the aristocratic woman whose coiffure is being built. Not a historical individual by name — a representative figure drawn from the class of Mangbetu noblewomen documented in Schweinfurth's account and in Schildkrout & Keim's study of the visual tradition.
- Role: embodied presence of the court aesthetic; the person through whom the elongated head and coiffure practice become understandable from within
- Honest limits: named as a representative figure; figures.md will disclose the composite methodology

**Amunzika** (composite figure)
The craftwoman building Nasakara's coiffure. Representative of the class of women specialists who maintained the aristocratic aesthetic tradition.
- Role: knowledge-at-method-level for the coiffure practice (v1.3/v1.5 Evidence requirement)

**The Pluriarc Musician** (unnamed, representative)
Court musician. The deliberate non-naming is thematic: court musicians at the Mangbetu court were specialists whose individual identity was secondary to their institutional role.

**Schweinfurth (historical figure, shadow presence)**
Not present in Era 2 (arrives in 1870, after Tuba's death). Present in the opening as a temporal marker — the horizon at which the external record begins. Will appear in greater detail in Era 3.

## 3. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The court as total artwork — aesthetic display as the mechanism of political authority.

**Mechanism:** In the Mangbetu state, the ruler's legitimacy operated through a coordinated system of visible distinctions: the elongated head (marking noble lineage across generations), the coiffure (maintained through skilled specialist labor), the architectural setting (the great hall as amplifier of scale), the musical tradition (pluriarc as ceremonial presence), the spatial arrangements of the court (controlling who stands where, at what distance from the Ne). These are not decorations added to political authority — they ARE the political authority. The ruler who can sustain all of these simultaneously controls the terms of aristocratic identity for everyone in the state.

**Structural parallels (for analytical depth, do not state):** Similar court-as-total-artwork logics appear in Heian Japan (sleeve color as political signal), Versailles (spatial arrangement as governance), Byzantine court ceremonial. The parallel is useful for the author's understanding but the chapter speaks only Mangbetu.

**Terminal condition:** The court aesthetic depends on a continuous chain of transmission — mothers who know how to bind, craftswomen who know the coiffure tradition, musicians who carry the repertoire. Belgian colonial conquest (Era 4) disrupts this chain by suppression (head-binding forbidden), demographic destruction (the state's population reduced), and the elimination of the court itself (no Ne, no court, no patronage for the musical tradition). The terminal condition is not external shock alone: the court's very sophistication — its concentration of knowledge in specialist roles sustained by royal patronage — makes it fragile. Destroy the court, destroy the specialists; destroy the specialists, destroy the tradition. A more distributed knowledge system would have survived. This one did not.

**Book-level consideration:** Era 2 is the peak. The structural engine (court aesthetic as political authority) is fully developed here. Era 3 shows the court under the external gaze (Schweinfurth), which begins to refract the engine. Era 4 shows the engine's destruction. The landing of Era 2 should plant the seed of that fragility — the court's beauty is real AND it is entirely dependent on conditions that can be eliminated.

## 4. Section Plan

**Section 1 — The Court's Form (~1,200w)**
Opens with the existing opening.md scene (Nasakara, Amunzika, the pluriarc musician). Expands to: the great hall's architecture and scale (Schweinfurth's description applied carefully to Tuba's era), the spatial logic of the court ceremony, the avenue of palms as processional space. Knowledge-at-method-level: the coiffure process in detail (Schildkrout & Keim on the specific technique), showing that this is a transmission-dependent skill system, not an improvised practice.

**Section 2 — The Knowledge in the Hands (~1,000w)**
The pluriarc tradition: the instrument's construction (gourd body, eight strings, tuning), the training relationship between master musician and apprentice, what specific ceremonies required specific musical forms. Source caveat: the repertoire is poorly documented; apply honest-uncertainty technique where specific forms are unknown. The architectural knowledge: what building at two hundred paces required in terms of material sourcing, labor organization, technical problem-solving in a forest environment. The palace as accumulated expertise.

**Section 3 — The State Behind the Court (~1,000w)**
Tuba's expansion: territorial growth, incorporation of subject populations (Keim's work provides context on the relationship between the Mangbetu ruling class and the populations they absorbed and enslaved). The court's dependence on labor — who built the great hall, who cleared the palm avenues, who produced the food. Achebe's note addressed here: subject populations rendered with dignity and specificity, not as background. The political mechanism of expansion: what Tuba offered subordinate groups (protection, access to trade goods, participation in the court aesthetic at lower levels) and what he required of them.

**Section 4 — The Peak and Its Logic (~800w)**
The Uele valley under Tuba at maximum extent. What the "greatest territorial extent" actually consisted of — the geography, the trade goods, the networks of tribute and alliance. The court aesthetic as the empire's ideological adhesive: if the Ne's court is the most elaborate and powerful demonstration of the aesthetic system that all aristocratic families aspire to, then allegiance to the Ne is also participation in the system.

**Section 5 — Landing (~500w)**
Return to the chamber. The ceremony concludes. The music ends. The specific, living moment is held. The reader has been told, in the final lines of the opening, that this has twenty years. The landing should close on the court's own understanding of continuity — the mother-to-child transmission, the master-to-apprentice transmission — which is both the thing that has kept the court alive and the thing that is most fragile. The landing should let the culture's own persistence mechanism deliver the final weight.

## 5. Panel Pressure Points

**Tuchman:** Voice consistency across political/structural sections. Solution: keep the court aesthetic as the organizing frame for all sections. Military expansion = the court's reach. Tributary relationships = the court's logic applied outward. Every structural passage is anchored in a specific human act or physical object.

**Ibn Khaldun:** Terminal condition must follow from engine logic. Solution: explicitly trace how the court's specialist-dependent, patronage-sustained structure creates the specific vulnerability that colonial conquest exploits. Not "and then the Belgians came" — but "the system was organized in a way that a single disruption of the Ne's court collapsed the entire transmission chain." PANEL-2 addition: The aesthetic franchise mechanism — whether subject-group aristocracies could adopt modified versions of the court's visual language — is the key analytical link between court interior and state exterior. If Schildkrout & Keim document this, it goes in Section 3 as the chapter's structural spine. The court aesthetic as asabiyyah: the Ne's legitimacy extended outward through aspiration and imitation.

**Achebe:** Subject populations. Solution: Section 3 explicitly addresses the people behind the court — with the dignity that their labor and incorporation into the Mangbetu system deserves, without collapsing into either idealization of the court or external moral judgment.

**Davis:** Knowledge at method level. Solution: Section 2 goes deep on both the coiffure and the musical tradition as intellectual/skill systems. Acknowledge source limits honestly; show what we CAN know at method level.

**Kapuściński:** Exterior and landscape. Solution: Section 4 takes the reader outside the hall — the avenue of palms, the scale of the capital settlement, the Uele River as the landscape's organizing fact, the sound and smell of the forest on the clearing's edge.

## 6. Board Corrections Applied

- **Mbala reference (Schildkrout):** The decorative woven palm-leaf work on interior surfaces is documented as *mbala* in the scholarly record (Schildkrout & Keim). Chapter uses "woven palm-leaf panels" consistently with the description; the specific term *mbala* is a production-bible entry for any future revision that cites the material more technically.

- **Slavery/captive labor (Keim):** The chapter describes "women from captured or tributary groups" and refers to "captured or tributary lineages" in multiple places. The scholarly record (Keim 1983) uses "slavery" for the status of these incorporated women. The chapter avoids this term in accordance with the No Verdicts principle — the moral weight of the term belongs to the people who experienced the conditions, and the chapter's register does not position it to carry that judgment. Production bible: the labor conditions described are what the scholarly record identifies as enslaved labor within the Mangbetu state.

- **Territorial extent phrasing (Vansina):** Applied. Chapter now reads "the later oral accounts would name as the widest it had ever been" rather than asserting as established fact.

## Voice Register

**Court — Ceremonial/Observational:** layered, unhurried, precise. Each detail carries weight. Sentences lengthen when the court's deliberateness requires it; shorten at moments of stillness or ceremony's completion. No em-dashes (the court does not interrupt itself). The register is interior: we are inside the chamber, inside the court's logic, inside Nasakara's understanding. 

The v1.4 Voice Spectrum guidance on Court Cultures: "Layered. Observational. Unfolds slowly, because court life unfolds slowly. Every gesture has meaning. Every silence is political. The prose lingers on fabric, on light, on the angle of a glance, because in court cultures these things are power."

This register must sustain through all five sections, including the political/structural passages. The test: each paragraph should sound like it was written from inside the chamber, even when it is describing territorial expansion or tribute networks.
