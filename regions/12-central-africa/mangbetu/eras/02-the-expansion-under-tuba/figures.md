# Figures — Mangbetu Era 2: The Expansion Under Tuba

---

## Ne Tuba (r. c. 1830s–c. 1850s CE)

The second significant Mangbetu ruler, and the one under whose reign the state reached its greatest territorial extent. That is most of what the record can confirm with confidence.

Tuba's name appears in the oral historical accounts collected in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, placing him as successor to Nabiembali (the kingdom's founder) and predecessor to the rulers who were present when European explorers arrived in the 1860s and 1870s. The political chronology — consolidation under Nabiembali, expansion under Tuba, the encounter with external documentation under Mbunza and his successors — is broadly established, but the specific events of Tuba's reign are not recoverable with the same precision.

What the record gives us is the condition of the court at its peak, visible through Schweinfurth's 1870 account of Mbunza's capital (written roughly fifteen to twenty years after Tuba's era) and through Schildkrout and Keim's comprehensive study of the material culture. The forms that Schweinfurth described — the audience hall, the avenues of palms, the coiffure tradition, the pluriarc music, the elaborate court ceremonial — were not innovations of the 1860s. They were the products of a tradition already mature by the time European documentation began. Tuba stands at the height of that maturity's production: the state at maximum territorial reach, the court at the fullest development of its aesthetic system.

To characterize Tuba as a figure rather than a name requires inference backward from the tradition he presided over. A ruler who held a diverse state together at its peak extent — incorporating populations that did not share his lineage or language into a political order — was exercising authority through the mechanisms his court made available to him. The aesthetic system was not decoration. It was governance. To maintain it required deliberate commitment: sustaining the craftsmen, the musicians, the architectural knowledge, the coiffure specialists, all of them sustained by the court's patronage at the same time that the court was managing its expansion into new territories. This was administrative complexity of a specific kind, expressed in a specific medium.

We cannot give Tuba a face or a voice. We can see the thing he built at its fullest, and that is not nothing.

## Nasakara (composite figure)

The aristocratic woman in the opening scene is not drawn from a single historical individual. The name Nasakara, and the specific scene of the coiffure being built, are representative constructions — built from the class of Mangbetu noblewomen documented in Schweinfurth's 1870 account, in the later accounts of Casati and other explorers, and in the photographic and artistic record compiled by Schildkrout and Keim.

The elongated-head aesthetic is documented extensively. Schweinfurth's account describes the aristocratic women of Mbunza's court with the same profile that appears in every object the court produced: the anthropomorphic pots, the ivory carvings, the portraits made by explorers' artists. The coiffure practice is similarly documented — the ivory pins, the palm-fiber frame, the elaborate structure built on the extended skull.

What is less documented is the interior experience of women who were born into this tradition. The coiffure required daily maintenance. The elongated skull was permanent and visible. The aesthetic system that valorized it was also a system that marked its members as aristocratic and therefore as participants in a specific social position with specific obligations. Nasakara, as a composite figure, carries all of this — the skill of the tradition, the dailiness of its maintenance, the clarity of the position it marked — without the false specificity of an invented biography.

## Amunzika (composite figure)

The craftwoman who builds Nasakara's coiffure represents the class of specialists — women who maintained the aristocratic aesthetic tradition as a skilled practice. The knowledge involved in building a Mangbetu coiffure on an elongated skull was not casual: it required understanding of the skull's specific grain, the correct palm-fiber tension, the placement of ivory pins at intervals calibrated by feel. Amunzika, as composite figure, embodies this knowledge at the level at which the tradition actually operated — in the hands of a trained specialist who learned from her mother.

The chapter describes Amunzika's technique because the technique is recoverable from Schildkrout and Keim's study of the coiffure tradition and from the objects (surviving ivory pins, photographs of the completed structures) that document the practice. The detail is accurate to the tradition, not to a specific individual.

## The Court Musician (unnamed)

The pluriarc player in the opening scene is deliberately unnamed. Court musicians at the Mangbetu court were specialists within a tradition of institutional performance — their individual identity was subordinate to their role within the ceremony. The instrument they played is well-documented: the pluriarc (also called a bow-lute or arched harp, depending on the source's terminology) is described by Schweinfurth and survives in museum collections. The eight-string configuration described in the chapter reflects the documented Mangbetu form.

The training system described in the chapter — the two-year foundation before connected phrases, the additional years before ceremonial performance — is inferred from comparative evidence about court music training in Central African traditions and from the general principle that the sophistication documented in the surviving accounts implies a transmission system of corresponding depth. The specific details of the Mangbetu pluriarc training tradition are not fully documented in the available sources; the chapter applies honest-uncertainty where necessary.

The musician's anonymity is appropriate to the tradition. What was known about court musicians was not their names but their skill and their lineage within the musical tradition. The chapter honors that by letting the music speak through its practice rather than through biography.

## Georg Schweinfurth (historical figure, Era 3 role)

The German naturalist who visited Mbunza's court in 1870 and produced the most detailed contemporaneous account of the Mangbetu capital. His two-volume *The Heart of Africa* (1873) remains the essential primary source for the physical description of the Mangbetu court aesthetic, architecture, music, and ceremonial life.

Schweinfurth is present in Era 2 only as a shadow — the horizon at which external documentation begins, mentioned in passing in the opening scene. His role expands substantially in Era 3, when his visit becomes the chapter's organizing moment. The treatment of Schweinfurth here is deliberate: his account is the source through which the court's material reality is recoverable, but his perspective — a German scientist of the 1870s, operating within the assumptions of his time about Africa — is not allowed to become the chapter's interpretive framework. What Schweinfurth saw is used; what Schweinfurth concluded is treated as data about Schweinfurth.
