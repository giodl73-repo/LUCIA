# Rozwi (Changamire) — "The Restorers of the Plateau"

The Changamire dynasty of the Rozwi state is one of the great episodes of African sovereignty successfully defended against European intrusion. Changamire Dombo, the founder of the Rozwi state as a political force, rose from a cattle-herding background in the Guruuswa region of the plateau, built a military force that the Mutapa state's army could not match, and in a series of campaigns in the 1680s–1690s expelled the Portuguese from the plateau entirely — killing the prazeiros, destroying the estates, and driving out the missionaries. The Portuguese were not able to return to the interior plateau in force for well over a century. In this same period, the Changamire dynasty also defeated the Mutapa state and established the Rozwi as the paramount power on the Zimbabwe plateau.

The Rozwi were Shona-speaking pastoralists and the Changamire dynasty maintained and extended the stone-walling tradition of Great Zimbabwe and the Mutapa period. The Khami and Naletale ruins — elaborate stone-walled enclosures south and west of Great Zimbabwe — are Rozwi constructions, demonstrating a continuation of the architectural tradition with new aesthetic developments: the decorated stone panels, the herringbone and checkered geometric patterns inlaid in the walls, are distinctively Rozwi innovations on the Great Zimbabwe tradition. The Rozwi state maintained the Zimbabwe plateau's sovereignty until the Ndebele (Matabele) invasion from the south in 1834, which destroyed the last Changamire ruler and ended the dynasty.

Key places:
- **Khami** — the major Rozwi capital; UNESCO World Heritage Site; decorated stone walls
- **Naletale** — the most elaborately decorated stone-walled site; geometric panels
- **Danangombe** — another major Rozwi center; the late Changamire capital
- **Guruuswa** — the Rozwi heartland; the cattle country from which Changamire Dombo came

## What Defined Them

**The Portuguese expulsion as sovereign assertion.** The Changamire campaigns of the 1680s–1690s were not simply military victories; they were the assertion of the plateau's political sovereignty against the Portuguese encroachment. The prazeiros had established hereditary estates on Mutapa territory, converted to local customs while maintaining Portuguese political alignment, and created a system of economic penetration that the plateau's resources subsidized. Changamire Dombo's destruction of this system — the killing of the prazeiros, the destruction of the estates, the expulsion of the remaining Portuguese traders and missionaries — was the most complete assertion of African political sovereignty against Portuguese colonial presence anywhere in southern or central Africa before the modern period.

**The decorated stone walls as aesthetic statement.** The Khami and Naletale ruins exhibit a development of the Great Zimbabwe dry-stone tradition that goes beyond the purely functional. The panels of geometric decoration — chevron patterns, check patterns, herringbone courses — built into the outer faces of the walls are purely aesthetic additions to a structurally complete wall. They represent a tradition that had developed the technical mastery of the medium to the point where it could elaborate beyond necessity into artistic expression. The Naletale enclosure, in particular, with its multiple bands of different geometric patterns, is among the most visually complex pre-colonial stone walls in Africa.

**The mhondoro and the Shona spiritual landscape.** The Changamire dynasty's authority was maintained partly through its relationship with the mhondoro — the royal ancestral spirits of the Shona tradition, who communicate with the living through spirit mediums (svikiro). The mhondoro are the spirits of the founding ancestors of Shona polities; when a mhondoro speaks through a medium, it speaks with the authority of the founding generation. The Changamire rulers maintained their connection to the Shona spiritual landscape through this tradition, legitimizing their rule through the acknowledgment of the royal ancestors rather than through the foreign Christian ceremonies that the Mutapa state had sometimes used.

**The Mfecane and the end.** The Ndebele (Matabele) invasion of the plateau in 1834 — part of the broader Mfecane (scattering) caused by the Zulu state-building and warfare in the south — ended the Changamire dynasty. The Ndebele, under Mzilikazi, were moving north to escape Zulu pressure, and they occupied the southwestern plateau and established their own kingdom in Matabeleland. The last Changamire ruler was killed; the Rozwi state was destroyed. The stone-walled capitals were abandoned or occupied by the new rulers. The plateau's sovereignty passed to a new dynasty — Ndebele-Zulu in origin, Shona in territory.

## The Chapters

**1 — Changamire Dombo:** A cattle herder has become a conqueror. His first victories against the Mutapa state's forces were unexpected. His second victories were calculated. He knows now that the plateau can be his. He is not surprised by this; he has been planning it.

**2 — The Portuguese Estate:** The prazeiro has lived here for thirty years. His grandfather settled the land; his father built the stone house; he has cattle, workers, and a musket. He does not know that Changamire's army is a week away. When he finds out, it will be too late to matter.

**3 — Naletale's Walls:** The masons are setting the chevron course into the wall face. This course does not make the wall stronger. It makes the wall beautiful. The Changamire who ordered this decoration knows what it means to build beauty into stone: it means you intend to stay.

**4 — The Ndebele Arrival:** 1834. The Ndebele impi is crossing the Limpopo. The last Changamire has no force that can stop this. The dynasty that expelled the Portuguese from the plateau and held it for a century and a half has run out of time.
