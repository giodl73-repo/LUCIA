# BOARD Review — B-peru-3 — Jan Szeminski
## La Conquista y El Virreinato — Peru Era 3

**Role:** Quechua vocabulary accuracy, cosmological concept precision, harawi formal properties
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Overall Assessment

The chapter uses Quechua vocabulary with care and, in almost all cases, with accuracy. The cosmological concepts are handled at the level of precision this material deserves. The harawi formal analysis is the most impressive aspect linguistically — the claim that the call-response structure made the political theology invisible to Spanish ears is consistent with what colonial records show about which musical traditions were suppressed and which were permitted to continue. The harawi was treated by colonial authorities as funerary and therefore permitted; the records of anti-idolatry campaigns focus on the taki onqoy's shaking and speaking-in-tongues, not on the harawi itself.

---

## Specific Observations

**Pachakuti:** The chapter defines pachakuti as "the return, the reversal, the coming-back-around." This is accurate to the Quechua etymology: pacha (world/time) + kuti (return/reversal). The definition does not simplify to "revolution" or "overthrow." Correct.

**Amaru:** The chapter defines amaru as "the great serpent — the snake that moves between worlds, between the inside of the earth and the surface, between Ukhu Pacha below and Kay Pacha here." This is accurate. Amaru in Quechua cosmology is the serpent that connects the underworld (Ukhu Pacha) with the surface world (Kay Pacha) and the upper world (Hanan Pacha). The chapter correctly names two of the three world-levels. The absence of Hanan Pacha from the explicit naming is a minor omission but not an error — the chapter's context makes the vertical structure clear.

**Tupaq:** The chapter defines tupaq as "royal, sovereign, brilliant." This is correct. Tupaq in colonial Quechua carries connotations of nobility, luminosity, and royal legitimacy.

**Ayni:** The chapter defines ayni as reciprocity and develops it as "the deep grammar of how the world worked." This is accurate and is used consistently throughout the chapter in its Andean meaning, not reduced to social exchange.

**Harawi:** The chapter describes the harawi as "a lament, a love song, a song of longing for what is absent" and gives its formal structure as "a lead voice that states the loss" and "a responding voice that carries the return." This is accurate. The harawi is one of the oldest documented Quechua song forms, and its call-response structure is attested in colonial descriptions. The chapter's claim about dual legibility — the lead voice audible to Spanish ears as grief, the responding voice carrying the cosmological argument — is historically consistent with the pattern of which songs were suppressed and which were not.

**Taki Onqoy:** The chapter correctly notes that onqoy means both "sickness" and the Pleiades. This is accurate. The chapter also correctly dates the Taki Onqoy to "around 1564" beginning in "the regions around Huamanga." Accurate.

**Quipucamayoc:** The chapter describes the quipucamayoc reading by touch "in low light" and mentions "the color of the fiber, the type of knot and its direction of twist." This is accurate to Urton's scholarship. The chapter's claim that the quipucamayoc "continued to read in private, running the strings in firelight" after the colonial prohibition is historically plausible and documented in some regional records.

**One flag:** The chapter states that the quipu was "forbidden in some jurisdictions" after the uprising. This is accurate but understates the earlier prohibitions. Quipu prohibitions appeared earlier in some regions (associated with anti-idolatry campaigns in the seventeenth century), not only after 1781. The chapter's framing suggests the prohibition was specific to the post-uprising reaction. A small clarification would strengthen accuracy: "renewed" or "extended" rather than simply "forbidden."

---

## Verdict

Quechua vocabulary is accurate throughout. Cosmological concepts are handled with precision. The harawi analysis is consistent with colonial linguistic evidence. One minor flag: the quipu prohibition framing understates the earlier restrictions.

**Correction required:** In Section VII, modify "The quipu was forbidden in some jurisdictions" to acknowledge prior restrictions (seventeenth-century anti-idolatry campaigns) rather than implying the 1781 prohibition was the first.
**Correction required:** In Section VII, "within a decade" (regarding the corregidor abolition) — corroborating Walker's flag: change to "within a few years."
