## The Sensory Plan

The notes acknowledge the need for sensory grounding in every section. Good. Let me be specific about what each section needs, because the saga voice's spare quality creates a particular risk: spare prose that is also sensorily thin becomes flat prose, and flat prose puts the reader nowhere.

**Section I (The Keel):** Already strong. The boatshed has temperature, light, material. Add: the sound of the adze on oak. The smell of tar. The bonde's hall needs birch smoke, the warmth of the longfire on one side and the cold draft from the door on the other, the sound of the animals in the byre.

**Section II (The Fjord World):** This section must be the sensory heart. The fjord itself: the black water, the rock walls rising, the narrowness that is the whole explanation for everything. The farms: the thin strip of arable land between cliff and shore, the stone walls, the grass that grows in the short summer and dies back in the long dark. The thing assembly: open-air, the sound of voices carrying across flat ground, the wind. The blot: blood on the altar stone, the smell of the sacrifice, the smoke rising.

**Section III (The Ship):** Navigation must be physical. The feel of the wind shifting. The spray on the bow. The way a longship rides differently in open water than in a fjord. The sound of the sail filling. The oars in the water, the rhythm of forty men pulling together. The navigator does not "use" the wind. He feels it on his face and reads it.

**Section IV (The Raids):** This is where the body matters most. The approach to a coast at dawn. The beach under the keel. The weight of weapons. The monks' stone walls. Silver plate heavy in the hand. The return voyage: loaded ship sitting lower in the water, the crew tired, the wind behind them. Each raid must be a place, not just a name and a date.

**Section V (The Eastern Road):** The portage. Forty men carrying a ship overland between rivers. The weight of it. The forest on both sides, birch and pine, the mosquitoes in summer, the mud. The river route: the current, the shallows, the different water. The market at the other end: the sounds and smells of a place where Norse furs meet Abbasid silver. The dirham in a Norse hand: small, silver, covered in writing they could not read.

**Section VI (Landing):** The ship hauled up on the beach. The tarring. The hull cooling. The winter coming. A body at rest after a season of motion.

The saga voice can carry all of this. Spare does not mean empty. "The wind had been fair" is four words, and it puts you on the ship. Every sentence in this chapter should do that much work.
