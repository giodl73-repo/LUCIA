# Ubwami bw'u Rwanda — "The Kingdom of the Hills"

Rwanda is a country of a thousand hills — ibikingi, the terraced ridges that descend from the central plateau, each one farmed in an elaborate system of terraced cultivation that has been built and maintained across centuries. The hills are not simply geography; they are the organizing principle of a political system in which the mwami (king) allocated access to hills and pasture through a network of chieftains, in which cattle — the primary form of wealth — moved between social categories through the ubuhake clientship system, and in which the elaborate oral traditions of the court poets preserved the political history and genealogy of the kingdom in forms that required years of training to master. The hills, the cattle, and the poetry are the three pillars of the Rwanda the kingdom made.

The Nyiginya dynasty that ruled Rwanda from approximately the seventeenth century onward built one of the most centralized states in the Great Lakes region, progressively integrating local powers into a administrative hierarchy centered on the mwami and extending the kingdom's territory to encompass most of what is now Rwanda by the nineteenth century. The court of the mwami was a sophisticated institution: the dynastic poets (ibyivugo) composed in strict metrical forms; the royal cattle (inyambo) were trained to perform at court ceremonies; the governance system allocated military, cattle, and agricultural chieftaincies as separate offices over the same territory — a deliberate distribution of power that prevented any single chief from controlling all the resources of a given area. This was not traditional stasis; it was political engineering.

## What Defined Them

**The mwami and the cattle allocation.** The mwami's power rested on his control of cattle distribution through the ubuhake system — a clientship in which a patron gave a client cattle in exchange for service and political loyalty. At the top of this system was the mwami, who distributed cattle to his chiefs, who distributed cattle to their clients, who were bound to service through these obligations. Cattle were not simply wealth; they were political relationships made material. The inyambo — the long-horned royal cattle, trained to perform at ceremonies — were the living expression of royal power, tended by specialists and paraded at court events. A mwami who controlled the cattle supply controlled the political system.

**The ibyivugo and the oral historical archive.** The court poets of Rwanda maintained an oral archive of extraordinary complexity. The ibyivugo (dynastic poetry) composed in strict meters preserved the names, deeds, and cattle of each king across generations. The abiru (esoteric specialists) maintained the ritual code of the kingdom in oral texts that could only be spoken in specific circumstances. The imigani (proverbs) encoded political and moral wisdom in compressed forms. Together these oral genres constituted a political library — the governance documentation of the kingdom maintained in trained human memory rather than in writing. The loss of large portions of this archive in the political violence of the 1990s represents one of the most profound cultural losses in recent African history.

**The administrative tripartite structure.** The Rwanda kingdom's administrative genius was the deliberate distribution of authority over the same territory into three separate chieftaincies: the chief of the men (the military and political authority), the chief of the cattle (managing the pasture and cattle allocation), and the chief of the land (managing the agricultural economy and land access). These three chiefs held authority over the same hill or district but governed different aspects of it, and they checked each other's power. This was a sophisticated system for preventing the concentration of power at the local level that might threaten the central authority. It was also complex to administer — which is why the court maintained extensive records through the oral specialists.

**The Hutu-Tutsi categories and the colonial distortion.** The categories of Hutu, Tutsi, and Twa in pre-colonial Rwanda were social categories related to occupation and status — primarily defined by whether one's livelihood was primarily agricultural (Hutu), pastoral (Tutsi), or forest-based (Twa) — rather than fixed racial categories. Movement between categories was possible through accumulation of cattle (a Hutu who acquired cattle could become Tutsi) or loss of cattle (a Tutsi who lost cattle could become Hutu). The Belgian colonial administration's introduction of identity cards in the 1930s, categorizing all Rwandans into fixed Hutu, Tutsi, or Twa categories on the basis of physical characteristics and cattle holdings, transformed a flexible social classification into a rigid racial system. This transformation — together with the church's preferential treatment of Tutsi in education and the subsequent political reversal in which Hutu became the dominant force in the independence period — created the structural conditions for the genocide of 1994. The pre-colonial categories and the colonial categories are not the same thing, and conflating them falsifies both.

**The 1994 genocide and the historical weight.** This overview cannot be written without acknowledging the genocide of 1994, in which approximately 800,000 Tutsi and moderate Hutu were killed in one hundred days — the fastest genocide in recorded history. The event is not an aberration to be noted and set aside; it is the terminus of a historical arc that runs through the kingdom, the colonial transformation of its social categories, the independence revolution, and the decades of political violence that preceded 1994. The Rwanda of today — the Rwandan Patriotic Front's reconciliation state, the contested question of how to write a history that acknowledges both the pre-colonial kingdom and the genocide — is living inside this history. This chronicle is not the place to resolve these questions, but it would be dishonest not to acknowledge them.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Hill:** A farmer on the central Rwanda hills is working the terraced ridge. Above her, the cattle are moving along the pasture track. Below her, the banana plantation catches the morning mist. Both activities feed the same household. The hill is the kingdom's basic unit.

**2 — The Inyambo:** The royal long-horned cattle are performing at the mwami's court. They have been trained for years to move on command. The court poets are composing praise verses for the cattle simultaneously. This is not frivolous; this is the kingdom displaying its wealth and its aesthetic standards simultaneously.

**3 — The Abiru's Archive:** An abiru specialist is teaching his apprentice. The text being transmitted is the ritual code — the laws of the kingdom encoded in verse. It cannot be written. It must be memorized exactly. The apprentice recites. The specialist corrects. The governance of the kingdom lives in this exchange.

**4 — The Identity Card:** A Belgian colonial administrator is entering "Tutsi" on a card. The person in front of him has more than ten cattle; the rule says that means Tutsi. The category is being fixed in a way it has never been fixed before. The card will outlast the administrator, the mandate, and the Belgian presence in Rwanda. It will be found in 1994.
