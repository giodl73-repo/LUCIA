# BOARD REVIEW — B-2: Canadian Political History / Confederation Era
## Canada Era 4: The National Policy and Its Discontents

**Domain expertise:** Canadian constitutional history, Confederation-era politics, English-French relations, conscription crises, Macdonald-era policy.

---

### Factual Accuracy Assessment

**Confederation (1867):** The chapter states Canada confederated as "four provinces — Ontario, Quebec, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia." Correct. The British North America Act (now the Constitution Act 1867) united these four provinces on July 1, 1867. Pass.

**The National Policy:** The chapter characterizes the National Policy as "tariffs, settlement, railway." This is the standard historiographical shorthand for Macdonald's three-part economic program (protective tariffs, western settlement, CPR). Pass.

**CPR completion:** "completed in November 1885." Correct. The Last Spike at Craigellachie, British Columbia, was driven on November 7, 1885. Pass.

**Riel and Manitoba:** The claim that Riel "negotiated Manitoba's entry into Confederation" is accurate in substance. The Manitoba Act (1870) resulted from negotiations between the Provisional Government of Assiniboia (headed by Riel) and the Dominion, and Manitoba entered as a bilingual province. The claim that this was "the first and only time an indigenous-led negotiation produced a lasting constitutional result" is defensible, though some historians would note that the Métis land guarantees in the Manitoba Act were largely defeated in implementation. The chapter's next sentence acknowledges this ("Then the implementation of those guarantees failed"). Pass.

**Conscription 1917:** The Military Service Act was passed in August 1917. Quebec's opposition is accurately characterized. The 1942 plebiscite and the 1944 implementation of overseas conscription are correctly dated. The chapter correctly notes that the 1942 result was split: Quebec 70% against, rest of Canada 70% for. Pass.

**Vimy Ridge:** "April 1917." Correct: the Battle of Vimy Ridge was April 9–12, 1917. The characterization of Vimy as "four Canadian divisions attacking together for the first time" is correct. The claim that it "became a founding myth of Canadian nationhood" reflects the historiographical consensus on how Vimy has been memorialized, though the mythologizing was retrospective (the "birth of a nation" framing emerged primarily after the battle, not during it). Acceptable. Pass.

**Bourassa:** "Henri Bourassa (1868–1952)" and "Founder of *Le Devoir* in 1910." Correct. His grandfather was indeed Louis-Joseph Papineau (leader of the 1837–38 Patriote rebellion). Pass.

**McClung and personhood:** "won them personhood under the law in 1929." The Persons Case (Edwards v. Canada) was decided by the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in London on October 18, 1929, ruling that women were "persons" under Section 24 of the British North America Act and therefore eligible for Senate appointment. Pass.

**Statute of Westminster 1931:** The chapter briefly references Canada's "emergence from WWI with Vimy Ridge as national myth" but does not mention the Statute of Westminster (1931) or the broader inter-war consolidation of Canadian autonomy. This is a gap in the nation-building track. The chapter covers 1867–1945 and the Statute of Westminster is the key formal marker of Canadian independence from Britain during this period. The board recommends a brief reference in Section V or the landing.

**Laurier on Riel:** "Had I been born on the banks of the Saskatchewan, I would myself have shouldered a musket." This is the standard paraphrase of Laurier's speech; the actual speech was in French and there are slightly varying translations. The chapter's version is within the acceptable range of the paraphrase tradition. Pass.

### Structural Assessment

The "1885 as structural hinge" is the chapter's most significant structural contribution. The year 1885 is indeed the year of the CPR completion, Riel's execution, the Chinese Head Tax, and the anti-ceremony amendments to the Indian Act. The convergence in a single year is factually correct and historically significant. The chapter makes good use of this.

The chapter's characterization of the conscription crisis as "a fracture... that would not fully close" is consistent with the historiographical consensus on the English-French fault line. Pass.

### Recommended Addition

The Statute of Westminster (1931) deserves one sentence in the landing or in Section V. It is the formal legal marker of Canadian sovereignty and belongs to the nation-building track that the chapter is maintaining. Its absence is noticeable to a specialist reader.

**Overall assessment:** The Canadian political history content is accurate and well-selected. The chapter's central analytical claim — that the National Policy required and concealed the suppression of Indigenous peoples as a structural condition — is consistent with the best current historiography on this period (Daschuk, Miller, Milloy). Board endorsement recommended with the noted suggestion.
