# Figures — O Infante e o Atlântico (1415–1460)

---

## Henrique, Infante of Portugal (1394–1460)

He was the third son, which meant the throne was not his. Duarte, the eldest, would be king. Pedro, the second, would be a power behind the throne. Henrique had to make himself otherwise. He chose the sea — not as a sailor but as an organizer, a patron, a director. He was twenty-one when he went to Ceuta, and the crossing of the Strait of Gibraltar and the conquest of the city showed him what lay beyond the maps: a world that was larger and more complex than the charts suggested, and that could be reached by water.

He was not the Navigator of the later legend — the solitary genius in his tower, drawing charts by candlelight. Peter Russell's biography, which strips the legend carefully, reveals a more interesting figure: a man of extraordinary patience and organizational capacity, deeply religious, personally austere, administratively effective, and absolutely convinced that what he was doing served God. He wore a hair shirt. He was celibate. He gave money away in quantities that alarmed his household. He was also the Grand Master of the Order of Christ, which meant he controlled revenues from lands across Portugal and could channel them into the African program without requiring the agreement of the king or the Cortes. This gave him a financial independence unusual for a prince.

He was responsible for the slave trade. This needs to be stated directly because the legend of Henry the Navigator has sometimes separated the navigational achievement from its human cost, as though they were two different projects. They were not. Henry authorized the first raid on the Mauritanian coast in 1441. He accepted the fifth of the 235 enslaved people brought to Lagos in 1444 — forty-seven people became his property that day. He petitioned the pope for the right to baptize and enslave "Saracens and pagans" encountered in the African expeditions. The Crusade ideology was genuine: he believed, apparently sincerely, that baptism was a good that offset the violence of capture. Zurara records him looking at the enslaved Africans at Lagos and saying that their souls were saved even if their bodies were in bondage. He was not performing this belief. He held it.

He died without seeing what he built reach its destination. The coast had not yet curved east. India was still a hypothesis. What he left was an institution, a method, a fleet, and a trained corps of pilots who knew how to go south and come back. That was enough.

## Gomes Eanes de Zurara (c. 1410–c. 1474)

Zurara was Henry's court chronicler, appointed Keeper of the Royal Archives in 1451. He was a man whose job was to praise the deeds of his patron and the glory of the Portuguese enterprise. He did this job. He also did something else: he looked.

His *Crónica de Guiné*, completed around 1453, is the primary source for the African expeditions from 1441 onward. It is a document in praise of Henry and the Portuguese program, and within that frame it records things that its frame would prefer to minimize: the terror of the first raids, the grief at the Lagos field, the sound of the people calling to each other across the separation. Zurara does not draw the obvious conclusions from what he records. He weeps and continues. He describes suffering and praises the enterprise that produced it. He is not lying. He is holding, without resolution, two things his framework cannot reconcile.

This makes him an unusual figure in the history of European colonialism: a witness who could not look away, who recorded what he saw with more specificity than his ideology required, and who produced in doing so a document that would be used five centuries later to understand what the people he was watching had experienced. He is not a hero of this history. He is something more interesting: an honest chronicler of a moment he did not fully understand.

## Gil Eanes (fl. 1434)

He had turned back before. Zurara records this without condemnation — the pilots who had tried to round Cape Bojador and retreated had done so because the cape was genuinely dangerous, the currents strong, the return winds unreliable, the coast unfamiliar. They were not cowards. They were navigators calculating risk.

In 1434, Eanes tried again. He went further west into the open Atlantic before turning south, avoiding the coast near the cape where the shoaling was worst. He rounded the cape. Beyond it, as Zurara records, was more coast. More sea. The world continued. He brought back roses from a beach beyond the cape to prove he had been ashore. The roses were a practical demonstration: plants grew there, the land was not poisoned, the zone of terror the sailors had imagined did not exist.

His later career is sparsely documented. He returned to the Guinea coast in subsequent expeditions. He was a working pilot, not a legendary figure — the legend came later, when the opening of the African coast made Cape Bojador retrospectively significant. At the time, it was one headland beyond another, made important by fear and now made important by the removal of fear.

## Nuno Tristão (fl. 1441–c. 1446)

Tristão was the pilot who commanded the 1441 expedition that brought the first enslaved Africans to Portugal. He was also the pilot who pushed furthest down the African coast during this period, reaching the Cape Verde peninsula and the mouth of the Gambia River. He died around 1446, killed by poisoned arrows on the Gambia River, shot by people who had read the intentions of the Portuguese caravels correctly and prepared accordingly.

His trajectory is the trajectory of the program in miniature: first contact, trade, raid, increasing resistance, death at the hands of people defending themselves. He was not a villain in his own self-understanding. He was a pilot doing what his patron required, going where the program sent him. The people who shot him on the Gambia River were doing what their own understanding required.

## The 235 at Lagos (August 6, 1444)

They do not have names in the surviving record. Zurara describes them — their grief, their postures, the mothers holding children, the fathers on their knees — but does not name them. They came from the Mauritanian and Senegambian coast, from communities that had their own names, their own kinship structures, their own political relationships with the Jolof state and with each other. Those names were not preserved.

Forty-seven of them became Henrique's property by right of the royal fifth. The rest were distributed among the merchants who had funded the voyage. Some of them, the chronicle notes, were eventually baptized. Some of them, over the following years, were freed by their owners and entered the population of Lisbon as freedmen and freedwomen. The social history of black Africans in Portugal — the freedmen communities, the manumission rates, the occupations — has been documented by A.C. de C.M. Saunders, and it shows a more complex social reality than simple enslavement would suggest: a small but significant population that navigated Portuguese society with varying degrees of autonomy. This does not mitigate what happened at Lagos. It complicates it, as the truth usually does.

They are the shadow that the opening deferred and the chapter tried to hold. The chart was wrong. The chart was being corrected, one expedition at a time, by the same men who brought these 235 people to the field outside Lagos and divided them like cargo. Both things are true. The chapter does not resolve the tension, because the tension is not resolvable. It is the condition of this era.

## Alvise Cadamosto (c. 1432–c. 1488)

A Venetian in Portuguese service — his presence is itself a fact about the program. Henry was not a Portuguese nationalist in the narrow sense; he employed the best available navigators regardless of origin. Cadamosto came to Henrique's service around 1455 and made two voyages to the Senegambian coast, reaching the Gambia River and (probably) the Cape Verde Islands.

His account, written in Venetian and later published, is one of the most candid documents of early Portuguese-African contact. He describes the Wolof merchants he traded with as sophisticated traders who drove hard bargains. He describes the interpreters — Africans who had already learned enough Portuguese to facilitate exchange — as essential to the transactions. He describes the goods: cloth from Flanders, copper vessels, silver, horses (the horses particularly valuable in a region where the tsetse fly made cavalry difficult). He describes what the Wolof wanted and what the Portuguese offered and what each side thought of the exchange. He is not Zurara; he is not writing to praise. He is writing to report, and his report shows a coast that was already, within fifteen years of first contact, being reshaped by the encounter.

His presence in the record — a Venetian writing in his own language about a Portuguese enterprise he witnessed as a participant — is its own kind of source asymmetry. The voices we have are European. The coast is there in all of them, describable, encountered, present. The people of the coast speak through the gaps in the documents, through the trading responses Cadamosto records, through the arrows that killed Tristão on the Gambia, through the grief Zurara saw at Lagos.
