# Figures — Era 1: Deep Time in the Forest
## Equatorial Forest Peoples

---

## On the Figures in This Chapter

This chapter covers a period of at least forty thousand years, during which no written records were produced by the forest peoples themselves. The primary sources for this era are ethnographic — accounts produced by outside observers in the twentieth century, principally Colin Turnbull's fieldwork with the Mbuti (Ituri Forest, 1950s–1980s), Serge Bahuchet's fieldwork with the Aka (Lobaye region, CAR, 1970s–1990s), and Jerome Lewis's fieldwork with the Mbendjele BaYaka (Republic of Congo, 1990s–2000s). The figures in this chapter are composite figures: not named individuals from the historical record, but embodied representatives of what the ethnographic record documents.

This is disclosed here rather than in the text because disclosure within the chapter would require the chapter to step outside its voice to address the reader as a reader, which the chapter has chosen not to do. The composite method follows the v1.4 amendment on Archaeological Figures: a figure does not need a name, but a figure needs a body. The figures below have bodies, practices, and specific knowledge. Their composite nature is acknowledged but does not diminish the historical validity of what they carry.

---

## Mambu — Mbuti man, Ituri Forest

Mambu is not a named figure from the historical record. He is built from Turnbull's accounts of Mbuti honey-hunting practice in *The Forest People* (1961) and *The Mbuti Pygmies: Change and Adaptation* (1983), and from the scientific documentation of the honeyguide-human communication system (Spottiswoode, Begg, and Begg, *Science* 2016). He represents the Mbuti tradition of the Ituri Forest.

A note on sources: the Spottiswoode et al. 2016 study that scientifically confirmed the call-and-response system between honeyguides and human honey-hunters was conducted among the Yao people of Mozambique and the Boran of Kenya, not among the Mbuti of the Ituri. The honeyguide mutualism is pan-African and attested across many forest and savanna peoples; Turnbull and other fieldworkers describe the relationship in the Ituri, though without the acoustic analysis of the Spottiswoode study. This chapter uses Mambu's encounter to represent the Ituri practice as attested ethnographically, with the acknowledgment that the scientific grain of the Spottiswoode research comes from a geographically different instance of the same relationship.

Mambu carries: the honeyguide relationship, the tracking knowledge, the ecological reading of the forest path. He is the chapter's primary figure in sections 1 and 2.

## The Unnamed Elder — Aka singer

The elder who begins the polyphonic singing in Section 3 has no name in this chapter. This is not an evasion but a choice that follows from the music's own logic: Aka polyphony is collective. There is no composer, no conductor, no named author of the sound. The voice that begins is not more important than the voices that join; the elder initiates but does not lead. Naming him would misrepresent the form.

He is built from Simha Arom's accounts of Aka vocal practice in *African Polyphony and Polyrhythm* (1991) and from Arom's fieldwork recordings made in the Central African Republic in the 1970s. The technical description of the hocket technique and the observation about the failure of Western notation systems are derived from Arom's scholarship.

The elder carries: the polyphonic music tradition, the epistemological relationship between music and forest ecology, the molimo ceremony (described briefly in relation to his singing).

## Ekali — Aka woman, Lobaye region

Ekali is a composite figure representing the Aka women's medicinal plant knowledge tradition as documented by Bahuchet (*Les Pygmées Aka et la forêt centrafricaine*, 1985) and the relational ethics documented by Lewis (the ekila system, from fieldwork with the Mbendjele BaYaka of the Republic of Congo, 2002). The ekila concept is documented specifically among the Mbendjele; the chapter uses it as a model for the relational ethics that characterizes forest peoples' relationships with the forest more broadly, with the acknowledgment that the specific form differs across communities.

Ekali carries: medicinal plant knowledge at method level (how it is learned, transmitted, verified), the relational ethics of the ekila system, the pedagogical relationship with her daughter.

Her name is Aka rather than Mbendjele to signal that she represents the Aka tradition documented by Bahuchet, while her carrying of the ekila concept represents a pan-forest-peoples relational ethic. The chapter has made a composite choice here; the disclosure is made so that readers who follow the sources can identify where the boundaries of the composite lie.

## The Honeyguide

The greater honeyguide (Indicator indicator) is treated as a figure in this chapter, not as an animal or a tool. This treatment is deliberate and follows from the chapter's argument: the honeyguide is a partner in a relationship, not an instrument. It has its own knowledge, its own judgment (it does not lead cheaters), its own transmission system (the bird population has learned across generations that specific human calls mean a specific human behavior will follow). The chapter gives it the same narrative weight as the human figures.

The honeyguide appears in sections 1, 2 (briefly), and in the landing. It is the structural throughline of the chapter, present at the opening and closing.

## On the Baka figures (Section 5)

The Baka figures in Section 5 — those at the forest margin negotiating the first exchange relationships with farming communities — are the most thinly attested in the record. The early period of Bantu-farming contact with forest peoples is reconstructed primarily from comparative linguistics and archaeological inference; the specific moments of first contact are not preserved in any ethnographic record. The Baka figures in Section 5 are therefore the most composite of all the chapter's figures, representing a structural situation rather than any documented individual encounter.

This thinness is acknowledged in the chapter's treatment: Section 5 describes the structural logic of the exchange relationship rather than any specific negotiation, and the Baka figures are unnamed and generalized. The chapter is honest about what can be known and what must remain structural inference.
