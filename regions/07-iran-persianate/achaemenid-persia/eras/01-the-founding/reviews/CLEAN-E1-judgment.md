**Finding 1 — P3 (borderline):**
Chapter, Lydia section: "Cyrus defeated Croesus in 547 BCE. Sardis fell. Croesus himself survived — what happened to him afterward is disputed, the sources offering alternatives that range from execution to a position of honor in Cyrus's court, and later tradition chose the honorable version so consistently that it became part of how Cyrus was understood."

The phrase "later tradition chose the honorable version" is slightly outside-view — it implies the author knows better than the tradition. The sentence is accurate scholarly observation and is doing legitimate source-criticism work (establishing that the sources about Cyrus are consistently favorable). It does not imply hierarchy between cultures; it implies caution about panegyric. This is honest uncertainty (Principle 13) rather than judgment. Verdict: acceptable; author may leave.

**Finding 2 — P3 (borderline):**
Cambyses section: "Whether Cambyses was mad (the Herodotean tradition), or competent but cruel (the cautious modern reading), or simply the victim of Egyptian priestly propaganda preserved through Greek sources that were themselves hostile to Persia (the skeptical scholarly reading) — the honest answer is that we cannot fully know."

The phrase "the cautious modern reading" positions modern scholarship as a middle position between two older frameworks. This is not judgment against any culture — it is source-critical framing. Acceptable. The sentence is doing exactly what Principle 13 requires: naming the disagreement rather than resolving it.
