# GATE-1 Scorecard — "The Songhai Succession" Opening

**Scorer:** LUCIA Rubric v1.1
**Date:** 2026-04-14

## Scores

| Dimension | Score | Evidence |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| Scene     | 5/5   | The opening lands in a wholly specific moment: the jelilu singing the full genealogy in a court that has contracted around them. "Bala Faseke's heirs stood before the mansa's court in the dry season of the year 853 of the Hijra (1449 CE) and opened their mouths and the names came out in the order they had always come." A named lineage, a named place, a named date, an action underway. The scene is alive from the first line. |
| World     | 5/5   | The culture's own logic generates the opening's central tension without any external explanation. The Kurukan Fuga's redistribution mechanism is rendered as operating reality: "The covenant bound the tributary states not through garrisons or terror but through redistribution." The jelilu's function is shown, not explained. The Songhai are introduced from inside their own logic: "their own memory, their own river, their own reasons." No sentence steps outside the Mande world to explain it. |
| Honesty   | 5/5   | No outside judgment anywhere. The contraction is not called tragic, inevitable, or a failure. "This was not a fall. The jelilu would not use the word." The Songhai are not villains. The mansalu of the contraction are not judged against Musa's standard. The text presents what happened through the covenant's own logic and lets the reader arrive at understanding without instruction. |
| Texture   | 4/5   | The opening has sensory ground: "The dust of the dry season lay over everything." The kora's low notes. The quiet compounds. The river at Gao. But the sensory detail is lighter than Era 3's opening, which had dust and shea butter and woodsmoke and the harmattan haze. The contraction period's thinner record partly explains this, but the opening could carry one more physical detail in the first paragraph to ground the reader's body before the genealogy begins. |
| Voice     | 5/5   | The prose sustains the griot cadence from Era 3 while carrying a different emotional register. "He sang Sundiata, who crawled and then walked and then conquered. Wali. Khalifa. Abu Bakr, who sailed and did not return. Musa, who gave everything and came home with knowledge." The accumulative rhythm is identical to Era 3's opening; what has changed is the room it fills. Short declaratives land with weight: "The jelilu sang anyway. The genealogy did not contract when the empire did." The voice is unmistakable and inseparable from the culture. |
| Pull      | 5/5   | The opening creates a pull that is specific and internal to the story. The tension between the full genealogy and the diminished court is a genuine narrative engine. The introduction of the Songhai at Gao, listening to the river while Niani listens to the kora, creates a dual momentum. "The river carried sounds the jelilu in Niani could not hear yet" points forward with specificity. The reader wants to know what happens next because the structural logic demands that something must. |
| **Total** | **29/30** | |

## Overall Assessment

This is a very strong opening that succeeds most fully in its structural logic and its voice. The central innovation is making the unchanging genealogy the measure of the empire's change: the jelilu sing the same song in a smaller room, and the gap between the song and the room IS the story. The voice is consistent with Era 3 while carrying entirely different weight. The No Verdicts discipline holds throughout: the contraction is not mourned, not judged, not retrodicted. The one area for potential improvement is sensory texture, which is lighter than Era 3's opening.
