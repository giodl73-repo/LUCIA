# Production Bible — When the Empire Reached the Sea

**Era:** The Expansion, c. 1255-1312 CE
**Chapter target:** ~5,000 words
**Status:** Pre-draft

---

## 1. Chapter Arc

The structural engine: **the Kurukan Fuga as self-correcting covenant**. The chapter's argument is that the Manden's survival through sixty years of succession instability is not a story of good kings and bad kings but of institutional resilience — the covenant testing itself against rulers who violated it and proving that the system was stronger than any mansa.

The arc moves through three phases:
1. **Expansion under the covenant** (Wali, the Dyula network, the goldfields): the system working as designed. The covenant's requirements — generosity, assembly authority, jeli oversight — produce a stable, expanding polity.
2. **The covenant tested** (Khalifa, the freed-slave period, succession disputes): the system under stress. Bad rulers arise. The covenant's response — deposition, reassembly, the jelilu's refusal to legitimate the illegitimate — reveals that the Manden was never a monarchy held together by royal power. It was a covenant held together by institutional memory.
3. **The covenant's furthest reach** (Abu Bakr II, the Atlantic, the Dyula at the coast): the system at maximum extension. Abu Bakr's departure is the pivot: the mansa vanishes, and the system not only survives but produces, in Musa, the ruler who will take it to its peak. The expansion is not just territorial. It is the covenant proving it can survive anything — even the loss of the king.

The landing must connect to Era 3: Abu Bakr goes, Musa rises, the covenant endures. The jelilu carry the names forward. The institutional memory that makes Era 3 possible was forged in Era 2's crises.

## 2. Section Plan

### Section 1 — The Canoes (Opening, revised)
**Word target:** ~800 words
**Anchor:** Abu Bakr II at Niani receiving the fleet's report; his decision to go himself
**What it accomplishes:** Opens with the chapter's most dramatic event, then pivots to the era's structural question. Abu Bakr's disappearance is the chapter's frame — beginning at the end, then unfolding how the Manden got here.
**Revisions from Panel-1:**
- Add sensory grounding to the courtyard: heat, smell, the physical setting of Niani (Kapuscinski)
- Add one physical image to Abu Bakr's departure — the loaded canoes, the river at the coast (Kapuscinski)
- Careful with "prudent" — remove evaluative adjective that edges toward outside judgment (Achebe)
- Ground the final paragraph with one physical image rather than pure analysis (Kapuscinski)

### Section 2 — The First Mansas
**Word target:** ~900 words
**Anchor:** Mansa Wali's pilgrimage to Mecca (the first Manden ruler to make the hajj) and the early expansion
**What it accomplishes:** Shows the covenant working as designed. Wali inherits from Sundiata and does what the covenant requires: he expands the trade routes, makes the pilgrimage, governs with the assembly's voice. The Dyula network begins its eastward and southward spread. The goldfields of Bambuk come under Manden control. This section establishes the baseline — what the system looks like when it works — so that the disruption in Section 3 has weight.
**Key details:**
- Wali as the model mansa: pious, expansionist, governing within the covenant's terms
- The Dyula merchants as the Manden's information and commercial network
- The goldfields: the Wangara communities, the secret of location, the institutional apparatus of the gold-salt trade
- The ecology of the expansion: savanna to Sahel to coast, each zone with its own demands

### Section 3 — The Covenant Tested
**Word target:** ~1,000 words
**Anchor:** Mansa Khalifa shooting arrows into the crowd; the assembly's response
**What it accomplishes:** The succession crisis as institutional test. Khalifa violates the covenant — the jelilu say he shot arrows at his own people from the palace — and the assembly deposes him. This is not chaos. This is the system's immune response. The section must show: the jelilu refusing to legitimate a mansa who violates the covenant's terms; the assembly's authority to remove a king; the brief period of freed-slave rule (Sakura) as evidence that the system could elevate a non-Keita when the Keita lineage failed. Each crisis reveals a different institutional mechanism.
**Key figures:**
- Khalifa: the violator — told from inside, not as villain but as test case
- The assembly (gbara): the covenant's enforcement mechanism
- Sakura: the freed slave who became mansa, expanded the empire, and made the hajj — evidence that the covenant valued competence over lineage when lineage failed
**Structural engine:** The covenant's self-correcting mechanism IS the plot. Each succession failure reveals a new institutional feature that the reader did not know existed.

### Section 4 — The Dyula Road
**Word target:** ~1,000 words
**Anchor:** A Dyula merchant arriving at a new market in the south
**What it accomplishes:** The expansion was not only military or political. It was commercial and informational. The Dyula trade network spread the Manden's reach far beyond its administrative borders. This section shows the network: the kola nut trade from the forest zone, the cotton cloth, the gold moving north, the salt moving south, the information flowing in all directions. The Dyula are the Manden's nervous system.
**Key details:**
- The Dyula as both traders and cultural carriers: Islam, the Mande language, the covenant's principles spread with the trade
- The goldfields of Bambuk and Bure: the Wangara mining communities, the secret-keeping, the relationship between miners and state
- The ecology of trade: the savanna-forest boundary, the Niger as highway, the Saharan crossing as the northern terminus
- The empire "reaching the sea": the Atlantic coast as commercial frontier, not just territorial boundary

### Section 5 — The Mansa Who Left (Landing)
**Word target:** ~800 words
**Anchor:** Abu Bakr's departure, Musa's ascension, the jelilu carrying the names forward
**What it accomplishes:** Returns to the opening's scene but now the reader understands what Abu Bakr left behind — not just a deputy, but a covenant that had been tested and proven, an empire whose institutions were stronger than any individual ruler, a trade network that stretched from the Sahara to the sea. Abu Bakr's departure is not a crisis. It is the final proof that the covenant holds. Musa inherits not a fragile kingdom but a resilient system. The landing connects to Era 3: what Musa will do with this inheritance is the next chapter's story.
**Landing technique:** The jelilu carrying the succession forward. The names — Sundiata, Wali, Khalifa, Sakura, Abu Bakr, Musa — are the covenant made audible. The landing should use the culture's own persistence mechanism (the jeli tradition) to carry the final weight, per v1.1 amendment.

## 3. Key Figures

### Mansa Wali (Wali Keita)
**Role:** Sundiata's son (or in some traditions, grandson). The first mansa of the expansion era. The first Manden ruler to perform the hajj.
**In the narrative:** Section 2. He is the model — the covenant working as designed. Pious, competent, governing within the assembly's terms. His hajj to Mecca (decades before Musa's famous journey) established the precedent.
**Human detail:** The sources are thin. Ibn Khaldun mentions him as a just ruler who made the pilgrimage. The jelilu carry his name in the succession. He is a figure defined by competence rather than drama. Handle honestly — the thinness of the record is itself a characterization.

### Mansa Khalifa
**Role:** Wali's brother (or successor). The mansa who violated the covenant. Shot arrows at his own people. Deposed by the assembly.
**In the narrative:** Section 3. He is the covenant's first great test case. Not a villain — a ruler who broke the covenant's terms and was removed by the system designed to remove him.
**Human detail:** The jelilu's account is specific: he shot arrows into the crowd. This is not random violence; it is a deliberate violation of the covenant's requirement that a mansa protect his people. What drove him to it, the jelilu do not say. The silence around his motives is part of his characterization. Handle with Honest Limits.

### Sakura (Mansa Sakura)
**Role:** A freed slave (jon) who seized power during the succession crisis and proved an effective, expansionist ruler. Made the hajj. Died on the return journey.
**In the narrative:** Section 3. He is the most extraordinary evidence that the Manden was a covenant, not a dynasty. A jon — a member of the third social order — could become mansa when the Keita lineage failed. This is not an aberration; it is the covenant's logic: authority flows to competence when lineage cannot provide it.
**Human detail:** He expanded the empire's eastern territories. He made the pilgrimage. He died returning from Mecca. The jelilu carry his name alongside the Keita mansas. Handle the social category (jon) honestly within the Kurukan Fuga's framework.

### Abu Bakr II
**Role:** The explorer-king. Sent the first fleet, then went himself, never returned. His deputy Musa became mansa.
**In the narrative:** Sections 1 and 5 (frame). The chapter's most vivid figure — a mansa who chose to follow the ocean. The jelilu carry his story with precision: he did not die, he did not come back.
**Human detail:** His question to the captain — "if the sea took them, where did it take them?" — is the chapter's opening hook. His decision to go himself transforms the narrative from maritime loss to royal disappearance. Handle the Atlantic voyage honestly: present what the jelilu say without imposing modern certainty.

### Musa (pre-accession)
**Role:** Abu Bakr's deputy. Inherits the mantle when Abu Bakr departs. Appears at the chapter's end as the inheritor of the covenant's proven resilience.
**In the narrative:** Section 5. He appears not as the golden king of Era 3 but as a deputy who receives the throne by contingency. What he inherits — not just power, but a tested system — is the chapter's final argument.

### Dyula merchant (composite)
**Role:** A Manden trader on the network that carried commerce, Islam, and the Mande language east and south.
**In the narrative:** Section 4. Composite figure drawn from multiple accounts of the Dyula trade network. Used to embody the commercial expansion. Disclosed as composite in figures.md, per v1.1 Honest Limits amendment.
**Human detail:** The physical texture of the trade: kola nuts wrapped in leaves, gold dust in leather pouches, the weight of a salt bar, the smell of a forest-zone market. The merchant carries the empire's reach in his goods and his language.

## 4. Sources

### Tier 1 — Primary / Oral
**The Sundiata Epic (jelilu oral tradition)** — The genealogy of mansas, the Kurukan Fuga's terms, the succession accounts. Multiple versions (Niane, Conde/Conrad, Johnson).
**Al-Umari, Masalik al-Absar** — Abu Bakr's fleet and departure, as reported by Musa to al-Umari's informants in Cairo.
**Ibn Khaldun, Kitab al-Ibar** — The succession of mansas from Sundiata to Musa, the most detailed written chronology of the period.
**Ibn Battuta, Rihla** — Physical description of the Manden and its trade routes (from his 1352-53 visit, later than this era but relevant for material culture).

### Tier 2 — Academic
**Levtzion, Nehemia. *Ancient Ghana and Mali*.** — Political succession, trade routes, chronology.
**Gomez, Michael A. *African Dominion*.** — Mande political philosophy, the covenant, inside framing.
**Conrad, David C. *Empires of Medieval West Africa*.** — Oral tradition, the jeli's role, succession accounts.
**Hunwick, John O. *Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire*.** — Trade network context.
**Austen, Ralph A. *Trans-Saharan Africa in World History*.** — Trade system infrastructure.

### Tier 3 — Scholarly Secondary
**Green, Toby. *A Fistful of Shells*.** — West African economic history, the gold-salt-kola triangle.
**Brooks, George E. *Landlords and Strangers*.** — The Manden's expansion into the Senegambia and the Atlantic coast.

## 5. Panel Risks

### Tuchman: The "middle era" problem. This era lacks a single defining event like the Battle of Kirina (Era 1) or the hajj (Era 3). The chapter must find its narrative engine in the succession crises and the Dyula expansion. Every section must build; no section can merely survey.

### Ibn Khaldun: The mechanism of institutional resilience. The chapter must not merely assert that the covenant held. It must show the specific features — assembly authority, jeli oversight, the ability to elevate non-Keita rulers — that made resilience possible. Pattern analysis, not anecdote.

### Achebe: Succession crises from inside. Khalifa must not be a villain. Sakura must not be an anomaly. The Manden's own categories — the covenant's terms, the assembly's authority, the jeli's judgment — must frame every event. No outside evaluation.

### Davis: The Dyula as knowledge system. The trade network is not just commerce. It is the Manden's way of knowing the world beyond its borders. The goldfields are institutions, not just mines. The ecology of each zone — savanna, Sahel, forest, coast — must be felt.

### Kapuscinski: Every section grounded. The harmattan heat. The Niger's smell. The goldfield laterite. The forest zone's humidity. The Atlantic coast's salt air. This empire spans ecological zones, and each must have its own sensory signature.

## 6. Sensory Palette

### Niani / Upper Niger (Sections 1, 5)
- **Sight:** Red laterite dust, mud-brick walls in morning light, the Niger brown and slow, baobabs along the bank
- **Sound:** Jelilu at the courtyard, the kora's low registers, river birds, the dundun
- **Smell:** Dust, shea butter, woodsmoke, the Niger's vegetable tang
- **Touch:** Dry-season heat pressing down, laterite grit between fingers

### The Expansion Zone — Savanna and Sahel (Section 2)
- **Sight:** The savanna thinning northward, grasslands turning to scrub, gold dust in leather pouches, camel caravans
- **Sound:** The silence of the Sahel at midday, camel bells, traders' voices
- **Smell:** Dry grass, the acrid smoke of dung fires, salt bars sharp in the nose
- **Touch:** The weight of a salt bar — 30 to 40 kilograms, carried on a man's back

### The Goldfields — Bambuk and Bure (Section 4)
- **Sight:** Laterite soil, riverbeds being panned, the green of the gallery forest along watercourses
- **Sound:** The sound of digging, water sluicing over gravel, women pounding grain in the mining camps
- **Smell:** Earth, river water, charcoal from smelting
- **Touch:** Alluvial gravel in the hand, gold flecks in the pan, the cool of river water on hot skin

### The Forest Zone and Atlantic Coast (Section 4)
- **Sight:** Dense canopy, the transition from savanna to forest, kola trees with their heavy fruit, the coast
- **Sound:** The forest's density of sound — insects, birds, rustling canopy, the distant crash of surf
- **Smell:** Humid air, rotting vegetation, the sharp bitterness of raw kola nut, salt wind at the coast
- **Touch:** Humidity on the skin, the waxy texture of kola nut, sand underfoot at the coast

## 7. Voice Notes

### Register Continuity with Era 3
This chapter must sound like it belongs to the same jelilu tradition as "The Great Hajj." The accumulative cadence, the declarative landings, the jeli's precision about what is known and what is not. The voice must be consistent across eras because the tradition that carries the story is the same tradition.

### The Middle-Era Challenge
Era 3's voice carries the drama of the hajj. Era 2's voice must carry a different kind of weight: institutional resilience, the quiet persistence of the covenant through crisis. The prose can be less spectacular and more steady. The jelilu do not only sing of gold and grandeur; they sing of the mansas who held the Manden together when holding it together was the hardest work there was.

### Em-Dash Budget
Opening: 3 used / 5 allowed
Full chapter: 8 allowed. Use sparingly. Find other rhythmic devices — colons, short declaratives after long sentences, the period as pause.
