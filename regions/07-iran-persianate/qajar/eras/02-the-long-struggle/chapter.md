# The Long Struggle — Mohammad Shah and Naser al-Din Shah (c. 1834–1896 CE)

The water-pipe had gone cold on the table beside the shah, and no one dared remove it.

It was the autumn of 1891, and the pipe had been cold for three weeks, since the letter had come from Samarra, from the aged scholar in his study above the shrine city, the man who had not set foot on Iranian soil in forty years and yet whose word traveled faster than the telegraph. Mirza Hasan Shirazi had written that tobacco was forbidden. Not taxed, not regulated, not transferred from one company to another through the fine language of renegotiated concession. Forbidden. To use it was to make war on the Imam of the Age.

Naser al-Din Shah had ruled Iran for forty-three years. He had survived the Russian ambassador's demands and the British ambassador's proposals, the Great Amirs who had turned viziers and then turned against him, the attempt on his own life in 1852. He had gone to Europe three times and seen what European states had built with their factories and their railways and their naval academies and their maps, maps on which Iran appeared smaller than Naser al-Din Shah believed it should appear, bordered by territories that had once been his.

He had given the Tobacco Regie concession to the British company in the spring. The concession was clean on its face: one company, one monopoly, tobacco grown and sold and exported through a single point of control. The company would share the revenue. The treasury needed the revenue. This was the calculation Naser al-Din Shah had made for forty-three years, the calculation every Qajar shah had made since the Treaties of Gulistan and Turkmenchay took the Caucasus, since the army that had marched to reclaim Herat had been turned back by the British naval squadron at the Persian Gulf, since it had become clear that Iran, sitting between the Russian south and the British north, could survive by concession or by war, and war was not possible.

The water-pipe on the table had been cold for three weeks. The women of his harem had put down their pipes the same morning the letter from Samarra circulated through the palace. The bazaars of Tehran had closed. In Isfahan, the merchants had piled their tobacco stock in the street and not touched it. In Tabriz, the coffee-houses that had been the social lung of the city, where merchants met to argue prices, where students argued theology, where news traveled faster than any official messenger, sat quiet and empty. The smell of tobacco, which had moved through every gathering in Iran the way incense moved through a mosque, was gone.

The British ambassador had said the concession must be honored. His government had paid. The company had sent its men. Contracts had been signed with all the correct seals.

Naser al-Din Shah understood what a contract was. He had signed enough of them. He understood also, standing in his palace in the autumn of 1891, that something had shifted in the architecture of power that no contract could describe. The clerical establishment, the *marjas* in Najaf and Karbala, the scholars in Mashhad and Qom, the preachers in every Friday mosque in every city in Iran, had spoken, and Iran had obeyed. The shah had not told his wives to stop smoking. No army had marched through the bazaars. No official decree had been posted. A scholar in Samarra, in his mid-seventies, who had never held a ministry or commanded a regiment, had written one sentence, and Iran had stopped.

He cancelled the concession that winter. The government would pay the compensation from reserves it did not have.

## The Inheritance

Mohammad Shah Qajar came to power in 1834 as a young man of twenty-five inheriting a throne his grandfather Fath Ali Shah had held for thirty-seven years. Fath Ali Shah had been many things: a man of formidable presence and a hundred documented sons, a shah who had signed the Treaties of Gulistan and Turkmenchay that surrendered the Caucasus and formalized the loss of what the Qajars had always understood as northern Iran. He had left Mohammad Shah a state that had learned, through two humiliating wars, the distance between the Iran it imagined itself to be and the Iran it could defend.

Mohammad Shah's reign was shaped by a minister more than by the shah himself. Hajj Mirza Aqasi was a Sufi-inclined cleric from the shrine city of Karbala who had been Mohammad Shah's tutor and became his prime minister, his *sadr-e a'zam*, on the accession. He was a man of considerable personal piety and limited administrative skill, more comfortable in the world of mystical Islam than in the world of armies and revenues. Under his guidance, the reform impulses carefully built under Crown Prince Abbas Mirza, who had died before ever reaching the throne having spent two decades trying to build a modern army on the northwest frontier, were set aside in favor of stability and religious propriety.

The consequence appeared in the east. Herat had been part of the Safavid empire and part of the Persianate world for centuries before the disintegration that had followed Nader Shah. Its population spoke Persian; its learning was Islamic; its culture was oriented toward Mashhad and Isfahan rather than toward Kabul. Mohammad Shah regarded its recovery as both a political right and a dynastic obligation. In the winter of 1837, the Iranian army marched.

The road from Tehran to Herat runs east across the plateau through Khorasan, the old heartland of the Samanids, through the dust-yellow flatlands around Nishapur and the shrine city of Mashhad before turning southeast across the rocky Afghan borderlands. The army made this journey in winter, which meant cold nights and dry watercourses and the slow pace of artillery on difficult ground. It reached Herat in the spring and began a siege.

The British response did not come from the east. It came from the south. A British naval squadron appeared at Bushire on the Persian Gulf coast, landed marines on Kharg Island, and made it clear to Tehran that if the Herat siege continued, the Gulf coast, Iran's maritime access to the world, would be held by the British navy. The Iranian army was winning at Herat. It withdrew anyway, because the calculation was simple: the plateau could be defended, but the coastline could not. The lesson arrived in 1838 and would not need to be taught again.

Mohammad Shah died in 1848, at thirty-four, of gout. He left the throne to his seventeen-year-old son.

## The Reformer and His End

Naser al-Din Shah Qajar came to power in September 1848, in the year 1264 of the Hijra. He came with a minister.

Mirza Taqi Khan Amir Kabir had risen through the Iranian diplomatic and administrative service, had spent time in Russia observing Russian military reform, had watched the Ottoman Tanzimat reforms from close enough to understand their method if not always their effect. He was thirty-seven years old to the new shah's seventeen; he was also the shah's new father-in-law, having married the shah's sister as a condition of his appointment. He was not a courtier. He was an administrator who believed that administration was the only thing standing between Iran and its gradual absorption into the spheres of the two great powers.

Amir Kabir moved quickly. He reformed the provincial tax system, reducing the capacity of the great *valis*, the provincial governors, to skim revenues before they reached Tehran. He reformed the army's officer corps, replacing some hereditary appointments with merit promotions. He limited the growth of the royal household's expenditures. He was, in other words, making enemies: of the great families who had depended on the old system, of the court factions that had profited from the previous arrangements, of the Russian and British ambassadors who had learned to navigate a court divided by competing interests and found a more efficient administration less manageable.

He also built. The Dar al-Funun, the House of Many Arts, opened in Tehran in the winter of 1851, in a newly constructed building not far from the royal palace. Its first students were sons of the military and administrative elite, perhaps one hundred young men between the ages of fourteen and twenty. Their teachers were mostly Austrian and French officers and physicians, hired through diplomatic channels: men who taught artillery mathematics and military engineering and anatomy and French and Russian languages. A Georgian-born physician named Polak, who had come from Vienna via Tabriz, conducted Iran's first modern anatomy lectures on cadavers that arrived weekly from the hospital next door. The students sat in the cold morning light of the new building's lecture rooms, their breath visible, writing in Persian what their instructor said through the translator who stood between them.

What Amir Kabir was building, through the Dar al-Funun and through his administrative reforms, was a state capable of managing its own modernization, capable eventually of not needing foreign concessions to function. Whether it would have worked is a question history did not allow. By the autumn of 1851, three years into his ministry, Amir Kabir had too many enemies and the shah had grown uncertain of a minister who had become, in the eyes of the court and of both foreign embassies, more powerful than the throne itself.

The shah signed the order of dismissal in November 1851. The order of execution followed in January 1852. Amir Kabir was taken to the Bagh-e Fin garden in Kashan, a Safavid-era pleasure garden of running water and old plane trees, and killed there in a bath of the garden's famous spring water. He was forty-two. The Dar al-Funun continued without him. The administrative reforms were reversed, selectively, by those who had opposed them.

The first teacher in the class that would govern Iran for the next forty years was the fact of Amir Kabir's death. The lesson was not that reform was wrong. The lesson was that reform depended on the person doing it, and persons were mortal, and courts were courts.

The Dar al-Funun's graduates went into the administration and the army and the foreign ministry. They processed the concession paperwork with competence. A few of them, in the 1870s and 1880s, began publishing newspapers and writing pamphlets that circulated, carefully, in Tehran and Tabriz and among the Iranian communities in Istanbul and Cairo. What they had been taught at the Dar al-Funun was not reform; Amir Kabir had not had time to build that curriculum. What they had been taught was that there were other ways of organizing a state, and the knowledge of that, even in the absence of the will to act on it, was a different condition from ignorance.

## The Concession System and Its Logic

For the next three decades, Naser al-Din Shah governed Iran through a combination of personal attention to detail, theatrical self-presentation, and a foreign policy built on the single insight the Herat campaign had demonstrated: Iran could not fight the British navy or the Russian army, but it could play each against the other, and the revenue from foreign concessions could finance a court that could not otherwise sustain itself.

The logic was not foolish. It was, in fact, the only logic available.

The Iranian state's revenue base was chronically inadequate to the demands of a modernizing court. The tax system relied on land assessments not updated since the Safavid period; the great provincial governors retained their traditional share of whatever they collected before sending anything to Tehran; the tribal populations occupying large parts of the plateau and the mountains paid taxes irregularly and through negotiation rather than through fixed rates. To build railways, which Naser al-Din Shah had seen in Europe and understood as the difference between an integrated state and a collection of loosely connected provinces, would cost vastly more than the treasury could generate from internal sources.

Foreign concessions filled the gap, at a price.

The Reuters Concession of 1872 demonstrated the price in its most extreme form. Baron Julius de Reuter, a British subject and the founder of the Reuters news agency, was granted by the shah a concession so comprehensive that the British press called it "the most complete and extraordinary surrender of the entire industrial resources of a kingdom to foreign hands that has probably ever been dreamed of in history." Reuter would operate Iran's banks, build its railways, mine its minerals, and take the revenues from its customs for twenty years. The concession was cancelled eighteen months later under Russian pressure; Russia had its own interests in Iranian infrastructure and would not allow a British monopoly. Iranian merchant and clerical opposition added weight to what Russia had already demanded. The bazaar merchants, who had not been consulted, had no intention of being governed by a foreign company's ledger.

But the cancellation required compensation. The treasury that could not fund modernization without concessions also could not compensate for cancelled concessions without further concessions. The Discount and Loan Bank of Persia, established in 1889 under British charter to replace the cancelled Reuters Concession's banking provisions, was itself a concession. The Imperial Bank of Persia's notes, printed in London, circulated in Tehran, were the currency of a state that had sold parts of its own financial infrastructure to maintain the fiction of sovereignty over the rest.

This was the arithmetic of the trap. The court required roughly ten million *tomans* per year to function: court salaries, the royal household, provincial governors' stipends, the small Cossack Brigade that Russia had agreed to train and fund as a check on British influence in the Iranian military. Tax revenues, optimistically assessed, covered perhaps six million. The shortfall was chronic. A concession payment of one million *tomans*, the Tobacco Regie's initial offer, represented the difference between a functioning and a non-functioning court for a year. The shah knew this number. His finance minister knew it. The British ambassador knew it, which was why British companies kept arriving with proposals.

Naser al-Din Shah went to Europe three times in this period: in 1873, in 1878, and in 1889. He kept detailed diaries of each journey, written in his own hand, careful and observant. In Paris he noted the wide avenues that Haussmann had cut through the old city, not aesthetic admiration but the recognition that a city redesigned to prevent barricades was a city governed by men who had studied their own history and rewritten it in stone and sightlines. He watched the Belgian railway network and calculated the travel time between Brussels and Antwerp against the travel time between Tehran and Tabriz: four days by horse, shorter in summer than in winter, impossible in the passes after heavy snow. He stood at the London docks and watched the movement of tonnage that in a single morning exceeded what the Persian Gulf ports would move in a season.

He compared these observations to what he had in Tehran: a postal system, a telegraph line built with British assistance that ran from the Gulf to the Russian border, the Dar al-Funun, the city's wide new avenues. He was not an unperceptive man. He saw the distance.

He also saw that the distance could not be closed quickly, and that attempts to close it quickly had historically destroyed the men who made them. He was not Amir Kabir. He intended to survive.

The city of Tabriz, four days northwest of Tehran, showed the gradient that ran through Qajar Iran. It was the crown prince's traditional seat and the first Iranian city where European goods arrived on the road from Tiflis and Istanbul, where Russian cloth and French mirrors and Belgian glassware appeared in the bazaar before Tehran merchants had even heard of them. Tabriz merchants were literate in Ottoman Turkish, familiar with Russian, sometimes conversant in French. They were also the merchants whose commercial networks extended deepest into the Caucasus that the Treaties of Turkmenchay had separated from Iran, who remembered, in the institutional memory of the bazaar, what trade had been before the border moved. They were not sentimental about this. They were precise: the border cost them exactly this much in tariffs, exactly this many days in detour, exactly this number of trusted partners now subject to a different law.

What Tabriz showed was that Qajar Iran was not homogeneous. The provincial capitals did not experience the concession system in the same way Tehran did. What reached Tehran as a diplomatic negotiation reached Tabriz as a price change in the bazaar. The tobacco concession meant something specific to the small farmers of Gilan and Mazandaran who grew tobacco in the Caspian coastal lowlands: a foreign company now held the right to set the price at which their crop could be sold. The price the company set was lower than the price they had received before. The farmers knew this in the same way that Tabriz merchants knew the tariff numbers, precisely, because precision was how they survived.

Between Tehran and Tabriz, between the court and the bazaar, between the diplomatic language of concessions and the arithmetic of farm prices, lived the Iran that the concession system was meant to govern and that the clerical network actually governed. The telegraph line that the British had helped build in the 1850s ran between the cities; the network that carried the fatwa ran through a different infrastructure, older and without wire.

## How a Fatwa Travels

Mirza Hasan Shirazi was born in Shiraz in the year 1230 of the Hijra, 1815 of the common era, and spent the latter part of his life in Samarra, in Ottoman Iraq, at the shrine of the tenth and eleventh Imams, the Askari shrine with its gleaming golden dome above the Tigris plain. He had spent decades in Najaf, the great Shia seminary city, studying and teaching under the scholars who defined the tradition of *ijtihad*: the right and duty of a qualified jurist to exercise independent legal reasoning when the texts required interpretation.

To become a *marja*, a source of emulation, a scholar whose rulings the faithful were obligated to follow, required decades of study and the recognition of peers. There was no appointment, no examination, no state certification. Authority accrued through reputation: through the quality of legal opinions issued over years, through students who spread and transmitted the teaching, through the assessment of other senior scholars who acknowledged one man's judgment as superior. When Mirza Hasan Shirazi became the pre-eminent Shia *marja* of his age, his authority was recognized across the Shia world, in Iran, in Iraq, in Bahrain, in parts of India, not because any state had granted it but because the community of the faithful had.

His income came from the *khums*, the religious fifth that devout Shia paid annually on their surplus wealth, directed to the marja of their choice. He was financially independent of the Iranian state. He lived in Ottoman territory. The Qajar shah could not tax him, appoint him, imprison him without an international incident, or silence him without making a martyr of a man in his mid-seventies in a shrine city. He was, from the Qajar perspective, ungovernable.

When the Tobacco Regie concession was granted in 1890, the reports that reached Samarra came through the network that served every senior marja: students who had returned from Iran with news from their home cities; merchants who traded between the Iranian bazaars and the Ottoman shrine cities, who paid their khums in Samarra and spoke with the scholar's assistants; clerics in Iranian cities who maintained correspondence with Najaf and Samarra as a matter of course. The picture these sources assembled was of a foreign company acquiring the right to buy tobacco from Iranian farmers at prices set by the company, sell it to Iranian merchants at prices set by the company, and export it at prices set by the company, paying a share of the profit to a government that had negotiated for itself and not for those it governed.

Mirza Hasan Shirazi issued his ruling in the spring of 1891, after reports had been building for months. The text was brief: the use of tobacco in any form was, while the concession stood, equivalent to war against the Hidden Imam. The ruling was not a political document. It was a legal opinion within the *ijtihad* framework: the scholar had assessed the situation, applied the principles of Islamic jurisprudence to the specific harm the concession caused to the Muslim faithful of Iran, and determined that abstention was obligatory.

The letter was carried from Samarra to Iran by students. It was read from the pulpit in Tehran's Friday mosque. The mosque's cleric, who himself followed Shirazi's authority on matters of law, repeated the ruling to his congregation. The merchant guilds of the bazaar, whose members were mosque regulars and whose senior members paid khums to Shirazi's network, heard the ruling from their clerics. The guild heads, who enforced the collective norms of their trades, determined that the bazaar would observe the fatwa. The household head, who was a guild member, told his household that the qalyan was put down. The shah's wives, who were devout, put down their pipes in the harem without waiting for an instruction from the throne.

No army marched. No police enforced. The network through which Islamic authority had moved in Iran for a century moved through the same channels it always moved through, and the country stopped smoking.

The Qajar state had no equivalent mechanism. It had tax collectors and provincial governors and a telegraph line and a small army trained by Russian-funded Cossack officers. It did not have a network that reached every household in Iran and had the trust of every household in Iran. It had sovereignty; the *marjaiyyat* had authority. In 1891, the difference between the two became visible.

## After the Pipe

The concession was cancelled in January 1892. The government paid compensation to the Tobacco Regie: five hundred thousand pounds sterling borrowed at interest from the Imperial Bank of Persia, the British-chartered institution that was itself a concession. The treasury that had needed the tobacco concession to function now owed the bank that had replaced the cancelled banking concession for the money to cancel another concession. The trap closed.

Naser al-Din Shah ruled for four more years after the Tobacco Protest. He continued to receive foreign ambassadors and sign foreign agreements; he continued to grant concessions in the sectors the clerical establishment had not moved against, mining rights in Khorasan, telegraph operations, customs administration. He was, as he had always been, a survivor. But the protest had changed the political landscape in ways that survival could not undo.

Jamal al-Din al-Afghani, the Pan-Islamic activist of uncertain Iranian origins who had been expelled from Tehran in 1891 for agitating against the tobacco concession, continued to write from Istanbul and London and wherever else would have him, arguing that the protest had shown the way: Islamic solidarity, organized through the clerical hierarchy, could resist imperial encroachment more effectively than any army. His argument circulated in the underground press and in the correspondence of reformist intellectuals who had learned, from 1891, that mass mobilization was achievable and that its engine was the mosque-bazaar-seminary network.

The merchants of the bazaar, who had their own reasons for participating in the protest, who had watched foreign companies undercut their traditional trading relationships and foreign-chartered banks displace their credit networks, drew their own lessons. The concession system was not merely a problem for the clergy. It was a problem for anyone who conducted commerce in Iran, which was everyone. The coalition that had gathered around the tobacco fatwa was not only a religious coalition. It was a coalition of grievances that happened to have found a form of expression the state could not suppress.

In May 1896, Naser al-Din Shah traveled to the Shah Abdol-Azim shrine at Rey, south of Tehran, to celebrate the anniversary of his reign. The shrine lay in a complex of tile-work and courtyards built around the tomb of a descendant of the seventh Imam, a few miles from the capital on the road that ran south across the flat plain below the Alborz mountains. The shah arrived in the early morning, before the midday heat, and moved through the outer courtyard where pilgrims were gathered. A man named Mirza Reza Kermani, who had been in al-Afghani's circle and had spent a year in prison on account of it, was in the crowd. He stepped forward with a pistol and fired.

Naser al-Din Shah died in the shrine precinct. He was sixty-four years old and had ruled for forty-eight years.

## The Architecture That Remained

The dynasty did not die with him. Mozaffar al-Din Shah, his son, would reign until 1907; Ahmad Shah, his grandson, until 1925. The Tobacco Protest's consequence was not the end of the Qajar state but the beginning of the end of a specific understanding of what the Qajar state was.

For a century, the Qajar shahs had governed Iran on the implicit premise that sovereignty was the capacity to make binding agreements. They could cede territory by treaty, Gulistan, Turkmenchay, because treaties were binding. They could grant concessions, Reuter, the Imperial Bank, tobacco, because concessions were binding. The premise that made this system coherent was that the shah's word, once given, held.

The Tobacco Protest had demonstrated that the shah's word could be unbound. Not by a foreign power, not by an army, not by a court faction. By an elderly scholar in a shrine city in Ottoman territory who had never met the shah and would never need to. The binding mechanism of the clerical network was stronger than the binding mechanism of the royal seal.

The reformist intellectuals who would draft the Iranian constitution in 1906, the document that would limit royal power, establish a parliament, and inaugurate the Constitutional Revolution, would look back at 1891 as the moment when it became clear that Iran's political architecture required reconstruction. The question they would argue over was not whether the royal power needed limits, but who would set them: the clerics, whose *marjaiyyat* had just demonstrated its independence, or a secular constitution that drew its authority from the people rather than from the *ijtihad* tradition.

That argument was not settled in Naser al-Din Shah's lifetime. It was not settled quickly after his death. The mosque and the constitution and the bazaar and the foreign ambassadors would argue it through the decade of the Constitutional Revolution and beyond, through the dynasty's final years, through the next century of Iranian political life.

At the Shah Abdol-Azim shrine in the autumn, the smell of rosewater and incense still drifted through the tile-work courtyards. Pilgrims arrived and left and arrived again. The shrine was the tomb of a descendant of the Imam; the Imam was in occultation; the *marjaiyyat* governed in the Imam's absence; the shah had died at the foot of the shrine, in the shadow of an authority he had never been able to govern.

This was the geography the dynasty had lived inside for a century and would live inside for another thirty years. The concessions could be cancelled. The treaties were signed. The water-pipe had gone cold, and what it had proved was not a lesson for the reign that had just ended.

It was the premise of everything that came next.
