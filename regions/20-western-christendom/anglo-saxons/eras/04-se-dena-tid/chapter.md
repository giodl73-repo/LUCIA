# Se Dena Tíd — The Danish Time
## Anglo-Saxons, Era 4 (c. 793–900 CE)

---

The man in the fenland did not look like a king.

His name was Alfred, son of Æthelwulf, of the house of Cerdic, king of the West Saxons — though in the winter of 878 there was less to that title than there had been. Guthrum had taken Winchester. Guthrum had taken everything that could be taken. The great army that had come out of the east nine years before had done what armies do: it had moved, and fought, and taken, and finally it had settled. The Danes were in Mercia now, and in East Anglia, and in Northumbria, and in the heart of Wessex, and Alfred was in the marsh.

The Somerset marshes in February. The reed beds silver with frost at dawn. The paths through the bog known only to the men who lived there, who cut their turf there, who moved their livestock along the dry ridges between the black water and the black water. Alfred knew none of these paths. The men who did know them had, for reasons that were not stated, given him shelter.

He had a small company with him. That is all the record says: a small company. They built a fort of sorts on the island of Ætheling — the marsh-island, the island of the prince — and from there Alfred raided. Not campaigns. Not battles. The kind of fighting a hunted man does: quick and small and aimed at staying alive rather than winning anything.

The tradition that found him there says he burned the cakes.

A herdsman's wife, not knowing who her guest was, set him to watch the bread baking on the hearth. His mind was elsewhere. The cakes burned. She scolded him. He took it. The story was remembered because it was true to something — because the image of a king sitting by a fire, failing at the simplest task, rebuked by a woman who did not know his name, carried a truth that grander stories did not. He had been stripped of everything a king uses to be a king. What remained was the man.

What he thought during those weeks in the marshes is not recorded. Men in extremity think differently than men in halls. The hall is full of counsel and obligation and the performance of lordship. The marsh has none of that. What remains when everything else is gone is the question of what it was all for.

He had been taught — by his father, by the churchmen, by the books he had been read as a child — that a king's purpose was to give his people good law and good protection. Law and protection. He had failed at the second. Guthrum had come and the burhs were not there to stop him, the fyrd was not there to meet him, the ealdormen's levies had melted in the cold. If a king who cannot protect his people is still a king, it is only because the idea of kingship survives the man who holds it.

He did not leave the marshes in despair. That is the crucial thing. He sent messages. He gathered men. By Easter — by the feast of the Resurrection, in April, when the marsh tracks were firmer — he had assembled enough that he could move. He met his people at Egbert's Stone, in the east of Selwood, where three shires join. They were glad to see him, the Chronicle says. That gladness is not a small thing. A thegn's gladness at seeing his lord return from the marsh is not an abstract sentiment. It is the relief of a man whose obligations — military service, land tenure, the whole structure of his life — depended on a lord who was still alive and claiming his seat. The structure of their world required him. And he was there.

At Edington, ten miles north of the great hill fort the old people had built before there were kings in England, Alfred met Guthrum's army. He defeated it. He did not pursue and slaughter — he pursued and besieged, two weeks on the Danes in their camp at Chippenham. Two weeks in the cold outside the earthworks, the smell of a besieged army drifting out — cookfires, mud, livestock brought inside the perimeter, the particular sourness of men who have not changed their clothes. Inside the walls, Guthrum waited. Outside, Alfred waited longer. The Danes came out. They agreed to leave Wessex. They agreed to be baptized.

Alfred stood as godfather at Guthrum's baptism. He gave the Danish king gifts. He gave him Æthelstan for his Christian name.

That is the hinge. Not the battle but the baptism. Not the defeat but the naming.

## Before the Marsh

To understand the Somerset marshes, you need to understand what filled the eighty years before them.

In the year that the English called 793 — the fifty-seventh year of the reign of Æthelred of Northumbria, a reign already troubled by politics and violence — the Norse came to Lindisfarne.

They came by sea. The monastery on the tidal island had stood for a hundred and fifty years, since Aidan brought the Irish tradition south from Iona. The scriptorium had produced the Lindisfarne Gospels — the most technically accomplished book in these islands, four Gospels written in the insular hand, illuminated with interlaced patterns of such complexity that the men who made them must have spent years on single pages. The monastery was not fortified. Monasteries were not fortified. They were sacred ground.

The Norse were not bound by that convention.

Alcuin of York, the English scholar who had gone to Charlemagne's court and was there when the news arrived, wrote letters back to Northumbria that survive. "Never before has such terror appeared in Britain as we have now suffered from a pagan race," he wrote. He was not wrong about the terror. He framed it as divine judgment — God's punishment for Northumbrian sins. Others would note later that the English had themselves come by sea to take land from the Britons, three centuries before. What was unprecedented was the direction. The raiders came from the north and the east, from Scandinavia, and they came with no interest in the theology of the ground they were taking.

For the next seventy years, the raids continued. Sporadic at first — Lindisfarne, Iona, the Irish monasteries, the coastal settlements. Then more systematic. In 865, the year the English reckoned from the birth of Christ, the thing the Chronicle calls the *micel hæþen here* — the great heathen army — landed in East Anglia. This was different from a raid. This was an army with the intention of staying.

Ivar and Halfdan and the other leaders whose names the Chronicle gives without sentiment led the army north through Northumbria, taking York (what the Romans had called Eboracum, what the Danes would call Jorvik) in 866. They went south through East Anglia. They killed Edmund, the East Anglian king, in 869 — he refused to renounce his Christianity, and they shot him with arrows and cut off his head. The Church made him a saint. The Danes made his kingdom their own.

In 871, the year of nine battles, Æthelred of Wessex died and his brother Alfred became king. Alfred was twenty-two. He had been to Rome twice — once as a small child, sent ahead of his father on what was apparently a normal royal pilgrimage, and once with his father. He was literate in English and learning Latin. He was not the expected king — three older brothers had preceded him, and in normal times he might have spent his life as a great ealdorman rather than a king. There were no normal times.

He fought the Danes for the next six years to a rough standstill. Bought peace at Wareham in 876 with hostages and an oath. The Danes swore on their sacred arm-ring, the oath-object of their tradition, and then broke the oath and rode for Exeter. Alfred pursued. More peace, more hostages. In January 878, the mid-winter feast of Twelfth Night, when the fyrd was at home and the ealdormen were in their halls, Guthrum struck Chippenham.

Alfred ran.

## What the Marsh Taught

The two months in the Somerset marshes are the hinge of the whole era, but what happened there is interior and therefore largely invisible. We have what the Chronicle records — the raids from Ætheling, the gathering at Egbert's Stone — and we have the tradition of the burned cakes. What we do not have is Alfred's thoughts.

We have, instead, what he did afterward. And what he did afterward was build a system, not an army.

The burh network came first. Not in 878 — the crisis was too immediate for that — but in the years after, as the peace with Guthrum held and Alfred had time and resources to think about why January 878 had happened. The answer was structural: there was nowhere for Wessex to concentrate its defense. An invading army could move through open country and strike at Chippenham or Winchester or anywhere else it chose, and the local levies would be too slow and too dispersed to respond. The solution Alfred built is recorded in the document called the *Burghal Hidage*, probably compiled in his son Edward's reign but reflecting Alfred's own planning. It lists thirty-three fortifications across Wessex and lists the number of hides — units of agricultural land — assigned to each, with a precise formula: one man per hide, four men per pole of wall, multiplied by the circuit of the wall. The arithmetic is verifiable. Modern archaeologists have measured the surviving remains of Alfred's burhs and found the formula correct. Oxford, Bath, Wareham, Wallingford — each positioned within a day's march of the next, each sized to provide refuge for every farmstead within reach. The burh network is not a military innovation so much as a territorial one: it makes Wessex legible. It gives the land a structure that can be defended, administered, and taxed.

Each burh needed men to garrison it. Garrison men needed to know what they were defending and why. Which meant they needed, at minimum, to be able to read a charter, understand an oath, know the king's law. Alfred's second system followed from the first.

He wrote to his bishops. The letter survives — the Preface to the *Pastoral Care*, the translation of Gregory the Great's guide to Christian leadership, which Alfred sent to every bishop in his kingdom along with a copy of the translation and an aestel — a pointer, a reading tool made of gold and silver — to be kept with the book. The letter reads as follows, in Alfred's own words: "It seems better to me, if it seems so to you, that we too should turn into the language that we can all understand certain books which are the most necessary for all men to know." He had looked at the bishops who served him and seen men who could not read the books that had been written in their tradition. He had looked at the monasteries and seen libraries full of Latin that no one in England could any longer make use of. He proposed to remedy this: not by training everyone in Latin, but by bringing the necessary books into English.

He recruited scholars to do it. From Mercia, Plegmund, who would become Archbishop of Canterbury. From Wales, Asser, the bishop who would become Alfred's biographer. From the continent, Grimbald from the monastery of St Bertin in Flanders, and John, called the Old Saxon, from a monastery in the region the English called Old Saxony — men who brought the scholarly traditions of the Carolingian world to a court that needed them. These men staffed Alfred's palace school, where the sons of nobles learned to read and write, where Alfred himself sat sometimes and listened and asked questions, and where the translations were produced.

The translations Alfred chose are a curriculum in the disguise of a library.

Gregory's *Pastoral Care*: how to lead a Christian people. Bede's *Historia Ecclesiastica Gentis Anglorum* — the history of the English Church and people, written in Latin by a Northumbrian monk a century and a half before: where the English came from, how they became Christian, what they shared. Orosius's *Histories Against the Pagans*: the whole of history from the beginning of the world to the fifth century, organized to show that Christian providence shaped the fate of kingdoms. Augustine's *Soliloquies*: the nature of the soul and what can be known about the life to come.

And Boethius.

Anicius Manlius Severinus Boethius was a Roman philosopher and statesman who held the highest offices of the Ostrogothic court and then was accused of treason, imprisoned, stripped of everything, and executed in 524. In the year between his arrest and his death, he wrote the *Consolatio Philosophiae* — the Consolation of Philosophy — a dialogue between himself and the figure of Philosophy, who appears to him in prison and argues that fortune is not real, that true goods cannot be taken, that providence orders all things toward a good end that is invisible from within the experience of suffering.

Alfred translated it in the Somerset marshes of his reign, not his life. He had come out of literal marshes into a kingdom half-lost. He was a man who understood what Boethius was writing about. He added passages to the translation that are not in the original — his own interpolations, in which he glosses the Latin argument in the terms of his own experience. Where Boethius speaks of fortune abstractly, Alfred inserts: the man who had the tools to work — wisdom, and the just ordering of land — could make the kingdom well. The tools are specific. The need is specific. The book is for a man who has just lost everything and must figure out whether he can get it back, and whether getting it back is worth the trouble.

That is not a book you translate unless you need it.

## The Danelaw and Its Uses

Alfred defeated Guthrum at Edington in May 878. He did not pursue destruction. He pursued a settlement.

The treaty with Guthrum, which probably reached its final form in the 880s after Alfred's recovery of London in 886, established a boundary: north and east of Watling Street was the Danelaw, the territory governed by Danish custom. South and west was Alfred's kingdom. The line ran roughly from London northwest to Chester, though it was contested at its edges and shifted over time. Within the Danelaw, Danish law applied. Wergilds — the compensation payments for injury and killing — were calculated by Danish custom. Land was held by Danish tenure. The language in the street was in some towns Old Norse.

A concession, some would say afterward. It was not that.

A recognized Danelaw was an entity. An entity could sign treaties, take oaths, be held to agreements. An unrecognized Danish occupation was a chaos that could not be managed because it had no acknowledged form. Alfred gave the Danelaw a form — acknowledged it, named it, drew its boundary — and in doing so created something he could deal with. The Guthrum who stood at the baptismal font was not only a Danish war-leader accepting a ritual. He was a recognized authority over a defined territory, who had sworn on his sacred arm-ring and now on the Christian oath to maintain the peace. Both oath-systems bound him. Alfred had doubled the binding.

The witan knew what was happening. Alfred's laws — the *Domboc*, the law code he compiled probably in the 880s — were issued with the counsel and consent of the witan, the assembly of wise men: senior clergy, the great ealdormen, the king's household officials. The preamble to the *Domboc* is explicit. Alfred had collected the law codes of his predecessors — Æthelberht of Kent, Offa of Mercia, Ine of Wessex — and from them he had "set down many of those which our forefathers held, those which I liked, and many of those which I did not like I rejected with the advice of my witan." The council is not ceremonial. The council is the principle: a king governs by counsel, and governance by counsel is governance by consent, and governance by consent is the only kind that holds when the king is gone.

The principle runs through everything Alfred built. The burh garrison is maintained by hide-obligation, a system the thegns and free farmers of Wessex understood and could enforce on each other. The translation program produces books that anyone who can read can possess — not manuscripts chained to a monastic library, but texts sent out, multiplied, distributed to the bishops and the major churches. The *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*, begun under Alfred's direction c. 890, was distributed to the monasteries in multiple copies: a history of the English people written in English, sent out into the kingdom, available to any literate man who walked through a monastic door.

Alfred was making Wessex legible to itself. Not just to its administrators, not just to its clergy — to the people who would have to go on being Wessex when Alfred was not there to explain it to them.

## The Kingdom Reads

Asser describes Alfred's methods with the detail of a witness. He describes how Alfred kept a small book — a *enchiridion*, a handbook — tucked inside his tunic, in which he wrote down prayers and passages that struck him, adding pages as the book filled. He describes Alfred's frustration that he had not learned to read Latin as a child — his father had neglected this, sending the boy to Rome but not to school — and Alfred's late-life effort to remedy it. He describes the candle-clock: Alfred divided his day into three parts, eight hours for prayer, eight for governance, eight for sleep and meals and exercise, and to measure the time he used candles of a standard size that burned for a known duration — but the candles were uneven in the drafts of the hall, so he designed lanterns of ox-horn pane to shelter the flames.

The candle-clock is an administrative innovation in the clothing of a personal discipline. Alfred measured his own day because he intended to use it fully. The kingdom needed governing. The texts needed translating. The schools needed staffing. The burhs needed their garrisons counted and their walls measured. He was working in physical pain — Asser describes his illness as something that had afflicted him since his young manhood, a pain that would seize him at feasts and gatherings and force him from the hall. He governed despite it, with it, around it. The illness is not separate from the man. It is part of the formation.

At Winchester, in the hall where the light came through slit windows and the rushes on the floor were changed when they became too wet, Alfred dictated and Asser or another scholar wrote. The scratch of the reed pen. The smell of the vellum, which was sheep or calf skin scraped and stretched and dried, and smelled of the animal still. The sound of a translation being read back for approval, the voice of a Welsh bishop finding the Old English equivalent for a Latin concept that might not have an equivalent, Alfred asking for it again, differently, until the English was not simply translated but made.

The Preface to his *Boethius* — Alfred's translation of the *Consolation of Philosophy* — contains a passage that is not in the original. Alfred has added a metaphor. He compares the work of thought to gathering wood in a forest. You take what you can carry. You build from it. You go back for more. He writes, in the preface, that the builder selects from the forest what he needs, and what he needs differs from what another needs. Alfred is describing himself. The king who went into the Somerset marshes with a small company came out with a program. The program was made of what he had been able to carry: the books he knew, the scholars he could recruit, the obligations he could call on, the surviving thegns who were glad to see him alive.

He built from what the forest provided.

## The Shape of the Era

The Danish Time is not simply the story of Alfred's survival and response. It is the story of what the Danish presence forced the English to become.

Before 865, Mercia had dominated the English kingdoms. Offa's Dyke — the great earthwork running along the Welsh border, built by Offa of Mercia in the late eighth century — was the most ambitious construction project in Britain since the Romans left. Mercia's scribal tradition, its churches, its administration: these were the models other kingdoms looked to. The Great Heathen Army broke Mercia first and last. After 877, Mercia was a Mercian kingdom only in name, governed by an ealdorman — Æthelred, who would marry Alfred's daughter Æthelflæd — who held his authority in Alfred's name.

The center of gravity moved south and west, to Wessex. And Wessex, under Alfred, became something new.

The language is the evidence. Old English in the ninth century was not a single language — it was a family of related dialects, Northumbrian and Mercian and West Saxon and Kentish, mutually intelligible at close range but different enough that a Northumbrian monk and a West Saxon scribe might find each other's writing unfamiliar. Alfred wrote in West Saxon. His translations were in West Saxon. The *Chronicle* was distributed in West Saxon. Within a generation, West Saxon was the prestige written dialect — the form other writers learned if they wanted their texts to circulate. The literary standard of medieval English, as it emerged, was Alfredian.

When Alfred insisted that the most necessary books be in English — the language "we can all understand" — he was insisting that the English were a people who shared a tongue worth the trouble of writing in. The act of writing the language down is the act of deciding it is a language, not merely a dialect, not merely a vernacular — a medium fit for law, for history, for the life of the mind.

The Danes were in the Danelaw. They were Christianized, legally recognized, a managed presence. Across the boundary, Wessex was building a different kind of state: one with a written administration, a literate clergy, a physical infrastructure of fortified towns, and a literary culture in its own tongue. The two systems would interact for the next century — Danes settling in English towns, English law spreading into the Danelaw, Danish words becoming part of the English language, English institutions becoming part of the Danelaw's governance. The English the island would go on to speak had Danish in its bones: sky, window, knife, take, they, them, their — from the Old Norse of the Danelaw settlers. The merger was real and lasting, and it happened in the Danish Time.

Alfred died in October 899. The *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* records it in the spare register he had taught it: the year, the event, the regnal length. Twenty-eight years and a half as king of Wessex. The burhs stood. The translations circulated. The schools produced literate men. His son Edward would take the fight north. His grandson Æthelstan would be the first king of all England, the man who could say *rex totius Britanniae* and mean it.

The kingdom Alfred had lost in January 878 was not the kingdom that survived him. It was larger, better governed, more legible to itself, more durable. He had come out of the marshes with a theory — that a people is made, not merely born, and that the making is an act of will, the will to write your laws in your own tongue and teach your children to read them — and he had spent twenty years proving it.

The cakes had burned. The kingdom had not.

The *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* — the document Alfred himself had set in motion, the history in English that would be copied into monasteries across Wessex — recorded his death and continued. Other men wrote in the same register, in the same language, in the same discipline of laconic truth. The tradition he had begun outlasted him. That was what he had intended. That was what he had worked out in the marshes, when the bread burned and he sat rebuked by a woman who did not know his name, and thought about what a kingdom actually was.

It was the record. It was the tongue. It was the law written down in the language of the people who would have to live by it.

It was still there.
