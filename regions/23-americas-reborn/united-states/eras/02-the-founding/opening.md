# The Founding — The Experiment Begins
## opening.md

Philadelphia, September 1787. The State House has been locked and shuttered for four months. The windows are nailed shut against the summer heat and the ears of passersby. The room smells of candle wax and damp wool and the particular exhaustion of men who have been arguing in close quarters for weeks. Inside, the delegates work without public record. James Madison sits near the front, writing. He writes everything. He writes faster than he sleeps. At the end of each session he goes back to his lodgings and expands his shorthand notes into full sentences, and those sentences will eventually become the most complete account of what happened in that room, and for more than thirty years no one will see them, because Madison has promised to keep them secret until the last delegate dies. He is, at the moment, thirty-six years old and the last delegate is not going to die for a long time.

Outside the State House, enslaved people serve the delegates their meals.

This is not a detail added later for correction. It was true while it was happening. The men who were writing a government founded on the proposition that all legitimate authority derives from the consent of the governed were waited on by people whose consent had not been asked. The men who were designing the mechanisms of republican liberty were attended by people for whom the republic would not, for the moment, include liberty. This was visible to everyone in the room. Several of the delegates owned the people who served them. Several of the delegates were uncomfortable about this and said so, in the room, in the record Madison was keeping. Several of the delegates were comfortable about this and said so too.

The argument about what the republic would mean was happening at the same moment the republic was being invented. These are not sequential events. They are the same event.

---

The document they were drafting had already changed form twice. The Articles of Confederation, ratified in 1781, had governed the United States for six years and governed it badly. The states sent money to the Congress when they felt like it, which was not often enough. The Congress had no power to regulate trade between states, no power to tax, no power to compel. Shays' Rebellion in Massachusetts the previous year — debt-burdened farmers closing the courts by force — had crystallized the fear that republican government without sufficient central authority would collapse into something worse. The delegates in Philadelphia had been sent to revise the Articles. They threw them out and started over.

What emerged by September 17, when thirty-nine delegates signed the document, was something genuinely new. A federal republic of continental scale. A government divided against itself by design: legislative, executive, judicial, each checking the others. A Supreme Court that would have the authority to strike down laws that violated the Constitution. A written document that was not merely law but supreme law, amendable only through a process deliberately difficult, carrying an authority that would eventually come to feel less like statute and more like scripture. When Americans in later generations argued about what they were allowed to do or forbidden to do, they argued about what this document meant. The argument has not ended.

The Three-Fifths Compromise is in Article I, Section 2. It reads: "Representatives and direct Taxes shall be apportioned among the several States which may be included within this Union, according to their respective Numbers, which shall be determined by adding to the whole Number of free Persons, including those bound to Service for a Term of Years, and excluding Indians not taxed, three fifths of all other Persons." Three fifths of all other Persons. The delegates did not use the word "enslaved." They did not use the word "slave." They did not use any word that named the institution directly. They wrote "other Persons." They wrote "such Persons as any of the States now existing shall think proper to admit" when they meant the Atlantic slave trade, which Article I, Section 9 protected from federal interference until 1808. The language was precise and the language was chosen. The document that created the mechanisms of American freedom built those mechanisms, in part, on the legal architecture of bondage.

James Madison knew this. He had written, in the notes he was keeping, of slavery as a "moral and political depravity." He owned, at the time he wrote this, more than one hundred people. Thomas Jefferson, who had drafted the Declaration of Independence in 1776 and was now in Paris as Minister to France, writing letters to Madison about the need for a Bill of Rights, owned 175 people. The contradiction was not unknown to the people who were building it in. It was a compromise without which there would have been no union. The southern states would not ratify a document that threatened the institution that organized their economy. The northern states needed the south. The union was the calculation. The calculation was not hidden.

What this produced was a founding argument rather than a founding settlement. The Declaration had said all men are created equal. The Constitution had said three-fifths of all other Persons. Between these two sentences the United States would spend the next two and a half centuries arguing. The Civil War was this argument carried to its military conclusion. The Reconstruction Amendments were the attempt to settle it by law. The Civil Rights Movement was the attempt to enforce the settlement. The arguments in Congress and the courts and the streets today are the same argument, wearing different clothes.

The argument began with the document.

Madison finished his notes. The windows stayed shut. Outside, the people who had served the delegates cleared the dishes and went home to whatever home meant for them. The Constitution went to the states for ratification. Nine were needed. It took nine months.
