## Innovations Flagged

**1. The Curtain as Structural Device.** The chapter uses the mai's physical curtain as simultaneously a scene element, a governance mechanism, a metaphor for the state's operational logic, and the chapter's recurring structural motif. The curtain appears in every section: the mai watches from behind it (I), the dynasty carries it across the lake (II), the wars are directed from behind it (III), the legal system extends its reach (IV), the post-Alooma mais sit behind it without the power Alooma had (V), and it "falls" in the final line while the names persist (VI). This is a technique the rubric does not anticipate: using a single physical object from the culture's own practice as the chapter's structural backbone. It operates as both evidence and engine, grounding the analytical argument about sacred kingship in a concrete, sensory image. This could be considered under Immersion (the curtain keeps the reader inside the world) or under Progression (the curtain's changing meaning drives the arc) or as a technique that bridges both.

**2. The Chronicler-Within-the-Chronicle.** Ahmad ibn Fartuwa is both the chapter's anchor figure and the source of the chapter's primary evidence. The chapter is, in a sense, a narrative about its own primary source: we watch Ahmad write the document that historians will later read. This creates a self-referential structure that deepens rather than breaks immersion, because the self-reference belongs to the culture's own historiographic practice. The chapter does not step outside to evaluate Ahmad's chronicle; it inhabits the act of its composition. This technique could be considered under Evidence (using the source's own creation as narrative) or under Figures (the chronicler as both character and method).

## Overall Assessment

This chapter succeeds most fully in its structural conception: the tension between sacred immobility and strategic action, embodied in the curtain, generates genuine narrative momentum across all six sections. The Saifawa system of incorporation (layer, do not replace) works as the structural engine, visible in every major decision from the founding of Ngazargamu to the integration of firearms to the layering of Maliki law over local custom. The decline in Section V is handled without verdict, as gradual systemic thinning rather than catastrophe. The landing is strong, finding the culture's own persistence in the dual stream of written chronicle and oral memory. The main area holding the chapter back from the highest tier is the occasional drift toward analytical register in the system-description passages, where the narrator's voice becomes slightly more scholarly than the chronicler's voice that otherwise governs the prose.
