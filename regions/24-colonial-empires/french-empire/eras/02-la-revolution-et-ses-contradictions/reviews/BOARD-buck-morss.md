# Board Review — Susan Buck-Morss (Philosophical Assessment)
## Domain: Hegel and Haiti, universalism and colonial exclusion, philosophy of history
## French Empire Era 2: La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

**Role:** Professor of Political Philosophy, City University of New York Graduate Center. Author of *Hegel, Haiti, and Universal History* (2009, University of Pittsburgh Press). Her work demonstrates that Hegel's master-slave dialectic in the *Phenomenology of Spirit* was directly informed by the Haitian Revolution, which Hegel was following through the *Minerva* newspaper during the period of its composition. This review assesses the chapter's philosophical argument.

---

## The Philosophical Engine

The chapter's central argument is philosophical: that universalism generates its own negation. A declaration that all men possess natural rights cannot remain territorially bounded; it becomes a weapon in the hands of those excluded from its application. This is not irony — it is the logical structure of universalism itself. The chapter renders this correctly.

My work on Hegel and Haiti makes a related argument: the master-slave dialectic in the *Phenomenology* (1807) is usually taught as a philosophical abstraction, but Hegel wrote it in Jena while reading newspaper accounts of Toussaint Louverture. The Haitian Revolution was the most visible example in the world of enslaved people achieving self-consciousness through labor and struggle, which is precisely what the dialectic describes. Philosophy was not explaining history; history was generating philosophy, in real time, in the newspapers. The chapter could make this connection if it wished to extend its scope, but the board does not require it for the chapter's argument to stand.

## The Universalism-as-Contradiction Argument

The chapter frames the contradiction as residing in the French Enlightenment's own architecture: the same system that produced the Declaration produced the 128 categories, the same text that promised universal rights explicitly excluded its application to Saint-Domingue. This is the correct diagnosis. The chapter goes further: it argues that the exclusion was not accidental but structural, built into the Declaration's implementation even as its language remained universal.

What the chapter does not quite say but implies: the universalist principle cannot be partially applied. Once stated, it creates a standard that any exclusion must contradict. The enslaved did not need to derive a new political theory; they only needed to apply the one the French had announced. This is philosophically precise and historically accurate.

## The Terminal Condition

The chapter's terminal condition — the Declaration working "exactly as its words required" — is philosophically elegant. It refuses both the cynical reading (the Declaration was always hypocrisy) and the charitable reading (the Declaration eventually overcame its contradictions). It holds the third position: the Declaration was genuine in its universalism, and its genuine universalism is what destroyed the system that wrote it. This is the most philosophically sophisticated claim a chapter of this kind can make, and the chapter makes it.

## One Extension the Board Recommends

The board notes that the chapter does not mention Hegel's connection to the Haitian Revolution. This is a deliberate editorial choice — the chapter stays inside the French imperial vantage and does not extend to the broader philosophical implications. The board endorses this choice for this chapter. A subsequent era or a cross-reference note might address the philosophical legacy. The chapter as written is complete without it.

## Verdict

The philosophical argument is sound and is rendered through narrative rather than through philosophical terminology, which is the correct approach for a chapter of this kind. The universalism-as-weapon framing is defensible and is the chapter's most important intellectual contribution. The board endorses the chapter.
