# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel-2 Targeted Review — Notes: La Conquista y El Virreinato

The notes handle the Taki Onqoy well in the section plan — placing it in Section 3 as the earlier eruption of the same theological physics, 220 years before the uprising. This is the right structural move. When the chapter writes the Taki Onqoy, it must not frame it as "a precursor to" the uprising. It must be its own complete event — the Andean world's own response to the conquest in its own language — and the connection to the 1780 uprising should emerge from structural similarity, not authorial annotation.

The landing question I raised in Panel-1 is addressed in the notes: close within the era (1781), using the taki and the quipu and the name as the persistence mechanism. The colonial banning of Inca dress, portraits, and the quipu after the uprising is the right closing image — because what the Spanish banned tells you what they feared, which tells you what the Andean world still had. The chapter does not need to say "and this tradition survived into the twentieth century." The banning says it better.

My remaining concern for the chapter's execution: the Atahualpa execution in Section 2. The notes plan to render this from the Andean side — the Bible put to the ear, the ransom room. This must be handled with absolute care. The scene has been told many times, usually with the Spanish as the implied subjects whose clever (or brutal) act is the event. The Andean account — what it meant to see the Sapa Inca humiliated, held, promised life, killed anyway — is rarely rendered with the same weight. The chapter should begin with what Atahualpa's capture meant in Andean terms: the Sapa Inca was the axis between the human world and the cosmic order. His capture was not the loss of a king. It was the severing of the cord between worlds. The Andean people who witnessed it experienced it as a cosmological catastrophe — which is precisely why the taki onqoy arose thirty years later: the wak'as had been trying to speak, and people were finally able to hear them because the world had already ended.

The women's leadership — Micaela, Tomasa Titu Condemayta — must be present in the chapter at full weight. The notes commit to this. The chapter should not explain that women held leadership roles; it should simply show them holding them, as a matter of Andean reality, because in the Andean world this was not remarkable.
