## B-1 — Nehemia Levtzion — Board Review of "The Songhai Succession"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Political and economic history of medieval West African empires, trans-Saharan trade, the Arabic source tradition, and the transition from Mali to Songhai hegemony in the western Sudan. Author of *Ancient Ghana and Mali* (1973).

**Historical accuracy:**

- The date "853 of the Hijra (1449 CE)" is used as a narrative anchor for the opening. 853 AH corresponds roughly to 1449-1450 CE. This is a reasonable date for the mid-contraction period. There is no specific event documented for this exact year at Niani; the date serves as a plausible narrative anchor rather than as a claim about a documented event. Acceptable, but the chapter should be understood as setting a scene, not recording a specific occurrence.

- The Tuareg seizure of Timbuktu is placed "around 1433." The scholarly consensus, based primarily on the Tarikh al-Sudan and Tarikh al-Fattash, places this event in approximately 1433-1434. The Maghsharen Tuareg and their chief Akil ag Amalwal are correctly identified. This is accurate.

- "Portuguese ships appeared along the Senegambian coast in the 1440s and 1450s." This is correct. Portuguese maritime exploration reached the Senegambian coast under the direction of Prince Henry, with significant contact beginning in the 1440s. The chapter correctly notes that this introduced a competing trade route for Wangara gold but did not immediately collapse the trans-Saharan routes.

- Sonni Ali's accession is placed "around 1464." The Tarikh al-Sudan places it in the early 1460s; 1464 is within the conventional range. Acceptable.

- The fall of Timbuktu to Sonni Ali is dated to 1468. This is the standard date from the Tarikh al-Sudan. Correct.

- The siege of Djenne is described as lasting "seven years, seven months, and seven days," with the chapter correctly noting that "the precision is literary, not chronological." The Tarikh al-Sudan gives this figure, and the chapter's handling of it as a formulaic number is scholarly sound. Djenne's submission is dated "around 1473." This is within the conventional range. Correct.

- Askia Muhammad's coup is dated to 1493, at the "battle of Anfao." The Tarikh al-Sudan records this. The name "Anfao" (also rendered Angoo or Ango) is the traditional site. Correct.

- Askia Muhammad's hajj is dated 1496-97. This is the standard dating. The chapter notes he received "the title of Caliph of the Sudan" from "the Abbasid caliph in Cairo." The title is correctly reported. However, the authority in question was the Abbasid shadow caliph maintained by the Mamluk sultans in Cairo, not an independent Abbasid caliph. The distinction matters: this was a legitimating title granted by a figurehead maintained by the Mamluk state, not by a ruling caliph. The chapter should clarify or at minimum not imply that the Abbasid caliphate was an independent power at this date.

- Askia Muhammad's deposition is dated to 1528. Correct per the Tarikh al-Sudan.

- Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti's dates (1556-1627) are correct. His deportation to Marrakesh after the 1591 Moroccan invasion and his eventual return to Timbuktu are accurately described.

- The Moroccan invasion is dated to 1591. Correct.

- Sonni Ali's death is placed in 1492. The Tarikh al-Sudan records his death in this year, reportedly by drowning while crossing a river. The chapter says "the circumstances of his death are unclear; the sources hint at different things." This is fair; the Tarikh al-Sudan gives the drowning account but the circumstances have been debated.

- The Kamabolon ceremony at Kangaba, described as a seven-year ceremony maintained by the Diabate lineage, is accurately described. This is well documented in Jan Jansen's fieldwork.

**Cultural authenticity:**

- The chapter's central argument — that the Kurukan Fuga's redistribution mechanism was both the source of Mali's cohesion and the structural cause of its inability to adapt when resources shifted — is analytically sound and represents a genuine engagement with the political economy of the Mali Empire's decline.

- The treatment of Sonni Ali is notably fair. The Tarikh al-Sudan's hostility toward Sonni Ali is acknowledged as the scholars' perspective, not as historical fact, and the chapter notes that the Songhai jesere would have told the story differently. This is good scholarly practice.

- The chapter correctly identifies that the transition from Mali to Songhai hegemony in the Niger bend did not involve a single decisive military confrontation between the two states. The process was gradual, driven by Mali's inability to project power rather than by Songhai conquest of Mali proper.

**What needs correction:**

- **The Abbasid caliph in Cairo.** The chapter should clarify that this was the shadow caliph maintained by the Mamluk sultans, not an independent Abbasid authority. The distinction is relevant to what the title "Caliph of the Sudan" actually meant in practice.

- **Sonni Ali's marriage to a Djenne woman.** The Tarikh al-Sudan records that Sonni Ali married into the Djenne elite after the city's submission, but the specific claim should be attributed to the source rather than stated as unqualified fact, given the Tarikh's distance from the events.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

The chapter is historically sound in its major claims and analytically strong in its treatment of the structural causes of Mali's contraction. The Abbasid caliph clarification and the Djenne marriage attribution are the only items requiring correction. With these fixes, the chapter is defensible.
