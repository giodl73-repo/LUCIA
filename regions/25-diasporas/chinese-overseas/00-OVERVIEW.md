# 华侨 — Huáqiáo: The Overseas Chinese

Where there is commerce, there are Chinese. This is not a stereotype — it is a structural fact of the global economy. For more than a thousand years, Chinese merchants and their families have maintained trade networks across Southeast Asia, the Indian Ocean, the Pacific, and eventually the Americas, Africa, and Europe, organized around the principles of guanxi (关系 — relationship networks), dialect community solidarity, and the mutual obligation of kinship and shared origin. The overseas Chinese (huaqiao — literally "overseas sojourner") did not merely trade; they built communities, founded schools and temples, organized mutual aid societies (huiguan), and maintained their language, their food traditions, their religious practices (a synthesis of Buddhism, Taoism, ancestor veneration, and local gods), and their commitment to the education of the next generation wherever they went. The result is a global commercial and cultural diaspora whose coherence has outlasted empires and survived persecution.

The Chinese overseas experience is not a single story. The Hokkien (Fujian) traders who built the nanyang (南洋 — "Southern Seas," Southeast Asia) network from the Tang dynasty onward are different from the Cantonese gold rush miners of California (1849) and the Hakka laborers who built the Transcontinental Railroad. The Singapore Chinese who built a city-state are different from the Peranakan (Straits Chinese) who absorbed Malay culture over generations while maintaining Chinese identity. The Chinese of contemporary Africa — the new migration of the post-1990 era — are different from the nineteenth-century indentured laborers of Cuba and Peru. What they share is the structural fact of being Chinese outside China: the maintenance of Chinese identity in the absence of Chinese state authority, and the use of community networks — the clan association, the dialect group, the secret society — to provide what the state was not present to provide.

## What Defined Them

**Guanxi — the network as the business.** The concept of guanxi (relationship networks) is the operating principle of Chinese diaspora commerce. Business in this world is conducted not through formal contracts enforced by courts but through networks of personal relationship, mutual obligation, and shared trust. A Hokkien merchant in Singapore extends credit to a Hokkien merchant in Manila because they are from the same district, speak the same dialect, belong to the same clan association, and therefore share a web of mutual accountability that makes formal enforcement unnecessary. The efficiency of this system — in the context of Southeast Asian markets where formal legal institutions were weak or absent — made Chinese overseas merchants extraordinarily competitive. The same system operates today in the transnational Chinese business networks that connect Singapore, Hong Kong, Taiwan, and the mainland.

**The huiguan — the self-governing community.** Wherever Chinese settled in significant numbers, they organized themselves into huiguan — mutual aid associations organized by dialect group, by clan (surname), or by place of origin. The huiguan provided what the state (usually hostile or indifferent) would not: welfare for the sick and elderly, funeral arrangements for the dead, arbitration of disputes, protection from violence, employment networks for new arrivals, and schools for children. The huiguan were the institutions through which Chinese diaspora communities governed themselves in the absence of — and often in opposition to — the formal state. They were also, frequently, the vehicles through which organized crime operated (the triads emerged from the same organizational structure). The line between mutual aid and protection racket was always contested.

**The education obligation.** Chinese diaspora communities have consistently prioritized the education of children at a level that other diaspora communities have not always matched. The Chinese school (huaxiao) — teaching Chinese language, history, and values alongside or instead of the local curriculum — was established in virtually every Chinese diaspora community. The result is a diaspora that has maintained Chinese language literacy across multiple generations outside China (though the balance between Mandarin, Cantonese, Hokkien, and other varieties reflects the heterogeneity of the diaspora) and that has produced disproportionate educational achievement in every country of settlement. The emphasis on education is not merely instrumental — it is a Confucian value: learning is the path to becoming fully human.

**Anti-Chinese violence — the persistent threat.** The history of the Chinese overseas is punctuated by episodes of anti-Chinese violence that are among the most serious yet least-known mass atrocities of the modern era. The 1740 Batavia massacre (10,000 Chinese killed by the VOC and Indonesian allies); the 1871 Los Angeles Chinese Massacre; the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act; the 1965 Indonesian massacres in which Chinese Indonesians were targeted as communists (estimates of Chinese dead range from thousands to tens of thousands); the 1969 Malaysian race riots — these are the recurrent context of Chinese diaspora existence. The community response has typically been to become indispensable enough economically to make violence costly, and to maintain enough internal organization to protect their members. It is not a comfortable arrangement, and it is not always successful.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Nanyang:** A Hokkien junk captain from Quanzhou is navigating the straits between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra. He has been making this passage twice a year for twenty years. The ports along this route — Malacca, Palembang, Tuban — have Chinese quarters where he can find food, interpreters, and credit extended on the basis of his dialect and his clan. The network is older than any of the states that currently claim these ports.

**2 — Zheng He:** Between 1405 and 1433, the Chinese eunuch admiral Zheng He leads seven voyages into the Indian Ocean, reaching Arabia and East Africa. His fleet — the treasure ships, the largest wooden vessels ever built — is not a commercial enterprise; it is a diplomatic one, asserting Chinese imperial presence. After his death, the Ming court abandons the voyages. The Chinese overseas merchants continue. The state withdrawal is the private sector's opportunity.

**3 — The Railroad:** 1865: Chinese workers — some 10,000 to 20,000 of them, mostly from Guangdong Province — are building the western section of the US Transcontinental Railroad through the Sierra Nevada. They bore tunnels through granite. They lower themselves down cliffs in wicker baskets to place blasting charges. They receive lower wages than the Irish workers on the eastern section. When the railroad is completed in 1869, the celebration photographs do not include them.

**4 — Exclusion:** May 6, 1882: the US Congress passes the Chinese Exclusion Act — the only US law ever to exclude a specific nationality from immigration. It remains in force until 1943. The Chinatowns that survive it — San Francisco, New York, Chicago — do so by becoming self-sufficient communities that provide internally what the law denied externally: employment, housing, education, protection. The exclusion that was supposed to dissolve the Chinese community produces its most cohesive form.

**5 — Singapore:** Lee Kuan Yew takes Singapore to independence in 1965 — a city-state of 600 square kilometers with no natural resources, a majority Chinese population in a Malay region, and a strategic position at the junction of the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea. By 2000, Singapore has one of the highest per capita incomes in the world, one of the best public housing systems, and one of the lowest corruption rates. The overseas Chinese city-state has proved something about Chinese diaspora capacity. Whether it proves something more general about governance is the subject of ongoing debate.

**6 — The New Migration:** In 2024, Chinese business communities are present in nearly every country in Africa. They have built infrastructure, run shops, and established small manufacturing operations. They are resented as exploiters in some communities, welcomed as investors in others, and complicated in most. The Belt and Road Initiative has added state backing to what was previously diaspora network. The relationship between the new Chinese migrants and the old Chinese diaspora communities — who have been in these countries for generations — is itself a complex question about what it means to be Chinese overseas.
