# PANEL-1 Review — Wade Davis
## Bakumatsu Opening

**Reviewer:** Wade Davis (anthropological wonder)  
**Date:** 2026-04-16  

---

The opening demonstrates something crucial that is easy to miss: Japan's deliberate seclusion for two centuries was not a failure of knowledge or capability. It was a choice — a *sophisticated* choice, a strategic choice, rooted in a particular vision of how a society should organize itself. The opening never exoticizes this. It presents it as normal, as the ground truth of Tōjirō's world.

This is the hardest thing to do in historical writing — to show that a completely different system of organizing knowledge, trade, military power, and authority was not "primitive" but *different*. The sakoku edict was not the result of weakness. It was the result of strength: the strength to enforce a boundary even in the face of enormous economic incentive to violate it.

Tōjirō has served for thirty years in a system that works. The edict is not explained as an unfortunate cultural artifact that needs to be overcome. It is presented as an elegant solution to a problem the shogunate identified: a closed system prevents the vulnerability that comes with foreign entanglement. This is not correct or incorrect — it is a *choice*, and the opening respects the intelligence of that choice.

The black ships arrive, and Tōjirō's world is forced to confront the fact that his system's logic — "we can enforce the boundary" — is confronted by a force that moves in ways his system cannot anticipate. The American ships are not described as "advanced civilization." They are described by what they do: they move against wind and tide with ease, they ignore the boundary, they present an option (negotiation) that the edict does not allow.

What Wade Davis understands is that cultures are *knowledge systems*. Japan's two-century closure produced knowledge about how to maintain social order without external trade. The appearance of the black ships requires knowledge about how to engage with external power without losing the internal order. Tōjirō's hesitation is the moment when two knowledge systems first touch, and neither yet understands the other.

**On the representation of the Japanese knowledge system:**
- The edict as law (governance knowledge)
- The boundary as maintainable through discipline (social knowledge)
- The assumption that authority flows through established channels (political knowledge)
- The expectation that the world respects Japan's decision (international knowledge)

All of this is presented not as primitive or quaint, but as the foundation of a functional society. When the black ships arrive, it is not that Japan is primitive and needs civilization. It is that two sophisticated knowledge systems are colliding.

**The image I cannot stop thinking about:** "The mathematical defiance of it." You do not say the Americans have better technology. You show that their ships move in ways that defy the expected relationships of size, wind, and water. This is *seeing* through Japanese eyes, not through a Western observer's interpretation.

**One question for the full chapter:**
The Meiji Restoration will eventually choose to adopt Western knowledge, Western military technology, Western institutional forms. Does this opening plant the seeds for that choice? I think it does — by showing that Japan's system is coherent but not inevitable, and that confrontation with a different system forces reconfiguration. The tragedy and the triumph of Meiji is that Japan chose to *adopt* Western forms rather than simply resist them. That choice is already implicit in Tōjirō's hesitation.

Keep this discipline. Do not exoticize. Do not rank. Show how two complete systems of understanding power, trade, and order encounter each other.

The opening does this beautifully.
