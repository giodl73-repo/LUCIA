## Re-Explanation Check

The compression editor looks for: scenes followed by explanation of what they showed; redundancies between sections; "this shows that" / "in other words" constructions; transitions that explain what the reader already understood.

**Section: "The Road That Was the Sea" — canoe-hull thinning passage:**
"There was no measurement he could show you. The knowledge lived in his hands."

These two sentences follow the description of the hull-thinning process. They are correct — they land the scene — but the second sentence ("The knowledge lived in his hands") articulates what the preceding narrative had already demonstrated through the showing. The scene earned its landing with the first sentence alone. The second sentence is not quite re-explanation (it does add something — "his hands" as the locus of knowledge), but it tips slightly toward instructing the reader. Flagged as P3 (author decides). If kept, it is defensible; if cut, the scene is stronger.

**Section: "The Book and the Dictionary" — Breton characterization:**
"He was more sympathetic to the Kalinago than most Europeans of his era, and less sympathetic than the Kalinago deserved."

This sentence in the figures.md is elegantly compressed. In the chapter itself, Breton's limitations are rendered through the dictionary entries and his inability to translate specific words. The chapter does not need to state the assessment of him directly because the scene carries it. The sentence is in figures.md, which is appropriate — the figures document can state what the chapter should show. No action needed in chapter.md.

**Section: "What the Sea Keeps" — Landing:**
"The proof was not abstract — it was written in the record of failed settlements, withdrawn expeditions, and the ongoing cost of attempting to hold islands whose mountains and seas refused to cooperate."

This sentence follows the assertion that the Kalinago had "given 150 years of proof." The list that follows explains the proof rather than trusting the reader to know what proof means in this context. The chapter has spent 5,000 words building exactly this record; the reader does not need the list to understand the claim. Consider cutting the sentence after the dash — "The proof was not abstract" is enough, or cut this sentence entirely and let the preceding narrative carry the claim. The list is redundant.
