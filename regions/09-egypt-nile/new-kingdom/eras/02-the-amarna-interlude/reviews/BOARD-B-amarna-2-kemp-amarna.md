## BOARD — Barry Kemp (Amarna) — Review of The Amarna Interlude

**Overall:** The chapter engages seriously with the archaeological evidence from Akhetaten. The workers' village evidence is correctly represented, and the gap between official theology and private practice is one of the chapter's strongest moments.

**Factual accuracy:**

1. **City construction:** "Built fast and it looked like it" is accurate. Mud brick, gypsum plaster, painted rather than carved decoration for most structures. Stone limited to temples and major palace buildings. ACCURATE.

2. **Workers' village:** Small houses, communal ovens, water by donkey. ACCURATE. The domestic deity evidence (Bes, Taweret figurines) is one of the most important findings from the excavation and the chapter uses it well. The line "the hand is harder to police than the temple" is not an archaeological claim but an inference, and a reasonable one.

3. **Great Aten Temple:** Open to the sky, no enclosed sanctuary, offering tables in the hundreds. ACCURATE. The reversal of the traditional progressive-darkness principle is correctly identified as the defining architectural innovation. The chapter correctly notes that this was a theological statement in architectural form.

4. **Desert environment:** The description of heat, wind, dust, and the cliffs radiating stored heat is consistent with the site's conditions. The absence of a natural spring is a key fact about the site's logistical challenges. ACCURATE.

5. **Amarna art:** The description of the family scenes (daughters on laps, kissing, the princess eating duck) is drawn from well-known reliefs. The interpretation as a "visual argument" about the Aten's relationship with creation through the royal family model is a fair reading of the evidence.

**Concerns:**

1. The chapter describes the city as "roughly halfway between Waset and the delta." This is approximately correct but slightly misleading. The site is closer to the midpoint of the Nile valley's cultivated length than to a geographic midpoint between Thebes and the delta. A minor point.

2. The workers' village section could note that the village was walled and controlled, with a single entrance, suggesting that the workforce's movements were restricted. This is relevant to the question of how the official theology was enforced (or not enforced) at the domestic level.

**Verdict:** The archaeological representation is sound. The chapter uses the Akhetaten evidence as it should be used: as the material reality beneath the theological program. The domestic deity evidence is handled with exactly the right interpretive weight. No revisions required.
