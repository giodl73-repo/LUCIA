# Brunei — Melayu Islam Beraja

We are the Sultanate of Brunei, and our identity has three pillars that we have made inseparable: Melayu (Malay), Islam, and Beraja (monarchy). This is not a political slogan — it is a description of how our civilization is constituted. The Malay language and its courtly literature, the Islamic faith and its legal and ethical framework, and the sovereign authority of the sultan and his aristocratic network are not three separate things layered on top of each other; they are three aspects of a single reality, and the word for that reality is Brunei.

At our height — from the fifteenth century through the seventeenth — we were the dominant maritime power of the western Pacific island world. Our sultan's authority extended over the coasts of Borneo, north into the Sulu Archipelago, south into Java's trading contacts, and east toward the Visayan islands of the Philippines. Our capital at Bandar (now Bandar Seri Begawan) sat at the mouth of the Brunei River on the northwest coast of Borneo, commanding the trade routes that connected the spice islands to China, India, and the Arabian world. The Chinese called us P'o-ni; the Malay world called us the crown of Borneo. We called ourselves Brunei, and we are still here.

## What Defined Them

**Melayu Islam Beraja — the trinity of Brunei identity.** The governing ideology of Brunei — Malay, Islamic, monarchical — was not invented by modern politicians. It is the authentic description of how Brunei organized itself for six centuries. The Malay language and literary tradition provided the cultural medium; Islam provided the legal and ethical framework and connected Brunei to the global Muslim world; and the monarchy — the sultan as the apex of an aristocratic hierarchy — provided political order. The three pillars reinforce each other: the sultan is the defender of the faith; the faith validates the sultan's authority; the Malay tradition gives both their cultural form.

**The entrepôt economy.** Brunei's prosperity rested on its position as a collecting and distributing point for the trade of Borneo. The forests of the island interior produced camphor, beeswax, hornbill ivory, forest resins, and the birds' nests prized by Chinese cuisine. The rivers that drained the Bornean interior all passed through or near the coast where Brunei's traders operated. The sultan's customs officers taxed the trade that flowed through the capital's wharves; the merchant networks — Malay, Chinese, Javanese, Arab — brought goods in and took goods out. Brunei did not produce the forest products itself; it organized their extraction and connection to global markets.

**The Philippine reach.** At Brunei's height, the sultanate's political and religious influence extended north and east into the Philippine Archipelago. The Islamic settlement of Maynila (pre-Spanish Manila) was connected to Brunei by trade and religion; the Sulu Sultanate was an offshoot of the Bornean Islamic world that Brunei represented. The first Spanish expedition against Manila (1570–71) encountered Muslim traders and rulers whose religious and commercial connections ran back to Borneo. Brunei's reach into the Philippines was not conquest but the natural extension of an Islamic maritime commercial network.

**The Chinese connection.** Brunei maintained formal tribute relations with the Ming court from the late fourteenth century, and a Brunei sultan (Muhammad Shah) died and was buried in Nanjing in 1408 — his tomb is still there, a monument to the depth of the Sino-Bruneian connection. The Chinese communities in Brunei were commercial partners and eventually permanent residents; the fusion of Malay and Chinese cultural elements in Brunei's aristocratic material culture reflects centuries of this contact. The Chinese records of P'o-ni are among the most detailed descriptions of Brunei's early history.

**Survival through adaptation.** Brunei's modern existence — as the smallest but wealthiest Bornean state, with a population under half a million sitting on the oil reserves that sustain one of the world's highest per-capita incomes — represents the completion of a long adaptation strategy. The sixteenth-century sultanate that commanded the Philippine trade routes contracted under Spanish, Dutch, and British pressure into the small coastal enclave that Brunei is today. The discovery of oil in 1929, and independence in 1984, translated the old Melayu Islam Beraja framework into a modern rentier state. The sultan is still the sultan; the ideology is still the ideology; the scale has changed.

## The Chapters

**1 — P'o-ni Sends Tribute:** The Brunei mission arrives at the Ming court. They carry forest products — camphor, beeswax, hornbill ivory. They receive a patent of recognition and trading rights at Fujian ports. The relationship is established. A century later, a Brunei sultan will die on Chinese soil, his tomb a permanent monument in Nanjing.

**2 — The First Sultan:** The conversion of Brunei's ruling house to Islam is recorded in the genealogies as a specific event: the arrival of a Muslim teacher, the sultan's profession of faith, the renaming that signals the new identity. Whether the exact genealogy is history or legitimating mythology is less important than what it established: Brunei is a Muslim polity, and that fact will organize its identity for the next six centuries.

**3 — The Golden Age Court:** The court at Bandar is receiving trade missions from Sulu, from the Visayas, from Java, from China. The sultan's protocol officers manage the ceremony; the harbor masters collect the duties; the khatib delivers the Friday sermon in the grand mosque. The capital is at the apex of the Bornean Islamic world, and everyone who passes through knows it.

**4 — The Spanish Expedition:** The Spanish fleet from Manila attacks Brunei in 1578. They capture the capital. They stay for several weeks. Then disease and Bruneian resistance drive them out. The capital is recovered. The sultan returns. The trade resumes. The Spanish attack did not end Brunei; it demonstrated that Brunei was too deeply rooted to be uprooted by a raid.

**5 — Brooke's Sarawak:** James Brooke, the English adventurer, receives Sarawak from the Brunei sultan in 1841 in exchange for help suppressing a rebellion. One territory gone. More cessions follow. The sultanate contracts, island by island, river mouth by river mouth, until what remains is the small enclave that will become the modern state. The sultan remains. The ideology remains. The territory is much smaller.
