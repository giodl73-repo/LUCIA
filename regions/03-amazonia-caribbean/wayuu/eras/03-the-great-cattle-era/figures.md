# Figures — Era 3: The Great Cattle Era (c. 1700–1900 CE)

---

## On the Record

The Great Cattle Era is not an era of named rulers and chronicled events. Wayúu society had no kings. The eirruku had senior women and maternal uncles who held authority within the clan's decisions, but authority did not concentrate into a single figure that outside observers would record, follow, and memorialize. Colonial documents at Riohacha name occasional "caciques" — a Caribbean term the Spanish applied to anyone who seemed to lead a group — but the men named in these documents were intermediaries in the trade relationship, not Wayúu rulers in any sense the Wayúu would have recognized.

What the colonial record preserves are transactions, frustrations, and names attached to specific disputes or agreements. From these fragments, along with the ethnographic record of palabrero practice and the oral tradition as recorded by Michel Perrin and others, the figures in this chapter are drawn. Where composite, this is noted. The thinness of the named record is itself evidence: the Wayúu social structure was not designed to produce figures that outside authorities could address, because the authority structure was distributed through the eirruku networks in ways that made any single "leader" a misreading of how decisions were actually made.

## Seyuuya — Woman of the Epinayuu (composite, c. 1780–1830)

Seyuuya does not appear in any colonial document. She is a composite figure drawn from the ethnographic record of Wayúu women's social roles in the cattle era: the keeper of the eirruku's daily rhythms, the transmitter of mochila knowledge to daughters, the one who reads the landscape for water and weather. She is also representative of the category of Wayúu women who, during this era, were the actual holders of the matrilineal authority that the Spanish kept misidentifying as belonging to men.

Her name is Wayuunaiki — it belongs to the Guajira, not to any colonial document. Her eirruku, the Epinayuu, is one of the historically documented Wayúu clans with a presence in the Alta Guajira during the 18th and 19th centuries.

Her knowledge is specific: she reads the sky for rain, she reads her daughter's thread for error, she knows the difference between a goat herd's normal range and an unusual drift. This knowledge is not presented in the chapter as surprising or remarkable. In the Wayúu world, this knowledge is ordinary — which means it was distributed across the women of the eirruku, maintained across generations, and treated as the kind of competence any responsible woman would develop. The chapter does not praise Seyuuya for being exceptional. She is representative.

## Jayariyuu — Palavrero (historically grounded composite, c. 1790–1810)

The palavrero tradition is extensively documented, and José Polo Acuña's 2012 study of the Guajira in the colonial-to-republican transition includes references to specific palabrero lineages and cases from the Riohacha archive. One figure, identified in colonial documents as representing the interests of the Epinayuu eirruku in cattle disputes with neighboring clans and with Spanish traders, appears across several documents in the 1790s and 1800s. His Hispanicized name in the records is probably approximate; the chapter uses a plausible Wayuunaiki name — Jayariyuu — that belongs to the register of names associated with the pütchipü'üi tradition.

His characterization is derived from Weildler Guerra Araújo's *La disputa y la palabra* (2002), the definitive study of Wayúu conflict resolution. The palavrero is not a judge. He holds no coercive authority. His tool is knowledge — of precedent, of the emotional register of both parties, of the material values assigned to different categories of injury, and of the long-term relationships between eirruku that any settlement must preserve or damage. The chapter shows him performing the calculation that makes a settlement hold: finding the framing that both parties can accept as a resolution rather than a defeat.

The specific case in the chapter — an Epinayuu cattle drift into Wouriyu pasture, two dead calves, settled as a gift of solidarity rather than an admission of liability — is plausible rather than documented. The parameters of the dispute (dry-season drift, pasture damage, calves lost) are derived from the types of cases Polo Acuña documents. The settlement mechanism (material acknowledgment framed as relationship maintenance rather than legal liability) is drawn from Araújo Pana's analysis of how the pütchipü'üi system typically resolves cases where culpability is ambiguous.

## Antonio de Narváez y la Torre — Colonial Governor (historical figure, 1740s–1812)

Narváez y la Torre is a real person. He served as governor of the province of Riohacha in the 1770s–1780s and later participated in colonial politics across Nueva Granada. His reports to the viceroyalty at Bogotá are preserved in the Archivo General de Indias and include extended commentary on the impossibility of administering the Guajira cattle trade under Spanish legal categories.

His document quoted in the chapter — the description of the property owner who "does not exist, or is always elsewhere, or is always multiple, or is always a woman who does not engage in commerce" — is paraphrased from his actual administrative correspondence, which registers the matrilineal structure as a bureaucratic problem without naming it in anthropological terms. The chapter uses his frustration as structural evidence: the colonial record's failure to capture the Wayúu property system is not an absence of information but a form of information. What he could not measure is exactly what prevented him from measuring it.

He is not a villain in the chapter. He is a capable administrator operating in a legal framework that could not accommodate what he was encountering. His records are useful precisely because his failure to administer the Guajira is documented in his own hand.

## The Senior Women of the Wouriyu Eirruku (composite, unnamed)

The women who receive Jayariyuu at the Wouriyu encampment and deliberate on the settlement are named figures in the logic of Wayúu governance, though not in any colonial archive. In Wayúu social structure, the senior women of the eirruku — the mothers and grandmothers of the matrilineal line — hold the real authority in decisions about the clan's resources. The maternal uncles negotiate externally; the senior women sanction internally.

The chapter shows them waiting, deliberating for an hour, and then accepting. Their deliberation is not shown in detail — this is both accurate (palavrero negotiations are traditionally conducted between the palabrero and the male representatives, with the women conferring separately) and an acknowledgment of the chapter's limits. ## Note on Voice and Composite Figures

The decision to use composite figures for Seyuuya and Jayariyuu rather than purely fictional characters follows the standard established across LUCIA for eras where the oral and ethnographic record is rich but the named individual record is sparse. A composite figure drawn from the documented patterns of Wayúu life in this era is more historically grounded than a fully invented character, and more honest than pretending that named individuals can be recovered where they cannot.

The risk of composite figures is romanticization — the tendency to make the composite exemplary, heroic, or representative of the culture's best rather than its real. The chapter attempts to avoid this by giving Seyuuya specific practical concerns (a missing goat, a daughter's technical error) rather than symbolic weight, and by giving Jayariyuu's palavrero work its real complexity (the calculation between legal liability and relationship maintenance) rather than presenting it as straightforwardly wise or effective. Palavrero cases sometimes fail. Not every settlement holds. The chapter's case holds because the conditions were right for it to hold, not because palabreros always succeed.
