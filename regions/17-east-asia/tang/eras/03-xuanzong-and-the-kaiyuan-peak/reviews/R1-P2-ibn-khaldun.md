## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of The High Tang (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening is vivid and well-placed, but it privileges the surface of cosmopolitanism without yet examining the structural conditions that produced it — or the structural weaknesses that would destroy it. The beauty is present. The mechanism is not yet visible.

**What works:**
- The keju examination system is introduced through Du Fu's failure, which does more than any explanatory paragraph could. Two hundred thousand candidates, fewer than thirty pass — the numbers alone reveal a system that produces enormous human investment for minimal reward. This is a machine for creating a literate bureaucracy and a generation of frustrated talent. Both matter.
- The kaiyuan tongbao passage is strong: a standardized currency from Guangzhou to the Tarim Basin is not merely a trade detail — it is evidence of institutional capacity. The mint as "common language" is precisely the kind of structural insight I look for.
- The phrase "the architecture of a confidence so immense that it could welcome any god without fearing for its own" captures the essential dynamic of Tang cosmopolitanism — it was a product of security, not idealism.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening does not yet explain WHY the Tang were confident. Confidence is asserted — "a civilization that believed itself the center of everything under heaven" — but the material foundations (the equal-field land system, the fubing military system, the Grand Canal's grain supply, the Silk Road revenues) are absent. Without these, the cosmopolitanism floats.
- An Lushan is introduced as a name and a threat but not as a product of the system. He was a frontier general in the jiedushi system — a system the Tang created to manage their borders. The rebellion was not an accident that befell a golden age; it was a consequence of the structures that produced the golden age. The opening should at least gesture toward this.

**The question I'd push on:**
The Kaiyuan period was not simply a cultural peak that an external shock destroyed. The conditions that made Chang'an cosmopolitan — the frontier military system, the reliance on non-Chinese generals, the concentration of wealth at the center — were the same conditions that made the rebellion possible. Will the full chapter trace this causal chain, or will it present the rebellion as a fall from grace?
