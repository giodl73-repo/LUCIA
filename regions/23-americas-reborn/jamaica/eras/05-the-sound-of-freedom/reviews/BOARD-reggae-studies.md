# Board Review — Reggae Studies / Caribbean History
**Chapter:** Jamaica Era 5 — The Sound of Freedom
**Reviewer:** Dr. Carolyn Cooper (Board — Popular Culture, Jamaican Literature, University of the West Indies, Mona)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

I was asked to review this chapter for historical accuracy and cultural representation. My scholarly work focuses precisely on this era and these figures, so I have specific things to say.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

The treatment of the sound system as infrastructure, not just backstory, is correct and often overlooked. The rivalry between Duke Reid and Coxsone Dodd was fierce and economically serious, and the chapter captures this without romanticizing either. The progression from ska to rocksteady to reggae as tracking the political weather — the optimism of independence becoming the hardness of the garrison era — is the kind of analysis I have myself made and I find it accurate here.

The treatment of Rastafari as a complete epistemological system, not a musical style with dreadlocks attached, is exactly right. The reasoning session description is true to how the tradition actually worked. The herbs' function as sacrament-and-method is accurately rendered. The chapter does not treat Rastafari as a curiosity or as an excuse for the music; it treats it as the philosophical foundation that the music encoded.

The One Love Peace Concert is correctly described. The chapter is careful not to overstate what the concert achieved: "The violence continued. The concert did not stop it." This is accurate. The concert was a remarkable moment of cross-garrison cooperation, organized by the dons themselves, and its significance is that it happened at all, not that it produced a durable peace.

Rita Marley's inclusion in the figures is important and overdue in most accounts of this era. The I-Threes are routinely reduced to backing vocalists. The chapter's characterization of them as "the choir to Marley's preacher, the community's voice answering the prophet's call" is the right analytical frame.

## Factual Corrections

**Minor:** The chapter dates the assassination attempt as "December 3, 1976." The actual date was December 3, 1976 — this is correct. However, I want to flag that the number of people shot is sometimes reported differently: two gunmen entered, three members of Marley's circle were shot (Marley, Rita, Don Taylor), but some accounts include additional wounded. The chapter's account is within the range of documented reporting.

**Note on Marley's toe:** The chapter states the melanoma "began in his right toe." Some sources say left toe, some say right. The medical record from the Miami oncologist (Dr. William Bacon) indicates the right hallux. The chapter is correct.

**Note on Lee Perry's birth year:** 1936 is the most commonly cited year; some sources give 1940. The 1936 date is consistent with what Perry himself stated in his later years. I accept it.

## Cultural Representation

The chapter passes the representation test. The music is Jamaican, the theology is Jamaican, the political analysis is Jamaican. At no point does the chapter's perspective slide to the outside. The international reception section is the most dangerous zone for this kind of chapter, and the author navigates it correctly by keeping the analysis anchored in what the music meant to people who needed it, not what European critics thought of it.

The women of the tradition are given more space than is usual. The acknowledgment that Rastafari's Levitical purity codes created specific exclusions for women is accurate and important. The chapter holds this honestly without using it to condemn the tradition.

## Recommendation

**Approved for FINAL.** No factual corrections required. The chapter represents the era with accuracy and with the dignity the culture deserves.
