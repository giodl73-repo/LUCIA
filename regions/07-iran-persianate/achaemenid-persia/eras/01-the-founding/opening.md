# The Founding — Cyrus and Cambyses (c. 550–522 BCE)
## Opening

The priests of Marduk knew the god's will before the army arrived.

In the year that Nabonidus, king of Babylon, took himself to Tayma in the desert and left the city without a king to perform the Akitu festival, Marduk's temple stood without its necessary rites. The New Year ceremony required a king to come before the god, to have his ears pulled by the high priest, to kneel and declare that he had not sinned against Babylon, that he had not struck the face of any protected person, that the city had not been neglected. Nabonidus was gone. The Akitu had not been performed. Year after year the god waited, and the city learned what it meant to be abandoned by its own king.

The Persian army crossed the Opis line and the Babylonian forces broke. The city itself did not resist. Gobryas, Cyrus's general, entered Babylon in the month of Tashritu — the seventh month, season of harvest — and the gates opened before him. Sixteen days later, Cyrus himself rode in. The Babylonian chronicles record this with the precision of temple accountants: the exact month, the exact sequence of events, the gates that were not broken, the people who were not harmed. When Cyrus entered, he came on foot. He took the hands of the god.

What he placed in the sanctuary afterward was a clay cylinder, baked and inscribed in Akkadian, the language of every king who had ruled this city since the world was young. The inscription said: *Marduk, the great lord, bestowed on me as my destiny the great magnanimity of one who loves Babylon, and I daily attended to his worship. My numerous troops moved about undisturbed in the midst of Babylon. I did not allow anyone to frighten the people of Sumer and Akkad. I sought the welfare of the city of Babylon and all its sacred centers.*

The cylinder did not say: I, Cyrus of Parsa, have conquered this city and its god now answers to me. It said: the god chose me. The god had been waiting. Now the god's city would be restored.

The priests accepted this. They had been waiting for a king who would perform the rites. Here was one.

---

This was not a deception. Or rather: it was a deception that was also true. Cyrus had taken Babylon by force of arms. His cavalry and infantry had moved through the countryside before the city fell. But the priests of Marduk had been in correspondence with his agents. The Nabonidus Chronicles — the records the temple scribes kept of these years — describe the Babylonian king's failures with a contempt that reads less like history than like a prosecution brief. Nabonidus had promoted the moon-god Sin above Marduk. Nabonidus had removed cult statues from their proper temples. Nabonidus had been absent when the festivals required a king to be present. When the Persians came, the temple establishment was not conquered. It was relieved.

Cyrus understood this. His cylinder was the documentation of an arrangement that had already been made. He would honor Marduk. He would restore the cult statues that Nabonidus had displaced. He would permit the peoples of the surrounding territories — the text lists them by name: the Ashur, the Eshnunna, the Zamban, the Me-Turnu, the Der — to return to their homes and their temples, carrying their gods with them. The gods who had been brought to Babylon in previous conquests, who had stood in foreign sanctuaries for generations, would go home. The peoples would follow their gods home.

This was the principle that Cyrus had already applied in Lydia, where Croesus ruled, and where the temples of the Greek cities on the Aegean coast had been left intact. It was the principle he would apply in every territory the Persians absorbed. It was not tolerance as moderns understand the word — the private freedom to believe what you choose. It was something more structural: the recognition that the gods of conquered peoples were real, that their sacred orders were legitimate, that a king who honored them would receive in return the legitimacy those gods could grant.

In the world Cyrus moved through, this was not a theological novelty. The Mesopotamian tradition had long held that the gods gave and withdrew their blessing from kings. Nabonidus had lost Marduk's favor. Cyrus had received it. The transfer was documented in clay.

---

The empire Cyrus was building had no precedent in scale. Lydia had fallen first, in 547 BCE — the wealthiest kingdom in the western world, whose king Croesus had given his name to the standard of riches so absolute it still carries his memory. Media had already been absorbed: Cyrus had deposed his maternal grandfather Astyages, the Median king, and made the Median nobility partners rather than prisoners. Now Babylon. To the east lay Bactria, Sogdia, the territories that ran toward the Indus. To the south, Egypt, which Cambyses his son would take. The empire that would result stretched from the Aegean coast to the borders of India, from the Caspian steppe to the first cataracts of the Nile — more than four thousand kilometers of diverse peoples, languages, gods, and landscapes held in one administrative structure.

Cyrus would not live to see all of it completed. He died in 530 BCE on the northeastern frontier, fighting the Massagetae queen Tomyris, by whose hands or by whose army accounts disagree. He was given a tomb at Pasargadae, the city he had built in the highlands of Parsa — a simple stone building on a stepped platform, surrounded by a garden. The inscription, which later visitors copied before it weathered away, said: *O man, I am Cyrus, who founded the empire of the Persians and was king of Asia. Grudge me not therefore this monument.*

The priests of Marduk did not grudge him. They had what they needed: a king who came before the god, who pulled back his ears, who knelt and declared that the city had not been neglected. Every Achaemenid king who followed him would return to Babylon, would walk into the sanctuary, would perform the same ceremony. The god required this. The king provided it. The empire was built, in part, from this recurring act of acknowledgment — the most powerful ruler in the world, kneeling before the god of a city he had conquered, year after year, because the city's priests required it and the empire's legitimacy demanded it.

What Cyrus understood, and what the cylinder encoded, was that an empire of fifty million people speaking dozens of languages and honoring hundreds of gods could not be governed by requiring everyone to become Persian. It could only be governed by finding, in each place, the sacred order that the people there already recognized — and placing the Persian king inside it, not above it.

The cylinder in the temple was not a proclamation of conquest. It was an application to govern.

Marduk accepted it. So did the people of Babylon. So did, in various forms and languages and theological frameworks, the peoples of Lydia, Media, Egypt, Bactria, and Sind. The empire that resulted was not a pyramid with Persian power at the apex and subject peoples at the base. It was a network: Ahura Mazda at the center of the Persian king's own devotion, and from that center, an acknowledgment radiating outward to every sacred order in the world — Marduk and Sin and the gods of the Greek temples and the fire altars of the Magi and the cults of Egypt's many-named divine — each held in its proper place, each receiving its due, each granting in return the legitimacy that no empire could manufacture on its own.

This was the founding insight. It would hold the empire together for two hundred years.
