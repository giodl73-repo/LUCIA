# Figures — Topa Inca's Reach (Era 3)

## Topa Inca Yupanqui, r. c. 1471–1493 CE

Tenth Sapa Inca. Son of Pachacuti. Extended Tawantinsuyu to its maximum territorial extent. Conquered the Chimu kingdom, campaigned into Ecuador, and reached the Bio-Bio River frontier in the south.

**What we know:** Topa Inca is well attested in the chronicles (Cieza, Betanzos, Sarmiento, Cobo). His campaigns are the best-documented of the pre-contact period, particularly the Chimu conquest and the southern expansion. Archaeological evidence corroborates the road extension and the establishment of provincial centers during this period.

**What we cannot know:** His strategic reasoning is inferred from outcomes. The decision to stop at the Bio-Bio is attributed in the chapter to structural logic (the cost-benefit calculation of the reciprocal system) rather than to any recorded statement, because no such statement survives. This is honest inference, not invented interiority.

**Sources:** Cieza de Leon (1553); Sarmiento de Gamboa (1572); D'Altroy, *The Incas* (2002); Hyslop (1984).

## Minchancaman

Last independent ruler of the Chimu kingdom. Defeated by Topa Inca's campaign, brought to Qusqu as a respected hostage.

**What we know:** Minchancaman is named in the colonial chronicles as the Chimu ruler who submitted to the Inca after the conquest. His relocation to Cusco is consistently reported. The archaeological evidence at Chan Chan shows a period of Inca occupation and administration following the conquest.

**Sources:** Cieza de Leon (1553); Moseley, *The Incas and Their Ancestors* (2001).

## The Chimu Smith (composite)

A metalworker from Chan Chan relocated to Qusqu after the Chimu conquest.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual Chimu smith from this period can be identified. The relocation of Chimu smiths to Cusco is well attested in the chronicles and confirmed by the appearance of coastal metalworking styles in highland Inca artifacts. The description of metalworking techniques is based on archaeological analysis of Chimu metalwork.

**Sources:** Moseley (2001); Lechtman, *Technologies of Power* (1993).

## The Mapuche Lonko (composite)

A leader of a Mapuche community south of the Bio-Bio.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual Mapuche lonko from the period of Inca contact can be identified in the Inca or colonial record. The description of Mapuche social organization (dispersed communities, autonomous lonkos, the koyang council) is drawn from ethnohistorical and ethnographic sources. The Mapuche's own oral traditions carry their account of the Inca encounter, but these are not fully represented in the sources available.

**Sources:** Dillehay, *Monuments, Empires, and Resistance* (2007); Bengoa, *Historia del pueblo mapuche* (1985).

## A Note on the Chimu

The Chimu kingdom deserves its own chapter in LUCIA (it exists in the Andes overview). The treatment here is necessarily from the Inca perspective, as this is an Inca chapter. The Chimu had a civilization as complex and as worthy of inside treatment as the Inca themselves, including the largest adobe city in the Americas, one of the most sophisticated irrigation systems in pre-modern history, and a metalworking tradition that exceeded the Inca's in skill and artistry. Their story told from inside would sound very different from their story told as a chapter in Inca expansion.
