## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of San, The Deep Time (Opening)

**Overall:** This opening treats the trance dance as what it is: a knowledge system of extraordinary depth and sophistication. I am satisfied that the San's way of knowing is not being reduced to "belief" or "ritual." But I want more.

**What works:**
- The n/um is not explained as a "belief." It is described as something the dancers feel, something that rises, something that has physical correlates (sweat, breathing, the bleeding from the nose). This is exactly right. The San's spiritual practice has measurable neurophysiological dimensions, and the opening honors both the experience and the knowledge that manages it.
- The therianthropes are connected to the eland, which is connected to n/um, which is connected to the hunt. The web of relationships is beginning to show. This is how indigenous knowledge systems work — not as isolated practices but as integrated understandings where the sacred, the ecological, and the practical are one system.
- "These are not decorations. They are theology, recorded on stone." Yes. The rock art tradition is treated as an intellectual achievement of the first order.

**What doesn't:**
- The tracking knowledge is absent from the opening. I understand the scene choice — the trance dance is the right entry point. But the tracking system is the other half of the San's epistemology: empirical, hypothetico-deductive, ecologically integrated. The chapter must give tracking the same depth as the trance dance. If the opening is the spiritual epistemology, the chapter must open the ecological epistemology with equal weight.
- The eland is mentioned but not yet fully realized. The eland is the keystone of the entire symbolic-ecological system: most sacred animal, most important food source, most difficult hunt, source of the fat used in medicine and paint. When a Ju/'hoan hunter kills an eland, it is simultaneously a material, social, and spiritual event. This integration is the San's distinctive intellectual achievement, and the chapter must render it fully.

**The question I'd push on:**
Will the chapter treat the San's ecological knowledge — tracking, plant identification, water-finding, weather prediction — as a genuine epistemology, or will it default to calling it "traditional knowledge"? The San tracking system involves the same cognitive operations as scientific hypothesis-testing. The chapter must show this without reducing it to Western categories. Show the tracker at work. Let the reader see the reasoning. Then the reader will understand without being told.
