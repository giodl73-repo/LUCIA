# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Notes Review: Kurdish Dynasties Era 3 — Saladin and the Ayyubid Moment

The bible has removed "Kurdish nationalist" from the framing notes and the author has noted the instruction to let the Kurdish worldview be the air rather than a position defended from outside. Good. Now the execution.

**The source asymmetry — addressed but needs more:**

The bible honestly names the problem: primary biographers are Arab and Persian, not Kurdish. This is the source-asymmetry condition that the v1.5 rubric amendment addresses. The technique that applies here is what the rubric calls "outsider-as-structural-evidence" — when an outside account is used, it reveals the structural relationship, not the culture's character.

Baha ad-Din saw Salah al-Din through an Islamic frame. His frame is evidence about what Salah al-Din needed to project to his contemporaries — the Islamic legitimizing language. The Kurdish register exists beneath that frame, visible in the family history, the commander networks, the specific methods. The chapter should use Baha ad-Din's frame as evidence of the surface, and the highland system as the explanation of the depths, and be explicit (without pedantry) about the distinction.

**Salah al-Din's own words:**

Baha ad-Din quotes Salah al-Din directly at several points. These are the closest we have to a Kurdish voice from inside the period. The chapter should use at least one direct quotation from Salah al-Din himself — in his own words, not paraphrased — and let that voice do work. It is not a Kurdish-language voice, but it is his voice.

**The ordinary Kurdish lord:**

The chapter plan is strong on Salah al-Din and the Ayyubid high politics. But the OVERVIEW's hook for Era 3 specifies: "the chapter must also hold the typical Kurdish story — local, durable, the valley lord negotiating pass-road taxes across dynasties." The production bible gives this one sentence in the intro (the highland-lord as context for Salah al-Din). The chapter must do more. At least one section or passage must show the non-exceptional Kurdish world during this period — the lords who served in Salah al-Din's armies and then went back to their valleys, who continued doing what Kurdish lords had always done while the world-historical moment unfolded.

This is not just completeness. It is the essential cultural argument: Salah al-Din is not the Kurdish story. He is the Kurdish story's exceptional outlier. The typical story is the valley lord. Without him, Salah al-Din floats free of his formation.

**The opening revision:**

The opening's first paragraph needs a physical anchor in the Tikrit house or the garrison yard — something that places the reader in the Kurdish world before it tells them about the Kurdish world. This is Kapuściński's concern too, but from my perspective it is also a cultural-ownership issue: the Kurdish reader should feel recognized from the first sentence, not from the third paragraph.
