# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Review of "The High Wagadu" Opening

**Chapter:** Soninke / Era 3: The High Wagadu
**Round:** 1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Where Am I?

I am almost there. Almost. The opening puts me at the edge of the desert, at a well, at dawn. But the well has no temperature. The sand has no weight. The virgin's feet touch no ground. I am told she walks north. I want to feel the morning air on my face, the dust under her feet, the silence of the procession, the sound of the desert wind.

The Al-Bakri passage is stronger. The gold-plated harness. The drum hollowed from a single trunk. The guard dogs in their collars. Here I can see the court. I can almost hear the drum. But I want more. What does the harmattan wind feel like at dawn in the Sahel? I have stood in that wind. It is not a breeze. It carries the Sahara on its back. It dries the lips. It coats the nostrils with fine pale dust. It turns the sunrise into something copper-colored and diffuse. The opening mentions the harmattan once, in passing. The harmattan should be a presence.

## What Puts Me Somewhere

"Calabash pans in water that ran the color of rust." Yes. I can see the water.

"The call to prayer drifting south on the harmattan wind toward the royal compound." Yes. Now I am between the two towns, and the sound connects them. This is good spatial construction.

"Soil that baked to clay four months of every year." The ground exists. I feel it.

The drum. "A drum hollowed from a single trunk, whose sound carried across the plain." The drum is the best sensory moment in the opening. It has physical presence: the hollowed trunk (sight, touch), the sound carrying across the plain (sound, distance). The drum should be given more space. In the Sahel, sound travels differently than it does in forest country. The flatness and the dry air carry sound for miles. The drum's reach is not metaphorical. It is acoustic physics shaped by geography.

## What Puts Me Nowhere

The Bida covenant paragraphs (2-3) are abstract. I am told about a contract between the Soninke and a serpent, but I am not at the well. I do not see the serpent's well. I do not feel the desert's edge. I do not hear the procession. I do not know what time of year this happens, what the light is like, what the people wear or say or do. The covenant is explained but not experienced.

"To the north, across the Sahara, lay the salt mines of Taghaza and Idjil." Geography as inventory. I know where things are on a map, but I cannot feel the distance. A caravan from Taghaza to Wagadu took two months. Two months of walking across the most inhospitable terrain on earth. The salt blocks cracked in the heat. The camels died. Men died. The trade route was not a line on a map. It was a feat of endurance. If the opening invokes the salt trade, it should make the reader feel the weight of a salt block, the heat of the crossing, the relief of arriving.

"The Soninke of Wagadu presided over the crossing of the world." A fine sentence, but it is a sentence written in an office. Put me at the crossing. What does a market day at the crossroads look like? The noise. The dust. The languages overlapping. The smell of livestock and leather and spices and the acrid smoke of cooking fires. The merchants unloading bundles wrapped in hide. The weighing of gold dust on small brass scales. The silent calculation behind the eyes of men who have crossed the desert for this moment.

## The Question I'd Push On

The well where the virgin is taken. This is the chapter's first image, and it should be the most physically vivid. A well at the edge of the Sahara is a specific place. The stone lip worn smooth by rope. The darkness below. The sound of water, or the absence of sound if the well is deep. The sand around it marked by the footprints of animals that come to drink at night. The smell of wet stone in dry air. Give me the well. Put me there. Let me feel the edge of the desert in my body, and then tell me that a girl walks to this place every year, and the story will be inescapable.
