# Panel-1 Review — Wade Davis
## Samanids Era 2: The Peak — Opening

**Lens:** Anthropological wonder — hidden condescension, worldview depth
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

What strikes me immediately is how fully the opening inhabits the worldview that made the Samanid project possible: the conviction that a language is not merely a tool for communication but an epistemological framework, a way of organizing the real. The question "What would you write if you were writing for us?" is not a question about words. It is a question about what knowledge system you trust to hold experience. When Rudaki answers with *farr*, he is not translating a political concept from Arabic into Persian. He is proposing that the Iranian world has its own theory of legitimate power — one that precedes and exceeds the Arabic vocabulary the administrative class has been using.

The opening earns full marks from me on the question of hidden condescension: there is none. The Samanids are not presented as "preserving" or "reviving" Persian culture in the sense of rescuing something nearly lost. They are presented as having made an active, sophisticated choice about which knowledge systems to invest in, for reasons that made complete sense in their own terms.

What is missing from the opening, and what the full chapter must supply, is the depth of the worldview difference between the Arabic philosophical tradition and the Persian literary-cosmological one. The Arabic tradition, as the Abbasid court had developed it, was heavily Aristotelian — analytical, categorizing, building knowledge through logic and taxonomy. The Persian tradition that the Samanids were sponsoring was mythological-cosmological: it understood the world through narratives of kingship and *farr*, through the great cycles of the *Shahnameh*, through imagery rather than argument. These are genuinely different ways of knowing. The chapter should show, not just name, what is different about them.

**The question I'd push on:** The paper economy is mentioned but its epistemological significance is not drawn out. Paper did not just make correspondence cheaper. It changed what kinds of knowledge could be preserved, transmitted, and accumulated. The Samarkand paper workshops were part of a knowledge revolution as significant as the printing press would be in Europe. How did the Samanid court think about this? Was there a sense that accumulation of manuscripts was itself an act of political power?
