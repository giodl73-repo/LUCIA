# The Century of Humiliation: 1839–1911

## The Summer of 1839

In the summer of 1839, Commissioner Lin Zexu stood on the shore near Canton and watched the foreign poison burn.

The opium lay in heaps—1,200 tons of it, seized from the warehouses where British merchants had been selling it to Chinese addicts for decades. Lin had ordered it poured into channels that led to the sea, mixed with saltwater and lime, and set alight. The smoke rose in pillars that could be seen from miles away. Scholars who were there wrote that it looked like the earth itself had caught fire.

Lin was sixty-three years old. He had spent a lifetime in the service of the throne, rising through the examinations the way a scholar was meant to—by understanding the Classics, by demonstrating virtue, by showing that he knew how a proper official should conduct himself. He had been a provincial governor, a military strategist, a man trusted to fix what the provinces could not fix on their own. When the Qing court looked at the problem of the foreign merchants dumping opium into China and making Chinese people addicts, they sent Lin to Canton to solve it.

His solution was elegant and complete. He did what any careful administrator would do: he studied the problem. The merchants were organized; he organized against them. They had protocols; he created protocols to match. They brought their goods in junks and brigs; he surveyed the harbors and recorded what came and went. He wrote memoranda. He issued proclamations. He explained the consequences of trading in the poison—death for the smugglers, punishment for those who used it. He even negotiated with the foreign merchants directly, meeting with them in the trading houses, discussing the rules of commerce as they understood it: you may bring tea, silk, porcelain. You may not bring opium. This is the law of the Middle Kingdom.

The British merchants ignored him.

So Lin asked the court for permission to seize and destroy what had been brought into China illegally. The court granted it. And in the summer of 1839, he destroyed it—all of it, systematically, methodically, the way one removes a disease from the body. He did not know he had just started a war.

The British government, when word reached London, did not see an administrator enforcing the law of his own country. They saw an act of piracy against their merchants. They saw merchants denied access to a market. They saw a nation that would not obey the rules that Europe had decided should govern trade in Asia. The response from London was immediate and surgical: send the navy.

The ships that arrived in Chinese waters in 1840 were not trading vessels. They were warships—steam-powered, iron-hulled, armed with cannon that could fire farther and more accurately than anything the Qing navy possessed. The Chinese junks, built for river navigation and coastal trade, caught fire when the cannon rounds struck them. The Chinese soldiers, armed with bows and early muskets, could not reach the foreign warships before their crews were dead.

The war lasted two years. It was never really a war, in the sense that requires two sides to be fighting. It was more like a demonstration of a principle: we have the power to enforce what we have decided, and you do not. The Treaty of Nanjing, signed in 1842, was called a treaty, but from the Chinese perspective it was a wound opening: Hong Kong ceded to Britain forever. Five ports opened to foreign traders—Canton, Amoy, Foochow, Ningpo, Shanghai—where foreigners could live under their own laws, answerable not to Chinese courts but to British judges. Most-favored-nation status granted, meaning any rights given to any power would automatically extend to Britain. Indemnities of 21 million silver dollars paid to the British government for the cost of the war.

Lin was not blamed for the defeat. He had fought as best he could with the tools he had. But he was removed from his position and sent to a minor provincial post, five thousand li away from the coast. There, in a room with a window that looked onto rice paddies and hills, he spent the rest of his life in administrative work that mattered to no one. The court tried to forget the whole episode. The dynasty had not expected to lose. It had not expected that its own laws could be overruled by a foreign power, on its own soil, with enough cannon.

## The Treaties as a System: 1842–1860

But something had changed. The foreign powers had discovered that the Qing, when pressed, would sign agreements that stripped its own authority. More importantly, they had discovered that these agreements could be renegotiated upward—that each war could produce treaties more expansive than the last.

Sixteen years after Nanjing, the British and French decided the treaties were insufficiently generous. British merchants wanted the right to travel inland without restriction. French merchants wanted legal protection for Christian missionaries. Both wanted even greater access to Chinese markets and ports. When the Qing court refused, the British and French simply went to war again.

The Second Opium War (1856–1860), also called the Arrow War, was less a military campaign than a methodical punishment. The British and French moved up the coast, burning fortifications, seizing ports. When the Qing court tried to negotiate from Canton, the foreigners simply bypassed them and marched toward Beijing. The Qing sent generals with armies; the generals retreated. The Qing sent envoys to negotiate; the envoys were ignored.

In October 1860, the foreign forces reached the Summer Palace, the vast gardens and pavilions where the imperial family had retreated outside the capital. The British and French soldiers moved through the grounds. They took whatever they could carry—jade, porcelain, paintings, calligraphy scrolls. And then they burned it. The smoke from the Summer Palace could be seen across Beijing. The wooden buildings, the silk furnishings, the accumulated treasures of generations—all of it went up in flames. The fire lasted for days.

The Xianfeng Emperor, watching from a distance, realized that the capital itself was about to be occupied. He fled. The Empress Dowager Cixi, regent for his young son, made a decision: she would negotiate. She agreed to the Convention of Peking (1860), which was even more expansive than Nanjing had been: additional ports opened, the Yangtze River opened to foreign trade, foreign diplomats given the right to reside in Beijing, more territory ceded to Russia. The foreigners occupied parts of Beijing briefly, then withdrew, satisfied.

The burning of the Summer Palace was a different kind of humiliation than military defeat. It was the destruction of a place that embodied the Qing's understanding of its own civilization—the beauty and order that came from the center of the world. Foreign soldiers had walked through the gardens where the imperial family had walked. They had taken objects that belonged to the Qing and carried them away. They had burned a structure that could never be rebuilt, because it did not belong to any foreign power; it belonged to the dignity of the dynasty itself.

The Qing court, in the aftermath, faced a question it had never faced before: what had changed? Not the world—the world was as the world had always been. What had changed was the court's understanding that the rules of the world were not the rules the Qing had written. And if the court could not enforce its own rules within its own borders, what was the court for?

Some of the court's wisest officials began to argue: we must build military power. If the foreigners understand only the language of cannon, then we must speak that language. The movement that followed—called the Self-Strengthening Movement—was an attempt to do something that had never been attempted before: to keep the Qing political system intact while completely rebuilding the Qing military capacity.

## The Attempt at Militarization: 1860–1894

For thirty years, from the 1860s through the 1890s, the Qing court tried to modernize. It established new ministries dedicated to military technology. It sent officials to Europe and America to study industrial production. It contracted with foreign engineers to build steamships and foundries. It created military academies where officers could study not the Classics but gunnery and steam engines and foreign languages.

Shanghai became a kind of laboratory for this transformation. In a warehouse district near the Huangpu River, foreign engineers and Chinese workers built the first Chinese steamship. The furnaces burned constantly. The sound of metal being worked—hammer on anvil, steam pressure hissing through pipes—became familiar. Young Chinese officers, many of them examination-system scholars who had chosen military service, studied the plans of foreign ships, tried to understand the logic of steam and iron.

The empress dowager Cixi, who had consolidated power after the Xianfeng Emperor's death, understood what was happening. She was not naive about military power. She had seen the Summer Palace burn; she had felt the humiliation of negotiating from a position of weakness. She knew that the Qing would need armies and ships if it was going to survive. But she also understood something else: if you created a military bureaucracy independent of the examination system, independent of the scholar-officials who had always been the spine of the Qing administration, you were creating a potential rival to the throne. A military man with his own army might decide he no longer needed the empress's approval.

So Cixi navigated a middle path. Yes, build modern military capacity. But keep it controlled. Appoint the military officials herself. Rotate them frequently so they could not accumulate power through long tenure in a single post. Trust those who had proven their personal loyalty to her, not those who had built loyalty through the examination system or through military accomplishment alone.

This worked, after a fashion. For a few decades, the Qing had a fleet and modern fortifications. By the 1880s, the Qing navy was one of the largest in Asia. The foreign powers could not simply march on Beijing the way they had in 1860. There were limits now. There was a cost to aggression. The demonstrations had their effect—foreign ships stopped at Chinese ports not just to demand but to negotiate.

But the limits were always limited. The Qing was trying to build a modern military while still keeping the classical examination system as the heart of the state. This produced a fundamental tension that no amount of good intention could resolve. The examination system produced scholar-officials who understood Confucian governance, moral philosophy, and the Classics. These men had spent fifteen or twenty years studying the same texts, thinking about the same problems of virtue and order. The military academies produced officers who understood gunnery and strategy and the properties of iron. They learned different languages—not the classical Chinese but the technical vocabularies of engineering. These were not the same people, and they were not sure how to talk to each other. The scholar-official and the modern officer saw the world through different frameworks.

Moreover, the self-strengthening effort was always underfunded. Money poured into arsenals and shipyards came from the traditional taxation system, which was already strained by indemnity payments to the foreign powers and by the costs of maintaining the vast bureaucratic apparatus. The court could not simply transform its entire financial system without destabilizing itself—and destabilization meant the warlords on the periphery might assert independence, the provincial governments might stop sending taxes to Beijing, the whole administrative hierarchy might unravel. So the modernization happened in fragments. Enough to look modern to the foreigners, not enough to fundamentally change how the system worked. Enough to maintain the fiction that the Qing was adapting, not enough to truly adapt.

During these thirty years, the foreign powers stopped attacking. There were negotiations, treaties, agreements. The foreign presence grew steadily—more treaty ports, more foreign enclaves, more foreign control over Chinese customs revenues and railways. But it grew through treaties and the pressure of commerce, not through wars. The Qing had bought time by trying to match military capacity. The strategy had worked, after a fashion.

And then, in 1894, Japan struck. The Meiji Restoration, which had transformed Japan from a feudal state into a modern nation in just twenty-five years, had produced something the Qing had not: a military machine that had been created as part of a total social transformation, not as an add-on to an existing bureaucratic structure. Japan's military was integrated into Japanese society the way the examination system was integrated into Qing society. When Japan went to war, all of Japanese society went to war. When the Qing went to war, only the military went to war—and the military was not sure what the central authority really wanted.

## The Sino-Japanese War and the Crisis of the System: 1894–1898

The Sino-Japanese War lasted less than a year. By every measure the Qing had, the war should have been impossible. Japan was a small island nation. China was vast. The Qing had more ships and more soldiers than Japan could possibly field. The Qing had built its fleet through the self-strengthening movement; the fleet was supposed to defend against foreign threats.

The Japanese attacked without warning, moving their faster, smaller ships with precision. The Qing fleet, which had been built for defense and show, was unprepared for aggressive maneuver. In a series of naval engagements, the Japanese destroyed or captured most of the Qing's modern ships. The land war followed the same pattern: the Japanese army, smaller and more mobile, outmaneuvered the Qing. The Qing retreated.

In less than a year, it was over. The Treaty of Shimonoseki (1895) was catastrophic: Japan received Taiwan, the Liaodong Peninsula, and indemnities that dwarfed the ones Britain had extracted. China's southern frontier, which had been thought secure, was now under Japanese control.

What was worse than the military loss was what the loss revealed. The self-strengthening movement had failed. Thirty years of effort to build military capacity had not produced a military that could defeat Japan. Why? Because the Qing had tried to build military power without transforming the political system. The fleet had been created by the scholar-officials, but it was not integrated into the way the Qing understood governance. When crisis came, the system could not respond flexibly. Orders from Beijing arrived slowly. Local commanders were not trusted to make decisions. The entire structure was geared toward preserving imperial authority, not toward winning wars.

The court was devastated. Cixi, who had guided the self-strengthening effort, was forced to confront a question: was the system itself broken? Could it be fixed through better administration? Or did it require more radical transformation?

For a moment, in the years after the Japanese War, a new possibility appeared. A group of younger officials—Kang Youwei, Liang Qichao, and others—began to argue that the system itself needed to be reformed. Not just the military, but the government structure. The examination system should be modernized to include science and modern knowledge, not just the Classics. A new bureaucratic structure should be created, one more capable of rapid decision-making. Perhaps even a constitutional government, with the emperor as the center but with institutional checks and balances.

The Guangxu Emperor, who had been placed on the throne by Cixi when he was a boy, was now a young man in his twenties. He had been reading the reformers' writings. In the summer of 1898, with the support of the reformers, he issued a series of edicts that attempted to do exactly what had been proposed: transform the government, reform the examination system, create space for new institutions.

It lasted one hundred days.

## The Hundred Days Reform and the Boxer Rebellion: 1898–1901

Cixi was watching. She saw the reforms and understood immediately what they meant: if the examination system was transformed, if new institutions were created, if the bureaucracy was restructured, then the imperial throne would lose control over the selection of officials. The throne would become less able to direct the state, not more.

The conservatives in the court—those who depended on the old examination system, those whose power derived from the classical bureaucracy—came to Cixi. They told her that the reforms were a threat to the dynasty itself. She believed them. She reversed the reforms, placed the young Guangxu Emperor under house arrest, and reasserted her control.

The reformers had to flee or die. Some escaped to Japan. Some went underground. The hopes for fundamental reform died in the autumn of 1898.

But something else was stirring in the countryside. Across North China, in villages and mountains, a movement was growing among rural people who had been devastated by foreign presence and foreign exploitation. The movement called itself the Boxers—young men, often from poor families, who practiced martial arts and saw themselves as defenders of traditional Chinese culture against foreign invasion.

The Boxers' understanding was different from the court's. The court was trying to figure out how to govern a nation in a world governed by different rules. The Boxers were saying: no, we will not accept foreign presence at all. We will drive them out. We will return China to what it was before the foreigners came.

This was, in its own way, coherent. The foreign presence HAD brought disruption—foreign trade disrupting local markets, foreign missionaries disrupting local religion, foreign authority disrupting local governance. For a farmer or a village, the foreigners were the most visible change in their world. Get rid of the foreigners, and maybe things would go back to normal.

By 1900, the Boxer movement had grown enough that the Qing court, facing the failure of the self-strengthening military and the collapse of the reform effort, made a desperate calculation. If the court could not defeat the foreigners, perhaps the Boxer movement could. Cixi declared support for the Boxers. The court gave them implicit backing. The Boxers, energized by imperial support, marched on the foreign legations in Beijing.

They were slaughtered. The foreign powers, seeing their diplomats under siege, sent an international military force to relieve them. The force defeated the Boxers with ease. They then occupied Beijing, forced the court to flee to Xi'an, and dictated a new treaty: the Boxer Protocol (1901). More indemnities. More territory. More foreign control.

The Boxer Rebellion was the moment when the system's internal contradictions became visible and violent. The court, having lost confidence in its own ability to govern, had reached back toward the traditional forces it had been suppressing. The Boxers had done exactly what rural movements often do: they had tried to restore an older order by resisting a newer one. Both groups were right about what they understood, and both were catastrophically wrong about what the world now was.

## The Long Dissolution: 1901–1911

In the aftermath of the Boxer defeat, with foreign troops still occupying parts of Beijing, Cixi made a final decision. She was sixty-six years old. She would not attempt the kind of radical constitutional reform that had terrified her in 1898. Instead, she would try to preserve the dynasty while accepting that the system needed to change. The changes would come slowly, under her control, modified at each step to protect imperial authority.

The examination system was abolished in 1905. No more would young scholars spend years in isolation studying the Classics to prepare for the exams that would launch their careers. The exams that had been the spine of the Qing for three hundred years would simply stop. A new school system would be created, modeled on European and Japanese education, teaching science and modern knowledge alongside (but not replacing) Chinese culture.

But here was the fatal problem: without the examination system, the Qing had no way to produce the officials it needed. The system had always reproduced itself through the exams. Young men studied the Classics, debated in the examination halls, took the exams three times (provincial, metropolitan, palace), became officials if they passed, and then taught the next generation the same texts. It was a complete circle. Break the examination system, and you did not just change what officials learned; you broke the mechanism by which the center could reproduce itself.

The new schools took years to establish. The first graduates from the new system would not be ready for official positions until after 1910. But the old examination system was already gone. For several years, there was a gap: no officials being trained through the old system, no officials ready from the new system. The court tried to appoint military men to bureaucratic posts. It tried to promote provincial officials who had already proven themselves. But these moves were ad hoc, lacking the systematic quality that had always characterized Qing governance.

A new proto-parliament was created, to look like constitutional government. But it had no real power. Constitutional government was promised for the future, but the terms were vague. The emperor would retain supreme authority; the parliament would advise. New ministries were created for modern governance—science, commerce, education, industry—but they existed alongside the old ministries of the classical bureaucracy, duplicating functions and creating confusion rather than clarity. A young official might find himself answering to two different superiors, each claiming authority, each expecting obedience.

The final decade of the Qing was a slow hollowing-out. Sovereignty was stripped piece by piece through treaties and concessions. In the railway rights concessions, foreign powers were given the right to build and control major rail lines across China. Japanese and British companies owned lines that transported Chinese goods; the profits went to foreign banks. In the mining concessions, foreign companies were given exclusive rights to extract minerals from Chinese territory. The customhouse—the crucial institution that collected tariffs on imports and exports—was staffed half by Chinese and half by foreigners. The Chinese staff could make recommendations, but the foreign staff could not be overruled by the Chinese staff, and in practice, when the two disagreed, the foreign perspective prevailed.

An old scholar stood in an examination hall in 1906, the year after the system was formally abolished. The hall was in a provincial city; it had been used for centuries for the examinations. No students had come to study for the exams because there were no exams anymore. The scrolls of past exam questions hung on the walls—records of three hundred years of selection, three hundred years of identification and training of the men who would govern the empire. The scholarly apparatus remained: the wooden desks where candidates had written their answers, the inkstones and brushes, the record books listing names and ranks. But it was a museum of itself now. No one came. The hall was silent in a way it had never been silent before. Even when it was empty of candidates (during floods or wars), people had come to maintain it, to prepare for the next examination. Now there would be no next examination. The door was locked. The scholar, who had spent fifty years preparing candidates, walked back to his house and did not look behind him.

In Beijing, in the Forbidden City, the Guangxu Emperor, thirty-three years old and still under house arrest, was dying. Cixi, in her seventies, was dying too. They would die on the same day in November 1908—Cixi in the morning, the Guangxu Emperor a few hours later in the afternoon. The official histories would record the deaths as near-simultaneous, but the timing had clearly been engineered: Cixi made sure the emperor died while she still had enough power to control the succession. With her final breath, she was still protecting the dynasty's order.

A child was placed on the throne—the Guangxu Emperor's young nephew, a boy of three. A regent was appointed to rule in his name. But there was no Cixi now. There was no figure with enough authority to command obedience from all the powers at play.

The Qing soldiers were no longer fighting for the dynasty; they were becoming warlords, ruling their own territories and answering to no central authority. General Yuan Shikai, who commanded the largest modern army the Qing had created during the self-strengthening era, was in a position to make decisions about who would rule and how. The provincial governments were slipping away from Beijing's control. Officials no longer took their authority from the examination system; they took it from whoever had the power to enforce orders. In some provinces, that was a general. In others, that was a foreign power that had established a special interest through treaties and concessions.

The examination system, which had been the thread linking the center to every province and every official, was gone. Its replacement, the new school system, did not yet exist in numbers sufficient to matter. The new ministries existed on paper but could not enforce their authority. The proto-parliament had been promised but given no real power.

The system that had ruled for 268 years was not conquered by foreign armies marching on Beijing. It was not destroyed by a revolution that captured the capital and declared a new order. It hollowed itself out in the attempt to preserve itself. It created a modern military without a modern political structure to command it. It abolished the examination system without having a replacement mechanism to produce officials. It promised reform without being willing to distribute power beyond the throne. It tried to modernize without transforming. Each choice had made perfect sense given the constraints; each choice had ensured the next crisis.

## The Last Transformation

In 1911, the dynasty finally broke. A rebellion in Wuhan spread across the provinces. The Qing army could not suppress it because the army was no longer united; the generals were no longer scholar-officials bound by Confucian hierarchy; they were military men with their own interests. The provincial governments declared independence one by one. In a few months, the dynasty that had seemed permanent, that had seemed immortal, simply ceased to exist.

The court could have ordered a crackdown. But the court was no longer a center with the power to order anything. It was an institution that had lost the mechanisms by which it reproduced itself and the authority to command obedience. When the end came, it did not come as a dramatic final battle. It came as an emptying, a quiet dissolution, a fading away.

Lin Zexu, who had died in 1850, never saw what came after. He never saw the self-strengthening movement, never saw the Japanese war, never saw the examination system abolished. He died in exile, in a provincial post, in a room that looked out on rice paddies. He had burned the opium. He had done what the court asked him to do. And the world had told him that the rules had changed, and his rule-making no longer mattered.

What he understood—that the world had decided the Qing was no longer the center—would take another seventy years for the rest of China to process, to argue about, to transform into something new. The May Fourth generation, the young intellectuals who would gather in the 1910s and 1920s, would be trying to answer the question that Lin's failure had posed: what must China become in order to survive?

The century of humiliation was the process by which the Qing discovered, step by step, that it could not enforce its own authority within its own borders. The dynasty's attempt to preserve itself through adaptation had been coherent and had failed for reasons that had nothing to do with the wisdom of the officials who tried. It failed because no amount of self-strengthening could compensate for the asymmetry of power, and because the system itself could not transform without destroying the very things that made it the Qing.
