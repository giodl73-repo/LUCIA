# Figures — The First Empire: Atlantic Commerce and Plantation

The figures of the First Empire are not straightforward protagonists. They are participants in a system that assigned them roles, and the interest of each figure is in the relationship between the role the system assigned and the person who occupied it.

---

## Robert Norris (c. 1726–1791)

Norris ran at least eleven slaving voyages out of Liverpool over a career spanning roughly three decades. He was not a peripheral figure in the trade; he was one of its practitioners who understood the commercial logic well enough to write about it. His *Memoirs of the Reign of Bossa Ahadee, King of Dahomy* (1789) is a description of the Gold Coast and the kingdom of Dahomey that reveals, in its account of the local slave trade and its interactions with European merchants, exactly how much Norris understood about what he was participating in. He did not misunderstand the system. He chose to maintain the first ledger's account of it.

His testimony before the Parliamentary committee in 1788 was detailed and systematic. He described the quarters aboard the slaving vessels as comfortable, the food as adequate, the crossing as no more dangerous than comparable voyages. He described the enslaved people as content. Every specific claim he made was rebutted by the evidence Clarkson and others assembled: the mortality tables, the ship's logs, the testimony of surgeons and sailors. Norris was not lying in the sense of saying things he knew to be factually false. He was maintaining the first ledger's logic with the same care he maintained his accounts. Within the accounting system he operated in, the forty-six dead on the *Dobson* were a loss, and a loss was a business event. He had a framework for understanding that. He did not have a framework for understanding what the crossing was to the people who crossed it.

He died in 1791, before the abolition vote. His testimony is in Hansard. His mortality records, extracted from his own logs, are in Clarkson's *History of the Rise, Progress and Accomplishment of the Abolition of the Slave Trade* (1808). Both documents survive. The first ledger and the second, bound in the same archive.

## Olaudah Equiano (c. 1745–1797)

The debate about Equiano's birthplace — whether he was born in what is now southeastern Nigeria, as his *Narrative* states, or in South Carolina, as some documentary evidence suggests — has been thoroughly examined by Vincent Carretta in the definitive biography. The documentary case is unresolved, and the scholarly consensus is that neither position can be proven definitively. What is not disputed is that Equiano was enslaved, that he experienced the Atlantic crossing under the conditions the *Narrative* describes (whether as a child of eleven or in some other capacity), and that the *Narrative* is one of the most strategically intelligent texts produced by the abolitionist era.

The strategy is worth examining directly: Equiano addressed the British Parliament through the language of Enlightenment natural rights, through Christian ethics (he had converted to Methodism and took his Christianity seriously), through commercial calculation (free labor is more efficient than slave labor; he demonstrates this from his own experience as a trader), and through the narrative of personal testimony (the first-person account of the crossing as evidence). He used every argument the first ledger's system recognized as valid. He also published the subscription list of the *Narrative* in the book itself, demonstrating — in the first ledger's terms — that his work was commercially viable and that he had the support of named persons of standing. He was not petitioning from outside the system. He was arguing from within it, in its own language, using its own instruments.

He traveled extensively in the years after publication: Dublin, Edinburgh, Birmingham, Manchester, Liverpool. He managed his own correspondence and his own finances. He died in 1797, ten years before the Abolition Act, having spent the last decade of his life in systematic public argument. His wife was Susan Cullen, of Cambridgeshire. His daughter Joanna outlived him and received the estate, which was modest but real. He had, in the end, written himself into the ledger on his own terms.

## William Roscoe (1753–1831)

The son of a market gardener in Mount Pleasant, Liverpool, who had no formal university education but taught himself Latin, French, and Italian from the library that the city's trade had assembled. He became a lawyer, then a banker, then a poet, then a historian. His biography of Lorenzo de' Medici (1796) made his reputation in London literary circles; his life of Leo X (1805) extended it. He was elected to Parliament for Liverpool in 1806.

He knew before he ran that his position on the slave trade was commercially heterodox. He was elected anyway, in a single-member constituency where the merchant interest was divided and the liberal reforming interest was organized enough to carry his candidacy. He voted for the Slave Trade Abolition Act in March 1807 and lost his seat at the October election, returned by a constituency that had reconsidered. He said after the defeat that he had done what he understood to be right and was content with the result.

His financial difficulties were real and eventually severe: the banking firm suffered in the post-Napoleonic depression, and by 1816 the library he had assembled over a lifetime — approximately two thousand volumes, some rare, some annotated in his own hand — had to be sold. The city of Liverpool, or rather a subscription organized by the city's gentlemen, bought the library back and deposited it with the newly founded Liverpool Athenaeum. The books were available, after that, to any subscriber. The library that the trade had purchased, augmented by the library of the man who opposed the trade, remained accessible.

Roscoe spent the last fifteen years of his life in Liverpool, writing, corresponding, continuing to argue on questions of political economy and cultural history. He was not a tragic figure in the sense of a man destroyed by principle. He was a man who accepted the cost of his position and continued. The cost was real. The continuation was also real.

## Thomas Clarkson (1760–1846)

Clarkson's method deserves attention because it was the methodological innovation that the abolitionist campaign most required: he used the first ledger against itself.

The ship's logs that Clarkson collected in Bristol and Liverpool between 1787 and 1790 were the trade's own records, kept for the trade's own purposes: crew mortality rates, cargo mortality rates, expenses and revenues, notation of incidents. Clarkson extracted from these logs the mortality data for sailors as well as enslaved people — the sailors died at rates that shocked a British public that had not considered the trade's cost to English maritime labor — and published the figures in forms the Parliamentary audience could assess. He collected and catalogued the instruments used on enslaved people: the thumbscrews, the speculum oris, the restraints, each of which he had purchased from the trade's own suppliers. He presented them in Parliamentary hearings as physical evidence, items that had prices and manufacturers and purchasers. He made the second ledger's contents legible in the first ledger's format.

He was a man of extraordinary physical endurance: he traveled some thirty-five thousand miles on horseback in his investigative work, covering England, Scotland, Wales, France. He continued his advocacy after Abolition, working against the illegal trade and the institution of slavery itself until the Slavery Abolition Act of 1833. He was eighty-five when Parliament finally passed the measure. He published his *History of the Rise, Progress and Accomplishment of the Abolition of the Slave Trade* in 1808, the year after the Act, as a documentary record of how the argument had been made. It remains one of the primary historical documents of the campaign.

## James Somerset (fl. 1760s–1770s)

James Somerset was the occasion for the legal argument, not its author. He was enslaved in Massachusetts, brought to England by his owner Charles Steuart in 1769, escaped in 1771, was recaptured, and was detained aboard a ship in the Thames preparatory to sale in Jamaica. Granville Sharp arranged legal representation. Somerset did not testify at length in the proceedings; the case turned on legal argument, not personal testimony.

What is known of his life after the Mansfield ruling is limited: he was free in England by the ruling, and the documentary record does not follow him further. The ruling that bears his name — Somerset v Stewart — established one thing and left everything else intact. But the one thing it established became the legal argument that Sharp and his successors used for the next three decades, in case after case, to extend the protection of English common law to enslaved people on English soil. Somerset himself disappears from the record. The argument he generated does not.

## Tacky (d. 1760)

The record of Tacky is partial and filtered through the accounts of his opponents. The Jamaican colonial records and the published accounts of the uprising by Bryan Edwards (*The History of the British West Indies*, 1793) are the primary surviving evidence, and Edwards was a planter who wrote from inside the planter interest's framework.

What the records establish: Tacky was a Coromantee man, born on the Gold Coast, who had been a leader — some accounts say a chief — before his transportation. He organized the 1760 Easter uprising in the parish of St. Mary with military precision, beginning with the seizure of the garrison at Fort Haldane. The uprising spread to other parishes. It was suppressed after several months of fighting, at considerable cost to the colonial military apparatus.

What the first ledger recorded: 60 white colonists killed, approximately 400 enslaved people killed or executed, 600 transported off the island. Insurance premium adjustments. Military expenditure. Labor supply reduction.

What the first ledger did not record: Tacky's knowledge of Gold Coast military organization and how it was applied. The communication networks among the enslaved populations of different estates. The decision-making process that selected Easter Sunday as the moment of action. The response to suppression in the quarters of the surrounding plantations over the following decade. These are in the second ledger, which was never written down in a form the first ledger could file.

Tacky was killed by Maroon hunters working under the treaty obligation that guaranteed Maroon freedom in exchange for assistance in suppressing uprisings. The Maroons had their own calculation: their freedom was contractually dependent on the colonial administration's goodwill. The treaty was the first ledger they had been given access to, and they maintained it. The history of that transaction — who gained what and at what cost — belongs to both ledgers simultaneously.
