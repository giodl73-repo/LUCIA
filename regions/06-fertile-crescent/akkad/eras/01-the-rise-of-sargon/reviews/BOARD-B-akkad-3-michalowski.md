## B-akkad-3 — Piotr Michalowski — Board Review of The Rise of Sargon

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Akkadian-period administrative texts, the bilingual literary tradition, the Sumerian scribal system under Akkadian political authority.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The description of the cuneiform script's adaptation for Akkadian (Sumerian sign values, Akkadian sound values, Sumerian logograms) is correct in its essentials. The hybrid character of the Akkadian writing system is accurately represented.
- The edubba description (learning by copying, sign lists, hymns, administrative formulas) is accurate for the general scribal tradition, though the specific character of Sargonic-period scribal education is less well-documented than the Old Babylonian period that provides most of our edubba evidence. The chapter could note that our detailed knowledge of the edubba comes primarily from later periods.
- The Sumerian King List's inclusion of Akkadian dynasties is correctly described. The King List's theological framework (kingship descending from heaven) is accurately represented.
- Enheduanna's composition "The Exaltation of Inanna" (nin-me-shar-ra) is correctly attributed. The theological content (Inanna elevated above other gods) is accurately described.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The Sumerian scribes' experience under Akkadian rule is handled with appropriate complexity. The passage about the scribe who "understood something about power that no royal inscription would ever state" is interpretive but culturally sensitive.
- The bilingual synthesis is not romanticized. The chapter correctly represents it as a product of conquest and necessity, not cultural generosity. The line "being valued for your indispensability is not the same as being free" is the chapter's strongest moment of cultural honesty.
- The statement that "Sumerian, which was an isolate, related to no known language" is the current scholarly consensus.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The physical description of bilingual scribal practice (same reed, same clay, same wedge shapes, different languages) effectively communicates the material reality of the tradition.
- The section avoids the common error of treating the bilingual tradition as a purely intellectual achievement divorced from political power.
- Enheduanna's placement at the junction of the two traditions is well-handled.

**What needs correction:**
- The edubba evidence should be noted as deriving primarily from the Old Babylonian period rather than from the Sargonic period itself. Our knowledge of Sargonic-period education is inferential rather than direct.
- The passage on Sumerian myths "retold with Akkadian names and Akkadian emphases" is broadly correct for the later tradition but may overstate the pace of literary transformation during the Sargonic period itself. The major bilingual literary works (Enuma Elish, Standard Babylonian Gilgamesh) postdate Sargon by centuries or millennia. The process was slow.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (two minor qualifications noted above)
