# L'Empire Français — La Mission Civilisatrice

France did not merely trade or exploit. France, in its own understanding, civilized. La mission civilisatrice — the civilizing mission — was the ideological framework within which the French Empire understood itself from the Enlightenment through decolonization: France possessed a culture so universal in its values, so rational in its organization, so superior in its civilization, that extending it to the world's peoples was not merely self-interest but a moral obligation. The French language was the medium of reason and civilization; French law was the codification of universal rights; the French Republic was the institutional form that liberty had found. The empire that carried these things to Algeria, Senegal, Indochina, and the Caribbean was, in this account, carrying humanity's common heritage to those who had not yet received it.

The civilizing mission is recorded here without endorsement. What it authorized — the systematic suppression of indigenous languages, the imposition of French administrative structures, the assimilation policy that demanded cultural self-erasure as the price of civic membership, the violence that maintained colonial order — was real and consequential. So was the genuine French commitment to certain universalist ideals, which occasionally produced unexpected results: the Haitian revolutionaries used the language of the French Declaration of the Rights of Man against the French slaveholders who had written it. Aimé Césaire, Léopold Sédar Senghor, and Frantz Fanon — the theorists of Négritude and anti-colonialism — all wrote in French, using the conceptual vocabulary the French educational system had given them. The empire gave its subjects the language of liberation. It did not intend this.

Key places:
- **Paris** — the "universal capital"; the city the empire served and that the empire's revenues made possible
- **Algiers (Alger)** — the jewel of the second empire; the settler colony where the mission civilisatrice met its most complex test
- **Saint-Louis (Senegal)** — the oldest French settlement in West Africa; the center of the assimilation policy in the "Four Communes"
- **Hanoi and Saigon** — the twin capitals of Indochine; the French colonial world that Marguerite Duras described and Ho Chi Minh dismantled

## What Defined Them

**Assimilation vs. association — the two colonial theories.** French colonial policy oscillated between two approaches. Assimilation held that colonial subjects could and should become French: learn the language, adopt the culture, accept the law, and eventually receive full citizenship. The Four Communes of Senegal (where residents had full French citizenship since 1848) represented the assimilation ideal. Association, which became dominant after 1900, held that colonial subjects should be governed through their own institutions under French supervision — not made French, but developed within their own cultural framework toward eventual self-governance. Neither policy was consistently applied; both were used to justify French rule. The tension between them is the tension between two versions of the civilizing mission.

**Algeria as the wound.** Algeria was not a colony in the French legal understanding — it was part of France, three departments of metropolitan French territory, with a settler population of one million European Algerians (pieds-noirs) who had been there for 130 years when independence became the question. The Algerian War (1954–1962) was therefore a civil war in the French legal framework and a war of national liberation in the Algerian framework. Its conduct — the systematic use of torture by the French army, the concentration camps, the mass displacement of civilian populations — was documented at the time and denied at the time. The French state did not officially acknowledge the use of torture in Algeria until 2001. The pieds-noirs who were expelled at independence, the Harkis (Algerian soldiers who fought for France) who were abandoned, and the Algerian veterans who received no recognition — all of these groups carry wounds from the same event. The Algerian War is the trauma France has not finished processing.

**Négritude — the empire's gift returned.** The Négritude movement was born in Paris in the 1930s among students from the French colonies: Aimé Césaire from Martinique, Léopold Sédar Senghor from Senegal, Léon-Gontran Damas from French Guiana. They wrote in French, published in Paris, and used the conceptual tools of European modernism — surrealism especially — to articulate the claim that Black African culture was not a deficiency to be overcome but a value to be celebrated. Négritude was simultaneously a product of French colonial education and a repudiation of that education's assimilationist goal. It became the intellectual foundation of African independence movements.

**The DOM-TOM — the empire that remains.** France retains more overseas territories than any other former colonial power: Martinique, Guadeloupe, French Guiana, Réunion, Mayotte (in the Indian Ocean), New Caledonia, French Polynesia, and several others. These are not colonies in the formal sense — they are French departments or collectivities, with representation in the French National Assembly and access to French social services. But their populations, overwhelmingly non-white and descended from enslaved or colonized peoples, live in territories that are, by any measure, economically dependent on metropolitan France. The empire did not end; it was reclassified.

## The Chapters

**1 — Cartier:** July 24, 1534: Jacques Cartier plants a cross on the Gaspé Peninsula and claims the land for France. The Iroquois chief Donnacona watches. His people have been here for thousands of years. What the cross means, and what it will eventually mean, is not fully apparent to anyone present.

**2 — Saint-Domingue:** In the 1780s, Saint-Domingue (western Hispaniola) produces 40% of Europe's sugar. Its 500,000 enslaved workers are the most productive and most brutalized labor force in the Americas. Moreau de Saint-Méry, a colonial administrator, writes a description of the colony's racial classification system: 128 possible racial categories, each with its own legal status and social position. The system is a work of bureaucratic precision applied to a system of terror.

**3 — Abd el-Kader:** The Algerian emir Abd el-Kader leads the resistance to French conquest from 1832 to 1847. He is a religious scholar and a military commander. His resistance is eventually suppressed. He is imprisoned in France, released, and spends his exile in Damascus, where in 1860 he protects the Christian population of Damascus from a massacre. The man France conquered is protecting people from the violence that France's presence in the region helped create.

**4 — Dien Bien Phu:** May 7, 1954: the French garrison at Dien Bien Phu surrenders to the Viet Minh after fifty-seven days of siege. France has lost 3,000 dead, 8,000 wounded, 10,000 captured. The fortress that the French generals believed could not be taken has been taken. The French Empire in Indochina is over. The Geneva Accords divide Vietnam at the 17th parallel. The American era in Vietnam is beginning.

**5 — La Torture:** Jean-Paul Sartre writes in 1958 that France is using torture in Algeria — systematically, as policy. He is correct. The Generals' Putsch of 1961 attempts to prevent Algerian independence by coup. De Gaulle faces down the generals and negotiates Algerian independence in the Évian Accords (1962). One million pieds-noirs leave Algeria in a matter of months. Most of them go to France. The country they arrive in has been shaped by the empire they built and lost.

**6 — La Françafrique:** In 1960, France grants independence to its African colonies. It simultaneously establishes a network of bilateral agreements, military bases, currency arrangements, and personal relationships between French officials and African leaders that maintains French influence over its former colonies' politics and economics. This system — called Françafrique by its critics — has been an object of criticism, reform promises, and continued practice for sixty years. In 2023, military coups in Niger, Mali, and Burkina Faso explicitly direct themselves against French influence. The African chapters of French imperial history are not yet finished.
