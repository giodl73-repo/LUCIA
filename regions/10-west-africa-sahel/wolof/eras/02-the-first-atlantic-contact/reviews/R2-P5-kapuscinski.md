# R2-P5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Targeted Review of Notes

**Chapter:** Wolof / Era 2: The First Atlantic Contact
**Round:** 2 (Notes review, focused on sensory grounding)

## What the Notes Get Right

The sensory palette in Section 6 is thorough. The notes identify specific sensory details for each section and each location. The distinction between coastal heat (humid, salt-laden) and interior heat (dry, heavy) is correct and important. The plan to ground Lisbon through Wolof senses (cold stone, church bells, strange food) is the right approach.

## What Needs Strengthening

**Section 2 (horse economy) is the highest sensory risk.** The section is about an ecological and economic argument (tsetse fly, horse mortality, trade dependency). The temptation will be to explain this argument. Instead: put me in the stable. Let me hear the flies. Let me see the horse's coat dulling as the disease takes hold. Let me watch the ceddo warrior who has ridden this horse for two years and knows it will not live to see a third. The argument must live in the body of the horse and the body of the rider, not in a paragraph about trypanosomiasis.

**Section 3 (Lisbon) must commit to the sensory inversion.** The notes plan to show Lisbon through Wolof senses. This must be absolute. No description of Lisbon as a European would experience it. Everything through the body of a Wolof nobleman who has never been cold in his life, who has never seen a building made entirely of stone, who has never heard a church bell. The disorientation is the sensory detail. But also: what would be familiar? The river (the Tagus). The fish market. The sound of traders haggling. The smell of the sea. Let Bemoim find what he recognizes before he is overwhelmed by what he does not.

**The Atlantic surf must be a recurring sensory motif.** The chapter tracks the shift from an inland-facing to an ocean-facing polity. The sound of the Atlantic should grow louder as the chapter progresses. In Section 1, the surf is background. In Section 4, the court of Cayor is close enough to hear it. In Section 5, it is the dominant sound. This is not a symbol. It is a geographic fact: the center of Wolof power moved toward the coast. Let the reader hear the movement.

**Do not forget the night.** The Senegambian coast at night: the sound of the surf in darkness, the stars over the Atlantic, the fires in the compound. The Sahel interior at night: the silence, the cooling air, the sound of livestock settling. At least one section should include a night scene, even briefly. Night changes the sensory landscape and gives the reader a different kind of presence.

## The Question I'd Push On in the Full Draft

Where is the physical act of trade? Horses are unloaded. Captives are... what? Led to the ship? Bound? The physical mechanics of the exchange are the chapter's most important sensory moment, and they must be rendered with specificity and without flinching. Not as spectacle. Not as judgment. As the physical reality of what the calculation looks like when it is carried out by human bodies. The horse walks one direction. The person walks the other. That image is the chapter's center of gravity.
