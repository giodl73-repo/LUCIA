# Majapahit — Nusantara

We are Majapahit, the Javanese empire that proclaimed the entire Indonesian archipelago as a single domain under a single sovereignty — Nusantara, the "island realm," a vision so grand and a claim so comprehensive that modern Indonesia invoked it when naming its new capital. Our court poet Prapañca wrote in his Nagarakertagama (1365 CE) of King Hayam Wuruk's realm stretching from Sumatra to the Spice Islands to New Guinea to the Philippines, a territorial sovereignty claim that was more vision than administrative reality but that expressed a genuinely original political idea: the archipelago as a coherent world, not a collection of separate islands.

We are not India; we are Java and the islands. Yes, we used Sanskrit vocabulary for our royal titles; yes, we built Shaiva and Buddhist temples; yes, our court culture drew on Indian literary models. But the Nagarakertagama is an Indonesian poem before it is anything else — it celebrates the specific geography of Java, the particular beauty of the rice paddies and the volcanoes, the processions through Javanese towns, the reality of this landscape and these people. When Majapahit said Nusantara, it was not translating a foreign concept; it was naming its own world.

## What Defined Them

**Nusantara — the archipelago as idea.** Gajah Mada's Palapa Oath (c. 1336 CE) — that he would not eat spiced food (palapa) until he had unified the archipelago under Majapahit — expressed the political vision that defines the empire: the island world is one world, and it should be governed as such. Whether Gajah Mada ever fulfilled the oath is historically debated; the vision he articulated was genuinely original. No previous Southeast Asian polity had conceptualized the entire Malay-Indonesian archipelago as a single political unit.

**The Nagarakertagama as civilization's self-portrait.** Prapañca's court poem (1365 CE), written in Old Javanese (kawi) verse, is both a royal panegyric and a detailed description of the Majapahit realm: the king's procession through the towns of Java, the territories that acknowledged sovereignty, the court ceremonies, the religious institutions, the geography of East Java. It is the most detailed account of any pre-modern Southeast Asian kingdom from inside and is the primary source for understanding what Majapahit actually was — which was somewhat different from what it claimed to be.

**Hindu-Buddhism as Javanese tradition.** Majapahit's religious culture was a synthesis of Shaivism and Buddhism that had evolved over centuries into a distinctly Javanese form: the Shiva-Buddha concept, in which the divine manifested in both forms simultaneously, and the royal ancestor cult that made deceased kings divine through their union with Shiva or the Buddha. This was not a confusion of traditions but a sophisticated theological position: the divine is one, and its manifestations are multiple. This tradition survived Majapahit's decline in Bali, where it continues today.

**The Gajah Mada administration.** The great prime minister Gajah Mada reorganized the Majapahit state with an administrative precision that went beyond previous Javanese courts. The Kutaramanawa law code (attributed to Gajah Mada's era) systematized legal procedures. The military was professionalized. Revenue collection was systematized. The network of vassal states was managed through a diplomatic protocol that distinguished between outer territories (under direct Majapahit claim) and allied states (acknowledging Majapahit supremacy without being directly governed). This was territorial governance as well as the prestige-based mandala model.

**The Islamic transition.** Majapahit's decline in the fifteenth century was closely linked to the rise of Islamic port-states on the Javanese and Sumatran coasts — states that offered merchants a framework of Islamic commercial law and the commercial networks of the Muslim world. The Demak Sultanate, the first Islamic state in Java (founded c. 1475), was the proximate cause of Majapahit's end; the Demak forces sacked the capital around 1527. But the transition was gradual, and the Majapahit cultural tradition did not simply end; it transformed into the syncretic Java that persists today, and it survived intact in Bali.

## The Chapters

**1 — Raden Wijaya:** The Mongol fleet has come to punish Kertanagara, the previous king of Singosari. But Kertanagara is already dead, killed by a rival. Raden Wijaya, his son-in-law, allies with the Mongols, defeats his enemies, then turns on the Mongols and expels them. He founds Majapahit from this double reversal. This is how the empire begins: with a trick.

**2 — Gajah Mada's Oath:** He stands before the court. He says: I will not eat spiced food until the archipelago is unified under Majapahit. The courtiers are skeptical. Gajah Mada does not care. He spends the next thirty years making it as true as any one man can make it.

**3 — Prapañca's Poem:** The court poet is traveling with the king's procession through East Java. He is writing down everything: the towns they pass through, the ceremonies, the rice paddies, the mountains, the temples. He is making a record of a civilization at its highest point. The poem will survive when the civilization does not.

**4 — The Islamic Port:** A merchant in a coastal Javanese town has converted to Islam. The Muslim trading network gives him commercial law, credit networks, and connections from Arabia to Java. The Majapahit agricultural empire and the Islamic maritime commerce are two different systems. The maritime system is growing.

**5 — Demak:** The Islamic Sultanate of Demak sacks Trowulan. The Majapahit empire ends. The Balinese priests have already left, carrying the texts and the sacred traditions to the island that will preserve them. Bali will be Majapahit's cultural heir for five more centuries.
