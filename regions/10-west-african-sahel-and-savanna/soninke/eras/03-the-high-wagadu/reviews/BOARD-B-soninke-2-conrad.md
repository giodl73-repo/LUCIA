# B-soninke-2 — David Conrad — Board Review of "The High Wagadu"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Mande oral traditions, the Soninke Dausi, and the historiography of the Almoravid-Ghana relationship. Co-author of "The Conquest That Never Was" (1983).

**Historical accuracy:**
- The four-Wagadus cycle (four foundings, four destructions, each for a form of vanity) is accurately drawn from the Dausi tradition as collected by Frobenius and Monteil. The specific vanities listed (vanity, falsehood, greed, dissension) are consistent with the standard versions. Correct.
- The Siya Yatabere story (suitor kills Bida, three heads fly to different directions, gold and rain cease) is accurately rendered from the Dausi. The name varies across versions (Siya Yatabere, Mamadu the Silent, and others), and the chapter appropriately notes this variation in figures.md. Correct.
- The Almoravid question is handled well. The chapter presents the 1076 disruption with appropriate ambiguity, cites my work with Fisher, and does not assert a single decisive conquest. The phrasing "Almoravid forces disrupted the Soninke capital" with the qualifier "whether this was a full military conquest... or a more gradual pressure... is debated" is fair and accurate.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's central move, treating the Bida covenant as the Soninke's own theory of political economy rather than as decorative mythology, is exactly right. The Dausi is not a collection of stories told for entertainment. It is a historiographic system that encodes the Soninke understanding of causation, obligation, and consequence.
- The cyclical historiography (founding-destruction-return) is correctly identified as the Soninke's own model, distinct from the linear narrative the Arabic sources impose.
- The tradition's claim that Wagadu will return a fifth time is accurately reported and appropriately used as the chapter's closing mechanism.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The relationship between the Dausi and the Arabic sources is handled as productive tension, not hierarchy. Neither source is subordinated to the other.
- The Bida covenant is treated as evidence, not as folklore.
- The Siya Yatabere story is told without rationalizing it (no "this legend may reflect a climate shift that...").

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter says the Dausi "holds the memory of the Soninke past." This is slightly imprecise. The Dausi is specifically an epic cycle associated with the Soninke of Wagadu, not a comprehensive oral history of all Soninke-speaking peoples. Other Soninke oral traditions (clan histories, migration narratives) exist outside the Dausi. A minor distinction but relevant to scholarly precision.
- The chapter presents the annual sacrifice as a consistent feature of the Bida covenant. Some versions of the Dausi emphasize the annual sacrifice; others present it differently (a single foundational sacrifice, or a periodic rather than annual offering). The chapter should acknowledge this variation rather than settling on "every year" as definitive.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS. The oral tradition is handled with genuine respect and analytical rigor. The two corrections are matters of scholarly precision, not fundamental errors.
