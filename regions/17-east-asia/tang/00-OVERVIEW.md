# Tang — Pax Tang — The Cosmopolitan Empire

The Tang dynasty made Chang'an the most cosmopolitan city in the world: a capital where Sogdian merchants, Nestorian Christian priests, Zoroastrian fire-worshippers, Buddhist monks from India and Korea, Arab diplomats, and Tibetan ambassadors moved through the same markets, ate in the same restaurants (the Hu — foreign — restaurants that specialized in Central Asian food were fashionable among the Tang elite), and competed for the same imperial patronage. The Tang were the empire that was open to the world because openness was profitable, intellectually stimulating, and the natural expression of a civilization sure of itself. Tang poetry — Du Fu, Li Bai, Wang Wei, Bai Juyi — is the summit of classical Chinese literary achievement. Tang Buddhism produced the philosophical schools that define East Asian Buddhist thought. Tang governance produced the institutions that Korea, Japan, and Vietnam would model.

The Tang called their capital Chang'an — "Enduring Peace" — and it held perhaps one million people: the largest city in the world in the seventh and eighth centuries. The grid of its streets, the double ward system, the markets divided by day and night — Chang'an was an argument in urban planning that a great empire expressed itself through the ordering of its capital. The Tang woman who dressed in Central Asian fashion, played polo, competed in the imperial examination's parallel tracks for women (the court ladies), and patronized Buddhist monasteries was not imitating a foreign model. She was being Tang.

## What Defined Them

**Cosmopolitanism as policy.** The Tang court actively recruited talent from outside the empire: Sogdian merchants traded freely; Korean princes studied at the Imperial Academy; Japanese students came to learn. The Tang music at court included Central Asian instruments — the pipa (lute), the konghou (harp) — and Central Asian rhythms. The Tang aristocracy adopted Hu (foreign, specifically Central Asian) fashions. This was not passive tolerance; it was a deliberate openness founded on the confidence that Tang culture could absorb and enhance everything it encountered.

**Tang poetry.** The shi poetry of the Tang is the standard by which Chinese poetry is measured. Li Bai wrote in the voice of the Daoist wanderer — spontaneous, wine-drunk, unimpressed by official obligation. Du Fu wrote in the voice of the Confucian official — morally serious, pained by the suffering of ordinary people, committed to the ideal of governance even as the empire he loved fell into civil war. Wang Wei brought Buddhist serenity to landscape poetry. These three voices together encompass the range of human experience that the Tang produced, and they have been memorized by Chinese schoolchildren for thirteen centuries.

**Wu Zetian — the woman emperor.** Wu Zetian was the only woman to rule China with the imperial title: first as empress consort, then as empress dowager, then as emperor of her own Zhou dynasty (690–705). She was a ruthless political operator who eliminated rivals and a competent administrator who expanded the examination system and promoted talent over aristocratic birth. She was also a Buddhist patron of the first order — the great sculptural programs at Longmen were partly funded by her. She was seventy-three when she was forced to abdicate. No subsequent Chinese dynasty permitted another woman emperor.

**The An Lushan Rebellion.** The rebellion of the frontier general An Lushan (755–763 CE) nearly destroyed the Tang. An Lushan — a man of Sogdian-Türkic descent who had risen to command three of the Tang's northeastern frontier armies — declared himself emperor and captured both Chang'an and Luoyang. The Tang survived by retreating south and calling in Uyghur military assistance, but the cost was permanent: the frontier military governors (jiedushi) who suppressed the rebellion were never again brought under central control. The Tang that emerged from the rebellion was a different, weaker state than the Tang that preceded it.

**Buddhism in China.** The Tang period was the apex of Chinese Buddhism: the Xuanzang's pilgrimage to India, the philosophical work of Huineng and the Chan school, the scholarly work of Fazang and the Huayan school, the Pure Land devotion that spread to every level of society. The translation bureaus produced definitive Chinese versions of the Sanskrit texts. The great Tang Buddhist temples — the Big Wild Goose Pagoda in Chang'an, the Longmen grottoes' Tang-era sculptures — are among the greatest artistic and intellectual monuments of the civilization.

## The Chapters

**1 — Taizong:** The emperor calls in his minister Wei Zheng, who has written a memorial criticizing the emperor's policy. Taizong is angry — but he reads it. He asks his ministers to keep criticizing him. He says: copper used for a mirror lets you adjust your dress; history used for a mirror lets you know the reasons for prosperity and decline; a person used for a mirror lets you know when you are right or wrong.

**2 — Wu Zetian:** She is seventy years old and has been ruling the empire for decades. Her minister Zhao Yan is reading the memorial requesting her abdication. She does not look up. She knows it is coming. She is calculating whether she has enough time to complete what she is building.

**3 — Li Bai and Du Fu:** They meet in 745 in Luoyang. Li Bai is already famous; Du Fu is young and unknown. They drink wine and write poems. Their meeting is the greatest literary encounter in Chinese history. In ten years, An Lushan's rebellion will separate them and change what Du Fu writes forever.

**4 — The An Lushan Rebellion:** The Tang court flees Chang'an in the middle of the night. The emperor's favorite Yang Guifei is strangled by soldiers who blame her family for the rebellion. The emperor watches. He is the Son of Heaven and he cannot prevent it. The poems Du Fu writes about this month are among the most painful documents in Chinese literature.

**5 — Late Tang:** A poet writes about a crumbling empire. A general in his provincial capital stops sending tax revenue to Chang'an. The exam candidates still come to the capital, still study the classics, still write their examination essays as if the empire they are preparing to serve is the same Tang that Taizong built. It is not.
