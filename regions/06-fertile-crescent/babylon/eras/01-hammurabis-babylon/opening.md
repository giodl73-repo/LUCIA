# The First Dynasty: Hammurabi's Babylon

The stele stood in the Esagila precinct, in the courtyard where the mud brick walls gave way to open sky and the morning sun fell clean across the stone. Black diorite, taller than a man, the only stone in a city built of river mud. At the top, carved in relief, the sun-god Shamash sat on his throne and extended toward the king the rod and the ring, the instruments of just measure. Hammurabi stood before him, one hand raised, receiving. Beyond the courtyard wall, the Etemenanki rose in stepped terraces against the flat sky, and the smoke of the morning offerings drifted across the precinct, and the date palms along the canal threw their shadows on the pavement. Below the image, in columns cut so fine the script could be read by anyone who stood close enough, the law began.

If a man bring an accusation against a man, and charge him with a capital crime, but cannot prove it, the accuser shall be put to death.

If a man steal the property of a temple or of the court, that man shall be put to death, and he who received the stolen thing from his hand shall also be put to death.

Two hundred and eighty-two cases. Carved into the stone in the Akkadian language, in signs any trained scribe could read. Not hidden in the temple archive. Not locked in the king's private chamber. Standing in the open, where the courtyard caught the morning sun and the stone warmed under the hand of anyone who touched it.

This was the claim: that the law was not secret. That a merchant in the karum outside the temple gate could, if he wished, walk into the courtyard and read the case that governed his contract. That a woman whose husband had died could find the clause that protected her bride-price. That a man accused of a crime could point to the stone and say: here is the judgment, and it was set down before this case arose, and neither the judge nor the accuser may change it. The stele did not say: this is my will and I am strong enough to enforce it. The stele said: this is the order of the cosmos, and I am the shepherd appointed by Shamash and Marduk to make it visible in the land.

Hammurabi was the sixth king of a dynasty that had begun as one Amorite family among many, governing one city among many, in a land where Sumer's old cities still held their gods and their canal systems and where the king who could hold the waterways held the plain. His grandfather Sumu-abum had taken Bab-ilim when it was a minor settlement on the Euphrates. His father Sin-muballit had spent his reign fighting Larsa, Eshnunna, and Elam behind them. Hammurabi inherited a kingdom that was viable but not dominant, and a political situation in which five kings of roughly equal strength watched one another and waited.

He waited longest. For twenty-nine years the year-names record canal digging, temple building, wall construction. The work of a king who understood that the canals fed the fields and the fields fed the treasury and the treasury fed the army. In his thirtieth year Elam moved against the plain. Hammurabi defeated them, then took Larsa, then Eshnunna, then Mari. In four years he unified the alluvial plain under one king for the first time since Sargon of Akkad, six centuries earlier.

Then he published the law.

The prologue to the code is not a preamble. It is a theological argument. Anu and Enlil, the highest gods, named Marduk to the headship of the peoples. They made Babylon great among the regions of the world. They established in it an everlasting kingdom whose foundations are as firm as heaven and earth. At that time, Anu and Enlil named Hammurabi, the devout prince who fears the gods, to cause justice to prevail in the land, to destroy the wicked and the evil, to prevent the strong from oppressing the weak.

The law did not begin with Hammurabi. Ur-Namma of Ur had published a code three centuries earlier, with its own theological prologue linking justice to divine mandate. Lipit-Ishtar of Isin had published another. What Hammurabi did was to make the publication itself the defining act of kingship at a scale and prominence none of his predecessors had achieved. The stele was not a reference document for judges. It was a monument: the king's primary duty was the maintenance of cosmic justice in the human world, and Shamash who held the rod and the ring held them for all the people, not for the king alone.

A merchant in the karum, the walled trading district outside the temple gate where the donkey caravans unloaded and the silver was weighed on balances in full view. He has walked from the noise and dust and bitumen-smell of the commercial quarter, through the gate, past the mud brick granaries, into the quieter precinct where the air carries the smoke of burnt offerings and the pavement is swept. He stands before the stele. His name does not survive in the archive; his seal impression does, pressed into the clay of contracts for silver loans and overland partnerships and the shipment of tin from Ashur and textiles from Babylon and copper from the gulf. He reads the case that concerns him. If a trader give an agent grain, wool, oil, or any goods to transport, the agent shall give a receipt for the amount, and compensate the trader therefor; then he shall obtain a receipt for the money that he gives the trader. The language is specific. The obligations are mutual. The receipt is the proof.

He stands in the courtyard, and the stone is warm under his hand, and the sun that Shamash rides is overhead, and the law is readable, and the law is public, and the idea that the law is public is the idea that will outlast the dynasty and the empire and the language itself.
