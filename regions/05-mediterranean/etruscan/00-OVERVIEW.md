# Rasenna — The People Who Read the Sky

We called ourselves Rasenna, and we built our cities on the hilltops of what others would later call Etruria — the land between the Arno and the Tiber, with the Tyrrhenian Sea to the west and the Apennines to the east. We were city-people: Caere, Veii, Tarquinia, Vulci, Populonia — each a sovereign city-state with its own magistrates, its own necropolis laid out on the hillside opposite the living city, its own harbor and territory and craftspeople and gods. The Romans who absorbed us thought they were taking something from us. They were taking almost everything: the arch, the city plan, the toga, the lictors with their fasces, the practice of reading omens, the very alphabet that Rome used to govern its empire. The *disciplina etrusca* — the Etruscan science of divination — organized Roman religious life for centuries after Etruria itself had ceased to exist as a political fact.

What the Greeks called us — Tyrsenoi or Tyrrhenoi, giving their name to the sea off our coast — tells us that they knew us as sailors and traders. What the Romans called us — Tusci or Etrusci — tells us that the land itself carried our name into the future even after our language had gone silent. What we called ourselves was Rasenna. We were a people who understood the world as a text to be read: the lightning's path across the sky, the flight of birds, the liver of the sacrificed animal all carried messages from the gods about what was coming, and the *haruspex* who could read those messages was among the most important people in any Etruscan city. The world was not random. The gods spoke continuously, if you knew how to listen.

Key places (Etruscan names):
- **Velch** (Vulci) — the great metalworking city; bronze and pottery production
- **Cisra** (Caere / Cerveteri) — the wealthy trading city closest to Rome; famous painted tombs
- **Tarchuna** (Tarquinia) — the oldest city; the most magnificent tomb paintings; the Tarquin kings of Rome came from here
- **Veii** (Veii) — the city that Rome fought for a century before destroying it

## What Defined Them

**The disciplina etrusca — reading the world as text.** The Etruscan understanding of divination was systematic and teachable — a discipline, not merely a gift. The *haruspex* read the liver of a sacrificial animal according to a detailed map of the organ's regions, each of which corresponded to a god and a domain of life. The *augur* read the flight patterns of birds, the *fulgurator* read the lightning. These were not superstitions. They were a coherent system for interpreting divine communication, and the Romans adopted it wholesale because it worked within the framework of Roman religious thought. Bronze liver models used for teaching haruspicy have been found in Etruscan contexts — the discipline was genuinely teachable.

**The dead city and the living city.** Every Etruscan city was doubled: the living city on its hilltop, the necropolis on the facing hill. The Etruscan investment in the afterlife was expressed architecturally — the great tumuli of Cerveteri, the painted chambers of Tarquinia — and was not separate from ordinary life but its continuation. The tombs were furnished like houses: pottery, bronze vessels, weapons, jewelry, occasionally the remains of food. The paintings on the tomb walls showed banquets, athletic games, music, and dancing — not the terrors of death but the pleasures of life continuing on the other side. The dead were neighbors, housed in the city-of-the-dead that faced the city-of-the-living across the valley.

**Bronze and iron from the land.** Etruria sat on top of the largest metal deposits in Italy: the copper of the Colline Metallifere, the iron of Elba island and the mainland coast at Populonia. Etruscan metalworkers produced goods — bronze vessels, fibulae, armor, weapons — that reached every part of the Mediterranean. The wealth this generated underwrote the painted tombs, the Greek pottery imports, the amber from the Baltic that has been found in Etruscan graves. The metal trade is the material foundation on which Etruscan culture was built.

**The banquet as the center of social life.** The Etruscan painted tombs are full of banquet scenes: men and women reclining together on the same couch — an arrangement that shocked Greek visitors, who kept their women separate at meals — being served wine, listening to music, watching dancers. The banquet was the site where social bonds were renewed, where alliances were maintained, where the deceased were celebrated. That women participated visibly and equally in the banquet space is one of the distinctive features of Etruscan social organization that the Greeks noticed and commented on. It was not license — it was a different organization of the household and the social space around it.

**The alphabet passed west.** The Etruscans received the Phoenician alphabet via the Greeks — the southern Italian Greek colonies at Pithecousai and Cumae — and adapted it for Etruscan sounds. They then passed a version of this adapted alphabet to the Romans, who adapted it further for Latin. The Etruscan alphabet is the physical ancestor of the Latin alphabet. This transmission, happening in the 8th–7th centuries BCE in the workshops and port towns of Tyrrhenian Italy, is one of the most consequential cultural transfers in Western history, and it happened through Etruscan hands.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Villanovan Foundation:** The iron-workers on the hilltops above what will be Tarquinia are building the first hut structures that will become a city. The ore from Elba comes by boat. Something is being organized that will last a thousand years.

**2 — The Archaic Florescence:** A painter in Tarquinia mixes the blue and red pigments and begins the figures of the banquet scene on the tomb wall. Above in the living city, a Tarquin king is preparing his delegation to the Latin settlements to the south. The alphabet has arrived from the Greek city down the coast, and the merchants are learning to use it.

**3 — The Age of the League:** The twelve cities send their delegates to the sanctuary of Voltumna for the annual meeting. Someone raises the question of the Syracusan threat to the Etruscan trading stations in the western sea. The league has no army it can call. Each city will decide for itself.

**4 — The Encirclement:** The Gauls have sacked Rome — and before sacking Rome they pushed through the Po Valley, past the Etruscan cities of the north. The Romans have recovered and are moving again. The *haruspex* at Veii reads the liver and the signs are not favorable.

**5 — The Roman Absorption:** The magistrate of what was Caere is now a Roman citizen, writing Latin in a script that came from Phoenicia through the Greeks through his grandfather's city. He offers the sacrifice and the *haruspex* reads the liver. The ceremony is Roman now, but the science is still Etruscan.
