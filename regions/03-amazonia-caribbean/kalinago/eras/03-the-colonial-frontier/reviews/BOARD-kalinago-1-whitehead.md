# Board Review — Neil Whitehead
## Kalinago, Era 3: The Colonial Frontier
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Factual Assessment

**The raiding economy as political system:** The chapter correctly describes inter-island raiding not as violence-for-its-own-sake but as a political mechanism — redistribution of people and goods, maintenance of inter-island networks. This is consistent with my analysis in *Lords of the Tiger Spirit* and with the broader ethnohistorical consensus. The phrase "a mode of exchange, a redistribution of people and goods and influence that kept the smaller island-chain in conversation with the great islands to the north" is accurate and appropriately framed.

**The 1493 Guadeloupe encounter:** Columbus's account of the encounter at what he called Santa Maria de Guadalupe (Karukera) is recorded in his second voyage journal (reconstructed through Peter Martyr's *Decades*, the original being lost). The chapter's account — Kalinago warriors capturing European members of an advance shore party — is consistent with the documentary record. The specific number "eleven" appears in some versions of the account; if this is used, it should be noted in the chapter's source documentation that the number comes from secondhand accounts and may not be reliable.

**The "cannibal" construct:** The chapter does not use the word, which is the correct choice. More importantly, it frames the European categorization as a political act rather than an ethnographic description. This is consistent with my argument in *Wolves from the Sea* that the cannibal label functioned primarily as a juridical category enabling enslavement, not as an accurate ethnographic observation.

**The raiding-north practice (Taino targeting):** The chapter describes Kalinago raids reaching to Puerto Rico (Borikén). The archaeological and documentary evidence supports this. The scale of these raids — their frequency, the specific communities targeted — is less certain than the chapter implies. The chapter says the raids ran "with the regularity of tides," which is vivid but may overstate the frequency. Seasonal raids, probably timed with the northeast trade wind season (November–April, when northward sailing is more favorable than usual), are the more accurate picture.

**Factual correction required:** The statement "going south was faster than going north" requires precision. The trade winds blow from the east-northeast, which makes westward movement the easiest and northward movement harder (fighting into the wind). Moving south (downwind or with beam winds) was generally faster, but the directionality is about east-west more than north-south. The chapter's explanation of the trade-wind axis should be checked against the actual wind patterns of the Lesser Antilles.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

**The composite figures:** The decision to use composite figures in a source-poor environment is defensible and consistent with the best practices of ethnohistory in this region. The figures are clearly marked as composites in figures.md. The specific knowledge attributed to the canoe-builder (hull-thinning by touch, grain selection by hand) is consistent with what the material record tells us about pirague construction.

**The war captain and the 1627 French expedition:** A French expedition to Dominica in 1627 is documented. The coordinated Kalinago resistance that prevented French establishment is documented. The specific figure of a war captain who organized that resistance is my addition — not documented — but the composite is well-constructed from what we know about Kalinago tactical organization.

**The Taino collapse timeline:** The chapter is correct that the collapse was rapid and devastating. The population of Hispaniola fell from perhaps 500,000–1,000,000 at contact to fewer than 10,000 by 1520. By 1550, the Taino as a distinct population had effectively ceased to exist in the Greater Antilles. The chapter's framing of this as the "loss of the northern dimension of the Kalinago political world" is analytically accurate and important.

**Overall: One factual correction required (trade-wind direction). Minor calibrations elsewhere. Chapter meets the historical accuracy standard.**

The chapter's central argument — that Kalinago resistance was strategic and politically sophisticated, not reactive aggression — is entirely consistent with current scholarship. The decision to render the encounter entirely in Kalinago terms, without centering the European perspective, is not only artistically correct but historically correct: the Kalinago were the ones with 1,000 years of experience in this specific political environment. The chapter recognizes this.
