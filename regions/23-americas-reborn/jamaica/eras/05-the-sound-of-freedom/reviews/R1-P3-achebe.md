# Panel-1 Review — P-3 Chinua Achebe
**Era:** Jamaica Era 5 — The Sound of Freedom
**Date:** 2026-04-17

This opening passes my test. The voice is right.

I will say this precisely, because it is not a small thing: this opening does not explain Jamaica to anyone. It does not position the reader as an outsider who must be guided in. It does not treat Rastafari as exotic or the offbeat emphasis as a charming quirk of Caribbean music. It treats the cosmology and the music as a system of thought that is internally coherent and complete, and it renders that system from inside.

The sentence "what Babylon is" is never defined in a way that positions the author as a helpful intermediary. The reader must enter the logic or remain outside it, and the text does not apologize for this. This is exactly correct.

**What I want to note specifically:**

The Haile Selassie passage uses the full honorific title — "Conquering Lion of the Tribe of Judah, King of Kings, Lord of Lords" — without quotation marks around it, without the distancing phrase "as Rastafari called him," without a parenthetical that translates it for skeptical readers. This is the right choice. The title is real. The Emperor bore it. The text treats it as real.

The reasoning session is described with the same matter-of-fact register as the music: "the herb opened the eye and the eye needed opening in Babylon." Not "marijuana, which Rastafari used as a sacrament." Not "what they called the chalice." The name is used and the purpose is stated in the culture's own terms, and the text moves on. This is ownership.

**What I would watch for in the full chapter:**

The temptation, in a chapter about a culture's global reach, is to tell the story through the global reception. "Audiences in London responded..." "Europeans discovered..." This is the chapter's greatest voice risk. If the narrative point of view shifts to the outside the moment the music leaves Kingston — if the chapter starts measuring reggae's significance by how many white people bought the records — the voice will have slipped and the culture will have been handed over to its reception history.

The story must remain: what did the people who made this music understand themselves to be doing? What did the theology mean to them? What did the political violence of the 1970s mean to a Rasta who had preached that Babylon would fall? The reception follows from the making. It does not constitute it.

**The question I'd push on:**

The women. The opening has three young men, which is accurate — the Wailers were three young men. But Rastafari in this era had a complicated relationship with women (Queens of the faith, but frequently subordinate in the reasoning sessions, excluded from certain rituals). Reggae's lyrics about women ranged from tender to misogynist. This is part of the culture's own truth and the chapter should not pretend it away. A full accounting of the era includes the women who carried the faith, who raised the children in the yards, who shaped the culture in ways the music does not always credit.

**Overall:** The voice is right. Protect it through the full chapter.
