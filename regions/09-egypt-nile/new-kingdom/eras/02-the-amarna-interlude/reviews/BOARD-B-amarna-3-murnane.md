## BOARD — William Murnane — Review of The Amarna Interlude

**Overall:** The chapter handles the primary sources with appropriate care. The Great Hymn is quoted directly and rendered from inside. The Restoration Stela is correctly identified as political theology, not neutral history. The succession confusion is acknowledged without being resolved. The chapter stays close to what the texts say.

**Textual accuracy:**

1. **Great Hymn:** The quoted passage is a recognizable paraphrase/translation of the standard text. The attribution to the tomb of Ay is correct. The interpretation (describing the world, not arguing for supremacy) is a fair reading.

2. **Boundary stelae:** Correctly described as Akhenaten's own declarations. The characterization of the rhetoric as "not a reformer seeking consent" but "a king who has received a truth and is implementing it" is supported by the stelae's language.

3. **Restoration Stela:** The quoted passage is a recognizable paraphrase of Tutankhamun's restoration text. The identification as political theology is correct: the stela was a statement of the new government's legitimacy, not a historical account. The parallel with First Intermediate Period language is an astute observation.

4. **Amarna Letters:** Rib-Hadda's correspondence is accurately summarized. The attribution to Abdi-Heba of Jerusalem is correct. The note about the archive's 1887 discovery is accurate.

5. **Succession:** The Smenkhkare/Neferneferuaten confusion is handled with appropriate uncertainty. "May or may not be Nefertiti under a throne name" is exactly the right formulation. The chapter does not resolve what the evidence leaves open.

6. **Nefertiti's disappearance:** Correctly placed around regnal year 12. The association with Meketaten's death is noted without asserting causation. "The record does not say" is the correct statement.

**Concerns:**

1. The name translations are handled well. "Amun-is-Satisfied" for Amenhotep and "Effective-for-the-Aten" for Akhenaten are standard renderings. "Living Image of the Aten" for Tutankhaten and "Living Image of Amun" for Tutankhamun are correct.

2. The chapter correctly avoids any comparison between the Great Hymn and Psalm 104. This is the right decision for a project that presents each culture from inside its own worldview.

3. One minor note: the chapter says Akhenaten's art showed "an elongated face, a narrow chest, wide hips, a long jaw." The scholarly debate about whether this represents reality, pathology, or theology is correctly noted ("This was not portraiture in any conventional sense. It was a new visual theology"). The chapter avoids taking a position, which is appropriate given the state of the debate.

**Verdict:** The textual foundation is solid. The primary sources are accurately rendered, the secondary interpretations are fair, and the uncertainties are honestly acknowledged. No corrections required.
