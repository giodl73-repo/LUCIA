## B-3 — Jan Jansen — Board Review of "The Great Hajj"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Mande political culture, the Kurukan Fuga as social charter, social categories in Mande society (horon, nyamakalaw, jon), fieldwork with Mande communities and jeli families in the Mande heartland. Specialist in how Mande political philosophy operates as a living system, not merely a historical artifact.

**Historical accuracy:**

- The chapter names the three social categories of the Kurukan Fuga: "the horon and the nyamakalaw and the jon." These are the three foundational categories of Mande social organization. The chapter correctly presents the Kurukan Fuga as defining "what each owed and what each was owed" — a framework of mutual obligation rather than simple hierarchy. This is accurate.
- The chapter states: "The Kurukan Fuga demanded generosity. A king who does not give is not a king." This is a correct rendering of the political philosophy. In the Mande understanding, authority (mansaya) is inseparable from redistribution. The mansa's legitimacy depends on the visible, public performance of generosity. The chapter grasps this.
- The description of the jon carrying gold staffs: "Five hundred jon carried gold." The chapter uses "jon" without extensive explanation, which is appropriate — the Overview has established the categories. However, the chapter should be aware that the status of jon in the Mande system is more complex than simple "slavery" in the Atlantic or Roman sense. Jon were a social category with defined rights and obligations within the covenant framework. They could hold property, marry, and their children's status was subject to specific rules. The chapter does not call them "slaves," which is good. But the absence of any comment on the complexity of the category risks allowing readers to import their own assumptions. A brief acknowledgment that the jon's role in the caravan was a function of the covenant's social structure — not of chattel servitude as understood elsewhere — would strengthen the passage.
- The Kurukan Fuga is correctly described as a covenant, not a constitution or a royal decree. The chapter's consistent use of "covenant" rather than "law" or "charter" reflects a genuine understanding of the Mande concept. The Kurukan Fuga was a mutual agreement between parties who recognized each other's legitimacy, not an imposition from above. The chapter gets this right throughout.

**Cultural authenticity:**

- The central argument of the chapter — that the gold distribution was the covenant in operation, not personal extravagance — is the most important claim in the text, and it is correct. This is how a Mande scholar would understand Musa's hajj. The mansa's authority is constituted by redistribution; the hajj was the largest stage on which that redistribution was ever performed. The chapter's repeated insistence on this point ("The gold was not extravagance. The gold was the point.") reflects a genuine understanding of Mande political philosophy.
- The observation that "the system that made him mansa, that required him to give, was the same system that undercut the material base of his empire's wealth" is analytically strong and culturally accurate. The Kurukan Fuga does not contain an economic theory of scarcity. It operates on a logic of social obligation that is indifferent to market consequences. The chapter correctly identifies this as a structural tension rather than an individual failure.
- The treatment of Inari Kunate is handled well. The chapter presents her as commanding a section of the caravan, which is consistent with what we know about the roles of senior wives in Mande political structure. The mansa's senior wife (muso ba) held genuine authority over her own household and retinue. The chapter's acknowledgment that "the record does not preserve" her inner experience, while insisting on her presence and agency, is the right approach — honest about the limits of the sources without erasing her from the narrative.
- The interaction with the Mamluk court is rendered as a negotiation between two systems of political authority, which is how it should be understood. Musa did not enter Cairo as a supplicant. He entered as a sovereign operating under a different political logic. The chapter correctly frames the prostration compromise as diplomacy, not submission.
- The return and the building section correctly presents the encounter between Sahelian and Andalusian building traditions as a conversation, not a correction. This is important. The common error in popular accounts is to treat al-Sahili as bringing "real" architecture to a place that had none. The chapter avoids this by emphasizing that "the builders were local men who knew mud-brick as their fathers had known it" and that "the new techniques merged with the old because the old were sound." A Mande scholar would recognize and approve of this framing.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The Kurukan Fuga is understood from inside — as a covenant that constitutes authority through obligation, not as an exotic custom described from outside. This is the single most important thing the chapter could get right, and it does.
- The social categories (horon, nyamakalaw, jon) are named correctly and are presented as a system of mutual obligation rather than a simple hierarchy. The chapter does not rank them by "superiority" or "inferiority" but by function and obligation. This reflects the Mande understanding.
- The logic of redistribution is correctly identified as the engine of political legitimacy, not as a personal trait of Musa's. The chapter is explicit: "Musa was not performing wealth for Cairo. He was performing the obligations of kingship for himself." This is exactly right.
- The treatment of Timbuktu's intellectual tradition as indigenous — existing before Musa, expanded by his patronage but not created by it — is culturally and historically accurate.
- The closing section's treatment of the oral and written traditions as complementary and co-equal is consistent with how Mande scholars and communities understand their own knowledge systems.
- The manuscript rescue of 2012 is correctly framed as continuity of custodianship, not as a crisis that appeared from nowhere.

**What needs correction:**

- **The jon category needs more context.** The chapter uses "jon" without comment on the complexity of the category. In the Mande system, jon status was defined by the Kurukan Fuga and carried specific rights and obligations. The five hundred jon carrying gold were performing a role defined by the social charter, within a system that (whatever its injustices) was structurally different from chattel slavery. The chapter's silence on this point is not wrong, but it leaves a gap that readers will fill with their own frameworks, which are likely to be inaccurate. Even a single sentence acknowledging that the Mande concept of jon does not map neatly onto other systems of servitude would help.
- **The Kurukan Fuga as "demanding" generosity.** The chapter repeatedly uses the formula "the Kurukan Fuga demanded" or "the covenant demanded." This is acceptable as a narrative device, but it slightly misrepresents the mechanism. The Kurukan Fuga does not "demand" in the sense of a law with enforcement. It constitutes. The mansa who does not redistribute is not punished by the covenant — he ceases to be mansa, because mansaya (kingship) is defined by redistribution. The distinction is between a rule you can break and face punishment, and a constitutive condition you cannot break without ceasing to hold the status. Recommend adjusting at least one key passage to make this constitutive logic explicit: not "the covenant demanded" but "the covenant defined" or "the covenant constituted."
- **The jatigui (host) category is absent.** The Overview mentions four categories: horon, nyamakalaw, jon, and jatigui. The chapter mentions only three. The jatigui relationship — the host-guest bond that structures much of Mande social interaction — is relevant to Musa's behavior in Cairo and at oasis stops, where he was functioning within a logic of hosting and being hosted. Its absence is a missed opportunity rather than an error, but including it would strengthen the chapter's argument about the covenant's logic governing the hajj.
- **"The Kurukan Fuga named the categories of the Manden."** This phrasing in Section I slightly overstates the Kurukan Fuga's role. The social categories predate the Kurukan Fuga — they are features of Mande social organization that the assembly at Kurukan Fuga formalized and codified. The assembly did not invent the categories; it defined their relationships within the new polity. A small adjustment — "the Kurukan Fuga defined the relationships between the categories of the Manden" — would be more precise.
- **The chapter does not mention sanankuya (joking kinship).** The cross-clan joking relationships that the Kurukan Fuga established are one of its most distinctive features and are directly relevant to how the caravan would have functioned internally — sanankuya relationships between clan members in the caravan would have structured daily interactions, resolved tensions, and maintained social cohesion across the months of travel. This is not a required correction but a significant missed opportunity.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

The chapter demonstrates a genuine understanding of Mande political philosophy. The Kurukan Fuga is understood as a covenant, not a curiosity. The logic of redistribution is correctly identified as constitutive of authority. The social categories are handled with respect and accuracy. The corrections I recommend are refinements — the jon category needs context, the constitutive logic of the covenant should be made more precise, the jatigui category deserves mention. But the foundation is sound. A Mande scholar would recognize this as a text written by someone who has done the reading and understood what they read. The chapter treats the Mande world as a world with its own logic, not as an example of something else. That is the essential test, and it passes.
