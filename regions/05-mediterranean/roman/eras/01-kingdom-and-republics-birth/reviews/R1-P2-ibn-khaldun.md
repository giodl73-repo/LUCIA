## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of Roman Era 1 Opening

**Overall:** The opening identifies the structural mechanism correctly: the oath as prohibition, the machinery as implementation, the mos maiorum as the legitimating fiction that allows change within apparent continuity. This is a political system whose logic I recognize. But I want to see the mechanism tested, not just described.

**What works:**
- The identification of the mos maiorum as Roma's "deepest law." This is correct. The ancestral precedent functioned exactly as this opening describes it: as a circular argument that was also a stabilizing mechanism.
- "Every revolution was a restoration." This captures the Republican political logic precisely. I would have phrased it as a general law: that political communities which derive legitimacy from ancestors can only innovate by claiming continuity.
- The salt road / river crossing analysis. Geography is always the first cause.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening does not identify the structural tension that will drive the chapter. The oath and the machinery are described, but where is the friction? The Republic's real engine was the tension between libertas (no man above the law) and imperium (the state's need for concentrated command). This tension should be planted in the opening.
- The kings are listed but their structural contribution is not analyzed. Servius Tullius's census reform is the most important institutional act in the opening, and it gets one sentence. The census bound property to military service to political voice. That is the Republic's structural foundation.

**The question I'd push on:**
What is the structural engine of this chapter? The oath and the machinery are the frame. But what drives the arc? I would argue it is the tension between the patrician monopoly on religious and political authority and the plebeian demand for formal, written, public law. The Twelve Tables are the resolution of that tension's first phase. Is that the engine? If so, plant it here.
