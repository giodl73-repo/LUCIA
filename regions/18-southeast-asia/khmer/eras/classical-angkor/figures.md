# Figures — Classical Angkor (~802–1220 CE)

## Jayavarman II (reigned c. 802–c. 835 CE)

Founder of the Angkor dynasty. Performed the devaraja ceremony on Phnom Kulen in 802 CE, establishing the ritual technology of the god-king that would underpin Khmer sovereignty for four centuries. Moved his capital multiple times before settling in the Angkor region.

**What we know:** Jayavarman II is attested primarily through the Sdok Kak Thom inscription (1052 CE), which describes the devaraja ceremony and the lineage of the Brahmin priests who conducted it. The inscription was carved two centuries after Jayavarman II's reign, based on the priestly family's own records and memory. He is also referenced in other inscriptions that establish him as the founder of the unified Angkor state. The Sdok Kak Thom states that he came from "Java" (whether this means the island of Java or a Javanese-influenced polity on the mainland is debated) and declared the Khmer independent of Javanese suzerainty.

**What we cannot know:** His personality, his appearance, his private motivations. The inscriptions describe his ritual acts and his political achievements but not his character. The chapter renders him through his founding acts and the institutional machinery he established, not through invented interiority.

**Sources:** Sdok Kak Thom inscription (K.235, translated by Coedes and Dupont); Chandler, *A History of Cambodia* (2008); Coe, *Angkor and the Khmer Civilization* (2003).

## Suryavarman II (reigned c. 1113–c. 1150 CE)

Builder of Angkor Wat. Took the throne through military force, defeating a rival claimant. Dedicated Angkor Wat to Vishnu rather than the Shiva tradition that had dominated previous reigns. Conducted military campaigns against the Dai Viet (Vietnam) and the Cham. Died during a campaign against the Dai Viet.

**What we know:** Suryavarman II is attested through multiple inscriptions and through the bas-reliefs of Angkor Wat itself, which depict him seated on his war elephant, shaded by parasols, leading his army. The scene of his coronation (or his victory over a rival) is described in inscriptions that use the conventions of Sanskrit court poetry. His dedication of Angkor Wat to Vishnu is architecturally and epigraphically confirmed. His military campaigns against Champa and the Dai Viet are recorded in both Khmer and Vietnamese sources.

**What we cannot know:** His inner life. The inscriptions praise him in the formulaic language of Khmer royal panegyric, which tells us what the court wanted him to be, not necessarily what he was. The chapter presents him through the building of Angkor Wat and through his representation on its walls, which is the evidence the Khmer chose to leave.

**Sources:** Angkor Wat inscriptions; Briggs, *The Ancient Khmer Empire* (1951); Jacques and Freeman, *Angkor: Cities and Temples* (1997); Chandler (2008).

## Jayavarman VII (reigned c. 1181–c. 1218 CE)

The Buddhist king. Took the throne after the Cham sack of Angkor in 1177. Built Angkor Thom, the Bayon, Ta Prohm, Preah Khan, and an extensive network of hospitals and rest houses across the empire. Converted the state's ritual orientation from Shaivism to Mahayana Buddhism.

**What we know:** Jayavarman VII is the most extensively documented Angkor king. His inscriptions survive in unusual quantity and detail, including the Ta Prohm inscription (listing the endowment of the temple dedicated to his mother), the Preah Khan inscription (temple dedicated to his father), and the hospital inscriptions (listing 102 hospitals with their locations and endowments). Portrait statues survive that show him in meditation posture with a serene, inward expression. The Bayon's 216 faces bear a resemblance to these portraits. The inscription that describes his compassion for his subjects' suffering is from the Ta Prohm stele.

**What we cannot know:** Whether his Buddhism was sincere devotion, political strategy, or both. The inscriptions are royal documents designed to legitimize his rule. The chapter presents both possibilities without choosing between them, because the evidence does not allow a choice. The reaction against Buddhism after his death (the defacing of Buddhist images, the restoration of lingas) suggests his Buddhist turn was contested, but the inscriptions from his own reign do not record opposition.

**Honest limit:** The chapter's statement "this may have been propaganda; it may have been sincere; it may have been both at once" is a deliberate acknowledgment that the available evidence cannot resolve the question of Jayavarman VII's personal motivation.

**Sources:** Ta Prohm inscription (K.273); Preah Khan inscription (K.908); Hospital inscriptions (K.395); Stern, *Les monuments du style du Bayon et Jayavarman VII* (1965); Coe (2003); Chandler (2008).

## Hiranyadama

Brahmin priest who performed the devaraja ceremony on Phnom Kulen in 802 CE, consecrating Jayavarman II as the god-king. The Sdok Kak Thom inscription describes him as the ritual specialist who brought the liturgical technology from a text called the Vinashikha, connecting the Khmer royal cult to a specific Shaiva textual tradition.

**What we know:** Hiranyadama is named in the Sdok Kak Thom inscription as the officiating Brahmin. The inscription was composed by descendants of his priestly lineage, which gives it a genealogical character: the priestly family is establishing its own authority by recounting its foundational role in the Angkor state.

**What we cannot know:** Anything about Hiranyadama beyond his ritual role. He exists in the record as a function, not a person.

**Sources:** Sdok Kak Thom inscription (K.235); Coedes, *The Indianized States of Southeast Asia* (1968).

## Zhou Daguan (visited Angkor 1296 CE)

Chinese diplomat from the Yuan court who visited Angkor for approximately a year and wrote *A Record of Cambodia* (Zhenla Fengtu Ji), the only surviving eyewitness prose account of the city in something close to its functioning state.

**What we know:** Zhou Daguan's account is dated and his identity as a member of a Yuan diplomatic mission is established. His descriptions of the palace, the markets, the bathing practices, the monks, the festivals, and the social organization of the city are detailed and internally consistent. His account is widely used by scholars as a primary source for daily life in late Angkor.

**What we cannot know:** How accurately Zhou understood what he saw. He was a Chinese diplomat observing a Khmer city through Chinese cultural categories. His surprise at mixed bathing, his assessment of the markets, his descriptions of the religious practices all carry the bias of an outsider. The chapter uses Zhou's account as evidence while acknowledging that the evidence tells us about Zhou's perspective as much as about Angkor's reality.

**Honest limit:** Zhou Daguan is used in the chapter with the outsider-bias technique: his observations are evidence, but his frame is his own, not the Khmer's.

**Sources:** Zhou Daguan, *A Record of Cambodia* (trans. Peter Harris, 2007); Chandler (2008).

## The Unnamed

The artisans who carved the eight hundred meters of bas-reliefs at Angkor Wat. The laborers who dug the barays. The farmers who worked the paddies. The women who ran the markets. The dancers, the musicians, the weavers listed in temple endowment inscriptions as numbers without names. The Khmer inscriptions record the kings who ordered the temples and the gods who received the offerings. They do not record the hands that held the chisels.

**What we know:** The Old Khmer inscriptions list categories of temple workers by function and number. The Ta Prohm inscription lists 79,365 people. The names of individual workers do not appear. The bas-reliefs of Angkor Wat depict soldiers, dancers, market women, and farmers with individualized features and clothing, suggesting careful observation of real people by the sculptors. But the sculptors themselves are not named.

**What we cannot know:** Individual identities. The chapter acknowledges this gap directly: "The artisans who carved this were not named." This is not an apology but a statement about whose memory the Khmer state chose to preserve in stone.

**Sources:** Temple endowment inscriptions (various, see Coedes, *Inscriptions du Cambodge*, 8 vols.); Greater Angkor Project surveys (Fletcher et al.); Evans et al., LiDAR surveys (2013).

## A Note on Sources

This chapter draws on three categories of evidence: (1) The Khmer epigraphic corpus (Sanskrit and Old Khmer inscriptions, primarily translated and catalogued by George Coedes); (2) The architectural and archaeological record, including recent LiDAR surveys that have revealed the extent of the hydraulic landscape; (3) Zhou Daguan's Chinese eyewitness account from 1296. There are no Khmer prose histories, no court chronicles, no personal narratives from the Angkor period. The Khmer wrote their history in stone, and the stone preserves what the stone was designed to preserve: kings, gods, endowments, and the cosmic order. The lives of ordinary people are recoverable only through the administrative lists in temple inscriptions and through the archaeological record. This is a limit of the evidence, not a limit of the people who lived it.
