# PANEL-1 Review — P-3 Chinua Achebe

**Chapter:** Kongo Kingdom / Era 2: The Portuguese Encounter
**Round:** R1
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (P-3 — Cultural Voice)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## The Central Question

Does this culture own its own story?

I read this opening with a particular attention to the question I always ask: who is the protagonist? Not who is named most often, but whose reality structures the prose. In too many accounts of African history, the European appears in the doorway and immediately becomes the organizing center — the Africans respond, react, accommodate, resist — but always in relation to the European, who arrives like a protagonist from off the page.

This opening resists that pattern. Afonso is the center. The letter is his instrument. His theological and political reasoning is treated as primary, not as an interesting footnote to Portuguese expansion. This is correct, and it is not easy to achieve when writing about a period where the European archive is so much louder than the Kongo archive.

## What Is Working

The cosmogram passage is the most important paragraph in the opening. "The crucifix looked, to a Kongo eye, like the cosmogram" — this is the moment where the chapter decisively tells you whose eye you are looking through. The reader is inside Kongo cosmology, not looking at it. The cross is not a Christian object that the Kongo happens to find meaningful; it is a Kongo object that happens to share a vocabulary with Christianity. The conversion is narrated as recognition, not as surrender. This is exactly right.

The phrase "He chose baptism" is important — the word "chose" does a great deal of work here. It insists on Afonso's agency. He was not baptized by missionary pressure; he chose it as a young man after consideration. The chapter should keep this emphasis throughout: Afonso is an agent making strategic decisions, not a subject of historical forces.

The ending — "he put the argument on record" — is the chapter's ethical core stated cleanly. The opening is saying: whatever happened after, the argument was made, and it was correct. This is dignity.

## What Needs Careful Attention

**The legacy sentence.** "He said, in the language of the people who would later claim that Africa had no history worth recording, that this was wrong." This sentence is close to stepping outside the worldview — it is the author commenting on the historical record rather than Afonso speaking from within 1526. I understand the impulse: to push back against the Hegelian erasure of African history. But the chapter can make this point more powerfully by staying inside Afonso's frame. The fact that he wrote in Latin and his letters are in the archive — that is the refutation. The author does not need to say it.

**The missionary encounter.** When the chapter mentions that missionaries "destroyed" nkisi as "fetishes," it is accurate — but it gestures at an entire world of theological conflict that the full chapter must inhabit more deeply. What did it mean, inside Kongo theology, when a nkisi was destroyed? The opening says "an entire system of law and social contract" — this is correct and important. The chapter must show this from inside: what did a Kongo believer feel when a missionary burned their nkisi nkondi? Not what the missionary felt, and not what a modern anthropologist observes, but what the covenant-holder felt when the covenant was violated.

**The mpu.** The mpu is mentioned once, at the end, and it is mentioned correctly — it is the symbol of legitimate authority. The full chapter should develop this more. What does the mpu mean, physically? What is it made of, how does it sit on the head, who has the right to place it there? The material culture of Kongo legitimacy needs to be as concrete as the architecture of the cathedral.

## The Question I'd Push On

**"What does Afonso I call himself, and what does he call the King of Portugal?"**

In his letters, how does Afonso address the relationship? Does he use the vocabulary of brotherhood, of peer sovereignty, of Christian brotherhood? Does he invoke the specific terms of the diplomatic relationship that the Kongo and Portugal had established? The chapter should give us Afonso's own language for the relationship he believed existed — and then show us how the Portuguese replies (when they came) used different language. The gap between those two vocabularies is the tragedy.
