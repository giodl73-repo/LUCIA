# BOARD Review — Domain Expert 1
## Medieval French Political History
### Philippe Auguste et la Grande Expansion, Era 3

**Reviewer profile:** Specialist in Capetian administrative history, 12th-13th century French royal institutions. Expertise in John W. Baldwin's work on Philip Augustus's government, the bailli system, and the trésor des chartes.

---

## Factual Accuracy

**Bouvines date and account:** Correct. July 27, 1214. Guillaume de Breton's eyewitness account is the primary source; the chapter uses it appropriately. The tactical detail about Philip's army retreating over the bridge when the coalition caught up is consistent with the historical record (Baldwin, *Government of Philip Augustus*, Chapter 7).

**Fréteval document loss, 1194:** Correct. The loss of the royal archive to Richard's forces was a genuine crisis that accelerated Philip's systematic archival program. The trésor des chartes development is accurately characterized.

**Château Gaillard:** Correct. The siege ran August 1203 to March 1204. The latrine-shaft detail is historically documented (though the exact entry point is debated in the scholarship; some sources say it was the privy tower, others the latrine drain). The chapter's "latrine shaft" is the standard popular account and is defensible.

**Bailli system:** Accurate characterization. Philip did begin appointing baillis in the 1190s as salaried royal agents bypassing seigneurial jurisdiction. The chapter correctly identifies them as a mechanism for converting feudal claim into administrative fact.

**Ingeborg:** Accurate. The marriage August 14, 1193; repudiation the following day; Innocent III's interdict of 1200; Philip's nominal compliance without genuine reconciliation. The chapter's claim that "she was still waiting when Philip died in 1223" is correct; she did not return to Denmark until after his death.

**Béziers, 1209:** The July 22 date is correct. Guillaume de Tudèle's *Canso* is appropriately cited. The legate Arnold Amalric's reported statement ("Kill them all...") appears in the *Caesarius of Heisterbach* chronicle, written c. 1223. The chapter attributes it to Arnold Amalric which is the standard scholarly attribution while noting the uncertainty.

**Philip's death:** Correct. July 14, 1223, at Mantes.

**University charter of 1200:** Accurate. Philip granted the charter following the 1200 street incident between the royal provost's men and students. The details are as described.

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter's treatment of thaumaturgical kingship is the chapter's most sophisticated achievement from a scholarly standpoint. The claim that the healing ceremonies were not "superstition" but a functioning political-theological institution sustained across four centuries is exactly the position advanced by Marc Bloch in *The Royal Touch* (1973) and the scholarly consensus that has followed it. The chapter renders this from within the Capetian worldview without requiring modern explanation, which is the correct approach.

The Capetian sacred-legal synthesis is accurately characterized. The rex Christianissimus title, the Holy Ampulla, the coronation at Reims: these are correctly presented as operative political facts, not ceremonial decoration.

---

## Corrections Required

**Minor correction:** The chapter states Philip "anointed at Reims with the Holy Ampulla, a vial of sacred oil said to have been preserved since the baptism of Clovis in the year 496." The year 496 is the traditional date for Clovis's baptism but is historically contested; modern scholarship suggests c. 499-508 CE. However, 496 is the date the Capetians themselves used and believed; the chapter is accurate to the Capetian worldview.

**Minor note:** The chapter says the trésor des chartes was stored at "the Louvre." More precisely, the early trésor was housed in the Temple (the Paris commandery of the Knights Templar), which served as the royal treasury and archive, before the Louvre was developed as a royal site. Philip used the Temple extensively as a financial institution. The Louvre was primarily military at this stage. This is a minor inaccuracy that does not affect the chapter's argument; recommend a small correction ("at the royal treasury" rather than "at the Louvre").

---

## Recommendation

APPROVE with one minor correction (trésor storage location). The chapter is historically accurate and culturally authentic. The Bouvines account, the bailli system, the thaumaturgical kingship, and the Ingeborg episode are all handled with appropriate scholarly care. The use of Guillaume de Breton and Guillaume de Tudèle as sources is correct and appropriate.
