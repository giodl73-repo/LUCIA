## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of San, The Deep Time (Opening)

**Overall:** The voice is nearly right. The opening stays inside the circle. But I have concerns about where it steps to the edge.

**What works:**
- The opening two paragraphs are faultless. The singing, the dancing, the n/um rising — all presented as simply how the world works. No explanation for an outside audience. No "they believed that..." framing. The cosmology is reality. This is what inside-voice means.
- /Twi!kxao is a person, not a demonstration. He sweats. He breathes hard. He describes his experience in his own words. He is not performing his culture for the reader.
- The closing line. The children learn by watching. This is how knowledge actually moves in an oral tradition, and the opening shows it without ever saying "oral tradition."

**What doesn't:**
- "|Kia is not a word that translates." This sentence, while true, is the author speaking to the reader about the culture rather than from within it. Inside the circle, |kia does not need to announce its untranslatability. Consider cutting this sentence and letting /Twi!kxao's own description do the work — which it does, powerfully.
- "As precise and as learned as any knowledge on earth." This is a defense of the culture's sophistication, and defenses reveal the expectation of attack. Inside the worldview, the knowledge simply is what it is. The women adjust the singing because that is what they do. They do not need the narrator to certify their precision.
- The "We do not know" paragraph. The honesty is necessary, but "we" is an outside voice. Who is "we"? Not the San. The San know what they know. The archaeological record's gaps are a concern of scholarship, not of the community. This paragraph may need to find a way to acknowledge uncertainty without stepping outside the circle.

**The question I'd push on:**
Can the full chapter maintain this voice when it must discuss rock art dating, genetic evidence, and archaeological silence? These are scholarly frames. How will the chapter bring the reader into these topics without becoming a narrator who stands outside the San world and reports on it? The opening almost manages it — the "we" paragraph is the one place it falters. The chapter will face this problem on every page.
