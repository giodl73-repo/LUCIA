# The Principate — The Age of Augustus

## I. The Settlement

The marble was new. That was the point.

Augustus stood in his Forum and looked at what he had built. The temple of Mars Ultor rose at the far end of the enclosure, its Corinthian columns cut from Luna marble, white and sharp-edged in the afternoon light. Augustus found Roma a city of brick, the tradition says, and left it a city of marble. The direction was right even if the claim was large. The brick was the Republic's material. The marble was what came after.

He was not a king. Every stone was shaped by this. The Senate still met. The consuls still served their year. The tribunes still said veto. The assemblies still voted. Augustus held the tribunician power, which made his person sacrosanct and gave him the veto. He held proconsular imperium over the provinces where the armies were stationed, which gave him command of every legion. He held these powers not as a king holds a crown but as a magistrate holds an office: granted by the Senate, renewable, theoretically revocable. The theory was the structure. The fact that the powers would never be revoked was the reality that the structure contained.

The Augustan settlement was not a deception. It was an engineering problem solved with the materials at hand. The materials were the Republic's own forms: the consulship, the tribunate, the imperium, the Senate's auctoritas. Augustus assembled these forms into a new configuration that gave one man effective control while maintaining the appearance of collective governance. The configuration worked because both sides needed it. Augustus needed the Senate's legitimacy, because legitimacy was what distinguished a princeps from a tyrant, and the distinction was the lesson Caesar's body had taught on the Senate floor. The Senate needed Augustus's order, because the alternative was civil war, and the Senate had lived through enough civil war to prefer almost anything else.

The Forum Augustum made the argument visible. Along the colonnades, statues of the summi viri stood in their niches: the Julian ancestors on one side, the Republican heroes on the other, Augustus at the culmination of both lines. Scipio Africanus was there, the man who had defeated Hannibal and whose individual glory had frightened the Republic. Now the glory was safely commemorated, absorbed into the principate's genealogy, the dangerous precedent domesticated into a decorative ancestor. The inscriptions told the visitor what to understand: the Roman past led here. The disorder was over. The res publica was restored.

The word "restored" did the heaviest work. Augustus used it constantly. The Res Gestae Divi Augusti, the account of his deeds inscribed in bronze at his mausoleum and copied across the empire, opens with the claim that he restored the res publica to the Senate and the people. The claim was true in the way the mos maiorum was true: it described what the Romani needed to believe about what they had done, and the description became the reality because no one with the power to contest it had the interest to do so.

## II. The City

Roma in the age of Augustus held perhaps a million people. No city in the Mediterranean had held this many before. The number is debated, but the scale is not: the largest city in the ancient world was being fed, watered, housed, and policed by the machinery of a state that had been designed for a community of farmer-soldiers.

The grain came from Egypt. After Octavian's conquest of Egypt in 30 BCE, the Nile valley's grain harvest became Roma's lifeline. The annona, the grain supply, was the most politically sensitive institution in the empire. The citizens of Roma received a monthly grain ration, and the disruption of the supply caused riots. Augustus placed the grain supply under the control of a praefectus annonae, an equestrian official answerable to the princeps, not to the Senate, because the grain was too important to leave to senatorial politics. The grain ships sailed from Alexandreia to Puteoli and later to Ostia, the port at the Tiber's mouth that Claudius and Trajan would expand into the largest harbor in the western Mediterranean.

Water came through aqueducts. By the age of Augustus, Roma was served by six aqueducts; by the second century, eleven. The Aqua Virgo, which Augustus's friend Agrippa built, carried water from springs eight miles east of the city to the Campus Martius, where Agrippa had built the first great public baths. The water arrived at the top of the system and cascaded down through public fountains, private connections, and the baths where Roma washed itself daily. The baths were not luxury. They were the infrastructure of urban life in a city where most people lived in cramped insulae, the multi-story apartment blocks that rose five or six floors above the narrow streets. The insulae were built of wood and brick, they caught fire regularly, and their upper floors had no running water. The baths provided what the apartments could not: heat, water, space, sociability, the sound of voices in the warm rooms where the business of the day was conducted as often as it was conducted in the Forum.

The streets smelled of everything. The fullers' shops, where urine was collected in public vats and used to bleach togas, occupied the ground floors of buildings where families slept above. The bakeries ran their ovens from before dawn, the smell of bread mixing with the sharper smell of the charcoal. The taverns, the thermopolia, heated food in counters set into the street, and the smells of lentils and beans and cheap wine carried into the traffic of pedestrians, slaves on errands, litters carrying the wealthy above the mud. The streets were narrow enough that the upper stories of facing buildings nearly touched, blocking the light, and the noise of the ground level carried upward through wooden floors and plaster walls. Juvenal, the satirist writing in the early second century, complained that Roma was a city where no one could sleep, where the wagons that were banned from the streets during the day rolled all night, and the noise of the iron wheels on the basalt paving stones kept the living awake and did not bother the dead.

The enslaved were everywhere. They worked the households, the workshops, the docks, the construction sites, the farms. A wealthy Roman household might hold hundreds of enslaved workers. A modest household might hold three or four. The enslaved came from every corner of the empire and beyond: Gaul, Britannia, Syria, Egypt, Germania. Some were born into slavery. Some were captured in war. Some were abandoned children. The system was not a single thing. An enslaved Greek tutor in a senatorial household, reading Homer aloud to children who might become consuls, occupied a different world from an enslaved laborer in the Iberian silver mines, worked to death in conditions the sources describe as unendurable. The distance between those two lives was the distance between the system's ceiling and its floor, and both were the system.

## III. The Roads and the Frontier

From Roma the roads ran outward: the Via Appia south to Brundisium and the crossing to Greece, the Via Flaminia north to Ariminum and the Po valley, the Via Aurelia up the coast to Gaul. The road system, begun during the Republic for military logistics, had become by the principate the nervous system of an empire. The roads were built to last: a foundation of large stones, a layer of gravel and sand, a surface of fitted basalt blocks, raised above the surrounding terrain on an agger that kept the road dry and visible. Milestones marked the distance in Roman miles, a mile being one thousand paces, and each milestone carried the name of the emperor who had built or repaired that section of road.

A traveler moving along the Via Domitia through southern Gaul in the second century CE passed through a landscape that was Roman from the inside. The towns that lined the road were built on the Roman model: forum, basilica, baths, temple, amphitheatre. The language of administration was Latin. The language of commerce was Latin and Greek. The local language, whatever it had been, persisted in the countryside but receded in the towns. The traveler could change money at any town because the coinage was imperial, the same denarius carrying the same portrait of the same emperor from Britannia to Syria. He could find a bed at a mansio, a government rest-house built for official travelers, and a meal at a taberna along the road. He could carry a letter that would be transmitted through the cursus publicus, the imperial postal system that moved correspondence across the empire at a rate of roughly fifty miles a day.

The legions held the frontiers. The Rhine and the Danube formed the northern boundary in Europe, not because the rivers were impassable but because Augustus, after the loss of three legions in the Teutoburg Forest in 9 CE, had decided that expansion into Germania was not worth the cost. The general Varus had led his legions into an ambush prepared by Arminius, a Cheruscan chief who had served in the Roman army and knew its weaknesses. Three legions, perhaps fifteen thousand men, were destroyed in the forest. Augustus, the sources say, was heard wandering through his palace calling out "Quintili Vare, legiones redde!" Quintilius Varus, give me back my legions. The numbers of those three legions, XVII, XVIII, XIX, were never used again.

In the east, the frontier ran along the Euphrates, and across that river was Parthia, the one power that Roma could not subdue and that Crassus's disaster at Carrhae had made all Romans respect. In Africa, the frontier was the desert. In Britannia, where Claudius had invaded in 43 CE for reasons that combined strategic calculation with the emperor's need for military glory, the frontier eventually became Hadrian's Wall, a stone barrier running eighty Roman miles from coast to coast across the north of the island, staffed by auxiliaries from every corner of the empire: Batavians from the Rhine delta, Hamian archers from Syria, Dacians from the Danube. The wall was not a sealed border. It was a controlled crossing point, a customs barrier, a statement in stone that said: this far, and the Roman order obtains.

## IV. The Law

The jurists were Roma's most durable product.

In the quiet of their studies, in the houses on the Caelian and the Aventine, in the offices adjacent to the basilicas where the courts sat, the jurists wrote the law that would outlast the legions, the roads, the aqueducts, and the empire itself. They did not write legislation. They wrote responsa, opinions on specific cases, and the opinions accumulated into a body of legal reasoning that the later codifications would organize into the most influential legal system in human history.

The ius civile, the law that applied to Roman citizens, had grown from the Twelve Tables through centuries of juristic commentary into a sophisticated body of rules governing property, contract, inheritance, and obligation. The ius gentium, the law that applied to everyone within the empire regardless of citizenship, was based on principles the jurists derived from reason and common practice. The distinction between the two was not a wall but a conversation: the ius gentium informed the ius civile and the ius civile refined the ius gentium, and the jurists who worked at the boundary between them were doing what the Romani had always done, which was to build from precedent, case by case, the structure of their obligations to each other.

Gaius, the jurist who wrote his Institutes in the middle of the second century CE, organized the law into three categories: persons, things, and actions. Every legal relationship could be described within these categories. A person was either free or enslaved, citizen or non-citizen, sui iuris (legally independent) or under the power of another. A thing was either corporeal (land, slaves, animals) or incorporeal (debts, rights, inheritances). An action was the legal procedure by which a wrong was remedied or an obligation enforced. The clarity of the system was its power. A Roman judge in Britannia and a Roman judge in Syria, applying the same categories to different facts, could reach consistent results, and the consistency was what made the empire governable by law rather than by force alone.

In 212 CE, the emperor Caracalla issued the Constitutio Antoniniana, extending Roman citizenship to virtually every free person in the empire. The edict completed a process that had been underway for centuries: the gradual extension of citizenship from the city on the Tiber to the world the city had conquered. A man in Gaul, a woman in Egypt, a merchant in Syria were now cives Romani, subject to the ius civile, entitled to its protections, bound by its obligations. The law that had begun as the Twelve Tables inscribed on bronze in the Forum was now the legal framework of a world.

## V. The Emperors

The principate's fiction required that each emperor appear to receive his power from the Senate. The reality was that the army chose, and the Senate confirmed, and the confirmation was the fiction doing its work.

Augustus had solved the succession problem through adoption. Having no surviving male heir, he adopted Tiberius, his wife Livia's son from her first marriage, and Tiberius succeeded him in 14 CE. The principle of adoptive succession became the principate's most effective mechanism for producing competent rulers. It failed when blood triumphed over merit. The Julio-Claudian dynasty, Augustus's biological and adoptive descendants, produced Tiberius (competent, suspicious, reclusive), Caligula (erratic, possibly mentally ill, assassinated), Claudius (dismissed as a fool, proved an effective administrator, possibly poisoned by his wife), and Nero (artistic, extravagant, tyrannical, the last of the line, who killed himself in 68 CE as his generals revolted).

The Year of the Four Emperors that followed Nero's death, 69 CE, revealed the principate's structural weakness: the army could make an emperor, and different armies could make different emperors. Galba, Otho, Vitellius, and Vespasian fought for the purple in a civil war that lasted a year, and Vespasian, the general commanding in Judaea, won. The Flavian dynasty he founded lasted until 96, when Domitian was assassinated by his own household staff.

Then the system produced its best. Nerva, elderly and selected by the Senate, adopted Trajan. Trajan adopted Hadrian. Hadrian adopted Antoninus Pius. Antoninus adopted Marcus Aurelius. Four generations of adoptive succession produced four emperors whom the tradition regards as the empire's finest: the builder (Trajan), the traveler (Hadrian), the administrator (Antoninus), the philosopher (Marcus). The Pax Romana, the two centuries of relative peace and prosperity in the empire's interior, reached its height under their rule.

Marcus Aurelius wrote his Meditations on the Danube frontier, where the legions were fighting the Marcomanni and Quadi, Germanic peoples pressing against the border. The Meditations are in Greek, the language Marcus used for philosophy, and they are addressed to himself: reminders, exhortations, the private discipline of a man who held the most powerful office in the world and understood it as a burden. "At dawn, when you have trouble getting out of bed, tell yourself: I have to go to work, as a human being. What do I have to complain of, if I'm going to do what I was born for, the things I was brought into the world to do?" The philosophy was Stoic. The tone was duty. The pietas that had organized Roman life since the regal period found its most articulate expression in a man who did not want power but held it because the res publica required it.

Marcus broke the adoptive principle. He named his biological son, Commodus, as his successor, and Commodus was not his father. The Senate that had grown accustomed to adoptive emperors who maintained the fiction of senatorial partnership now faced an emperor who did not maintain it, who fought in the arena as a gladiator, who renamed the months after himself, who identified with Hercules rather than with the sober virtues of the Antonine tradition. Commodus was assassinated on December 31, 192 CE, and his death opened the next crisis.

## VI. The Limit

Marcus Aurelius died on the Danube frontier on March 17, 180 CE. The tradition says his last words were "Go to the rising sun; I am already setting." The tradition shaped the words to mark the moment, and the moment marked the beginning of what would, in retrospect, look like a change.

The change was not a decline. The word "decline" imposes a narrative of deterioration on a transformation that was more complex than a single direction. What happened after Marcus was that the principate's mechanisms, the mechanisms Augustus had built, began to produce results that Augustus had not anticipated. The army that chose emperors chose them more frequently and more violently. The provincial economies that had sustained the legions began to feel the weight of taxation and disruption. The frontiers that had held for two centuries began to face pressures that the existing garrison forces could not always meet.

But this is the next chapter's story. What Era 4 built was the world that Era 5 would inherit and transform. The roads. The law. The cities across three continents that looked like Roma and governed themselves by Roma's rules. The citizenship that now embraced every free person. The Latin language in the west and the Greek language in the east that carried the empire's administration and its literature. The Stoic philosophy that Marcus practiced and the Christian faith that was spreading through the communities of the east and the poor of the western cities, both offering answers to the question of how to live in a world governed by forces larger than any individual. The marble that Augustus had placed over the brick, and the brick that held the marble up.

The Forum Augustum still stood. The statues of the summi viri still looked down from their niches. The inscriptions still named the achievements of the men who had built the Republic, and among them, now, the achievements of the men who had maintained the principate, because the mos maiorum had expanded, as it always did, to include the new tradition inside the old. Augustus was an ancestor now. His Res Gestae was copied on temple walls from Galatia to Roma. The fiction he had built, that the Republic endured, had become so deeply embedded in the structure of governance that even the emperors who followed believed it, or acted as if they believed it, which amounted to the same thing.

The geese on the Capitoline were still fed. The augural tradition was maintained by the colleges of priests, though the emperors now held the chief priesthood themselves. The Senate still met, though its functions had narrowed to the governance of the senatorial provinces and the judiciary and the dignified confirmation of what the princeps had already decided. The consuls still served their year, their names still marking public time, though the year's meaning had changed from the Republic's loan of power to the principate's ornament of honor.

The roads ran outward from Roma in every direction. On those roads, the imperial post carried the princeps's edicts to every province. On those roads, the legions marched to the frontiers where the empire met the peoples it had not absorbed. On those roads, the merchants carried the wine and the oil and the garum to the towns that wanted them. And on those roads, in the second and third centuries, the followers of a teacher from Judaea carried letters to each other, organizing communities that the Roman legal system would eventually have to accommodate, because the communities were growing, and the thing they offered, a membership that did not depend on citizenship or property or census class, was spreading through the empire's fabric the way water spreads through brick, and the marble would have to account for it.
