# Notes: The Colonial Frontier
## Kalinago, Era 3: c. 1492–1650 CE
**Production Bible — compiled 2026-04-17**

---

## 1. Sources

**Source situation:** The Kalinago left no written records. The primary source base for this era is entirely external — Spanish, French, English, and Dutch accounts, all produced by people the Kalinago were actively fighting or managing. This is a source asymmetry problem of the highest order. The v1.5 amendment (Source Asymmetry as Narrative Technique) mandates turning this into technique rather than apologizing for it.

**Technique selected: Outsider-as-Structural-Evidence.** Every European account reveals the structure of the encounter, not the character of the Kalinago. Columbus describes "Caribs" to justify policy decisions he has already made. Las Casas corrects some of the wilder Spanish claims but writes from the position of a priest who wanted to save souls. The French accounts of the 17th century are written by missionaries and traders whose interests shape what they record. The chapter will treat each outsider account as evidence about the outsider, and use that evidence to construct the structural situation the Kalinago were navigating.

**The usable record:**
- Columbus's journals and letters (1493, Guadeloupe encounter): Tier 1 (primary, but read against the grain)
- Peter Martyr d'Anghiera's *Decades of the New World* (1516): Tier 2 — humanist scholar, more careful than Columbus, but still working with secondhand information
- Jean-Baptiste du Tertre's *Histoire Générale des Antilles* (1667–71): Tier 2 — French Dominican friar, direct observation of Kalinago; more sympathetic than most, still colonizer-position
- Raymond Breton's *Dictionnaire Caraïbe-François* (1665): Tier 1 — the Kalinago language itself, preserved by a French missionary who lived among them; the words tell you what the Kalinago thought was worth naming
- Philip Boucher's *Cannibal Encounters* (1992): Tier 2 — the scholarly case for reassessing the European record
- Neil Whitehead's work on Carib/Kalinago archaeology and ethnohistory: Tier 2 — the leading scholarly reconstruction of Kalinago political structures
- Lapointe (1978) on Kalinago resistance strategies: Tier 2
- Irving Rouse's archaeology of the Lesser Antilles: Tier 2

**The Breton dictionary** is the most important source. Raymond Breton lived among the Kalinago on Dominica from 1642 to 1654 — the very end of the era this chapter covers. His dictionary preserves Kalinago vocabulary: words for kinship, warfare, sea navigation, plant use, cosmology. The words are the evidence. What a culture names, it attends to. What it does not name, the European record cannot access.

## 2. Key Figures

**Source problem:** No named Kalinago individuals appear in the historical record for the early contact period (1493–1600). The Europeans who recorded encounters did not preserve Kalinago names — or if they did, the names were transliterated inaccurately and cannot be matched to specific individuals. For the later period (1600–1650), some names appear in French and English records, but their Kalinago-language names and actual roles are uncertain.

**Solution: v1.1 Amendment (Honest Limits as Technique) + v1.4 (Archaeological Figures at Embodied Presence).** The chapter will use composite figures constructed from what the Breton dictionary and archaeological record reveal, disclosed honestly in figures.md. The figures will be given specific knowledge and specific decisions, drawn from what we know was known and decided, without false names.

**Figure 1 — The Kalinago War Captain (composite):** A man who knows the sea lanes between Karukera and Martinique as a carpenter knows their tools — the currents, the reef passages, the best departure times for a northern raid, the safe anchorages. He has led war canoes north and returned with captives. He has sat in councils where the question was whether to attack a new European settlement before it could be fortified or to wait and assess. He knows these waters have been navigated by his father and his father's father. The chapter will follow his knowledge system and his decisions at method level (v1.3 requirement).

**Figure 2 — A Woman of the Women's House (composite):** Kalinago society had a significant gender division: men conducted warfare, women managed the agricultural and household economy. Women's knowledge of the forest — the plants that healed, the plants that fed, the plants that could be used as poison — was a parallel knowledge system. The women taken in raids and integrated into Kalinago communities often came with their own knowledge; integration was not simply absorption but exchange. This figure will appear in the agricultural and domestic knowledge sections.

**Figure 3 — A Captive Turned Kalinago (composite, for the integration/exchange dimension):** The raid economy produced a specific kind of figure: the person (usually a woman or child) taken from Taino or, later, African slave communities, integrated into a Kalinago community, and over time becoming Kalinago. The identity of the Kalinago was not biologically fixed; it was produced through practice, language, and the specific knowledge of island life. This figure is important for the chapter's terminal section, where African and Kalinago communities on St. Vincent begin the process that will produce the Garifuna.

**Figure 4 — Raymond Breton (historical, used as the "chronicler-within-chronicle" technique, v1.5):** Breton is the most valuable source. He is also a character: a French Dominican who learned Kalinago well enough to compile a dictionary, who lived on Dominica, who was trusted enough to observe ceremonies that no other European recorded. The chapter will use Breton's act of recording — and what he chose to record and what he couldn't understand — as a structural device. His dictionary entries will serve as chapter section pivots. The word for "war canoe" in Kalinago; the word for "enemy" vs. the word for "the different people"; the word for "the island." The language is evidence.

## 3. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The war canoe as the political instrument of Kalinago sovereignty.

**Mechanism:** The war canoe was not just military technology. It was the product of a specific social organization (cooperative labor, specialist direction, accumulated knowledge of materials), the instrument of a specific political economy (the raid-and-integration system that connected the island chain), and the medium through which Kalinago sovereignty was communicated (a fleet of canoes offshore is a message before it is a threat). The canoe required a community capable of producing it. The canoe protected the community that produced it. They were self-sustaining.

**The engine under stress:** European colonialism attacked the canoe's social basis before it attacked the canoe itself. Epidemic disease killed the canoe-builders. Forced labor took the men who would have paddled. Destruction of villages eliminated the cooperative networks that organized construction. The chapter must show this degradation not as catastrophe-from-outside but as the engine consuming its own fuel.

**Terminal condition:** The Kalinago defense system — mountain-and-sea, retreat-and-raid, island-knowledge-as-weapon — was effective against every force the Europeans deployed in the first 150 years. What it could not address was the mathematics of demographic collapse combined with the permanent establishment of fortified European settlements. The terminal condition is not military defeat but the exhaustion of the base that the engine drew from: the canoe-builders, the sea-readers, the island-knowers. The knowledge survives into Era 4 (the Garifuna) but the social structure that sustained it at scale does not.

**Non-chronological organizing principle (v1.5):** The chapter will organize around the war canoe itself as a material object-structural principle. Each section is oriented by what the canoe does or cannot do: the canoe as communication; the canoe as construction; the canoe as resistance; the canoe as the thing that cannot be built when the builders are gone. The object's properties determine the narrative's logic.

## 4. Narrative Arc — Section Plan

### Section 1: The First Encounter (c. 1493–1500)
Opening integrates here. The 1493 Guadeloupe encounter from Kalinago perspective. The Spanish record vs. the Kalinago reality. Raymond Breton's dictionary will provide the Kalinago word for "enemy" and what it actually meant in their political vocabulary — not permanent hatred, but a category of relationship that carried specific obligations and possibilities. The misunderstanding established.

### Section 2: The Sea Road (the canoe at full capacity)
The war canoe at its peak: construction, sea knowledge, the political economy of raiding. What does a warrior know who can navigate from Karukera to Borikén against the trade wind? The knowledge system at method level — specific navigation practices, reading wind and swell, the reef knowledge. This is the engine at full capacity. The Kalinago are not defending against the Europeans; they are operating their normal political world. The Europeans are an unusual arrival, handled in familiar ways.

### Section 3: The Calculus (strategic resistance, 1493–1600)
The Kalinago develop specific resistance strategies: attack new settlements before they can be fortified; use the mountain interior as refuge; maintain inter-island communication; negotiate temporary accommodations when necessary. Each of these follows from the engine's logic. The mountain interior is the island version of the sea road — a route only the island-knowers can use. The chapter will follow one specific tactical situation in detail (a settlement attack or a mountain retreat) to show the resistance at method level.

### Section 4: The Book and the Dictionary (knowledge systems in collision)
Breton's dictionary as structural pivot. The act of writing down Kalinago words: what does it mean to capture a language in a European book? What does the dictionary reveal about what the Kalinago attended to? The Spanish book that Columbus used to record the Kalinago before meeting them; the French book that Breton used to record them after living among them. Two very different relationships to the act of writing. The Kalinago are not literate in the European sense, but they have their own recording practices — the canoe itself is a kind of record, embodying the knowledge of its builders.

### Section 5: The Demographic Wall (1600–1650)
Epidemic disease arrives in the Lesser Antilles, slower than in the Greater Antilles but inevitable. Not a sudden collapse but a gradual erosion of the base. Fewer canoe-builders. Fewer paddlers. Islands that once coordinated now begin to communicate less. The terminal condition beginning to operate. The chapter shows this through the engine: canoes that are not replaced when damaged. Raids that are smaller than they used to be. Islands that used to be Kalinago that are now European.

### Section 6: The Knowledge Survives (landing)
The Kalinago were not defeated in this era. Several islands — Dominica, St. Vincent — remained beyond European control through 1650. The war canoe had not disappeared. But the social base that sustained it at full scale had been reduced. The chapter closes at St. Vincent, where the knowledge is still alive, where the next era's story will be born from the meeting of Kalinago and African communities. The landing uses the Breton dictionary: the Kalinago word for "the island," which is also the word for "the world." The island is the world. The world is still there.

## 5. Panel Pressure Points Anticipated

**Tuchman:** Will push for a specific Kalinago figure driving a specific decision. The composite war captain should appear early and be followed through multiple sections. Each major structural move should be attached to a person making a choice.

**Ibn Khaldun:** Will push for the terminal condition to be explicitly mechanical — not "they were overwhelmed" but "the canoe-building social structure was disrupted by X, which reduced Y, which meant Z." The demographic wall section must name the mechanism.

**Achebe:** Will push on any sentence where the author's explaining voice intrudes. Watch every instance of "this was," "this meant," "this shows." These are outside-lens alerts. The chapter must trust the scene to carry the analysis.

**Davis:** Will push for the knowledge systems at method level. The navigation knowledge, the botanical knowledge, the canoe-construction knowledge all need to be rendered at method level — not "they knew the sea" but what specifically they knew about the sea and how that knowledge was held and transmitted.

**Kapuściński:** Will push for sound and smell. The chapter has strong visual and tactile grounding from the opening. The full chapter needs to add sound (the sea, the wind, the speech of two languages not understanding each other) and smell (the volcanic soil, the salt, the wood of the canoe).

## 6. Open Questions

1. **The Taino collapse connection:** When the Taino communities of the Greater Antilles collapsed under Spanish contact (1494–1510s), what did this mean for the Kalinago raid economy that had depended on them as both target and partner? The chapter should address this even if the answer is uncertain.

2. **African-Kalinago contact in Era 3:** Escaped African enslaved people were present in the Caribbean from the 1500s. The formal alliance on St. Vincent that produces the Garifuna is an Era 4 story, but the first contacts belong to Era 3. Should the chapter acknowledge these early contacts? Probably yes — they set up Era 4 without colonizing it.

3. **The women's knowledge system:** The raid-integration economy meant women from Taino, and later African, communities were present in Kalinago villages. Their botanical and agricultural knowledge would have been in dialogue with Kalinago women's knowledge. The chapter should find at least one way to render this — Breton's dictionary may have vocabulary here.

4. **Raymond Breton's reliability:** Breton was sympathetic but not without bias. Where his accounts conflict with the archaeological record or other sources, the chapter should note the discrepancy (Style Guide Principle 13: Honest Uncertainty).

## 7. Voice Register

**Voice: Island-Maritime.** Close to Maritime Accumulative (Swahili) but with a different quality — less tidal, more tectonic. The Lesser Antilles are volcanic; they erupt. The prose should have the character of volcanic islands: green and steep and sudden, beautiful and resistant. Short declarative sentences for tactical moments (the capture, the council). Longer, gathering sentences for the sea passages and the knowledge transmission. The Breton dictionary entries land like carved stone.

**Em-dash target:** 0. The register finds its pauses through periods. The volcanic-island prose does not qualify or parenthesize; it states and moves.
