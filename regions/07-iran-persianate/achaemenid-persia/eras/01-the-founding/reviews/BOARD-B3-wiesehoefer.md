**Chapter:** Achaemenid Persia, Era 1 — The Founding
**Date:** 2026-04-17

## Domain Assessment

My expertise is particularly in the Zoroastrian dimension of Achaemenid self-understanding and in the relationship between the Persian religious tradition and the empire's administrative practices. The chapter has handled the Zoroastrian element with appropriate restraint — it is present, correctly characterized, and not overdetermined. I want to add a few observations on the theological framework.

## The Ahura Mazda Dimension

The chapter mentions that the Persian kings "honored a god called the Wise Lord" and references the rejection of the *daiva*, but it does not develop the Persian theological framework in this era's chapter. This is a defensible authorial choice — the Zoroastrian material is more explicit in Darius's inscriptions, which belong to Era 2. However, I want to flag one tension that the chapter should at least acknowledge in this era.

The Cyrus Cylinder's claim that Marduk chose Cyrus raises a question within Persian theology: was Cyrus himself a Zoroastrian, or was he a member of the older Iranian polytheist tradition? The Old Persian inscriptions from Cyrus's time are sparse; the royal Zoroastrian framework is more clearly developed in Darius's Behistun and Persepolis inscriptions. Some scholars (including myself and Briant) have argued that Cyrus may have been from a tradition that was proto-Zoroastrian or Mazda-worshipping but not yet the systematized Zoroastrianism of the Gathas. The chapter treats Ahura Mazda as "the Wise Lord" but does not engage with the uncertainty about the depth of Cyrus's personal theological commitment versus his successors'.

This does not require correction in a chapter that focuses on the founding policy rather than Cyrus's personal theology. But Era 2 (Darius) should engage with this question directly.

## The *Daiva* Inscription

The chapter refers to the rejection of the *daiva* in the context of Darius's era (correctly) and as background to this era. The *Daiva* Inscription — Darius's explicit rejection of the false gods — belongs to Darius's reign and should not be cited as founding-era evidence. The chapter does not cite it as such; it references the rejection of the *daiva* as the Persian theological framework generally. This is acceptable.

## Factual Review

**The *paradayadam* etymology:** Accurate. The Persian *pairidaeza* (walled garden/enclosure) entered Greek as *paradeisos* and English as "paradise." The claim in the chapter that Pasargadae's garden contributed to this etymology is a slight overreach: the *pairidaeza* as a concept predates Pasargadae and is a general Iranian institution. The chapter says Cyrus "planted" this garden; more accurate would be that the Pasargadae palace complex included garden enclosures typical of Persian royal estates. Minor.

**Cambyses:** Agree with Briant and Kuhrt on the Apis bull correction. The chapter's treatment of Cambyses's madness as a source-critical problem rather than a historical fact is exactly right.

**Cyrus's death:** The chapter notes that "his body was recovered and brought back to Pasargadae." This is accepted but the evidence is thin — the accounts of Cyrus's body's recovery are partly legendary. A brief "the tradition holds that" would add appropriate hedging.

## Cultural Authenticity

The Persian sacred tradition — fire altars, Magi, the cosmic dualism of Ahura Mazda and the forces of the Lie — is backgrounded but present. The chapter does not reduce Persian religion to a cynical political tool, which would be anachronistic. It correctly presents the sacred-acknowledgment system as reflecting a genuine theological framework ("the king's obligation was to maintain *asha* — truth, right order — against the forces of the Lie," from the Overview) operating alongside political calculation. This dual register — sincere and calculated simultaneously — is historically correct and reflects the ancient Near Eastern understanding of royal religious obligation.

## Corrections Required

**P2 — Minor clarification:** The Apis bull: apply Briant's correction. State that Egyptian records indicate natural death.

**P3 — Optional hedge:** "His body was recovered and brought back to Pasargadae" — consider "the tradition holds that his body was recovered." Not required.

**P3 — Minor:** The Pasargadae garden note could acknowledge that *pairidaeza* gardens were a standard feature of Persian royal estates rather than Cyrus's personal invention. Minor; author may leave as is.

## Endorsement

The chapter's handling of the Persian theological tradition is appropriate for a founding-era chapter that focuses primarily on the Babylonian encounter. The Zoroastrian dimension is correctly staged for development in Era 2. Endorsed with P2 correction applied.
