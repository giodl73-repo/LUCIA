# Opening — Era 3: The Great Cattle Era (c. 1700–1900 CE)

---

The goats come back with the smell of scrub thorn in their wool. Seyuuya knows this before she opens the gate. The particular dry-season smell of *yaito* and *trupillo*, the brittle-branched trees that cling to the red earth of the Alta Guajira and will not let go. The herd passed through the gully near the dry well and found nothing there, so they pushed east toward the Macuira highland. They are thin. The wet season was short this year, shorter than her grandmother said it used to be, and the pasture toward the Serranía de la Macuira is already brown-stalked and spent.

She counts them through the gate. Eleven goats. One gone.

She will tell her brother Maushi before she tells anyone else, because it is his responsibility. He is uterine brother and the one who handles the eirruku's herd negotiations with the neighboring clan. But she already knows what he will say: the lost one may have died at the gully, or it may have wandered east into Wouriyu territory, and if it wandered, someone will need to find it or we will need to ask for it back, and asking for it back is a conversation that requires a pütchipü'üi.

This is how things are resolved. Not by going to recover the goat yourself. That risks turning a lost animal into a confrontation, a confrontation into an insult, an insult into a blood debt. You send for the word-bearer instead, the one trained to carry the language of obligation between people who cannot speak to each other directly yet. The Wayúu have known this for longer than anyone can remember: the words are the weapons. The right words, offered at the right time with the right gifts, prevent the wrong words. The wrong words get people killed.

Seyuuya's daughter Apijiyu sits inside the enclosure, crosslegged in the low afternoon light, working a mochila. She is eleven. She has been learning the first patterns for three years now, and this year Seyuuya judged her ready for the eirruku design. The Epinayuu mark. The one that belongs to their matrilineal clan, the one that will identify her for the rest of her life as a woman of this lineage. The colors come from the dye-plants near the water sources. The pattern comes from the mothers, who learned it from their mothers, who learned it from the ones before.

"You went too wide on the fourth row," Seyuuya says, dropping down beside her.

Apijiyu does not argue. She studies the problem, pulling the thread back four passes, beginning again. The mochila will take weeks. The pattern is not a decoration; it is a statement of who she is and where she belongs. When she is older and carries this bag through a market, or brings it to a ceremony, or negotiates a marriage settlement, the Epinayuu mark will precede her introduction. It will say: she has people. She is not alone in the world.

The cattle came later than the goats. In the Alta Guajira now, the cattle matter more. The Spanish brought them, first to the Riohacha ranches in the lowlands, then moving north onto the Guajira as the Wayúu learned what the new animals could do. What they could do was graze the dry-season scrubland that goats alone could not sustain, push further into the Alta Guajira's hardest reaches, and accumulate into herds that the matrilineal clans could move and manage the way they had always moved and managed water access and pearl-fishing rights. A Wayúu household's wealth is measured in cattle now. A bride price is paid in cattle. A blood debt, the compensation owed for a death or an insult, is settled in cattle, in jewelry, in cloth.

The Spaniards thought this would give them leverage. They were wrong. The Wayúu adopted the cattle and the cattle became Wayúu. The animals moved with the seasonal rains, north toward the Serranía de la Macuira in the wet season, south toward the Baja Guajira and the trade ports when the water dried. They were herded by men, managed by women, tracked through the eirruku's networks of obligation and reciprocity. No hacienda owner could plant a permanent ranch on the Alta Guajira because no one could control the water. The Wayúu controlled the water, and with it the cattle, and with the cattle everything that the cattle gave.

Apijiyu looks up from the mochila. "How long before the rain?"

Seyuuya studies the sky. The particular flat haze of late dry season, the brownish horizon, the absence of cloud. She knows the signs better than she can explain them: the temperature of the east wind, the quality of light over the Serranía, the behavior of the cattle when real moisture is coming. She has read this sky every year of her life.

"Two months," she says. "Maybe three."

The girl bends back to her work. Outside, the goats shift against each other in the enclosure, patient with the patience that animals in the Guajira learn because there is no other choice. The missing one is still out there, at the gully, in the eastern scrub, somewhere. But this is the meanwhile, and the meanwhile is most of life: working the pattern, watching the sky, counting what you have, waiting for the rains that will come or will not come, and weaving the record of who you are into something you can carry.
