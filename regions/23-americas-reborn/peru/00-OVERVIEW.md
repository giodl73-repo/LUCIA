# Perú — El País de los Cuatro Suyus

Peru is a layered world. Beneath the republic — proclaimed in 1821 by a creole elite who wanted independence from Spain — lies the colonial viceroyalty. Beneath the viceroyalty lies Tawantinsuyu, the Inca empire whose Andean capital at Cusco was the largest city in the Americas before the conquest. And beneath Tawantinsuyu lie the cultures that preceded it: the Wari, the Tiwanaku, the Chimú, the Moche, the Nazca, and the dozens of regional traditions that developed in the extreme ecological diversity of the Andean world — coastal desert, highland valleys, tropical cloudforest, Amazonian lowland — over more than five thousand years of continuous cultural development. The Peruvian state claims this entire deep history as its national patrimony. The Quechua, Aymara, and Amazonian peoples who are the living descendants of these civilizations are, in the state's account, Peru's glorious past. Their present — structural exclusion from political power, land dispossession, bilingual education still inadequate, the highest rates of poverty concentrated in the highlands and the jungle — is the subject the official narrative finds harder to address.

The tension between the celebrated Andean past and the marginalized Andean present is the deepest unresolved contradiction in Peruvian national life. It runs through the Indigenismo movement (the early twentieth-century intellectual project of claiming the indigenous heritage as the foundation of a new Peruvian national identity), through the Velasco Alvarado military government's land reform (1969–1975, which redistributed the haciendas and renamed the country's indigenous language "el quechua" as an official language for the first time since the conquest), through the Shining Path (Sendero Luminoso) insurgency whose roots were in the highland communities where the disconnect between constitutional promise and lived reality was most stark, and through the contemporary movements for indigenous rights and environmental protection that continue today.

Key places:
- **Cusco (Qusqu)** — "the navel of the world" in Quechua; the Inca capital whose colonial churches are built on Inca stone foundations; the living capital of Andean culture
- **Machu Picchu** — the Inca citadel never found by the Spanish; visited by Hiram Bingham in 1911; the most recognized archaeological site in South America
- **Nazca Lines** — the desert geoglyphs; enormous figures (spiders, hummingbirds, lines) visible only from the air; created by the Nazca culture c. 500 BCE–500 CE
- **Lake Titicaca (Titicaca)** — at 3,812 meters the highest navigable lake on Earth; in Inca cosmology, the place of creation; the Uros people live on floating reed islands

## What Defined Them

**The Andean cosmovision — Pachamama.** In Andean thought, Pachamama — the Earth — is not a resource but a living being with whom humans are in a reciprocal relationship. The concept of ayni (reciprocity) governs not only human social relations but the relationship between humans and the natural world: you take, you give back; you receive, you return. The agricultural terraces (andenes) of the Inca are not merely engineering; they are the physical form of the relationship with Pachamama — the careful tending of the land's capacity to support life. Contemporary movements for the rights of nature in Ecuador and Bolivia draw directly on this Andean cosmovision, which has survived five centuries of colonial Christianity.

**The quipu and Andean knowledge.** The Inca recorded information — census data, tribute records, calendar calculations, and possibly narrative — on quipus: knotted strings in which the type of knot, the position on the string, and the color of the fiber all encoded meaning. The quipu is one of the most intriguing unsolved problems in the archaeology of communication: most of the census and accounting quipus have been interpreted, but the narrative quipus (if they exist) have not been decoded. The quipu is the Andean alternative to writing — a different cognitive technology for storing and transmitting information, adapted to the conditions of a society without paper or clay.

**La Raza de Bronce — Indigenismo and the political claim.** The Indigenismo movement of the early twentieth century — associated with intellectuals like Luis Valcárcel and the Marxist José Carlos Mariátegui — argued that the authentic foundation of Peruvian national identity was not Spanish but Andean, and that the political liberation of the country required the liberation of the indigenous majority from the hacienda system. Mariátegui's *Seven Interpretive Essays on Peruvian Reality* (1928) is the founding text of Peruvian socialist Indigenismo: the argument that socialist revolution and indigenous rights were the same project, because the poverty of the indigenous communities was the poverty that capitalism and colonialism had created. This argument was taken to its violent conclusion by the Shining Path, which claimed Mariátegui as a forefather while practicing a violence he could not have endorsed.

**The internal conflict and its 70,000 dead.** The Shining Path (Sendero Luminoso) insurgency (1980–1992) and the smaller MRTA insurgency produced the bloodiest period in Peru's republican history. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission (2003) estimated 70,000 dead, the majority in the highland communities of Ayacucho and the surrounding regions — predominantly Quechua-speaking, predominantly indigenous, predominantly poor. The perpetrators were both Sendero and the Peruvian armed forces; the victims were the communities caught between them. The conflict's origins in the specific conditions of highland poverty and state neglect — the communities with the highest rates of poverty, the lowest rates of state services, and the strongest traditions of local governance — are the record of what the republic failed to deliver.

## The Chapters

**1 — Caral:** In the Supe Valley, 5,000 years ago, the people of Caral are building the oldest urban complex in the Americas — pyramids, plazas, and residential areas contemporary with the earliest Egyptian dynasties and the Sumerian cities of Mesopotamia. They have no writing and apparently no weapons. The civilization that preceded the Inca by three millennia is still being excavated.

**2 — Tawantinsuyu:** A runner on the Inca road system (Qhapaq Ñan) is carrying a quipu message from Cusco to Quito — 2,000 kilometers in less than a week, by relay. The road system is 30,000 kilometers of engineered trail, suspension bridges, and rest houses (tambos). The Inca organized labor, not cash: the mit'a system required every household to contribute labor to state projects in exchange for the state's provision of food and materials. This is not exploitation in the modern sense. It is a different logic of obligation.

**3 — La Conquista:** November 16, 1532: in the plaza of Cajamarca, the Inca Atahualpa meets Francisco Pizarro. A priest holds up a Bible; Atahualpa, who has never seen a book, puts it to his ear (some accounts say he drops it). The Spanish attack and capture him. The ransom that follows — a room filled with gold, and another with silver — does not save him. He is strangled on July 26, 1533.

**4 — Tupac Amaru II:** November 1780: José Gabriel Condorcanqui, who has taken the name Túpac Amaru II in honor of the last Inca executed by the Spanish, leads the largest indigenous uprising in colonial Latin American history. His forces lay siege to Cusco. He is captured, tortured, and executed in the plaza of Cusco in a manner designed to be as visible and terrible as possible. His name will be taken by the next generation of revolutionary movements.

**5 — La Interna:** An anthropologist from Ayacucho named Abimael Guzmán has built, from his university base, a disciplined Maoist insurgency called Sendero Luminoso. In May 1980, the day before the first democratic elections since the military government, Sendero burns the ballot boxes in the village of Chuschi. The internal conflict has begun. It will kill 70,000 people over the next twelve years, most of them the Quechua-speaking communities Sendero claims to represent.

**6 — El Presente Inestable:** Peru has had six presidents in five years (2016–2021). The Constitutional Tribunal has removed two presidents on dubious grounds; a third has fled to another country; a fourth has been arrested. In 2021, a rural schoolteacher from the highlands named Pedro Castillo is elected president — the first president to come from the communities most affected by the internal conflict and by structural poverty. He is removed from office by Congress in December 2022 after a failed self-coup. His vice president, Dina Boluarte, becomes president. The protests in his support kill dozens. The republic is still working out what it is.
