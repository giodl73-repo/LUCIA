# Figures — La Conquista: La Espada y la Cruz

---

## Juan López de Palacios Rubios (c. 1450–1524)

The record does not tell us what Palacios Rubios looked like, or whether he hesitated when he wrote the Requerimiento. It tells us he was born in Salamanca, that he studied law at the university his city had been building for three centuries, that he went to Bologna for canon law, that he joined the Council of Castile, that he was trusted by Ferdinand and Isabella and then by their grandson Charles. He was, by any measure, one of the most learned jurists in Spain.

He wrote the Requerimiento because he was asked to. The Crown needed a legal instrument — a form that could be performed before hostilities, that would establish the just cause that Catholic theology required before war could be legitimate. He built one from the materials available: the doctrine of papal temporal authority, the theory of just war that Aquinas had developed from Augustine, the legal framework of the Reconquista. It was, within its own terms, a serious piece of legal reasoning.

Las Casas reported that when someone described to Palacios Rubios how the document was actually being used — read to no one, read at night, read from ships — the jurist laughed. It is a different laughter from Las Casas's. Las Casas laughed at the legal absurdity, and then stopped laughing at the moral gravity. Palacios Rubios, if the story is true, laughed at something else — perhaps at the gap between the instrument he had crafted and the use to which it had been put. He died in 1524, before the conquest of Mexico was complete. He did not see Potosí.

## Bartolomé de las Casas (1484–1566)

He came to the Caribbean in 1502, eighteen years old, on the same ships that were already making the pattern visible. He was an encomendero for nine years. He held the labor rights to indigenous people. He participated in the conquest of Cuba in 1511. He watched.

The Montesinos sermon of 1511 is usually the turning point the accounts give, and perhaps it was. What is certain is that by 1514, Las Casas had renounced his encomienda and begun to argue, in public, against the system. He joined the Dominicans in 1522. He argued before the King. He argued before the Council of the Indies. He sailed back and forth between Spain and the Americas for decades, gathering testimony, writing reports, making the legal-theological argument that the indigenous peoples were *inocentes* — not guilty of any offense that could justify war — and that the conquest was therefore a crime against God as well as against the people.

He was not a simple man, and the *Brevísima relación* is not a simple document. It is the most honest account of the conquest written by a participant who was also a sincere Catholic priest, using the framework of Catholic moral theology to indict what Catholic civilization was doing. It was used by Spain's Protestant enemies for three centuries as anti-Spanish propaganda — the Black Legend. Las Casas had not intended that use. He had intended an argument before the Crown. Both uses were possible from the same document, and this was not his failure. This was the document's condition: it was true, and truth is available to whoever reads it.

He died in 1566, in Madrid, at age eighty-two. He was still writing. There is a painting of him in the sacristy of Seville Cathedral — old, severe, certain. He knew what he had seen. He believed it could be changed. He was wrong about whether it would be changed, but not wrong about what he saw.

## Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda (c. 1490–1573)

He had read more Aristotle than almost anyone in Spain. He had translated the *Politics* into Latin. He was a humanist — a man of the Renaissance, trained in the Greek and Latin texts, committed to the recovery of classical learning as the foundation of a renewed civilization. He was also a man of the Church, and a man of Spain.

His argument at Valladolid was not careless. It was the application of a considered reading of Aristotle to a genuine political-theological problem. Aristotle had argued that some human beings were, by nature, suited to be governed rather than to govern — that their subjugation was not tyranny but the correct ordering of a relationship that served both parties. Sepúlveda applied this to the indigenous peoples of the Americas. He had not met them. He had read accounts. The accounts described human sacrifice, cannibalism, idolatry. These were, in Sepúlveda's reading, evidence of the incapacity for self-governance that Aristotle had described.

He was wrong in his conclusion. The question of whether he was wrong in his method — whether the application of Aristotle's categories to a situation Aristotle had not imagined was inherently flawed — is one his opponents at Valladolid did not fully resolve, because they shared more of his framework than they rejected. Las Casas also read Aristotle. Las Casas also believed in the authority of Aquinas and the traditions of scholastic philosophy. Both men were arguing within the same tradition. The tradition did not resolve the argument.

Sepúlveda outlived Las Casas by seven years. He continued to argue his position. He never accepted that he had been wrong.

## Moctezuma II (c. 1466–1520)

He had been *tlatoani* — speaker — of the Mexica for seventeen years when Cortés arrived. He had expanded the tribute network. He had built. He had organized. The city Bernal Díaz described was, in large part, the city Moctezuma had maintained and supplied.

He was not a simple actor. The reports of the Spanish arrival had come in for two years. He had sent gifts — gold, featherwork, the elaborate work of the craftsmen who served the Mexica court — and the gifts had made things worse, because the Spanish had seen them and wanted more. He had tried to prevent Cortés from reaching Tenochtitlan by diplomacy and by encouraging other peoples to block the route. None of it worked. When Cortés arrived in the city in November 1519, Moctezuma received him because the protocols of his world required a formal reception of someone arriving under the circumstances Cortés arrived under. The cosmological reading of the situation was not certain — the year One Reed and the return of Quetzalcóatl were possibilities the omens had made relevant, not certainties Moctezuma had committed to. He was managing a situation. He managed it incorrectly, by the outcome. Whether he managed it incorrectly by the standards of knowledge available to him at the time is a different question.

He died in June 1520. The Spanish said the Mexica killed him when he tried to calm their uprising. The Mexica accounts said the Spanish killed him. The truth is not established. What is certain is that by the time of his death, his political position was impossible: he had received the Spanish as guests and found that his guests could not be managed, and his own authority over his people had been destroyed by the appearance of accommodation. He died in circumstances that neither side has ever fully explained.

The city fell to Cortés and his Tlaxcalan allies in August 1521. The causeways were broken. The aqueducts were cut. Tenochtitlan was demolished, methodically, and the city of Mexico built on its foundations. The Templo Mayor, the great pyramid at the center of the Mexica world, was buried under the foundations of the Metropolitan Cathedral. It was excavated by archaeologists in 1978, still there, waiting.

## Felipe Guaman Poma de Ayala (c. 1535–after 1616)

The dates of his birth and death are not certain. What he looked like is preserved in his own drawings — a man in Andean dress, standing before Spanish officials, holding papers. He drew himself as a man who had mastered the Spanish documentary form and was using it to argue for his people. He drew himself that way because that is what he was.

His father had served the Spanish as an interpreter in the early years of the conquest. This position gave Guaman Poma access to the colonial administration from both sides. He learned Spanish. He learned the legal vocabulary. He learned how the encomienda system worked, how the tribute system worked, how the mita labor draft worked, and who was benefiting from them and who was not. He spent decades traveling the Andean world, gathering testimony from indigenous communities about what was being done to them, translating the testimony into Spanish legal categories, and constructing the argument.

The letter he sent to Philip III was not naive. He knew the king could not personally supervise the behavior of every administrator in Peru. He was arguing about the structure, about the laws, about the gap between what the Crown said and what its agents did. He was asking for the Crown's intervention because he believed — on the evidence of the New Laws, on the evidence of Las Casas's partial victories before the Council — that the Crown was capable of intervention if it chose.

The letter was lost. This was not unusual. Letters from the periphery of the empire arrived regularly and were filed and sometimes forgotten. The Royal Library of Copenhagen acquired manuscripts from various European collections in the seventeenth century; Guaman Poma's letter was among them; no one who catalogued the collection in the eighteenth century knew what it was. The Danish philologist Richard Pietschmann found it in 1908 and recognized it. It was published in 1936.

It is now in Copenhagen. You can read it. It says what he intended it to say. The answer still has not come.

*Note on figures: Palacios Rubios's interior life is reconstructed from the institutional record and Las Casas's account; the laughter episode is reported by Las Casas and may be apocryphal but is consistent with what the sources suggest about Palacios Rubios's character. Moctezuma's thoughts at the meeting on the causeway are not recoverable; the chapter renders him as a political actor, not a psychological subject, precisely because the record does not support more. Guaman Poma's letter is primary source; the chapter quotes its logic rather than its specific words. All figures appear in their own historical record; none is composite.*
