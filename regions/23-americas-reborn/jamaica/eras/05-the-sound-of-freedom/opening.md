# Era 5: The Sound of Freedom — Reggae and Rasta
## Opening

Kingston, 1963. The government yard on First Street, Trench Town.

Count the beats and you will miss it. The music lives on the one that isn't played.

In a room that was not much of a room — corrugated zinc above, a cement yard outside, the smell of the whole tenement district coming through on a breeze that was not cool enough to call relief — three young men were working out something that had not yet been worked out. Bob Marley was twenty. Peter Tosh was nineteen. Bunny Wailer was not yet eighteen. They had come to Kingston from the hills and the country, drawn by the sound systems that threw music from speakers taller than a man, by the promise that the city held something the land had used up. The city did not hold what they expected. It held something else.

Trench Town was government housing built after a hurricane cleared the slums, and it had already become a different kind of slum: zinc and concrete instead of wood, but hunger the same, police the same, the feeling the same that the country called independent was not yet independent for the people who lived here. The Union Jack had come down in August 1962, eight months ago. The Union Jack coming down had not changed the price of cornmeal.

What they were doing in that room, in that yard, was playing music. They were playing the music that was moving through the sound systems — ska, the Kingston rhythm built from Jamaican folk and American R&B and something that came from neither, a rhythm where the guitar and piano hit on the upbeat, the off, the space where Western music expected silence. The saxophone shrilled and the bass walked. You danced to it. But you also listened, because the men who played it were listening, and what they were listening to was bigger than the music.

Hailé Selassié I, Conquering Lion of the Tribe of Judah, King of Kings, Lord of Lords. The Emperor of Ethiopia, who had been crowned in 1930 and whose coronation had been read in Trench Town and in West Kingston and in the hills by men and women who had heard Marcus Garvey say: look to Africa for the crowning of a Black king, and know that the day of deliverance is near. The men who read that coronation had made something from it. They called it Rastafari, from Ras Tafari Makonnen, the Emperor's name before he was emperor. They called Babylon the whole of the world's arrangement — the colonial system, the plantation memory, the police in the yard, the price of cornmeal — and they set against Babylon the vision of Zion, which was Africa, which was Ethiopia, which was the place the soul was trying to reach.

Bob Marley was not yet fully committed to the faith. He would be. By the early 1970s he would wear his hair in locks that took years to grow and would not touch them with scissors because Samson's strength was in his hair and the Nazarite vow said leave what grows, leave what God made, and your locks are your antenna to Jah. But in 1963 in the yard, what he was working out was the music.

Here is what the music was doing: it was taking the offbeat and making it the beat. Western music — the march, the waltz, the blues, the rock and roll that came up through the radio — gave its emphasis to the one and the three, to the downbeats, to the places where the foot comes down. The ska and the rocksteady and the reggae that came from them gave their emphasis to the two and the four, to the upbeats, to the spaces. The guitar chop, the piano skank — they landed where the Western ear expected rest. What the ear heard as a space, the music heard as a statement.

This was not an accident of preference. This was philosophy. To invert the rhythmic hierarchy was to say: what you built your world on, we build our resistance on the space you left. Your one, our two. Your three, our four. The beat that was not the beat became the beat that drove the world.

Bunny Wailer was playing rhythm. Peter Tosh was working the guitar. Bob Marley was writing words. The words came from the yard, from the Bible, from the reasoning sessions where Rastafari men sat in a circle and passed the chalice — the long clay pipe — and spoke what they saw clearly, because the herb opened the eye and the eye needed opening in Babylon. The reasoning was not casual. It was theology. It was also political analysis. It was also history: the history of Africa before the Middle Passage, the history of the plantation, the history of what Marcus Garvey called the Redemption that was coming.

Outside, Kingston was turning. The two parties were building their garrison communities, giving out housing and jobs and loyalty in exchange for votes, and the loyalty was beginning to have guns behind it. The guns were not here yet in 1963. They were coming. But in the yard, the answer being worked out was not the garrison. It was the music. It was the word set to the offbeat, the message carried on the frequency that passed through walls.

The music would leave this yard. It would leave Jamaica. It would find people in London and Lagos and Johannesburg and Tokyo who had never heard a sound system, who did not know Trench Town from any other address on Earth, and it would speak to them. Not because it was catchy — though it was. Because it was honest. Because somewhere in the skank and the bass and the words that said Babylon is falling and Zion is coming and every man has a right to his freedom, something was being said that those people needed said. That the world was arranged against the poor and the dispossessed and the Black and the forgotten, and the world did not have to remain so arranged, and a man with a guitar and a theology and a three-hundred-year inheritance of suffering and survival could say this in three minutes and make you believe it.

In the yard on First Street, the three young men were not thinking about the world. They were thinking about the song. This is how the world gets changed: someone stops thinking about the world and thinks about the song.
