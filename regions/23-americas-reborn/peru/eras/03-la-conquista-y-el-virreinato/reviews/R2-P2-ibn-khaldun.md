# R2-P2 — Ibn Khaldun
## Panel-2 Targeted Review — Notes: La Conquista y El Virreinato

The notes address my primary concern from Panel-1 — the coalition fracture question — with precision. The production bible correctly identifies the unevenness of the kuraka network and places it in Section 5. What I now want to see executed carefully:

**On the Inca mit'a vs. colonial mita distinction:** The notes name it. The chapter must show it at the level of specific obligation. Under the Inca mit'a: a household contributes labor, receives food from state storehouses (the qollqa) during the work period, is provisioned for the round trip, returns to its fields having fulfilled a cosmic obligation. Under the colonial mita: the same word, a radically different contract. The miner walks to Potosí (sometimes months away), receives a nominal wage that does not cover food and coca, inhales mercury, and is expected to supplement his forced wage labor with additional "free" labor (the minka) to survive. The chapter should show this distinction through one person's experience — a single mita worker's passage — not through institutional summary.

**On the coalition fracture:** The notes plan correctly places the explanation in Section 5. What I want to see: the specific interest-calculus of a kuraka who sided with Spain. This is not a moral question (though it may feel like one). It is a structural question. A kuraka who had built his local authority through the colonial administrative network — who collected tribute, administered repartos, certified mita labor — had material interests in the system's continuity that the Andean cosmological claim of ayni-violation did not override. His asabiyya was attached to the colonial network, not to the Andean community beneath him. The chapter should name one such figure and show his calculus, even briefly. This is not condemnation. It is structural analysis.

**On premise non-recognition as terminal condition:** The notes identify this correctly. I want the chapter to make the asymmetry precise: Condorcanqui argued within the Spanish legal framework AND within the Andean cosmological framework simultaneously, believing both were binding on the Spanish side. The Spanish recognized only the legal framework when convenient, and overrode it when not. The Andean cosmological claim was simply invisible to them as a binding obligation. The terminal condition is not military defeat; it is the impossibility of a covenant between parties operating from incompatible theories of obligation.

The Guaman Poma reference is excellent. A man who spent years writing a 1,200-page letter to a king who never read it is the premise-non-recognition engine in biographical form. If the chapter can integrate Guaman Poma's letter as a structural parallel — the petitioning voice that the sovereign does not hear — it will carry the systemic argument with specific human weight.
