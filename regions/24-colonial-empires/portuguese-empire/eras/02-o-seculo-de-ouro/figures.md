# Figures — O Século de Ouro
## Portuguese Empire, Era 2 (1498–1580 CE)

---

## Vasco da Gama (c. 1460–1524)

He was not a man who inspired affection. The accounts of those who served under him describe a commander who was precise, effective, and capable of violence that he understood as instrumental rather than personal. When he burned the Miri — the Arab pilgrim ship he encountered on his second Indian voyage in 1502, with its crew locked below and the hatches sealed — he did so in the manner of a man completing a calculation, not a man giving way to rage. The calculation was: if the Muslim merchants of the Indian Ocean believe that they can trade without Portuguese permission, they will trade without Portuguese permission. If they believe that the cost of defiance is their ship and their lives, they will pay the toll.

His first voyage (1497–1499) is the event; his second and third voyages show the man. On the second, he destroyed Calicut harbor with his cannon because the Zamorin had not yet accepted Portuguese terms. On the third (1524), he arrived as Viceroy of India and died of fever in Goa three months after landing, still trying to reform the administration he had helped to create and that had already, in nine years, acquired the corruption that sustained establishments acquire. His bones were sent back to Portugal in 1539, then returned to Goa in 1898 for the celebration of the four-hundredth anniversary of the first voyage. He has been moved more times dead than he was born to be.

What he opened was not what he intended. He intended a route to the spice trade. He opened a century of Portuguese presence in the Indian Ocean. He intended to break Venetian and Arab commercial dominance. He succeeded for approximately sixty years. He could not have intended the Goa Inquisition or the casados or Camões's poem or Alcácer Quibir; he died before most of these were yet conceived. He opened the door. What came through the door was the whole Golden Century, which was more than any one man could have planned.

## Afonso de Albuquerque (1453–1515)

The Governor of Portuguese India for six years (1509–1515) was a small, precise man with a long white beard and the specific quality of attention that military administrators develop when they understand that attention is the only resource they have enough of. He did not have enough soldiers. He did not have enough ships. He did not have enough money from Lisbon. He had enough attention to use what he had with minimal waste.

His letters to King Manuel are the primary document of the feitoria system's logic. He explains, with the patience of a man who knows his reader is not on the ground and does not fully understand the ground, exactly why Hormuz, Aden, and Malacca are the three points that control the entire Indian Ocean trade. He explains the mathematics of it: if you control the mouth of the Persian Gulf, all trade between the Gulf and the rest of the world passes through you. If you control Malacca, all trade between the Indian Ocean and the China Sea passes through you. Aden he could not take; it was the gap in the system that would eventually allow the Muslim trade to recover. But Hormuz and Malacca he took, and they held for a century.

His personal project, never completed, was the diversion of the Nile — the engineering fantasy of drying out Egypt by rerouting its water, collapsing Mamluk Egypt as an agricultural and commercial power, and leaving the road to Mecca and Medina open for Portuguese assault. He requested the engineering plans from cartographers in Lisbon. The project was not carried out, partly because it was physically impossible, partly because the King had other priorities, and partly because Albuquerque died at sixty-two in the roadstead off Goa before he could begin. He was replaced before he died; his replacement was already in the harbor when his ship arrived. He died knowing this and knowing that the letter to the King he was writing — explaining why the replacement was wrong and the empire needed continuity — would arrive after he was already gone.

He asked to be buried in Goa. He was buried in Goa. Goa was his architecture; he intended to remain in it. The Portuguese stayed in Goa until 1961. He was right about the city's durability even if he was wrong about the diversion of the Nile.

## The Casado — A Composite Figure

The casado was not one person. He was a category that contained thousands of people across the empire's entire span, each living a version of the same structural position.

He had left Portugal in his twenties, sailing the Carreira da Índia as a soldier or a sailor or a trader with a license from the Crown. He had arrived at a feitoria — Goa, Malacca, Hormuz, Mozambique, Macau — and found that the feitoria's functioning depended on his staying rather than returning on the next convoy. The Crown offered inducements: a land grant, livestock, farming equipment, a government stipend. He married a local woman, or had children with a local woman outside marriage, and the children were baptized and given Portuguese names and attended mass at the feitoria's church and learned to read and write in Portuguese and also learned their mother's language and their mother's community's knowledge of local markets and local politics.

By the second generation, the casado family at Goa had cousins who were local Hindu merchants and cousins who were priests and cousins who were soldiers in the garrison and cousins who were trading, outside the Crown monopoly and inside it simultaneously. The Crown complained about the private trade. The casado continued trading. Both needed each other too much to stop.

Gilberto Freyre, four centuries later, would look at the casado and see evidence for what he called Lusotropicalism: the Portuguese capacity for racial and cultural mixing, the unique humanity of Portuguese colonialism, the multiracial societies that Portuguese settlement produced. The casado himself, if asked, would probably have had a more pragmatic account. He stayed because it was cheaper to stay. He married locally because there were not enough Portuguese women in Goa. He traded privately because the Crown monopoly did not provide enough to live on. He raised his children as Portuguese because Portugal held the fortress.

That his children also spoke Konkani, that his grandchildren also cooked with coconut and tamarind, that the music at the feitoria's festival mixed Portuguese melody with local rhythm — these were real. They are still real. Goa's Konkani-Portuguese culture is real, five hundred years later. The Lusotropicalism myth erred not in the observation but in what it concluded from it: that the mixing made the extraction less extractive, that the cultural hybrid made the legal hierarchy less hierarchical. It did not. The casado's children who were baptized had legal standing. The enslaved people working on São Tomé's sugar plantations were baptized too, and the baptism did not change what they were to the law.

The casado is the empire's most complex figure because he is the figure at which the empire's stated logic (Crusade, mission, monopoly) and the empire's operational reality (accommodation, trade, mixing) met and negotiated a daily peace that served neither entirely. He is the figure at which the Lusotropicalism myth was produced and at which it fails. He lived in the gap between what the empire said it was and what the empire actually required him to be.

## Fernão Mendes Pinto (c. 1509–1583)

He went and came back. That was the empire's basic plot, and Pinto lived it more fully than most. Twenty-one years in the Estado da Índia: five shipwrecks, three enslaved, twice ransomed. Japan, China, Malacca, India, Ethiopia, Arabia. He served briefly as a Jesuit novice under Francisco Xavier, who was the Jesuits' greatest missionary in Asia and who, Pinto felt, was doing something genuinely important that was being systematically undermined by the Portuguese merchants who followed the missionaries to trade in the countries the missionaries had opened.

He came home in 1558 with money, a wife, a small farm, and twenty-one years of accumulated dissonance. He spent the rest of his life writing it out.

Pinto was writing retrospectively; his accounts of China are demonstrably embellished, his dates do not always align with other records, and he was known during his life as someone who stretched a story. The Portuguese proverb "Fernão, mentes?" — "Fernão, are you lying?" — was a pun on his name that circulated during his lifetime. He acknowledged that his accounts were hard to believe. He wrote them anyway.

What makes *Peregrinação* essential for the Golden Century is not its reliability as a factual record. It is its reliability as a record of what the empire felt like from inside, from someone who was not a hero and not a saint and not an administrator, but an ordinary man of limited resources and considerable resilience who spent twenty-one years watching what the Estado da Índia actually did and returned unable to pretend it was what *Os Lusíadas* said it was.

He is not a critic in the modern sense. He is an observer who cannot stop being disturbed by what he observed. The empire's violence appears in his account not as editorial condemnation but as the texture of daily life in the Estado da Índia: the torture of prisoners, the slave trade, the corruption, the missionaries who baptized thousands and then watched them return to their previous lives. He does not conclude that the empire is evil. He concludes that the empire is the empire, and that a man who spent twenty-one years inside it is not entirely the same man as the one who sailed from Lisbon in 1537.

Saudade in its full form: he went, and he came back, and neither the going nor the coming back had resolved the ache of being somewhere else. He died in 1583 on his farm near Lisbon, five years after completing his manuscript, before it was published. He did not see the book. He had written it anyway, which is the act of a man who has made his peace with the possibility that what he says will not be heard until after he is gone.

## Luís de Camões (c. 1524–1580)

He lost his right eye at the siege of Ceuta in 1546. He was imprisoned in Lisbon in 1552. He sailed to India in 1553 and spent seventeen years in the Estado da Índia: Goa, Macau, the Mekong Delta, the Cape of Good Hope, and back. He was shipwrecked at the Mekong's mouth and swam to shore with the manuscript of *Os Lusíadas* held above the water in one hand. He arrived in Lisbon in 1570 with the manuscript and very little else. He published the epic in 1572. He died in 1580, the year the Portuguese crown passed to Philip II of Spain, having reportedly said that he was dying with his country.

His epic was the empire telling its own story at the highest register it could reach. Homer and Virgil were his models; Vasco da Gama was his Achilles and his Aeneas; the Portuguese sea was his Troy and his Mediterranean. The pagan gods of antiquity argued over Portugal's destiny in the heavens while the Portuguese pilots argued over navigation in the ships below. The combination should not have worked. It worked.

That it worked tells you something about what the empire believed it was. It believed it was continuous with the classical world's heroic tradition, that the Portuguese sailors were the inheritors of Odysseus's curiosity and Aeneas's mission. The cross on the sail was the same cross that Aeneas would have carried, translated from Rome's civic religion to Rome's eventual faith. The empire saw itself in the mirror of classical epic and found the reflection adequate.

What the mirror showed and what the mirror omitted are equally informative. The people at the far end of the route — the Zamorin, the Malaccan sultan, the Gujarati merchants, the Japanese lords who would eventually expel the Jesuits — appear in *Os Lusíadas* as they appeared in the empire's self-understanding: as the world that the mission was intended for, not as protagonists of their own stories. The epic is the empire's story. It was never intended to be anyone else's.

Camões is buried in Lisbon, in the Jerónimos Monastery at Belém, the church built from the profits of the spice trade. He was interred there in 1880, in the three-hundredth anniversary year of his death, in a ceremony organized by the Portuguese state that was then administering its African empire and finding *Os Lusíadas* useful as a national text. He lies across from Vasco da Gama, who was reinterred in the same monastery in the same ceremony. The two men who made the empire — one who sailed it, one who sang it — were placed opposite each other in the church that the empire built, and they remain there.

*Note on sources and reliability:* The figures in this section draw on C.R. Boxer's *The Portuguese Seaborne Empire*, A.J.R. Russell-Wood's *The Portuguese Empire*, and the primary accounts of Gaspar Correia, João de Barros, and Fernão Mendes Pinto. Pinto's *Peregrinação* is used as a first-person witness to the empire's texture rather than as a factual record; its embellishments are acknowledged. The "casado" composite figure is drawn from Boxer's social history and Russell-Wood's structural analysis; no single casado is named because no single casado's full account survives.
