# The High Tang

## I. The Failed Candidate

Du Fu arrived in Chang'an sometime around the twenty-third year of the Kaiyuan reign (735 CE), and the city was larger than anything he had prepared himself for.

He came from the east, through the Chunming Gate, where the road from Luoyang entered the capital along the central avenue that ran straight as a bowstring from the eastern wall to the western. The avenue was wide enough for twelve carriages abreast. On either side, the walls of the wards rose — rammed earth faced with plaster, each ward a city within the city, gated and closed at the drumbeat of curfew. The air had the particular dryness of the Wei River plain in autumn, dust suspended in gold light, the northwest wind carrying the first cold off the Qinling Mountains. He could not see over the ward walls. He could not see the end of the street. Chang'an held perhaps a million people within its walls, and another million in the suburbs and satellite towns that clung to it, and all of them lived in the gridded order that the Sui had laid down and the Tang had filled with the noise and mess of a civilization that believed itself the center of everything under heaven.

Du Fu was twenty-three. He had failed the jinshi examination that spring. The keju system's promise was absolute: any man who could master the classics, compose regulated verse in the approved forms, and write an essay that demonstrated both erudition and original thought could rise to serve the Son of Heaven regardless of birth. Two hundred thousand candidates sat for the various examinations each year. Fewer than thirty passed the jinshi, the "presented scholar" degree that opened the highest doors. Du Fu had written his poems, composed his essays, and he had not been among the thirty. He would try again. He would try again for the next decade, and fail each time, and the failure would feed his poetry like rain feeds a river in flood.

But that autumn he was not yet the poet of suffering. He was young, and he was in Chang'an, and the city was astonishing.

The West Market opened at midday, when the drum in the market tower sounded and the gates swung wide. Du Fu would have smelled it before he saw it: sesame oil frying in the Hu restaurants where Sogdian cooks made nang flatbreads stuffed with lamb and spices that had no Chinese names, because the spices came from Persia and the cooks came from Samarkand and the recipes belonged to a world that stretched from Chang'an to Baghdad along roads the Tang kept open. In the market, a Persian could sell glass. A Silla Korean could sell ginseng. An Arab could sell frankincense. A Uyghur could sell horses. They traded in Tang copper cash, the kaiyuan tongbao, stamped with the same four characters in every city from Guangzhou to the Tarim Basin. The mint was the common language of a commercial world whose reach exceeded any army's.

There were Nestorian Christians in Chang'an, their liturgy conducted in Syriac within the Daqin monastery while the monks wore Tang robes and presented themselves to the court as bearers of the "Luminous Religion." There were Zoroastrians tending their sacred fires, the crackle and smoke of the eternal flame rising three wards from the imperial palace. There were Manicheans and Jews and practitioners of every Buddhist school from Theravada to Tantric. The court did not merely tolerate them. It debated with them, gave them land for temples and monasteries, invited their scholars to translate texts. Buddhist bells sounded at dawn across a dozen monasteries. The call of the muezzin had not yet reached Chang'an, but Islam was known, and Arab envoys came and were received. The court was so certain of its position that foreign wisdom was simply additional material, absorbed without threat.


## II. The Roots of Confidence

The confidence had roots.

The equal-field system, juntianzhi, distributed agricultural land to adult farmers in measured allotments: eighty mu of personal-share land, twenty mu of permanent land, per household head. The farmers owed grain tax and corvee labor in return. The system did not operate perfectly (aristocratic estates bent its rules, and enforcement varied by province), but in its working form it accomplished something specific: it generated tax revenue without requiring a large professional army or an extensive bureaucracy, because the farmers who worked the land also served as the militia. The fubing system took those same farming households and organized them into rotating military service: a farmer for most of the year, a soldier when called, equipped at his own expense. The twin systems meant that the Tang state could field large armies and collect reliable revenue without the costs that broke other empires.

The Grand Canal carried southern rice north. It had been built by the Sui at enormous human cost. Hundreds of thousands of conscripted workers, many of whom did not survive the construction, dug the channels. The Tang inherited it as finished infrastructure, the way an heir inherits a house and forgets what it cost to build. The canal moved grain from the Yangtze delta to the northern plain, feeding Chang'an and Luoyang and the frontier armies. Without it, the capital would have starved. With it, the Tang could concentrate a million people in a city that the local agriculture could not have supported.

And the Silk Road fed the other appetite. Silk, porcelain, tea, and paper traveled west from Chang'an through the Hexi Corridor, through Dunhuang, through the oasis cities of the Tarim Basin, to Samarkand and beyond. Gold, silver, glass, horses, gems, spices, and people traveled east. The Tang maintained garrison-towns along the routes and extracted duties from the caravans, and the revenue funded the court's splendor. The Sogdian merchants who sold their flatbreads in the West Market were the human face of a trade system that connected the Tang to the Abbasid Caliphate, and both empires profited.

But the foundations were shifting even during the Kaiyuan's early years. The equal-field allotments were being absorbed into private estates as powerful families accumulated land that the system was supposed to redistribute. Fewer farmers held allotments; fewer farmers served in the fubing. The frontier armies, which had once been staffed by rotating farmer-soldiers, were now professional forces under career commanders, the jiedushi, "regional military commissioners," who recruited their own troops, including Turks, Sogdians, Khitans, and other non-Chinese soldiers who fought for pay rather than obligation. The jiedushi system was efficient. It was also, by design, independent. A frontier commander who recruited his own men, paid them from local revenues, and operated thousands of li from the capital was loyal because he chose to be, not because the system compelled it.

No one in Chang'an in Kaiyuan 23 was worried about this. The jiedushi held the borders. The Silk Road was open. The grain arrived. The examinations continued. The system was working, and working systems do not advertise their vulnerabilities.


## III. The Two Poets

In the autumn of the third year of the Tianbao reign (744 CE), Du Fu met Li Bai in Luoyang.

Du Fu was thirty-two and still had not passed the jinshi. He had traveled (south to the Yangtze, east to the coast, north to the frontier) and he had written poems that no one of importance had read. Li Bai was forty-three, already the most famous poet in the empire, a man who had been summoned to the Hanlin Academy by Xuanzong himself and then dismissed from court, some said for drunkenness, others for offending the eunuch Gao Lishi by asking him to pull off the poet's boots. Li Bai did not deny any version of the story. He preferred the ones where he was drunk.

They drank wine. They wrote poems. Du Fu, in a poem written later, called their time together the happiest of his life. "In the days when I drank with Li Bai," he wrote, "we would go out to find the wine-shop, take off our coats, and pour out what was in our hearts." The two men could not have been more different in temperament and yet they recognized each other immediately: two minds operating at the highest level of the art that defined their civilization.

Li Bai wrote as if the world were a guest house and he were passing through. His poems are spontaneous, luminous, drunk on moonlight and wine and the Daoist promise that the immortals were real and the mountains were doors to another world. "I raise my cup to invite the moon," he wrote. "With my shadow, we are three." In "Bring the Wine" he declared: "Heaven gave me talents that must have their use; a thousand gold pieces scattered and gone will all come back again." He did not care about the examination system. He did not care about official rank. He wrote as if the Tang's order were irrelevant, and the freedom in his voice was so complete that even the emperor recognized it as something the court could not produce and could not quite control.

Du Fu was the opposite. He cared about everything Li Bai dismissed. The examination system, the duty of the official, the suffering of ordinary people, the moral weight of governance. These were not just Du Fu's subjects; they were his obligations. He believed, with the Confucian tradition that formed him, that a poet who saw suffering and did not write about it was complicit. He had not yet seen the worst suffering. That was coming. But even in the Kaiyuan years, before the rebellion, Du Fu wrote about conscription: "The army carts rumble and creak / the war horses whinny and neigh / the conscripts, each with a bow and arrows at their waist / their fathers and mothers, wives and children, run to see them off / the dust rises so thick you cannot see the bridge at Xianyang." The poem is called "Ballad of the Army Carts," and it was written before An Lushan marched. The conscription was already real. The grief was already present. Du Fu saw it because he was looking.

Wang Wei was the third voice. A Buddhist, a landscape painter, a court official who survived the rebellion by being captured rather than by fleeing, Wang Wei wrote poems of such stillness that they seem to have emptied themselves of everything except what is. "Empty mountain, no one in sight / only the sound of someone talking far away / returning light enters the deep forest / and shines again on green moss." The poem sees and hears. It does not comment. It does not grieve. It does not celebrate. It is present, and its presence is the achievement.

Three poets, three ways of being in the Tang world: Li Bai's Daoist freedom, Du Fu's Confucian witness, Wang Wei's Buddhist stillness. The candidates who sat for the keju knew what the shi form could hold. They had memorized the masters of the early Tang and the Six Dynasties canon before they wrote a line of their own, and among them, without knowing it, walked the poets whose names future generations would memorize in turn.

The candidates who gathered in Chang'an each spring for the examinations understood this. They arrived from every province, carrying their best poems in manuscript rolls, hoping for the attention of a patron who might recommend them before the tests began. The examination halls were open courtyards where candidates sat on mats, brushes and ink before them, writing from dawn until the light failed. The test for the jinshi required composition of regulated verse, lushi, in strict tonal patterns, each line eight syllables, the middle couplets in parallel structure, the whole poem a demonstration that the candidate could think within constraints and make the constraints beautiful. A man who could do this, the system assumed, could govern. The assumption linked aesthetic judgment to political competence in a way that no other civilization attempted at this scale, and the link held for three centuries.


## IV. The Court of the Brilliant Emperor

In the Daming Palace, on Chang'an's northern edge, Xuanzong held court.

The palace complex covered more than three square li. Its audience halls were roofed in glazed tiles that caught the sun, their columns lacquered vermilion, the floors polished stone. The Hanyuan Hall, where the emperor received officials on state occasions, stood on a raised platform approached by dragon-carved ramps, and from its terrace you could see the city's grid stretching south to the walls. Xuanzong had come to power through violence: a coup against the Empress Wei's faction in 710, a second move against his aunt, the Princess Taiping, in 713, the year he adopted the Kaiyuan reign name. He was decisive, intelligent, and in the early Kaiyuan years he governed with real attention. He chose competent ministers, Yao Chong and Song Jing, and he listened to them.

He was also a musician. He composed, he played the jie gu drum with a precision that his courtiers praised and his musicians genuinely admired. He established the Pear Garden conservatory in a walled garden within the palace complex, where three hundred of the court's finest performers trained under his personal direction. The sound of rehearsal carried across the palace grounds in the afternoons: pipa strings, the wooden clack of clappers keeping time, voices running through the modes of the yanyue court music that blended Chinese melodies with Central Asian rhythms brought east along the Silk Road. It was in the Pear Garden that Yang Yuhuan first danced for him.

She was twenty-six, the former consort of his son Li Mao, when the emperor took her into his own household. The sources record this transfer carefully, because the impropriety was recognized even at the time. She was beautiful — the histories say this, the poems say this, everyone who wrote about her said this, and then they paused, because beauty in a court is never just beauty. Yang Yuhuan became Yang Guifei, "Precious Consort," the highest rank below empress, and the court rearranged itself around her.

She danced the Nishang Yuyi, the "Rainbow Skirt and Feathered Garment," to music that Xuanzong himself had composed, and the performance was not entertainment. It was a political statement: the emperor's artistry, the consort's grace, the court's capacity to produce something this exquisite. The fabric of her costume, layered silk in gradations of color that moved like water when she turned, was the Tang at its most confident, art and power fused so completely that separating them would miss the point.

Her family rose with her. Yang Guozhong, her cousin, became chancellor. The Yang clan accumulated offices, estates, and influence that provoked resentment among the older aristocratic families and among military commanders who saw civilians enriching themselves while the frontier armies went underequipped. Yang Guifei herself held no formal political power. She did not need to. Her proximity to the emperor was the power, and everyone at court knew it, and the knowledge shaped every decision made in her presence.

Xuanzong, as the Tianbao years advanced (742–756), withdrew from the daily labor of governance. He spent more time in the hot springs palace at Huaqing, outside the capital, where he and Yang Guifei bathed in the warm water and listened to music while the chancellor managed the empire. He had ruled well for thirty years. He was tired. He was in love. The combination was dangerous, not because love is dangerous, but because an empire that depends on the attention of its sovereign cannot afford a sovereign who is looking elsewhere.

The man who would exploit this gap was already in place.

Outside the capital, the empire that paid for this beauty looked different. A farmer in Henan, working what remained of his equal-field allotment after the local magnate had absorbed the rest, owed the same grain tax his father had owed on a full allotment. His son, who under the old fubing system would have served a rotation in the local garrison, had been replaced by a professional soldier on the frontier, a Khitan cavalryman who had never seen the Wei River plain and owed nothing to the farmer's village. The farmer did not know the name An Lushan. He knew the tax collector, the price of grain, the fact that the canal boats still moved north carrying rice to feed a city he would never visit. The Tang's golden age, for the farmer, was the regularity of the seasons and the hope that the tax would not increase. For the conscript laborer repairing the Grand Canal's banks, it was the chance to survive the work rotation and return home. The peak looked different from below, but from below it was still stable, and stability was its own kind of wealth.


## V. The Rebellion

An Lushan was born on the frontier, the son of a Sogdian father and a Turkic mother, raised in the borderlands where Chinese authority met the steppe. He spoke six languages. He was enormously fat — the histories note his belly with a fascination that borders on obsession, and he used his bulk as theater, performing a Sogdian whirling dance before the court that made Xuanzong laugh and Yang Guifei call him her "adopted son." The intimacy was deliberate. An Lushan cultivated the court's affection the way a merchant cultivates a customer, and the court, charmed, gave him what he asked for.

What he asked for was military command. By the Tianbao period, An Lushan held the jiedushi commissions of Fanyang, Pinglu, and Hedong, three of the ten frontier military commands, controlling perhaps 180,000 soldiers along the northeastern border. His troops were loyal to him personally. He paid them from local revenues, equipped them with weapons manufactured in his own workshops, and recruited Khitan, Xi, and Turkic fighters who had no attachment to the Tang court and every attachment to their commander. Chancellor Yang Guozhong saw the danger and warned Xuanzong. The emperor did not act. An Lushan was his favorite general. The border was quiet. The man danced for him.

In the eleventh month of the fourteenth year of the Tianbao reign (December 755 CE), An Lushan marched south from Fanyang with 150,000 men.

The Tang garrisons between the northeast frontier and the Yellow River were not prepared for a full-scale assault from one of their own commanders. An Lushan's army moved fast. Luoyang, the eastern capital, fell within weeks. An Lushan declared himself emperor of a new dynasty, the Yan, and sat on a throne in Luoyang while his armies continued west toward Chang'an.

Xuanzong received the news and did not believe it, then believed it and did not know what to do. He ordered Geshu Han, another frontier general, to hold the Tong Pass, the narrow defile that guarded the approach to Chang'an from the east. Geshu Han held it for months, but the court, panicking, ordered him to attack. He attacked, lost, and the pass fell. The road to Chang'an was open.

On the fifteenth day of the sixth month of the fifteenth year of the Tianbao reign (July 756 CE), the emperor fled. Before dawn, in darkness, with a small escort of palace guards and whatever family members could be roused and mounted, the imperial party left Chang'an through the Yanxing Gate on the city's western side. They rode without drums, without banners, without the ceremonial escort that marked an emperor's movement. Behind them the city woke to find the throne empty. Soldiers broke into the imperial storehouses and fought over silk and grain. Fires lit the eastern wards. Smoke rose over the grid of streets that the Sui had drawn for permanence. The million people who could not flee closed their ward gates and waited for what was coming.

At the Mawei post-station, fifty miles southwest of the capital, the imperial escort stopped. The soldiers were hungry, exhausted, and furious. They killed Yang Guozhong first. Then they surrounded the emperor and demanded Yang Guifei. The historical accounts diverge on what was said. What they agree on is the outcome: Xuanzong consented. The eunuch Gao Lishi carried out the execution. Yang Guifei was strangled with a silk cord behind the Buddhist chapel at the post-station. She was thirty-eight years old.

Xuanzong continued to Sichuan. His son, Li Heng, broke away from the imperial party and rode north to Lingwu, where he declared himself Emperor Suzong and began organizing the reconquest. The Tang would survive. Chang'an would be retaken, with Uyghur cavalry doing much of the fighting, in the autumn of 757. The rebellion would grind on until 763, costing, by the census figures, thirty-six million lives, though whether that number reflects actual deaths or the collapse of the census system itself is a question scholars still argue.

Du Fu was in Chang'an when the rebels occupied it. He had been trying, once again, to secure a government appointment. Instead he found himself a captive in a fallen city. In the spring of 757, from the occupied capital, he wrote "Spring Prospect":

The nation shattered, mountains and rivers remain.
In the city, spring — grass and trees grow deep.
Moved by the times, flowers draw tears.
Hating separation, birds startle the heart.
The beacon fires have burned for three months.
A letter from home is worth ten thousand gold.
I scratch my white head — the hair grows thinner.
Soon it will not hold a hairpin at all.

The poem is eight lines long. It contains the fall of a world. The mountains and rivers remain because they are not human; they do not shatter. Everything human has shattered. The flowers and birds are still there, doing what flowers and birds do in spring, and the poet cannot look at them without weeping because their constancy measures the depth of what has been lost. The beacon fires, the signal fires that warned of invasion, have been burning for three months. He has heard nothing from his family. His hair is falling out from grief.

Eight lines. The fall of a world.


## VI. What the River Carries

The Tang that survived the rebellion was a different state. The jiedushi who had helped suppress An Lushan's forces were rewarded with permanent regional authority they would never relinquish. The equal-field system, already failing, was formally abandoned. The court's control shrank to the capital region and the south while military governors ran their own territories as semi-independent domains. The Silk Road, disrupted by the rebellion and by the expansion of the Tibetan Empire into the Hexi Corridor, carried less and less. Chang'an never recovered its Kaiyuan population. The Sogdian merchants thinned. The foreign temples emptied.

Du Fu spent his remaining years in exile — in Qinzhou, in Chengdu, on a boat drifting down the Yangtze. He wrote constantly. The poems from these years are not the poems of a young man in the West Market. They are the poems of a man who has seen the world he loved pulled apart, who keeps writing because writing is what he knows how to do, because the Confucian tradition that formed him says you do not stop witnessing simply because the witness is unbearable.

In Chengdu he wrote about his thatched cottage, about the wind tearing off his roof, about the rain soaking his bedding, about the cold. "The autumn wind howls; it tears three layers of thatch from my roof." The poem continues: he imagines a great mansion that could shelter all the poor scholars of the world, unmoved by wind or rain, and he says he would gladly let his own hut be destroyed if such a building could stand. The poem is about a roof. It is about the duty of the state to shelter its people. It is about a man lying in the rain, thinking about everyone else who is lying in the rain, and finding that thought more urgent than his own discomfort.

Li Bai died in 762, the year before the rebellion officially ended. The stories say he drowned trying to embrace the moon's reflection in the Yangtze, drunk, reaching for something beautiful and impossible. The story is almost certainly apocryphal. He probably died of illness, old and diminished, in the household of a relative. But the Tang remembered the version with the moon, because that was the Li Bai they needed to remember — not the old man coughing in someone's spare room, but the poet reaching for the moon, the same moon he had raised his cup to, the same moon that hung over Chang'an when the city still believed in itself.

Du Fu died in 770, on a boat on the Xiang River. He was fifty-eight. He had never held a significant government position. He had never passed the jinshi. He had written approximately 1,500 poems, of which perhaps 1,400 survive, more than from any other Tang poet, and they constitute the most complete record of what it was to live through the Tang's destruction. Thirteen centuries later, Chinese schoolchildren memorize his lines. "Spring Prospect" is taught in every classroom in China. The mountains and rivers remain.

The Tang dynasty itself continued for another 137 years after the rebellion, but what continued was the frame, not the substance. The High Tang, the Kaiyuan peak, the cosmopolitan capital, the poetry, the court, became memory, and memory became the standard by which later dynasties measured themselves and found themselves wanting. Song historians would write that they surpassed the Tang in wealth, Ming historians that they surpassed it in reach, Qing historians that they surpassed it in duration. But when the Chinese imagined the civilization at its most fully realized, the city open to the world, the poetry equal to experience, the government at least attempting to match the ideal of service that the keju promised, they imagined the Tang.

Not the Tang that was. The Tang they carried forward in Du Fu's lines and Li Bai's moonlight and the memory of a city where a million people lived in the grid that the Sui had drawn and the Tang had filled with everything the Silk Road could carry.

On the Wei River plain, the foundations of the wards are still visible from the air, rectangles in the wheat fields, the ghost of a grid. The Daming Palace is an archaeological park. Tourists walk where Xuanzong held court. The Big Wild Goose Pagoda still stands, its brick darkened by thirteen centuries of weather. A vendor sells calligraphy outside its gate. Du Fu's lines, brushed on paper, the characters the same ones Du Fu would have written.

The mountains and rivers remain. The poem remains. The city that Du Fu walked into as a young man who had failed his examination is underneath the modern city, layer upon layer, the rammed earth dissolving slowly into the soil of the Wei River plain, carrying its dust into the river, carrying the river to the sea.
