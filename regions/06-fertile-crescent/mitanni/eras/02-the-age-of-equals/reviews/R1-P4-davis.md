## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of The Age of Equals (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening treats the Mitanni diplomatic system with the intellectual seriousness it deserves. The gift-exchange protocol is not described as quaint or archaic; it is presented as a sophisticated mechanism of statecraft. This is how these chapters should work.

**What works:**
- The horse-training tradition is presented as a genuine knowledge system, not as a colorful detail. The mention of the Kikkuli manual's Indo-Aryan technical vocabulary situates it within a specialist tradition of transmitted expertise. This is the right approach.
- The chariot is presented not just as a weapon but as the basis of a social and political order. The connection between military technology, social status, and diplomatic standing is drawn with clarity.
- The gift-exchange logic is rendered at full intellectual depth. The sentence about gold that is "not a price" captures the complexity of a system where material value and symbolic value are inseparable. This is an economic logic as rigorous as any; it simply operates on different axioms than market exchange.

**What doesn't:**
- The Hurrian base of Mitanni society is mentioned only in passing (the geometric patterns, the language note). The opening rightly centers the diplomatic world of the *maryannu* elite, but the Hurrian majority was not simply a passive substrate. Their religious traditions (Shaushka, the Kumarbi cycle), their music (the Hurrian hymn tablets from Ugarit), their artistic sensibility shaped the kingdom as much as the chariot aristocracy shaped its military power. A brief acknowledgment that the diplomatic world rests on a deeper Hurrian cultural world would prevent the opening from inadvertently reducing Mitanni to its ruling class.
- The knowledge dimension could go deeper. The Kikkuli manual is not just a training regimen; it is evidence of a sophisticated understanding of equine physiology, interval training, and nutritional science. Naming what the trainers *knew* (not just what they *did*) would elevate the intellectual register.

**The question I'd push on:**
Is the Mitanni world being presented as its *full* cultural reality, or only as its diplomatic and military elite? The chariot aristocracy is the most visible layer, but the Hurrian world beneath it is a complete civilization in its own right. The chapter will need both.
