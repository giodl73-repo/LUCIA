## The Body in the Scene

I have traveled to Africa many times. I have stood in rooms where history was being made and noticed that what I remembered afterward was not the argument but the smell of the room — the sweat and the oil lamp and the particular quality of the light. History is made by people with bodies in places with weather. This opening knows that. But it does not yet know it fully enough.

The letter is the opening's anchor, and it is a good one. But a letter is a document. I need to know what it felt like to write it. Where was Afonso sitting? What was the light like? What did the cathedral smell like in the morning — stone and candle wax and the particular dust of the Kongo plateau? What was the quality of the air in Mbanza Kongo in 1526? The opening gives me the argument and the architecture. I need the body.

## What the Sensory Field Has and Has Not

**Present:**
- The cathedral as a built fact
- Mpinda as a place where ships are present
- The archive in Lisbon as a distant destination

**Absent:**
- The smell of the writing room
- The sound of Mbanza Kongo — the city sounds, the market sounds, the cathedral bells if there were any
- The feel of the letter itself — quill on parchment, the resistance of the material
- The bodies of the enslaved people moving through Mpinda — not as statistics, but as physical presences
- The Kongo plateau in the dry season or wet season — what does the air smell like, what does the ground feel like underfoot
- The mpu as a physical object — its weight, its texture, the raffia's smell when it is warm from a head

## The Ships at Mpinda

The opening mentions "the ships at Mpinda" and "their cargo" — and then moves on. This is the place where the sensory field should break open. If there is any moment where I need to feel the weight of what is happening, it is here. The ships at Mpinda are not an abstraction. They are wooden vessels smelling of salt water and bilge and something worse. The people moving through that port are not "cargo" in any neutral sense. The chapter should let the reader stand on the dock for one sentence — feel the heat, hear the sounds, smell what is present — without editorializing. Let the senses carry the moral weight. The reader will understand.

## The Kongo Plateau

Africa is not a generic place. The Kongo kingdom sat on a plateau above the forest — a savanna landscape of red laterite soil, of baobabs and grass that goes golden in the dry season, of a particular quality of light that is different from the forest below and different again from the coast. Mbanza Kongo was on this plateau. Afonso's cathedral was on this plateau. The letter was written on this plateau. I need to be on the plateau with him — not to be distracted from the argument, but to know that the argument was made by a body in a place with a particular sky above it.

## What Works

The opening's restraint is mostly correct. This is not a chapter that should be overwhelmed by sensory detail — the epistolary register requires a kind of formality that sensory excess would break. What I am asking for is not immersion in the manner of a tropical jungle chapter. I am asking for three or four specific sensory anchors that place the reader in the body of someone in Mbanza Kongo in 1526. Three details, precisely chosen, embedded in the argument rather than decorating it — that would be enough.

## The Question I'd Push On

**"What did the quill feel like in Afonso's hand when he wrote 'we cannot reckon how great the damage is'?"**

I mean this almost literally. The letter was written by a person with a hand. That hand was holding an instrument. The movement of writing — the physical act of forming Latin words in a space that smelled of Kongo, that looked out at the Kongo plateau — was a specific sensory event. The chapter should find one moment of that physical specificity and let it be the scene's ground. Not the whole scene, not a description of writing — just one moment where the reader is inside the body of the person who made the argument.
