# The Kingdom and the Republic's Birth

The senators carried the body through the Forum in silence.

Lucius Junius Brutus was dead, killed in single combat with Arruns Tarquinius, son of the king he had expelled, and the women of Roma mourned him for a year, the way they mourned a father. This detail is in Livy, who wrote it down two centuries after the Republic ended, working from traditions that were already old when he received them, shaping them into the moral architecture the Romani told themselves they lived by. Whether the mourning lasted exactly a year, whether the single combat happened as described, whether Brutus himself existed as Livy drew him, these are questions the sources cannot answer and the tradition does not ask. What matters is what the story meant to the Romani who carried it: that the man who freed them from the king died defending the freedom, and the city honored the death, and the honor was the foundation.

The Forum where they carried him was not yet the marble expanse Augustus would build. In the sixth century before the common era it was a drained marsh between hills, the ground still soft in places, the wooden and tufa buildings of a city that was not yet sure what it was becoming. The Capitoline hill rose to the northwest with the temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus under construction, or recently finished, its terracotta roof decorations made by Etruscan craftsmen from Veii whose skill the Romani hired because they did not yet possess it themselves. The Palatine rose to the south, its slopes dotted with the houses of the families who would become the patrician class, the families whose ancestors, they claimed, had been present at the founding. The smell of the place was river mud and woodsmoke and the faint sourness of the hides curing in the workshops near the Tiber, a river that flooded regularly and deposited the silt that made the low ground fertile and foul.

Roma sat on seven hills above a river crossing. That was the strategic fact. The Tiber was navigable from the sea to this point, and the island in the river, the Insula Tiberina, made the crossing possible. Salt from the coastal marshes at Ostia came up the road the Romani would call the Via Salaria, the Salt Road, and the salt trade was old, older than the city, older than the name. The hills gave defensible high ground in a landscape of malarial lowlands. The river gave water and transport and the fish the poor ate when grain was short. The crossing gave control of the route between Etruria to the north and Campania to the south, the two richest regions of the Italian peninsula, and the city that held the crossing held the tax on everything that moved between them.

The Romani told themselves a different story about why Roma existed. Not the salt road and the river crossing. Not the strategic geography. They said Romulus had drawn a furrow with a bronze plow around the Palatine hill and killed his brother Remus for crossing it, because the boundary of the city was sacred, and to cross it without permission was to violate the thing that made the city a city. They said Romulus had founded the Senate, had divided the people into curiae, had established the auspices by which the gods communicated their will through the flight of birds. They said Numa Pompilius, the second king, had given Roma its religious calendar and its priestly colleges and the temple of Janus whose doors stood open in war and closed in peace. They said Servius Tullius, the sixth king, had built the city wall and organized the citizenry into the census classes that determined military obligation and voting weight, binding property to duty in a knot the Republic would never untie.

These stories were the constitution. Not a written document. A set of precedents carried in memory, debated in the Senate, cited in speeches, revised by each generation that needed them to justify what it was doing. The mos maiorum, the way of the ancestors, was Roma's deepest law, deeper than the Twelve Tables that would later be inscribed on bronze and displayed in the Forum, deeper than any magistrate's edict or senatorial decree. The ancestors had done it this way. Therefore this was how it was done. The argument was circular, and the circularity was the point. A people who derived their law from their ancestors could not change that law without claiming the ancestors had intended the change, and this fiction of continuity within change was the Republic's most powerful political technology. Every revolution was a restoration. Every innovation was a return to the founders' intent. The Romani who expelled the kings did not say they were creating something new. They said they were recovering what Romulus had established and the kings had corrupted.

Brutus, if Livy's tradition holds, called the citizens together and made them swear: no more kings. Never again would one man hold permanent power over Roma. The oath did not prevent one-man rule forever. It prevented it for five centuries, which is long enough to have meant something. And the machinery the Romani built to keep the oath, the two consuls who checked each other, the Senate whose authority rested on collective deliberation, the assemblies where citizens voted in groups weighted by property and military service, the annual rotation of magistracies that ensured no man held power long enough to forget that the power belonged to the res publica, the public thing, not to him, this machinery was the most influential political engineering of the ancient Mediterranean, and its influence outlasted the Republic that built it.

The body of Brutus passed through the Forum. The women wept. The senators walked in their togas, the woolen garment that was Roma's uniform and its symbol, the heavy undyed wool draped in folds that took practice to manage and that marked the wearer as a citizen the way the cumbi cloth marked an Inca lord, because in both cases the fabric was the message. The toga said: I am a Roman citizen. I have duties. I fulfill them. The folds concealed and revealed the body beneath in a way that required dignity to carry, and dignity, dignitas, was not a feeling. It was a public fact, earned by service, defended by action, diminished by any failure of the obligations the res publica imposed.

The Republic was born in the space between the oath and the machinery. The oath said: never again a king. The machinery said: here is how. Between the two, in that narrow space, the Romani would govern themselves for five hundred years, and the space would hold until the machinery itself became the weapon that tore it apart.
