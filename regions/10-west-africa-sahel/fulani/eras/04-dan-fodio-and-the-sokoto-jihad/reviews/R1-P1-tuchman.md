## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of "Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad" opening

**Overall:** This is one of the best openings I have reviewed. It understands something that most historical writing about revolutions does not: that the most dangerous moment is not the battle but the letter. The flag on the shelf is a superb closing image, and the mechanism of distributed scholarly revolution is made thrillingly visible. I have two structural concerns, but they are refinements, not corrections.

**What works:**

- "The letter arrived in Katsina before the rains." Seven words, and the reader is placed. Something is arriving. Something is about to change. The seasonal marker ("before the rains") grounds the moment in the Sahel's own calendar without explanation. This is a first line that earns the second line.

- The mechanism is the star of this piece: "A letter. A flag. A scholar in a distant city who had been reading the same texts, attending the same arguments, arriving at the same conclusions for years." This is how you make a reader understand a revolution's infrastructure in three sentences. The author has found the story inside the system, not beside it. I could not have done it better.

- "And inside the green cloth bundle was a second flag." A single-sentence paragraph that changes everything. The reader realizes the letter is not just information; it is a commission. The bundle makes it physical. The flag makes it irreversible. This is pacing of the highest order.

- The closing two lines are perfect: "He folded the flag and placed it on the shelf beside his books. Not yet. But soon." The flag is beside the books. The revolution is on the same shelf as the scholarship. That is the entire story in an image. And "Not yet. But soon" creates a forward pull that the reader cannot resist. I would keep reading.

- The passage listing the flag destinations works because each city carries one identifying detail: "the dye pits where the indigo cloth was beaten and folded" for Kano, "a scholar whose students numbered in the hundreds" for Zaria. These are not decorations; they are the network made visible.

**What doesn't:**

- The middle section where the author describes the flag distribution across multiple cities loses its scene. We leave Umaru Dallaji's compound and enter a kind of aerial survey. The prose remains strong, but the reader's feet leave the ground. The passage beginning "They did not all rise at once" is telling us about the jihad's coordination rather than showing us any single moment of it. I would anchor at least one more flag-holder in a specific scene: the scholar in Kano receiving the flag at the dye pits, or the moment in Zaria when Musa's students see the green cloth for the first time. One scene is worth a paragraph of summary.

- The passage on the *Kitab al-Farq* and the scholarly tradition is necessary but currently reads as exposition dropped into the middle of a scene. "He had been teaching for more than thirty years, and he had written more than a hundred treatises" is information the reader needs, but it arrives as a biographical insert rather than as something Umaru Dallaji is thinking or holding in his hands. Could the treatise be physically present in the compound? Could Umaru be looking at his own copy of the *Kitab al-Farq* on the shelf where the flag now rests?

**The question I'd push on:**

Where is the complication? This opening establishes the mechanism beautifully, but the mechanism works too smoothly in the telling. The flags go out, the scholars receive them, the consensus holds. What about the scholar who hesitated? The flag-holder who had doubts? The community where the local Fulani were divided? The jihad was not unanimous. Some Fulani fought on the Hausa side. Some scholars disagreed with the Shehu's timing. A single moment of doubt, one flag-holder who turned the green cloth over in his hands and was not sure, would make the consensus that followed feel earned rather than inevitable. Right now, the revolution arrives as a completed argument. A crack in the certainty would make it a story.
