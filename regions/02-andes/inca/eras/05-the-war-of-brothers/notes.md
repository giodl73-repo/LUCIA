# Production Notes — The War of Brothers (Era 5)

## Structural Engine

**The reciprocal system destroying itself.** The structural engine is the civil war as the mit'a consuming its own reserves. Two centers, two claims on the mit'a, two armies supplied by the same storehouses — the system built for abundance through redistribution now destroys its reserves through war. When Pizarro arrives, he finds a system that has exhausted itself from the inside.

**Book-level arc completion:** This chapter completes the five-era arc:
- Era 1: the seeds (ayllu, quipu, vertical archipelago)
- Era 2: the scaling (Pachacuti builds the system)
- Era 3: the limits (the system reaches its edges)
- Era 4: the peak and first crack (epidemic, succession crisis)
- Era 5: the self-destruction (civil war) and the encounter (Cajamarca)

The three threads at their terminal state:
1. **Mit'a** — exhausted by civil war; both sides have drawn the mit'a to its limit for military purposes; the reserves are gone
2. **Vertical archipelago** — disrupted by epidemic, civil war, and the displacement of populations; the ecological connections strained
3. **Quipu** — the quipucamayoc record the end with the same precision they recorded the peak; the knots cannot explain what has happened but they can count the cost

## Key Rules for This Chapter

- **Atahualpa's perspective, not Pizarro's.** The encounter at Cajamarca is told from INSIDE the Inca world. Pizarro is a figure who enters the Inca story, not the other way around.
- **No Verdicts on the conquest.** Present what happened. Do not say "this was wrong" or "this was inevitable" or "this was tragic." Let the reader arrive at their own response.
- **No Verdicts on the civil war.** Neither brother was "right." Both were operating within a system that had no mechanism for the situation they faced.
- **The quipucamayoc close the book.** The final image should be the Inca's own record-keeping system, because that is what this book has been about: a non-writing empire whose memory was held in knots.

## Chapter Arc

1. **The Hot Springs** (opening material) — Atahualpa at Cajamarca; the civil war won; the messengers
2. **The Civil War** — Huascar and Atahualpa; the campaigns; the exhaustion of the mit'a
3. **The Broken Balance** — The qollqa emptied by war; the road carrying soldiers instead of tribute; the quipucamayoc recording the deficit
4. **Cajamarca** — The plaza; Atahualpa's procession; Valverde and the book; the ambush; the capture. All from inside the Inca world.
5. **After** — The ransom room; Atahualpa's execution; the system without a Sapa Inca; the panaqas, the ayllus, the quipucamayoc — what survived and what did not
6. **The Knots** — The closing: the quipucamayoc and the silence; what the knots hold; what the knots lost; the thread that was not writing and the empire that was not over

## Key Figures

- **Atahualpa** — The last effective Sapa Inca; the chapter's protagonist; told from inside
- **Huascar** — The rival; the brother; the other claimant; his defeat and imprisonment
- **Chalcuchima** and **Quisquis** — Atahualpa's generals; the men who won the civil war
- **Francisco Pizarro** — The outsider; enters the Inca story as the strangers from the coast; rendered from the Inca perspective
- **Fray Vicente de Valverde** — The priest at Cajamarca; the book offered; the moment of contact

## Research Anchors

- John Hemming, "The Conquest of the Incas" (1970) — definitive account of the conquest
- Betanzos, "Suma y Narracion" (1557) — the closest Inca-perspective account (Betanzos married Atahualpa's consort)
- Cieza de Leon (1553) — multiple perspectives on the civil war
- Nathan Wachtel, "The Vision of the Vanquished" (1977) — the conquest from the Andean perspective
- Guaman Poma de Ayala, "El Primer Nueva Coronica" (c. 1615) — illustrated Andean account
