## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of Hammurabi's Babylon Opening

**Overall:** The opening identifies the correct structural engine: the publication of law as the act that transforms kingship from power to legitimacy. The causal logic is sound and well-sequenced.

**What works:**
- The causal chain in the conquest paragraph is precise: canals feed fields, fields feed treasury, treasury feeds army, army is useless without canals. This circular logic is exactly how hydraulic states operate. The opening understands the material basis of Babylonian power without reducing it to economics.
- The distinction between prior law codes and Hammurabi's code is correctly placed. The argument is not that the law was new but that the publication was new. This is a genuinely historical insight delivered inside the narrative, not outside it.
- The theological argument of the prologue (Anu and Enlil naming Marduk, Marduk naming Hammurabi) is presented as the culture's own causal chain: divine mandate flowing downward through cosmic hierarchy to political authority to legal publication. This is asabiyyah of a specific kind: group cohesion through shared law.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening does not yet explain why the law needed to be public at this moment. Hammurabi had just unified a multi-city plain with diverse legal traditions (Sumerian, Akkadian, Amorite). The code's publicness was not an abstract philosophical choice; it was a political necessity. The chapter body should develop this: law as the instrument of unification, replacing the customary law of individual cities with a single published standard.
- The five-king balance of power is mentioned but not analyzed. What was the mechanism of this balance? Was it roughly equal canal systems? Trade routes? Military capacity? The chapter should identify the specific factor that kept five kingdoms in equilibrium for a generation and the specific event that broke it.

**The question I'd push on:**
What happens to the cultures whose customary law the stele replaced? The cities of the south had been governing themselves under Sumerian legal tradition for a thousand years. When Hammurabi published a uniform code in Akkadian, he was not merely recording existing practice. He was overwriting local custom with royal law. The opening gestures at this ("the king who could hold the waterways held the plain"), but the tension between local tradition and royal publication deserves more pressure.
