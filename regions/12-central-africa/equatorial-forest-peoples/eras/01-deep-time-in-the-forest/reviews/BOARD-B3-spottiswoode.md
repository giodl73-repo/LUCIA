## Accuracy of the Honeyguide Representation

The chapter's description of the communication system is accurate. The honeyguide gives a call, moves, gives another call from a new position, waits for the human response — this reflects the documented recruitment behavior. The human response being a specific learned call that the bird distinguishes from non-signaling sounds — this is documented. The bird waiting near the hive and going still — this is documented guiding behavior.

**Accuracy flag on the "cheaters" claim:** The chapter states "the honeyguide does not lead cheaters back to honey." This is an extrapolation from the documented mutualism that is plausible but not directly verified in the scientific literature. My 2016 study documented that the birds respond preferentially to the traditional honey-hunting calls over other sounds, and that they do not guide people who do not signal correctly, but the specific question of whether a bird that has previously been "cheated" (not given the honeycomb portion) will avoid that individual in the future — this has not been experimentally verified. The claim as stated implies a longer-term individual-recognition memory in the honeyguide that the evidence does not fully support.

**Recommendation for revision:** Change "The honeyguide does not lead cheaters back to honey" to something more precise: "The honeyguide leads partners who respond correctly and leave what is required. The relationship works because both sides show up as the other has learned to expect." This is accurate and makes the same narrative point without claiming a punishment-memory in the bird that has not been documented.

**Geographic scope note:** The chapter's figures.md correctly notes that the Spottiswoode et al. 2016 study was conducted in Mozambique and Kenya, not in the Ituri. The chapter itself does not flag this, which is the right editorial choice — the geographic breadth of the mutualism is established across multiple populations, and the specific Ituri instance is ethnographically attested through Turnbull. No revision needed in the chapter text; the figures.md disclosure is adequate.

## Assessment of the Relational Framing

The chapter's decision to treat the honeyguide as a figure — as a partner in a relationship, with its own knowledge and its own judgment — is scientifically defensible. The bird does have its own learned behavioral repertoire that is adapted to the human partnership. The characterization of the partnership as mutual (both parties benefit, both parties must behave correctly for the benefit to flow) is accurate.

**Endorsement:** The chapter is scientifically accurate in its core representation of the honeyguide mutualism. The one factual adjustment needed is the "cheaters" claim. Everything else reflects the documented relationship correctly.

---

## Recommendation

Accept with one revision: the "cheaters" language should be replaced with a description that captures the mutual-behavior requirement without claiming an individual punishment-memory in the bird. This is a small change with significant accuracy implications.
