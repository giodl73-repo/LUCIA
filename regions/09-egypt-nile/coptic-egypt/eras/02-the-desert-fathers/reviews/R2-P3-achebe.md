## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Notes (Targeted)

**Focus:** Cultural voice and source integrity.

The notes correctly identify the source distinction: Athanasius (Greek literary) vs. Apophthegmata (Coptic oral). The plan to use both is right, but the chapter must be transparent about which source it draws on at each moment. The Life of Anthony is Athanasius telling the world about an Egyptian. The Apophthegmata is Egyptians telling each other about themselves. The difference is the Achebe Test in miniature.

Shenouda in Section 7 is critical. He is the Coptic voice at full power. He writes and preaches in Coptic, not Greek. His placement late in the chapter is structurally correct (the Coptic voice emerging as the era's arc matures), but he must not be reduced to "the first major Coptic-language writer." He is a preacher, a reformer, a man who governed thousands. His language choice is political and theological, not merely practical.

The desert mothers section is correctly placed. One concern: Amma Syncletica's saying is powerful, but it should be contextualized within the tradition's own framework, not presented as a feminist recovery. The desert mothers were in the tradition because the tradition included them, not because we have rediscovered them. The chapter should treat their presence as normal, not as notable.

The Coptic language commitment is good. One addition: the chapter should at some point render a Coptic phrase and let the reader hear the connection to ancient Egyptian. The word "ankh" (life) survives in Coptic as "onkh." The Coptic liturgical response "nai nan" (have mercy on us) carries pharaonic grammatical structures. These connections should be audible, not just claimed.
