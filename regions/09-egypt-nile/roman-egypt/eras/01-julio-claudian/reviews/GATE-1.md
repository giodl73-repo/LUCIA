| Pull      | 4/5   | The opening creates genuine tension: what happens when a ruler who will not look at the bull confronts a population that has been managing rulers like him for millennia? | **Total** | **28/30** | |

**Notes:** Strong opening. The administrative-commercial voice is distinctive and sustained. The Augustus/Apis bull scene is historically grounded (Cassius Dio, Suetonius) and serves double duty as character moment and thematic statement. The twin engines (grain and India trade) are clearly planted for the chapter. The priests' deep patience is the Egyptian counter-voice to Roman extraction, and this tension will carry the chapter.

**Weaknesses to watch:** The middle section risks becoming institutional description without enough embodied figures. The chapter will need named Egyptians, not just "the Egyptians who worked the land." The India trade paragraph needs a specific merchant, a specific ship, a specific cargo manifest to match the Augustus scene's specificity.

**GATE-1 VERDICT: PASS (28/30)**
