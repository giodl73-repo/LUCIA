# R2-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Panel-2 Review: Notes — Aimsir na Manach

The section plan has the right physical destinations. What I need from each:

**Section 2:** The interior of a fili recitation. Sound, not just content. Irish poetry has a specific acoustic texture — the internal rhymes and consonance are made to resonate in a stone hall or a dark room, not to be read silently from a page. When Ferdomnach composes, what does the composition sound like forming in the body? Not as metaphor — as physical sensation. The vibration in the chest, the movement of the breath, the way the meter forces a particular pace. I want the reader's body inside the composition.

**Section 3:** Patrick's physical landscape. The *Confessio* was written in Latin in Ireland, which means Patrick was working in a language that was not the language of the people he was addressing. What did it feel like to pray in Latin in a place where the sacred was embedded in the Irish-speaking land? The holy well at Struel: grey stone, cold water rising from limestone, the smell of watercress. Specific. The landscape of Down where Patrick spent his final years is a coastal landscape — the mountains of Mourne running down to the sea, the light particular to that coast.

**Section 4:** The currach crossing. The notes flag this and I am glad. The currach was made from ox-hide stretched over a frame of hazel rods and sealed with tallow. In summer weather, a crossing from the northeast of Ireland to the Scottish coast of Dal Riata takes perhaps eight to twelve hours. In late spring, the water in the North Channel is cold enough to kill. The peregrini carried almost nothing — the *Navigatio Brendani* (a later text, but reflecting real currach practice) describes a crew of monks with a bag of meal, dried fish, and water. The sound of the hide against the water is a specific sound. The monks would have sung the offices on the water. The singing, in that context, was navigation — it marked time, kept the crew's rhythm, and filled the hours of an open-sea crossing with the structure the monastery provided on land.

**Section 5:** The scriptorium in winter. A specific moment: a monk illuminating in December, when the light in the latitude of Iona or Clonmacnoise fails by mid-afternoon. The lamp is a rush light burning in tallow — it gives warmth as well as light, and the smell of burning tallow is specific. The vellum stiffens in cold. The pigments must be kept warm or they become viscous. The work is done in a cold so particular to that latitude and that season that the illuminator's breath would occasionally mist the page. I want this moment in Section 5.
