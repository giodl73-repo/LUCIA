# 解放 — The Standing Up: China, 1949–1957

## Part One: October 1, 1949 — Tiananmen

The red paint of the gate held the afternoon light as if the gate had been waiting. Mao Zedong stood at its foot in a grey tunic, his Hunanese accent carrying the countryside inside it, and he spoke into the microphone with the simplicity of someone announcing the obvious.

*Zhongguo renmin zhanzqi lai le. The Chinese people have stood up.*

The announcement moved through Tiananmen Square like a current. A million people — and the number was uncertain because some came from the villages and would return when night fell, but a million was the feeling of it — filled the space in front of the gate. The dust of October in Beijing, the dust that had covered this city through warlordism and occupation and the Nationalist defeat, rose in the air like something being shaken off.

Gao Rong, a math teacher from Chengdu, stood in the crowd. He had not fought in any war. He had spent the Japanese occupation teaching students in a school that was sometimes open and sometimes not, watching the Japanese planes, watching the Nationalist government lose the country and leave. When the voice came through the loudspeaker — distorted by early technology, the voice both Mao's and not quite Mao's — something in his chest moved. His eyes burned. He did not understand why.

Around him, other people were crying too, but no one could say exactly what they were crying for. The ending of the waiting. The beginning of something. The relief of the burden. The fear of what came next. All of it at once and none of it separate.

The Century of Humiliation was ending, or so the voice said. The unequal treaties had been a wound in the Chinese language itself: *bainian guochi*, a hundred years of shame. The treaty ports where Chinese were not permitted in certain streets. The occupation of the coast by Japanese guns. The watching of others take what belonged to you, and the inability to stop them. The knowledge, carried in every child's understanding of history, that China — which had been the world's largest economy, the center of civilization — had become something else: semi-colonial, divided, at the mercy of foreign powers.

Now the voice said: that is over. We have stood up. The century of shame has ended.

The gate had been built five centuries earlier, in the Ming Dynasty. Beneath it, or rather inside it, was the Forbidden City — the palace that had been closed to all but the imperial family for centuries, and then closed again after 1911, when the last emperor was removed. The Communists had left it standing, unburned. The revolution was not destroying the past; it was inheriting it. The gate belonged to the people now, or that was the claim. And in the afternoon light, with the voice coming through the loudspeaker and a million people holding their breath, the claim felt true.

In the ministries around the square, the new officials were bent over desks, their hands moving across paper in the failing light. They were inventorying. A country in ruins. Warlord holdouts. An economy on the edge of collapse. Centuries of backwardness to overcome. The revolution had won the war, but now it had to run a country, and the men writing in those offices could feel the weight of it beginning to settle. One man paused, his pen above the page. He wondered if anyone in the square had understood what the standing up would require.

Then he put the pen down and continued writing.

## Part Two: Land Reform, 1950–1951 — The Transfer

In Henan Province, the dust of the village in November had a particular smell — grain dust mixed with earth and the approach of cold. Wang Mingyu stood at the edge of the village square. He had been a tenant farmer for all his adult life. He owned nothing. His hands were calloused from work on land that was not his. His wife was thin from three decades of giving what they produced to the landlord.

The square was filling. The whole village was gathering, because the Party had announced that this would happen today: the trial of the landlord, the transfer of the land, the proof that the standing up meant something real.

Li Xingwang came to the square in the morning. He was sixty-five years old. He had owned the land — actually owned it, his name on the deed — for thirty years. Before that, his father had owned it. The land gave him power and money and the right to be obeyed. Now the Party said that was over.

The trial was not a legal proceeding. It was a testimony. A village official — Chen Guangxi, the Party cadre responsible for the district, age forty-two and carrying the burden of quotas he did not yet fully understand — stood before the crowd and read a list of Li Xingwang's crimes: exploitation of the peasants, hoarding grain during the Japanese occupation, collaboration with the old Nationalist government, parasitism on the people's labor.

The crimes were accurate. Li Xingwang had taken everything the peasants produced and given them barely enough to survive. He had hoarded grain. During the occupation, he had made deals with whoever held power. His wealth came from the peasants' poverty.

"How do you respond?" Chen asked.

Li Xingwang stood silent. Behind him, two young militia members — peasants who had been given authority for the first time in their lives — kept their hands on the landlord's arms.

Someone in the crowd called out: "He should pay for what he has done."

The crowd picked up the words. Pay. Pay. The rhythm was not quite an argument; it was something older, a kind of drumming in the chest.

Chen nodded. The revolution had decided what would happen, and now the village was performing the decision. "The land is no longer his," Chen said. "It is being returned to the people."

The deed was brought forward. It was a physical object, paper with characters written on it. The paper was being transferred from Li Xingwang's house to the village, and from there to the people who worked it.

Wang Mingyu's hand did not shake as he took the deed. The paper was rough. The characters were Li Xingwang's name, crossed out, and Wang Mingyu's name, written in, in a different hand. The transaction was administrative. The meaning was existential.

He owned land. For the first time in his life, Wang Mingyu was a landowner. The feeling was so large that it could not quite fit in his body. Relief and fear and gratitude and a guilt he could not name — guilt at the taking of it, perhaps, or guilt at the relief, or guilt at the knowledge that somewhere inside this joy was a violence he had witnessed and had not resisted.

Li Xingwang was removed from the village. Some landlords were executed. Some were sent to labor camps. Some were allowed to leave. Li Xingwang was allowed to leave. He walked out of the village with one bag and did not return.

That evening, Wang Mingyu stood on the land that was now his. The deed was in the house, and the harvest was coming — the harvest he would keep, all of it. Or most of it. Or enough of it. The details were not clear, because Chen had not explained whether there would be quotas, whether the state would take a portion. In the glow of the standing up, these questions seemed small.

## Part Three: Korea, 1951–1953 — The Proof

Peng Jian was nineteen years old. He had joined the revolutionary army at fifteen, a peasant boy from Jiangxi who had never owned land and had believed that the revolution meant he might own land someday. By 1950, the standing up meant Korea.

The order came in the summer. The United States had invaded Korea — or rather, had supported the invasion of North Korea by South Korea, or had invaded directly; the details depended on which voice was telling the story. What mattered was that UN forces were pushing north, approaching the Chinese border, and Mao had decided that China would enter the war.

The decision was not unanimous. Other Party officials thought China was too weak, too poor, too new. But Mao believed the standing up had to be proven. If enemies stood at the border and China did not resist, then the standing up was incomplete.

Three million soldiers were prepared for Korea. Peng Jian was one of them.

The cold came in November. The cold was absolute. It came from the sky and from the earth simultaneously. The temperature dropped to thirty below zero. The soldiers were equipped for a warm war, not a cold war. Some had jackets. Some did not. All of them had boots, but the boots let in the cold.

Peng Jian's hands became numb after the first week. After the second week, his fingers turned white. He looked at them and understood, in the way that knowledge comes without words, that he had frostbite. The knowledge that they were his hands came after the knowledge that they were damaged.

The taste of food was the taste of cold. The rice was frozen. The way to eat it was to hold it in the mouth until body heat warmed it enough to chew, and then swallow. The hunger was constant. The cold was constant. The sound of the war was distant, then close, then distant again. In the silence between the sounds, the cold was the only thing that existed.

Why are we here? No one asked the question aloud. The answer was understood: to prove that the standing up is real. To show that China can stand against the West. To prove that the revolution has created a people strong enough to do this.

Peng Jian believed this. He held it inside the cold and the hunger as a kind of heat.

The war lasted three years. The United States brought air power and artillery. China brought numbers. The front moved back and forth. By the end, the lines were almost exactly where they had started. Korea remained divided, almost at the same place it had been divided at the beginning of the war.

Peng Jian survived the cold. His fingers recovered enough to move, but he never regained full sensation in them. He returned from Korea in 1953, and there was a hero's welcome — the soldiers were celebrated, the newspapers described the victory, the voice of the revolution claimed that China had proven itself.

But the victory was not a victory. It was a stalemate. The standing up had been proven, but proven in a way that cost 400,000 Chinese lives and three years of resources that China did not have.

Peng Jian carried the knowledge of this contradiction in his body. His feet ached from the cold, even in summer. His hands remembered the sensation of frostbite. And he carried the knowledge that the standing up had required this cost, and the cost had not purchased what the voice claimed it had purchased.

By 1957, Peng Jian was working in a factory in Shanghai. He worked on assembly lines. His hands, still not fully recovered, performed small repetitive movements. He did not speak of Korea. Sometimes, in the winter, his feet would hurt in a way that made him think of the Korean cold. He would stand at the factory line and think about the millions who had stood there with him, and the cold, and the war that proved nothing.

## Part Four: Building, 1953–1956 — The Five-Year Plan

The factories were being built. This was the revolution's second promise: not just the standing up, but the building up. The Five-Year Plan, borrowed from Stalin's Soviet model, said that China would industrialize. Steel mills, power plants, roads, dams. The proof of the standing up would be written in concrete and steel.

Liu Shu was twenty-three years old when he was assigned to the steel mill on the outskirts of Shanghai. He had worked in small manufacturing before the revolution. He believed in modernization. He believed that the revolution meant China would catch up to the West, that peasants like his parents could become workers building something new.

The furnace was visible from across the factory floor. It glowed orange-red in the afternoon, beautiful and terrible. As Liu Shu approached on his first day, the heat became a physical force. The temperature gradient was real. The body felt the radiation before it entered the zone of direct heat.

The sound was industrial — machinery, but underneath it was the furnace itself, a low roar. The smell was of burning metal. Hot iron, hot steel, the smell of oxidation. The workers' hands were blistered. Some of the blisters had burst and left raw skin. The pain was constant. The hunger was constant. The quota was constant.

Five tons a day. Ten tons a day. Fifteen tons a day. The quotas rose with each plan period, and the workers rose to meet them, or tried to. The logic was that if you pushed hard enough, the numbers would follow. The numbers did follow, at least the numbers that were reported.

In a small office near the factory, the manager reported the daily production. Five tons became ten tons on paper, even if the furnace had only produced seven. Ten tons became twenty tons on paper. The falsification was systematic. The manager did not do it from malice. He did it because the quota had to be met, and if he reported accurate numbers, he would be accused of being "rightist" — not supporting the revolution, not believing in the standing up.

The workers understood this without being told. The quota was the number you had to report, not the number you had to produce. But the workers were pushed to produce as much as they could, in the hope that if they worked hard enough, they might actually come close to the reported number.

Liu Shu worked double shifts. The shift system was: work twelve hours, rest six, work twelve hours. After a few months, this became the only rhythm his body knew. The pain in his hands became normal. The hunger became normal. The noise of the furnace became the background to all thought.

The exhilaration of building something new — that lasted about a month. After that, the work became mechanical. But the sense of purpose remained. He was building China. The mills would prove that China could industrialize. The standing up required this sacrifice.

By 1956, the factories were reporting enormous production numbers. Steel production had increased. The Five-Year Plan was declared a success. The newspapers reported it. The voice of the revolution claimed victory.

What was actually happening was more complex. Some of the quota numbers were real. Some were falsified. The gap between what was reported and what was actually produced was growing. And the gap between what the workers produced and what they were given to eat was also growing. The ration was designed for normal work, not for the intensity of the quota chase. The workers were slowly becoming hollow.

Liu Shu's hands became stronger. His back became stronger. His hunger became stronger. The balance tipped, and the work that had been building something new became the work of using up the body to reach a number.

## Part Five: The Hundred Flowers and the Reversal, 1956–1957 — The Opening and the Closure

Lin Mumin was a literature professor in Shanghai. He was twenty-eight years old. He had studied May Fourth literature — the renaissance of the early twentieth century when Chinese writers had believed that literature could change society. He had become a Communist because he believed the revolution could allow free thought, could allow literature and art to flourish in a truly modern China.

In May 1956, Mao announced: *Let a hundred flowers bloom; let a hundred schools of thought contend.* The Party was strong enough, he said, to allow criticism. Intellectuals could speak. The revolution had nothing to fear from internal debate.

Lin Mumin believed Mao. That was the mistake, and also not a mistake, because the opening was real, and the trap was real, and it was impossible to know, in the moment, which would prevail.

He began to write. In his office, the particular smell of paper and ink and dust surrounded him. The windows were tall and let in weak light. The cold of the building was part of the work. The pen in his hand was familiar from years of writing.

But tonight, the pen felt different. What he was writing was dangerous.

He wrote about censorship. He wrote about the Party's suppression of genuine intellectual life. He wrote about the contradiction between the revolution's claim to support free thought and its actual suppression of dissent. He wrote carefully, using literary references and allusion rather than direct political statement. But the meaning was clear.

The internal debate — hope against fear — manifested as a tremor in his hand. The words on the page were clear. The danger they carried was clear. The decision to submit the essay to a journal affiliated with the Literary Gazette was the decision to surrender the act to a system that might destroy him for it.

But in 1956 and early 1957, it seemed as if the opening was real. Dozens of intellectuals were speaking. Professors, writers, journalists, artists were publishing criticism of the Party, the bureaucracy, the suppression of freedom. The debate seemed real. The possibility seemed real.

Universities became centers of intellectual ferment. Students were debating. Teachers were speaking in classrooms in ways they had not spoken before. The feeling was of something beginning, a spring after a long winter.

And then, in the spring of 1957, the temperature changed. The response from the Party became cooler. The essays and speeches began to be criticized, not as contributions to debate but as attacks on the revolution. The language shifted. The intellectuals who had spoken were being labeled "rightists" — not just wrong, but anti-revolutionary, hostile to the people.

Lin Mumin felt the shift before he understood it. A colleague who had spoken stopped speaking. The literary magazine that had published criticism began to publish denunciations of the same critics it had published weeks before. The world had not changed, but the world's meaning had inverted.

The summons came in May. He was to attend a meeting at the university. The meeting was a denunciation session. Dozens of colleagues were present. The intellectuals who had spoken were brought forward, one by one, and accused of rightism.

When it was Lin Mumin's turn, he stood before the room and heard his own essays read back to him, but the meaning had changed. What he had written as gentle criticism was now presented as bitter attack. What he had offered as internal debate was now presented as betrayal. The colleagues who had praised the essays weeks before now remained silent, or added their voices to the denunciation.

Lin Mumin was told that he had a choice: he could recant, admit that he had been wrong, beg forgiveness, and agree to study the thoughts of Mao more deeply. Or he could be sent to a labor camp for re-education.

He chose to recant. He wrote a statement admitting that his thinking had been poisoned by bourgeois ideology, that he had been wrong to question the Party, that he understood now that the Party was always right. The words felt like they were being written by someone else.

He was removed from his position as a professor. He was transferred to a lower post. The essays were removed from the library. They could no longer be read or quoted. It was as if they had never existed, except that everyone remembered them, and everyone would remember that Lin Mumin had written them, and that he had then renounced them.

## Part Six: The Closing — The Standing Up and Its Shadow

By the end of 1957, the era had taken a shape. The standing up was real. The century of humiliation was ended. The proof was everywhere: the revolution had won the war, unified the country, built factories, distributed land, moved mountains.

Wang Mingyu owned his land. The deed was in his house. He had produced grain that was his own, although the state had taken a quota. The amount of the quota was growing each year, and the margin between what he produced and what he kept was shrinking, but the standing up was real — the landlord was gone, the land was his, the future seemed to belong to him.

Peng Jian carried the memory of Korea in his body. The cold had left traces in his hands and feet that would never fully heal. But he had stood for the standing up. He had proven that China could resist. The sacrifice had been real.

Liu Shu's hands were strong now, and scarred. The factory was producing steel, and the quotas were being met, and the voice of the revolution said that this was proof of the standing up. That China was becoming an industrial power. That the century of being backward was ending.

Lin Mumin had learned something about the revolution that he could not speak. The opening was real, and the trap was real, and they were the same thing. The revolution could invite criticism only if it believed in its own strength. But if the criticism became genuine, the revolution felt threatened, and the trap closed. The spring became winter. The flowers were not allowed to bloom; they were required to bloom and then punished for blooming.

What no one could see yet was that the system was already inscribed into the revolution's logic. The quota system, learned in land reform, was now the governing method for all agriculture and all industry. The false reporting, necessary to show that quotas were being met, was becoming systemic. The gap between the reported world and the actual world was growing.

The officials in the ministries — the ones who had been at desks in October 1949, inventorying a country in ruins — were now reading reports that said the country was being built. The factories were producing. The farms were producing. The quotas were being met. The Five-Year Plan was a success. The standing up was real and verifiable and proven in numbers.

They did not know that many of the numbers were false.

The Party did not yet know that it was constructing the logic of catastrophe. The system that would generate the Great Leap Forward was already being built. The belief that mobilization through impossible quotas *works* was already being established. The willingness to suppress information that contradicted the narrative of success was already habitual.

The standing up was real. The cost was being paid, and the full cost was not yet visible. The era closed with the genuine achievement and the genuine shadow fused together, impossible to separate.

In 1958, the Great Leap Forward would be announced. The quotas would be doubled. The targets would become not just impossible but absurd: millions of tons of steel from backyard furnaces, agricultural production increases that defied the laws of agriculture. The system that had worked well enough to appear to succeed would be pushed beyond the point of return.

But in December 1957, that was still in the future. The standing up had happened. China was no longer waiting. The waiting was over.

What would replace it was not yet clear.
