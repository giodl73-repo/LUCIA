# BOARD Review — B-2: Cécile Fromont

**Chapter:** Kongo Kingdom / Era 2: The Portuguese Encounter — Afonso I
**Reviewer:** Cécile Fromont (Specialist in Kongo visual and material culture; Christian-Kongo art history)
**Primary work:** *The Art of Conversion: Christian Visual Culture in the Kingdom of Kongo* (2014)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Material Culture and Visual Accuracy

**The cosmogram:** The chapter's treatment of the cosmogram is correct and well sourced. The dibundu/four-movement cosmogram, the kalunga line, the intersection as the site of power — these are accurately described. The chapter's claim that the Portuguese missionaries showed the Kongo court the crucifix in 1491 is historically correct (the first missionaries arrived with the Portuguese expedition of 1491).

**The nkisi nkondi:** Accurately described as a covenant-recording instrument with iron nails. The description ("their surfaces studded with iron nails and blades, each nail recording a covenant") is correct in essentials.

**The cathedral:** The characterization of the Cathedral of São Salvador as built in stone and built by Kongo masons is accurate. The cathedral was one of the most significant built projects of Afonso's reign, and the chapter correctly notes its political significance as a statement of sovereign Christian statehood.

---

## The Conversion Narrative

My primary scholarly contribution to this topic is the argument that the Kongo's adoption of Christianity was neither passive acceptance nor resistant accommodation — it was creative engagement, a genuine cultural work of translation that produced a new form of Christian practice. The chapter captures this correctly. The sentence "He converted because the Christian framework, understood in Kongo terms, offered resources he could use" is exactly right.

The chapter correctly notes that the cosmogram was "close enough to a Kongo category that it could be inhabited, revised, made meaningful." The word "inhabited" is the right one. This was not merely intellectual recognition; it was a lived practice.

---

## One Concern

The chapter does not address the visual and material culture of the Christian-Kongo synthesis — the crucifixes made by Kongo craftsmen in local materials, the ivories carved for the Portuguese market that incorporated Kongo cosmological symbols, the visual vocabulary that emerged from the encounter. For the purposes of this chapter (which is primarily political and diplomatic in focus), this is an understandable omission. The notes.md should flag this as material for Era 3 (the Atlantic Trade's Corruption), where the material culture of the synthesis becomes historically central.

**Recommendation:** Add one sentence or brief passage in "The School and the Ship" acknowledging that the Christian-Kongo synthesis was not only textual and institutional but also material — that Kongo craftsmen were making Christian objects that were not European objects but something new. This would deepen the chapter without requiring a full treatment of the material culture, and it would prepare the ground for Era 3's fuller engagement with this topic.

---

## Overall Assessment

The chapter's treatment of the Kongo-Christian synthesis is historically accurate and culturally respectful. It correctly centers the Kongo's own intellectual and diplomatic agency in the encounter. The material culture dimension is a gap that should be flagged for Era 3.

**BOARD PASS** with the recommendation to add a sentence on the material synthesis.
