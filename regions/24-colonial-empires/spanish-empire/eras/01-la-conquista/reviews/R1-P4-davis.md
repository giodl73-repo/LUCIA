## On the Encounter of Worldviews

Every civilization is built on a set of premises that its members take as self-evident — premises about the nature of reality, about the proper ordering of human relations, about what constitutes a legitimate claim on land and people and life. The catastrophe of the Spanish conquest was not that one civilization was weak and another was strong. It was that two incompatible sets of premises met, and one of them had ships, steel, and epidemic disease.

The opening does something crucial: it presents the Spanish premises as sincere. Palacios Rubios was not performing cynicism. He genuinely believed that the Pope had authority over all temporal rulers by right of the Donation of Constantine, that this authority extended to non-Christian peoples, that submission to the Church was both a requirement and an offer of salvation. These premises were internally coherent within the Catholic theological tradition. They were also entirely incomprehensible to the people on the receiving end of the Requerimiento — not because those people were unsophisticated, but because they were operating from a completely different set of premises about what land meant, what authority meant, what the relationship between the human and the sacred looked like.

The opening honors this incomprehensibility without explaining it. The village is empty because the people understood what the ships meant, not because they failed to understand the Requerimiento. This distinction is important. They did not fail to comprehend the requirement. The requirement was not comprehensible because it was not addressed to them — it was addressed to a legal fiction of them.

## What the Opening Captures Well

**The knowledge system embedded in the document itself.** The Requerimiento is not just a political instrument. It is a theological argument. It carries within it an entire cosmology: the relationship between God and Pope, Pope and temporal rulers, temporal rulers and subjects, subjects and the conditions of legitimate war. This cosmology was the knowledge system of Catholic scholastic philosophy at its most developed. The opening presents this correctly — not as propaganda, but as a genuine intellectual construction, built by a man trained at Salamanca and Bologna, applying the best legal and theological scholarship of his day to a new problem.

**The indigenous knowledge system rendered in absence.** The empty village is a form of knowledge. The people who went into the forest knew something that Palacios Rubios did not know: what the ships meant. Their knowledge was experiential, collective, rapidly transmitted across communities that had no written communication. Within two decades of Columbus's arrival, news of what the Spanish did had spread through indigenous networks across the Caribbean and into the mainland. The empty village is the record of that transmission. The opening presents this correctly without explaining it.

## What the Chapter Must Not Do

**Render the indigenous knowledge systems as absence.** The empty village is powerful precisely because it is all that survives — the people in the forest do not speak in this opening. That is correct for the opening. But the chapter cannot sustain this treatment across 5,000 words. The knowledge systems of the Mexica, of the Quechua speakers, of the peoples of the Caribbean were as complex, as internally coherent, and as sophisticated as the scholastic theology of Palacios Rubios. The chapter must find a way to present them — not as victims, not as objects of the Spanish gaze, but as the holders of genuine and irreplaceable epistemologies that the conquest was in the process of destroying.

**The destruction of knowledge as the chapter's deepest tragedy.** What the conquest destroyed was not only population — though the population loss (perhaps 90% in the first century in some regions, through disease as much as violence) was catastrophic. It destroyed knowledge systems that had taken millennia to develop: astronomical, agricultural, medicinal, philosophical. The calendar systems of the Maya and Mexica were more accurate than the Julian calendar the Spanish brought. The agricultural knowledge encoded in the milpa system had been refined over thousands of years. None of this was preserved in the Requerimiento's logic. The chapter should find at least one scene where this dimension is present.

## The Question I'd Push On

**The 1519 meeting of Cortés and Moctezuma II in Tenochtitlan.** This encounter has been rendered endlessly from the Spanish side. But Moctezuma was not a passive victim. He was a sophisticated political actor operating from a completely coherent worldview — one in which the arrival of strangers from the east at the predicted year of the god's return was a significant cosmic event requiring careful management. His worldview turned out to be catastrophically wrong in its assumptions about what Cortés was. But it was a worldview, not a vacuum. The chapter that renders this meeting from inside BOTH worldviews simultaneously — holding the Spanish theological premise and the Mexica cosmological premise in the same scene — would be doing something exceptional.
