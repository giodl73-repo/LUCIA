# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman

**Chapter:** Naqada I — The Amratian
**Stage:** PANEL-1
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## First Read

I read this straight through and I did not stop. That is the first test and it passed.

## What Works

The opening line is perfect in its modesty: "The copper was cold when he pulled it from the mold, but it would not stay cold for long." It is quiet, physical, and it moves. The reader is in a workshop before they know whose workshop or why it matters. This is scene-first writing.

The structural choice to withhold the second craftsman until the third paragraph is good instinct. "He did not know that three days' walk downriver, at a settlement where the wadi met the floodplain, another man was shaping the same object from the same ore." This is the narrative engine: two unknowing participants in a pattern neither can see. It opens a question without announcing that it is a question.

The material catalog in paragraph five works because each item carries its own story: the thin-walled pottery showing pink, the white lime hippos, the ochre for living and dead skin. These are not listed. They are inhabited.

The burial paragraph is the best section. "Every placement was a sentence in a language the living spoke to the dead without writing it down." This is the kind of line that earns a reader's trust.

## What Needs Attention

The copper-discovery paragraph (paragraph four, beginning "The copper was the same because the source was the same") does good work explaining how smelting was discovered, but the mode shifts slightly from scene to explanation. "Somewhere in those generations, someone noticed..." is retrospective summary, not inhabited moment. The voice recovers, but the paragraph is the opening's weakest stretch narratively.

The flood paragraph risks becoming a set piece. "The flood was everything" is a strong declaration, but the sentences that follow it are context-delivery rather than scene. Compare this to the burial paragraph, where every sentence is both context and story simultaneously. The flood paragraph is mostly context.

The closing works well. "He picked up the next piece of ore and blew on the coals" is exactly the right register: quiet, forward-moving, refusing to announce its own significance.

## The Question I'd Push On

Can the flood section be grounded in the craftsman's own experience rather than delivered as a narrator's explanation? What does the flood mean to the man holding the awl? If this chapter is about the network forming through the hands of makers, let the flood arrive through his hands too.
