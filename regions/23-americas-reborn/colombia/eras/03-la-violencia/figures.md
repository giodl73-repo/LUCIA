# Figures — La Violencia
## Colombia, Era 3: 1930–1958

---

## Jorge Eliécer Gaitán (1903–1948)

Born in Bogotá to a humble family — his father sold books from a street cart, his mother was a schoolteacher — Gaitán worked his way to a law degree in Rome and returned to Colombia in the 1930s with both the education of the ruling class and a permanent awareness that he was not of it. This double position was not a liability. It was his instrument.

He understood the país político from inside it — he had studied in its language, used its courts, wore its suits — but he spoke to the país nacional in theirs. His stump speeches are still recognizable, sixty years later, as a specific kind of Colombian public rhetoric: long on moral clarity, short on policy specifics, built around the dichotomy between "the people" and "the oligarchy," phrased in the cadences of popular speech rather than parliamentary debate. He was not Marxist — he did not propose collectivization or revolution — but he was something the Colombian elite found equally threatening: a man who could turn electoral arithmetic into genuine mass politics.

His 1948 "Oración por la Paz" — the silent march of 100,000 in the Plaza de Bolívar — reveals the strategic sophistication the oligarchy feared most. He understood optics. He understood that 100,000 people standing in black silence are more politically legible, internationally and domestically, than 100,000 people shouting. He was making Colombia's violence visible in a form that could not be dismissed as chaos. The silence was the argument.

Whether he could have governed is a question Colombia has been arguing about since 1948. The evidence suggests he would have faced the same structural constraints as every other Colombian president: a fragmented Congress, regional elite power, a state too weak in the periphery to implement anything. But the question presupposes a Colombia in which he was allowed to try.

He was forty-four years old. He had a briefcase in his hand when he walked out of his office. He was going to lunch.

## Rosa — Composite Figure

**Disclosure:** This figure is a composite assembled from testimonial accounts in the Centro Nacional de Memoria Histórica archive, particularly the testimony collected for *¡Basta Ya!* (2013). No single named individual; the specific details drawn from multiple documented accounts of displaced coffee-farming families in Tolima. The practice of composite figuration follows the standard for oral/testimonial cultures established in the project's figures guidance — the archival basis is real; the consolidation into a single narrative voice is an acknowledged technique, not an invention.

Rosa's family had farmed coffee above Salento, Quindío, for two generations. Her grandmother had cleared the original plot in the 1890s, during the colonización antioqueña, the wave of smallholder settlement that moved through the coffee frontier. Her family were Liberals by inheritance — not by conviction in any ideological sense, but by the fact that her grandfather had fought with the Liberal forces in the Thousand Days War (1899–1902) and come back to his farm with the association permanently made.

After Gaitán's assassination, the Conservative municipality of Salento reorganized its local power structure. The accounts collected in the CNMH archive document this pattern across dozens of municipalities: the alcalde, the police, and the landowners coordinating to identify Liberal families with property worth acquiring. The coordination was informal — no written orders, no documentation — but the pattern was too consistent and too systematic to be coincidental.

Rosa described the morning the men came in language that CNMH researchers heard again and again from other testimonies: the specific detail of the pre-dawn arrival (organized, not impulsive); the absence of any stated charge; the implicit threat of what would happen if they refused; her husband's silence, which was not submission but calculation — he understood that resistance in that moment meant death, and he had children. The cast-iron pot. The grandmother's saint. The one backward look.

She was in her forties when she gave her testimony. She described the smell of the coffee flowers with the precision of someone who has spent fifty years not forgetting. She said: I did not know, at the time, that we would never go back. I thought we were going somewhere until it was safe. But there is no "until" in this country. There is only what happens.

She and her husband settled eventually in the outskirts of Ibagué, the departmental capital of Tolima. Her sons grew up in a city, not on a farm. One became a bus driver. One died young. She outlived her husband by twenty years. She gave her testimony voluntarily, in the late 1990s, for the record. She said she wanted people to know not what happened to her specifically but what the displacement was like — the specific texture of it, the objects you carried, the thing you smelled on the road that made you understand you were not going back.

The CNMH estimates one to two million people experienced something comparable.

## Efraín González, "El Cóndor" (c. 1930–1965)

González was born in the coffee zone of Quindío, Conservative by origin, and became one of the most documented figures in the secondary phase of La Violencia — the period when partisan conflict had transformed into what scholars call "social banditry": armed groups that had lost their explicit partisan character while retaining complex relationships with their local communities.

He began as a partisan killer in the early 1950s, operating within the Conservative armed network. The precise circumstances of his break with that network are not entirely clear in the historical record — whether it was a specific dispute, a gradual drift, or a deliberate choice. By the mid-1950s, he was operating independently, in the mountains of Quindío and Risaralda, with a following that included men from both parties.

The scholarship on González — particularly Sánchez and Meertens' fieldwork in *Bandits, Peasants, and Politics* — documents the specific codes of conduct that distinguished him in local memory from other armed figures of the period. He reportedly refused to kill children. He reportedly spared priests, unless they were functioning as informants for the army, which some were. He reportedly respected the neutrality of certain cantinas and market spaces that served the local economy. These codes were not legal and had no institutional basis; they were the product of a practical calculation that a man living in a mountain community needs the community's tolerated presence to survive. The codes were also, according to local testimony, enforced — men who violated them were disciplined or expelled from the group.

This is not to romanticize González. The scholarship is also clear that his group engaged in extortion, kidnapping for ransom, and killings that were not defensible even on the group's own stated terms. The "social bandit" framework — drawn from Eric Hobsbawm's typology and applied by Sánchez and Meertens to the Colombian case — does not require moral heroism; it describes a specific relationship between an armed actor and the local population, a relationship in which some reciprocity exists and some protection is offered in exchange for some degree of tolerance.

González was killed on June 9, 1965, by an army patrol. He was approximately thirty-five years old. By the time of his death, the official phase of La Violencia had ended seven years earlier; the National Front agreement was in its seventh year. He had continued because the structural conditions that had produced the violence — the absent state, the unresolved land question, the mountain communities with no other form of protection — had not changed when the party agreement was signed.

He is the figure who shows where La Violencia went when it did not end: into the mountains, into the long institutional memory of the men who had lived it, into the zones that would produce the FARC a year before his death.

## Gabriel García Márquez (1927–2014)

Born in Aracataca, a banana-plantation town on the Colombian Caribbean coast, García Márquez was twenty years old and a law student in Bogotá when he watched the Bogotazo from his boarding house window. This biographical fact is well documented in *Vivir para Contarla* — his 2002 memoir — and in multiple interviews.

His relationship to La Violencia was double. As a Caribbean costeño, he was outside the Andean-interior social world where the killing was most intense; the Caribbean coast had its own rhythms of violence (the banana company massacre of 1928, documented in his novel, was closer to his family's direct experience than La Violencia proper). But as a journalist and intellectual based in Bogotá and then Barranquilla through the 1950s, he was an acute observer of how the era's violence shaped the culture's self-understanding.

His journalism from the early 1950s — columns in *El Heraldo* in Barranquilla — shows a writer trying out registers for what he would later perfect: the matter-of-fact narration of the extraordinary, the refusal to explain what the scene itself shows, the accumulative style that lets violence and miracle sit side by side without either disturbing the other's narrative status.

*Cien Años de Soledad* (1967) is the fullest expression of what he was reaching for. The Buendía family's participation in seventeen civil wars, all Liberal, all lost, is drawn from the pattern of the Liberal-Conservative conflict across Colombian history. Colonel Aureliano Buendía is not Gaitán and not González and not any single historical figure, but he is recognizably a figure from a Colombia where civil wars happen to specific families in specific towns and leave specific kinds of residue — in the men who fought them, in the women who waited, in the houses where the chairs still smell of old gunpowder and old grief.

The little gold fish that Aureliano makes and melts and makes again at the end of the novel are the purest image in the book: futile repetition not as despair but as the only available form of fidelity to something that cannot be named. Colombia, in García Márquez's understanding, is a country that has always been making the fish and melting them and beginning again. La Violencia was one of the meltings. The fish were being made again.

He received the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1982, in Stockholm. His acceptance speech — "La soledad de América Latina" — addressed the isolation and misreading of Latin American reality by the outside world. He said: "We have not had a moment's rest. A promethean president, entrenched in his burning palace, died fighting an entire army, alone; and two suspicious airplane accidents, never clarified, cut short the life of another great-hearted president and that of a democratic soldier who had restored the dignity of his people." He was speaking of Latin America as a whole, but Colombia in particular: a country that had spent its modern history burying its leaders and waiting for a letter that would not come.
