# PANEL-2 — Ryszard Kapuscinski (targeted)
## The First Empire: Notes Review

The section plan is now a plan. The opening revision will integrate the quayside: March cold, the Mersey, the specific weight of loading. Good.

My concern for the full chapter: Section 2 (The City) risks becoming architectural inventory. The Salthouse dock, the Exchange, the new streets — these are real and important but they are stone and brick without bodies. The city needs people moving through it. A rope-maker on the dock in the early morning. A merchant's clerk walking from the counting house to the coffee house on Castle Street where the ship captains gather to discuss the next season's voyages. The sound of a city organized around departure — not just investment, but the physical fact of ships being made ready, goods being loaded, men being hired.

Section 4 (The Second Ledger) will be the chapter's most demanding passage because Equiano and Clarkson are working in prose and argument, not in sensation. The *Brookes* diagram is the chapter's best sensory detail in this section: dimensions, numbers, bodies as units of measurement. The diagram was printed and distributed as a broadsheet. What did it feel like to hold it? What did a person think who had never seen the inside of a slave ship, confronted for the first time with the measured space allotted to each body? That reader's response — not described, but placed — is the chapter's best sensory moment in Section 4.

The landing I want: someone standing on the Liverpool dock in 1807, watching the last authorized slaving voyage clear. Not described as the last; the person watching does not know it is the last. The ordinary morning. The manifest filed. The ship gone. Then the reader knows what the person watching does not.
