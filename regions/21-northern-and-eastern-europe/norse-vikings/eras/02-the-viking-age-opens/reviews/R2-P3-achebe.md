## The Voice Plan

The notes identify the register as "saga-spare" and list techniques: proverb-weight sentences, laconic transition, understatement. This is a good foundation. But I want to push on what this voice means for the hardest passages.

**The raid descriptions.** The chapter will describe the Norse taking silver from monasteries, killing monks, carrying off people. From the inside, this is straightforward: men went, took what was there, came home richer. From outside, it is violence against the helpless. The saga voice handles this naturally, because the saga tradition does not moralize. "He killed three men and took their silver" is a saga sentence. No judgment. No admiration. No horror. The reader supplies their own response. The notes should explicitly commit to this: the raids will be rendered in the saga's own moral register, which is no register at all. Statement of fact. The reader is the jury.

**The slave trade.** The notes mention "slaves moving south" on the eastern routes. This requires care. The Norse traded in human beings. From inside the Norse world, a thrall was a category in the social order, not a moral crisis. The chapter must render this from inside without flinching and without editorializing. Do not soften it. Do not emphasize it for shock. State it the way the saga would: as a fact of the world these people lived in. If the chapter handles the raid violence correctly (saga-spare, no verdict), it will handle the thrall trade correctly by the same discipline.

**The gods.** The blot is mentioned. The cosmology is not yet planned in detail. The full chapter should let the gods be real. Not "the Norse believed in..." but the world as the Norse inhabited it: Odin's ravens flying over the battlefield, Thor's hammer worn at the neck, the seeress speaking what the runes revealed. The gods are not a belief system. They are the world.

## One Structural Note

The notes plan to address Achebe's concern about "learned to fear" and the editorial "technology" line. Good. But the deeper commitment must hold through the full chapter: this is entirely the Norse story. When Alcuin or the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle are quoted, they appear as outside voices that the Norse world encounters, not as the narrative frame. The Norse do not define themselves by others' fear. They define themselves by what they do.
