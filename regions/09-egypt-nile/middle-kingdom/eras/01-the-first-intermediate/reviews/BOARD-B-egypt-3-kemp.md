## BOARD — B-egypt-3 — Barry Kemp — Review of The Fragmentation

**Scope:** Social structure, provincial administration, economic systems, the centralization-to-fragmentation arc.

**Assessment:** This is the chapter that sits most squarely in my domain. The structural analysis of how the Old Kingdom's center-periphery relationship broke down is well-handled, and the feedback loop framework captures the dynamic correctly.

**What is accurate:**
- The transition from royal appointees to hereditary nomarchs is correctly traced through the provincial tomb evidence. The shift in tomb inscription subject matter from king-centered to self-centered is a real and well-documented phenomenon.
- The characterization of the revenue shift (grain surplus staying local instead of flowing to the center) is structurally correct and reflects the economic analysis I have advocated in my own work.
- The treatment of the First Intermediate Period as "diffusion, not collapse" is exactly right. This was not a Dark Age. It was a redistribution of power and cultural production.
- The Ankhtifi material is handled well. His inscription is correctly characterized as self-aggrandizing, and the famine passage is correctly noted as formulaic while acknowledging the underlying reality.
- The political geography of the period (Herakleopolis/Thebes/contested middle) is accurately rendered.

**What needs minor attention:**
- Line 39: "The decline of the Old Kingdom had structural causes that preceded the climate shift." This is correct and reflects the current scholarly consensus. The monocausal climate explanation (Bell 1971) has been significantly qualified by Hassan (1997), Butzer (2012), and more recent work. The chapter's balanced treatment is appropriate.
- The Instruction of Merikare passage is correctly located as a Herakleopolitan text and correctly characterized as cautious and reflective. The admission regarding Thinis is real and is one of the most remarkable passages in Egyptian royal literature.
- The el-Tarif saff tombs are correctly described as architecturally distinct from northern traditions. Arnold's documentation is the standard reference.

**Factual errors found:** 0

**One suggestion:** The chapter mentions "the forty-two nomes of the Two Lands" (line 25). The number forty-two is the traditional canonical count (twenty-two in Upper Egypt, twenty in Lower Egypt), but the actual number of functioning administrative units varied over time. The canonical forty-two may not have been fully established until the Middle Kingdom. This is a minor point, and the chapter could note "approximately forty-two" or simply use "the nomes of the Two Lands" without specifying the number. This is an advisory, not a required correction.

**Verdict:** Structurally sound. The feedback loop model is a legitimate analytical framework, and the chapter applies it without overstatement. The treatment of decentralization as productive rather than catastrophic is exactly the approach this period requires.
