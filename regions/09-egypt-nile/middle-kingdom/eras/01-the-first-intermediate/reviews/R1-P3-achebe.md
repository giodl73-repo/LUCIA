## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of The Fragmentation (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening passes the test. Khety tells his own story, in his own terms, from inside his own crisis. There is no outside observer judging the fragmentation as failure or praising the nomarchs as proto-democrats. The story belongs to the people who lived it.

**What works:**
- The voice is correctly pitched. This is not the confident certainty of the pyramid builders. It is the voice of a man making practical decisions in a world where the cosmic guarantees have failed. The shift from the Old Kingdom register is earned through content, not through stylistic imposition.
- "I fed my people. I held my nome. I did what the king could not do, or would not do, or was no longer there to do." This is the culture speaking for itself. The three-part construction mirrors the inscriptional tradition while carrying the political claim. It sounds like something carved on a wall, because it was.
- Ma'at is handled with care. It is not explained to the reader as a concept. It arrives as a practical problem: the flood does not come, the rituals are distributed among twenty temples, the order is no longer singular. The theological crisis is felt, not lectured.

**What doesn't:**
- I want to hear from more than one social register. Khety is an elite, a nomarch. The Eloquent Peasant is one of the defining texts of this era, and it tells the story of a commoner who demands justice from the system. The opening need not include a peasant, but the full chapter must. The First Intermediate Period's cultural achievement was not only the nomarchs' self-assertion but the emergence of voices from below. If the chapter tells only the nomarchs' story, it will be a story about elites told from inside, which is half the Achebe Test.
- The phrase "what the king could not do, or would not do, or was no longer there to do" is strong, but watch that "or was no longer there to do" does not imply that the king's absence is the only relevant fact. The people of the nomes did not merely fill a vacuum. They generated a culture that was new, not merely compensatory.

**The question I'd push on:**
Whose fragmentation is this? The opening tells it from the nomarch's perspective, which is honest to the elite inscriptional record. But the era's literary revolution includes voices of doubt, complaint, and philosophical questioning that belong to scribes and commoners, not only to governors. The chapter must find room for the Eloquent Peasant's world alongside Khety's.
