# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Panel Review — Opening: La Conquista y El Virreinato

I have reported from places where a name changes everything. In Addis Ababa after Haile Selassie fell, to say certain names aloud was to step outside the law. In Lagos, I saw a man become untouchable for three hours because a crowd decided he had been chosen. I know, from experience, how physical the act of naming is — how it moves through a crowd like a current through water. This opening understands that.

"The corregidor's body was still warm when José Gabriel Condorcanqui put on the name." That sentence is as good as any opening I have read in this project. It has temperature in it. The body is warm — not dead-cold, not symbolic — warm, still in the process of dying, and in that same moment of dying a man who is not yet Túpac Amaru is becoming Túpac Amaru. The author understands the physics of the moment: everything is in transition, nothing is settled, and the crowd in the plaza is the medium through which the transition occurs.

The sensory work in this opening is generally strong. The cold gold grass of the high valleys. The wool cloaks. The men who "breathed differently" after Potosí. The azogue, the mercury, the amalgamation patios — these are specific enough to be trusted, specific enough to be real. I can smell the mercury if I try. I can feel the altitude in the wool.

What is missing is the crowd. The opening tells us that Condorcanqui had the sentence read in Quechua, that the "gathered crowd — Quechua-speaking, wool-cloaked, watching" was present. But the crowd is described, not felt. What was it like to be in that plaza? What sound did the crowd make — or not make — when Arriaga was hanged? What moved through the bodies of the people standing there when Condorcanqui put on the name? The author describes what happened. I want to feel what it was like to witness it.

There is one moment where the sensory register lapses into the analytical without completing the fusion: "In Quechua, tupaq means royal, sovereign, brilliant. Amaru is the great serpent..." This is the opening's most pedagogical passage. The cosmological explanation of the name is important and correct, but the register shifts slightly — becomes more like a footnote than like being in the plaza. The chapter's full version should find a way to carry the name's meaning through sensation rather than definition.

The Micaela passage recovers the register beautifully. "The men who came back from Potosí breathed differently" is the kind of sentence that does more than any amount of explanation. Everyone in the highlands knew it, the author says. That "everyone" carries the whole social knowledge-system in three syllables.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening promises us the plaza of Cusco — the torture, the execution, designed to be remembered. It withholds the scene deliberately, and correctly, for the chapter. But the chapter must deliver it. What was it like to be in the plaza of Cusco on May 18, 1781, when the Spanish did what they did to Condorcanqui and Micaela Bastidas and their family in public? I need to feel the temperature of that plaza, the sound, the bodies of the crowd. The Spanish designed it to terrorize. The chapter's task is to render it as the Andean crowd experienced it — not as terror, but as something that confirmed everything the uprising had been saying about the colonial world. How the plaza of execution became the plaza of transmission.
