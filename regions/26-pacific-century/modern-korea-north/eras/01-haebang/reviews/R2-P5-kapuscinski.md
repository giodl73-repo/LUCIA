# PANEL-2 Review — Ryszard Kapuscinski (Sensory Immersion Gap Response)

**Reviewer:** Ryszard Kapuscinski (P-5)  
**Date:** 2026-04-16  
**Focus:** Moving from concept to embodied experience

---

The production bible says: "The sensory experience of what colonization did to the body" and "The wound was the mythology trying to heal." These are the right questions. Now you have to answer them in the body of the chapter, not in abstract terms.

**Here's what I want to feel:**

**In Section 2 (What Was Lost):**
A child in a Korean classroom during Japanese occupation. Not told that "the language was suppressed." Instead:
- The teacher (Japanese) enters the classroom.
- A student speaks Korean to another student.
- The teacher hears. The punishment is immediate (a slap, a caning, the child's hand struck).
- The child's body learns: Korean words cause pain.
- Later, when Korean is permitted again, the child is silent for years, even though no one will hit them anymore. The silence has become part of their body.

This is not metaphor. This is how power works on flesh. Give me the smell of the classroom. The feel of the wooden desk. The moment when the child's mouth opens to speak Korean and cannot.

**In Section 3 (The Soviet Installation):**
When land is redistributed, a peasant family approaches their new land for the first time. Not as abstract redistribution, but as:
- The father walking the boundary of what is now his.
- The feel of the soil. Is it rich? Is it worn? What does it mean that this particular earth is now his responsibility?
- The mother coming behind him, understanding what redistribution means for survival (food, security).
- The child understanding: this is what liberation feels like. Not idea. Ground under your feet.

**In Section 4 (The Name and the Legend):**
When the mythology is taking hold, show it at the level of muscle memory:
- A schoolchild learning to write Kim Il-sung's name (the stroke order, the character formation).
- Learning to bow before his photograph with the correct depth and duration.
- Learning the phrases of loyalty: not as propaganda, but as the repetition that becomes reflexive.
- By 1950, the child does not think about loyalty anymore. The body does it automatically.

**The landing:**
"End with: a North Korean person in 1950, fully believing, not knowing the history of the doubt."

Yes. And I want to feel that belief in their body. Maybe they're standing before a portrait of Kim Il-sung. Maybe they're in a demonstration, singing. Maybe they're in a study session. I want to know what belief feels like from the inside, through sensation: the warmth of the crowd, the power of the collective voice, the relief of having an answer to the question "What will we become?"

**Verdict:** The structure is right. The sensory detail will come if you commit to rendering everything through bodies, not through ideas about bodies. Show me a hand being struck for speaking Korean. Show me feet walking a boundary. Show me a child's mouth learning not to speak. Show me what the mythology feels like to live inside — not intellectually, but in the flesh.
