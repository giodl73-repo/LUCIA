## B-fulani-3 — Beverly Mack — Board Review of "Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Nana Asma'u's poetry and prose; the *yan taru* system; women's education in the Sokoto Caliphate; women in West African Islamic intellectual traditions.

**Historical accuracy:**
- Nana Asma'u's birth date (1793, making her eleven at the time of the hijra in 1804) is correct.
- Her presence at the hijra is historically plausible and consistent with the community's character as a family migration, not a military campaign.
- The characterization of her multilingual output (Arabic, Fulfulde, Hausa) is correct. Her over sixty works are well-documented.
- The *yan taru* system is correctly described as an association of women teachers who traveled to rural communities. The identification marker (*malafa*) is correctly named.
- Her death date (1864) is correct.
- Her role as an educator and organizer is accurately described. She was not merely a poet; she was a builder of institutions.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter correctly presents women's participation in the Torodbe scholarly tradition as structural rather than exceptional. The Shehu's insistence on women's education is well-documented in his own writings, and the chapter reflects this.
- The inclusion of women students in Umaru Dallaji's compound ("the young women of the Torodbe households who studied Maliki law alongside the men, as the Shehu had insisted they must") is historically accurate. The Shehu wrote explicitly about the obligation to educate women.
- Maryam's presence at the hijra and her role in organizing care after Tabkin Kwotto, while not specifically documented in these terms, are consistent with the role of senior women in the Torodbe community.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- Nana Asma'u is presented as a structural figure, not a footnote. Her *yan taru* system is described as a parallel scholarly network, which is the correct analytical frame.
- Her appearance at the hijra as an eleven-year-old who would later write about the events establishes her as a witness, which is historically sound.
- The characterization of the *yan taru* as "the closest thing to the Shehu's original project" is a strong and defensible interpretive claim.
- The figures.md treatment of Asma'u is substantial and accurate.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter describes the *malafa* as "a distinctive cloth." It was more specifically a distinctive hat or head covering, not a cloth worn on the body. This should be corrected.
- The chapter says Nana Asma'u "watched all of this from within the Shehu's household." By her twenties, she was married and had her own household within the broader community. She was not merely a daughter watching from her father's house; she was an independent scholar with her own domestic establishment. The framing should be adjusted to reflect her adult status.
- The elegies mentioned in the chapter ("elegies for the jihad's martyrs") are correct but could be more specific. Her most famous elegy is for the battle of Gawakuke (1836), which is outside this chapter's timeframe. The chapter should not imply that the elegies were written during the period covered (1804-1817) when most were written later.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (3 minor corrections above)
