# Vikingalond — The Viking Age Opens

## I. The Keel

The ship was finished in spring.

They had built her through the winter in the boatshed above the tideline at Kaupang, working by firelight when the days shortened to nothing and the fjord froze from the shallows out. The keel was a single oak trunk, split and hewn, sixty feet of heartwood running from stem to stern without a join. The strakes were riven, not sawn, split along the grain so the wood kept its strength, and they were lapped one over the next and riveted with iron nails the smith had drawn from bog ore smelted in the autumn. The ribs were lashed to the strakes with spruce root, not nailed, so the hull could flex in open water, could ride the swells of the North Sea without fighting them. A ship that fights the sea breaks. A ship that bends with the sea lives.

The man who would captain her was a farmer. He held land in the district around the Oslofjord, enough to feed his household through the winter if the harvest was fair and the stockfish held, not enough to portion out to a second son or a third. He had a wife, a hall with a longfire down the center where the birch smoke hung in the rafters and the heat reached the sleeping benches on one side while the wind from the fjord crept under the door on the other. Six cows in the byre. The standing of a man who had gone i viking twice before and come back with silver. In the language of his people, he was a bonde, a free farmer, and the freedom was the thing that mattered. He owed no lord his service. He owed the jarl his attendance at the thing, the assembly where free men settled disputes and made law, and he owed the gods their share at the blot, the seasonal sacrifice. Beyond that, his choices were his own.

He stood at the door of the hall in the early light and looked at the fjord and the land between the rock walls and the water, and he counted what he had and what he did not have, and he chose to go west.

The reasons were the reasons that had sent his father west and his father's father before that: the fjord was narrow, the land was thin, the sons were several, and the ship could go anywhere the water went. The Nordmenn had been moving along the coasts and across the open water for generations. They had traded with the Frisians and the Franks. They had settled the Northern Isles, Orkney and Shetland, where Norse farmers already worked land that was flatter and more forgiving than anything the fjords could offer. They had sailed to the Baltic, to Birka and Hedeby, where furs and amber and walrus ivory moved south toward the markets of Christendom and east along the river routes toward the Abbasid silver economy.

What changed, in the years around 793 of the Christian reckoning, was not the impulse. What changed was the reach.

The ship made the reach possible. A broad-beamed knarr could carry cargo across the North Sea and back. But the ship the bonde had built was not a knarr. It was long, narrow, shallow-drafted, fast enough to cross from the Oslofjord to the Northumbrian coast in three days with a fair wind and light enough that forty men could carry it overland between rivers. The wool sail, woven tight and greased with fat, caught the wind that the oars alone could not match. Before the sail, the Norse were coastal. After the sail, they were oceanic.

On a morning in June of 793, the monks of Lindisfarne looked out from their island priory on the Northumbrian coast and saw ships on the horizon. The shape was wrong. These were not the blunt cargo vessels of the Frisian traders. These were long, narrow, low to the water, and they were moving fast.

Alcuin of York, writing afterward from the court of Charlemagne, called it a judgment from God. Never before, he wrote, had such terror appeared in Britain. The Church of Saint Cuthbert, the holiest site in Northumbria, had been torn open. Monks killed. Treasures taken. The altar stripped.

From the fjords, the story was simpler. Men had sailed west, found wealth that was undefended, and taken it. The monastery held silver plate, gilded book covers, fine metalwork. It sat on an island with no wall and no garrison. The monks had no weapons. The crossing had taken three days. The wind had been fair.

They would come back. They would come back every summer for the next sixty years, in larger numbers and to more places, following the coastlines south and west and east, raiding and trading and settling, until the map of northern Europe was rewritten by the routes their ships had opened. This is that story. It begins with the keel.

## II. The Fjord World

The bonde came home in autumn with silver, and the silver bought what the fjord could not grow.

The Oslofjord country was better land than most of the Norwegian coast. The fjord ran inland and the ground on either side was lower, gentler, with stretches of arable soil where barley and oats could be grown in the short season between the last frost and the first. But even here the farms were small, pressed between rock and water, and the margin between sufficiency and hunger was a bad harvest wide. To the west, where the mountains met the sea in the great fjords of Vestlandet, the arable strips narrowed to nothing and the farms perched on ledges above the water, catching what soil and sunlight the cliff walls allowed. A man could stand at the door of his hall and throw a stone into the fjord on one side and into the mountain on the other. The land did not invite expansion. It invited departure.

The rhythm of the year turned on two seasons. In winter, the dark came early and stayed. The fjord froze in the shallows, and the ice crept outward until only the deepest channel ran black and open. The household drew in. The longfire burned from one end of the hall to the other, and the smoke found its way out through the roof-hole or it did not, and the air inside was thick with it: birch smoke, the breath of the cattle in the byre at the far end, the smell of stored fish and tallow and the wool that the women spun by the fire's light. The smith worked iron in the forge-shed, beating out rivets and tools and the long nails that held a ship together. The children listened to the stories the old ones told, stories of gods and heroes and the dead who waited in Valhalla for the last battle, and the stories were the winter's work as much as the spinning was.

In spring the ice broke. The sound of it carried up the fjord, a slow cracking that meant the world was opening again. The ships came out of the boatsheds. The fields were planted. The cattle went to the summer pasture in the hills, the seter, where the grass grew thick in the long light and the women who went with the herds made butter and cheese in the mountain huts and did not come down until autumn. The men who were not needed on the farm looked at the ship and the sea and the season's possibilities.

A household had more hands than the land could use in summer, and the ship could take those hands somewhere that the hands could earn what the land could not provide. A man went i viking the way he went to the summer pasture. It was seasonal. It was rational. It required a ship, a crew, a captain, and a destination, and the destinations multiplied as the ships improved and the sailors learned the routes and the word came back, from crew to crew and hall to hall, of what lay waiting on the coasts to the south and west.

The thing assembly met in spring, before the sailing season opened. The free men of the district gathered at the thingstead, an open field or a hillside where the ground was flat enough to stand on and the voice could carry. There was no building. The law was spoken in the open air, where the sky and the gods could witness it. A man accused of theft stood before his neighbors and answered. A boundary dispute was settled by the testimony of men who had walked the ground. A killing was weighed: had it been done openly, with witnesses, or in secret? A killing done openly could be settled with silver. A killing done in secret was murder, and murder put a man outside the law, made him an outlaw, a man without protection whom anyone could kill without penalty.

The thing was not a court. It was an assembly of free men making decisions together, and the jarl who presided did not command. He proposed. The free men accepted or rejected. A jarl who lost the thing's confidence lost his authority, because his authority rested on the assembly's consent, and the consent could be withdrawn. The bonde who sailed west in summer came home to this: a system where his voice carried weight because he was free and armed and his neighbors could not afford to ignore him. The jarl needed the bonde's ship for the next season's expedition. The bonde needed the jarl's connections to dispose of the silver he brought back. The relationship was negotiated, not dictated.

The gods received their share at the blot, the sacrifice held at the turning points of the year. At the autumn blot, when the sailing season closed and the harvest was in, the animals were killed at the hov, the hall or the grove where the gods were honored. The blood was caught in bowls and sprinkled on the walls and on the people, and the meat was boiled in great cauldrons over open fires, and the smell of roasting horsemeat and the steam from the cauldrons mixed with the smoke of the sacred fire and hung in the air of the grove like weather. The gods were not abstractions. Thor was the thunder that cracked over the mountains in summer. Odin was the one-eyed wanderer who traded his eye for wisdom and hung on the wind-tree for nine nights to learn the runes. Freyr was the green that came back in spring, the force that made the barley grow and the cattle breed and the women's bellies swell. The Nordmenn did not believe in their gods the way a theologian believes in a doctrine. They lived with their gods the way they lived with the weather: as forces that were present, unpredictable, and had to be honored if you wanted to survive.

## III. The Ship and the Sea

The ship was the thing itself.

A man could farm without a ship. He could not go i viking without one. The ship was wealth, transportation, weapon, status, and at the end of a man's life, his grave. The Oseberg woman was buried in her ship, surrounded by the things she would need: sledges, a cart, textiles, kitchen implements, the bones of horses and dogs. The Gokstad man was laid in his ship with his weapons and his gaming pieces. The ship carried you through life and carried you out of it.

Building a ship required knowledge that lived in the hands. The shipwright chose the oak by looking at it standing: the curve of the trunk determined the curve of the keel. He split the trunk with wedges, working along the grain, because a riven plank is stronger than a sawn one. The grain runs unbroken from end to end. A saw cuts across the grain and weakens the wood at every cut. The Norse knew this not as theory but as practice, the way a body knows the difference between a good grip and a bad one. The strakes were shaped with an axe, not a plane. The adze marks are still visible on the planks recovered from Roskilde Fjord, each mark the signature of a specific stroke, a specific angle, a specific hand.

The clinker method meant that each strake overlapped the one below it, like the scales of a fish, and the overlap was riveted with iron nails driven through from outside and clenched over a small iron plate, a rove, on the inside. The hull breathed. It flexed. In a heavy sea, a clinker-built longship moved like a living thing, the planks sliding against each other at the rivets, absorbing the force of the waves rather than resisting it. A man lying in the hull could feel the sea through the wood, could feel the swells as a rhythm transmitted through the keel and up through the ribs into his back. The ship was not a container sitting on the water. It was part of the water. The crew and the sea spoke to each other through the hull.

Navigation was body-knowledge. The Norse had no compass, no charts, no instruments beyond their own senses and the accumulated experience of generations who had sailed these waters and come back to tell what they had learned. They read the wind: a northwesterly in summer meant fair crossing to Britain; a shift to the south meant weather coming. They read the waves: the deep Atlantic swell rolling from the west told them the open ocean was behind them; a confused chop with short intervals meant shallow water or a coast nearby. They read the sky: the position of the sun at noon gave them latitude; the color of the clouds at the horizon told them whether land was near, because clouds over land look different from clouds over open water, carrying the green reflection of fields or the white reflection of ice. They read the birds: gannets flew out from their nesting cliffs and returned to them, and a man who followed a gannet's evening flight followed it toward land. They read the sea itself: the color of the water shifted from the deep blue of the open ocean to the green-grey of coastal shallows, and a man who knew his waters could feel the change before he saw it.

The crossing from the Oslofjord to Lindisfarne was three days with a fair wind. Three days of open water, the land sinking behind and not rising ahead until the second day, when the Northumbrian coast appeared as a dark line on the western horizon. Three days during which the crew ate dried fish and flatbread and drank water from leather bags and relieved themselves over the side and slept in shifts on the deck boards with the spray wetting their cloaks. Three days during which the captain stood at the steering oar, a broad blade mounted on the starboard side at the stern, and held the ship's course by the feel of the water against the rudder and the angle of the wind on his face and the position of the sun or the stars overhead. The word starboard comes from styrbordi, the steering side. The language remembers what the body knew.

## IV. The Raids

They came back the next summer.

In 794, they struck Jarrow, the monastery on the Tyne where Bede had written his history of the English church. The monks at Jarrow had warning. The memory of Lindisfarne was a year old. Some of the raiders were killed on the beach, and a storm drove their ships onto the rocks. The Northumbrians took the survivors and killed them. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle recorded the event with satisfaction. But this was one landing on one beach. The coastline was long, and the Norse came from many fjords, and no warning system could cover every bay.

In 795, they reached Iona, the island off the western coast of Scotland where Columba had founded his monastery two centuries before. They reached the coast of Ireland in the same year. They struck Lambay Island, north of where Dublin would later stand. The Irish annals record them: sea-heathens, foreigners, dark and fair, coming in their ships.

The pattern was seasonal. The crews sailed in late spring when the weather turned and the wind was fair. They raided through the summer, striking coastal settlements, monasteries, trading ports. They returned in autumn before the winter storms closed the sea. The targets were not random. Monasteries held concentrated portable wealth: silver vessels, gold reliquaries, gilded book covers, the accumulated offerings of generations of the faithful. They sat on islands or headlands, accessible by water, undefended. The monks' defense had been the sanctity of the place, and sanctity meant nothing to men who honored different gods.

A man who went raiding shared the risk and shared the profit. The captain took a larger share. The crew divided the rest. Silver could be melted down, cut into pieces, weighed on a small balance that a man carried in his belt pouch. Hack-silver, the archaeologists call it: fragments of arm-rings, chopped-up coins, bits of plate, all valued by weight alone. A Norse raider did not care whose face was on a coin or what prayers a chalice had held. He cared what it weighed. The scales and weights found in Norse graves are as common as weapons, because the two activities were two ends of the same voyage.

And the same man who raided one coast might trade at the next. The distinction that the victims drew, between the pirate and the merchant, did not hold from the Norse side. A shipload of men approaching a settlement made a decision based on what they found. A strong town with armed men and a market? Trade. An undefended monastery with silver plate? Take it. A farmstead with livestock? Assess the risk. The word viking, in the Old Norse, meant the activity, not the identity. A man went i viking for a season. He came home a farmer.

The Frankish coast drew them south. In 799, raiders struck the monastery at Saint-Philibert on the island of Noirmoutier, at the mouth of the Loire. Charlemagne, who had spent decades conquering the Saxons and the Lombards and the Avars, now faced an enemy he could not reach. The Norse came by sea and left by sea, and a land army could not catch a fleet. He ordered coastal defenses built: watchtowers, beacon fires, ships of his own to patrol the river mouths. The Frankish Royal Annals record his frustration. The Emperor who had unified half of Europe could not keep fishing villages safe from men in boats.

By the 820s, the raids had become a regular feature of the summer. Fleets, not single ships. Organized expeditions led by men the Frankish sources called kings and the Norse sources might have called jarls or sea-kings, men whose authority came from their ability to attract crews and lead profitable expeditions. The self-reinforcing logic was clear: success attracted followers. Followers required a leader who could organize larger expeditions. Larger expeditions required more targets. More targets were found. The circle turned. Each season's success funded the next season's ambition.

In 834, a fleet struck Dorestad, the great Frankish trading town on the Rhine, still one of the wealthiest markets in northern Europe though the river was already silting beneath it. They struck it again in 835, 836, and 837. The repetition was the point. Dorestad was not a random target. It was the node where the Frankish economy met the North Sea trade, and the Norse knew it because they had been trading there for decades before they began raiding it. The knowledge that made them traders made them effective raiders. They knew the channels, the tides, the distances, the wealth.

The thralls were part of the cargo. The Norse traded in human beings with the same practical directness with which they traded in furs. A man captured in a raid on the Irish coast might be sold on the Volga to a Bulgar merchant who would sell him again in Baghdad. A woman taken from a Frankish farmstead might end up in the household of a Norse chieftain or on a ship heading east. The thrall was a category in the Norse social order, below the karl and the jarl, a person whose labor belonged to another. The sagas mention thralls the way they mention livestock: as property, without commentary, because within the Norse world this was how things were.

In Ireland, the pattern shifted earlier. By the 830s, the Norse had moved from summer raiding to winter camps. They built longphorts, fortified ship harbors, at points along the coast and on the river mouths. These were not settlements in the farming sense. They were forward operating bases from which raids could be launched deeper inland, upriver, into the heart of the Irish kingdoms. The longphort at Dublin, established in 841, would become a permanent town. The shift from raiding to wintering to settling was not a plan. It was an accumulation of decisions made by men who found that staying was more profitable than leaving.

## V. The Eastern Road

The other direction was the rivers.

While the western raids filled the chronicles of the English and the Franks and the Irish, the Norse were also moving east, and the eastern expansion was older, quieter, and in some ways more consequential. The eastern Norse, predominantly from the Swedish lands around Lake Malaren and the island of Gotland, had been trading into the Baltic and beyond since the mid-eighth century, before Lindisfarne, before the western raids had begun.

Staraya Ladoga, on the Volkhov River south of Lake Ladoga, was the first waypoint. The river was broad and flat, running through birch forest and marshland that smelled of peat and standing water, a different country from the fjords entirely. Archaeological layers show Scandinavian presence from the 750s: combs of Norse design carved from antler, glass beads from the workshops at Ribe, iron rivets of the type used in clinker-built hulls, fragments of the material culture of a people who had come by water and stayed. From Ladoga, the rivers led south. The Volkhov ran to Lake Ilmen and then to the headwaters of the Dnieper, which flowed south through the forests and the steppe to the Black Sea and Constantinople. The Volga ran southeast, through the lands of the Bulghars and the Khazars, to the Caspian Sea and the markets that connected to Baghdad and the Abbasid silver economy.

The portage was the price. Between the river systems, where the headwaters did not quite connect, the crew hauled the ship overland. They cut rollers from the forest, birch and pine, and laid them on the muddy ground and dragged the ship across, forty men heaving on ropes while the mosquitoes swarmed in the summer heat and the forest pressed in on both sides. The ship that could cross the North Sea in three days could also be carried through a forest, and this double capability was the thing that made the eastern road possible. A ship that could only sail or only be portaged was useless on the rivers of the Rus. The Norse ship could do both.

The goods moved along the rivers in a pattern that the Abbasid geographers documented. Ibn Rustah, writing in the early tenth century, described the Rus traders: they carried furs (sable, marten, fox, the pelts bundled and baled and smelling of the forests they came from), amber from the Baltic coast, walrus-tooth ivory, honey, wax, and slaves. They carried them south to the markets of Bulgar on the Volga bend, where the Volga Bulghars acted as middlemen, and to Itil, the Khazar capital at the Volga's mouth on the Caspian. The goods went south. The silver came north. Abbasid dirhams, minted in Baghdad and Samarkand and the cities of Central Asia, flowed up the river routes in quantities that filled the hoards Scandinavian farmers buried in their fields. Gotland alone has yielded more than 80,000 Islamic silver coins. A farmer on Gotland in the ninth century might own silver minted in a city he would never see, stamped with writing he could not read, valued by weight alone, cut into pieces with a chisel when the transaction required less than a whole coin.

The eastern traders and the western raiders were not separate peoples. They were the same people going different directions. A man from Vestfold might go west to the English coast one summer and east to the Volga the next, depending on the season, the crew, the opportunity. The Oseberg burial, from the Vestfold district, contained objects from the western trade (insular metalwork) and the eastern trade (silk, possibly of Central Asian origin). The network was a single network. The ship was the same ship. The distinction between east and west, between trader and raider, between viking and merchant, existed in the victims' records but not in the Norse world itself.

## VI. The Keel at Rest

By the middle of the ninth century, the world the bonde had known from his father's stories had been remade.

The seasonal raids had become sustained campaigns. The single ships had become fleets. The summer crossings had become winter camps, and the winter camps were becoming towns. In the east, the river routes had thickened into permanent trade arteries with Norse-founded stations at their nodes. In the west, the coastlines of Britain, Ireland, and Francia had been redefined by men who came from the sea and settled where the water met the land.

The next generation would bring the Great Heathen Army to England, the settlement of Normandy, the founding of the Althing in Iceland. The world was about to become larger still. But the mechanism was the same mechanism that had put the bonde's ship in the water at Kaupang in the spring of a year he did not number the way the monks did. The fjord was narrow. The sons were several. The ship could go anywhere the water went.

The ship came home in autumn. The crew hauled her up the beach above the tideline, the keel scraping on the gravel, and they turned her over and inspected the hull for damage, running their hands along the strakes, feeling for loosened rivets, for planks that had worked open in the sea. They tarred her. They covered her with hides against the rain and the snow that would come. The fjord would freeze again. The smith would beat out new rivets in the forge-shed, the sparks flying in the dark. The women would spin wool for the next season's sail. The stories would be told at the longfire, and the stories would be longer now, because there was more to tell.

The ship waited in the boatshed, dark and still, her keel resting on the blocks where the shipwright had laid it a year before. She would go out again in spring. The ice would break. The wind would turn. And the bonde, or his sons, would stand at the door of the hall and look at the fjord and count what they had and what they did not have, and they would choose.

The keel was ready.
