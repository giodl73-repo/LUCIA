# The Word That Bound

The rains had failed again. Ramnaresh sat in the doorway of his house, which was not his house. The zamindar owned the house, and the land it stood on, and the mango tree that had dropped its fruit into the dust of the yard, and in a sense the dust itself. Ramnaresh owned what he could carry. After the second failed monsoon, what he could carry did not include enough rice to feed three children through the winter.

The arkatti came in December.

He came on foot from the direction of Arrah, wearing a cotton shirt cleaner than anything in the village, and he sat on the charpoy under the neem tree and accepted water and spoke in Bhojpuri so clean it sounded like a man who had learned it for a purpose. He said there was work. He said the work was across the sea, in a place he called Doonidhad. Trinidad, he meant, but the word had changed shape passing through so many mouths that it no longer sounded like any place on a map. He said five years. He said wages: five rupees a month for a man, four for a woman, food and housing provided, passage paid. He said the word girmit.

Girmit. The word was agreement, the Bhojpuri mouth's way of holding the English word that would hold them. The contract had traveled from London to Calcutta to the sub-depot at Arrah to this doorstep, and at each stage it had shed something. In London it was a legal instrument with clauses and obligations on both parties. By the time it reached Shahabad, it was a word spoken by a man on a charpoy who said five years, five rupees, and the sea.

What the arkatti did not say was as precise as what he said. He did not say that the depot in Calcutta held families for weeks in conditions the colonial surgeon would later describe as requiring improvement. He did not say the ship crossed two oceans in ninety days and that the space allotted to each coolie, the word the British used, was less than the space allotted to a convicted prisoner on a transport vessel. He did not say that the five rupees a month would be docked for sickness, for absence, for refusal, for any reason the overseer chose to name. He did not say that the five years ended in a place ten thousand miles from this doorstep with no money for return passage and no provision for it. He did not say that the housing was a barracks and the food was rationed and the work was sugarcane, which had to be cut by hand in heat that made Bihar's summer feel like a cool morning by comparison.

He said five years. He said wages. He said girmit.

Ramnaresh listened. He was not a fool. He knew that men who promised easy money were not usually offering it. But he also knew that the zamindar's collector would return, and that the moneylender in Arrah charged interest that doubled the debt each season, and that his children would eat in the winter or they would not. He calculated. The calculation was rational and the information was incomplete, and this was the system's design: to arrive at the moment when a rational man had no rational choice but to sign.

He had a wife named Dulari and three children whose names the indenture records do not preserve. The records preserve this: male, age approximately thirty, wife, three dependents, origin Shahabad district, Bihar, registered at the Calcutta depot in January of 1881, assigned to the ship Doonidhad-bound, contract number among the hundreds processed that month. The number was the beginning of a new identity. In the colony they would be called by it. Not Ramnaresh. Not the son of his father. A number, assigned at a depot, attached to a contract written in a language he could not read, binding him to conditions he had not been told, for a term that would prove to be the rest of his life.

Dulari packed what could be carried. The brass lota for water. A small murti of Hanuman wrapped in cloth. A handful of turmeric tied in a rag, because turmeric was medicine and spice and the color of auspicious things, and wherever they were going they would need all three. Rice that would not last the journey to Calcutta, let alone the journey after. The Ramcharitmanas, Tulsidas's verses, not a bound book but a knowledge held in the body: she could sing the Sundarkand from memory, and she would sing it on the ship, and her daughters would learn it from her singing, and their children would sing it in a country that had no Ganga and no Ayodhya but would have, because of her, the verses that made those places real.

They walked out of Shahabad in January. The arkatti walked ahead. Behind them, the village continued. The zamindar's collector came again that month. Other families listened to other arkattis. Between 1838 and 1917, approximately 1.5 million people would make this walk, or one like it, from the villages of Bihar and eastern Uttar Pradesh and Tamil Nadu and elsewhere, toward depots and ships and contracts and places whose names they could not yet pronounce. They called themselves girmitiya: the people of the girmit, the people of the agreement, the people bound by a word.

The word bound them. But the word was not the whole of what they carried.
