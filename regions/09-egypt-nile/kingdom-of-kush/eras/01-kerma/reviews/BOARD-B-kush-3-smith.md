# BOARD Review — Stuart Tyson Smith

**Chapter:** Kingdom of Kush, Era 1: Kerma
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Cultural Dynamics Assessment

The chapter handles the Egyptian-Kerma relationship with appropriate nuance. The key interpretive claims are:

1. **Egyptian imports = trade, not submission.** Defensible. The Kerma tombs contain Egyptian objects alongside (not replacing) local production. This is consistent with prestige-goods acquisition through trade networks.

2. **Kerma pottery tradition as independently superior.** Defensible. The black-topped redware of the Classic Kerma period is widely regarded as among the finest hand-built ceramics in the ancient world. The comparison with Egyptian wheel-made pottery is fair and supported by the literature.

3. **Execration Texts as evidence of Egyptian fear.** Defensible. The Execration Texts are an Egyptian source that reveals Egyptian attitudes. The chapter correctly uses them as evidence about Egypt's perception of Kerma, not as evidence about Kerma itself.

4. **"Wretched Kush" as political formula.** Correct. This is a standard designation in Egyptian royal inscriptions that functions as a political categorization, not a description.

5. **Hyksos-Kerma alliance.** Appropriately cautious. The evidence for coordination between the Hyksos and Kerma against Thebes is found in the Kamose texts and is debated. The chapter's language ("suggests," "possibly") is appropriately hedged.

## Concerns

1. **The claim that "Kerma was a kingdom before Egypt knew it was there."** This is a strong claim. The archaeological evidence supports an early Kerma polity, but the dating of the earliest phase is debated, and the degree to which early Kerma constituted a "kingdom" (as opposed to a chiefdom or complex society) is not settled. Consider softening to "Kerma was old before Egypt took notice."

2. **Horn shaping.** The chapter states that cattle horns were "trained and shaped over years into sweeping curves." Horn-shaping practices are attested ethnographically in East African pastoral societies, and the Kerma burial record shows cattle with distinctively curved horns. However, it is not certain that the Kerma herders practiced deliberate horn-shaping as opposed to selective breeding for horn form. The distinction matters.

## Recommendation

**APPROVE with minor corrections.** The chapter's representation of Egyptian-Kerma cultural dynamics is accurate and nuanced. The two concerns are matters of scholarly precision that can be addressed with minor language adjustments.
