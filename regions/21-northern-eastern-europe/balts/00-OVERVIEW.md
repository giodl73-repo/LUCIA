# Lietuviai, Latvji, Prūsai — "Lithuanians, Latvians, Old Prussians"

We were the last pagans of Europe. When Poland converted in 966, when Bohemia converted in the tenth century, when the Norse kingdoms adopted Christianity in the early eleventh, the Baltic peoples — Lithuanians, Latvians, Estonians, Old Prussians — held to the old ways. The sacred oak groves, the eternal fires tended by priestesses, the *diemedžiai* (god-trees), the funeral rites involving horses and bronze goods: these were not the survivals of an archaic people unable to adapt. They were the deliberate choices of societies that had seen what happened to their neighbors when the Church arrived — the loss of customary law, the subordination of local authority to distant bishops, the military conquest that Christianity often preceded or followed. The Baltic peoples resisted because they understood what they were being offered.

The Grand Duchy of Lithuania at its fourteenth-century height was the largest state in Europe — stretching from the Baltic coast to the Black Sea, from present-day Poland to present-day Ukraine. It was built by pagan grand dukes who commanded Christian subjects without being Christian themselves, who welcomed Orthodox Rus princes into their state and gave them religious freedom, who defeated the Mongol successor states at the battle of the Blue Waters in 1362 and pushed the Muscovite and Tatar frontier eastward. When Jogaila finally accepted Christianity in 1386 to marry the Polish queen and secure the union with Poland, it was the last pagan empire in Europe converting on its own terms, for its own reasons, at a moment of its own choosing. This is not defeat. It is the sophisticated statecraft of a people who had survived everything the Christian world could send against them.

## What Defined Them

**The last pagan Europe — the oak grove and the eternal fire.** Lithuanian religious practice centered on the *romuva* — the sacred site where eternal fires were tended, where the priest (*vaidilutė* — a sacred fire-keeper) maintained the connection between the human world and the divine. The sacred oaks, the thunder god Perkūnas, the serpent (*žaltys*) kept as a household protector: these were not the remnants of a dying religion but the living practice of a society that had sufficient military and political strength to resist the conversion that the Teutonic Knights were imposing on their neighbors by the sword. The Lithuanian refusal to convert until 1386 — and even then, the conversion that came was a political act of Jogaila's, not a popular mass movement — preserved Baltic religious memory longer than anywhere else in Europe.

**The language that remembered everything.** Lithuanian is the most archaic living Indo-European language — it preserves forms that Latin, Greek, and Sanskrit have lost, and linguists use it to reconstruct Proto-Indo-European grammar. This conservatism is not simply a curiosity; it reflects a society that maintained its cultural forms over a very long period with unusual consistency. The Lithuanian language changed slowly because Lithuanian society was relatively stable and self-contained — not because it was isolated, but because it had strong reason to preserve what it had. The language itself is an archive.

**Mindaugas and the calculated conversion.** Mindaugas, who ruled Lithuania in the mid-thirteenth century and was the only Lithuanian ruler to be crowned king (1251), accepted Christianity, received the royal crown from Pope Innocent IV, and then effectively renounced it when the political advantage no longer outweighed the domestic resistance. He was later assassinated. The episode illustrates the Baltic political calculation: Christianity was a diplomatic tool, potentially useful, potentially ruinous, never simply a matter of personal faith. The Lithuanian rulers who resisted conversion were not irreligious — they were pragmatic about what Christianity's institutional demands would cost them.

**Gediminas and the open letter.** Grand Duke Gediminas sent letters to the cities of the Hanseatic League and to the Pope in 1323 inviting craftsmen, merchants, and clerics to settle in Lithuania, promising religious freedom to all comers. The letters survive, and they demonstrate a politically sophisticated ruler who understood that a diverse, commercially active population was more valuable than a religiously uniform one. Gediminas himself was pagan; his court included Orthodox Christians, Catholic priests, and Jewish merchants. This was not tolerance as an abstract principle — it was tolerance as economic and political strategy. The Grand Duchy was valuable precisely because it was a space outside the religious wars that were raging between the Teutonic Order and its pagan neighbors.

**Grunwald and the end of the Crusading Order.** The Battle of Grunwald (Žalgiris in Lithuanian, Tannenberg in German) on 15 July 1410 was one of the largest battles of medieval Europe — somewhere between 20,000 and 40,000 men on each side. The Polish-Lithuanian army destroyed the army of the Teutonic Knights, killing the Grand Master and most of his senior officers. The battle did not immediately end the Knights' political existence, but it permanently broke their military supremacy in the Baltic. Lithuania had defeated the crusade sent against it — the crusade that had been running for two hundred years. Grunwald is still celebrated as a national holiday in Lithuania and Poland.

## The Chapters

**1 — Old Lithuania:** A Baltic priest kindles the sacred fire at the romuva. The fire has been burning for as long as anyone can remember — there are stories of it burning for centuries, tended by the same lineage of keepers. The Teutonic Knights have destroyed the sacred groves of the Old Prussians to the west. The fire burns anyway.

**2 — The Great Struggle:** Mindaugas receives the papal legate and accepts baptism. He is a pagan chief who has just been told that a piece of paper from Rome will give him recognition as a king by every Christian ruler in Europe. He takes the paper and the baptism. He does not intend to change anything else.

**3 — The Age of Gediminas:** Gediminas reads the draft of his letter inviting German craftsmen to settle in Lithuania. His secretary has written it in Latin. Gediminas does not read Latin — he rules a state where three languages and three religions coexist, and he speaks them all, but Latin is the Church's language and he is not the Church's ruler. He approves the letter anyway. Craftsmen are useful. The language they use is not his business.

**4 — The Grand Duchy at its Height:** Vytautas the Great surveys the territory of the Grand Duchy from Trakai. It stretches, in principle, from the Baltic to the Black Sea. He controls more land than any other ruler in Europe. He also controls fewer of the people on that land than any Western king controls: he rules over Orthodox Rus princes, Catholic Polish nobles, Tatar communities, Jewish merchants, and Lithuanian pagans, each with their own law and their own loyalty. He is governing a negotiation, not a state.

**5 — Conversion and Union:** Jogaila is baptized and takes the name Władysław. He is the Grand Duke of Lithuania, and he is being baptized in the Kraków cathedral to marry the Polish queen. Outside the cathedral, his court is calculating the advantages: papal protection, Teutonic Order neutralized, Polish military support against Muscovy. The calculation checks out. The last pagan empire of Europe converts not because of faith but because of politics. This does not make the conversion less real.
