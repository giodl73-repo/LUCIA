# The Model Parliament
## Era 4 — Plantagenet England, 1272–1327 CE

---

The writ went out in the king's name, as writs did. The clerk who copied it had copied a hundred like it. The formula was old. But one phrase had not appeared in quite this form before, and the clerk who read it aloud in the chancery that autumn of 1295 paused, perhaps, at the words, or perhaps he did not. The men in the chancery dealt in phrases. This phrase would be remembered.

*Quod omnes tangit ab omnibus approbetur.* What touches all must be approved by all.

Edward I needed money. He needed it for Wales, already taken. He needed it for Scotland, not yet taken. He needed it for Gascony, held by exhaustion and negotiation against the French king's appetite. He had been king for twenty-three years. He understood that money came from taxation, that taxation required consent, that consent required assembly, and that assembly required the right bodies in the room. The barons and bishops had always been there. What had not always been there, what was not reliably there, not yet, were the knights of the shires and the citizens and burgesses of the towns.

He put them in the summons. He needed the money enough to put them in the summons.

The hall at Westminster that November held a different kind of assembly than England had seen reliably before. The great lords in their fur-lined robes, the bishops and abbots in their vestments, and at the far end of the hall, in the colder air where the ceremony was thinner, the knights of the shires and the men from the boroughs. Merchants, some of them. Men who kept accounts and knew the price of a bolt of cloth and the cost of a road toll. Men who had never been to Westminster and might never come again. Men doing their duty to their county, their borough, their king, in a hall where the Latin of the summons rang off the stone and carried further in the cold than any voice.

This is how constitutional precedents are made. Not by vision. By need.

## I. The Legal Edifice

Robert Burnell had been dead three years by the time the Parliament of 1295 was called. He had been Edward's chancellor for eighteen years, a churchman of substantial vices and incomparable administrative talent, the man who had spent those years converting the king's habits of mind into permanent legal form. He was a Bishop of Bath and Wells who had been twice proposed for the Archbishopric of Canterbury and twice rejected, the first time because he had illegitimate children, the second time because his financial dealings were irregular enough that the papacy would not sanction him. He served as chancellor anyway, because Edward needed him and because Burnell, whatever else he was, knew how a legal system should be built and what it needed to run. He was the kind of man history tends to forget: the architect whose name does not appear on the building.

The building was the common law made statutory. In the first decade of Edward's reign, Burnell drafted or oversaw the drafting of three statutes that together remade the legal landscape of England. The Statute of Westminster of 1275, with its fifty-one chapters, took the customs that had accumulated through two centuries of royal court practice: the writs, the assizes, the forms of action that Bracton had catalogued a generation earlier, all written into law that could be cited, enforced, and appealed. The Statute of Gloucester in 1278 restricted certain legal actions to the royal courts, pulling jurisdiction out of the baronial and ecclesiastical courts that had always competed with the crown. The Statute of Winchester in 1285 organized the local machinery of peace-keeping, the watch and ward, the assizes of arms, the obligation of every free man to maintain weapons for the defense of his county, into a system the county courts could administer.

These were not noble experiments. They were solutions to known problems, drafted by men who knew the law from the inside and understood what the courts needed to function. The common law was not a code descended from philosophers. It was a practice that had grown case by case through two centuries of royal justice, and by Edward's reign it had grown too large and too various to navigate without written guides. The statutes gave it structure without killing it. The law remained, as it had always been, a body of decided cases. The statutes told the courts how to read them. They organized the precedents without replacing them. The common law continued to grow through the decisions of actual cases argued and decided after 1275 as it had before, but now it grew in a more orderly landscape.

A clerk named Roger of Seaton, sitting in a county town hall on an autumn morning in 1279, had a case before him. Two men were disputing the ownership of twenty acres in Lincolnshire. One claimed he had been unjustly dispossessed. He had obtained a writ of novel disseisin from the chancery, a standard form, available for a fee, initiating a royal assize. Roger read the writ, read the case, identified the relevant prior judgments in the rolls he carried with him on circuit, and gave his decision. He did not consult the king. He consulted the precedents. The losing party appealed. The appeal went to Westminster. A record was made.

This was the system working as it was designed to work. The justice was royal in origin, common in application, professional in its practitioners. The writ that Roger carried and the rolls he consulted were the products of the chancery's accumulation of two centuries of decided cases. Roger was not applying the king's will. He was applying the law that the king's courts had built, case by case, across the reigns of Henry II, Richard I, John, and Henry III. The king himself, had he appeared before Roger's court on a question of land title, would have stood under the same procedure. This was not theory. This was practice. Bracton had described the theory later, to explain what was already being done.

That record, along with thousands like it, became the year books: the first systematic collection of English case law, compiled and circulated among the growing body of trained lawyers who made their living arguing before the common law courts. The year books were not official publications. They were practitioners' notes, copied and shared, organized and commented upon by men who needed to know what had been decided before and why. They were the common law's own knowledge system, knowledge that grew by the accumulation of decided cases, that could be cited in the next argument, that was always already in motion.

Bracton had written, a generation before Edward's reign, the phrase that the common law's practitioners kept returning to: *Rex non debet esse sub homine, sed sub Deo et lege.* The king ought not to be under man, but under God and the law. Bracton was not writing constitutional theory. He was describing what he understood the common law courts to be doing, applying a body of legal principle to which even the king was subject. The king's judges were applying the king's law. But the law, once written down, cited, and precedented, was no longer simply the king's.

Edward understood this. He had built the machine. He knew what the machine could do, and he knew what it could do to him if he pushed it wrong. He was careful. He litigated. He used the law as a tool for extending royal authority. The *quo warranto* inquiries were the visible instrument of this: What warrant, by what right, do you exercise this jurisdiction over your tenants? The lords who had inherited their jurisdictions from the Conquest were told to produce charters. Those who could not were stripped of their private courts.

The campaign ran from 1278, the royal justices riding to every county, sitting in judgment over the lords' claims to high and low jurisdiction, to market tolls and gallows and wreck of the sea. Some lords came with charters. Some came with witnesses who would swear to immemorial custom. The royal lawyers replied with the court's most withering formulation: the Devil himself had been in continuous possession since time immemorial, and that had not given him a charter. A charter, or nothing. Many lords settled for less than they had held. The royal courts' jurisdiction expanded correspondingly. The legal machinery used to discipline the lords was the same machinery that one day would be used to discipline the king. Edward did not see this. He was busy using it.

## II. De Montfort's Inheritance

Thirty years before the Parliament of 1295, in August 1265, on a day the chroniclers noted was particularly hot and close, Simon de Montfort, Earl of Leicester, was caught in a loop of the River Avon near the town of Evesham. He had built a Parliament that included knights and burgesses. He had governed England for nine months as the effective regent of a captive king. He had made enemies among the great lords who did not like the direction this governance was taking, and he had made an enemy of the king's son, Prince Edward, who had escaped capture and proved himself, in the months since, to be a military commander of the first order.

At Evesham, the prince closed the loop. He had positioned his forces on the hills above the town, cutting off every road north. De Montfort's cavalry broke against the hills. The infantry was trapped in the river bend. By the end of the day, de Montfort was dead, and his body was mutilated on the field before it was quartered for distribution. Head, hands, feet, each sent to a different lord as a statement about what happened to men who overreached. The chroniclers wrote it down. They were appalled. The law of chivalry was supposed to protect even enemies from this. Edward, who would become in a decade England's great legislating king, stood over the field and did not intervene.

What the chroniclers could not know was that de Montfort had already done what he needed to do. The Parliament of January 1265 had been called and had met. The knights of the shires and the burgesses of the towns had ridden to Westminster and taken their seats in the assembly. The precedent existed in the parliamentary record: an assembly of barons, bishops, knights, and burgesses, convened by a governing authority to obtain consent and hear petitions. De Montfort's body could be distributed across the Midlands. The parliamentary form he had used was enrolled in the chancery and would be cited again.

The Parliament de Montfort had called in 1265 was different from Edward's Parliament of 1295 in one crucial respect: it was an instrument against the king, not for him. De Montfort needed the support of the commons to legitimate a government that the great lords were beginning to desert. He called the knights and burgesses not because they had a right to be there but because he needed their political weight. The form was the same. The purpose was inverted.

Edward, inheriting the throne seven years later, inherited the form along with the crown. He did not invent the expanded Parliament. He found it in the record of his father's reign and his own early years, used it when he needed it, and used it in November 1295 in a configuration not assembled quite this way before: magnates, bishops and abbots, knights of the shires, citizens and burgesses, all summoned together, all asked to consent to a unified tax.

What made 1295 constitutionally generative was not the presence of the commons. Earlier parliaments had included knights and burgesses. It was the unity of the consent. All three estates, magnates, clergy, and commons, were asked together, in the same assembly, to approve a tax that would fall on all three. *Quod omnes tangit.* The formula named what it was doing. The tax touched all. All approved it. The assembly that approved it was, in that moment, the realm. Not a part of the realm petitioning the king. The realm consenting.

No one in the hall stated it that way. But the clerk wrote the formula in the writ, and the writ was enrolled, and the enrollment was cited in the next summons and the one after that. The case had been decided. The year book made it available.

## III. The Conquered and the Unconsumed

Edward's Parliament was assembled to pay for wars. The men who rode from the shires to grant the king his money were granting it, in 1295, for two purposes: the defense of Gascony against the French king, and the ongoing effort to subdue Scotland. Wales was already finished, finished in the sense that the legal work was done, the conquest complete, the machinery of English administration in place.

The conquest of Wales had taken two major campaigns, 1277 and 1282–84, and a level of castle-building that was among the most ambitious of the medieval period. Harlech, Conwy, Caernarfon, Beaumaris: fortresses placed at strategic points along the Welsh coast and river valleys, garrisoned by English soldiers, supplied by English ships. The castles were the military solution. The legal solution was the Statute of Rhuddlan, issued in 1284.

The Statute of Rhuddlan imposed English legal forms on the new counties of north and west Wales. This meant not simply a change of rulers but a change of legal systems. Wales had its own law, the Laws of Hywel Dda, compiled in the tenth century, organized by custom and community rather than by royal writ, regulating property, kinship, and obligation through principles that had governed Welsh communities for centuries. The Statute of Rhuddlan did not acknowledge the Laws of Hywel Dda in the new county courts. It introduced the English writs, the English county structure, the English judicial forms. Where Welsh law had treated certain offenses as matters for compensation between kinship groups, English law made them capital crimes. The displacement was real, though not immediate and not complete. Welsh legal customs persisted in practice for decades in many jurisdictions, and Welsh scholars continued to copy and study the laws of Hywel Dda in manuscript long after the Conquest. The tradition went underground. It did not disappear.

The knights who rode to the Parliament of 1295 from the new English counties of Wales were not Welsh. The Statute of Rhuddlan had reorganized the region into counties administered by English-appointed officials. The Welsh who appeared in the new courts appeared under English procedure. They had not been asked to consent to the law that now governed them. The formula, what touches all must be approved by all, did not reach to Gwynedd.

Scotland was different because Scotland had not yet been conquered. The Scottish nobility had its own traditions: their own Parliament, their own legal customs, their own king who was currently in an English prison (John Balliol, deposed by Edward in 1296, a year after the Model Parliament). The Scottish campaign was long, expensive, and ultimately inconclusive. Edward won battles. He garrisoned castles. He appointed English administrators to Scottish offices. He took the Stone of Destiny, on which Scottish kings had been crowned at Scone for centuries, and brought it to Westminster Abbey. He understood symbolic action. What he could not do was make the Scots accept that their kingdom had been absorbed into his.

The resistance was not simply military. It had a legal and theological articulation that Edward's lawyers could not simply override. The men who wrote the Declaration of Arbroath in 1320, fifteen years after Edward's death, drew on the same Roman legal principle that the English chancery had used in the 1295 summons. *Quod omnes tangit ab omnibus approbetur* had been in the canonical texts for centuries, available to any tradition that read Latin, and the Scots gave it a sharpness the English Parliament had not yet found: "For it is not for glory nor riches nor honour that we fight, but for freedom alone, which no good man gives up except with his life." The Scots had a theory of consent, a theory of freedom, a theory of legitimate governance. Edward's Parliament financed the war against it.

The Plantagenet legal mind did not judge this. The law was English. Scotland was being made English. Wales had been made English. The Parliament was the instrument through which the realm consented to the cost. The realm, as the Parliament understood it, did not include the Welsh or the Scots.

## IV. The Machine Learns

Between 1295 and Edward I's death in 1307, Parliament was called nine times. Not every assembly included the full three-estate configuration; some were predominantly clerical, some predominantly magnate. But the pattern of the 1295 summons became increasingly the norm. What was happening in those nine parliaments, beneath the formal business of taxation and petition, was that the participants were learning what Parliament could do.

The most important development was the common petition. In the early parliaments, counties and boroughs submitted individual grievances: the sheriff is abusing his office, the purveyors are taking goods without payment, the road to market is being blocked by a lord who has enclosed the common land. These petitions were answered (or not) by the king's council on an individual basis. Gradually, the knights and burgesses began to coordinate. Grievances that appeared in multiple petitions from multiple counties were combined into common petitions, single documents presenting a grievance shared by the commons as a whole, requesting a remedy that would apply across the realm.

The common petition was the moment the commons became a political body. An individual county's complaint could be dismissed as a local matter. A common petition, endorsed by the knights of forty shires and the burgesses of fifty boroughs, could not. It represented a constituency. The king's council, answering a common petition, was for the first time engaging with the commons as a collective rather than as an aggregate of individual subjects.

The issues that generated common petitions were not constitutional abstractions. They were practical grievances rooted in the mechanics of royal governance. Purveyance: the royal right to requisition goods and labor for the king's household and army. The purveyors who rode ahead of the king's court, taking grain, horses, and carts at prices they set themselves, were among the most resented instruments of royal power in the countryside. The commons petitioned against purveyance repeatedly. The king answered that it was a royal prerogative. The commons answered that it was causing hardship. The negotiation continued. Over decades, the commons learned that Parliament was the place where this negotiation happened and that consent to taxation was their leverage.

The leverage was not always used consciously. The men who sat in the commons in the 1290s and 1300s were not strategic actors constructing a constitutional framework. They were men of their counties and their boroughs doing their duty to the king while also doing their duty to their constituents. But the practice of withholding final consent until grievances were addressed began to appear in the parliamentary record. The form was not yet fixed. The precedent was being made.

Alongside the common petitions, something else was accumulating: the legal profession. The common law courts' expansion under Edward I had created a class of trained lawyers who made their livelihoods from the courts' authority. Serjeants-at-law argued before the King's Bench and Common Pleas. Attorneys managed cases for clients who could not appear themselves. Apprentices learned the forms at the feet of established practitioners and in the informal legal colleges that were beginning to coalesce around the Inns of Court in London. These men had professional interest in the principle that the king was under the law. Not because they were constitutionalists. Because their practices depended on the courts' independence from arbitrary royal interference. A king who could override the common law courts' decisions could also override the lawyers' livelihoods. The lawyers were, without intending it, a constituency for the rule of law.

A knight named Henry de Cobham, elected for Kent in the Parliament of 1306, sat through the deliberations with the county's instructions in his memory: a complaint about local sheriffs, a request about road maintenance, a question about military service obligations. He presented what he was asked to present. He voted with the commons to grant the king the taxation requested. He received, at the Parliament's close, a letter addressed to the county of Kent summarizing the common petitions and the king's responses. He carried it home. It was read aloud at the county court in Canterbury. The process was complete.

Henry de Cobham did not know he was practicing constitutional government. He was doing his job.

## V. The Engine's Consequence

Edward I died in 1307 on his way to another Scottish campaign, which tells you what kind of king he was. He had built a legal edifice that would govern England for centuries, called a Parliament that would become the template for representative assemblies, conquered Wales and failed to permanently conquer Scotland, expelled the Jews from England in 1290 in an act of confiscation that his legal machinery processed smoothly, and died moving north at sixty-eight, unwilling to stop. He was buried at Westminster. His tomb bore the inscription *Scottorum malleus*, the Hammer of the Scots. The Scots survived him by several centuries. The Parliament he built survived him longer.

His son Edward II inherited the legal edifice, the Parliament, the Scottish war, and a court faction problem that would destroy him. Edward II was not suited for the particular demands of his reign. He was neither militarily competent nor politically shrewd. He preferred the company of favorites, Piers Gaveston first, then the Despensers, to the counsel of the magnates who expected to advise the king. The magnates used the Parliament to respond.

In 1311, the Lords Ordainers, a committee of magnates appointed by Parliament, issued the Ordinances: a set of requirements that Edward accept baronial control over royal appointments, foreign policy, and the conduct of the Scottish war. The Ordinances required that Parliament meet at least once a year, that the king's appointments be confirmed by the magnates, and that Gaveston be exiled. Edward accepted, revoked, accepted again. Gaveston was captured and executed by the earls of Lancaster and Warwick in 1312 without trial. The magnates had used Parliament to discipline the king's behavior. Parliament had not sanctioned the execution. The law of the land, if applied, would not have allowed it. But the power to assemble had become the power to assert.

The final consequence arrived in 1327. Isabella of France, Edward's queen, had been negotiating with Mortimer, the great Marcher lord who had escaped from the Tower of London and been living in France. In 1326, Isabella and Mortimer landed with a small army. The Despensers were executed. Edward II was captured. He was not dead. He was the king.

The problem was how to remove a living king without making his removal look like simple murder and usurpation. The solution was Parliament. A Parliament was assembled in January 1327. It did not formally depose Edward II; it was not clear that a Parliament had the authority to depose a king, and no one wanted to establish that precedent too nakedly. Instead, Parliament was used to convey to Edward II a series of articles of accusation and to receive his renunciation of the throne, which he gave, weeping by the accounts, before a parliamentary commission that had ridden to Kenilworth to receive it. Parliament then accepted the renunciation and acclaimed his son Edward III. The removal was complete by consent, or by the form of consent. The Parliament gave the transition its legitimacy.

What had happened was not parliamentary sovereignty in any fully articulated sense. Mortimer had the army. Isabella had the political coalition. Parliament had neither. But the need to pass this transition through a parliamentary form, to assemble the magnates, the clergy, and the commons and have them witness the renunciation, demonstrated that Parliament had become, in the fifty years since the Model Parliament was called, the institution that gave major political events their legitimacy. You could not do this without Parliament. Not because Parliament would stop you. Because without Parliament the thing would not be legitimate.

Edward I needed the money enough to put the knights and burgesses in the summons. He had no idea what he was summoning.

## VI. What the Formula Made

The hall at Westminster in November 1295 was cold. The great lords sat nearest the king, where the candles were thick and the fire nearest. The knights and burgesses sat at the far end, in the colder air, at the longer distance from power. They had ridden from their counties. They carried their grievances and their instructions. They sat through the ceremony of the summons being read aloud in Latin, *quod omnes tangit ab omnibus approbetur*, in a language most of them did not fully command, in a formula written by a chancellor who had been dead three years.

They granted the king his taxes. They presented their counties' petitions. They received, before they left, the king's answers rolled into parchment: sealed, addressed to the county court, carried home in the saddlebag against the winter road. At Canterbury, at York, at Lincoln, at Bristol, the Parliament's results arrived and were read aloud and acted upon. The institution reached backward into the shires.

The formula that Edward's clerk had written into the writ was not a theory. It was a procedure. It became, through repetition, a custom. It became, through challenge and confirmation, a law. It became, through Edward II's renunciation and Edward III's accession, the ground on which legitimate power in England stood.

The men who gathered in November 1295 had not been building anything they could name. They had been doing what men of their station did: the magnates advising, the bishops praying and advising, the knights representing their counties, the burgesses representing their towns. Each group had a function in the hierarchy of the realm and a form for performing that function. The form was parliamentary. The function was consent. What they could not see was that form and function, practiced often enough, become institution. The institution, once it exists, does not require anyone to believe in it. It requires only that it be used.

The writ still went out. The knights still rode. The formula still read: what touches all must be approved by all. No one had given Parliament this authority. No one had needed to. It had accumulated it, case by case, Parliament by Parliament, petition by petition, in exactly the way the common law accumulated its knowledge: through decided instances, enrolled and cited, available for the next argument.

The king is under God and the law. Bracton had written it. Edward I had used it. Edward II had discovered what it meant.
