# North America

North America is not one land but a stack of worlds running from the Arctic ice to the Gulf shore, from the Pacific redwood coast to the Atlantic pine barrens. What holds it together as a region for this chronicle is not shared geography — the continent is too vast for that — but the fact that its peoples were in contact with one another, and that contact left traces: in the spread of maize agriculture northward along river valleys, in the copper that moved from the Great Lakes to the Gulf Coast and back, in the ceramic and iconographic connections between Cahokia's mound-builders and the cultures of the lower Mississippi, in the horse that changed the Great Plains nations within a century of its introduction. This is a continent where goods, ideas, and peoples moved — sometimes slowly across generations, sometimes with revolutionary speed.

The region's dozen peoples here represent the major cultural and geographic zones of the continent's pre-contact world: the mound-building urban traditions of the Mississippi Valley, the confederacy diplomacy of the northeastern woodlands, the agricultural pueblos of the arid southwest, the bison-following nations of the Great Plains, the maritime cultures of the northwest coast, the forest peoples of the boreal north, and the ice-adapted peoples of the Arctic. They do not all know one another directly. But they share a continent whose resources and ideas moved between them across networks of trade, war, diplomacy, and migration that are still being mapped. To write any one of them well, you must know where they sit in relation to all the others.

Key features:
- Mississippi River system: the continent's central artery; Cahokia and the Mississippian world built at its confluence with the Missouri and Illinois rivers
- Great Lakes (the Sweetwater Seas): the Anishinaabe and Haudenosaunee world; source of copper that entered trade networks as early as 3000 BCE
- Great Plains: the bison-following world; transformed by the horse (reintroduced from Spanish stock after 1680) into a new mode of life for Lakota, Comanche, and others
- Colorado Plateau and Rio Grande valley: the Pueblo and Diné world; the most arid inhabited landscape on the continent, made livable through extraordinary water management
- Northwest Coast: the rainforest shore where salmon abundance and red cedar created a surplus economy without agriculture; Haida and Tlingit potlatch as the organizing institution
- Boreal forest: the Cree and Anishinaabe homeland; the canoe as the technology that made it navigable and the fur-trade economy that would later transform it

## How They Connect

Copper is the oldest evidence of long-distance connection on this continent. Great Lakes copper — hammered cold into tools and ornaments — appears in burial contexts from the Gulf Coast to the Atlantic seaboard as early as 3000 BCE, in what archaeologists call the Old Copper Complex. The routes copper followed were also routes for everything else: shell from the Gulf, obsidian from the Rockies, catlinite (sacred pipe stone) from the Minnesota quarries. By the time Cahokia rose around 1050 CE, it sat at the hub of a trade network that connected the Great Plains, the southeast, and the Great Lakes, and its cosmological symbolism — the Birdman figure, the Morning Star ceremony, the cross-in-circle motif — spread along those same routes into the Mississippian world of the southeast. The Haudenosaunee in the northeast, the Cherokee and Creek in the south, and the Muscogee towns of the Gulf plain all share iconographic and ceremonial elements that trace back to this Cahokian moment.

The Haudenosaunee Great Law, attributed to the Peacemaker and Hiawatha (Aiionwatha), brought the five nations of the original confederacy — Mohawk (Kanien'kehá:ka), Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, and Seneca — into a single council fire around 1450 CE (with Tuscarora joining in the early eighteenth century). The Great Law was not just a diplomatic achievement; it was a philosophy of governance that distinguished between the power to act in war and the obligation to seek peace first. It governed not through coercion but through consensus — unanimous agreement required at the Grand Council. The confederacy's influence spread through alliance, threat, and diplomacy across the northeastern woodlands, and its principles would later inform (and in some cases directly instruct) the framers of the United States Constitution.

The horse, reintroduced to the continent by Spanish colonists in the southwest after 1598, reached the Great Plains cultures through a cascade of exchange and raiding that moved north and east within a century. The Comanche acquired horses from Pueblo peoples by the 1680s and within a generation had built Comancheria — an enormous, horse-mounted trading and raiding power that controlled the southern Plains and kept Spanish Texas and New Mexico at bay for over a century. The Lakota reached the Black Hills and the northern Plains through a comparable transformation, abandoning forest and wild rice country for the bison-following life that the horse made possible. The Crow, Cheyenne, Arapaho, and Blackfoot underwent similar reorganizations. Within a century, what outsiders would misread as ancient Plains nomadic culture was in fact a rapid and deliberate adaptation — peoples choosing a new way of life and making it their own with extraordinary speed.

The northwest coast peoples — Haida, Tlingit, Tsimshian, Kwakwaka'wakw — built a regional economy on salmon abundance and red cedar without ever adopting agriculture. The potlatch (a word from Chinook jargon, but the institution was ancient in each nation's own terms) was the mechanism that kept wealth moving: a chief who hosted a potlatch gave away significant property to guests, and in doing so established his claim to the name, rights, and status he was asserting. The guests witnessed, and by accepting gifts they acknowledged the claim. This was law made visible through generosity. Trade connections ran south to the Columbia Plateau and north to Alaska, and a shared visual vocabulary — formline art, the family crest system, the conventions of ceremonial regalia — stretched across the entire coast despite the many distinct languages and polities it encompassed.

## Cross-References

- Mesoamerica (Region 01) — maize agriculture moved north from Mesoamerica along river valleys, reaching the southwest by c. 2000 BCE and the Mississippi Valley by c. 100 CE; the Mississippian ball game and platform mound traditions show direct Mesoamerican parallels, likely transmitted through Gulf Coast exchange
- Andes & Western South America (Region 02) — no direct contact; copper-working traditions developed independently in both regions
- Amazonia, Caribbean & Lowland South America (Region 03) — the Arawak-speaking Taino of the Caribbean had contact with the Florida peninsula and Gulf Coast peoples; the northernmost reach of Mesoamerican and Caribbean networks overlaps with the southernmost reach of the Mississippian world
- Americas Reborn (post-contact) — European contact, beginning in the Caribbean after 1492 and reaching the North American continent through multiple vectors (Spanish southwest, French northeast, English Atlantic coast), transforms every people described here between 1500 and 1900 CE
