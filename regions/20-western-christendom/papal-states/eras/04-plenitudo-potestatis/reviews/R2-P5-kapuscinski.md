# R2-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński (PANEL-2, targeted)

**Chapter:** Papal States — Plenitudo Potestatis
**Stage:** PANEL-2 — Notes review
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The notes promise what I asked for: one physical anchor in the revised opening, Innocent III with a body, and the Anagni scene as the chapter's most physical moment. I want to hold the author to all three.

For the Anagni scene: September 7, 1303. Boniface VIII is at his summer palace at Anagni, the town of his birth. Guillaume de Nogaret arrives with armed men — the numbers vary in the sources, but the core is clear: men with weapons, a pope in his robes, a confrontation in a room. The chronicles describe Boniface as maintaining his dignity, sitting enthroned in full papal vestments, telling them to do what they came to do. He was in his seventies. He had been pope for nine years. He was seized on a Friday and held until Sunday, when the people of Anagni rose and freed him.

He returned to Rome. He died thirty-five days later.

The chapter should be in that room. Not through a modern historian's account of what "happened" — through the physical reality of the space: a palace room in September heat, the old man in white, the soldiers who did not quite know what to do with him once they had him. The sources record that Nogaret and Sciarra Colonna (the Italian noble who accompanied him) argued about what to do — Colonna reportedly wanted to kill him, Nogaret restrained this. Boniface reportedly offered his neck.

Whether or not the "slap of Anagni" occurred (the chronicle sources are divided), what occurred was extraordinary enough: the man who held the fullness of power, who had written that every human being's salvation required submission to him, sat in a room full of soldiers and was held there for two days. That is the chapter's terminal image. That is where the legal-public voice should land with its full weight: declarative, quiet, irresistible.

The priest in northern France I also want. Not named — the documents don't give us his name — but present. The annual confession requirement is the most intimate and the most far-reaching act of the Fourth Lateran Council. It reaches further than any crusade: into every household in Latin Christendom, into the private space between a human being and the person who now, by law, must hear them once a year. The chapter should feel this reach. The priest who now has this obligation is the machine's other end.

**Verdict:** The notes have all the right pieces. The chapter must execute the sensory moments — the Lateran hall, the Anagni room, the parish confession booth — with the same precision the legal argument receives. Neither sensory nor intellectual should outweigh the other. They should carry the same weight, in the same register.
