# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe

**Chapter:** Kingdom of Kush, Era 1: Kerma — The Kingdom at the Third Cataract
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (Opening Review)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Whose Voice?

This is the right question, and for once I can answer it quickly: the voice belongs to the culture. The opening does not explain Kerma to anyone. It does not translate Kerma into Egyptian terms or European terms. It describes what the people of Kerma did and lets their actions carry their own meaning. The cattle have meaning because the text gives them meaning from inside the world, not because the text explains their significance to an outside reader. The retainer sacrifice is presented without apology and without spectacle. This is the Achebe Test, and the opening passes it.

## What Works

**The retainer paragraph is the most important paragraph in the opening, and it is handled with exactly the right discipline.** Retainer sacrifice is the moment where a less careful writer would have stumbled. The temptation is either to linger (giving the modern reader's discomfort too much space) or to explain (finding a reason that makes it palatable). This opening does neither. It describes what happened. It notes what the evidence shows. It draws a boundary around what we cannot know. "The distinction between those two things belongs to them and not to us." That sentence is the entire editorial philosophy of this project in fourteen words.

**The deffufa paragraph refuses the derivative framing without being defensive about it.** "It was not an imitation of a pyramid. It shared nothing with the structures rising at that same moment six hundred miles to the north except the ambition to make power visible." This is how you establish independence: not by arguing for it, but by describing what was there and letting the reader see for themselves. The comparison to Egypt appears and is immediately dissolved. Kerma is its own center.

**"The troublesome southerners had been burying their kings in mounds visible from the river for five hundred years."** The phrase "troublesome southerners" is borrowed from the Egyptian perspective and turned back on itself. The irony is quiet and effective: the Egyptians wrote about Kush as a problem, but the opening shows that Kush had been a kingdom long before Egypt noticed. This is a technique the chapter should carry forward: when the Egyptian perspective appears, let it reveal Egypt's limitations, not Kerma's character.

## What Needs Work

**One small thing.** The sentence "The Egyptians built in stone for eternity" grants Egypt a kind of monumental permanence that the text does not grant Kerma. The mud-brick that follows is presented as the material of the floodplain, which is correct, but the juxtaposition gives Egypt "eternity" and Kerma "the floodplain." Consider whether this comparison serves Kerma's story or subtly diminishes it. The deffufa has stood for four thousand years. That is its own kind of eternity.

## The Question I'd Push On

The opening mentions "the land the Egyptians called Kush." What did the people of Kerma call themselves? If we do not know, the chapter should say so honestly. If we have a possibility, the chapter should offer it. The name we use for a people is the first act of respect, and "Kush" is the Egyptian name. Whose name is missing?
