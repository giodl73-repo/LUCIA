# The National Policy and Its Discontents
## Canada, 1867–1945

### Opening

**Kamloops Indian Residential School, British Columbia, 1920**

Her name was Qwenqwent. She was seven years old, and she had traveled three days on roads that smelled of pine dust and horse sweat to reach a building larger than anything she had seen: red brick, three stories, a cross above the roof that caught the September light. The mountains she knew rose behind it. They were the same mountains. Everything else was wrong.

The nun came with scissors.

Qwenqwent had not understood, until this moment, that they would cut her braids. Her grandmother had braided her hair that morning, standing behind her in the early dark, singing quietly in Secwepemctsin, the song that was also a teaching about how water moves through the land. The braids were done. The singing was done. Her grandmother pressed her palms against Qwenqwent's shoulders once, and then Qwenqwent's father carried her luggage to the wagon, and no one cried because that would have made it worse.

The scissors were cold. The cut was quick. The braids fell to the floor and lay there like something that had once been living.

A priest came with a ledger. He wrote a number in it. He said a name she did not recognize. He said it twice, louder the second time, as if she were far away. The nun pointed to Qwenqwent and said the name again. She understood, then, what the number meant. What the name meant. What was being asked of her.

She was standing in the Kamloops Indian Residential School, one of 139 such schools operating across Canada, part of a system that would touch the lives of 150,000 children over a century. It was funded by the federal government under the authority of the Indian Act of 1876, which had been designed, in the words of the deputy superintendent of Indian affairs, "to do away with the tribal system and assimilate the Indian people in all respects with the inhabitants of the Dominion." The school's purpose was stated in policies that used the language of care and civilization. The practice was different.

She was not allowed to speak Secwepemctsin. She was not allowed to practice her ceremonies. She was not allowed to go home. She slept in a dormitory with forty other girls. Some of them knew her language; they whispered it at night, under their breath, the words growing smaller as the weeks went on.

---

Across the country, in the same years, a different kind of story was being told.

The Canadian Pacific Railway, completed in 1885, had made the nation. That is the story. Macdonald's National Policy, tariffs and settlement and iron rails, had bound together a continental dominion and set it on the path to prosperity. The prairies had been made available by the numbered treaties his government signed with the Plains nations through the 1870s and 1880s. The Ojibwe, Cree, Assiniboine, and Blackfoot Confederacy had negotiated in good faith, had understood these treaties as agreements about sharing the land, about a relationship that would endure. The government had understood them as transfers of title. These were not the same understanding. The gap between them is where much of what follows lives.

Canada in 1867 was a confederation of four provinces with a design for the west and a problem with the east. The problem was Quebec: French, Catholic, remembering the Conquest of 1759 as a wound that had never fully healed, watching the confederation with a wariness that would define the country's politics for the next century. The design for the west was settlement, farmers and domestic markets and iron rails. By certain measures, it worked. The country was built.

The child in Kamloops was not in the story. She was the condition the story required.

---

The discontents of this era are multiple and do not agree with one another. Louis Riel, the Métis leader who led two uprisings in defense of the western settlements and was hanged in 1885, is a discontent. Quebec, which watched Riel's execution as an English Protestant assault on the French Catholic west, is a discontent. The farmers of the prairies, organizing against railway monopolies and collapsing wheat prices, are a discontent. The men who went to fight in two world wars, in a country where conscription cracked the English-French fault line open, are a discontent.

But the oldest discontent, and the one the National Policy required and concealed, is the one that the child in Kamloops carries.

In 2021, ground-penetrating radar will scan the grounds of the Kamloops Indian Residential School and find 215 anomalies consistent with unmarked graves. This was not a surprise to the Tk'emlúps te Secwépemc Nation. They had been saying it for years. Their grandmothers and grandfathers had said it before them. What changed in 2021 was that the instruments confirmed what the oral tradition had carried for generations, and the flags across Canada went to half-mast, and a country that had told itself a certain story had to find a way to hold two things at once.

Qwenqwent kept the language. Not everyone could. Some of the children who came to Kamloops and schools like it never heard their mother tongue again, watched the words they had grown up inside thin to silence over generations. Some carried it home, buried in the body, and gave it to their grandchildren when it was safe again, or when there was no more time not to. The word for water moving through land did not appear in any federal ledger. The scissors did not know what they had cut.

The mountains were still there. They had not been waiting for Confederation to name them. They did not recognize the name.
