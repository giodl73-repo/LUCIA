# Ganden Phodrang — The Dharma-State

We are the government of the Dalai Lamas — the Ganden Phodrang, named for the Dalai Lama's original palace at Ganden Monastery — and we governed Tibet from 1642 until 1959 as a theocracy in which the Dalai Lama was simultaneously the spiritual head of the Gelug school, the temporal ruler of Tibet, and the embodiment of Avalokitesvara, the bodhisattva of compassion. This is not three separate roles; it is one identity expressed in three registers. We are Chodé — the dharma-state — the attempt to organize an entire society according to Buddhist principles, with the living embodiment of compassion at its head.

The Fifth Dalai Lama, Ngawang Lobzang Gyatso — the "Great Fifth" — established this state in 1642 with the military support of the Khoshut Mongol ruler Gushri Khan. The relationship between Mongol military power and Tibetan religious authority — the chö-yon, patron-priest dynamic — that had governed Tibet's relationship with outside powers since the Sakya-Mongol alliance of the thirteenth century here reached its formal expression. The Fifth Dalai Lama was not only a religious figure; he was a statesman who managed the competing claims of Mongol patrons, Chinese Qing emperors, and Tibetan regional lords with extraordinary skill. The Potala Palace he commissioned still dominates Lhasa's skyline.

- **Lhasa** — the capital; the Potala Palace (17 stories, built by the Fifth Dalai Lama); the Jokhang temple

## What Defined Them

**Avalokitesvara in governance.** The Dalai Lama institution rested on the claim that each Dalai Lama was the reincarnation of his predecessor and the embodiment of Avalokitesvara, the bodhisattva of compassion. This was not merely theological; it was the foundation of the state's legitimacy. A sovereign who is the living compassion of the universe does not need genealogy or conquest to rule; he rules because his presence is itself the blessing of the dharma. The institution's authority derived from a metaphysics of compassionate sovereignty that had no equivalent elsewhere in the world.

**The tulku system — reincarnate masters.** The Ganden Phodrang governance operated through a network of reincarnate lamas — tulkus — who provided institutional continuity across generations. When a tulku died, his successor was sought among children born after his death, identified through tests and oracular pronouncements. This system gave Tibetan Buddhism an extraordinary mechanism for perpetuating lineages and institutions: the knowledge, the relationships, and the authority were understood to have passed intact to the new body. The system also created vulnerability — a child Dalai Lama left power in the hands of regents, who were the source of many of Tibet's internal crises.

**The Potala as cosmos.** The Potala Palace — rebuilt and massively expanded by the Fifth Dalai Lama — is both the residence of the Dalai Lamas and a physical embodiment of the Buddhist cosmos. Its thirteen stories, its white lower section (the White Palace for governance) and red upper section (the Red Palace for religion), its storage of the remains of past Dalai Lamas, its chapels and libraries — all constitute a building that was simultaneously a palace, a monastery, and a reliquary. To enter the Potala was to enter the Dalai Lama's dharmic presence.

**Qing patronage and its complications.** The Qing emperors — especially the Kangxi, Yongzheng, and Qianlong emperors — were genuine patrons of Tibetan Buddhism, which served their political purpose of managing the Mongol and Tibetan populations of their empire. The Dalai Lama institution received Qing support; in return the Qing exercised oversight through Ambans (imperial residents) in Lhasa. This arrangement was genuinely complex: neither pure subjugation nor pure independence, but a patron-priest relationship with a very large patron. The Tibetan interpretation held that the Dalai Lama was the spiritual teacher and the Qing emperor the patron; the Qing interpretation held differently.

**The Thirteenth Dalai Lama's modernization.** The Thirteenth Dalai Lama (1876–1933) was the most politically active of the Dalai Lamas — twice driven into exile, first by the British invasion (1903) and then by Chinese forces (1910), he returned each time and attempted to modernize Tibet's military and administrative apparatus. He failed to create the modern state he envisioned, but he proclaimed Tibetan independence in 1913 and left a political testament warning of the dangers that would come after his death. He was right about what would come.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Great Fifth:** The Fifth Dalai Lama arrives in Beijing to meet the Shunzhi Emperor. It is 1652. The Qing emperor has come to meet him a distance from the city — a matter of protocol that encodes a question about who is sovereign and who is patron. The Fifth Dalai Lama navigates this with the skill he applies to everything.

**2 — The Sixth's Secret:** The Regent Sangye Gyatso has concealed the Fifth's death for fifteen years. He has ruled in the Dalai Lama's name while continuing the institutional machinery. Now the secret is out. The Sixth Dalai Lama writes love poems. He will be deposed.

**3 — The Qing Arrives:** The Qing forces expel the Dzungar Mongols who have occupied Lhasa. They install a new Dalai Lama. The Ambans take up residence. The 1793 convention is signed. The patron-priest relationship now has imperial supervision built into it.

**4 — Isolation:** The world is changing. The Thirteenth Dalai Lama reads the reports from his officials who have seen British India. He knows what is coming. He writes: unless we protect our own country, it will now happen that the Dalai and Panchen Lamas will be made to disappear.

**5 — 1959:** The Fourteenth Dalai Lama leaves Lhasa on the night of March 17th. He crosses into India. He will not return. The institution that governed Tibet since 1642 continues in exile in Dharamsala. The Potala Palace is preserved as a museum.
