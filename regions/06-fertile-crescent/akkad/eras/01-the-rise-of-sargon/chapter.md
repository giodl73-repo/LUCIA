# The Rise of Sargon

## I. The Cupbearer

Sargon poured the wine for the king of Kish.

He stood in the court of Ur-Zababa, a cupbearer, a man whose name appears in no royal genealogy and whose father's name the later tradition replaced with silence. The texts say his mother was a priestess who conceived him in secret and his father he did not know. They say she set him in a basket of rushes sealed with bitumen and placed him on the river, and the river carried him downstream through the reeds and the brown water and the flat country where the sky met the plain in every direction, and a gardener named Aqqi drew the basket from the current and raised the boy among the date palms and the irrigation ditches of his estate. Whether the story is true or useful does not matter here. What matters is what the story told the people who repeated it for two thousand years: that this man came from nothing recognizable. That no bloodline supported his claim. That Ishtar looked on him with favor, and favor was enough.

A cupbearer in the court of Kish was not a servant. He was a man who stood close to the king, who knew the king's moods and the king's counselors and the names of the men who came with petitions and the names of the men who left without answers. The court of Kish was reed-matted floors and the smell of beer from the temple brewery and the murmur of scribes dictating to each other in the corridors that connected the palace to the temple precinct. Sargon learned this court the way a scribe learned the sign list: by proximity, by repetition, by watching which gestures carried power and which carried only the appearance of it.

Kish was the city that the King List named first after the flood. Its kingship was old, its legitimacy deep. Ur-Zababa held Kish, and Kish held the memory of the first post-diluvian kingship, and Sargon stood beside Ur-Zababa and poured his wine. The tradition preserved in later Akkadian texts says that Ishtar appeared to Sargon. She loved him. The verb the texts use is the verb of divine election, the choosing that precedes all power. When Enlil granted kingship, it was a political act, a cosmic ratification. When Ishtar loved a man, it was something more personal and more dangerous: a bond between the goddess of war and desire and the mortal she had selected. Her favor could make a gardener's son into a king. Her withdrawal could unmake everything.

What happened at Kish is unclear. The sources fracture. One tradition says Ur-Zababa learned of Sargon's destiny through a dream and tried to have him killed. Another says Sargon gathered Akkadian-speaking men from the settlements north of the Sumerian cities, men who farmed the rain-fed land between the rivers and who spoke a Semitic language that was not the language of the temple scribes. These men became his army. They followed him because Ishtar loved him, and a man loved by Ishtar was a man whose wars would be won.

The army that followed Sargon was not a temple levy. The Sumerian cities raised their armies through the temple system: the en or the lugal called the laborers from the fields and the workshops and armed them and sent them home when the campaign was over. Sargon's men were different. The later tradition says five thousand four hundred soldiers ate bread before him daily. This was a standing force, maintained year-round, loyal to the king rather than to a city's god. The soldiers were Akkadian speakers from the northern settlements, men whose connection to the Sumerian temple system was tenuous at best. They were Sargon's men the way the cupbearer had been Ur-Zababa's man: bound by personal allegiance, fed by the king's stores, deployed at the king's command. The standing army was as new as the empire it built. Both depended on the same man.

He built a city. He called it Akkade. No one has found it. The capital that organized the first empire that spanned the plain lies somewhere under the alluvial soil of central Iraq, its mud bricks dissolved back into the mud they were made from, its location a gap in the map that two centuries of excavation have not closed. But the city existed, and from it Sargon marched south.

The plain between Akkade and the Sumerian cities was flat and hot and cut by canals that glittered in the sun where they held water and lay dry and cracked where they did not. Date palms lined the rivers in thick groves, and the dust rose behind the army as it moved, and the dust was visible for miles across the plain, which meant the cities knew he was coming before he arrived. Lugalzagesi held the south. He was en of Umma and lugal of Unug and he had conquered or coerced the major cities of the Sumerian heartland into a loose confederation under his authority. He had done what Sumerian kings had always attempted: to hold the cities together. He had done it the old way, by alliances and marriages and the recognition of each city's god.

Sargon fought thirty-four battles. The number is from the royal inscriptions, and whether it represents thirty-four distinct engagements or a literary convention for totality, the meaning is clear: the conquest was thorough. He defeated Lugalzagesi and brought him in a neck stock to the gate of Enlil's temple at Nippur, because even a conqueror who spoke Akkadian needed Enlil's recognition, and the display of the defeated king before Enlil's gate was the act that converted military victory into theological legitimation. He took Unug. The inscriptions say he pulled down its walls. He took Ur and Lagash and Umma and the cities of the southern marshes. In the streets of Unug, which had known its own kings since before the King List began, Akkadian soldiers walked in the dust that Gilgamesh's feet had walked in, and the temple of Inanna, who had stolen the me from Enki and made Unug great, now served a king who spoke the goddess's name in his own language: Ishtar.

He washed his weapons in the Lower Sea. The phrase is from his inscriptions. It means he stood on the shore of the gulf with an army that had marched the full length of the alluvial plain and conquered every city between. The salt water took the blood from the bronze. The campaign was over. The cupbearer held the cup.

## II. The Road from Akkade

The empire that Sargon built was held by roads.

Not roads in the Roman sense, paved and engineered. Roads in the Mesopotamian sense: routes that runners carried messages along, paths that the tamkarum followed with their donkey caravans, the lines of communication between the capital and the governors who held the cities in the king's name. When Sargon took a city, he did not leave it to its own devices. He removed the city's ruler and replaced him with an ensi, a governor appointed from Akkade, a man whose authority derived not from the city's god or the city's tradition but from the king who had sent him.

This was the difference. Lugalzagesi had confederated. He had acknowledged each city's god and each city's system and held them together through the recognition of a shared overlord. Sargon administered. He imposed. He replaced. And the instrument of replacement was the ensi.

Consider a man sent from Akkade to govern Umma. He arrives in a city that has just been conquered. The audience room still holds the furniture of the previous ruler. The reed mats on the floor were woven by Sumerian craftswomen. The incense that burns in the corner is the same incense the previous en burned for the city's patron god, and the patron god is still in the temple, and the temple still owns the land, and the farmers who work the land still speak Sumerian. The governor speaks Akkadian. His seal reads Akkadian. His reports travel north to Akkade in Akkadian. But the ledgers in the storeroom are in Sumerian, because the scribes who keep them were trained in the edubba of this city, and the edubba taught in Sumerian, and the accounting system that tracks the grain and the labor and the canal maintenance was built in Sumerian by Sumerian scribes over centuries of accumulated practice.

The governor cannot replace this system. He cannot fire the scribes and import Akkadian ones, because Akkadian scribes trained in Akkadian administrative practice do not yet exist in sufficient numbers. The empire is younger than the scribal tradition it has conquered. So the governor learns. He sits with the Sumerian scribes and reads their reports and slowly acquires the vocabulary he needs. Or he brings a translator. Or he relies on bilingual subordinates who can move between the two languages. The administrative apparatus of the Akkadian empire was, from its first year, a bilingual operation conducted by conquerors who needed the conquered more than the conquered needed them.

The ensi's authority was real but derivative. He held his city because the king held the empire, and the king held the empire because Ishtar loved him. Remove any link in that chain and the governor was a stranger in someone else's audience room, sitting on someone else's mats, burning incense to someone else's god. The governors knew this. The inscriptions show them careful to acknowledge Sargon in every document, to date their records by the king's regnal years, to stamp their seals beside the king's name. The chain of legitimation was visible, physical, inscribed. It ran from Ishtar through Sargon through the governor's seal into the wet clay that held the record.

Sargon standardized. Weights and measures, which had varied from city to city in the Sumerian period, were brought into alignment across the empire. A shekel in Umma now weighed the same as a shekel in Ur. A kor of barley in Lagash matched a kor in Kish. The standardization was an act of control. When the weights are the same, the tribute can be calculated, the rations can be distributed, the tamkarum's accounts can be audited. The road carries messages. The standard weights make the messages legible. The ensi files the reports. The king in Akkade reads them. An empire that spans a thousand kilometers of river plain can function because the numbers mean the same thing at both ends.

The canal system presented the hardest test. The canals of each city had been maintained by temple labor under temple direction for centuries. The schedules of digging and dredging, the allocation of water between fields, the repair of levees before the spring flood, all of this was organized by the temple administration in Sumerian, recorded in Sumerian, managed by Sumerian-trained officials who knew which channels silted first and which fields could tolerate salt and which could not. An Akkadian governor who neglected the canals would lose the harvest, and a governor who lost the harvest would lose his city, because a population that starved did not care whose seal was on the administrative tablets. The canals were the empire's most practical lesson: that conquering a territory and managing it were different skills, and the second required the people who had been doing it before the conqueror arrived.

The tamkarum moved along these roads. They were not independent merchants. They were trading agents who carried goods on the king's behalf and on their own, and the distinction between royal and private commerce was permeable, and the permeable distinction was itself a feature of the system. The tamkarum brought tin from the east and timber from the north and copper from the gulf and returned with grain and textiles and the oil that the southern cities pressed from sesame. They traveled with donkey caravans, the animals loaded with leather sacks and clay jars stoppered with bitumen, and the dust of the road covered the goods the way the dust covered everything in the flat country between the rivers, and when the tamkarum arrived at a city and unloaded the goods, the governor's scribes recorded the shipment in the same Sumerian accounting system that had recorded temple deliveries for a thousand years. The roads were not empty. They carried the empire's blood.

## III. The Two Languages

In the edubba of the conquered cities, something was happening that no royal inscription recorded and no general ordered. The scribes were learning a second language.

The Sumerian scribal tradition was old. It was the oldest literate tradition in the world, and it had produced the techniques of writing, the methods of accounting, the literary forms of the hymn and the lament and the mythological narrative, and the educational system that transmitted all of it. When Akkadian-speaking administrators arrived in the Sumerian cities, they did not find a tradition they could discard. They found a tradition they could not function without.

The Akkadian language was Semitic. Its grammar, its vocabulary, its way of building words from roots differed fundamentally from Sumerian, which was an isolate, related to no known language. But the writing system was Sumerian. The cuneiform script, the wedge-shaped marks pressed into clay, had been designed for Sumerian. When Akkadian scribes adopted it, they adapted it: using some signs for their Sumerian values and others for their Akkadian sound values, borrowing Sumerian logograms wholesale where the concept was the same, creating a hybrid writing system that required competence in both languages to read.

A scribe in the Akkadian period sat in the edubba and learned Sumerian the way his predecessors had always learned Sumerian: by copying. Our detailed knowledge of the edubba comes from later centuries, but the practice was old, and the Sargonic-period tablets confirm its continuity. He copied the same lists of birds and fish and professions. He copied the hymns to Inanna and the administrative formulas and the mathematical tables. His hand learned the Sumerian sign combinations, the same muscle memory that Sumerian scribes had developed for a thousand years. Then he learned to use those same signs to write Akkadian. The reed was the same. The clay was the same. The wedges were the same shapes pressed at the same angles. But the language they encoded was different, and the scribe's mind held both, and the culture that emerged from this double competence was something new in the world: a literate bilingualism that would persist for two thousand years and produce the great literary works of Mesopotamian civilization.

The Sumerian scribes who trained the Akkadian students did not choose this arrangement. Their cities had been taken. Their kings had been removed. The scribes were valued because their knowledge was indispensable, and being valued for your indispensability is not the same as being free. The edubba continued to function because the empire needed it. The scribes continued to teach because teaching was what they did, and the system they had built was larger than the political order that now controlled it. But a Sumerian master scribe teaching the sacred hymns to an Akkadian administrator's son, watching the boy press the signs for words he would use to write reports for a governor who answered to a king who had conquered the city where the hymns were composed, understood something about power that no royal inscription would ever state: that the conqueror needed the script, and the script did not need the conqueror, and this asymmetry was the most durable fact in the room.

The literary tradition absorbed the contact and was changed by it. Akkadian poets began composing in Sumerian forms. Sumerian myths would, over the centuries that followed, be retold with Akkadian names and Akkadian emphases. Inanna became Ishtar. The process was slow, stretching across a millennium before it produced the great bilingual literary works, but it began here, in the generation when two languages first shared the same classroom and the same clay. And from the heart of this bilingual world came a voice that fused both traditions into something the Sumerian edubba had not anticipated.

Enheduanna was Sargon's daughter. He appointed her en-priestess of Nanna at Ur, the moon god's city, the most prestigious religious office in the Sumerian south. The appointment was political: an Akkadian king placing his daughter in the holiest Sumerian position, binding the conquered city's divine authority to the conqueror's family. But Enheduanna was more than a political instrument. She composed. She wrote hymns to Inanna in Sumerian, hymns of such force and precision that the scribes copied them for centuries after her death. "The Exaltation of Inanna" is hers. In translation, the opening lines address the goddess as lady of all the me, queen of all given powers, radiant light, unfailing woman. The language is Sumerian. The theological vision is Akkadian: Inanna elevated above all other gods, supreme, universal, the divine force behind the king and behind the priestess who serves the king. Enheduanna's name appears on the tablets. She is the first author in history whose name we know and whose work we can read. She stands at the junction of two traditions and speaks in both, and the voice that emerges belongs to neither separately.

The King List, maintained by Sumerian scribes, now included Akkadian dynasties, because the theological framework of kingship descending from heaven applied to Akkadian kings as surely as it had applied to Sumerian ones. The framework was larger than the people who created it. It held whoever sat in the seat of power, because the framework was the seat.

## IV. Akkade and the Four Quarters

The goods arrived at Akkade from every direction.

Cedar from the forests of the Amanus and the Lebanon, carried down the Euphrates on rafts and cut into planks that smelled of resin and the mountains. Copper from Magan, shipped across the gulf in boats whose hulls were sealed with the same bitumen that sealed the tablets. Lapis lazuli from the mines beyond the Zagros, blue as deep water, carried overland by donkey caravan through passes where the air was thin and the paths were narrow and the stone was worth its weight in silver. Tin from somewhere to the east, perhaps Elam, perhaps farther, its source uncertain then and now, but its presence essential because bronze required it, and bronze was the metal of weapons and tools and the vessels the king drank from. Diorite from Magan, black and hard, the stone Sargon's sculptors would carve into the royal portraits that stared forward with Akkadian eyes, full-bearded, heavy-browed, the face of a dynasty that had invented itself.

The marketplace at Akkade, if the literary texts preserve anything of its character, would have smelled of cedar resin and sesame oil and the particular dust of the alluvial plain that settled on everything and turned the air golden in the late afternoon. Akkadian was the language of command here, but the traders spoke whatever was needed, and a tamkarum back from the gulf might shift from Akkadian to Sumerian to whatever language the Dilmun sailors spoke within a single negotiation. The city was new. Everything in it had been built within a generation. The mud-brick walls were not yet weathered. The temple precincts were still being expanded. A city built by a king who had built himself, and both were works in progress, and neither would last in physical form.

Akkade was the center. Not by geography. The city sat in the northern part of the alluvial plain, where the Tigris and Euphrates ran closest together, not at the natural center of anything but at the center of the road network that Sargon built and that connected the cedar forests to the west, the silver mountains to the north, the gulf trade to the south, and the highland routes to the east. The city was the capital because the roads converged on it, and the roads converged on it because the king lived there, and the king lived there because this was where he had built his throne when Ishtar told him to build.

The inscriptions name the trade routes as conquests. Sargon claims to have reached the Cedar Forest and the Silver Mountain. He claims the ships of Meluhha, Magan, and Dilmun docked at the quay of Akkade. Whether these claims are literal descriptions of military campaigns or ideological assertions of commercial reach, the practical result was the same: the empire's capital received goods from a territory that stretched from the Mediterranean to the Indus Valley. No single city in Ki-en-gi had ever held that position. The Sumerian cities had traded widely, but each city traded for itself, through its own temple, by its own arrangements. Akkade concentrated the flows. The tamkarum carried the goods, the roads carried the tamkarum, and the king's authority guaranteed the roads.

Sargon took titles to match. Sharru-kin. The name itself was an argument: the king is legitimate. Not "the king is powerful" or "the king is victorious" but "legitimate," because legitimacy was the problem a self-made king had to solve every day. The other titles accumulated. King of Akkade. King of Kish. King of the Land. King of the Four Quarters. Shar kishati. King of the Universe. Each title extended the claim. The Four Quarters was a cosmological assertion: north, south, east, west, the totality of the known world, held by one man. The scribes carved these titles into stone and stamped them into clay and the titles traveled the roads the way the goods traveled, and at the end of each road a governor read the king's name and understood that the name was a claim and the claim was the empire and the empire was the name.

The Sargon Legend was already forming. Within a generation of his death, or perhaps during his lifetime, the literary tradition began shaping the story: the priestess, the basket, the river, the gardener, Ishtar's love, the rise from nothing to everything. The legend was not a distortion of history. It was the culture's own explanation of how the impossible had happened. A man with no lineage held the four quarters. The explanation could not be political or military, because politics and military power did not explain why this man and not another. The explanation had to be theological: Ishtar chose him. The legend was the proof. The birth, the river, the rescue, the ascent. Every element demonstrated that the gods had arranged a destiny no human plan could have produced.

The legend would outlast the empire. Assyrian kings a thousand years later would copy it onto their own tablets. The Moses story would echo its structure. The pattern of the exposed child who becomes a ruler entered the world's narrative vocabulary through a story told about a man whose city no one has ever found.

## V. What Holds and What Cannot Hold

Sargon ruled for fifty-five years if the King List is to be believed.

The length of the reign is itself a statement. Fifty-five years of holding a territory that had never been held by one authority, of suppressing the revolts that began almost immediately and recurred throughout his rule, of maintaining the chain of governors and roads and standards that kept the empire functional. The inscriptions, even in their formulaic praise, record the strain. Sargon put down rebellions in the south. He campaigned in the east. He fought in the north. The thirty-four battles of the initial conquest were not the end of fighting but the beginning of a permanent campaign of maintenance, and the maintenance was the empire's real character: not a conquest completed but a conquest endlessly renewed.

The revolts told a story the royal inscriptions tried to suppress. The cities that Sargon had taken did not accept Akkadian rule as permanent. They waited, and when the king's attention turned elsewhere, they expelled his governors and reinstated their own rulers and returned to the temple systems that had governed them before the Akkadian army arrived. Sargon marched back and took them again. The cycle was the empire's heartbeat: conquest, rebellion, reconquest. The empire held because the king held. The king held because Ishtar loved him. And the question that no inscription could answer sat at the center of everything: what would happen when Ishtar's beloved died?

A personal bond cannot be inherited. A throne can pass from father to son. A title can be pronounced over a new head. A seal can be carved with a new name. But the love of a goddess is not a seal. It is not a title. It cannot be transferred by decree or testament. Sargon's sons, Rimush and Manishtushu, would inherit the empire and the enemies and the roads and the governors. They would not inherit Ishtar's love. Each would have to earn it, or claim it, or pretend it. Each would be tested by the same cities that had tested their father. Each would be killed. Not in battle. In palace conspiracies. The empire's internal politics, which the royal inscriptions mention only in fragments, suggest that the men who served the king understood what the theology implied: if the king's authority rested on a personal bond with Ishtar, then replacing the king was not treason against a system but a bet that Ishtar's favor had shifted.

Sargon did not know this. Or if he knew, the inscriptions do not say. What the inscriptions say is what they always say: I conquered, I took, I held, the gods loved me. The formula was the empire's public face. Behind the formula, the man who had been a cupbearer and a gardener's son sat in a city he had built from nothing in a territory he had taken by force held together by roads he had organized and legitimated by a goddess whose love could not be written into a succession document.

The last years are dark. The sources thin. A man who had been campaigning since he left Kish, who had marched the length of the plain and fought in the mountains and stood on the shore of the gulf, was old now. Fifty-five years of kingship meant fifty-five years of the campaigning season's heat and the winter's council meetings and the unending stream of reports from governors in cities that wanted their own kings back. The body that had poured wine for Ur-Zababa and washed weapons in the Lower Sea carried decades of accumulated labor. Sargon may have faced revolts he could not suppress as easily as before. The empire may have contracted at the edges as the king aged. The King List says he died and his son Rimush took the throne, and the list moves on with the flatness of an administrative record: next name, next reign, next entry. Behind the flatness, the transfer of power was a crisis that the empire's own ideology made inevitable.

In Akkade, the city no one has found, the king who had been carried on a river as an infant and had risen to hold the four quarters of the world was buried. The burial site is unknown. The city that held it is unknown. The name survives. Sharru-kin. The king is legitimate.

Whether the gods would say the same of his sons remained to be heard.
