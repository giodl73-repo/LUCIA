# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## PANEL-1 Review | Dutch Empire, Era 1 — De Gouden Eeuw | Opening

---

I read this with the question I always bring: whose story is this, and does it belong to the people it is about?

The answer here is more complicated than it is for most chapters in this project, and I want to think carefully about why.

This is not a chapter told from inside the Bandanese worldview. It is told from inside the Dutch commercial worldview. The culture being rendered is the VOC's culture — the culture of the subscription table, the Heeren XVII, the prospectus, the ledger. And within that rendering, the opening is honest and precise. The Dutch merchant community of 1602 believed that commerce was a legitimate end in itself, that profit was not apology but purpose, and that the structure they were building — the joint-stock corporation with sovereign powers — was a rational solution to the problem of organizing capital across vast distances. The opening renders that belief without irony or anachronistic condemnation. Pieter Bas is not a villain. He is a baker making a rational investment.

This is the correct approach, and I want to be explicit about why: this is Region 24, Colonial Empires, and the charter for this region is to tell the empires' story from inside their own logic. The Achebe Test here is not "would a thoughtful Bandanese reader recognize their world in these pages?" — the Bandanese have their own chapters in their own regions. The test is: "would a thoughtful Dutch commercial historian of 1602 recognize their world in these pages?" And the answer is yes.

But the Bandanese do appear, briefly, and I want to examine how they are treated. "The Bandanese had grown and traded nutmeg and mace for centuries before any European ship arrived." This is sufficient. It establishes their prior existence and agency without making them backdrop. The chain of trade — Bandanese to Javanese to Gujarati to Mediterranean — is named with some specificity. The violence is named: "the negotiations and the coercions and the threats and the small violences that preceded the harvest." The phrase "small violences" is honest without being either dismissive or performatively graphic.

What I want the full chapter to hold carefully: when Jan Pieterszoon Coen appears, and he will appear (the Banda massacre of 1621 is in the brief), the chapter must render him from within his own worldview without becoming an apologist for him. Coen believed what he wrote to the Heeren XVII: that force was a commercial tool, that the monopoly justified the methods, that the Bandanese who refused to sell exclusively to the VOC were in breach of a commercial order that the VOC had the right to enforce. The chapter must inhabit that belief long enough for the reader to understand it — not agree with it, understand it — and then must hold the Bandanese deaths as equally real without stepping outside the frame to deliver a verdict.

This is the hardest thing about writing this empire from within: the Dutch were honest about their purposes in a way that makes their violence visible without excuse. Multatuli was a Dutch voice denouncing Dutch practice from within Dutch moral vocabulary. The chapter should find that voice.

**The question I'd push on:** When the opening names "the negotiations and the coercions and the threats and the small violences that preceded the harvest," whose vocabulary is this? Is it the VOC factor's own accounting language, or is it the author stepping outside the frame? I think it is close to the line. The chapter should be attentive to this boundary: describe the violence in language that a VOC factor might have used in a letter to the Heeren XVII, and the reader will feel the distance more acutely than if the author reaches for more morally weighted language.
