# Figures — The Age of the Great Pyramids

## Merer

**Historical basis:** Named individual from the Wadi al-Jarf papyri, discovered in 2013 by Pierre Tallet. The papyri constitute Merer's logbook, recording the transport of limestone from Tura to Giza during the 27th year of Khufu's reign. He managed a crew of approximately 200 men organized into phyles. The papyrus records daily activities in tabular form: dates, destinations, cargo types. It is the only contemporary administrative document directly connected to the construction of the Great Pyramid.

**Treatment in chapter:** Merer serves as the inside-voice anchor for the opening and closing. His logbook's tone (logistical, matter-of-fact, without commentary on the scale of the project) provides the chapter's emotional register. The chapter does not invent interiority for Merer. It uses the papyrus record's laconic quality as characterization: a man who did sacred work and recorded it as routine. The closing returns to Merer's name and crew to complete the frame.

**Limits:** The papyrus tells us what Merer did, not what he thought. The chapter respects this gap by never attributing specific feelings or motivations beyond what the logistical record and the cultural context support.

## Khufu (Cheops)

**Historical basis:** Fourth Dynasty pharaoh, builder of the Great Pyramid at Giza. Reigned approximately 2589-2566 BCE (dates vary by chronology). His only surviving likeness is a 7.5 cm ivory figurine found at Abydos by Flinders Petrie. The Wadi al-Jarf papyri and the workers' village excavations confirm the administrative scale of his pyramid project. Later tradition is contradictory: Herodotus (2nd century BCE Greek informant accounts) describes him as a tyrant; the Westcar Papyrus (Middle Kingdom) portrays him as a curious and accessible king.

**Treatment in chapter:** The chapter names the contradiction in the sources without resolving it, following the Honest Uncertainty principle. The detail of the small ivory figurine (the man who built the largest structure on earth represented by an object that fits in a hand) is allowed to speak for itself without editorial commentary. The chapter does not attempt Khufu's interiority because the record does not support it.

## Hemiunu

**Historical basis:** Khufu's vizier and nephew. His mastaba tomb (G 4000) at Giza contained a life-size seated limestone statue (now in the Roemer-und-Pelizaeus Museum, Hildesheim). His title "Overseer of All the King's Works" and the placement of his tomb adjacent to the Great Pyramid support his identification as the pyramid's architect. The statue shows a heavy-set man of physical authority.

**Treatment in chapter:** Described from his statue. The chapter uses the physical description as characterization without extending it into psychology. The title is cited as evidence of his role. The chapter treats him as the engineer who executed the plan, complementing Merer's perspective from the labor side.

## Sneferu

**Historical basis:** Fourth Dynasty pharaoh, Khufu's father. Builder of at least three pyramids: the Meidum pyramid (attribution debated), the Bent Pyramid, and the Red Pyramid at Dahshur. The Palermo Stone records his reign. The Westcar Papyrus portrays him favorably. His pyramid-building program consumed more stone than Khufu's single pyramid, making him the most prolific builder of the Old Kingdom.

**Treatment in chapter:** Framed as the experimentalist whose engineering progression (step to bent to true) made the Great Pyramid possible. The Bent Pyramid's angle change is described as a correction preserved in stone, not as a failure. The chapter uses Sneferu to establish that the Great Pyramid did not appear from nothing but from a generation of trial and revision.

## Imhotep

**Historical basis:** Chancellor, physician, and architect under King Djoser (Third Dynasty). Designer of the step pyramid at Saqqara, the first monumental stone structure. Later deified; identified with Asclepius by the Greeks. His historical existence is attested by a statue base inscription naming him alongside Djoser.

**Treatment in chapter:** Brief appearance in Section II as the inventor of the stone-mountain idea. The chapter credits him with the conceptual leap (from flat mastaba to stepped pyramid) that made all subsequent pyramid building possible. His later deification is not treated as the point; the architectural innovation is.

## The Work Crews (Collective)

**Historical basis:** The gang names preserved on pyramid blocks (red ochre paint in hieratic script) and the workers' village excavated at Giza by AERA (Lehner and Hawass, 1990s-present). Gang names include "Friends of Khufu," "Drunkards of Menkaure," "The Crew that is the Vigor of the White Crown." The village evidence includes bakeries, breweries, a medical facility, sleeping galleries, and refuse deposits showing beef consumption. Crew organization was by phyle (approximately 40-50 workers) within larger divisions (approximately 200 workers, as in Merer's unit).

**Treatment in chapter:** The work crews serve as the chapter's collective protagonist. The gang names carry the argument that the builders were organized, proud, competitive, and self-identifying. The village evidence (bread, beer, medicine, beef) carries the argument that they were valued. The chapter treats the crews as the human infrastructure of the cosmic instrument, not as anonymous laborers.

## Harkhuf

**Historical basis:** Sixth Dynasty nomarch of Elephantine. His tomb autobiography at Aswan records four trading expeditions to Nubia. The letter from the young Pepi II about the dancing dwarf is inscribed on his tomb wall and is one of the most frequently cited documents of the Old Kingdom.

**Treatment in chapter:** Appears in Section VIII as evidence of the nomarchs' growing independence and as a source of the Pepi II letter, which the chapter uses to humanize the child-king and to illustrate the structural loosening of central authority. The letter is treated as evidence, not as anecdote for charm.

---

## Pepi II

**Historical basis:** Sixth Dynasty pharaoh. Acceded to the throne at approximately age six. Traditional sources assign him a reign of 94 years (Manetho), which would make it the longest in Egyptian history, though some scholars argue for a shorter reign. His pyramid at Saqqara contains Pyramid Texts. His long reign is associated with the decentralization that led to the First Intermediate Period.

**Treatment in chapter:** Described through the Harkhuf letter (the child-king's excitement about the dwarf) and through the structural consequences of his long reign (the nomarchs becoming hereditary rulers). The chapter does not assign blame to Pepi II for the decentralization; it describes the structural logic by which a very long reign allowed provincial power to accumulate.
