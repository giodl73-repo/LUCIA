# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński: Panel Review (Opening)

**Chapter:** Sassanid Era 1 — The Founding
**Round:** Panel-1 (opening review)
**Reviewer:** Ryszard Kapuściński

---

I have been to Iran. I have been to Naqsh-e Rostam on a winter afternoon when the light came across the plain from the west and the cliff face went golden and then pink before going cold. I know what that place feels like. And reading this opening, I can feel it — which is not easy to arrange on the page.

"The limestone takes the chisel cleanly in Fars, fine-grained and pale, the kind of stone that holds an edge for centuries." Yes. The physical quality of the stone — this is what grounds the chapter in the actual world rather than the world of ideas. A man who has not thought about limestone does not write that sentence. The stone is not metaphor. It is material. The whole political claim of the Sassanid dynasty is expressed through the physical properties of Fars limestone and what a mason can do with it in afternoon light.

What I want more of: the road below the cliff. The chapter sets up — correctly — that Ardashir's relief is positioned to be read by travelers on the road from Persepolis. But I do not feel the road. What moves on it? Camel caravans? Cavalry detachments? Pilgrims to Istakhr? A merchant from Ctesiphon seeing the relief for the first time — what is his body doing? Does he slow his animal? Does he look up from below, craning his neck, the pale cliff face bright in the morning sun? The composition's power derives from its placement in relationship to that road. Show me someone on the road responding to what they see, and the political function of the image comes alive.

The paragraph about the magi and the fire temple stays somewhat abstract: "He was not creating religion. He was building it into the bones of the state." This is right, and the phrase is good. But what does this feel like in the physical world? The fire temple at Istakhr had a sound — the priests' prayers, the crackle of the sacred wood, the bells or drums that marked the ritual hours. It had a smell — wood smoke, ash, the specific incense that the tradition prescribed. It had a quality of interior light, the fire in its enclosure, the stone walls, the way the smoke rose. If I stood at its threshold in 226 CE, what would my body tell me? Give me one moment of embodied fire-temple presence, and the political-religious fusion becomes not a concept but a fact I have experienced.

The closing is strong. "The cliff face holds the light for another hour after sunset." That is the kind of sentence that earns its place by being observed rather than invented. I believe it. I believe a man who has watched the light at Naqsh-e Rostam wrote it.

**The question I'd push on:** The Battle of Hormozdgan is mentioned but not felt. "The last Arsacid king died — some sources say in single combat." I want to feel the weight of that moment from inside the Sassanid tradition. Not the battle itself, necessarily — there will be time in the chapter — but the moment after. The news arrives in Istakhr. The fire at the temple burns. The magi perform some rite that acknowledges the change. What does the first day of Sassanid Iran feel like on the ground, in a body, in a place?
