# Panel-1 Review — Ryszard Kapuscinski
## Hausa Era 3: The Manufacturing Peak (Rewrite v2)

**Voice:** Sensory immersion — heat, smell, sound, texture

The cloth has weight. The market has a particular noise. The cup of water was cool an hour ago. This opening knows where it is.

What is excellent: "the weave too loose, the beat-glaze missing." The beat-glaze is a detail that proves research — it is the specific surface quality of finished Hausa cloth, the result of burnishing after dyeing, the thing that makes the cloth stiff and the color permanent. A reader who has never seen Hausa cloth understands immediately what Ibrahim is feeling and why it matters. This is how sensory detail does analytical work.

The market's "particular noise that said business was serious" is a sound that most readers have never heard but will immediately recognize: the sound of people who are not talking for pleasure, whose words are worth money. This is the urban-manufacturing register operating at its highest level — not describing the market but inhabiting the mood of the market.

**For the full chapter:**

Amina. The cross-compare said "the seasonal water, the mud, the mosquitoes." This is what the chapter needs for Amina's campaign section. I want to feel the transition between the open Sahel and the transitional belt south of Zaria — the landscape thickening, the air heavier, the sight lines shortened. I want the sound of horses nervous in unfamiliar terrain, the smell of a camp thrown up in wet ground, the specific weight of the harmattan turning into southern humidity. Amina must have a body in the mud.

The Bori ceremony needs sound above everything else. The drums of the Bori are described in every source as distinct from every other drum the city plays. What specific rhythm? What does it do to the people who hear it, even people who are not participating? The sound is the door into the Bori that the chapter needs to find.

The dye-pit opening from the original had excellent smell. This new opening has excellent touch (cloth weight) and sound (market noise). The full chapter should find a moment where all five senses converge in a single scene — the Bori ceremony is the obvious candidate: the drums, the incense, the heat and press of bodies, the smell of the compound at night, the visual shock of the possession.

**The question I would push on:** The opening ends with Ibrahim going to meet the eastern caravans. Does the chapter ever give us what he finds? The reader will want to know if the better cloth arrived.
