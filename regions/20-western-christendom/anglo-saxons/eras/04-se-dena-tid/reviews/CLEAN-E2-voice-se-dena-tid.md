## Baseline Voice (from opening)

Register: Saga-Spare. Characteristics:
- Short declaratives as default
- Objects carry weight without commentary
- Understatement at emotional peaks
- The period as pause mechanism
- Verbs do the work; adjectives are rationed

Established in opening: "The man in the fenland did not look like a king." "He had a small company with him. That is all the record says: a small company." "The cakes burned. She scolded him. He took it."

## Voice Scan

**Section 1 (Before the Marsh):** Consistent. "They came by sea." "The monastery was not fortified. Monasteries were not fortified. They were sacred ground." The register holds. The slight elevation of "In the year that the English called 793" maintains the inside-calendar principle without breaking the register.

**Section 2 (The Marsh):** Integrates the opening correctly. Voice stable.

**Section 3 (Edington and After):** One passage drifts slightly toward explanatory mode:

> "This settlement is sometimes read as a concession. It is more correctly read as a governance technology."

The phrase "is sometimes read" / "is more correctly read" brings in a faint scholarly voice — as if the author is adjudicating competing historiographical positions. This is not catastrophically wrong (the Saga-Spare register can carry brief analytical turns), but "sometimes read" is a note of modern historiographical debate that sits slightly outside the chapter's world. P2 — revise to maintain the chapter's own voice. Possible rewrite: "A concession, some would say afterward. It was not that."

**Section 4 (The System Built):** The paragraph on Alfred's *enchiridion* and candle-clock holds the voice well. "He measured his own day because he intended to use it fully." Correct register. The sensory passage ("scratch of the reed pen... the smell of the vellum") is excellent voice work — texture embedded in thought, not applied as decoration.

**Section 5 (The Shape of the Era):** One P3 note. The sentence:

> "The English the island speaks today has Danish in its bones: sky, window, knife, take, they, them, their — all from the Old Norse of the Danelaw settlers."

The phrase "the island speaks today" brings modern time into the chapter, which otherwise closes within the ninth century. This is a brief prospective turn — not wrong, but the phrase "today" is the only word in the chapter that places the reader explicitly in the present. It could be cut ("The English that would develop on the island had Danish in its bones") or the sentence omitted entirely in favor of the chapter's own close on Alfred's death and the Chronicle's continuation. P3 — author decides.
