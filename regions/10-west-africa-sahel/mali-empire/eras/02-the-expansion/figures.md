# Figures — When the Empire Reached the Sea

## Mansa Wali (Wali Keita)

He inherited the Manden from Sundiata and did not waste it. The jelilu carry Wali as the first mansa of the expansion: the ruler who secured the gold routes to Bambuk, who negotiated the relationship with the Wangara mining communities that would sustain the empire's wealth for generations, who sent the Dyula traders eastward and southward along paths that became the Manden's commercial skeleton.

He was the first mansa to make the hajj. He went to Mecca before Musa, before the gold crashed Cairo, before the world beyond the Sahara had any reason to pay attention to the Manden. He went because the faith required it of those who could, and he could. He went without spectacle, and he came back, and the Manden was steady under his hands. Ibn Khaldun, writing a century later from North African sources, names him as a just ruler, though the record gives little else. What the jelilu carry is that he honored the covenant. He gave when the covenant demanded giving, he governed within the assembly's bounds, he left the Manden stronger than he found it. In a dynasty that would include kings who shot arrows at crowds and kings who paddled into the ocean, Wali's virtue was competence. It is not the most dramatic quality a mansa could have. It may be the most necessary.

## Mansa Khalifa

The jelilu say he shot arrows into the crowd. This is what they carry: not why he did it, not what drove him to turn weapons on the people the covenant bound him to protect, but the act itself. The silence around Khalifa's motives is the jeli's judgment. A jeli carries what happened. If the tradition does not carry the reason, the reason was not worth carrying.

Khalifa ruled after Wali, inheriting a stable and expanding empire. What went wrong, the sources do not agree on. Ibn Khaldun's account gives the succession but not the psychology. The jelilu's account gives the act but not the cause. What both agree on is the consequence: the gbara, the council of clan heads that held authority alongside the mansa, removed him. The covenant that had made him mansa unmade him. This is Khalifa's significance in the Manden's story. He is not a villain. He is a test case. The covenant was designed to prevent tyranny because the covenant was born from the defeat of a tyrant. Khalifa proved the mechanism worked.

## Sakura (Mansa Sakura)

He was a jon, a member of the Kurukan Fuga's third order, the social category whose position was most constrained within the Manden's system. That he became mansa at all is the most striking evidence that the Manden Kurufaba was a covenant, not a dynasty. The Keita lineage had produced candidates who could not hold the Manden together during the succession crisis that followed Khalifa's removal. Sakura could. The gbara elevated him. The jelilu sang his name alongside the Keita kings.

He expanded the empire's eastern territories, reaching toward the Niger bend and the routes that connected to the Hausa states. He made the pilgrimage to Mecca, as Wali had done. He died on the return journey, and his body was carried back to the Manden. The brevity of the record does not diminish him. Sakura's significance is not in the details of his reign but in his existence within the genealogy. A jon could become mansa. The covenant's logic permitted this because the covenant served the Manden, not the Keita family. The jelilu carry Sakura's name with the same cadence and the same respect as the Keita mansas before and after him, and that is the tradition's verdict on the question of whether he belonged. He belonged. The covenant said so.

What we cannot know is what Sakura himself experienced in the transition from jon to mansa. The record does not preserve his interior life, and to invent it would be to impose certainty where the sources give silence. What we know is what he did: he governed, he expanded, he made the pilgrimage, and the jelilu carry his name. The rest is the silence that the tradition has chosen to keep.

## Abu Bakr II

He asked the ocean a question and went to hear the answer. This is how the jelilu carry him. Not as a madman, not as a visionary, not as a tragic figure, but as a mansa who wanted to know what was west of the world he knew, and who went to find out.

The account of his Atlantic expedition reaches the written record through Mansa Musa, who told the story to al-Umari's informants in Cairo during the hajj of 1324-25. Abu Bakr sent an initial fleet of two hundred canoes. Most did not return. He then assembled a larger fleet and went himself. He appointed Musa as his deputy. He provisioned the fleet. He left the Manden in order. And then he paddled west into the Atlantic and did not come back.

What happened to Abu Bakr is not known. The jelilu do not say he died. They say he did not come back. Scholars have debated whether the fleets could have reached the Americas, whether the Atlantic currents in the region could have carried canoes westward, whether the account itself is historical or literary. The debate is real and unresolved. What is certain is that the Manden's oral tradition carries this story, that Musa told it to al-Umari as the reason he became mansa, and that the tradition handles the uncertainty with the precision of a knowledge system that distinguishes between what is known and what is not. Abu Bakr went. He did not come back. The ocean has not answered his question. The jelilu are still waiting.

## The Dyula Merchant (Composite)

The Dyula merchant who appears in Section IV is a composite figure. He is not a single named individual from the historical record. He is drawn from multiple accounts of the Dyula trade network as described in the work of Levtzion, Conrad, Green, and Brooks, and from the ethnographic and historical literature on Mande commercial practice.

This disclosure is not an apology. The Dyula network operated through thousands of individual traders over centuries, and no single trader's name survives from the expansion period in the sources available for this chapter. The composite is built from the texture of the trade itself: the routes, the goods, the methods of exchange, the role of Islam in commercial relationships, the ecology of the savanna-to-forest journey. Every detail is drawn from the historical and ethnographic record. The figure is honest. The technique is disclosed. The Dyula merchant in the chapter carries kola and salt and Islam along the routes because that is what Dyula merchants did, and the physical texture of his journey is grounded in the environments he would have moved through. He is not invented. He is assembled from the evidence of what the trade looked like when it was happening.

## The Gbara (Council)

The gbara is not a figure but an institution, and it deserves notice here because it functions as a character in the chapter. The council of clan heads and elders held authority alongside the mansa within the Kurukan Fuga's framework. It was the assembly that had ratified the covenant after Kirina, and it retained the authority to enforce the covenant's terms, including the removal of a mansa who violated them.

The gbara's deposition of Khalifa is the chapter's central institutional moment. The sources do not give us a detailed account of the proceedings. How the gbara deliberated, who spoke, what arguments were made, the record does not preserve. What it preserves is the outcome: the mansa was removed. The covenant's enforcement mechanism worked. In a chapter where the structural engine is institutional resilience, the gbara is the institution that made resilience possible.
