## B-maya-3 — Ethnographer — Board Review of Maya Late Classic

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Living Maya communities, highland Guatemala, K'iche' calendar tradition.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The claim of "more than six million" Maya language speakers: current estimates range from 6 to 7 million across Guatemala, Mexico, Belize, and Honduras. Accurate.
- The list of Maya language groups (K'iche', Yucatec, Tzeltal, Tzotzil, Mam, Q'eqchi'): accurate and representative of the major groups.
- The description of the aj q'ij (daykeeper) tradition: accurate. The 260-day count is maintained by aj q'ijab' (plural) in highland Guatemala, particularly in K'iche' and Kaqchikel communities.
- The claim that "they have never stopped counting": this is the scholarly consensus. Barbara Tedlock's work (*Time and the Highland Maya*, 1982) documents the unbroken continuity of the 260-day count. However, "never stopped" should be understood as meaning the tradition has been continuously transmitted, not that every community maintained it without interruption. Some communities lost the count during the colonial period; others preserved it. The chapter's framing is acceptable but could note this nuance.
- The Popol Vuh's provenance: correctly described as "a colonial-era alphabetic transcription of what had been an oral and possibly a codical tradition."

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter does NOT fall into "lost civilization" framing. The transformation is presented as a political reorganization, not an ending.
- The final section explicitly states "The Maya did not end."
- The 6 million figure is placed at the close, giving the living tradition the final word.
- The aj q'ij passage treats the contemporary calendar tradition as a continuation, not a remnant.
- The phrase "minds are harder to burn than bark paper" is powerful and honors the knowledge-keepers.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The landing is excellent. The dual-stream close (stone inscriptions and living count) avoids privileging either the archaeological record or the living tradition.
- The chapter refuses the "mystery" framing for the transformation.
- The Popol Vuh is introduced with proper provenance rather than treated as a timeless text.

**What needs correction:**
- Consider adding a note that the continuity of the 260-day count, while well documented, was maintained through active effort against colonial suppression, not simply by inertia. The "surviving the destruction of the books and the suppression of the ceremonies" phrase partly addresses this but could be strengthened.

**Verdict:** APPROVED
