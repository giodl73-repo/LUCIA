## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Ptolemaic Egypt, Era 1 Opening

**Overall:** This opening faces a difficult challenge: telling the story of a foreign dynasty that ruled Egypt from inside. Whose inside? The opening navigates this honestly by making the inside Ptolemy's calculation rather than pretending to be Egyptian. This is the right choice. But it must go further.

**What works:**
- The Egyptian priestly perspective is present and given its own weight: "The priests understood Ptolemy." This is not a throwaway line. The priests are agents, not spectators. They recognize that the way to manage a Macedonian is to make him Egyptian. The intelligence runs in both directions.
- "In Egypt, legitimacy was not seized. It was constructed." This is an Egyptian axiom stated as an Egyptian would state it. The sentence belongs to the culture it describes.
- The absence of judgment is complete. There is no "remarkably" or "sophisticated" or any word that positions the narrator above the material. The Ptolemaic project is presented on its own terms.

**What doesn't:**
- The Egyptian voice is present but secondary. The opening is Ptolemy's story, told from Ptolemy's perspective, with Ptolemy's logic driving the narrative. The priests appear as characters he must satisfy, not as agents with their own project. For this chapter to pass the Achebe Test fully, the Egyptian priestly establishment needs its own agency, its own calculation, its own understanding of what it is doing when it crowns a Macedonian pharaoh. They are not being fooled. They are managing him.
- The Jewish community of Alexandria is mentioned once ("Jewish merchants who had come because the new city was tax-friendly") and then disappears. If they appear, they must matter. If they do not matter to this opening, they should not appear.

**The question I'd push on:**
Who tells this story? Ptolemy is Macedonian. The culture is Egyptian. The city is Greek. The chapter must decide whose inside it inhabits, and it must inhabit that inside without pretending the others do not exist. The opening leans toward Ptolemy's perspective, which is honest, but the chapter will need to show the Egyptian priestly perspective with equal depth and agency. The priests of Ptah at Memphis are not a backdrop. They are co-architects.
