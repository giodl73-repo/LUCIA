## Domain Accuracy Assessment

**On the Saxon campaigns and Verden:**

The chapter correctly states that the Saxon campaigns ran "from 772 to 804."
This span is accurate; the final Saxon submission came in 804 with the
deportation of the remaining unconverted Saxons to Frankish territories.

The Verden massacre is handled with appropriate care. The chapter does not
sensationalize, does not add modern judgment, and presents the tension between
Charles's decision and Alcuin's response accurately. The claim that Alcuin
"found troubling" the massacre is supported by his letters, though his
criticism was characteristically indirect — he wrote about the problems with
forced baptism more than he condemned Verden directly.

**One factual note on Alcuin's language:** The chapter paraphrases Alcuin as
writing that "a person could not be made Christian by the sword." His actual
language (in his letters to Arno of Salzburg and others) was about the
necessity of preaching before baptism, and the priority of instruction over
compulsion. The chapter's paraphrase is accurate in substance. The figures.md
could include the more precise formulation.

**On forced baptism:**

The chapter describes baptism as understood "as membership in the Frankish
political community, partly correct, more than as transformation of the soul."
This rendering is inside the Carolingian understanding: modern scholars
(Matthias Becher, Janet Nelson) have indeed argued that Saxon "conversion"
in the early stages was understood in political as much as religious terms by
both parties. The formulation honors this complexity.

**On Saxon children at Aachen:**

The chapter describes them as "hostages technically though educated as students."
This is accurate: the *Vita Karoli Magni* and the capitularies both document
Saxon hostages at the Frankish court who received education there. Some, like
Aio, became administrators or churchmen in the Frankish service. The
characterization is correct.

**What is missing (optional enrichment, not required):**

The chapter does not mention the Saxon chieftain Widukind, who led the most
serious revolts (including the 782 ambush at Süntel that precipitated Verden)
and who eventually submitted and received baptism in 785, with Charles as his
godfather. This is one of the most remarkable moments of the Saxon conversion:
the leader of the resistance becoming a Christian at the font with the king
as godfather, the relationship of spiritual kinship overwriting the relationship
of military enmity. The chapter's scope may not require this, but it would
deepen Section IV significantly.

**On the "Franci" identity:**

The OVERVIEW uses "Franci — the free men" as the culture's self-designation.
The chapter does not use this term, though it refers to "Frankish" throughout.
This is correct for an era in which the Carolingian project explicitly
constructed a pan-ethnic Christian empire; the self-designation "Franci"
was being actively generalized to include conquered peoples within the
Christian empire rather than restricted to ethnic Franks.

---

## Verdict

**Historically accurate. No errors found.** The Verden treatment is the
chapter's most delicate passage and it succeeds: accurate, undramatized,
from inside the Carolingian frame.

**One optional enrichment:** The Widukind-as-godson moment (785) would
strengthen Section IV's argument about what *correctio* did to its subjects.
If space permits in a future revision, consider adding it. Not required for
this chapter to pass.

**One precision for figures.md:** Alcuin's criticism of Verden should be more
precisely attributed to his concerns about the method (compulsion before
instruction), not presented as a general humanitarian protest. The chapter
gets this right in the body; the figures.md should match.
