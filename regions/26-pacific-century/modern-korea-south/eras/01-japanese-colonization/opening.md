# Opening: The Haegeum and the Name

**1912 — Seoul, Gyeongbokgung Palace Quarter**

The woman's fingers found the haegeum's two strings in the darkness before the song found her voice. This was how the music worked: the strings came first, waiting for the voice to know what they meant. She had been playing since she was seven — forty-three years now — and her fingers still asked the strings the same questions they had asked then: *What do you know that I don't?*

The Japanese administrator sat across from her in the small room, his boots catching the lamplight. He had a document folded on his lap. He had not come to hear the haegeum. The music had been playing only as she waited for him, the way a person hums while the kettle boils.

"Your name," he said, in Korean that was precise and untouched by the accent of home. "Your family name."

"Kim," she said.

"Your given name."

She told him. It meant *brightness* — the character her father had given her, written the way the aristocracy wrote, with the weight of generations in its structure.

The administrator unfolded the document. He did not show it to her. He was reading it to himself, as if confirming something he already knew. "This name," he said, "will need to be replaced."

The haegeum was still in her lap. She could feel it warming from her hands.

"Not today," he said. "Not yet. But when the policy comes — and it will come — you will choose a Japanese name. Or one will be chosen for you." He refolded the document, exactly as it had been. "The music you play. What is it called?"

"Sanjo," she said. "Or jangdan, if there is dancing. This is neither — this is just the strings asking what they need to ask."

The administrator stood. He had not called her by her name. He had only told her it would be gone.

She waited until his footsteps were fully outside — until they had crossed the courtyard and were lost in the sound of the city — before she lifted the haegeum and played. The song came from the same place it always came from, the place where the fingers knew before the mind did. The strings carried the melody the way the Han River carried water: relentless, old, aware that they had been flowing this way long before this room existed and would continue long after this man had forgotten that he had ever come.

In Seoul, in 1912, the administration was still taking time to systematize the erasure. The policies would come. The names would be taken. The schools would teach in Japanese. The newspapers would close if they printed in Korean. The forests would be cut. The rice would be shipped north. But in 1912, there was still room for the haegeum to ask its questions. The strings had not yet been told that the music they carried was forbidden.

The woman knew this would not last. She knew what annexation meant. She had heard what had happened in Taiwan, in Manchuria. She knew the logic: when you capture a people, you must first capture their names. Their language. Their memory.

The haegeum in her lap was already two hundred years old. The strings were made of twisted silk. The wood of the upper chamber had darkened with the sweat of a dozen hands — a dozen women like her, asking the same questions to the same strings, in the same voice that had no name for itself except the name that would be taken.

She set the haegeum down in the darkness and did not play again that night.

By morning, the city had already begun its forgetting.
