## BOARD — B2 (Matras) — Romani Era 1: O Lungo Drom

**Overall:** The linguistic evidence is handled with care and, for a narrative text, with reasonable accuracy. Several points require comment.

**Accuracy findings:**

1. **"Romani preserves features of early medieval Prakrit":** This is a simplification but a defensible one. More precisely, Romani preserves morphological and phonological features consistent with a Central Indo-Aryan language of the early medieval period. The term "Prakrit" is broad, but in context it communicates the right idea to a non-specialist reader. **No change required.**

2. **The loanword strata (Persian > Armenian > Greek):** Correctly ordered. The chapter correctly identifies Persian loanwords as the deepest non-Indian layer, Armenian loanwords as intermediate, and Greek as the most numerous. This is consistent with the scholarly consensus. The chapter correctly notes that the depth of Persian borrowing suggests prolonged contact.

3. **Specific Romani words:** The family terms (dej, dad, phen) are correctly identified as Indo-Aryan. Grast (horse) is correctly traced to Sanskrit. Pani (water) and yag (fire) are correctly identified. The chapter's claim that "foriya" is a Persian loanword for "market" requires a note: the etymology is debated, and some scholars derive it from Greek. **Recommendation:** Either remove foriya or add a caveat. Minor issue.

4. **"The grammar stayed Indo-Aryan":** Correct and important. The borrowings in Romani are predominantly lexical, not grammatical. The core morphology (verb conjugation, case system) remains Indo-Aryan even in the most contact-influenced dialects. This is well-stated.

5. **The genetic evidence paragraph:** Appropriately cautious. The chapter correctly notes the genetic bottleneck and its approximate dating. One caution: the genetic evidence does not straightforwardly "confirm" the linguistic evidence. The two sources of evidence are consistent with each other but operate on different timescales and measure different things. **Recommendation:** Consider "would corroborate" rather than "would confirm." Minor.

6. **The dating of the departure ("somewhere around the tenth or eleventh century"):** Consistent with the current linguistic consensus. Some genetic studies suggest earlier departure with a bottleneck event around this period. The chapter's honest uncertainty here is appropriate.

**Verdict:** PASS. Two minor recommendations (foriya etymology, "confirm" vs. "corroborate"). No factual errors requiring correction.
