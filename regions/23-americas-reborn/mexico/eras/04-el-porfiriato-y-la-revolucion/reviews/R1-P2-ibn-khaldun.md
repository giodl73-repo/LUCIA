# PANEL-1 Review — P2: Ibn Khaldun
## Mexico Era 4 — El Porfiriato y La Revolución

**Lens:** Historical systems — patterns, causation, cycles

---

The structural engine is named clearly in the opening: the covenant between the revolutionary promise and the villages that the promise was made to. This is a system with identifiable mechanics — the ejido as the unit of measurement, the Constitution of 1917 as the written covenant, the gap between the text and its implementation as the terminal condition. What I find satisfying is that the opening already demonstrates the system's logic rather than merely asserting it. The iron box is not a metaphor; it is the actual mechanism by which the system functioned: the village's claim exists in writing, the claim is suppressed by a state apparatus (courts, rurales, hacienda law), and the revolutionary moment is the attempt to restore the claim to its original authority.

What is not yet clear in the opening, and must be addressed in the chapter: the *structural reason* the hacienda system expanded under Díaz rather than contracting. The opening attributes this to Díaz's choices and the protection of elite interests, which is accurate as far as it goes. But the deeper mechanism — that the Ley Lerdo and later the Ley de Baldíos classified communally-held land as "unused" because it did not produce for the market, and therefore made it legally available for transfer — is the hinge on which everything turns. The villages were not merely deprived by corruption; they were dispossessed by a legal system that did not recognize their form of land-holding as ownership. The chapter must explain this mechanism, because without it the hacienda's expansion seems merely political rather than structurally produced.

Also: the relationship between Díaz's political system (the jefes políticos, the caciques, the patronage networks) and the economic system (foreign capital, railroads, the Científicos advising on development policy) is the real motor of the Porfiriato. The opening mentions railroads and telegraph lines; the chapter should treat them as causally linked to dispossession, not merely as background modernization.

**The question I'd push on:** What is the causal mechanism connecting the railroad network to land dispossession in Morelos specifically? The sugar haciendas of Morelos expanded precisely because the railroad gave them access to national markets. The expansion required land; the land came from the villages. This chain — market access, commodity demand, hacienda expansion, village dispossession — is the structural engine the chapter needs to show at the level of mechanism, not just assert.
