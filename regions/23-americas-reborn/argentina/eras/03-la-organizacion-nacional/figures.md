# Figures — La Organización Nacional y la Generación del 80

---

## Julio Argentino Roca (1843–1914)

Roca came from Tucumán, from a family that had been in Argentina since the colonial period — not estanciero rich, but military and professional. He went to war young: at nineteen, fighting for the Unitarios against the last of the Federal caudillos. At thirty-five, he commanded the Campaña del Desierto and opened Patagonia. At thirty-seven, he was president of the republic.

He was not a passionate man in the way that Argentine political life often demanded passion. He was a builder and a calculator. He saw the state as an engineering problem: the territory needed to be organized, the railways needed to run, the immigrants needed to settle, the British capital needed to flow. He pursued each of these with the same methodical efficiency. The fourteen thousand Mapuche and Tehuelche prisoners and the thousands who died in the Patagonian campaigns were, in his accounting, the cost of the project rather than its subject.

He was president twice (1880–1886, 1898–1904) and dominant in Argentine politics throughout the period between. His vision for the country — European in culture, cattle and wheat in economy, oligarchic in governance — was realized almost exactly as he imagined it. That he could not have anticipated the consequences of the immigration he sponsored does not make him less responsible for them. He wanted workers and got citizens. He wanted a European Argentina and got Buenos Aires.

He died in 1914, having lived to see the reforms he would not have chosen: the Ley Sáenz Peña, the mass suffrage, the political organizations of the immigrant second generation. He did not comment extensively on these developments. He was not a man given to commentary on what he could not control.

## Carlos Pellegrini (1846–1906)

Pellegrini's father, Carlos Enrique Pellegrini, had come to Buenos Aires from Savoy in 1828 as an engineer hired to work on the early public works projects of the Argentine government. He married a Buenos Aires woman, built a career, became a recognized figure in the city's life. Carlos grew up bilingual in Spanish and French, studied law, entered politics as a member of the Partido Autonomista Nacional's inner circle.

He was the vice president who became president when the 1890 financial crisis forced Juárez Celman to resign. The Baring crisis — the near-collapse of Argentina's primary British creditor bank due to the country's overleveraged debt — required a negotiated settlement with European creditors, and Pellegrini conducted it. He was skilled at this. He had the manner of a man that European bankers could trust: the suit, the French, the institutional connections. He stabilized the economy without fundamental change to the model that had nearly broken it.

The irony of Pellegrini's position was not one he would have recognized as irony. He was the son of an immigrant who had become the president of the oligarchy. His presidency was a demonstration that the assimilation the Generación del 80 expected was possible: if you arrived early enough, married into the right families, and adopted the right forms, you could complete the journey. What he could not see — because it had not yet happened when he died in 1906 — was that the mass immigration of the 1880s and 1890s would produce a generation that had a different relationship to the journey. The second-generation Italian and Spanish Argentines who would vote for Yrigoyen in 1916 had not completed Pellegrini's assimilation. They had invented something else instead.

## Ángel Villoldo (1861–1919)

Villoldo is the most important figure in the early tango's transition from the conventillo to the world, and he is also largely invisible in the standard historical record. He was born in Buenos Aires. He had some formal musical education. He worked as a circus clown, a street vendor, a typographer — the itinerant employment of a man who was also a musician, in a period when music was not a stable profession. He began writing tangos in the 1890s and by 1903 had produced "El Choclo," which became one of the defining pieces of the early period.

What made Villoldo significant was not his musical genius — other composers of the period were equally skilled — but his mobility. He moved between the conventillo milongas and the middle-class dance halls. He traveled to Paris in 1907 and 1913 and recorded there, understanding before most of his contemporaries that the international market existed. He wrote in Lunfardo, the Buenos Aires street argot that mixed Italian, Spanish, and African-derived vocabulary, and he wrote without apology. His lyrics were not refined. They were funny, melancholy, sometimes bawdy, and entirely specific to the world that had produced them.

He died in 1919, just as the tango he had helped shape was completing its transformation into an internationally recognized art form. He did not die wealthy. He had been, throughout his career, a working musician in a city that was learning to recognize what working musicians had made.

## Hipólito Yrigoyen (1852–1933)

Yrigoyen is the era's terminal figure. He appears in this chapter not as the protagonist of his own era — that is Era 4's task — but as the form the European-validation loop's legitimacy failure took when it finally arrived.

He was enigmatic by design. He gave almost no interviews. He wrote few letters that survived. He did not explain himself. He was a school teacher turned customs official turned political organizer who spent twenty-five years building the Radical Civic Union into a national network of local committees before he won a single national election. His patience was absolute and his methods were opaque. People who worked with him for decades said they did not know what he believed, only what he did.

What he did was become the first president of Argentina elected under conditions that approximated genuine democracy. The immigrant second generation — the children of the conventillo, the grandchildren of the estancias, the sons and daughters of the people who had built the tango without being able to claim it — voted for him in enormous numbers. They recognized in his refusal to be validated by anyone — not by the oligarchy, not by foreign powers, not by the European financial system — something that corresponded to their own experience.

He was not romantic about Argentina's European aspirations. He was not interested in European validation at all. This was, in 1916, a genuinely new posture in Argentine national politics.

## The Afro-Argentine Community

George Reid Andrews, in his 1980 study *The Afro-Argentines of Buenos Aires*, estimated that Afro-Argentines had constituted as much as thirty percent of Buenos Aires' population in the early nineteenth century. By the time of the 1887 census — the last Argentine census to record race — they were approximately two percent of the city's population. The causes were multiple: high casualty rates in the independence wars and the War of the Triple Alliance, the 1871 yellow fever epidemic, demographic absorption into the arriving European immigrant population through intermarriage, and the simple statistical dilution of a constant population by the massive European arrivals of the 1880s and 1890s.

What did not disappear was the musical tradition. The candombe, the African-derived ritual percussion form maintained through the carnival comparsas, had been the bass line under the early tango's development. The tango's characteristic syncopation, its rhythmic relationship between the 2/4 pulse and the melodic phrases that cut across it, carries the candombe's structural logic. This was not acknowledged in the period's musical commentary, which emphasized the Italian and Spanish folk music contributions. It has been documented by later musicologists working from recordings of early tango and from comparative analysis with the surviving candombe traditions.

The three Afro-Argentine women in the conventillo on Calle Defensa are not figures whose names are recorded. They are present as the fact of continuity: they were there before the Italian from Genoa arrived, and the music being played in the courtyard carried their tradition's rhythm without anyone in the courtyard necessarily knowing this.
