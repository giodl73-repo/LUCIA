# Yanomami — "Human Beings"

They call themselves Yanomami — "human beings" — a name that locates the boundary of full humanity within the community rather than as a universal category. This is not unusual in the world's cultures; it is, in fact, the norm. What makes the Yanomami's situation particular is that this name has come to stand, in twentieth-century anthropological and political discourse, for one of the most studied and simultaneously most contested peoples in the world — a community whose internal life was observed, described, disputed, romanticized, and weaponized by outside observers with an intensity that the Yanomami themselves have been contesting from the moment they could find platforms from which to respond.

Their world is the highland forest between the Orinoco and Amazon headwaters — the Parima highlands and the Amazonas territory of Venezuela and Brazil, a landscape of elevated forest, tepui (table mountain) formations, and the first small rivers that become the Orinoco and the Amazon. The Yanomami live in the shabono — the large circular communal house that is the Yanomami world's most distinctive physical expression. The shabono is a continuous structure, its sections assigned to family groups, its central plaza open to the sky. The shabono is the community: everyone who lives under the same roof is part of the same political and social unit, and the decision to build a new shabono in a new location is the decision to constitute a new community. The house is the people.

Key places:
- **Parima highlands** — the mountainous core of Yanomami territory; the watershed between the Orinoco and Amazon drainage systems; the densest Yanomami population
- **Marahuaka mountain** — in Yanomami cosmology, a sacred mountain; the mythological source of certain plants and forces; the center of the world in some Yanomami accounts
- **Catrimani, Demini, Toototobi rivers** — major rivers in the Brazilian Yanomami territory; shabono communities along these waterways were the focus of extensive missionary and anthropological contact in the twentieth century
- **Yanomami territory (TIYAN)** — the Indigenous Territory formally demarcated by the Brazilian government in 1992; the legal basis for the Yanomami's territorial rights

## What Defined Them

**The shabono as the world's center.** The circular communal house is not simply a building. It is the community — its architecture is the community's social structure made physical. Family sections arranged around the central plaza, no doors (entry through any section), the roof thatched from the forest materials that the community manages. When a shabono deteriorates (every few years) and the community moves to build a new one, the move is also a social reset: the new building is the opportunity to renegotiate spatial arrangements, to accommodate new alliances and estrangements, to constitute the community fresh in the physical space. The shabono is not permanent. The community is.

**The hekura and the shamanic cosmos.** The Yanomami cosmos is populated by hekura — small spirit beings who inhabit mountains, waterfalls, animals, plants, and the bodies of the shamans (shapori) who invite them in. The shapori is the person through whom the hekura act in the human world — healing, diagnosing illness, negotiating with the forces that govern the weather and the forest. Becoming a shapori is a demanding calling: the initiation involves extended fasting, the consumption of yakoana (a hallucinogenic powder made from the Virola tree), and the sustained effort of learning to host and direct the hekura. The shapori's work is continuous, not occasional — the shamanic management of the cosmos is an ongoing obligation.

**Alliance as the constant work of politics.** Yanomami political life is organized around the management of alliance — the creation, maintenance, and sometimes rupture of relationships between shabono communities through feasting, trade, marriage exchange, and ceremony. Because no shabono is self-sufficient in all the goods and social connections it needs, every community is in ongoing negotiation with its neighbors. The feasts (reahu) at which communities host each other are political events as much as social ones: the host community demonstrates its productive capacity and its generosity; the guests acknowledge the relationship. Feasting is diplomacy.

**Reahu — the feast of the dead.** The Yanomami consume the ashes of their dead. After death, the body is cremated and the bones reduced to ash; the ash is mixed with plantain soup and consumed by the community at a ceremony (reahu) that transforms the dead person's physical substance into the community's ongoing life. This was, in early anthropological accounts, the detail that was most sensationalized by outsiders. In Yanomami understanding, it was the most intimate possible form of honoring the dead: to incorporate the ancestor's substance into the bodies of those who loved them, to refuse the separation of death by making it literal.

**The Yanomami as witnesses.** The Yanomami have lived through a twentieth century of extraordinary disruption: epidemic disease, missionary pressure, gold rush violence, anthropological controversy, and governmental indifference or malice. They have also produced, in Davi Kopenawa, one of the most eloquent indigenous voices of the twentieth century — a Yanomami shaman and spokesman whose account of the Yanomami world and its destruction, The Falling Sky (written with Bruce Albert), is one of the great documents of indigenous testimony. The Yanomami are not simply the objects of outside attention. They are its critics.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Parima World:** A shapori in the Parima highlands is calling the hekura at sunset. The yakoana smoke rises. The hekura of the waterfall is being invited in. The community in the shabono behind him is going about its evening — children playing in the central plaza, women tending fires, men returning from the hunt. The shapori's work is not separate from this ordinary life. It is its maintenance.

**2 — The Frontier Encounter:** The first rubber tapper to reach the edge of Yanomami territory is greeted with arrows and retreats. The Yanomami community that shot the arrows discusses what they have seen: men with metal tools and an unknown disease that some of the traders they encounter on the margins are already carrying. The discussion about what to do about the frontier is the discussion about the next generation's world.

**3 — The Anthropological Century:** A Yanomami community near the Catrimani river has been visited by a missionary doctor, an anthropologist, and a Brazilian government agent in the same month. Each has brought something: medicine, questions, a census form. Each has taken something: blood samples, ethnographic data, a record of names and locations. The community is trying to figure out which visitors are safe and which are not. The criteria are not obvious.

**4 — The Territory and Its Defenders:** Davi Kopenawa is speaking at the United Nations. Behind him, in the Yanomami Territory in Brazil, the garimpeiros are again moving their illegal gold dredges into the rivers. The international speech and the river poison are happening simultaneously. The shaman who speaks for the forest is also the man whose people's children are sick from mercury.
