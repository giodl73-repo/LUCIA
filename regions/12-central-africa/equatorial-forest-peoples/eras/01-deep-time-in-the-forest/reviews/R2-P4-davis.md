# R2-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Review, Round 2 — Notes

**Chapter:** Deep Time in the Forest — Era 1
**Reviewer:** Wade Davis (P-4)
**Stage:** PANEL-2

---

The production bible has responded to the key questions from Round 1. The epistemological framework — relational knowing, knowledge verified by the non-human world — is now named and present across all five sections. The identification of the honeyguide relationship as the template for all other knowledge practices in the chapter is exactly right. When the reader has understood how the honeyguide call works, they have understood how tracking knowledge works, how medicinal plant knowledge works, how the molimo ceremony works. The template does real structural work.

I am satisfied with the treatment of the ekila concept. The notes are careful: "the restriction is not superstition — it is a management system." But the chapter must go further than calling it a management system. "Management system" is still an outside-frame term — it describes what the restriction does from a functional perspective, not what it is from inside the worldview. From inside, the ekila restriction is about maintaining relationship. You do not eat an animal you are in a particular relationship with because that would break the relationship, and the relationship is what gives you access to the animal's world. The chapter should find the inside formulation.

The diversity issue (Mbuti vs. Aka vs. Baka) is handled well in the notes. The decision to name specific groups in specific sections is right. What connects them is not identity but a shared epistemological orientation — the forest as a relationship to be maintained, not a resource to be extracted. The chapter should make this explicit once, then demonstrate it through the specifics.

My remaining concern: Section 3, the music. The notes describe the Aka hocket technique as argument — many independent streams weaving into coherence without a conductor. This is right. But the chapter must render the sound, not just describe its structure. Arom's accounts give us technical descriptions that are analytically precise but phenomenologically thin. The chapter needs to find a way to put the reader inside the sound — what it feels like to be surrounded by Aka polyphony, what the body experiences when the voices weave. This is the Kapuscinski problem applied to music: sensory rendering, not structural description.

**Assessment:** The production bible is ready. The music section needs careful execution — the difference between describing complexity and rendering it. All other sections are well-planned.
