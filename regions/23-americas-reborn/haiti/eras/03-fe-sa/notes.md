## 1. Sources

### Tier 1 — Primary
- Toussaint Louverture, *Mémoire du général Toussaint L'Ouverture* (written at Fort de Joux, 1802) — his account of the revolution and his relationship with the French; primary voice for Toussaint's self-understanding
- Jean-Jacques Dessalines, proclamation abolishing slavery and declaring independence, Gonaïves, January 1, 1804 — the Act of Independence as primary source; Dessalines's voice is direct and absolute
- Toussaint Louverture, *Constitution of 1801* — the political theology of the revolution written in law; Toussaint as author of a republic that abolished slavery while nominally remaining French
- The prayer attributed to Boukman Dutty, Bois Caïman, August 14, 1791 — fragment, disputed translation; handle with care per Achebe P-3 review

### Tier 2 — Academic
- C.L.R. James, *The Black Jacobins: Toussaint L'Ouverture and the San Domingo Revolution* (1938, rev. 1963) — foundational; James's Marxist lens is valuable for the structural analysis but should not crowd out the Vodou-as-political-theology dimension
- Laurent Dubois, *Avengers of the New World: The Story of the Haitian Revolution* (2004, Harvard) — the best modern scholarly synthesis; essential for the factual skeleton
- Laurent Dubois, *Haiti: The Aftershocks of History* (2012) — background for Era 4 but useful for understanding the full arc
- Sidney Mintz, *Sweetness and Power* (1985) — the plantation economy; used in the opening for the ledger/seven-year calculation
- Karen McCarthy Brown, *Mama Lola: A Vodou Priestess in Brooklyn* (1991) — Vodou as lived practice; essential for Davis P-4's demand for method-level engagement with the knowledge system

### Tier 3 — Scholarly Secondary
- Wade Davis, *The Serpent and the Rainbow* (1985) — Haitian Vodou from an ethnobotanist's perspective; useful for sensory and experiential detail, but Davis himself has noted that the book's sensationalism should be read critically
- Carolyn Fick, *The Making of Haiti: The Saint Domingue Revolution from Below* (1990) — crucial for the perspective of ordinary enslaved people, not just Toussaint; key for figures.md

### Note on Source Asymmetry
Most surviving accounts of Bois Caïman come from French colonial sources, written to discredit the uprising. The blood oath, the pig's sacrifice — these details may be accurate, may be embellished, may be partially fabricated. Per RUBRIC v1.5 (Source Asymmetry as Narrative Technique): treat the French account's biases as structural evidence about the French, not as primary evidence about the ceremony. What Haitian oral tradition and subsequent Vodou scholarship say about Bois Caïman should carry equal or greater weight.

## 2. Key Figures

### Boukman Dutty (d. December 1791)
A coachman (which meant freedom of movement) and a Vodou houngan (which meant spiritual authority). Presided at Bois Caïman. Captured and executed by December 1791, his head displayed at Cap-Français. His death did not end the uprising. He is honored in Haitian memory as a founding figure of the revolution.

**Figure note:** Boukman's historical record is thin — we know his role but not his interior life. Per RUBRIC v1.1 (Honest Limits as Technique): the thinness is characterization. What Boukman IS in Haitian memory is more alive than what the colonial record preserved of him. Figure in figures.md should work from the tradition's own construction of him.

### Cécile Fatiman (c. 1771–1883)
Attended Bois Caïman; described in subsequent accounts as the woman who came into possession and danced, who wielded the machete. Mixed-race (reportedly her mother was enslaved, her father a French planter). Lived to 112 years. Her longevity is part of the tradition's telling of Bois Caïman — she survived to remember. Handle her possession not as hysteria or performance but as the tradition understands it: the lwa arrived.

### Toussaint Louverture (c. 1743–1803)
Born into slavery; freed before the revolution; literate; organized the northern uprising's military structure after Boukman's death. Allied with Spain against France, then switched — declared for the French Republic when the French abolished slavery in 1794. Defeated British forces. Became governor-general for life under the 1801 Constitution. Captured through treachery in 1802 (invited to negotiate, then arrested). Died at Fort de Joux in April 1803 in a cold cell in the Jura Mountains, asking: "In overthrowing me, you have done no more than cut down the trunk of the tree of Black liberty in Saint-Domingue. It will spring back from the roots, for they are numerous and deep."

**Figure note:** Toussaint is the figure the chapter MUST not make into a hero-tale. He is complex: brilliant organizer and military commander, but also authoritarian, willing to ally with former slave-owners, willing to coerce free Black people back to plantation labor after liberation. James and Dubois both handle this; the chapter must too. His greatness is not diminished by his contradictions — it is completed by them.

### Jean-Jacques Dessalines (c. 1758–1806)
Born into slavery; worked under brutal conditions on north plain plantations; became one of Toussaint's generals; took over after Toussaint's capture. Ordered the massacre of the remaining French white population in early 1804. Declared Ayitian independence on January 1, 1804. Named himself Emperor Jacques I in 1804. Assassinated 1806.

**Figure note:** Dessalines is the figure the chapter must not flinch from. His violence — the massacre of the French — is documented, deliberate, and cannot be minimized. But it must be rendered from inside his logic, not judged from outside. He had spent his life watching calculated brutality made into a business. The chapter should show his calculation, not condemn it. The Achebe Test at maximum here.

### Marie-Jeanne Lamartinière (dates uncertain)
A soldier of the revolution, one of several women who fought in battle; known for her defense of Crête-à-Pierrot (1802). Her story represents the revolutionary participation of women that the male-centered military history tends to erase.

### An Unnamed Figure
Per Tuchman P-1's recommendation: one ordinary person whose experience threads through the arc. I will use the device of a composite figure drawing from Carolyn Fick's *The Making of Haiti* — a field laborer on the north plain who participates in the uprising, lives through the military campaigns, and reaches January 1, 1804. This will be disclosed in figures.md as a composite drawn from documented accounts.

## 3. Structural Engine

**Engine:** Libète — freedom as absolute and non-negotiable claim.

The Haitian revolutionary engine is not a simple "freedom versus slavery" binary. It is a specific claim: that liberty is not divisible, not negotiable, not conditionally granted. The French Republic abolished slavery in 1794 — but conditionally, strategically, in the context of war. Toussaint allied with this. Dessalines did not trust it. The 1801 Constitution abolished slavery while nominally keeping the island within the French Republic. France reneged; Napoleon sent an army to restore slavery. This is when the engine reveals its logic: the moment anyone tried to put conditions on libète, the resistance escalated.

**Mechanism:** Every attempt to qualify or roll back freedom generates a proportionally larger assertion of it. The French try to restore slavery in 1802; the formerly enslaved fight to the last. The British offer terms; Toussaint negotiates. Napoleon offers terms; Dessalines burns the plantations.

**Terminal condition (for this era):** The independence declaration of January 1, 1804 does not terminate the engine — it transforms it. The enemy that required this particular form of libète (the colonial slave system) is defeated. But the engine of absolute freedom-as-claim will continue to operate in subsequent eras, now turned against the internal contradictions of the new republic: who gets to claim libète in Ayiti? The debt to France will reveal that the terminal condition of Era 3 is not victory but a new battlefield.

Per RUBRIC v1.6 (Terminal Condition Types): This is closest to **Vindicating** (Armenian type) — the engine's founding claim (libète as absolute) is vindicated by the revolution's success, but the vindication reveals the next challenge. The engine does not end; it is proven, then tested again.

## 4. Voice

**Discovered register:** Vodou-Revolutionary (new, flagged in GATE-1 for potential Innovation Log)

**Primary rhythm:** Call-and-Response at paragraph level — a building sentence (the invocation, the gathering, the argument) followed by a short declarative sentence that holds the covenant (the lwa's answer, the fact of the burning, the fact of independence). The gap between the invocation and the answer IS the voice's pause mechanism.

**Secondary rhythm:** Scholarly-Revolutionary — the network, the runners, the organizational structure beneath the surface of the colonial order. The Fulani/Dan Fodio register contributes the sense of knowledge being mobilized, of a distributed system becoming a governing order. Here it is a spiritual network becoming an army.

**Em-dash discipline:** The register's pause mechanism is the period. Every place where an em-dash might appear should instead become a new sentence or be eliminated. The rhythm does not interrupt itself; it calls and waits for the answer.

**Sensory plan (per Kapuściński P-5):**
- Burning cane: smell of sweet smoke, the color of the flames at night, visible from ships
- Cap-Français: the city's architecture, the colonial capital's physical layout, the sounds at dawn versus the sounds during the siege
- Mountains: Ayiti = "land of mountains" — the terrain as a military and symbolic fact
- Plantation bell versus forest drum: the two soundscapes of the revolution's geography
- Fort de Joux, Jura Mountains: where Toussaint died — the cold, the altitude, the contrast with Saint-Domingue

## 5. Section Plan

**Opening (~800w — already written):** Bois Caïman, August 14, 1791. Boukman, Cécile Fatiman. The covenant. The colonial context. The structure beneath the surface.

**Section 2 — The Rising and Its Generals (~900w):** The first weeks of the uprising. Boukman's death and the continuation. The emergence of Jean-François and Biassou as early commanders. The transition to organized military force. Toussaint's emergence. Use Toussaint's own account where possible; he is a literate primary source.

**Section 3 — Three Wars at Once (~1000w):** 1791–1798. The revolution is simultaneously: an uprising against the French planters, a war between the British and French for the colony, a war between the formerly enslaved and the gens de couleur (free mixed-race population), a war between Spain and France. Toussaint's switching of sides in 1794 when France abolished slavery. The British defeat (1798) — the largest British military defeat in the Caribbean to that point. Show the structural complexity without losing the inside perspective.

**Section 4 — The 1801 Constitution and Napoleon's Betrayal (~800w):** Toussaint writes a constitution. He is governor-general for life. Napoleon sends Leclerc's army to restore the old order (1802). Yellow fever decimates the French. Toussaint is captured through treachery. His last words at Fort de Joux. The revolution continues without him.

**Section 5 — Dessalines and the Fire (~700w):** Dessalines takes command. The burning of the plantations and the towns the French used as bases. The massacre of the French population in early 1804. The logic of this act from inside the revolution. The naming of the island: Ayiti, not Saint-Domingue. Not the French name. The Taíno name. Reclaiming the land's own name is reclaiming the land.

**Section 6 — January 1, 1804, Gonaïves (~500w):** The declaration. Dessalines's words: "I want the assets of the country to be equitably divided." The first Black republic. What it meant to the hemisphere. What it would cost — the isolation, the French, the Americans, the embargo that is Era 4's story. The landing: the engine of libète is proven. It is now being tested in a different register.

## 6. Panel Pressure Points (anticipated)

- **Achebe:** The treatment of the massacre of 1804 will be the highest-stakes Achebe Test in the chapter. From inside Dessalines's logic, it is the closing of a door that the French had held open for returning. From outside, it is mass killing. The chapter must render the inside without flinching, without apologizing, and without winking at the reader. Dessalines knew what he was doing. Show him knowing.

- **Davis:** The Vodou knowledge system must be shown at method level, not summary level. Which lwa? Which rite? What is the specific meaning of the blood covenant in terms of the religious tradition's own logic? Karen McCarthy Brown's work is essential here.

- **Ibn Khaldun:** The structural complexity of the three simultaneous wars must be analyzed, not just narrated. WHY did Toussaint's switch in 1794 make strategic sense? What was the system of loyalties and betrayals? The asabiyyah of the formerly enslaved is the engine; the chapter must show its operating logic.

- **Kapuściński:** Terrain, smell, sound, temperature throughout. The north plain in August. The mountain passes in November. The cold cell at Fort de Joux.

## 7. Open Questions

1. The Bois Caïman ceremony: which elements are from the Haitian/Vodou tradition's own memory, and which are French colonial embellishment? The chapter should distinguish between these with appropriate care.

2. Cécile Fatiman: the sources describe her but give her no further narrative after Bois Caïman (except that she lived to 112). Her longevity is itself a fact — what did she say, in whatever account exists, about what she experienced?

3. The gens de couleur: the revolution's most internally complicated relationship. Alexandre Pétion (mixed-race) fought with the revolution; many gens de couleur initially allied with the planters. The inside voice should not paper over these divisions.

4. Voice discipline: does the Vodou-Revolutionary register hold through 5000 words? The test is in the writing. Zero em-dashes. Every pause is the period held between the call and the answer.
