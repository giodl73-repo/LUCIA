## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of The Great Expansion (Opening)

**Overall:** This is the opening I have been waiting for. The navigation knowledge system is treated with the intellectual depth it deserves. This is not "wayfinding as folk wisdom." This is a cognitive technology rendered at the level of method.

**What works:**
- The swell-reading is shown at the level of practice. Kautai does not "sense" the island. He reads interference patterns in wave propagation caused by land mass deflection. The opening describes an empirical practice with the same precision one would use for celestial mechanics, and it does so without translating it into Western scientific terms. This is exactly what I have spent decades arguing for.
- "The songs were the charts. The body was the instrument. The ocean was the world." This is the paragraph that justifies the entire chapter. Three sentences. Each one a thesis. Each one presented as self-evident. No qualifiers, no "remarkably," no "surprisingly." Just what is.
- The moving-island reference frame is the most important concept in the opening, and it is delivered correctly: as a navigational paradigm, not as a "mental trick" or a "belief system." It is how the navigator actually operates. The framing makes it clear that this is not a lesser version of Western navigation with fixed charts and compass headings. It is a different paradigm, and it works.
- The tabua described as "polished to the color of old cream, heavy in the hand" is a small detail that does enormous work. You can feel it. This is material culture rendered with the specificity the knowledge system demands.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening does not yet convey the SCALE of the achievement. A Tongan navigator sailing from Tongatapu to Fiji or the outlier atolls is crossing hundreds of miles of open ocean. The opening makes the voyage feel like a day trip. The chapter needs to make the reader feel the vastness of the Pacific and the precision required to cross it. This is not a Mediterranean crossing where you can see land from land. This is open ocean.
- I want more on the apprenticeship. The grandfather scene is a flash. In reality, navigational training lasted years, sometimes decades. A boy committed to memory the positions of hundreds of stars, the seasonal patterns of dozens of swells, the behavioral patterns of multiple bird species. The depth of the training is part of the achievement.

**The question I'd push on:**
How does the chapter handle etak? The moving-island reference frame is mentioned but not named. The concept needs its Tongan name, and it needs to be explored at genuine depth. This is the single most intellectually significant concept in Polynesian navigation, and the chapter should treat it the way the Mali chapter treated the Kurukan Fuga: as the structural and intellectual center of the culture's achievement.
