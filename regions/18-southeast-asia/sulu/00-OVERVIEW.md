# Sulu Sultanate — The Sultanate of the Sea

We are the Sulu Sultanate — the Islamic maritime state of the Sulu Archipelago, the string of islands between the Philippines and Borneo — and we are a kingdom that no colonial power fully reduced. The Spanish fought us for three centuries. The Dutch fought us. The British fought us. All of them won battles; none of them pacified the Sulu Sea. We are still here, in one form or another, as our history approaches the present, because a maritime polity that trades, raids, and retreats among a thousand islands in an inland sea is genuinely ungovernable by a land-based colonial administration.

We are Muslim — Islamized from Borneo in the fifteenth century — and the sultanate was from its foundation an Islamic state with all that implies: shari'a law for the community, the ulama (scholars) alongside the datu chiefs, the hajj pilgrimage to Mecca that connected us to the global Islamic community, and the Islamic commercial network that gave our merchants access to markets from Arabia to Java. But we are also distinctly Sulu: our Islam is lived in outrigger canoes, in the annual trading expeditions across the Sulu Sea, in the coral-reef maritime world that defines our geography. The Sulu Sultanate was Islam in a canoe civilization.

## What Defined Them

**The Sulu Sea as domain.** The Sulu Sultanate's sovereignty was maritime, not territorial: the sultanate governed the sea-lanes between its island ports, taxed the trade that moved through them, and projected power through the fleet rather than the garrison. The coral islands of the Sulu Archipelago were not agricultural bases but commercial nodes — places where ships provisioned, goods exchanged, and the sultan's authority was displayed. This maritime sovereignty was genuinely difficult for land-based colonial administrations to destroy.

**Islamic authority and the datu system.** The Sulu Sultanate combined two authority structures: the Islamic chain from sultan to imam to ordinary Muslim, based on religious learning and correct practice; and the indigenous datu hierarchy, based on genealogy, wealth, and followers. The sultan occupied both simultaneously: he was the supreme datu by birth and the supreme Muslim authority by appointment. The ulama scholars provided legitimacy; the datu warriors provided force; the merchants provided revenue. The system was resilient because it was multiple.

**The slave trade.** Sulu's political economy was substantially organized around the capture and trade of slaves from surrounding areas — the southern Philippines, Borneo, and the Celebes. The Iranun and Balangingi raiders who operated under Sulu sponsorship were among the most feared maritime warriors in the region, conducting raids hundreds of miles from the Sulu heartland. The slaves they captured were used in the Sulu pearl-diving and fishing industries, traded to other Asian ports, and incorporated into the patron-client relationships of Sulu aristocratic households. The slave-raiding economy was the material foundation of the sultanate's power.

**Ungovernable geography.** The thousand islands and coral reefs of the Sulu Sea were the sultanate's greatest military asset. Spanish warships could bombard Jolo but could not prevent the sultan and his court from simply moving to a different island. Dutch gunboats could intercept Sulu trading vessels but could not prevent them from finding alternative routes through the archipelago. The colonial campaigns against Sulu repeatedly captured the capital and repeatedly found that capturing the capital did not end the sultanate — because the sultanate was the sea, and no colonial power could control the sea.

**The Sabah claim.** The Sulu Sultanate's 1878 agreement with the British North Borneo Chartered Company — in which Sabah was "leased" (the Sulu side says) or "ceded" (the British side says) — created a territorial dispute that continues to the present day. The Republic of the Philippines, as successor to the old Sulu Sultanate, claims Sabah as Philippine territory based on the Sulu original sovereignty. Malaysia administers Sabah as one of its states. The dispute is formally unresolved.

## The Chapters

**1 — Sharif ul-Hashim:** He arrives from Johor with his religious credentials and his Arabized genealogy. He marries the local chief's daughter. He becomes the first Sultan of Sulu. The islands' Austronesian culture is about to be layered with Islamic governance and connected to the global Muslim commercial network.

**2 — The Market at Jolo:** Ships from Borneo, Macau, Visayas, Celebes, and Java are in the harbor. The market is organized by commodity: pearls on one side, beeswax on another, forest products, dried fish, imported cloth. The sultan's tax collectors are present at every transaction. The Sulu economy is working.

**3 — The Spanish Campaign:** The Spanish governor has sent the ships against Jolo again. They are the third Spanish expedition this decade. The previous two captured and burned the market town. The sultan moved. The market relocated. The Spanish left. The market returned. This is not failure; this is the Sulu strategy.

**4 — The Balangingi Raiders:** The raiding fleet has left for the northern Philippines islands. Three hundred vessels. They will return with captives, stolen goods, and the fear that enforces the Sulu dominance of the sea-lanes. The colonial power will respond with gunboats. The raiders will return to the reefs they know and the colonials do not.

**5 — The Last Stand:** The American expedition against Sulu's last strongholds encounters resistance at the Battle of Bud Dajo (1906) and Bud Bagsak (1913). The American forces win. The sultanate's formal resistance ends. The sultan becomes a ceremonial figure. The Sulu people remain.
