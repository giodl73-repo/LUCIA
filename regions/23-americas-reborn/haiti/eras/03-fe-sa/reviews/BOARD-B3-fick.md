# BOARD Review — B-3: Carolyn Fick
## Haiti, Era 3: Fè Sa — The Revolution

**Role:** Author of *The Making of Haiti: The Saint Domingue Revolution from Below* (1990); historian of the revolution's popular dimension and the participation of ordinary enslaved people
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Assessment of the Popular Dimension

My book argued that the historiography of the Haitian Revolution had been dominated by its leaders — Toussaint, Dessalines, Pétion — to the near-total exclusion of the hundreds of thousands of people who actually made the revolution by their labor, their survival, and their insistence on the kind of freedom they understood. The composite figure in figures.md is a direct engagement with that argument, and it is the most welcome element of the chapter.

The composite woman of the north plain is methodologically disclosed, drawn from documented accounts, and rendered with appropriate specificity — her relationship to the plantation bell, her knowledge of the lwa through her mother, her presence in or near the uprising, her survival. The final image (she noticed the bell did not ring on January 1, 1804) is the right ending for that figure: small-scale, bodily, specific, and carrying more weight than any proclamation.

**The key contribution:** The final paragraphs of figures.md — on the smallholding and subsistence farming decisions of ordinary freed people after independence — are historiographically important. This is the popular class's response to the question "what kind of freedom is this?" They answered by moving away from the plantation rows. The chapter correctly frames this not as failure or ingratitude but as the assertion of *libète* against a new set of conditions. This is my argument from *The Making of Haiti* rendered in narrative form.

## Factual and Contextual Notes

**Jean-François and Biassou:** The chapter names them as early commanders, which is accurate. It might be worth noting (perhaps in notes.md rather than the chapter) that Jean-François actually received a pardon from the Spanish crown and emigrated to Cuba after the revolution turned in 1794, which is one of the more complicated outcomes of the early revolutionary leadership.

**The gens de couleur:** The chapter handles this complexity appropriately. The note on Vincent Ogé is accurate — his uprising excluded the enslaved and he was executed. The inclusion of Alexandre Pétion as a figure who fought for the revolution is accurate. The chapter correctly notes that "the lines were not clean" without pretending they were or explaining away the class dynamics.

**Women in the revolution:** Marie-Jeanne Lamartinière's inclusion in figures.md is appropriate and her role at Crête-à-Pierrot is documented. The chapter could also have named Sanité Bélair, who fought with Toussaint, was captured, and was executed in 1802 — refusing the blindfold at her execution. She is a figure whose inclusion would strengthen the representation of women in the military dimension. This is a suggestion for consideration, not a requirement.

## Overall Assessment

The chapter achieves what I spent my academic career arguing was possible: a treatment of the Haitian Revolution that keeps the people at the center without erasing the leaders, and that renders the popular class's understanding of freedom as a serious political claim rather than a romantic aspiration.

**Board recommendation:** APPROVE with optional addition of Sanité Bélair to figures.md if word count permits.
