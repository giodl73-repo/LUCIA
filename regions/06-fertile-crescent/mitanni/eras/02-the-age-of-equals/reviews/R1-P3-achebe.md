## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of The Age of Equals (Opening)

**Overall:** The voice is right. I do not detect an outsider telling this story. The Mitanni world is rendered from inside its own logic, and the diplomatic system is presented as self-evident rather than exotic.

**What works:**
- "The gold was not payment. It was friendship rendered material, brotherhood made visible, and its quantity mattered precisely because it was not a price." This is the culture speaking. No outside observer would frame it this way. The sentence inhabits the diplomatic worldview rather than describing it.
- The Kikkuli manual's Indo-Aryan vocabulary is handled with precision: it is not exoticized, not presented as a curiosity, but as a fact of the tradition. "An older language from the east" lets the detail live without making it a spectacle.
- The absence of judgment is genuine, not performed. The opening does not say "remarkably sophisticated" or "complex system." It simply shows the system operating, and the sophistication is self-evident.
- The king's calculation regarding his daughter is presented as rational within his world. Tadukhipa is not a victim offered up; she is a diplomatic instrument wielded by a king who understands the game. The agency is the court's, not the reader's pity.

**What doesn't:**
- I notice that the opening is told almost entirely through the male court perspective. Tadukhipa is the subject of the transaction but has no presence of her own. I understand that the diplomatic correspondence is the primary source and it is dominated by the kings, but the three hundred and seventeen women who accompanied Gilukhipa suggest a female world around this transaction that could be rendered, even briefly. The princess who crosses the distance is not a parcel.
- "The messenger came from Egypt" presupposes Egypt as the initiator. In the Amarna letters, Tushratta is as often the one demanding and reminding as the one receiving. The opening should ensure that Mitanni's agency is not subtly diminished by presenting them as the respondent rather than a full participant.

**The question I'd push on:**
Whose story is this? If it is Mitanni's story, then Tushratta should not merely "consider" the request. He should be the one shaping the terms. The letters show him as a persistent, sometimes sharp negotiator. I want to see the king's hand on the lever, not just his eye on the calculation.
