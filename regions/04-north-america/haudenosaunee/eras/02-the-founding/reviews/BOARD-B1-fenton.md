## B-1 — William N. Fenton — Board Review of The Founding (Kaianere'kó:wa)

**Domain:** Haudenosaunee political structure, the Great Law, the condolence ceremony

**Factual assessment:**

The chapter's representation of the Grand Council structure is substantially accurate. The fifty sachems, the Elder Brother / Younger Brother moiety system, the Onondaga as Firekeepers, the consensus requirement, and the deliberative process are all consistent with the recorded tradition and the scholarly literature.

**Specific findings:**

1. **Condolence terminology:** The chapter uses "ohén:ton karihwatéhkwen" for the condolence ceremony (line 43). This term actually refers to the Thanksgiving Address ("the words that come before all else"), which is related but distinct from the condolence ceremony proper. The condolence ceremony has its own structure and name. The Thanksgiving Address opens formal gatherings; the condolence ceremony specifically addresses the death of a sachem and the installation of his successor. The chapter should either use the correct term for the condolence ceremony or clarify that the Thanksgiving Address and the condolence ceremony, while related, serve different functions within the same framework of clearing the mind before governance.

2. **Council procedure:** The chapter's description of how a question moves through the council (Kanien'kehá:ka to Seneca, then to Oneida and Cayuga, then to Onondaga) is a reasonable simplification. In practice, the procedure was more complex: the question could be sent back multiple times, and the Firekeepers had specific deliberative responsibilities beyond simple confirmation. Acceptable for a narrative chapter, but the simplification should not mislead readers into thinking the process was linear.

3. **Wampum description:** The chapter's treatment of wampum as a mnemonic technology is excellent. The description of the Hiawatha Belt is accurate. The observation that the knowledge "lived in the conjunction of bead and voice" is a strong and correct formulation. One note: the chapter says "A trained keeper could recite the entire Great Law from the belts" — this is broadly correct, but the recitation from the belts was not a solo performance. The recitation involved multiple keepers and could take several days. The chapter should note that the full recitation was a collective, multi-day process.

4. **Fifty sachems:** The number fifty is traditional and widely cited, but the actual count depends on how the seats are distributed across the nations (9 Mohawk, 9 Oneida, 14 Onondaga, 10 Cayuga, 8 Seneca — which totals 50). The chapter uses the number without breakdown. Acceptable.

**Cultural authenticity:**
The chapter handles the founding narrative with appropriate respect for the oral tradition. The serpent-combing, the stone canoe, and the Tree of Peace are presented within the tradition's own framework rather than being rationalized or labeled as mythology. This is correct.

**Verdict:** APPROVE with 1 factual correction required (ohén:ton karihwatéhkwen / condolence distinction) and 2 minor notes for consideration.
