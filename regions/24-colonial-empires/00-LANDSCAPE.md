# Colonial Empires & Systems

Between roughly 1415 and 1975, a small group of European states organized themselves into world-systems: networks of territories, peoples, trade routes, and coercion that linked every inhabited continent into a single hierarchical order centered in Europe. This is not the story of what those empires did to others — that story belongs to the regions where they arrived. This is the story of what the empires were, from inside their own logic: how they understood their purpose, how they justified their methods to themselves, what structures they built, what ideologies they maintained, and how those ideologies eventually failed to contain what they had set in motion.

The empires told themselves coherent stories. The Portuguese saw themselves as crusaders carrying the faith around the world, fulfilling a prophetic mission to encircle Islam and bring the whole earth under Christian civilization. The Spanish built on the same foundation but added the legal architecture of encomienda and the theological debates about whether indigenous peoples had souls — debates that produced Las Casas and the Laws of Burgos even as the conquest continued. The Dutch saw themselves as the rational, Protestant, commercial alternative to Catholic imperial excess: their empire was a corporation before it was a state, and it made no apology for that. The French built a civilizing mission — la mission civilisatrice — that declared French culture universal and French language the medium through which all peoples would be elevated. The British Empire, the largest and last, held all of these justifications simultaneously and added the language of free trade, rule of law, and the white man's burden. None of these self-understandings are endorsed here. They are recorded here because they were real, they organized real institutions, and they shaped the world every subsequent generation inherited.

Key features:
- Lisbon and the Cape route: the Portuguese opened the Indian Ocean to European ships via the Cape of Good Hope (1488); every subsequent empire used this route
- Tordesillas Line (1494): the papal division of the world between Spain and Portugal; the first global territorial agreement, and the template for all subsequent imperial partition
- Atlantic triangle: the triangular trade connecting Europe, West Africa, and the Americas — manufactured goods south, enslaved people west, raw materials (sugar, tobacco, cotton) back east
- Batavia (Jakarta): the nerve center of the Dutch VOC's Asian empire; the model for corporate colonial administration
- Calcutta / Bombay / Madras: the three presidencies that organized British India; the administrative model later exported to Africa
- Berlin Conference (1884–85): the European powers dividing Africa among themselves without a single African representative present; the most explicit demonstration of the system's logic

## How They Connect

The empires were rivals and co-creators simultaneously. They competed for the same trade routes, the same colonies, and the same enslaved labor — and in competing, they built a shared system that none of them fully controlled. The Portuguese opened the Cape route; the Dutch seized most of it in the early seventeenth century; the British eventually dominated it. The Spanish silver from Potosí and Zacatecas funded the Habsburgs' European wars and, via leakage, the Dutch and English commercial economies that were building the power to challenge Spanish dominance. The French and British fought the same war six times between 1689 and 1815 (King William's War through the Napoleonic Wars), and each iteration redistributed colonial territory between them. What looks like competition was also a system: the balance of power in Europe was maintained, in part, by the wealth extracted from non-European territories.

The slave trade connected all five empires in a moral economy they all participated in and all, eventually, tried to reframe. The Portuguese began the Atlantic slave trade to Brazil in the 1520s; by the seventeenth century, Dutch, French, and English slavers were all major actors. The triangular trade was not a British invention or a Portuguese one; it was a European one, rationalized by shared legal frameworks (Africans as property, not persons) and sustained by shared demand for sugar, tobacco, and cotton. The abolitionist movements that eventually dismantled legal slavery were themselves products of the imperial world — British abolitionism, the most effective, used imperial power to suppress the slave trade it had previously protected.

The Scramble for Africa (1880–1914) brought all the surviving empires — Britain, France, Belgium, Germany, Portugal, Italy, Spain — into a final, explicit act of collective imperial ambition. The Berlin Conference of 1884–85 drew borders across the African continent according to European strategic interests, cutting across existing African kingdoms, ethnic territories, and trade networks. Those borders, largely unchanged, are the borders of today's African states. The imperial logic was most nakedly expressed here: not trade, not the cross, not civilization, but the map and the gun.
