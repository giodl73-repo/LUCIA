# Romani — The People of Roma

We are the people of Roma, and we organized the world by law. Not the law of a god dictating to a prophet — the law of men reasoning together about what obligations bind a community, what rules must govern the use of force, what procedures allow a city of strangers to function without destroying itself. The Twelve Tables, the first written code of Roman law, were displayed in the Forum so that every citizen could read the rules that governed him. The *ius civile* grew from those tables through centuries of juristic commentary into the most sophisticated legal system the ancient world produced, and when the western empire fell, its law survived in manuscripts and in the eastern Roman inheritance that Justinian would codify and that every European legal system still draws on.

We called ourselves Romani — the people of Roma, the city on the Tiber that grew from a collection of hilltop villages into the city that would call the Mediterranean *Mare Nostrum*, our sea. What made us was not ethnic unity — the Romans absorbed every people they conquered and eventually gave them all citizenship — but *pietas*: the reciprocal obligation owed to the gods who protected Rome, to the ancestors whose decisions had built Rome, and to the state that made Roman life possible. The man who fulfilled his *pietas* was the Roman ideal: the soldier who fought, the magistrate who governed, the *paterfamilias* who maintained his household's religious obligations, the senator who debated and decided. The citizen was the city, and the city was the citizen, and the law was the bond between them.

## What Defined Them

**The law as the substance of civilization.** For the Romans, law was not a constraint on civilized life. It was civilized life. The Roman legal system, developed over centuries by jurists who debated cases with the same rigor that Greek philosophers debated nature, distinguished between *ius civile* (the law applying to Roman citizens), *ius gentium* (the law applying to everyone, based on universal principles of reason), and *ius naturale* (the natural law that underlies all human law). This framework — the idea that there are laws discoverable by reason that bind all human beings regardless of their citizenship — is one of Rome's most durable contributions to subsequent thought.

**Pietas — the obligation that organized life.** The Roman concept of *pietas* was not piety in the modern, passive sense. It was a demanding, reciprocal obligation: you owed the gods their sacrifices and honors; you owed your ancestors memory and respect; you owed your family its maintenance and protection; you owed the state your service and your loyalty. Aeneas carrying his aged father on his back out of burning Troy was the image of *pietas*. The man who abandoned these obligations was not merely impious — he was a threat to the social fabric that made Roman life possible. *Pietas* was the load-bearing wall of Roman culture.

**The army and the road.** The Roman legions built the roads that held the empire together, and the roads fed back into the legions' capacity to move quickly to any threat. The Roman road system was not primarily built for commerce, though commerce used it heavily. It was built for military logistics: the ability to march an army at a standard pace from any province to any other province in a predictable number of days. The milestones that still stand along Roman roads in Britain and Jordan are markers not of distance but of the empire's reach — the physical embodiment of the claim that Rome could be everywhere at once.

**Res publica — the thing that belongs to the public.** The Roman Republic's central political concept was that the state was *res publica* — the public thing, belonging to all citizens, not to any king or family. This concept underwrote the elaborate constitutional machinery of the Republic: the two consuls who checked each other; the Senate whose authority rested on collective decision; the assemblies where citizens voted. The concept survived the transition to the Principate — Augustus was careful never to call himself king, maintaining the fiction that the Republic's forms still operated — because the idea that the state belonged to the people was too deeply embedded in Roman political culture to discard openly.

**The absorption of what Rome conquered.** The Roman empire expanded not just by conquest but by incorporation: the people of conquered territories became first allies, then Italian citizens, then Roman citizens. The emperor Caracalla in 212 CE extended Roman citizenship to virtually every free person in the empire — an act that completed a process that had been underway for centuries. The result was an empire that was simultaneously Roman and Greek and Egyptian and Syrian and Gallic, in which a man from North Africa (Septimius Severus) and a man from Syria (Philip the Arab) could be emperor, and in which the language of the New Testament was Greek and the language of the law was Latin and the city that gave the empire its name was, by the empire's final centuries, not even particularly central to its operation.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Kingdom and the Republic's Birth:** The tradition says Brutus expelled Tarquin the Proud and the Romans swore never to have a king again. The oath was kept, after a fashion, for five hundred years. The constitutional machinery the Romans invented to govern themselves without a king is the most influential political engineering of the ancient world.

**2 — The Conquest of Italy and the Punic Wars:** Hannibal crosses the Alps and wins at Cannae. The Senate sits in its chamber and refuses to discuss terms. Scipio takes the war to Africa. Something is decided at Zama that will shape the Mediterranean for three centuries.

**3 — The Late Republic and the Crisis:** The Gracchi brothers try to redistribute land and are murdered for it. Marius changes who can serve in the legions. Sulla marches his army on Rome. Caesar crosses the Rubicon. The Republic is eating itself.

**4 — The Principate:** Augustus stands in the Senate and offers to give back his powers. The senators ask him to keep them. The fiction is accepted by everyone who needs to accept it. The *Pax Romana* begins — two centuries of relative peace in the empire's interior, during which the roads are built, the law is codified, and Virgil writes the founding poem.

**5 — The Crisis and the Late Empire:** Diocletian divides the empire in two to govern it better. Constantine makes Christianity the favored religion and builds a new capital on the Bosphorus. The western empire fragments. In 476 CE, the last western emperor Romulus Augustulus is deposed by Odoacer. In Constantinople, the eastern empire continues.
