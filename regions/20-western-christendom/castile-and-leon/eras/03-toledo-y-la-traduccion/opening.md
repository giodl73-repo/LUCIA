# Toledo y la Traducción — Toledo and the Translation
## Opening

The first thing Gerard of Cremona understood about Toledo was that it smelled of two cities at once.

From the south, the river. The Tagus wrapped the city's rock on three sides, cold even in August, carrying the particular mineral sharpness of water that had come down through limestone country. From the north and east, the city itself: baked stone and roasting mutton, the resinous smoke of cedar being worked in the Jewish quarter's workshops, the bright sting of acid from the tanneries. And underneath all of it, in the narrow streets that wound from the old mosque toward the cathedral, something older — the smell of manuscripts. Dust and goatskin and the faint sweetness of the oils used to cure the vellum against the dry Castilian air. It was this smell that had brought him from Lombardy, in the year 1145, by mule and by boat and by exhausted walking.

He had come for Ptolemy's *Almagest*.

The *Almagest* — the mathematical treatise that fixed the movements of the stars, that made the sky comprehensible — existed in Toledo in Arabic, translated centuries before from the original Greek by scholars whose names were now lost. In all of Latin Christendom, Gerard knew, there was no copy he could read. The mathematics of the sky belonged to a language that most Christian scholars could not approach. He had heard, from a Lombard merchant who traded cloth for silver in Burgos, that in Toledo — the city Alfonso VI had taken from the Moors sixty years before, in 1085 — the libraries were open. That Jewish scholars could read Arabic and Latin and their own Hebrew. That Christian canons and Muslim jurists still walked the same streets, used the same bathhouses, argued about the same texts.

He did not believe it entirely. He came anyway.

The canon who met him was a man named Dominicus Gundissalinus, an Archdeacon of Segovia who divided his time between Toledo's cathedral and the manuscript collections the new Christian administration had, by some governance miracle or simple pragmatism, left intact. Gundissalinus spoke Latin; he spoke no Arabic. He introduced Gerard to a Jewish scholar named Abubacer, who spoke Arabic and Hebrew and enough Mozarabic — the Romance dialect of the old Christian communities who had lived under Muslim rule — to make the chain work.

This is how it went: Abubacer read the Arabic aloud. He rendered it into the spoken vernacular, stumbling sometimes, searching for the word that would carry the meaning across. Gundissalinus listened and wrote in Latin. Gerard stood behind both of them and watched and asked questions about the mathematics, about the values Ptolemy had assigned to the orbital radii, about the method for computing an eclipse. Sometimes the question required all three languages to answer. Sometimes all three men stopped and argued about what a word meant — not what it meant in Arabic or Latin, but what it meant for the thing the text was describing, for the actual motion of a planet against the fixed stars.

Outside the cathedral's workshops, Toledo continued in its ordinary life. The Wednesday market in the Plaza del Zocodover — the name itself Arabic, al-suq al-dawabb, the market of the beasts — drew traders from Castile and Andalusia both, buyers who spoke one language selling to buyers who spoke another, with a Mozarabic dialect serving as the working tongue of commerce the way Latin served the Church. The bells of San Andrés rang the hours. From the old mosque that Alfonso had converted, a muezzin no longer called, but the building had not been converted yet into a cathedral — that would take another generation, the stone walls too beautiful to tear down, the vaulted ceiling too fine for a hasty patron to risk the craftsmen's opinion of him.

Gerard stayed forty years.

He translated eighty-seven texts. The *Almagest* took him seven years. Then Euclid's *Elements*, which had also survived only in Arabic. Then Avicenna on medicine. Then al-Farabi on the sciences, and al-Kindi on optics, and a dozen others whose names the Latin West did not yet know and would know, after Gerard, as the foundation of its universities. He learned Arabic thoroughly enough, by the end, to work without Abubacer's mediation — though he continued to use it, because Abubacer understood inflection in the original texts that a learner could not yet hear.

When Gerard died in Toledo in 1187, his students wrote a brief account of his life. They listed his translations with a scholar's precision, each text named, each source acknowledged. They noted that he had left Lombardy to find the *Almagest* and had found, instead, that the *Almagest* was the least of what Toledo contained.

This is what a conquered city can be: the place where what was kept in one language becomes available to others. Not because the conqueror was wise, though Alfonso was not foolish. Not because tolerance was a policy, though it was, unevenly, sometimes. Because a market has its own logic, and a manuscript is worth money, and a scholar's labor can be purchased for less than the value of what he produces, and the men who fund cathedrals also fund translation — and Alfonso's son Alfonso VII had called himself *Imperator totius Hispaniae*, emperor of all Hispania, which is an ambitious title requiring a great city, and Toledo, city of three cultures and the greatest libraries north of Córdoba, was the only city that could begin to carry it.

The stars moved overhead in the patterns Ptolemy had described. The Tagus went where it always went. In the workshop off the cathedral cloister, Gerard bent over a manuscript while the candles shortened and Abubacer's voice moved through the Arabic in the unhurried cadence of a man who understood that the words he was saying would outlast the room in which he was saying them.
