# Sāmāniyān — The Persian Renaissance

We were the dynasty that gave the Persian language back its voice. After two centuries of Islamic rule, during which the educated classes of Iran had conducted their scholarly and administrative lives in Arabic, the Samanid emirs of Khorasan and Transoxiana made a different choice: they would govern as Muslim rulers, honor the Arabic scholarly tradition, and simultaneously patronize the restoration of Persian as a literary language. It was our court at Bukhara that commissioned and supported Ferdowsi's predecessors and contemporaries — the poets who rebuilt Persian literary tradition from the inside out, using the Islamic world's forms and knowledge while insisting that the Iranian spirit they encoded was irreplaceable.

We called our state *Al-Dawla al-Samaniyya* in Arabic — the Samanid state — and our administrators wrote their official correspondence in both languages. We were a Muslim dynasty: we supported Islamic scholarship, maintained mosques and madrasas, participated in the scholarly life of the wider Islamic world. We were also an Iranian dynasty: we believed that the Persian language, the Persian administrative tradition, and the Persian literary heritage were civilization's inheritance, not an embarrassment to be abandoned for the conqueror's tongue. The two commitments were not contradictory. They were the creative tension that made Samanid Khorasan one of the most intellectually productive places in the medieval world.

## What Defined Them

**The Persian literary renaissance.** The Samanid court's patronage of Persian poetry was the hinge event in the history of Persian literature. Before the Samanids, Persian had been used for local administrative purposes but lacked the prestige of Arabic in the scholarly and literary world. The Samanid emirs commissioned Persian translations of Arabic scholarly works, patronized Persian poets who were developing the *qasida* and *masnavi* forms in the Persian language, and created the intellectual environment in which Rudaki — the first great Persian poet — and the predecessors of Ferdowsi could work. The resulting tradition — Classical Persian poetry — spread from the Samanid court to every subsequent Persian-influenced dynasty, from the Ghaznavids to the Mughals.

**The library and the translation project.** Bukhara under the Samanids was the intellectual capital of the eastern Islamic world. The palace library was renowned throughout the Islamic world for its comprehensiveness. Ibn Sina (Avicenna), born near Bukhara in 980 CE, described using the Samanid library as a young man and finding there books he had never encountered elsewhere. The translation project that the Samanids sponsored — bringing Arabic scientific and philosophical texts into Persian — was the mirror image of the Abbasid translation movement that had brought Greek texts into Arabic. The Samanids were completing the circuit: knowledge from Greek into Arabic, from Arabic into Persian, making it accessible to the Iranian world in its own language.

**Administrative sophistication.** The Samanid state was one of the most effectively administered of the medieval Islamic world. Their bureaucratic system — combining Abbasid Islamic administrative forms with the older Iranian divan traditions — set the model that subsequent dynasties of the eastern Islamic world followed. The tax system was systematic, the postal network was maintained, the military was professionalized. The Ghaznavid dynasty that displaced the Samanids, and the Seljuks who came after, both used Samanid administrative frameworks as their model. The bureaucracy outlasted the dynasty.

**Islam and Iranian identity — the synthesis.** The Samanids demonstrated that the Islamic faith and the Iranian cultural identity were not in contradiction. An Iranian Muslim could pray in Arabic, study the Quran and hadith in the orthodox manner, and simultaneously take pride in the Persian language, the pre-Islamic Iranian literary heritage, and the courtly culture of the ancient plateau kingdoms. The *Shahnameh* that Ferdowsi completed shortly after the Samanid dynasty's end tells the story of the ancient Iranian kings — Zoroastrian, pre-Islamic — as a national heritage to be honored rather than a pagan past to be abandoned. This synthesis, pioneered under Samanid patronage, became the default Iranian identity for the next thousand years.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Rise:** The Samanid governor in Bukhara receives his appointment from the Abbasid caliph's representative and immediately begins building the administrative infrastructure that will make Khorasan governable without constant Abbasid supervision. The poetry at court is still mostly in Arabic. But someone is asking what the Persian word would be.

**2 — The Peak:** Ismail Samani defeats the Saffarid rivals and the Abbasid caliph confirms his authority over Khorasan. The court at Bukhara is receiving scholars from Baghdad and poets from every corner of the Iranian world. Rudaki, the blind poet, is composing the qasidas that will define the Classical Persian poetic tradition. The library is being assembled book by book.

**3 — The Cultural Florescence and the Political Decline:** Ferdowsi is composing the *Shahnameh* somewhere in the Samanid world while the Ghaznavid military commanders are replacing Samanid nobles in the provincial administration. Ibn Sina is studying in the Bukhara palace library before the Ghaznavids take the city. The dynasty is ending; the tradition it created will outlast every dynasty that follows.
