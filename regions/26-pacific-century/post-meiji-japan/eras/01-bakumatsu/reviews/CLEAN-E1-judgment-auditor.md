### Anachromatic Morality Check

The chapter describes events without modern judgment:
- Sakoku is presented as a choice made by the shogunate, not as backwards isolation or fear-based withdrawal.
- The negotiation with Americans is presented as necessity, not as betrayal or cowardice.
- The assassinations (both by imperial loyalists and by the shogunate) are presented as facts, not as barbarism or sophistication.
- The domains' arming is presented as response to visible weakness, not as rebellion or disloyalty.
- The Meiji transformation is presented as deliberate choice, not as Japan "catching up" or "modernizing" in a Western-defined sense.

No anachronistic judgment detected. ✓

### Romantic or Demonizing Language Check

The chapter is cool throughout. No romanticization of the samurai code. No demonization of the Americans. No elevation of any figure as heroic. Ii Naosuke is not presented as villain; Yoshinobu is not presented as hero. They are presented as men attempting to manage impossible situations.

No excess detected. ✓

### Achebe Test — Self-Recognition Check

**Would a Japanese person, living in Japan in 1853–1868, feel their story was told from inside with dignity?**

Reading through the opening and closing sections specifically:

Opening: A harbor master is watching the boundary break. He understands sakoku as a policy that worked, not as superstition. He is competent in his role; the system fails, not his comprehension. The dignity is present. ✓

Sections 2–3: The domains are shown as understanding what the shogunate is slow to understand — that transformation is survival. The imperial loyalists are wrong about expulsion but correct about authority needing to shift. Iesada, Ii, Yoshinobu are presented as intelligent men managing unmanageable situations. ✓

Section 4–5: The Meiji leadership is shown as having made a deliberate choice. The transformation is not forced on them; they choose it, and they choose it because they understand the choice they are facing. The landing acknowledges the cost and the conviction that Japan's character is worth preserving. ✓

**Assessment:** A Japanese reader should feel their story is told from inside. The perspective is not external. The judgment is not applied from above. The institutions and choices are presented on their own terms.
