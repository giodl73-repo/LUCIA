# Sarmatians — The Federated Steppe

We are the Sarmatians — the confederation of Iranian-speaking pastoral peoples who dominated the Pontic-Caspian steppe for eight centuries, from the Volga to the Danube, from the time when we pressed westward past the Scythians until the time when the Hunnic migrations swept us and everyone else into the Roman Empire's orbit. We are not a single people but a family of related peoples: the Roxolani, the Alans, the Iazyges, the Aorsi, each with their own pasture territories and their own federation leaders, all sharing the mounted warrior tradition and the Iranian-derived speech that makes us recognizable as Sarmatian rather than Scythian or Hunnic.

The Roman world knew us from multiple angles: as enemies on the Danube frontier, as federates (allied warriors in Roman service), and as a source of some of their most effective cavalry. The Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius settled eight thousand Iazyges in Britain after defeating them in the Danube wars — and those Sarmatian cavalrymen on the Roman Wall of Britain, speaking their Iranian dialect in the rain of the north, may be the historical foundation of the Arthurian legend's knights. We move through the edges of other people's histories because we are a people who moved: across the steppe, into the Roman world, and eventually, as the Alans, into Spain and North Africa ahead of the Vandals.

## What Defined Them

**The Sarmatian cavalry tradition.** Where the Scythians had pioneered light horse archery, the Sarmatians developed the cataphract — the heavily armored cavalry warrior on an armored horse, fighting with the long lance (kontos) rather than the bow. This innovation in heavy cavalry would become the template for Roman heavy cavalry, Parthian cataphracts, and ultimately the armored knight of medieval Europe. The Sarmatians' military contribution to Eurasian warfare was the development of the shock cavalry tradition that complemented the Scythian missile cavalry tradition.

**The federated political structure.** Sarmatian political organization was confederate: the various Sarmatian groups (Roxolani, Alans, Iazyges, Aorsi) maintained their own leaders and territories while cooperating for major military campaigns and negotiating collectively with outside powers like Rome and the Parthian Empire. This federation model — flexible, non-centralized, adaptable — was the standard steppe political form and was highly effective for the steppe environment, even if it frustrated Roman diplomats who wanted a single interlocutor.

**The warrior women.** Ancient sources — Greek, Roman, and Scythian — consistently noted that Sarmatian women fought alongside men, and archaeology has confirmed this: a significant proportion of female burials in the Sarmatian archaeological record contain weapons, riding equipment, and warrior accoutrements. The Greek legend of the Amazons — warrior women somewhere to the east of the Greek world — is almost certainly a cultural memory of the Sarmatian military tradition. Whether Sarmatian women routinely served as warriors or whether the phenomenon was more limited, the cultural impact of the observation was enormous.

**The Roman relationship.** The Sarmatians' relationship with Rome ranged from destructive frontier raids to federate military service and eventual partial assimilation. The settlement of Iazyges cavalrymen in Britain by Marcus Aurelius is documented; the possible cultural transmission of Sarmatian warrior mythology into Romano-British tradition is speculative but intriguing. More concretely, Sarmatian cavalry models influenced Roman military organization, and the Alans who crossed into the western Roman Empire in 406 CE eventually reached Spain and North Africa as part of the Vandal confederacy — the last Sarmatian people to be traceable in the historical record.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Westward Push:** The Sarmatian federation is moving its herds past the Don River into the lands the Scythians have held for three centuries. There is no single battle, no declaration of war. There is pressure: more horses, more pastoralists, better organized. The Scythians compress toward Crimea. The steppe changes its people.

**2 — Tanais Market:** At the trading city at the Don's mouth, Sarmatian nobles sell horses and receive Greek pottery, wine, and silver in return. The Sarmatian woman sitting in the transaction is armed; she will ride home armed; if raiders come, she will fight. The Greek merchant notes this in his mental file of strange customs.

**3 — The Danube War:** The Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius has been fighting the Iazyges for years. Now they are making terms. Eight thousand Iazyge warriors will serve in the Roman army, some of them sent to Britain. The negotiations are in Latin on one side; the Sarmatian leader on the other side speaks through an interpreter. Both sides agree to terms neither fully trusts. The cavalrymen go north.

**4 — The Hunnic Horizon:** The news from the east is bad: new riders, fast, numerous, organized under a ferocious leader. The Alans on the Caspian steppe are the first to feel the pressure. Some submit and join the Hunnic confederation. Others flee west. The ripple effect will reach the Danube, and then the Rhine, and then the whole western Roman Empire.

**5 — The Alans in Spain:** The last Sarmatian people are crossing the Pyrenees with the Vandals. They will settle briefly in Hispania, then cross to North Africa. Their Iranian language will not survive. Their military tradition will be absorbed. The Sarmatian people, eight centuries after they first pushed the Scythians west, disperse into the peoples of the late Roman world and are gone as a distinct identity.
