## Historical Accuracy Assessment

**Kisalian phase chronology:** The chapter correctly identifies approximately the 8th century CE as the transition point at which copper appears alongside iron in the Lake Kisale burials. My excavations at Sanga and Katoto document this sequence accurately. The chapter does not conflate the Kisalian phase (c. 700–1000 CE) with the later Kabambian phase (c. 1000–1700 CE); it treats the whole span from 500–1500 CE as the "Upemba tradition," which is a reasonable periodization for narrative purposes.

**Cemetery sites:** The chapter names Sanga, Katoto, and Kikulu correctly. The "copper rings on every finger" description is consistent with the burial evidence from the richest Kisalian graves, though I note that the specific individual described is a composite. The figures.md correctly acknowledges this.

**Iron technology:** The furnace description (shaft furnace, four tuyere pipes, forced-draft bellows) is consistent with what Central African Iron Age technology shows, though I note one ambiguity: the Upemba Depression furnace design is not definitively characterized in my published reports; the chapter draws on the dominant Central African design type, which is a reasonable inference but should be acknowledged as inference rather than established fact. This is minor.

**Ore and charcoal knowledge:** The description of ore-reading and charcoal-species selection is consistent with ethnographic accounts of Central African iron-smelting knowledge systems and is plausible as a historical reconstruction for this period.

**Copper handa:** The cross-shaped copper ingots' appearance at Lake Kisale in approximately the 8th century CE is consistent with my findings. The chapter correctly identifies them as a currency circulating across the interior, though the exact distribution network's reach is a matter of ongoing research.

---

## Corrections Required

**Correction 1 (factual clarification):**
The chapter states the furnace stood "two meters high." The Central African shaft furnaces vary considerably in height — some reach two meters, others are closer to one to one and a half meters. The specific height for Upemba-tradition furnaces is not established in the archaeological record. I recommend changing "two meters high" to "a meter and a half, perhaps two" to reflect the variability.

**Correction 2 (sourcing):**
The figures.md cites "Nenquin, J. 1963. Excavations at Sanga, 1957." The correct full citation is: Nenquin, J. 1963. *Excavations at Sanga, 1957.* Tervuren: Musée Royal de l'Afrique Centrale, Annales, Sciences Humaines, no. 45. The current citation is incomplete.

---

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter's approach to the Upemba tradition as a lacustrine society organized around iron and fish is consistent with what the archaeological evidence shows. The emphasis on the exchange network and the social differentiation visible in the graves is the correct interpretive framework for this period. The chapter does not over-interpret the evidence toward political structures that belong to the kingdom period.

**Recommendation: PASS with corrections noted above.**
