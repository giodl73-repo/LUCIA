## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — PANEL-2 Review of Production Bible

**Focus:** Sensory plan, embodiment, physical grounding across all five sections

**What the production bible gets right:**

The sensory plan for Section I is strong: the Katsina market, the leather-tanners, the compound. Section II promises "the heat, the dust, the weight of manuscripts wrapped against the journey." Good. The battle at Tabkin Kwotto is planned to be "rendered from inside, with the specific physical experience of the fighters." Good.

**What still concerns me:**

1. **Section III has no sensory plan.** The section on the flag-holders rising across the Hausa states is planned as a strategic survey ("each flag-holder assessed local conditions and chose his moment") with a single anchored scene at Kano. But the Kano scene is described in economic and political terms: "the dye pits, the leather market, the commercial wealth." These are facts about Kano, not the experience of being there. What does the dye pit smell like? (Indigo fermentation has a sharp, sour smell that clings to everything.) What color are the workers' hands? (Stained dark blue to the elbow.) What is the sound of the cloth being beaten? These are the details that make a city real. Plan them now, or they will not appear in the draft.

2. **The Shehu's writing hand is planned but not specified.** The production bible notes "the Shehu at his writing desk" in Section IV. What does the desk look like? Is it a desk at all, or does he write on a board across his knees, as many Sahelian scholars did? What is the ink made of? (Gum arabic and lampblack, typically, in this region.) What does the reed pen feel like in the hand? What is the sound of writing in a room where others are also studying? The physical act of Islamic scholarship in the Sahel is a rich sensory world, and the chapter must enter it.

3. **The battle of Tabkin Kwotto needs physical specificity.** The production bible says "render from inside." Render what? The fighters were likely on horseback and on foot. The Fulani used bows and swords. The Gobir army had cavalry. The battle was at a lake. What did the ground feel like? Was it marshy? Was the lake seasonal? What was the sound of horses on that ground? The battle is the moment where the scholarly community discovers it can fight. The physical experience of that discovery is the scene.

**Recommendation:** For each section, plan at least three specific sensory details drawn from the historical and ethnographic record. Not invented atmosphere but grounded specifics. The dye pits' smell. The reed pen's scratch. The Lake Kwotto ground underfoot. These are the bones of immersion.
