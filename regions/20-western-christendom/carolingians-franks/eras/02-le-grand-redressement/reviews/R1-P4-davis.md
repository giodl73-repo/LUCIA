# Panel-1 Review — P-4 Wade Davis
## Le Grand Redressement — Opening

**Reviewer:** Wade Davis (P-4)
**Stage:** PANEL-1
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

This is a chapter about a literate empire's self-conscious reform of its own
knowledge transmission system — which is exactly the kind of chapter that
tempts a writer to treat literacy and text-culture as self-evidently superior
to oral tradition. I am reading carefully for that temptation.

On this evidence, the chapter resists it well. The opening does not present
Carolingian literacy as the natural direction of human progress, awaiting only
the right king to catalyze it. It presents it as a specific response to a
specific problem: the chain of personal loyalty requires reliable information
flow; information flow requires text; text requires the ability to read what
has been written. The logic is closed and internal. The reform is not
civilization arriving — it is a particular kind of civilization solving its
own particular administrative problem.

The passage I want to examine more closely: "The reform of the Church and the
reform of the state were the same project." This is true as a statement of
Carolingian political theology. But the chapter should be careful not to
present this fusion as an obvious good. Many of the peoples who encountered
Carolingian reform — the Saxons most visibly, but also the Lombards and the
peoples of the eastern marches — had knowledge systems, legal traditions, and
religious practices that the Carolingian project sought to replace or
standardize. The chapter can render the Carolingian worldview from inside —
*correctio* as theological duty — while still being honest about the fact
that what is being corrected includes other ways of knowing.

I do not expect the opening to carry this weight. But the chapter must.

**What the opening does right:**
- The corrupted manuscript as the entry point is excellent because it locates
  the knowledge-transmission problem at the level of a specific human artifact
  with a specific material existence. This is not abstract intellectualism.
  This is a scribe pushing harder on the parchment because the words no longer
  make sense, and that pushing is visible in the ink. Knowledge systems fail
  materially, in specific ways, for specific reasons. The opening shows this.
- Alcuin's curriculum — grammar, rhetoric, logic, the seven arts — is named
  but not glorified. It is "the preparation for understanding Scripture," which
  is the Carolingian understanding of what education was for. Accurate.
- The Carolingian minuscule is introduced correctly: not as an aesthetic
  achievement but as a functional tool — "more legible, more portable." This
  is the right level of analysis for a knowledge system rendered at method
  level.

**What I would push on:**
The chapter should, at some point, reckon with what the palace school was
teaching alongside grammar and rhetoric: a particular version of Christianity,
a particular version of Roman law, a particular version of who is entitled to
govern and on what basis. The intellectual sophistication of the Carolingian
project should not occlude the fact that the project also produced the
conditions for the erasure of other intellectual traditions — the oral law
traditions of the Saxons, the customary practices of the Lombards. I am not
asking for modern judgment. I am asking for the chapter to register, from
inside the Carolingian worldview, what *correctio* was correcting FROM as
well as what it was correcting TOWARD.

**The question I'd push on:**
The missi dominici are a fascinating knowledge-transmission mechanism: a
layman and a clergyman traveling in circuit, hearing complaints, correcting
abuses, reporting back. This is an epistemological as well as an administrative
system — it is how the center learns what the periphery is doing, and how
the periphery learns what the center wants. What happens in the encounter
between a missus and a community that does not want to be corrected? That
exchange — the moment where two knowledge systems meet at a county boundary
— is where the chapter's intellectual depth will be tested.
