## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Review of The Tang-Song Nanyang (Opening)

**Overall:** I can see the water. I can smell the pepper. I cannot feel the heat, and I cannot feel the body.

**What works:**
- The water-color distinction (brown for silt, green for sand) is a sensory detail that does structural work. It is not decoration; it is how the man navigates. This is the kind of detail I cross oceans for.
- The camphor sublimating in open air, the sandalwood losing scent when wet, the pepper smell carrying across the deck. These are not in a catalogue; they are in the body. A trader who knows these things knows them through his nose, his hands, his experience of loss. The cargo paragraph works because every item carries a sensory consequence.
- "The association remembered debts the way the sea remembered currents: silently, structurally, without forgiveness." The simile is sensory and structural simultaneously. The sea as a metaphor for social enforcement is felt, not merely clever.

**What doesn't:**
- Lin's body is absent. He stands at the stern in the closing paragraph, but I do not feel him standing. Where is the sun on his skin? The salt on his lips? The pitch of the deck under his feet? He has been at sea for weeks. His hands should show it. His eyes should be narrowed against the glare. The closing paragraph gives me landscape (green hills, low shoreline, hazy heat) but not the man in the landscape.
- Temperature is missing. This is the equatorial strait. The heat should be a presence in every paragraph. The humidity that rots silk, the sweat on the crew's backs, the way the deck planking burns underfoot at noon, the relief of the evening breeze when the wind comes up. The opening mentions humidity once (cloth that rots in it) but does not make the reader feel it.
- Sound is weak. A junk at sea has sounds: the creak of the hull, the slap of water against the planking, the calls of the crew, the rattle of cargo in the hold, the wind in the sail. Lin's world should have an acoustic signature. The opening is almost entirely visual and olfactory. Give me the sounds.
- Taste is absent. What does a Hokkien captain eat on a passage? Rice with dried fish? Preserved vegetables? Tea brewed in the galley? Taste is the most intimate sense, and its absence makes the opening slightly abstract.

**The question I'd push on:**
Put Lin's body on the deck. Let me feel what he feels. The water-reading works because it is sensory knowledge. The closing should work the same way: the strait narrowing, the wind shifting, the heat, the man's body responding to all of it. The story is carried by a body, not by a brain.
