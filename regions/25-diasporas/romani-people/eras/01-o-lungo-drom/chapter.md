# O Lungo Drom — The Journey from India

## The Word

The word for road is drom. It comes from the Sanskrit druma, the way, the path, the thing a person walks. A thousand years after the departure from India, the word is still there, in the mouth of every Romani speaker from Andalusia to the Balkans, carrying inside it the first fact of this people's existence: they walked. They walked west out of the plains of northwestern India, through Persia, through the lands of the Byzantine emperors, and into the forests of the Balkans, and the language they carried recorded every place they passed through. The Persian word for friend entered the vocabulary: dost. The Armenian word for road joined the old Sanskrit one. The Greek words for time and flesh and the names of certain tools settled into the grammar as naturally as the dust of those countries had settled into the hems of their clothing. Every borrowing is a footprint. The language is the map.

No one wrote down why they left.

This is not a gap in the record that scholarship has failed to close. It is the condition of the record itself. The people who became the Roma were not the kind of people for whom chronicles were kept. They were not kings. They were not priests whose temples required inventories. Some scholars believe they were members of the Dom caste, musicians and metalworkers whose skills made them mobile and whose status placed them outside the structures that generate written records. Others point to the armies of Mahmud of Ghazni, who raided northern India seventeen times between 998 and 1030, and suggest the ancestors of the Roma were camp followers, smiths, soldiers swept into the current of military movement. Others simply note that northwestern India in the eleventh century was a place of upheaval, that people moved, and that no one with a pen thought them worth recording.

What remains is the language. And the skills in the hands: a smith working a portable forge at the edge of a village, shaping iron into blades and hinges for the settled people, buying with his craft the right to camp for a night, a week, a season. When the welcome thins, the forge packs onto the wagon and the road opens again.

The Romani word for mother is dej. For father, dad. For sister, phen. For horse, grast. For water, pani. For fire, yag. Linguists have traced these words back through the Indo-Aryan family to their Sanskrit roots, and the words reveal a departure point: the Punjab, the Rajasthan, the northwestern edge of the subcontinent where the plains dry and the trade routes run west toward Persia. The grammar confirms it. Romani preserves features of early medieval Prakrit that place the separation somewhere around the tenth or eleventh century. The language is not merely evidence of origin. It is the origin itself, carried forward in the mouths of children who had never seen India but who said pani when they wanted water and grast when they meant the animal that pulled the wagon and drom when they spoke of the road.

## The Forge

The Dom worked metal. In the caste structure of northwestern India, the metalworker occupied a place that was essential and low: the village needed him for its plows, its blades, its cooking vessels, its axles, and it kept him at the margin because the work was considered polluting. The fire, the smoke, the animal skins used for bellows, the physical closeness to molten material. The metalworker's hands were blackened. His status was fixed. But his skill was portable in a way that the farmer's was not, the weaver's was not, the potter's was not. The potter needed a kiln and a clay source. The weaver needed a loom that did not travel well. The metalworker needed a hammer, a set of tongs, a portable bellows, and fuel. He could set up anywhere there was iron to work and people who needed it worked.

This is the skill that opens the road.

Consider what the smith carries. The knowledge of temperature: the color of the metal tells him when it is ready. Cherry red for bending, bright orange for drawing out, white for welding. He reads the fire the way a navigator reads swells, by a knowledge stored in the body rather than in a book. The angle of the hammer strike determines whether the metal spreads, thins, or folds. The quench in water produces a different hardness than the quench in oil. The sound of the anvil changes as the metal moves from raw to shaped, and the smith hears the difference the way a musician hears pitch. This knowledge takes years to acquire. A boy begins by working the bellows, learning the rhythm of the air that feeds the fire. He graduates to holding the piece with tongs while the master strikes. He learns to read the sparks: their color, their pattern, the way they scatter or cluster tells the experienced eye what the metal contains and what it will tolerate. By the time he swings the hammer himself, the knowledge is in his shoulders and his wrists and the soles of his feet, which feel the vibration of the anvil through the ground.

Alongside the smith traveled the musician. In the Dom communities of India, music and metalwork were twin occupations, sometimes practiced by the same families. The drummer at the wedding, the singer at the funeral, the player who accompanied the seasonal festivals. Music, like metalwork, was essential and low-status, needed and kept at the margin. And like metalwork, music was perfectly portable. The drum travels. The voice travels. The repertoire of songs, carried in memory, weighs nothing and takes no space in the wagon. In every country the travelers passed through, music would be their second passport, the skill that earned a welcome when the forge could not.

A third portable skill was the reading of fortunes. Palmistry, card reading, the interpretation of signs. Settled people viewed this with fascination and suspicion, which was precisely its value: it drew them close enough to do business. The fortune-teller's art required no equipment beyond the client's own hand and the practitioner's knowledge of human anxiety. It was, in its way, the most portable skill of all, and it would follow the Roma across every border they crossed. The settled world would later use this skill as evidence of Romani dishonesty and sorcery. From inside, it was a profession, learned and transmitted like any other, with its own vocabulary and its own expertise.

These three skills, the forge, the song, and the reading of futures, formed the economic foundation of a people on the move. They were not merely ways to earn a living. They were the mechanisms of a portable civilization. Everything the settled world built into buildings, the Roma built into capacities. The school was the apprenticeship at the forge. The concert hall was the campfire. The court of law was the kris, the community tribunal where elders settled disputes according to a code carried in memory, not written in statute. The architecture of Romani civilization was human, not material, and this is why it could travel.

## The Persian Road

The road west from India ran through Persia, across the Iranian plateau where the dust hangs in the air like a second sky and the distances between water sources are measured in days, not hours. It was not an empty road. The trade routes connecting the subcontinent with Mesopotamia and the Mediterranean were ancient, and the peoples who moved along them were many: merchants with camel trains, soldiers in transit, pilgrims, itinerant scholars, and now these traveling metalworkers and musicians from the east, their wagons heavy with tools, their horses picking their way through the same tracks that Silk Road traders had worn into the earth for centuries. The travelers who would become the Roma entered this traffic at some point in the eleventh century, perhaps earlier, and the Persian language left a deep mark on theirs.

The Persian loanwords in Romani cluster around the vocabulary of trade: words for money, for love, for certain colors, for the concept of debt. These are the words of commercial life, and they tell a story: the travelers arrived in Persia as workers and traders, not as conquerors or refugees fleeing in panic. They entered the existing system of exchange. They brought skills the Persians needed. They acquired the vocabulary of the marketplace because they used it.

Firdowsi's Shahnameh, completed around 1010 of the common reckoning, tells a story that may be older than the Roma's journey but resonates with it. The Sassanid king Bahram Gur, the story says, asked the Indian king to send him ten thousand musicians, the Luri, to entertain his people. The musicians came. They were given grain and cattle to settle as farmers, but they ate the grain and let the cattle die and returned to their music, and Bahram Gur, laughing or exasperated, told them to go and wander the earth with their instruments. This is a Persian story, told for Persian purposes, and it says more about Persian assumptions (that music is not real work, that musicians are by nature improvident) than about the people it describes. But it preserves something: the memory that Indian musicians traveled west, that they were welcomed for their skill and dismissed for their way of life, and that the road, not the farm, was their element.

The Persian period may have lasted two centuries. The depth of Persian loanwords in Romani suggests prolonged contact, not a brief transit. During this time, the travelers were not merely passing through. They were living, working, marrying within their community, raising children who spoke a language that was now layered: an Indo-Aryan core wrapped in Persian vocabulary, like a tree with new bark over old wood. The core held. The family words stayed Sanskrit. The grammar stayed Indo-Aryan. The borrowings were from the outside world, the marketplace and the authorities, the surface of daily transaction. The deep structure, the language of the family, the campfire, the kris, remained the language from India.

What held them together through Persia was the structure they carried. The familia, the extended family group, was the basic unit of social organization. Several related families traveled together, shared labor, shared risk, and governed themselves through consensus and the authority of the eldest. The boundaries of the community were maintained not by walls or borders but by practice: who ate together, who married whom, who was inside the circle of the campfire and who was outside it. The code of conduct that would later be formalized as the kris was already operating, even if it had not yet acquired that name. Disputes were settled by elders. Obligations between families were tracked and honored. The group's survival depended on internal cohesion, and the road taught them, generation by generation, that cohesion was not a luxury but the condition of existence.

The horse was the fourth portable asset. The Romani word for horse, grast, is from the Sanskrit, one of the oldest words in the language, and the knowledge of horses was one of the oldest skills. A good horseman reads the animal the way the smith reads the metal: by watching, by touching, by years of accumulated judgment. The condition of the hoof, the set of the shoulders, the way the animal carries its head. The Roma became known as horse traders wherever they traveled, and the reputation was mixed, as reputations of middlemen always are. The settled farmer saw cunning where the Roma saw expertise. But the expertise was real: the ability to assess, breed, train, and heal horses was a knowledge system transmitted through practice, and it made the Roma indispensable to every agricultural and military society they encountered.

The camp at nightfall. The horses unhitched and watered. The forge packed and stowed. The children gathered, the fire lit, the evening meal prepared from whatever the day's work had earned. The settled people of the village nearby saw the smoke and heard the voices and knew the travelers were there, and the relationship between the camp and the village was the central relationship of Romani life: close enough to work, far enough to remain separate, temporary by mutual understanding. In the morning the forge would be set up and the smith would work, and the musician would play, and the fortune-teller would sit with crossed legs and study the lines on a stranger's palm, and the village would receive their skills and pay for them and be relieved when they moved on. The drom si lungo. The road is long.

## The Mountain Pass

From Persia, the road led through the Armenian highlands. The change was physical and sudden: the dry plateau giving way to mountains, the air thinning, the temperature dropping as the road climbed, the track narrowing into passes where a wagon scraped between rock walls and the horses strained against the grade. The Armenian loanwords in Romani are fewer than the Persian ones but distinctive: words for certain tools, for features of the landscape, for the cold. The travelers passed through quickly enough that the linguistic imprint is lighter, but slowly enough that it is there.

The Armenian highlands in the eleventh and twelfth centuries were a place of upheaval. The Seljuk Turks had defeated the Byzantine army at Manzikert in 1071 and were moving west through Anatolia. Armenian kingdoms were falling. The roads were contested. Into this disruption moved the travelers, and their mobility, which in peaceful Persia had been an economic strategy, became in wartime Armenia a survival skill. A people with no fixed position, no fortress to lose, no harvest to be burned, could move through a war zone more easily than the settled populations around them. The war touched them, as it touched everyone on the road, but it did not destroy them the way it destroyed the people who had built their lives into buildings.

Through the mountains, the children grew up speaking a language their grandparents would have partly recognized and partly not. The Sanskrit core was there, the family words, the words for the body and its needs. The Persian layer was thick. Now Armenian words arrived, and with them the first words from Turkic languages, picked up from the Seljuk soldiers and administrators who were the new power in the region. The language was becoming a document of the journey, each layer dated by the political and linguistic map of the lands it had passed through.

The herbal knowledge traveled too. The women of the traveling communities carried a pharmacology learned from the plants of northern India, expanded with each new landscape. In Persia, new plants meant new remedies, new poisons, new dyes. In the Armenian highlands, the flora changed again, and the knowledge adapted. The healer in a Romani camp was not the village herbalist who knew one region's plants. She was a practitioner whose knowledge spanned half a continent, updated at every stop, cross-referenced against a botanical memory that included the medicinal plants of the Punjab, the opium poppies of the Iranian plateau, and the mountain herbs of the Caucasus. This knowledge, like the smith's, was carried in practice, transmitted from mother to daughter in the same way that the forge was transmitted from father to son. It would later be called sorcery by the settled world. It was medicine.

It was in this transit through Anatolia that the travelers likely first encountered Christianity as a dominant social force. In India, they had known Hindu and Buddhist practice. In Persia, Islam had been the religion of the settled world. In the Armenian highlands and Byzantine Anatolia, the church was the institution that organized community life, dispensed charity, and controlled the categories of belonging. The travelers observed this. They did not convert, or if some did, the conversion sat lightly on a culture that had its own relationship to the sacred, expressed through practice rather than doctrine, through the seasonal observances of the traveling community rather than through the theology of the settled church. In the centuries to come, the Roma in Europe would be described by Christian chroniclers as both Christian and pagan, as converts and as heretics, as believers and as fortune-tellers, and these contradictions reflected less the Roma's own confusion than the inability of settled religious categories to contain a people whose spiritual life was organized differently.

The pass compressed everything: the landscape, the weather, the distance between the world they had known and the world they were entering. The Persian road behind, the Byzantine road ahead, the passage difficult but not impossible for people who had been walking for generations. On the other side of the mountains, the Byzantine Empire waited. It was the largest, richest, most bureaucratically organized state the travelers had yet encountered. It would notice them. It would name them. And in naming them, it would begin the process that would define the next thousand years of Romani experience in Europe: the categorization of a free people by the institutions of the settled world.

## The Arrival

They descended from the mountains into the lands of the Byzantine Empire, and the Greek language flooded into theirs. The Greek loanwords in Romani are the most numerous of all the borrowed layers, and they are the words of institutional life: the words for road tax, for marketplace regulations, for the categories the bureaucracy used to classify people. The travelers had entered a world that kept records, and for the first time in their journey, they began to appear in other people's documents.

The earliest probable reference comes from a hagiographic text associated with Mount Athos, dated to approximately 1068. It describes a group called the Athinganoi who are summoned to deal with wild animals troubling the imperial grounds. They are described as snake-charmers, as practitioners of arts that are useful but suspect. The text does not call them travelers from India. It does not know where they came from. It knows only what they do: they handle animals, they work with their hands, and they know things the monks do not.

Later Byzantine references add texture. A document from 1283 mentions Athinganoi working as leather tanners and metalworkers near the fortress of Monemvasia in the Peloponnese. Another, from the fourteenth century, records them as musicians and acrobats at a provincial fair. What these fragments reveal is not a single event but a pattern: a people established in the margins of Byzantine economic life, occupying the niches that their portable skills fit. The Byzantine state, unlike the Persian or Armenian worlds they had passed through, was a bureaucracy that classified its populations. And the moment of classification is the moment of danger for a people who do not fit the available categories.

From Athinganoi, the name traveled. Tsigane in French. Zigeuner in German. Zingaro in Italian. Cingane in Portuguese. Each version drifted further from whatever the original word meant, and none of them were the name the people used. In English, they were called Egyptians (corrupted to "Gypsies"), based on a medieval belief that they had come from Egypt. They had not. They had come from India, through Persia, through Armenia, through the Byzantine lands, and their own name for themselves carried the proof. Roma. From the Prakrit rama. People. The word that had traveled the entire length of the road.

By the thirteenth century, Romani communities were established across the Balkans. The Greek loanwords in their language stabilized: the flood became a steady current. They lived on the margins of Byzantine society, in the spaces between the settled villages and the authorities, working metal, making music, reading fortunes, trading horses. The settled world needed them and mistrusted them. Their skills were useful. Their mobility was suspicious. Their refusal to fit the categories of the church or the state marked them as outsiders in a society that organized belonging through baptismal registers and tax rolls.

The Balkan landscape shaped the new chapter of Romani life. The mountains and forests of Serbia, Bulgaria, and Greece offered a different environment from the open plateau of Persia or the high passes of Armenia. The valleys between the mountains created natural circuits: a traveling group could move from village to village through a region, returning to the same places in the same seasons, establishing the kind of recurring relationship that made the skills economy work. The smith who returned each spring was more trusted than the smith who appeared once. The musician who knew the local songs alongside his own Romani repertoire was more welcome than the musician who played only for strangers. The circuit, the seasonal round of villages and fairs and markets, became the Romani equivalent of territory: not a place you owned but a path you traveled regularly enough that the people along it knew your face and your work. The horse fairs were the high points of the year. A Romani horse trader arriving at a Balkan market brought not only animals but knowledge: which breeds suited which terrain, which bloodlines threw strong foals, which ailments responded to which treatments. He could assess an animal's age by its teeth, its temperament by the set of its ears, its soundness by the way it placed its feet. This was knowledge earned on the road, tested across landscapes the settled buyer had never seen, and it gave the Roma a commercial advantage that was simultaneously the source of their welcome and their reputation for trickery. The gadje farmer could not tell whether he was being served or deceived, because the knowledge gap was too wide. The Roma could tell. They simply could not explain it in terms the settled world would accept as legitimate.

The kris, the community tribunal, was now fully functioning as the Roma's own legal institution. Disputes between families were adjudicated by a council of elders according to the customary law the community carried in memory. The authority of the kris rested not on the power to punish but on the power to exclude: a family judged to have violated the community's code could be declared marime, polluted, cut off from the social and economic life of the group. For a people whose survival depended on mutual aid and shared labor, exclusion was the severest sanction. The kris did not need jails. The road was both the community's freedom and its boundary.

Genetic studies conducted centuries later would corroborate what the language had been saying all along. The Romani population carries genetic markers consistent with a founding population from northwestern India, with a bottleneck event (a period when the population was small enough that genetic diversity narrowed) dating to approximately the eleventh or twelfth century. The genetics and the linguistics converge on the same story: a group, or groups, departing from India around 1000 CE, passing through a narrow corridor of time and space, and expanding again in the Balkans. The body carries the evidence alongside the tongue.

The Roma in the Balkans were not a single group. They had never been a single group. The people who left India may have departed in waves over decades or centuries, and by the time they reached Europe, the traveling communities varied in size, in specific dialect, in the mix of skills they practiced, and in the degree to which they interacted with settled populations. What held them together was not centralized authority but shared practice: the language, the family structure, the kris, the code of purity and pollution that marked the boundary between the Romani world and the gadje (the non-Romani world). Gadje is not a word of contempt. It is a word of distinction. It means those who are not Roma, those who live differently, and its existence in the language is itself evidence of a people who have maintained a boundary between inside and outside for a thousand years.

## The Long Road

The drom si lungo.

The road is long, the Romani proverb says, and it does not say this with sorrow. The road is long the way the sky is wide, the way the fire is hot: it is a statement of the condition of existence, and the condition is not something to be escaped but something to be lived. The road began in the dust of the Punjab with a group of people whose names are lost and whose reasons for leaving are unrecoverable, and it passed through Persia and the Armenian highlands and the Byzantine Empire, gathering words and skills and survival strategies at every stop, and it arrived in the Balkans in the thirteenth century with a people who were still themselves. The language was layered now, Persian over Sanskrit, Armenian over Persian, Greek over all, but the core held. The family words were from India. The grammar was Indo-Aryan. The name was Roma. People.

What the road had built was something the settled world did not have a category for: a civilization without territory. The Roma had law without legislature, education without schools, music without conservatories, a medical tradition without hospitals, a history without chronicles. Everything that the settled peoples of Europe had embedded in institutions and buildings and written records, the Roma carried in practice, in memory, in the skills of the hands and the stories told around the fire. This was not a lack. It was a design. The portable civilization was built to survive exactly the condition the Roma lived in: permanent movement through a world that granted them no permanent place.

The road did not end in the Balkans. It continued. Westward into Hungary, into the German lands, into France and Spain and England, and at every border the same exchange: the skills welcomed, the people mistrusted, the stay permitted and then revoked, the road opening again. In Romania, the Roma would be enslaved for five centuries. In Spain, the Gitano communities would forge the deep song, the cante jondo, out of their grief and their genius and the musical traditions of every land the road had passed through. In Central Europe, the Romani violin would define a national music that listeners would mistake for their own. The Europe that received the Roma in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries would respond with laws of expulsion, with branding, with forced labor, and eventually, in the twentieth century, with genocide. All of that is ahead, and none of it will break what the road built.

A mother teaches her daughter to say pani for water. A smith sets up his forge at the edge of a village he has never seen before. A child learns a Greek word for bread and a Romani word for home that does not mean a building. The fire is lit. The camp is made. In the morning, the road.
