# Opening — La Conquista y El Virreinato

## The Name He Chose

Tungasuca, November 1780.

The corregidor's body was still warm when José Gabriel Condorcanqui put on the name.

He had the corregidor arrested on a Saturday — Antonio de Arriaga, collector of the mita, drainer of the village stores, the man who sent Andean men down into the Potosí mines and waited, not always, for them to come back. The trial lasted as long as it needed to. On Sunday morning, in the plaza of Tungasuca, in the high valleys above Cusco where the grass goes gold in the cold, Arriaga was hanged. The gathered crowd — Quechua-speaking, wool-cloaked, watching — heard the sentence read in their language. Condorcanqui had seen to that.

Then he put on the Inca's name.

Túpac Amaru. The last Sapa Inca by that name had been beheaded in this same Cusco province, in the plaza of the city itself, in 1572, by order of Viceroy Francisco de Toledo, because Toledo had decided that a living Inca heir, however powerless, was an idea the colony could not afford. Toledo had been right. The idea had waited two hundred years. Now it had a new body to wear — a kuraka, a village chief, a man who had spent years petitioning the colonial courts about the abuses of the mita and the repartos, who knew the laws, who had read the records, who had tried every channel the viceroyalty offered and found each one blocked or bought.

The name was not nostalgia. It was a theory of legitimacy.

In Quechua, tupaq means royal, sovereign, brilliant. Amaru is the great serpent — the snake that moves between worlds, between the inside of the earth and the surface, between Ukhu Pacha below and Kay Pacha here. The name meant something cosmological. It meant the world was turning. The Andean word for this turning is pachakuti: pacha, the world-and-time, and kuti, the return, the reversal, the coming-back-around. Every Andean person who heard Condorcanqui take that name in November 1780 understood that he was not declaring a rebellion. He was declaring a pachakuti. The world had turned before. It was turning again.

Micaela Bastidas Puyucahua, his wife, had been planning longer than he had.

She was thirty-five years old. She had been corresponding with every sympathetic kuraka in the southern highlands for months, timing the uprising to the fiestas when the corregidores' guards would be scattered and drinking. When Condorcanqui hesitated — when he waited, when he deliberated, when he sent one more letter to one more official who would not reply — it was Micaela who wrote to him with the Quechua word that does not translate gently: qonqarqanki. You are forgetting. She meant: you are forgetting what we are. She meant: the moment is now. She meant all of this in a language that the Spanish administrators of the Viceroyalty of Peru had spent two hundred and fifty years trying to subordinate, tax, convert, and contain, and which had survived all of it, intact, in the mouths of the people who planted the potatoes and wove the cloth and carried the silver down from the mountain that ate men.

Potosí. The mountain.

In Quechua they called it Sumaq Urqu — beautiful mountain. In the two hundred and thirty years since the Spanish had found the silver vein inside it, somewhere between eight and nine million people had worked in its galleries. The number of dead is not agreed upon. The mercury used to process the silver — azogue, they called it, the quick silver — was poured over the crushed ore in amalgamation patios and then the men breathed it. The men who came back from Potosí breathed differently. Everyone in the highlands knew what a man who had worked the mercury processing looked like. The mita sent them there whether they wanted to go or not. The mita was the labor obligation the Spanish had borrowed from the Inca — the word was Quechua, the mechanism was Andean, the silver went to Madrid.

Ayni is the word the Andes uses for reciprocity. You help me plant, I help you harvest. You give to Pachamama — the earth, the living mother — and Pachamama gives back. The universe runs on return. This was not metaphor. This was physics, Andean physics, the deep grammar of how the world worked. The mita was not ayni. The mita was a river that flowed one direction and did not return. The Andean body understood this as a violation at the level of cosmology, not merely economics. You could say: the tax is unjust. You could also say: the world is broken. In 1780, in the valleys above Cusco, both sentences were the same sentence.

Túpac Amaru II's rebellion would become the largest indigenous uprising in the history of colonial Latin America. His forces would surround Cusco. They would not take it. He would be captured in April 1781, in an act of betrayal, brought to the plaza where his namesake had been killed two centuries before.

What the Spanish did to him there, in that plaza, with the crowd watching, was designed to be remembered.

They did not understand: in the Andes, what is remembered does not die. The taki — the song — is also the record. The quipu — the knotted string — holds what the archive refuses to hold. The name Túpac Amaru was already traveling, already moving through the Quechua-speaking world like water through the irrigation channels the Inca had built and the Spanish had let fall into ruin. The name would not stop. It has not stopped.

The corregidor's body was still warm when Condorcanqui put on the name. Everything that followed — the fire, the siege, the torture, the two centuries of echoes — was already inside that act. A name taken in public, in the plaza, in Quechua. A pachakuti beginning. The world turning again.
