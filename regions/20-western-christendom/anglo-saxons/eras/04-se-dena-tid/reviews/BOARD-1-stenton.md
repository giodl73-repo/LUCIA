# BOARD-1 — Anglo-Saxon Political History
**Reviewer Profile:** Specialist in Anglo-Saxon political and institutional history; expertise in Alfred's reign, the West Saxon dynasty, the Danelaw, and the formation of early England. Works in the tradition of Frank Stenton, Simon Keynes, and Richard Abels.
**Chapter:** Se Dena Tíd — The Danish Time (Anglo-Saxons, Era 4)

---

## Assessment

The chapter is historically sound on the main structural points. Alfred's program — burhs, schools, translations — is correctly characterized and the interdependence of the three elements is well understood. The *Burghal Hidage* formula is cited accurately, though the document's dating (c. 914, thus from Edward's reign) is correctly noted.

**Factual findings:**

1. The chapter states Alfred "recovered London" in 886. This is accurate: the Chronicle records Alfred taking London in 886 and placing it in Æthelred of Mercia's charge. The chapter's implication that this strengthened Alfred's claim to a pan-English authority is well-supported by the scholarly consensus.

2. The chapter identifies Guthrum's treaty as establishing the Danelaw boundary along Watling Street. This is correct. The treaty text (which survives in several versions) uses "the road that goes from the Thames, and then along the Lea...to Bedford, then up the Ouse to Watling Street" — the chapter's summary is accurate.

3. "Edmund refused to renounce his Christianity, and they shot him with arrows and cut off his head." The martyrdom tradition is the primary source — it is possible (some scholars have argued) that Edmund died in battle rather than ritual execution. The chapter could note this uncertainty without requiring a rewrite; alternatively, the hagiographic tradition is close enough in time to the events to treat as historical evidence. The chapter's current handling is defensible.

4. The dating of the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*'s composition "c. 890" is the scholarly consensus and is correct.

5. Asser's dating as "c. 853–908/9" is accepted. The authenticity debate is real (Alfred Smyth famously argued the *Life* was a later forgery) but the Keynes-Lapidge position — that it is authentic — is now the scholarly consensus. The chapter's treatment of Asser as "a witness with interests" is exactly the right epistemic stance.

**One correction required:**

The chapter describes Alcuin as receiving news of the Lindisfarne raid "at Charlemagne's court." This is correct — Alcuin was at court when the news arrived. The chapter says he "wrote letters back to Northumbria that survive." This is accurate; several survive. No issue.

However: the chapter says "He was not wrong about the terror. But he was wrong to imply it was unprecedented." Alcuin did not describe the raids as unprecedented in the sense implied — he described them as divine punishment for Northumbrian sins, which is a different framing. The chapter's gloss ("he was wrong to imply it was unprecedented — the Angles and Saxons had themselves come by sea to take land from the Britons") is a modern historicist reading, not a correction of Alcuin's own claim. Alcuin made a theological argument; the chapter turns it into a historical claim and then rebuts it. This is a minor anachronistic category error. Revise to: "He framed it as divine judgment. Others would note later that the English had themselves come by sea." Or similar — honoring Alcuin's actual framework rather than correcting a claim he did not make.

**Overall:** The chapter reflects reliable scholarship. One revision required. All other historical claims are accurate and well-sourced.
