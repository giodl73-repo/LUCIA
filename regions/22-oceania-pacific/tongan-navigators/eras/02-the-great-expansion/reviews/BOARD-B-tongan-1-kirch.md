## B-tongan-1 — Patrick Kirch — Board Review of The Great Expansion

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Polynesian archaeology and prehistory, with specific focus on the evolution of complex chiefdoms and the archaeological evidence at Mu'a/Lapaha.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The description of the langi at Mu'a is accurate in its essentials: stepped pyramids of coral limestone, fitted without mortar, the Paepae-o-Tele'a as the largest. The height estimate of "roughly four meters" is consistent with published measurements.
- The attribution of the expansion period to the thirteenth through fifteenth centuries CE is consistent with current archaeological and genealogical estimates, though I would note that the expansion likely began earlier, perhaps in the twelfth century, with the Lapita cultural complex providing the deep background.
- The description of the Tu'i Tonga as a sacred chief whose authority was based on mana rather than military force is accurate. The chapter correctly distinguishes between the sacred and political functions of the chieftainship.
- Tu'itatui as the eleventh Tu'i Tonga and the figure associated with the Fiji expansion is consistent with the genealogical traditions as I know them. The Ha'amonga 'a Maui attribution to Tu'itatui is the standard oral tradition.
- The chapter's treatment of the Samoa-Tonga relationship as "older and more complex" than the Fijian connection is correct.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The prestige-exchange network as described is consistent with the scholarly understanding of western Polynesian political systems. The emphasis on mana as the currency of the system, rather than military force, is exactly right.
- The treatment of women's role through fine mat production is important and correct. The 'ie tonga description is accurate.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The structural engine (prestige-exchange as self-reinforcing system) is an accurate representation of how the Tu'i Tonga network functioned. This is the correct causal mechanism.
- The honest treatment of dating uncertainty. The chapter does not pretend to precision it cannot have.
- The description of the Ha'amonga 'a Maui and the scholarly uncertainty about its astronomical function.

**What needs correction:**
- The kalia description says "Sixty feet or more in length." The largest Tongan kalia were approximately 30 meters (about 100 feet), but many were smaller. "Sixty feet" is reasonable for a large kalia but should not be presented as if all kalia were this size. Consider "up to a hundred feet" or specifying a range.
- The chapter does not mention the Lapita cultural complex, which is the deep background for all of Polynesian expansion. The Tongans were among the first Polynesian-speaking peoples to settle the western Pacific, and their navigational and exchange traditions have roots in the Lapita culture that preceded them by a thousand years. A brief mention in Section II or V would strengthen the historical depth.
- The chapter mentions "red parrot feathers" from the Lau Islands. The feathers traded in the western Pacific exchange system were primarily from the kula (collared lory), not generic "parrots." The specific identification matters.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (3 minor corrections above)
