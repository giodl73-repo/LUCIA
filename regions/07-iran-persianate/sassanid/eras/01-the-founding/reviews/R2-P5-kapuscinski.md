# R2-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński: Panel-2 Review (Notes)

**Chapter:** Sassanid Era 1 — The Founding
**Round:** Panel-2 (notes review)
**Reviewer:** Ryszard Kapuściński

---

The section plan addresses what I asked for. Section 2 opens with "the fire temple's physical presence (smoke, prayer, stone, light)" — good. Section 1 adds a body at the road below the cliff — good. Section 4 gives me the material reality of Roman prisoners building Sassanid cities, including the bridge at Band-e Qaysar. These are real additions, grounded in real places.

But I want to press on Section 4 more specifically. The Roman prisoners at Bishapur are described in the notes as "70,000 Roman prisoners and the cities they build." This is a number and a function. What I want is a person. One of those prisoners — maybe a Roman officer, maybe an engineer — stands at the Shapur River and builds a bridge that will still be there 1,800 years later. He uses Roman arch technique because that is what he knows. The arch rises. Shapur rides across it. What does this moment feel like in the body of the Roman who built it? He is not a prisoner building a symbol of his humiliation — he is a craftsman building the only thing a craftsman can build when given materials and a problem: the best bridge he knows how to make. The finished bridge outlasts Shapur, outlasts the Sassanid dynasty, outlasts Rome. The craftsman's body goes into it.

The three sacred fires need to be felt, not just named. Shiz/Takht-e Suleiman in Azerbaijan — the volcanic lake, the fire burning above the water (the site itself is a physical wonder: an eternal flame above a crater lake, which is why the tradition chose it for the warriors' fire). Adur Farnbag in Fars — closer to Ardashir's heartland, the priests' fire. The landscape of each fire is different because Iran is a landscape of dramatic variations, and the fires were placed in dramatic places on purpose. Give me each fire's landscape in a sentence or two. Not a description of what the fire means — the landscape where it burns. The reader should feel the altitude at Shiz, the dryness at Farnbag.

One concern about the landing: "stone and fire as the last images." Yes, but make sure the fire is still burning. The landing should not be elegiac about the dynasty's eventual fall — that comes much later. The fires of the founding era are not extinguished; they are consecrated. The landing should feel like consecration, not loss.

The chapter is ready to write. Do not lose the limestone.
