# Production Bible — Las Guerras de Independencia — La Manigua

**Era:** Cuba Era 2 | 1868–1902 CE
**Voice:** Discover (exile-writing as state-building precursor; manigua as republic)
**Engine:** Martyrdom as legitimacy transfer mechanism
**Terminal Condition:** Premise non-recognition (v1.6 type 4) — the revolution performs correctly; the United States does not recognize its conclusions as binding

---

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** Exile-writing as state-building precursor / martyrdom as legitimacy transfer

The independence war generates its own legitimacy problem: an insurgency organized by exiles for fighters who are not exiles, aimed at a sovereignty that a third power (the United States) will not honor regardless of the war's military outcome. The engine that drives this era is the relationship between what Martí writes (the republic in language) and what the mambises build (the republic in the manigua), and the way Martí's death is the necessary act that transforms the former into the latter.

**Mechanism:** The exile networks produce political infrastructure (the Cuban Revolutionary Party, the exile press, the fundraising apparatus among tobacco workers) that the manigua fighters cannot produce for themselves, but that infrastructure lacks military and moral credibility until the letter-writers enter the field. Martí's presence in Cuba is required not to contribute military capacity (he has none) but to perform the transfer of legitimacy from the exile republic to the manigua republic. His death is the performance's completion.

**Terminal Condition:** Premise non-recognition. The revolution performs correctly on its own terms — it organizes, funds, fights, sacrifices, and wins military victory. It presents these achievements as the basis for full sovereignty. The United States does not recognize the premises as binding. The Treaty of Paris (1898) is negotiated between Spain and the United States; Cuba is not a party. The Platt Amendment (1901) codifies the non-recognition by reserving for the United States the right to intervene in Cuban affairs. The system did not fail because it was weak. It failed because the key party (the United States) operated by different rules and different interests.

This is the v1.6 Terminal Condition type 4 (Premise non-recognition), which applies to "diplomatic/covenant-based engines meeting parties that operate by different rules."

## Sources

**Primary:**
- José Martí, field diary (April 11–May 18, 1895): the most intimate record of his return to Cuba; the jasmine passage, the hammock, the sensation of Cuban air
- José Martí, letters to Máximo Gómez and other commanders: the political argument for the necessity of his own presence in the field
- José Martí, "Nuestra América" (1891): the philosophical vision of Latin American solidarity that frames the independence project
- Máximo Gómez, war diaries and correspondence: the military commander's perspective on Martí's death and the war's subsequent conduct
- Antonio Maceo, correspondence: the mulato general's letters reveal the racial complexity the opening must address

**Tier 2-3 Academic:**
- Ada Ferrer, *Cuba: An American History* (2021): essential; covers the wars of independence with full attention to racial complexity and the role of Afro-Cuban soldiers
- Louis A. Pérez Jr., *Cuba: Between Reform and Revolution* (2011): the structural analysis of why the wars produced the Platt Amendment rather than full sovereignty
- Ada Ferrer, *Insurgent Cuba* (1999): specifically on race, nation, and revolution in the independence wars — the most important academic source for the Antonio Maceo dimension

**Cultural:**
- Fernando Ortiz, *Cuban Counterpoint* (1940): the framework for understanding Cuban culture as the counterpoint between tobacco and sugar, which maps directly onto the class and racial geography of the independence movement (tobacco workers were more free, more literate, more politically active; sugar workers were more enslaved)

## Key Figures

**José Martí (1853–1895):** Poet, essayist, revolutionary organizer. Exiled at 17; spent most of his adult life in exile in Spain, Mexico, Guatemala, Venezuela, and New York. Founded the Cuban Revolutionary Party in 1892. Returned to Cuba April 11, 1895. Killed at Dos Ríos, May 19, 1895. His death is the engine's key transaction.

**Antonio Maceo (1845–1896):** The "Bronze Titan." Son of a Venezuelan immigrant father and Afro-Cuban mother. The most brilliant military commander of the independence wars. His racial identity made him more threatening to the existing Cuban social order and to the United States than Martí's universalism acknowledged. Survived Martí by eighteen months, dying in December 1896. His death removes the military commander most capable of holding the revolution to its promises.

**Máximo Gómez (1836–1905):** Dominican-born general, commander of the Cuban Liberation Army. Gave Martí the order not to ride into the Spanish column. Continued the war after Martí's and Maceo's deaths. Lived to see the Platt Amendment.

**Guillermón Moncada (1841–1895):** Afro-Cuban general, died in the same campaign as Martí. Represents the Eastern Cuba military tradition that was the war's backbone.

**The cigar factory lectors:** Not individually named in history but structurally essential. The lectores (readers) who read to workers in the Ybor City and Tampa factories were the dissemination mechanism for revolutionary literature and the community tie between exile workers and the revolution. Their role deserves a named-figure treatment (composite if necessary) in the chapter.

## Narrative Arc — Section Plan

**Section 1: The Republic in Language** (~900 words)
Opens with the manigua passage from the opening (integrated into chapter). Expands to establish the geography and character of the manigua republic — not metaphor but function. The Lucumí knowledge system in the manigua: the orixás, the specific plants, the protections. How the manigua fighters organized themselves, governed themselves, sustained themselves across the failed Ten Years' War and into the new campaign.

**Section 2: The Republic in Exile** (~900 words)
Ybor City and Tampa. The cigar factory in full sensory detail: the tobacco smell, the galería platform, the lector's voice above the sound of rolling, the workers' hands moving while their minds are somewhere else. The Cuban Revolutionary Party as organizational structure. How wages became weapons. The specific mathematics of exile fundraising: Martí's speaking circuit, the workers' pledge of one day's wages per week. The discover voice carries the analytical weight here — each word Martí wrote was also a ledger entry, also a vote, also a brick.

**Section 3: The Proof** (~700 words)
The landing at Playitas, April 11, 1895. The moonless night, the surf before the beach, the small boat. The thirty-eight days in the field. Martí's diary as the primary evidence of his discovery of Cuba as a physical country rather than a concept. The jasmine. The hammock. The specific sensation of Cuban air after fifteen years of northern winters. The man learning to ride. The man who cannot fight learning that he must be seen trying.

**Section 4: Dos Ríos — The Scene** (~700 words)
The morning of May 19th. The Contramaestre and the Cauto in eastern Cuba — a specific place, its specific geography (the two rivers, the ceiba trees, the heat of eastern Cuba in May). The Spanish column encountered. Gómez's order. Martí's decision. The forty meters. The three bullets. The discovery of the body. The Spanish decision to photograph and display it. The attempt to use the burial to settle the question. The question that was not settled.

**Section 5: The War Continues** (~700 words)
What the war looked like without Martí. Antonio Maceo at his most formidable — the western invasion (October 1895), the march through the entire length of Cuba in three months. Maceo's death in December 1896 and what it removed from the revolution's equation: the commander whose racial identity was the most direct embodiment of the war's racial promise. The war ground on. The reconcentration camps (Spain's trochas and reconcentración policy): the civilian population concentrated into fortified towns, their farms burned, disease and starvation deployed as weapons. The human cost.

**Section 6: The United States Arrives** (~700 words)
1898. The USS Maine in Havana harbor, February 15. The explosion (cause unknown, attributed immediately to Spain by the American press). The Spanish-American War, which lasted three months and was won entirely by the United States. The Treaty of Paris, December 1898: Spain cedes Cuba, Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines to the United States. Cuba is not at the table. The Cuban Liberation Army, which had fought for three years and bled for thirty, is not permitted to enter Santiago de Cuba when the Spanish surrender. American military occupation begins.

**Section 7: The Republic That Was Not** (~400 words)
The Platt Amendment (1901). Cuba is "independent" but the United States has reserved the right to intervene. Guantánamo Bay leased as a naval station. The question of what the blood required — Martí's blood, Maceo's blood, the tens of thousands who died in the reconcentration camps — is answered in a document that Cubans did not sign and could not refuse. The manigua republic has become a formal republic. The formal republic is not what the manigua republic was for.

**Landing:** Return to the discover voice. The letters in New York had become blood in Cuban soil. The blood had become a flag. The flag flew over a republic whose sovereignty was conditional. What that condition required would be the question for the next sixty years, and after.

## Panel Feedback Integration

**From Tuchman:** Dos Ríos needs a full scene. Section 4 is dedicated to it — place, geography, the specific morning, the horses, the sound of the Spanish column. The logic of the death has been established in the opening; the chapter must now deliver the death as a physical event.

**From Ibn Khaldun:** The structural gap between revolution and outcome must be explicit. Section 6 names the mechanism: the United States operated by different rules and was not bound by the revolution's premises. The Platt Amendment is the terminal condition, not a historical accident. The Ten Years' War failure vs. 1895 war — the structural difference was the U.S. presence, not the Cuban military capacity.

**From Achebe:** Antonio Maceo is a central figure, not a secondary one. He appears named in the figures section and substantively in Section 5. The chapter does not resolve the racial complexity into Martí's universalist vision; it holds both Martí's vision and Maceo's embodiment of a different and more demanding version of it.

**From Davis:** Section 1 develops the Lucumí knowledge system in the manigua. Not as background but as function — what the orixás provided, what the botanical knowledge meant, how the manigua's spiritual geography organized the mambises' community in the field.

**From Kapuściński:** Cigar factory sensory detail is in Section 2. The manigua's physical character is in Sections 1 and 3–4. The smell and sound and temperature of the world must be present wherever the argument is made.

## Voice Notes

**Register:** Discover — the Linguistic-Nomadic register adapted for Cuba's specific discover mode: words that travel (from Taíno into Spanish into the mouths of fighters), letters that travel (from New York into the manigua), a man who travels (from exile into the field into the ground). The discover voice accumulates. Each sentence builds on the last. The pause mechanism is the period after a short declarative ("Cuba was dark." / "He did not want to die in New York."). Zero em-dashes.

**Pause mechanism test:** The discover register pauses at the moment of arrival, the moment of completion, the moment of naming. The cigar factory reading pauses when the lector stops. The landing pauses when they feel the beach. The charge pauses when Martí falls.

**Em-dash discipline:** Zero. The voice finds its pauses in the period.

## Open Questions

1. What specifically happened at the confluence of the Contramaestre and Cauto on May 19? (Martí's diary stops on May 18; the battle account comes from Gómez and Spanish reports.) The chapter should be honest about the source asymmetry here — we know the facts of the death from accounts written after it.

2. The Ten Years' War (1868–1878) ended with the Pact of Zanjón — a negotiated truce that many Cubans, including Maceo, refused (the "Protest of Baraguá"). This is the moment of the era's earlier phase. The chapter should acknowledge the Protest of Baraguá as the Afro-Cuban tradition of refusing compromise — the lineage Maceo inherited and embodied.

3. The reconcentración camps: Weyler's policy concentrated an estimated 300,000+ Cubans, of whom some 100,000+ died of disease and starvation. This is a scale of civilian suffering comparable to any military campaign of the era. The chapter must render it at appropriate weight without making it the chapter's center, which belongs to the independence movement itself.
