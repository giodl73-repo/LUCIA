## B-portugal-2 — A.C. de C.M. Saunders — Board Review of O Infante e o Atlântico

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Author of *A Social History of Black Slaves and Freedmen in Portugal 1441–1555* (Cambridge, 1982), the foundational academic study of the Atlantic slave trade's first generation and its social consequences in Portugal. My work documented the legal status, occupations, manumission rates, and community formation of enslaved Africans and their descendants in Portugal from 1441 onward.

**Historical accuracy:**

- **CORRECT:** The 1441 raid as the first instance of enslaved Africans brought to Portugal. Correct date, correct commanders (Tristão and Gonçalves), correct location (Mauritanian coast).
- **CORRECT:** The 235 at Lagos in 1444. August 6. Six caravels. Zurara's account. All correct.
- **CORRECT:** The chapter correctly notes the royal fifth: forty-seven people to Henry's household. The mathematics is accurate (235 × 0.2 = 47).
- **CORRECT:** The chapter's note that "some of them, over the following years, were freed by their owners and entered the population of Lisbon as freedmen and freedwomen" is accurate and reflects the social reality my research documented. The freedmen community in Lisbon was significant and had its own legal standing. The reference to my work in the figures section is appropriate.
- **CORRECT:** The chapter's characterization of approximately 700 enslaved Africans per year for the Lisbon market by 1460. This figure is consistent with the estimates available in the secondary literature.
- **CORRECT:** The chapter correctly describes the slave trade as the economic engine that sustained the exploration program, not merely a side effect of it.

**The human side:**

The chapter's treatment of the Lagos 1444 scene — using Zurara's actual words, rendering the physical details of the separation — is historically responsible. It does not over-sentimentalize. It does not minimize. Zurara's text is used correctly as primary evidence. The acknowledgment in figures.md that the names of the 235 were not preserved is accurate and honest.

**What I find under-represented:**

The figures.md notes that some of the 235 were "eventually baptized" and that some became freedmen. The chapter could note that baptism was not merely symbolic: it created a legal category distinction in Portuguese law, separating "baptized Christians" from "Moors" and "pagans" in terms of their rights and potential for manumission. This distinction shaped the entire social history of the first generation of enslaved Africans in Portugal. It is not essential to the chapter's argument, but it would deepen the reader's understanding of why Henry framed the slave trade in theological terms: baptism was a legal as well as spiritual category.

**Cultural authenticity:**

The chapter correctly presents the Jolof Empire (referred to as "Buurba Jolof") as a functioning state. It correctly characterizes the Senegambian coastal peoples as political actors reading the Portuguese presence from within their own framework. The chapter does not render the Africans as passive.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The integrated treatment of slave trade and exploration as one program
- Zurara as a primary source used at the right level of specificity
- The acknowledgment of what the record does not preserve (the names of the 235)
- The economic structure of the licensed trade after 1443

**What needs correction:**

No factual errors requiring correction. One enrichment suggestion: consider a single sentence noting that baptism created legal-category consequences in Portuguese law, not merely spiritual ones, as this explains why Henry framed the slave trade in Crusade terms.

**Verdict:** APPROVED
