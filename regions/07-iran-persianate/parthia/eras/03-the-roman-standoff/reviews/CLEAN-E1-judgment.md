## Outside-Judgment Violations — Full Scan

Reviewing every sentence for implicit modern moral sensibility, anachronistic framing, ranking culture against a modern baseline, or romanticization.

**Item 1 — MINOR — "the richest man in Rome":**
> "Crassus, the richest man in Rome — the man who had crushed Spartacus and now wanted a war to call his own — had brought forty thousand soldiers east to take Parthia."

This is written from a Parthian point of view, and the characterization of Crassus is accurate to the historical record (including his own ambitions as stated in Roman sources). The phrase "wanted a war to call his own" is a characterization of Roman elite ambition, not a modern judgment on warfare. The framing remains inside the era's own logic. **No action required.**

**Item 2 — MINOR — "the Parthian problem":**
> "He died in 117 CE without having solved the Parthian problem he had set out to solve."

"The Parthian problem" is Trajan's frame, not the chapter's frame. It is attributed to him through the sentence structure. The chapter is narrating Trajan's experience, not endorsing his categorization of Parthia as a "problem." **No action required.**

**Item 3 — WATCH — "managed competition":**
> "This was not stalemate. This was managed competition, conducted at a level below the threshold of full war, expensive for both powers but less expensive than the alternative."

The GATE-2 scorer flagged this as slightly meta (author's analytical frame briefly visible). E-1 concurs: "expensive for both powers" and "less expensive than the alternative" are phrased as a modern cost-benefit analysis applied from outside the Parthian worldview. The Arsacid court did not think in terms of "expense" vs. "alternative" in this register. **Recommend revision:** ground the analytical point in the Parthian strategic logic rather than in an outside cost-benefit frame.

**Suggested revision:**
> "This was not stalemate. This was managed competition, the Parthian side of it conducted by the Karen house with a precision the Suren had applied to the Syrian plain: use the terrain, let the distances do the work, never commit to a confrontation that resolves nothing and costs everything."

This keeps the analytical point inside the Parthian worldview.

**Item 4 — CLEAR — Orodes II execution of the Suren general:**
The chapter presents this as the system's own logic without passing judgment on whether it was right or wrong. "The general had to die. The family endured." No verdict on the act. **Correct.**

**Item 5 — CLEAR — Euripides scene:**
"The audience laughed, or wept, or both." No judgment on the act of using Crassus's head in a theatrical performance. The scene is presented as the Arsacid court's own cultural expression. **Correct.**

**Item 6 — CLEAR — The Abgar frame:**
"Whether he was acting on Arsacid instructions, on his own calculation, or simply following a well-paid contract..." No judgment on the act of betrayal. The chapter declines to name it as betrayal. The chapter also declines to name it as heroism. **Correct.**

## Achebe Test — Final Check

Could a thoughtful member of Arsacid Parthian culture read this and feel their story was told with dignity — from inside — regardless of whether they agreed with every detail?

**Assessment:** Yes, with one caveat. The chapter tells the Parthian story from the Parthian center of gravity throughout. The noble-house system is presented as an intelligent adaptation, not as primitive decentralization. The Silk Road is presented as an administered infrastructure, not as lucky geography. The Euripides scene is presented as the Arsacid court's own cultural expression. The Suren's execution is presented without moral judgment. The Chapter does not explain Parthia TO a Roman or Western reader; it presents the Parthian world as self-evident.

The one caveat is Item 3 above. Fix it before BOARD.
