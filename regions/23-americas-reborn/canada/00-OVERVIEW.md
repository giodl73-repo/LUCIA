# Canada — "Not the United States": The Mosaic

Canada defines itself in part by what it is not. Not the melting pot — the mosaic. Not the revolutionary republic — the constitutional monarchy that evolved by deliberation rather than war. Not the manifest destiny — the peaceable kingdom, the middle power, the country that defines itself through contrast with its enormous and dominating neighbor to the south. This negative self-definition is both Canada's peculiarity and its genuine contribution to political thought: the argument that a large, diverse, multilingual nation need not insist on cultural uniformity to hold together; that the mosaic — multiple cultures maintaining their distinctiveness within a shared legal and political framework — is not a failure of integration but a different and possibly better model of how diverse peoples can coexist.

The complexity of this claim is considerable. Canada came into existence in 1867 as a confederation of British North American colonies, built on land that belonged to hundreds of indigenous nations whose treaties were mostly honored in the breach. The "two founding nations" mythology — English and French — explicitly excluded the First Nations, Métis, and Inuit peoples on whose territories both European nations had settled. The Quiet Revolution in Quebec in the 1960s produced a French Canadian nationalism that came within a single referendum percentage point (1995: 49.4% for separation) of breaking the country apart. Multiculturalism, enshrined as official policy in 1971 and in the Charter of Rights and Freedoms in 1982, represented a genuine commitment to the mosaic model — but it was a commitment made by a state still in the process of coming to terms with its treatment of the peoples whose land it had settled on.

## What Defined Them

**The two solitudes.** Hugh MacLennan's 1945 novel gave its name to the fundamental tension of Canadian political life: the English and French communities passing each other in the same country, connected by law and economics but separated by language, religion, history, and a mutual incomprehension that periodic crises brought to the surface. The Quiet Revolution transformed Quebec from a conservative, Church-dominated society into a modern secular one in less than a generation, and produced a nationalism that saw confederation not as a partnership but as a colonial relationship. The two referendum campaigns (1980, 1995) are the high-water mark of this tension; the 1995 result — 50.6% for staying — is the most ambiguous democratic verdict ever delivered in Canada.

**The Indian Act and its long shadow.** The Indian Act of 1876 is the legal framework that has governed Canada's relationship with its First Nations peoples for 150 years. It defined who was an "Indian," confined First Nations people to reserves, prohibited potlatch and Sun Dance ceremonies, took control of band governance, and established the residential school system that forcibly removed children from their families to assimilate them into European-Canadian culture. The residential schools operated from the 1880s to 1996 (the last one closed in 1996, not 1969 as is sometimes stated). The Truth and Reconciliation Commission's 2015 report documented the deaths, the abuse, and the cultural destruction of what it called "cultural genocide." The unmarked graves discovered at former residential school sites in 2021 brought this history into international awareness. Reconciliation is Canada's most urgent unfinished project.

**Peacekeeping as national mythology.** Canada invented the modern form of United Nations peacekeeping in the 1956 Suez Crisis, when External Affairs Minister Lester Pearson proposed a UN Emergency Force to separate the belligerents. Pearson won the Nobel Peace Prize for this. The image of Canada as the world's peacekeeper — impartial, trusted, constructively present in conflicts where others cannot go — became central to how Canadians understood their place in the world. The image has been complicated by the reality of Canadian military operations since the 1990s, by the Somalia Affair, and by participation in the Afghanistan war. But the mythology of the peacekeeper persists, and the aspiration it encodes — to be useful in the world without dominating it — is a genuine part of Canadian political culture.

**The Charter as Canadian identity.** The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (1982) is, for many Canadians, the clearest statement of national values: equality rights, language rights, mobility rights, and the reasonable limits clause (Section 1) that acknowledges that rights can be limited by law in a free and democratic society. The Charter differs from the US Bill of Rights in its explicit acknowledgment of collective rights (language minorities, Aboriginal peoples) alongside individual rights — a reflection of the mosaic model. For the Quebec government, which never signed the Constitution Act that included the Charter, it represents an imposition on provincial autonomy. For most Canadians, it represents the country's moral self-portrait.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Land Before:** Hundreds of nations organized their lives across a continent. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy, whose Great Law of Peace influenced the American Constitution's framers, had been governing the eastern woodlands for centuries before Europeans arrived. The Mi'kmaq navigated the Atlantic coast in birchbark canoes. The Anishinaabe managed the boreal forest. The Plains nations had organized a sophisticated buffalo-based economy. None of them had agreed to give any of this up.

**2 — The Conquest:** On the Plains of Abraham outside Quebec City, September 13, 1759: the British general Wolfe and the French general Montcalm fight a fifteen-minute battle that determines who will claim North America. Both generals die. The French lose. The question of what French Canada will become within the British system — and eventually the Canadian system — has not been resolved in the two and a half centuries since.

**3 — Confederation:** July 1, 1867: the Dominion of Canada comes into existence. John A. Macdonald, the first prime minister, is already thinking about the transcontinental railway that will hold the country together. Louis Riel, the Métis leader of the Red River Colony, is already thinking about what confederation means for the people who were not consulted.

**4 — The Residential Schools:** A child is taken from her family in northern Ontario and brought to a residential school. She is forbidden to speak her language. Her name is replaced with a number. She will not return home for ten years. What happens to her between these years is what the TRC found in testimony after testimony: beatings, abuse, hunger, and the systematic destruction of a culture's capacity to transmit itself to its children.

**5 — The October Crisis:** October 1970: the FLQ (Front de libération du Québec) kidnaps British trade commissioner James Cross and Quebec cabinet minister Pierre Laporte. Prime Minister Trudeau invokes the War Measures Act, suspending civil liberties across the country. Laporte is found murdered. Trudeau, asked how far he will go to maintain order, says: "Just watch me." The line between necessary firmness and authoritarian overreach is the subject of Canadian debate ever since.

**6 — Reconciliation:** The Truth and Reconciliation Commission issues its final report in 2015. Ninety-four calls to action. The ground-penetrating radar scans of former residential school sites begin in 2021. The flags across Canada are lowered. The country is trying to understand what it has done and what it owes. The work is not finished.
