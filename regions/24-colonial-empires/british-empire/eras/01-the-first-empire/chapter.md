# The First Empire — Atlantic Commerce and Plantation
## 1583–1783 CE

It was a March morning, cold by the standard of the season, the Mersey running dark and quick between its banks. The men loading the *Thetis* worked in wool jackets that smelled of lanolin and river damp, their breath visible in the air above the quayside, and the iron bars they carried aboard — fourteen hundredweight, each bar roughly the weight of a grown man's arm — rang against the deck planking as they went down. The cotton cloth was packed in oilskin. The kegs of gunpowder were handled with deliberate care and stowed below. The muskets came last, crated, each crate stenciled with the Birmingham manufacturer's mark.

The *Thetis*, registered to the firm of Heywood and Leigh, carried outbound from Liverpool: 4,200 yards of printed cotton cloth, 180 muskets, 72 kegs of gunpowder, 12 gross of knives, 400 gallons of Jamaica rum, and 14 cwt of iron bars. The captain's name was Robert Norris. The intended port of exchange was Anomabo, on the Gold Coast of Africa. The manifest did not say what the *Thetis* intended to carry on the return leg because the return leg was handled by a different ledger, maintained separately in a counting house on Duke Street, kept in a hand so careful and regular that a visitor seeing it for the first time might have taken it for a printer's specimen.

Captain Norris would later testify before a Parliamentary committee that the enslaved people aboard his vessels were cheerful, well-fed, and better off than the laboring poor of England. He said this under oath. He had also, a decade before that testimony, chained two hundred and sixty-seven men, women, and children into the lower deck of a vessel called the *Dobson* for the seven-week crossing to Barbados, and brought two hundred and twenty-one of them alive to market. The remaining forty-six were entered in the accounts as loss.

Both things were true: the ledger, and the crossing. The counting house, and the lower deck. Liverpool understood this. The city had organized its entire material life around the fact of both being simultaneously, continuously true.

## The Manifest

The British Empire did not begin in Liverpool. It began, if it can be said to have begun anywhere, in a series of royal charters issued in the late sixteenth century to companies seeking trading rights in distant places. The Muscovy Company (1555), the Levant Company (1581), the East India Company (1600), the Royal African Company (1660): each was a commercial instrument granted a monopoly by the Crown in exchange for a percentage of the profits and the obligation to establish and defend trading posts. The Crown did not conquer directly. It chartered private organizations and let them conquer commercially.

The Atlantic dimension of this system began with the colonization of Virginia (1607) and Bermuda (1612), but it took its definitive form with the sugar islands. Barbados was settled in 1627. Jamaica was taken from Spain in 1655. Sugar changed the calculation. Sugar required intensive labor on a scale that indentured white servants from England and Ireland could not supply — partly because the mortality rate in the Caribbean was high enough that labor supply was a constant emergency, and partly because indentured servants, having served their terms, became free men who then required wages and land. Enslaved Africans could be held in perpetuity. Their children could be held. The mathematics of plantation economics made this conclusion not just possible but, within the system's own accounting, efficient.

The Royal African Company was chartered in 1660 with a monopoly on the English slave trade. Its motto was *Regio Floret Commercio, Commercio Regnum* — by trade the king flourishes, by trade the kingdom. The motto was not ironic. It was a precise statement of the company's theory of itself: commerce and state power as co-constitutive, each requiring the other. The Company's governor was the Duke of York, later James II. Among its shareholders were members of the Privy Council, judges, and members of Parliament. The slave trade was not a marginal activity conducted by rough men at the edges of respectable commerce. It was organized from the center.

The monopoly was broken in 1698, when Parliament opened the trade to all English merchants on payment of a ten-percent duty on exports. The reason for this liberalization was not moral but commercial: the monopoly was inefficient, the Company was unable to supply the labor the plantations required, and the separate traders — the interlopers who had been smuggling their way into the trade for decades — were more responsive to market conditions. By 1750, when the *Thetis* cleared Liverpool on its March morning, the Royal African Company was dissolved and the trade fully privatized. The state's role was now to regulate, to license, and to collect the revenue. The commercial logic had absorbed the moral question and declared it settled.

In 1750, Liverpool was responsible for approximately forty percent of the British slave trade and approximately twenty-five percent of all European slaving voyages. The city had financed two hundred and twenty voyages in the previous decade. The triangular trade had paid for the Salthouse dock, the Exchange building on Water Street, the Bluecoat School. It had paid for the library on Duke Street, established in 1758 — any gentleman of the town could join for a small annual fee and have access to the best new books from London.

A clerk named James Penny, employed by a merchant house on Old Hall Street, walked each morning from his lodgings in the north quarter past the dock, past the warehouses where the West India sugar was stored before distribution, past the coffee house on Castle Street where the ship captains gathered in the early afternoon to discuss the next season's voyages. He had never been to Africa. He had never been to Barbados. His ledger contained figures for cotton cloth and rum and iron bars and, on the return column, figures for hogsheads of sugar and bales of raw cotton. He was a careful accountant. He kept two ledgers.

## The Coast

At Anomabo, on what the British called the Gold Coast, the commercial arrangements were considerably more complicated than the outbound manifest suggested.

The Fante people who controlled the trade at Anomabo were not passive suppliers. They were sophisticated commercial actors who had been conducting international trade with European merchants since the Portuguese arrived in the fifteenth century. They understood the commodity market they were operating in. They set prices. They maintained the barracoons — the compounds where captives were held before purchase — as commercial infrastructure and charged the British merchants for their use. They required advance payment in goods, which meant the British merchants bore the full financing risk before a single transaction was completed: the cotton cloth and the iron bars were transferred before the captives were produced. The Fante traders understood their leverage.

The captives at Anomabo came from varied origins. Some were prisoners of war taken in the conflicts among the Akan-speaking states of the interior. Some were convicted criminals whose punishment their societies had commuted to sale. Some were debtors. Some were people seized by raiding parties who had no prior claim on them. The categories were not stable, and the British merchants did not inquire too closely — the accounting category that mattered was "sound," and the transaction that mattered was the price. But the Fante traders who sold them had their own knowledge of what they were doing. The distance between the Fante concept of pawning and debt-bondage and the Atlantic concept of chattel slavery was considerable, and the British trade systematically exploited that distance, purchasing people within one framework and holding them under another. The Fante knew this. Some Fante leaders protested the conditions of the British trade and negotiated for protections that were not always honored. The coast had its own second ledger.

Captain Norris spent approximately six weeks at Anomabo completing the exchange. The negotiations were conducted through interpreters. The captives were examined for fitness by the ship's surgeon, who noted their condition in his log with the vocabulary of livestock assessment: sound teeth, clear eyes, no visible disease. They were then transferred to the *Thetis* in canoes, in groups, under guard. When the manifest was complete — the number of captives matching the number the merchants had contracted for — the *Thetis* set its course west-southwest, toward Barbados.

## The Second Ledger

The crossing took seven weeks in ordinary conditions. In the lower deck of the *Thetis*, in a space roughly seven feet in height and as wide as the ship's beam, the captives were allocated plank space in the ratio that the trade had standardized: each adult male approximately sixteen inches of width, each adult female approximately fifteen inches. They lay on their sides in rows, shackled at wrist and ankle, unable to sit upright when the deck above was in place. The smell of the lower deck in the second week of the crossing, ship's surgeons recorded, was a compound of urine, dysentery, sweat, and the sweet rot of early gangrene that could not be treated below the waterline.

Thomas Clarkson arrived in Liverpool in 1787. He was twenty-seven years old, a Cambridge graduate who had written a prize essay two years earlier on the question of whether it was lawful to enslave men against their will. The essay had led him to abolition, and abolition had led him to Liverpool, where the evidence was. He walked the docks. He visited the counting houses. He found the ship's surgeons who were willing to talk, and he collected their testimony in notebooks. He found the ship's logs that recorded mortality by voyage. He found the *Brookes*: a Liverpool slaving vessel whose internal measurements had been documented and from which a diagram had been produced, a diagram showing the measured allotment for each body in the hold.

The *Brookes* diagram was printed and distributed as a broadsheet. A merchant in Manchester who had never seen the inside of a slave ship could hold it in his hands and see the dimensions: six feet by one foot four inches for each adult male, chained in rows like the teeth of a comb. The diagram took the trade's own accounting logic — bodies as units of cargo — and applied it at human scale. It made the second ledger visible.

Clarkson gave his notebooks to the Manchester Anti-Slavery Committee and the Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade, founded in London in 1787. The Society used the data to petition Parliament. William Wilberforce introduced the first Parliamentary motion for abolition in 1789. It was defeated. He introduced it again in 1791. Defeated again. The trade was generating approximately three and a half million pounds per year in profit for its Liverpool investors. The West India planters' lobby in Parliament was well-organized and well-funded. The argument that the trade was a national commercial necessity — the first ledger's argument — was answered each time with evidence from the second ledger, and each time the first ledger won.

Olaudah Equiano had been taken from what is now southeastern Nigeria at approximately eleven years old. He remembered the smell of the ship's hold with the precision of a person who has spent seven decades trying to understand how a childhood ends: "The closeness of the place, and the heat of the climate, added to the number in the ship, which was so crowded that each had scarcely room to turn himself, almost suffocated us." He was sold twice in Africa before the Atlantic crossing, sold again in Barbados, held in Virginia, taken to sea by a Royal Navy officer who happened to treat him with something approaching respect. He was sold again. He worked as a sailor in the Mediterranean trade and in the Seven Years' War, buying and selling small goods on his own account, accumulating enough credit over nearly a decade to purchase his own freedom in 1766 for forty pounds sterling.

He learned to read and write with the same systematic determination he had applied to every other instrument of survival. He understood that the argument for abolition had to be made in the language the British Parliament would accept: not the language of Igbo cosmology, not the language of the Orisha or the knowledge held in his body across the Atlantic, but the language of natural rights and Christian ethics and commercial calculation. His *Interesting Narrative* was published in 1789, the same year Wilberforce brought his first motion. The book sold subscription copies before publication. Equiano managed his own distribution network. He traveled to speak at public meetings in the industrial north, in Manchester and Birmingham and the towns that had built their wealth on the trade, addressing the cities that had organized their prosperity around his transportation.

He stood in rooms that had been built with money from voyages like the one that had taken him, and he spoke in the English that his captors had used, and he laid out the argument in the terms that the first ledger understood: the commercial inefficiency of slave labor versus free labor, the moral inconsistency of a Christian nation that held men as property, the legal contradiction of a country that had abolished villeinage at home and maintained slavery abroad. He was a better accountant than Norris. He had read his Adam Smith. He understood that the second ledger was not a supplement to the first; it was the first ledger's own argument, applied consistently.

## The Legal Architecture

The system's legitimacy rested on a specific legal framework, and the framework had a gap.

The gap was identified by a legal scholar, Granville Sharp. In 1765, Sharp encountered a man named Jonathan Strong, badly beaten and near death on the streets of London, and arranged for his treatment. Strong recovered and found work. Two years later, his former master reappeared and had him arrested with the intention of selling him to a Jamaican planter. Sharp contested the sale on the grounds that no English law authorized the holding of enslaved people on English soil. The case was settled without a ruling, but Sharp began compiling the legal argument.

The Somerset Case of 1772 was the crystallization. James Somerset had been brought to England from Massachusetts by his owner, Charles Steuart, and had escaped and been recaptured. Sharp arranged representation for Somerset before Lord Mansfield in King's Bench. The case turned on a single question: did English common law authorize the master to seize, detain, and forcibly remove to another jurisdiction a person who had not committed any crime against English law?

Lord Mansfield's ruling was precise and limited. He found that English common law contained no such authorization. He did not rule that slavery was illegal in England, or that the slave trade was illegal, or that the Barbados Slave Code that governed the plantations was invalid. He ruled only that the master could not compel the return of Somerset to Jamaica by force. The first ledger could not, in England, enforce itself against a person on English soil who had not been convicted of an English crime.

The merchants of Liverpool read the ruling carefully. Their counsel advised that the ruling had no effect on the colonies, where the slave codes remained in full legal force. They were correct. But Sharp and his colleagues read it differently: if the common law of England did not sanction slavery, then the common law of England was, in principle, available to any enslaved person in England who claimed its protection. The legitimacy vocabulary was not consistent. The first ledger had been shown to have a page it could not account for.

## Tacky's War

In the spring of 1760, in the parish of St. Mary on the north coast of Jamaica, a Coromantee man named Tacky organized what would become the largest enslaved uprising in the Caribbean before the Haitian Revolution.

Tacky had been a chief in the Gold Coast — some accounts say a chief of the Fante, the same people who had organized the trade at Anomabo — before capture and transportation. He knew the military organization of West African warfare: the ritual preparation that bound fighters to each other before action, the organization of recruits through the cultural networks that crossed estate boundaries the way they had crossed village boundaries on the Gold Coast. He recruited from among the Akan-speaking people on the surrounding plantations, people who shared a language and a cultural framework and who understood, as Tacky understood, that the plantation system's coercive power depended on preventing exactly this kind of organized, culturally-grounded resistance. The uprising began on Easter Sunday, when the garrison was reduced and the planters were at church.

The rebels killed the garrison at Fort Haldane and took its weapons. They moved from estate to estate, recruiting as they went. The British response was immediate and organized: the regular troops, the Maroon fighters under treaty obligation to suppress slave uprisings, the militia. Tacky was killed in the field. The uprising was suppressed. The official count was sixty white colonists killed, and approximately four hundred enslaved people killed or executed in the aftermath, with hundreds more transported off the island. The planters' accounts recorded it as a financial event: the cost of suppression, the value of the executed enslaved people as a loss, the reduction in the labor supply.

In the counting houses of Liverpool, Tacky's War appeared as a risk premium. The insurance rates on Jamaica plantation investments went up. The planters lobbied Parliament for increased military presence. The merchants adjusted the terms of their financing arrangements to account for what the accounts called "the Jamaica risk." What the accounts did not record was the strategic logic of the uprising, or the fact that the Maroons who helped suppress it had themselves won their freedom from the British through armed resistance a generation earlier, or the knowledge that Tacky brought from the Gold Coast, or the decisions made in secret in plantation quarters on a hundred estates across the Caribbean in the years after 1760 by people who understood exactly what the first ledger would and would not record.

The second ledger had its own entries.

## The American Complication

The American colonies declared independence in 1776. Their argument was the first ledger's argument: natural rights, liberty, the rule of law, the illegitimacy of taxation without representation. They used the language that the British Empire had developed to justify itself, and they turned it against the British Empire. Samuel Johnson, in London, asked the question that was obvious to anyone paying attention: "How is it that we hear the loudest yelps for liberty among the drivers of Negroes?" The question was not answered. It was noted.

The contradiction was visible in the colonies as well. The argument for American independence and the argument for the abolition of slavery shared the same vocabulary. Thomas Jefferson drafted the Declaration of Independence and owned over six hundred enslaved people across his lifetime. The Continental Congress removed from the draft a passage attributing the slave trade to the British Crown — the colonial delegates from the southern states would not sign a document that condemned slavery. The first ledger stayed intact in Virginia.

In the Caribbean and in British North America, some fifteen thousand enslaved people had accepted the British offer of freedom in exchange for military service. Lord Dunmore's Proclamation of 1775 offered freedom to enslaved people belonging to rebel colonists who could reach the British lines. The offer was not universal — enslaved people belonging to Loyalists were excluded — and it was strategic rather than principled. But it was acted on. People crossed the lines. People served in the Black Loyalist regiments. When the war ended and the British evacuated, the Black Loyalists evacuated with them, to Nova Scotia, to Sierra Leone, to the Caribbean. They had fought for a freedom the British offered instrumentally and then struggled to honor.

The loss of the thirteen colonies closed the First Empire. Britain now had no major presence in continental North America. The Caribbean remained, and India was becoming the new focus. But the loss of the American colonies also removed the argument that the slave trade was necessary for the survival of the colonial system: the thirteen colonies were gone, and the empire continued. The commercial case for the trade could now be challenged on its own terms.

## The Abolition

The Slave Trade Abolition Act was passed by Parliament on the twenty-fifth of March, 1807.

The vote was 283 to 16. The debate had taken twenty years. William Wilberforce had introduced the motion eighteen times. Each previous defeat had been documented, the arguments against recorded in Hansard, the commercial lobby's position maintained through two decades of Parliamentary majorities. The shift came not because the merchants of Liverpool changed their minds but because the commercial calculus had changed: the slave trade was now less profitable than it had been, the West Indian planters were in relative decline, the free-trade argument had shifted to suggest that free labor markets were more efficient than coerced ones, and the moral argument had been made often enough and in enough public forums that the members who voted against it faced a different political calculation than their predecessors had.

William Roscoe voted for the Act. He was Liverpool's Member of Parliament, elected in 1806 on an anti-slave-trade platform. He knew before he voted that the vote would end his Parliamentary career; the Liverpool electorate that had elected him was split, and the merchants who had not voted for him were organized. He voted for the Act and lost his seat at the next election. He returned to Liverpool. He continued to live there, to write there, to work in the banking firm he had built there, to use the library that the city's commerce had purchased.

His library was eventually sold to pay debts in 1816. The city bought it back. It became the foundation of Liverpool's public library system. The books that Roscoe had used to write his histories of Renaissance Florence, the books that Equiano might have consulted when he was assembling his argument, the books that had been purchased with the profits of two centuries of triangular trade: they remained in Liverpool. They remained available to any member of the public who wished to read them.

The first legal British slaving vessel cleared Liverpool in 1807, before the Act's implementation date. It was a March morning. The manifest was filed. The ship was gone before the Act came into force.

Captain Norris had been dead for sixteen years by then. His Parliamentary testimony was in the record. His mortality figures from the *Dobson* were in the record, too — Clarkson had found them and published them. Both ledgers were now public documents.

## Landing

The *Thetis* made Anomabo in late April. The exchange was completed. The captives were loaded. The ship set course for Barbados.

Of the forty-six people who died on the *Dobson* in 1769 and were entered in the accounts as loss, the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database preserves the voyage's mortality figures but not their names. They were not named in the records kept by the men who transported them. What they were called, what they had been called before they were taken, what languages they spoke: these were not recorded. The second ledger has its own silences.

What persisted was not the names but the argument. Equiano's *Interesting Narrative* went through nine editions in his lifetime, was translated into Dutch and German, and was circulating in Liverpool and Manchester and the industrial towns of England through the decade of the Parliamentary debates. The argument he made — that the system's own language, applied consistently, condemned the system — was available in the libraries that the system had built.

The Slave Trade Abolition Act of 1807 ended the legal British trade. It did not end slavery in the British Empire; that required the Slavery Abolition Act of 1833. It did not end the illegal trade, which continued for decades. It did not compensate the people who had been transported. When the Slavery Abolition Act was passed in 1833, the British government compensated the slaveholders — approximately twenty million pounds — for the loss of their property. The enslaved people received nothing. The second ledger was closed, but the first ledger's final accounts went to the owners.

The library on Duke Street still stands, in a different building now, housing different books. The dock at Salthouse is a heritage site. The Exchange has been rebuilt twice. Liverpool is a different city. The city's museums document the trade explicitly, in the language of the second ledger.

What the city built, it used to understand what it had been. The contradiction does not resolve. It produces.

The manifest is still being filed. The entries are in a different hand.
