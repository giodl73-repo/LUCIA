# R2-P2 — Ibn Khaldun (PANEL-2, targeted)

**Chapter:** Papal States — Plenitudo Potestatis
**Stage:** PANEL-2 — Notes review
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The production bible has answered my primary concern. The structural engine is correctly named and the terminal condition follows from the mechanism rather than being imposed by external shock. "Legitimacy-debt" is exactly the right concept: the papacy holds the credential that makes secular authority recognizable, and every king who needs to be recognized must negotiate with the creditor. This is asabiyyah in an unusual form — not the solidarity of a warrior band but the solidarity of a confessional community, and the papacy is its court of final appeal.

The terminal condition is also correctly identified. The fiscal machine (fees, benefice sales, reserve provisions) erodes the very legitimacy the institution claims to hold. I have seen this pattern before: in the Abbasid caliphate, where the caliph's spiritual authority and the actual power of the Turkish guard diverged until the caliph was a ceremonial figure; in the Mamluk sultans, whose military legitimacy and dynastic claims were always in tension. The papacy's version of this pattern is the claim pushed to its extreme (the *Unam Sanctam*) meeting the political reality that France is no longer willing to treat that claim as binding. The legitimacy machine stops working when a major party simply refuses it.

One addition I would recommend for Section 2: the chapter should name the investiture settlement's actual ambiguity. The Concordat of Worms (1122) divided temporal and spiritual investiture — but who decides which bishops are spiritually qualified? If the pope controls spiritual investiture, and a bishop's temporal authority depends on his spiritual office, then the pope ultimately controls the appointment even when the emperor holds the scepter. This is the point the canonists immediately recognized and exploited. The machine was built on a deliberately ambiguous settlement.

**One structural concern:** The notes plan five sections covering 1122–1303. This is 181 years. The chapter risks becoming too dense if each section tries to carry equal weight. My recommendation: Sections 1 and 2 (1122–1180) can be relatively brief — they establish the engine. Section 3 (Innocent III, 1198–1215) should carry the chapter's weight. Section 4 (Gregory IX, 1216–1260) can be focused on the machine's self-continuation. Section 5 (Boniface VIII, 1294–1303) can be brief and precise — the terminal condition does not need extensive narration, only one decisive scene. Weight the chapter toward the engine at full operation, not toward its establishment or its failure.

**Verdict:** The structural argument is sound. The chapter is ready to write. Watch the pacing of the five sections — do not give equal time to each.
