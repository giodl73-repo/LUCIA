# The Bornu Empire

The muskets arrived in Ngazargamu wrapped in oiled leather, packed on camels that had crossed the Sahara from Tripoli in the care of men who spoke Turkish and Arabic and who knelt to pray at the same hours as the scholars of the Bornu court. Ahmad ibn Fartuwa, imam and chronicler to Mai Idris Alooma, counted them as they were unloaded in the courtyard of the royal compound. He counted everything. It was his function. The mai had charged him with recording the wars and the victories and the instruments by which the victories were won, and Ahmad took the charge as a scholar takes a commission from God: with precision.

The muskets smelled of oil and iron and the particular dust of a desert crossing. They were heavy. A man could not swing one like a sword or hurl it like a javelin. It had to be braced against the shoulder, the powder measured, the ball seated, the match lit. The musketeers from Tripoli demonstrated the loading sequence in the courtyard while the mai's cavalry watched from horseback, sweating under quilted cotton armor that weighed as much as the muskets themselves, their spears upright, their war horses shifting in the heat, their own weapons older than anything the men from Tripoli carried. Ahmad noted the demonstration. He noted the sound, which he had not heard before, a crack that scattered the pigeons from the walls and left a haze of smoke that the harmattan wind pulled apart.

Mai Idris Alooma watched from behind the curtain.

This was how a mai watched. The curtain was heavy cotton, dyed dark, and it hung from the wooden frame of the reception chamber so that light passed through it only as a glow. The Saifawa had ruled from behind curtains and through intermediaries for longer than anyone in Ngazargamu could count. The mai's body was the body of the state. It was sacred, set apart, maintained by rituals that predated the court's Islam and were reinforced by the five centuries of Islamic learning that had become the dynasty's own. No one saw the mai eat. No one addressed him directly. The waziri spoke for him, leaning close to the curtain to catch the words and then turning to deliver them in the full voice the court could hear. The kaigama commanded his armies in his name. The mai was present and hidden, powerful and veiled, and this was not weakness. It was the architecture of a sovereignty that had survived the loss of Kanem to the east, the crossing of Lake Chad, the rebuilding of an empire on the western shore, and the seven hundred years of the Saifawa before any of that.

The muskets were the mai's idea. Alooma had been corresponding with the Ottoman Porte. A letter had gone north across the desert to Istanbul, written in Arabic on paper the court purchased from Egyptian merchants, sealed with the mai's mark, and carried by a messenger who rode the same caravan routes that carried salt south from Bilma and ivory north to Fezzan. The messenger had returned with an answer and with the men who now stood in the courtyard reloading their weapons. The Ottomans were interested in an ally south of the Sahara. They were fighting their own wars on a dozen fronts, and a friendly state controlling the eastern Saharan trade corridor was worth cultivating. What Alooma wanted was firearms and the men who knew how to use them. What the Ottomans wanted was access, influence, and the continuation of a trade that brought them leather and civet and enslaved people from the Lake Chad basin. The exchange was not charity. It was statecraft, and both sides understood its terms.

Ahmad ibn Fartuwa understood it too. He sat in the shade of the compound wall, his reed pen and ink pot beside him, the paper on his knee already marked with the morning's observations. He was not a mere scribe. He was a Maliki jurist, trained in the legal tradition that governed the learned courts of the central Sudan, and he saw in the mai's correspondence with Istanbul something the cavalry officers watching the demonstration did not: the mai was placing Bornu inside a larger world. The muskets were instruments. The correspondence was architecture. A state that could write to the Ottoman sultan and receive an answer was a state that existed in the calculations of powers beyond the lake, beyond the desert, beyond the horizon the cavalry could see from horseback.

The lake itself was enormous and inconstant. Lake Chad shifted with the rains, swelling in the wet season until its southern shores turned to marsh and the fishing villages rebuilt on higher ground, shrinking in the dry until the water pulled back and exposed flats of dried mud where cattle herders drove their animals to graze on the receding green. Ngazargamu sat west of the lake, a walled capital of tens of thousands of people, its compounds built of mud-brick and timber, its markets drawing merchants from Hausaland to the southwest and from Fezzan to the north. The city hummed with the sound of Kanuri and Arabic and the half-dozen languages of the tributary peoples the Saifawa had incorporated over centuries, not by erasing them but by layering the mai's authority over their own.

Ahmad finished his count. The muskets, the musketeers from Tripoli who would train the mai's men. Powder and lead packed in separate bundles. He dipped the reed pen in the lamp-black ink, and on the page before him the Arabic numerals took shape: the inventory of a transformation the mai had ordered from behind the curtain. This was the chronicle he was composing, the history of the first twelve years of the mai's reign, a book that would survive the centuries and the fires and the silting of the lake and become, for scholars yet unborn, the most detailed portrait of any Saifawa ruler.

He did not know this. He knew only that the mai had asked him to write, and that the muskets were here, and that the wars were coming.
