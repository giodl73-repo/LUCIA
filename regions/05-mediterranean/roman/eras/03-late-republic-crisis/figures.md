# Figures — The Late Republic and the Crisis (Era 3)

## Tiberius Sempronius Gracchus (c. 163-133 BCE)

Tribune of the plebs 133 BCE. Proposed enforcement of existing land-holding limits. Beaten to death on the Capitoline.

**What we know:** Attested in Plutarch, Appian, and Cicero. His agrarian law and its consequences are well-documented. His killing in the Forum is one of the best-attested political murders of the Republic. The speech attributed to him by Plutarch cannot be his exact words but captures the substance of the agrarian argument.

**Sources:** Plutarch, *Ti. Gracchus*; Appian, *Civil Wars* 1.7-17; Stockton, *The Gracchi* (1979).

## Gaius Sempronius Gracchus (154-121 BCE)

Tribune 123-122 BCE. Extended his brother's reforms to grain subsidies, provincial taxation, and jury composition. Died in the violence of 121 BCE.

**Sources:** Plutarch, *C. Gracchus*; Appian, *Civil Wars* 1.21-26; Stockton (1979).

## Gaius Marius (157-86 BCE)

Seven-time consul. Defeated Jugurtha and the Cimbri. Opened the army to the propertyless. The man who created the personal army.

**What we know:** Well-attested in Plutarch, Sallust (*Jugurthine War*), and Appian. His military reforms are documented through their effects rather than through a single legislative act. His rivalry with Sulla and his proscriptions of 87 BCE are in the sources.

**Sources:** Plutarch, *Marius*; Sallust, *Bellum Iugurthinum*; Keppie, *The Making of the Roman Army* (1984).

## Lucius Cornelius Sulla Felix (138-78 BCE)

First general to march on Roma. Dictator without time limit. Author of the Sullan constitution. Resigned and died.

**What we know:** Attested in Plutarch, Appian, and fragments of his own memoirs. The proscriptions are documented in detail. The Sullan constitution's provisions are recoverable from the sources. His resignation remains one of the most discussed acts in Roman political history.

**Sources:** Plutarch, *Sulla*; Appian, *Civil Wars* 1.55-105; Keaveney, *Sulla: The Last Republican* (2005).

## Gaius Julius Caesar (100-44 BCE)

Conqueror of Gaul. Crosser of the Rubicon. Dictator perpetuo. Murdered in the Senate.

**What we know:** Caesar is one of the best-documented figures of the ancient world. His own *Commentarii* on the Gallic and Civil Wars survive. He is extensively covered by Plutarch, Suetonius, Appian, Cassius Dio, and Cicero's correspondence. The assassination is documented from multiple angles.

**What we cannot know:** His intentions. Whether he aimed at permanent monarchy or some other settlement. The chapter presents the structural problem (dictator perpetuo abolished the time limit) without claiming to know the mind.

**Sources:** Caesar, *De Bello Gallico*, *De Bello Civili*; Plutarch, *Caesar*; Suetonius, *Divus Iulius*; Gelzer, *Caesar: Politician and Statesman* (1968); Goldsworthy, *Caesar* (2006).

## Marcus Tullius Cicero (106-43 BCE)

Orator, senator, consul, political theorist. The Republic's most articulate defender. Killed in the proscriptions of 43 BCE.

**What we know:** Cicero is the most extensively documented private individual of the ancient world. His speeches, letters, and philosophical works survive in large quantity. His correspondence with Atticus provides an unparalleled view of late Republican politics from inside. His death in the proscriptions is in Plutarch and Appian.

**Sources:** Cicero, *De Re Publica*, letters; Plutarch, *Cicero*; Everitt, *Cicero* (2001).

## A Note on Sources

The late Republic is paradoxically the best-documented and most contested period of Roman history. Cicero's letters provide real-time political commentary. Caesar's Commentarii are primary sources shaped by their author's political needs. Sallust writes as a partisan. Appian and Plutarch write from the perspective of the Empire, looking back at the Republic's crisis through the lens of what it became. The chapter uses these sources while maintaining awareness that each has an agenda, and presents the structural crisis rather than adjudicating between competing personal accounts.
