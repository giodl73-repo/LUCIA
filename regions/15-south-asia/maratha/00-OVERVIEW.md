# Maratha — Swaraj

We are the Maratha — the people of Maharashtra, the Deccan plateau, the hard basalt country between the Western Ghats and the Mughal empire's southern reach — and we built something the Mughal state could not prevent and the later British could not fully absorb until they had fought us four times. Our word is swaraj: self-rule, self-sovereignty, the governance of one's own country by one's own people. Shivaji Maharaj, who founded our state in the name of this principle, was not a rebel against a distant overlord; he was a statesman constructing a new polity from the materials of the Deccan's own military and social traditions, legitimized by the Sanskrit coronation he insisted upon and the dharma he claimed to uphold.

We governed through a confederacy — not through a single throne but through the Peshwa at Pune and the great sardars who held territories across the subcontinent. At our height, Maratha power reached from the Arabian Sea to Bengal, from the Deccan to the borders of Delhi. The Marathas extracted chauth — tribute — from almost every power in the subcontinent. We did not always build lasting institutions everywhere we raided. But the Peshwa's administration at Pune was a genuine state, with revenue records, bureaucratic apparatus, and a political culture that was distinctly Brahmin, distinctly Deccani, and distinctly modern in some of its practices.

## What Defined Them

**Swaraj as principle.** Shivaji articulated the idea of self-rule as a positive obligation — not merely resistance to Mughal power, but the construction of a legitimate Hindu sovereignty in the Deccan. His coronation in 1674 was a deliberate political act: bringing a Brahmin scholar from Varanasi to perform the Vedic rites, taking the title Chhatrapati, claiming descent from Rajput lineage, making the Maratha state's legitimacy legible in the Sanskrit political vocabulary. This mattered because it answered Mughal sovereignty with a different kind of sovereignty claim.

**The forts and the Ghats.** The Western Ghats gave the Maratha military its distinctive character. The mountain forts — Raigad, Sinhagad, Pratapgad, hundreds of others — were the skeleton of the early Maratha state. Light cavalry that could disappear into the Ghats after a strike, supply lines running through mountain passes that Mughal infantry could not follow, intelligence networks based on the farming communities of the Deccan — this was a military system adapted to terrain, not copied from the Mughal model.

**The Peshwa and the Brahmin state.** After Shivaji's death, the Peshwa — the prime minister — became the effective ruler, while the Chhatrapati remained the ceremonial sovereign. The Peshwa line was Brahmin, and the Peshwa state at Pune developed a bureaucratic culture that was both distinctly Brahmin (literate, record-keeping, administratively sophisticated) and distinctly Maratha (aggressive, expansionist, militarized). The combination produced one of the most capable administrations in eighteenth-century South Asia.

**Chauth — the economics of sovereignty.** The Maratha claim to levy chauth — one-quarter of a territory's revenue — from non-Maratha lands was the fiscal expression of sovereignty. You paid chauth because the Marathas had the power to collect it and because paying meant they would not raid you. This was not straightforward taxation; it was a tribute system that the Marathas justified as their right as the defenders of the Hindu dharma and that others experienced as extortion. Both characterizations contain truth.

**Panipat and resilience.** The Third Battle of Panipat (1761) was a catastrophe: much of the Maratha leadership was killed, the army destroyed, and the northward expansion ended permanently. The confederacy recovered, but it never recovered fully. The sardars — Scindia, Holkar, Bhonsle, Gaekwad — continued as regional powers. The Peshwa continued at Pune. But the vision of a Maratha-dominated subcontinent died at Panipat, and the British moved into the vacuum.

## The Chapters

**1 — Shivaji's Swaraj:** The young Shivaji takes the Torna fort with a small band. It is the first fort, but he already knows it is not the last. He is twenty years old. He is building a state one fort at a time.

**2 — Sambhaji and Aurangzeb's War:** Sambhaji is captured. Aurangzeb offers him life if he converts. Sambhaji refuses. The execution is deliberate and prolonged. The Marathas do not surrender. They fight harder.

**3 — Baji Rao I:** The Peshwa rides north at the head of the light cavalry. He will reach Delhi in 1737. He will not stay. He does not need to. He needs only to demonstrate that he can.

**4 — Panipat:** The Maratha army faces Ahmad Shah Durrani. The battle turns in the afternoon. The Peshwa's relative Vishwasrao is killed. When Bhau hears this, the command structure collapses. The retreat becomes a rout.

**5 — Anglo-Maratha Wars:** The British are patient. They fight three wars spread over forty years. After the third, the Peshwa Baji Rao II surrenders and is exiled to Bithur on a pension. He never returns to the Deccan.
