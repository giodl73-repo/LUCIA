## Assessment of Theological-Legal Framework

The chapter makes its argument through the internal logic of Catholic theology, and it does so accurately. Specific assessments:

**The just-war tradition:** The chapter correctly locates the Requerimiento within the tradition of theological-legal justification for war. It correctly implies (without over-explaining) that just war in the scholastic tradition required: (1) legitimate authority, (2) just cause, (3) right intention, (4) last resort. The Requerimiento was designed to establish (1) — papal authority over all temporal rulers — and (2) — the refusal of the gift as the just cause. The chapter does not enumerate these categories explicitly, which is correct for the register; it allows the reader to understand them from within the document's logic.

**Palacios Rubios's theological sources:** The chapter describes his argument as derived from "the doctrine of papal temporal authority, the theory of just war that Aquinas had developed from Augustine." This is accurate as a summary. More precisely, Palacios Rubios was working from the Gratian tradition in canon law and from the papal plenitudo potestatis doctrine — the claim of full papal authority over all temporal rulers that Innocent III and Boniface VIII had developed and that Palacios Rubios accepted as settled law. The chapter's framing is sufficient.

**Sepúlveda's Aristotle:** Correctly characterized. The *Politics* Book I, Chapter V on natural slavery is the source. The chapter states "Aristotle had held that some human beings were, by nature, better fitted to obey than to govern, and that their subjugation was not tyranny but the correct ordering of a relationship that served both parties." This is accurate to Aristotle's argument and to Sepúlveda's application of it.

**Las Casas's counter-argument:** The chapter correctly identifies Las Casas as arguing that the indigenous peoples were *inocentes* — a technical term in just-war theory meaning "not guilty of an offense that constitutes just cause for war." The chapter uses the word once, attributed to Las Casas's "theological vocabulary," which is precisely correct.

**The Hatuey scene's theological significance:** The chapter renders Hatuey's choice (to refuse baptism to avoid going to the same heaven as Christians) as "rational" within the evidence Las Casas had observed, and notes Las Casas's theological conclusion: "the judgment damned not Hatuey but the Christianity that had produced this result." This is an accurate representation of Las Casas's own theological logic. He was arguing that the practice of the Spanish had made Christianity repellent to the people it was supposed to save — a failure of mission that was, in his framework, a sin against the missionary obligation.

**The Valladolid logical impossibility:** The chapter's analysis — "either the indigenous peoples were persons, in which case the conquest was unjust; or they were not persons, in which case the framework of just war did not apply" — is analytically sound and is consistent with what contemporary canon lawyers and theologians found troubling about the Sepúlveda position. Sepúlveda's argument in fact cut against the entire project of the Requerimiento: if the indigenous peoples were natural slaves, the form of the Requerimiento (which was addressed to rational beings capable of choice) was superfluous. The chapter correctly identifies this tension as one of the reasons the panel could not verdict.

## One Clarification

The chapter describes Alexander VI's papal bull as the source of the "donation." More precisely, the relevant bull is *Inter caetera* (1493, with multiple versions), which granted Spain the right to Christianize the people of the newly discovered lands — a missionary mandate, not a simple territorial donation. The territorial title Spain claimed from it was a Spanish legal interpretation of what the bull authorized, not the bull's explicit grant. The chapter's framing ("the donation of Alexander VI") is the Spanish Crown's own vocabulary and is therefore correct for the inside-voice register. No revision required; the distinction is appropriately left implicit.

## Overall Assessment

The chapter handles Catholic moral theology with accuracy and with the internal logic the chapter's voice requires. It presents the theological debate as the Spanish Empire experienced it: not as a pretext for conduct its actors knew was wrong, but as a genuine intellectual crisis within a tradition that was, by its own standards, capable of generating both the conduct and its theological indictment. This is historically and theologically accurate.
