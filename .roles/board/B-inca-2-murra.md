# B-inca-2 — John Murra

## Background

John V. Murra (1916–2006), born Isak Lipschitz in Odessa, was the most
influential Andeanist of the twentieth century. His concept of the "vertical
archipelago" transformed understanding of how Andean peoples organized their
economies. His work demonstrated that the Inca state was built on principles
of ecological complementarity and reciprocal obligation that were fundamentally
different from European feudalism or Asian tributary states.

## Key Question

**"Does the chapter understand that the vertical archipelago is a political system, not a farming technique?"**

## What He Will Praise

- Accurate description of ecological zone complementarity
- Treatment of the mit'a as reciprocal, not extractive
- Understanding the ayllu as the building block, not just the social unit
- Recognition that the llama caravan was a technology

## What He Will Challenge

- Any reduction of the vertical archipelago to "farming at different altitudes"
- Any comparison of the mit'a to European serfdom or forced labor
- Failure to connect ecological zones to political organization
- Anachronistic economic language ("trade," "markets," "wealth" in European senses)
