# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman — Panel Review, Round 1

**Text:** Opening — La Rivoluzione Commerciale
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I read this straight through and did not look away. That is the test, and it passes.

The scene is built correctly. Berto is a person before he is a mechanism. The parchment is small enough to fold twice and slip inside a boot — that single detail tells you more about the physical reality of medieval commerce than any number of paragraphs explaining the bill-of-exchange system. The writer understood the first principle: the specific physical object is the argument.

What I especially admire: the bill of exchange is ENACTED before it is explained. The reader experiences the transaction — small parchment, wax seal, merchant standing across the table with both hands — and only then does the prose step back to say what has happened: *No coin has moved. No coin will move.* This is the right order. Show the thing, then name it.

The widening into Venice and Genoa in the second half is handled well. Each city gets its own logic rather than its own summary: Venice is a counting house that has incorporated itself into a republic; Genoa is a city that cannot trust its families politically but has invented ways to trust them commercially. These are not descriptions, they are characterizations, and they earn their places.

**The question I would push on:**

The opening introduces three cities — Florence, Venice, Genoa — and gestures at three distinct commercial models. The chapter will need to sustain all three without losing the reader in the movement between them. The risk is that a chapter covering this much geographic and institutional ground collapses into a survey. What is the single narrative spine that runs from Berto in London to the chapter's close? The opening hints at it (the bill-of-exchange as the infrastructure of trust), but the notes need to lock it down before the chapter is written.

Berto's final image — "He does not find this beautiful. He finds it profitable. That is enough." — is the best line in the opening. Use it. Do not let the chapter drift from this register into admiration of the commercial revolution from the outside. Stay in Berto's counting-house worldview to the end.
