## Implicit Judgment Review

**Finding 1 — P3 (borderline)**
Section 5, paragraph: "The system that moved them operated by different physics than the system that had moved copper, and the broker who ran both through the same calculation was applying a logic that was correct for one kind of trade and different in ways the calculation alone could not measure."

Assessment: This sentence is the chapter's most philosophically ambitious moment. It does not judge the broker. It does not condemn the trade. It observes that the broker's commercial logic — which has been presented throughout the chapter as accurate and effective — is being applied to a system with different properties. The word "different" is doing careful work: not "wrong," not "inadequate," not "insufficient." Different.

The sentence does risk being read as implicit authorial commentary — a stepping-back from the broker's voice to make an observation the broker could not make about himself. This is the GATE-2 scorer's "authorial voice most visible" moment.

Recommendation: P3 — Author decides. The sentence is not a forbidden framing, and it does not project modern moral judgment backward. It describes a structural mismatch in the commercial logic, which is the chapter's honest accounting of what the era's terminal condition consisted of. One alternative: anchor the observation more firmly in the broker's own perception — "He would not have put it this way, but the road was carrying something the calculation could not fully read." This keeps the observation inside the broker's experience rather than above it. Either version is defensible.
