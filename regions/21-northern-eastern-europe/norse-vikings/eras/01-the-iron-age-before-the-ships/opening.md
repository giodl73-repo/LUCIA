# Before the Ships — The Iron Age

The smith worked in the dark.

The forge was a pit dug into the floor of the shed, lined with clay, and the clay had been fired so many times it had the color of old blood. The bellows were two leather bags worked by a boy who had been at it since before dawn. The boy's arms were shaking. The smith had noticed and had not said anything, because the shaking would pass or it would not, and speaking about it would change nothing.

The bloom lay on the anvil. It had come out of the furnace in the night, a lump of iron and slag the size of a man's two fists, grey-black, pocked, unpromising. The smith had smelted it himself over three days from bog ore gathered the previous autumn. He had built the shaft furnace of clay and stone, loaded it with charcoal and ore in layers, worked the draft until the fire glowed white at the base, then broken the furnace open, taken out the bloom, let it cool, and begun the real work, which was the beating.

The beating drove the slag out of the iron. Hammer-blow by hammer-blow. Fold. Hammer. Fold. Hammer. The slag came away in glassy flakes and in running tears when the iron was hot enough. What remained was a bar small enough to hold in one hand. From the bog, a hundred pounds of ore. From the ore, ten pounds of bloom. From the bloom, one pound of good iron. The smith thought in these ratios without needing to name them.

The iron would become a spearhead. A man had commissioned it. He had a farmstead and three sons, and the eldest had reached the age at which a father gives a weapon. He had come to the smith in the autumn with a measure of grain and the understanding that the spearhead would be ready before the next assembly at the thing. The smith had agreed. It would be a spearhead before the snow softened.

Beyond the shed, the farm slept. Six cows in the byre. A ewe that had lambed early and lost the lamb. The longhouse held eleven people under one turf roof, and the fire down the center had burned all night, because in this month the cold pressed through the door-skins before dawn, and a fire allowed to go out had to be rebuilt from flint and tinder in the dark.

Up the slope, above the treeline, a cairn stood. The smith's grandfather was buried there. The grave goods had gone in with him: a bronze pin from the south by routes no one remembered, a spearhead of the old style sharpened to half its length by a lifetime of whetting, a comb of antler, a pot with some of the funeral beer. The cairn held the grandfather. The cairn also held the land. A farm without a cairn was a farm without a claim. The dead were the argument for the living.

The smith did not look up the slope often. He knew it was there.

In the hall, the women were awake. One came across the yard with a bowl of porridge and set it on the bench inside the shed door and went back without speaking. The smith nodded at the bowl. He would eat when the iron allowed.

He turned the bar on the anvil. The iron had cooled. He put it back in the forge. The boy pushed the bellows. The coals brightened. The smith watched the color change through dull red and brighter red to the orange at which the iron could be worked, and he took it out and set it on the anvil and struck.

The hammer was not large. A man who worked iron all his life did not need a large hammer. He needed a hammer whose weight matched his arm exactly, and he needed to know where on the anvil the blow would land without thinking, because thinking was slower than the iron's cooling. The smith struck. The iron bent. He turned it. He struck again.

On the coast, three days' walk south, boats were drawn up above the tideline on rollers of peeled pine. They were flat-bottomed, broad, heavy. They moved under oars and sometimes under a square of sail that did not take the wind well. They did not go out of sight of land. The men who built those boats were not yet building the hull that would cross open water. The other hull was coming. A keel like a single tree. Strakes lapped and riveted. Ribs lashed with root so the frame could flex in swell. The shape was already in the hands of the boat-builders, working itself out across generations, pushed by the narrowness of the fjords and the thinness of the land and the count of sons.

The smith did not know about the boats. The boat-builders did not know about the smith. But the iron he was beating would become a rivet in a hull that had not yet been built, or a spearhead carried on a voyage that had not yet been imagined, and the land the cairns held was the land that, in a few generations, the water would reach past.

The bloom had become a bar. The bar was becoming a spearhead. The boy's arms had stopped shaking, or he had learned to hide the shaking. The fire held. The smith struck again.
