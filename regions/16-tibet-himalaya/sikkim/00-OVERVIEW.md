# Sikkim — Beyul Demojong — The Hidden-Land Kingdom

We are the Chogyal — the Dharma King — of Sikkim, the hidden-land kingdom that the great master Padmasambhava sealed in the eighth century for the benefit of future beings. Beyul Demojong: the hidden land of the valley of rice — this is what our country was called in the sacred texts that named it as a place of refuge to be opened at the appropriate time, when the world's difficulties would make its unique spiritual conditions available to those who sought them. When the Chogyal Phuntsog Namgyal was enthroned in 1642 with the blessing of three Tibetan lamas from different traditions, the hidden land was being opened. We were not just establishing a state; we were fulfilling a cosmic appointment.

Our kingdom sat at the eastern end of the Himalayan arc, between Tibet to the north, Nepal to the west, Bhutan to the east, and the British Indian territories of Darjeeling and the Bengal plains to the south. We were small in territory but large in symbolic significance: our capital Gangtok was connected by pilgrimage routes to the great Tibetan and Bhutanese sacred sites; our monasteries held traditions from multiple Tibetan Buddhist lineages; our Chogyal maintained the cosmic and temporal governance in a single person. When Sikkim ended in 1975 — absorbed into India as its twenty-second state — it was the last of the small Himalayan Buddhist kingdoms to survive the modern world.

## What Defined Them

**Beyul — the hidden land.** The concept of beyul — hidden lands sealed by Padmasambhava for future use, opened by tertons (treasure-discoverers) at the appointed time — is a specifically Nyingma Tibetan Buddhist teaching, and Sikkim was the first and most famous of the recognized beyuls. This gave the kingdom a cosmological significance beyond its political size: pilgrims traveled from Tibet, Bhutan, and Nepal to experience the sacred geography of a place that the tradition identified as uniquely blessed. The Chogyal governed a beyul; that was not a metaphor.

**The Chogyal as dharma king.** The institution of the Chogyal held the cosmic and temporal governance together, as in Tibet and Bhutan. The Chogyal was not merely a political ruler; he was the protector of the hidden land's sacred conditions and the guarantor of its dharmic character. The enthronement ritual, conducted with the blessing of multiple Tibetan Buddhist lineages, was the cosmic appointment through which the hidden land's governance was legitimized.

**Three peoples, one state.** Sikkim's population was always multi-ethnic: the Lepcha (the original inhabitants, who called themselves Rongkup, children of the rocks and snow), the Bhutia (Tibetan-descended people who formed the ruling class), and the Limbu and later Nepali communities. The kingdom managed this diversity through a formal agreement between the Chogyal and the Lepcha chief, which gave the Lepchas religious rights and land protections in exchange for their acknowledgment of Chogyal authority. This pluralism — fragile, contested, but real — was the hidden land's social expression.

**The Darjeeling cession and its consequences.** The cession of Darjeeling to the British East India Company in 1835 was presented as a gift but was in practice under duress. Darjeeling's transformation into a tea-growing colonial economy, and its rapid demographic change through the immigration of Nepali labor, eventually made it the base for the Indian political pressure that ended Sikkimese sovereignty. The 1975 merger was prepared by decades of demographic and political changes initiated by the Darjeeling cession a century and a half earlier.

**The last Chogyal.** Palden Thondup Namgyal, the twelfth and last Chogyal, was a charismatic and sophisticated ruler who married an American, Hope Cooke, in 1963. His attempt to modernize Sikkim while maintaining its traditional Buddhist character was undermined by Indian political intervention and demographic pressures. The 1975 referendum that merged Sikkim with India was conducted under Indian military presence. The Chogyal refused to accept the result. He died in 1982, still calling himself Chogyal, in exile in New York.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Triple Enthronement:** Three lamas from three different Tibetan Buddhist lineages arrive at the appointed place. They enthrone Phuntsog Namgyal as Chogyal. The hidden land is being opened. The ceremony takes three days.

**2 — Wars and Survival:** The Gurkha forces come from Nepal. The Bhutanese pressure comes from the east. The Chogyal retreats to Tibet, returns with Tibetan support, and fights his way back to his throne. Three times.

**3 — Darjeeling:** The British official presents the document. The cession of Darjeeling is called a gift. The Chogyal signs. He has preserved his kingdom by yielding the hill station at its edge. He does not know that the hill station will eventually grow into the instrument of the kingdom's absorption.

**4 — Great Game:** Sikkim's mountain passes are the subject of Russian-British-Chinese strategic calculation. The Chogyal's kingdom is too small to matter and too strategically placed to be ignored. The 1890 Anglo-Chinese Convention defines Sikkim's status without consulting the Chogyal.

**5 — The Referendum:** The Indian Army is in Gangtok. The ballot boxes are distributed. The Chogyal will not recognize the result. The count shows overwhelming support for merger. On April 26, 1975, Sikkim becomes India's twenty-second state. The last Chogyal goes into exile. The hidden land becomes an Indian hill district.
