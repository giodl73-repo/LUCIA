# La Corona d'Aragó — "The Crown of Aragon"

We began as a small mountain kingdom in the Pyrenean foothills, and we ended as a Mediterranean empire. The transformation came through union with the County of Barcelona — the House of Barcelona, which had been building commercial and political connections along the coast for two centuries. What the union created was something unusual in medieval Europe: a maritime empire built by a largely inland people, driven by a commercial aristocracy that thought in trade routes rather than in feudal hierarchies. Aragon proper — the old kingdom of the Pyrenean highlands — contributed warriors, legal traditions, and the characteristic Aragonese insistence on contractual governance. Catalonia contributed the sea, the merchants, and the financial instruments that turned military conquest into lasting empire.

The *Corts* — the Aragonese-Catalan parliament — is one of the constitutional experiments that deserves to be known as widely as Magna Carta. By the mid-thirteenth century the *Corts* had established the principle of *pactisme*: that the king governed under a compact with his subjects, that taxation required consent, and that the king who violated the compact could be resisted. The *Justicia* of Aragon, the great judicial officer, held the power to restrain the king himself in defense of Aragonese law and custom. This was not empty ceremony. Aragonese nobles used it, repeatedly, to check royal power. The constitutional vocabulary that would animate later European political thought — consent, compact, resistance — was being developed in the mountain kingdom of Aragon a century before it appeared in England.

## What Defined Them

**Pactisme — the covenant of governance.** The Aragonese constitutional tradition rested on a specific legal concept: that the king's authority derived from a pact with his subjects, not from divine grant alone. The famous formula attributed to Aragonese nobles — "We who are as good as you swear to you who are no better than us to accept you as our king and sovereign lord, provided you observe all our liberties and laws — and if not, not" — may be a later invention, but it captures a real political culture. The *Corts* could and did refuse taxation, negotiate legislation, and appeal to the *Justicia* against royal violations of Aragonese custom. This is constitutional monarchy before the term existed.

**The commercial empire.** Catalan merchants built a commercial network that stretched from the Levant to the North African coast by the thirteenth century. The *Consolat de Mar* — the consulate of the sea — was a commercial court that heard maritime disputes under the *Llibre del Consolat de Mar*, one of the earliest codifications of maritime commercial law. Barcelona's banking families financed the military campaigns that expanded the empire; the commercial intelligence networks that Catalan merchants maintained gave the Crown of Aragon diplomatic information that pure military power could not buy. The empire was a business.

**The island strategy.** James I of Aragon understood something that most medieval rulers did not: that islands in a commercial maritime network are more valuable than contiguous territory. Majorca (1229), then Sicily (1282), then Sardinia (1323) — the Aragonese built an island chain across the western Mediterranean that gave them the ability to tax, control, and disrupt the trade routes between Italy, North Africa, and the Atlantic. This is strategic thinking of a different order from the land-based frontier warfare of Castile. The Crown of Aragon was thinking about sea lanes before the age of sail.

**Valencia — the prized possession.** James I's conquest of Valencia from the Almohad Muslims in 1238 added the most fertile and commercially valuable territory in Iberia to the Aragonese crown. The Valencian kingdom retained its own laws, its own *Corts*, and its own language (Valencian, related to Catalan). Its population included large Moorish *moreria* communities who continued to farm the irrigated lands they had developed under Arab rule. Valencia's wealth — silk, rice, citrus, the great port trade — was the financial backbone of Aragonese Mediterranean ambition.

**The Catalan Company — freelance empire.** When Roger de Flor led the Catalan Company into Byzantine service in 1303 and then turned against their employers, carving out a state in Greece that lasted eighty years, they were acting on a characteristically Aragonese logic: if there is a power vacuum and a capable military force, fill it. The Duchy of Athens, administered from Barcelona for three generations, is the furthest geographical extension of the Aragonese-Catalan world — and also the clearest demonstration of what happens when commercial and military energy operates without adequate political direction.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Origins:** The marriage contract between Petronilla of Aragon and Ramón Berenguer IV of Barcelona, 1137, is signed when Petronilla is one year old. Her father, Ramiro the Monk, was briefly recalled from his monastery to produce an heir and then returned to his vocation. The Crown of Aragon begins in a union negotiated by a monk.

**2 — The Aragonese Reconquest:** James I stands before the walls of Valencia in 1238. He has been fighting for this city for nine years, since he was thirteen years old. Inside those walls are fifty thousand Muslims who will, under the surrender terms he is about to offer, remain on their land as his subjects. He needs them to stay.

**3 — The Hour of Compact Government:** The *Justicia* of Aragon receives a complaint against the king himself. He examines it, decides it has merit, and sends the royal chancellor a formal notice of restraint. The king is furious. The *Justicia* holds firm. This is either an act of extraordinary courage or the normal operation of a constitutional system, depending on who is telling the story.

**4 — The Aragonese Mediterranean:** Peter III of Aragon receives a secret message from the Sicilians: they are going to rise against the Angevins. Do the Aragonese want Sicily? Peter has no legal claim to the island. He has a fleet. He decides to sail anyway.

**5 — The Union and the End:** Ferdinand of Aragon marries Isabella of Castile and spends the next forty years trying to ensure that his kingdom's distinct institutions — its *Corts*, its *Justicia*, its Catalan commercial law — survive the union. He mostly succeeds. His grandson Charles V mostly undoes it.
