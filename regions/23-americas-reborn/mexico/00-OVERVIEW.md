# México — El País de la Raza Cósmica

México no se entiende desde afuera. Mexico does not make sense from the outside. It does not fit the categories that European political theory created for nations: it is neither simply European nor simply indigenous, neither colonial residue nor pre-Columbian survival, but something that was forged in the collision and has been arguing about its own nature ever since. The official ideology of the Mexican state — mestizaje, the celebration of racial and cultural mixture as the foundation of national identity — was articulated most grandly by the philosopher José Vasconcelos in his 1925 essay *La Raza Cósmica*: the cosmic race, born from the mixing of all the world's races on American soil, destined to synthesize what each had contributed and produce something greater than any of them alone. It was a beautiful and problematic idea. Beautiful because it acknowledged what no other Latin American nation would admit — that the African, the indigenous, and the European were all constitutive of the national identity. Problematic because it deployed the indigenous past as a founding myth while the living indigenous present remained dispossessed, marginalized, and largely outside the structures of political power.

Mexico City sits on Tenochtitlan. The great pyramid of Templo Mayor was discovered under the colonial city center in 1978 when workers laying electrical cables broke through its foundation. The Aztec capital is not a metaphor for the palimpsest of Mexican history; it is the physical ground on which Mexico City stands. The Nahua, Zapotec, Maya, Mixtec, Totonac, and dozens of other peoples who built the civilizations the Spanish conquered are not the ancestors of Mexico alone — they are also Mexico today, in the 68 indigenous languages still spoken, in the communities that continue to practice forms of governance and ceremony that predate the state, in the ongoing argument about what Mexico owes to the people it claimed as its founding mythology.

## What Defined Them

**La Virgen de Guadalupe as national identity.** The apparition of the Virgin Mary to the indigenous man Juan Diego in 1531 — presenting herself with brown skin, speaking Nahuatl, appearing on the hill sacred to the Aztec goddess Tonantzin — is the founding myth of Mexican Catholicism and one of the most effective examples of religious and cultural synthesis in the Americas. The image of Guadalupe is not merely religious; it is the national symbol that unites the mestizo and the indigenous, the colonial and the pre-Columbian. Hidalgo carried her banner at the beginning of the independence movement. She appears on political banners across the ideological spectrum. She is the one symbol that Mexico agrees on.

**La Revolución as the unfinished covenant.** The Mexican Revolution of 1910–1920 produced the Constitution of 1917 — one of the most progressive constitutional documents of its era: land reform (Article 27 guaranteed the ejido, communal land), labor rights (Article 123), the separation of church and state, and the claim that Mexico's natural resources belonged to the nation. The gap between the constitution's promise and its implementation has been the central theme of Mexican political life ever since. The Zapatista uprising of January 1, 1994 — timed to the day NAFTA took effect — began with the statement that the revolution was not over because the constitution had not been honored.

**El muerto y el vivo — the living dead.** The Mexican relationship with death — most visible in the Día de los Muertos (Day of the Dead) celebrations but present throughout the culture — reflects an indigenous understanding (primarily Nahua and Mixtec) that the dead remain present and relevant to the living. The family altar (ofrenda) with its photographs, marigolds, food offerings, and candles is not a morbid performance; it is a welcome-home, an insistence that the dead belong in the world of the living for at least this time each year. The great muralists — Rivera, Orozco, Siqueiros — made death a recurring subject. Octavio Paz argued in *The Labyrinth of Solitude* that Mexicans' intimacy with death is the key to their national character. The tradition is older than the argument about it.

**Lo indígena as foundation and fracture.** Mexican nationalism claims the pre-Columbian past as its heroic foundation — the Aztec eagle is on the flag; the Museo Nacional de Antropología in Mexico City houses the most magnificent collection of pre-Columbian objects in the world. But the living indigenous peoples of Mexico — 68 language groups, roughly 15% of the population — frequently find that the state's celebration of their ancestors is accompanied by indifference to their present. Land rights, bilingual education, constitutional recognition of indigenous governance systems — these have been demands of Mexican indigenous movements since at least the Revolution, and they remain only partially fulfilled. The fracture between the symbolic indigenous past and the living indigenous present is the deepest unresolved tension in Mexican national identity.

## The Chapters

**1 — La Colonia:** The city of Mexico-Tenochtitlan has been rebuilt over Aztec foundations. A Dominican friar named Sahagún is interviewing elderly Nahua informants, writing down in Nahuatl what they remember of the world before the conquest. The *Florentine Codex* that results will be one of the most important records of any pre-Columbian civilization — compiled by a Spaniard, preserved in Nahuatl, and largely ignored for three centuries.

**2 — El Grito:** September 16, 1810, before dawn: Father Miguel Hidalgo rings the bell of his church in Dolores. His speech — the Grito de Independencia — calls the people to rise. Within weeks, an army of indigenous and mestizo workers and peasants is marching toward the capital. The elite who will eventually win independence are not yet sure they want it.

**3 — El Siglo de la Anarquía:** A general named Santa Anna is president of Mexico for eleven separate terms. During his tenure, Mexico loses Texas, California, New Mexico, Arizona, Nevada, Utah, and parts of Colorado and Wyoming to the United States. The country that emerged from independence holding nearly half of North America now holds its southern third.

**4 — Zapata:** A revolutionary named Emiliano Zapata is fighting for Tierra y Libertad — land and liberty — in the state of Morelos. His manifesto, the Plan de Ayala, demands the return of land stolen from village communities. He will be assassinated in 1919. The village communities whose land he was defending are still waiting.

**5 — 1968:** October 2, 1968: ten days before the Mexico City Olympics, the Mexican army fires on a student demonstration at Tlatelolco. The official death toll is 25; the actual toll is disputed (probably in the hundreds). The PRI government suppresses all reporting. Mexico is hosting the world while killing its students. The event is the founding wound of the modern Mexican left.

**6 — El Presente:** January 1, 1994: the Zapatista National Liberation Army (EZLN) takes San Cristóbal de las Casas. Their communiqué: "Today we say ENOUGH." They are fighting for the constitutional rights that have been promised since 1917. Subcomandante Marcos is wearing a mask because, he says, he is no one — every indigenous person who has been invisible to the Mexican state.
