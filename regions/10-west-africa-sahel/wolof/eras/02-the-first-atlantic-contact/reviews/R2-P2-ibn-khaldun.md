# R2-P2 — Ibn Khaldun — Targeted Review of Notes

**Chapter:** Wolof / Era 2: The First Atlantic Contact
**Round:** 2 (Notes review, focused on structural gaps)

## What the Notes Get Right

The structural engine is correctly identified: the Atlantic trade creates a second axis that empowers the periphery at the expense of the center. The self-consuming logic is clear: the more successfully the confederacy's members trade with the Portuguese, the less they need the confederacy. This is a genuine structural mechanism, not a narrative convenience.

The section plan correctly places the horse economy (Section 2) as the material foundation for the political argument. Without understanding why horses mattered so critically, the axis shift is merely political. With it, the shift is material, ecological, and therefore inevitable.

## What Needs Strengthening

**The confederacy's structural weakness must be named.** The notes describe the Burba Jolof's authority as depending on mediation of the Saharan trade. But why was this authority so fragile? A paramount chief whose power rests on mediation rather than direct military control is structurally vulnerable to any bypass of his mediation. The Jolof Confederacy was a loose federation, not a centralized state. The buur of each state retained significant autonomy, their own armies, their own courts. The Burba Jolof could not compel the coastal buur to stop trading with the Portuguese because he did not have the institutional mechanisms of compulsion. This is the deep structural explanation for why the Atlantic trade was so destabilizing: the confederacy was held together by shared interest, not by centralized force, and when the interest diverged, nothing held.

**The ecological constraint needs quantification.** Section 2 mentions the tsetse fly and horse mortality, but the production bible should specify: in the Senegambian zone, horses typically survived 3-5 years before succumbing to trypanosomiasis. A buur's cavalry was not a permanent asset; it was a flow, a constant expenditure. The Portuguese horse trade was not just a new supply route; it was a new rate of supply for a commodity that was always being consumed. The exchange ratio (7-15 captives per horse) must be set against horse lifespan to reveal the true cost: a buur needed to trade captives continuously just to maintain his existing cavalry strength.

**Section 4 needs a mechanism for the Burba Jolof's failure to adapt.** The notes ask the right question ("was it structural, institutional, or personal?") but do not propose an answer. The answer is geographic and institutional: the Burba Jolof's inland seat was too far from the coast to control the Atlantic trade directly, and the confederacy's loose structure gave him no institutional tool to prevent the coastal buur from trading independently. He could fight (and eventually did), but he could not regulate.

## The Question I'd Push On in the Full Draft

What happened to the Saharan trade? The chapter tracks the Atlantic trade's rise but should also track the Saharan trade's relative decline (or persistence). Did the Saharan merchants notice the competition? Did the terms of the north-south trade change as the east-west axis grew? The two trade systems coexisted for decades. The chapter should show this coexistence and the moment when the Atlantic axis became dominant. This is the structural fulcrum: the point at which the Burba Jolof's mediation of the Saharan trade was no longer sufficient to hold the confederacy together.
