# Figures — When Kingship Came Down from Heaven (Era 1)

## Gilgamesh (c. 2700 BCE?)

En (lord/high priest) of Unug. Two-thirds divine, one-third mortal. Builder of Unug's walls. Protagonist of the oldest extended narrative in the world.

**What we know:** Gilgamesh is attested in the Sumerian King List (as the fifth ruler of the First Dynasty of Uruk), in the Tummal inscription (which describes his building activity at Nippur), and possibly in brick stamps from Uruk. The literary Gilgamesh appears in five independent Sumerian poems (dating to the Ur III and Old Babylonian periods) and in the Standard Babylonian Epic of Gilgamesh (compiled by Sin-leqi-unninni, probably in the late second millennium BCE, with the fullest copy from Nineveh). The archaeological record at Warka confirms a massive fortification wall consistent with the period.

**What we cannot know:** Whether the literary Gilgamesh corresponds to a single historical individual. The Sumerian King List assigns him a reign of 126 years, which places him in the formulaic rather than the historical portion of the list. The relationship between the historical en of Unug and the literary hero who seeks immortality is not recoverable. The chapter presents both the historical attestation and the literary character without collapsing them into one.

**Sources:** George, *The Babylonian Gilgamesh Epic* (2003); George, *The Epic of Gilgamesh: A New Translation* (1999); the Sumerian Gilgamesh poems (ETCSL); the Sumerian King List (multiple editions).

## The Scribe at Unug

The chapter's opening figure. Not a named individual. A type: the temple scribe who produced the earliest administrative tablets.

**What we know:** The archaic Uruk tablets (c. 3200 BCE) were produced by scribes working in the Eanna temple precinct. The tablets are small, palm-sized, and record receipts and disbursements of agricultural products, primarily barley. The scribes used cut reeds to make impressions in wet clay. The specific "forty-two units of barley" is illustrative, not drawn from a single identified tablet, but the type of transaction is well attested in the archaic corpus.

**What we cannot know:** No individual scribe from the archaic Uruk period is known by name. The earliest named scribes appear centuries later. The scribe in the chapter is a type, not a composite: he represents the scribal profession as attested by the tablets themselves, not an individual constructed from multiple sources.

**Honest limit:** The chapter uses the scribe as a narrative anchor for the invention of writing. He is rendered through the material evidence (the tablets, the reeds, the clay, the archive rooms) rather than through invented biographical detail.

**Sources:** Nissen, Damerow, and Englund, *Archaic Bookkeeping* (1993); Schmandt-Besserat, *Before Writing* (1992); the archaic Uruk tablet corpus (Englund and Nissen, *Die Lexikalischen Listen der Archaischen Texte aus Uruk*, 1993).

## Inanna

Goddess of Unug. Deity of love, war, and the morning and evening star. Not a human figure, but the divine presence that organizes the chapter's theological world.

**What we know:** Inanna is one of the most extensively attested deities in the Sumerian corpus. Her temple precinct at Uruk (Eanna) is among the oldest monumental temple complexes. The narrative of her theft of the me from Enki (*Inanna and Enki*) is preserved in multiple copies. Her hymns, including those attributed to Enheduanna, are among the earliest named-author literary works.

**Sources:** Jacobsen, *The Treasures of Darkness* (1976); Black et al., *The Literature of Ancient Sumer* (2004); the ETCSL corpus (Inanna texts).

## Ningal

Goddess of Urim. Wife of Nanna (the moon god). The voice of the Lament for the Destruction of Ur.

**What we know:** Ningal's lament for Ur is preserved in the text known as the Lamentation over the Destruction of Ur (Michalowski, 1989). Her role as petitioner before Enlil and as witness to the city's destruction is the lament's dramatic center. She is attested in the Ur III administrative corpus and in later literary texts.

**What we cannot know:** Ningal is a deity, not a historical person. The chapter presents her as the Sumerians understood her: a real presence whose grief was the proper theological response to a city's destruction.

**Sources:** Michalowski, *The Lamentation over the Destruction of Sumer and Ur* (1989); Jacobsen, *The Treasures of Darkness* (1976).

## A Note on Sources and Figures

This chapter covers the longest chronological span in the Sumer sequence (c. 4500-2004 BCE). Named historical individuals are extremely rare before the Early Dynastic period (c. 2900 BCE). The chapter addresses this by anchoring the narrative in the material evidence (tablets, temples, cities) and in the literary tradition (Gilgamesh, the laments), which together provide a richly attested world even when individual biographies are absent.

No composite figures are used. The scribe at Unug is a type, not a composite: he represents the profession as attested by thousands of surviving tablets, not an individual assembled from multiple sources. Gilgamesh is presented as both a possibly historical figure (King List, Tummal inscription) and a literary character (the epic), with the distinction acknowledged. Inanna and Ningal are divine figures presented on the culture's own terms.

The primary tension in the evidence: the archaic Uruk tablets (the earliest writing) are poorly understood, with many signs still undeciphered. Claims about the content and purpose of the earliest tablets rely heavily on the work of Nissen, Englund, and Schmandt-Besserat, whose reconstructions are widely accepted but not unanimously. The chapter follows the dominant scholarly narrative while acknowledging uncertainty where it exists.
