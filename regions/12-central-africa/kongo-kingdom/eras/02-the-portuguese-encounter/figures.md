# Figures — Kongo Kingdom / Era 2: The Portuguese Encounter

**Era:** c. 1482–1543 CE
**Stage:** WRITE (Stage 7)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Afonso I (Mvemba a Nzinga, c. 1465–1543)

He is the chapter's only named protagonist, and the record allows us to go further into him than most figures in this project — not because the record is rich in the ways that domestic detail is rich, but because his own words survive. The letters are primary Tier 1 sources. They are Afonso's voice, shaped by his secretaries' Latin but built from his arguments, his grievances, his specific knowledge of what was happening in his kingdom. When the chapter quotes him, it is quoting him.

What we know: He was born around 1465, the son of Manikongo João I (Nzinga a Ntinu Wene). He was baptized as a young man — the date is uncertain, likely in the early 1490s after the Portuguese missionaries arrived. He fought a succession war against his brother Mpanzu a Kitima around 1509 and won, attributing his victory to the intervention of Saint James, who his soldiers claimed to have seen fighting on the Christian side. He reigned until approximately 1543.

What we know from the letters: He was precise. His prose — even filtered through secretarial Latin — is argumentative rather than rhetorical, building from premise to conclusion. He cites specific instances of seizure. He names categories of person who should not be enslaved. He references the prior correspondence with the Portuguese crown. He is not appealing to emotion; he is building a case. This is the mind that comes through in the archive: not a petitioner, but a litigator.

What we cannot know: His private response to the inadequacy of the replies. His feelings when his son Henrique died. Whether he believed, in his last decade, that the letters were still doing anything. The record leaves us on the outside of these questions. What we can do is note the fact that he kept writing — in 1529, in 1533, in 1540 — and let the persistence of the act speak for the person who performed it.

**A complexity the chapter carries:** Earlier in his reign, Afonso traded captives himself — prisoners from the wars through which he consolidated his kingdom's authority. He was not opposed to the institution of captive trade as such. What he opposed, with increasing anguish, was the trade's expansion to include free Kongo subjects — his own people, outside the category of legitimate war captives. This distinction matters for the chapter's honesty. Afonso's argument was not a universal abolition argument. It was a sovereignty argument: these people should not be taken because they are mine, because they are free subjects of a Christian kingdom that the Portuguese have recognized. The argument was correct and important. It was also specific to its own frame. The chapter holds both.

## Dom Henrique (c. 1495–c. 1531)

Afonso's son, the bishop. He is in the archive mostly as a fact: educated in Portugal and Rome, consecrated by Pope Leo X in 1518, returned to Kongo, died young. The letters do not tell us much about his personality. What they tell us is what his existence meant to his father's project: proof that the Kongo kingdom could produce, from within itself, a bishop capable of corresponding with Rome as an equal.

He was sent to Portugal at approximately eight years old. This means he grew up in Lisbon, not in Mbanza Kongo. He learned Portuguese before he learned to navigate the Kongo court. He learned Latin before he learned the language of the nkisi. What the Kongo court made of him when he returned — a man shaped by Europe, holding a European title, having spent most of his life on the other side of the Atlantic — the record does not say. What it says is that he took his place at his father's court and that when he died, around 1531, Afonso did not find a replacement.

There was no second bishop from Kongo for a very long time.

## The Secretary (composite, unnamed)

The person who held the quill is not named in the archive. The letters identify themselves as coming from Afonso I, Manikongo; the handwriting is that of a trained scribe. What we know about this figure: educated in Lisbon (the Latin is correct; the conventions of diplomatic correspondence are observed); working at the Kongo court (the letters know the internal political situation with specificity); one of the Kongolese nobles' sons who were sent for European education as part of Afonso's diplomatic investment.

The secretary is a composite figure drawn from the known fact that multiple secretaries served Afonso over a reign of thirty-plus years. The figure who drafted the 1526 letter is not the same person who drafted the 1540 letter. What they share is the training, the vocation, and the position: the translator between the Manikongo's Kongo-language reasoning and the Latin that would reach Lisbon.

What the secretary's existence tells us: the Kongo investment in European education was not superficial. Training a secretary capable of producing correct diplomatic Latin required years of education in Portugal, at cost to the kingdom, with no guarantee the person would survive the trip. The letters in the archive are the visible product of this investment. The secretary is as much the chapter's figure as Afonso is; the argument would not have traveled without the hand that formed it.

## Dom João III of Portugal (1502–1557)

The recipient of the letters. He is present in this chapter not as a villain but as a structural actor — a king operating within a system whose logic overwhelmed his individual will, even if he had the will to enforce the covenant Afonso was invoking.

He replied to Afonso's letters. He issued regulations. In 1529 he sent a mission to the Kongo with formal instructions to limit trading abuses. He was not indifferent; his replies acknowledge the substance of Afonso's complaints. But the acknowledgment was not enforcement, and the enforcement could not have been what Afonso needed it to be: the Portuguese Crown's authority did not extend into the Kongo interior, and the interior was where the trade operated.

João III's structural position: the slave trade was profitable for enough actors within the Portuguese system — traders, merchants, the Crown itself through taxation — that effective enforcement would have required overriding a network of financial interests that extended far beyond the Kongo coast. He could express concern. He could issue regulations for the behavior of Portuguese nationals at the Kongo trading posts. He could not stop the internal Kongo political competition that was driving the trade's expansion inland, and he was not in a position to enforce the theological covenant Afonso was invoking in terms that would have actually constrained the commercial system.

This is not an exoneration. It is a structural account. The chapter's No Verdicts discipline applies here: Dom João is shown as he was, making the decisions that the structure of his position made available to him, without the chapter stepping outside to condemn him.

## Source Notes

The primary source for all figures in this chapter is Afonso I's letters, preserved in the ANTT and published in António Brásio's *Monumenta Missionaria Africana*, vol. I (1952). John Thornton's translations and analyses — in *The Kingdom of Kongo* (1983) and *Africa and Africans in the Making of the Atlantic World* (1998) — are the authoritative modern rendering. Anne Hilton's *The Kingdom of Kongo* (1985) provides essential political context for all figures. Cécile Fromont's *The Art of Conversion* (2014) is primary for the cosmogram-crucifix connection and the visual culture of the Kongo Christian project.

Dates for Afonso and Henrique are approximate and disputed in the scholarly literature. The chapter uses Thornton's preferred estimates without claiming certainty.
