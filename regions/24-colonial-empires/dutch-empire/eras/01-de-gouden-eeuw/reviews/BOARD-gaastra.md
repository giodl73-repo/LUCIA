## Institutional and Financial Accuracy

The chapter's institutional description is accurate. The Heeren XVII composition (Amsterdam eight seats, Zeeland four, Rotterdam/Delft/Hoorn/Enkhuizen one each, seventeenth rotating) matches the charter structure. The subscription figures (6.45 million guilders, six weeks, 1,143 shareholders) are consistent with the Nationaal Archief records as documented in my 2003 monograph.

The 18% average annual dividend figure requires slight precision. The 18% figure is the commonly cited average, but it masks significant variation: the early decades (1610–1640) delivered lower averages as the company's capital base was still being established and the monopoly was not yet secure; the mid-century decades (1640–1680) delivered higher averages as the spice monopolies were consolidated. The chapter uses the figure correctly as an 18% average over the 200-year history of the company — but the full chapter should note in a single sentence that the figure was not uniform across the period. Recommendation: add one phrase, e.g., "averaging 18% annually over the company's full history, with the highest returns concentrated in the decades when the spice monopolies were most secure."

**On Pieter Bas:** The subscription records at the Nationaal Archief document small investors including tradespeople and artisans, though I cannot confirm the specific name "Pieter Bas, baker of Kalverstraat" without a specific archival citation. If this name is drawn from a specific source (Israel 1989, or the founding subscription ledger itself), that source should be cited in notes.md. If it is a representative composite, it should be disclosed in figures.md. The chapter's figures.md treatment ("his name is real; his address is real; his investment is real") implies a specific archival source; that source should be named.

**On the Amsterdam Exchange Bank (1609):** The chapter correctly identifies the bank's relationship to VOC capital needs. One minor addition: the Amsterdam Exchange Bank also enabled the VOC's bill-of-exchange system for paying Asian factors, which is part of the distance mechanism the chapter traces. This is a small detail but consistent with the chapter's structural argument.

**Overall institutional accuracy:** High. No material errors in the governance description. Minor additions recommended on dividend figure precision and Pieter Bas source citation.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

The chapter renders the Dutch commercial worldview of 1602–1672 accurately. The combination of Calvinist moral vocabulary and commercial purpose, the accountability to the dividend rather than the crown, the treatment of the distance mechanism as an institutional feature rather than a moral failure — all of this is consistent with how Dutch commercial culture of the period understood itself.

**One authenticity note:** The Synod of Dort reference in the chapter places the Synod in 1618–1619 and suggests it ran simultaneously with the Banda campaign preparations. This is chronologically correct. However, the chapter implies a connection between Calvinist moral theology and the eventual Multatuli critique that may be slightly overdrawn for Era 1. The capacity for self-critique within Dutch Reformed culture was real but took two centuries to produce *Max Havelaar*; the suggestion that the connection was visible from 1619 is a reasonable analytical observation, but it should be framed as retrospective rather than as contemporary awareness.

**Board verdict:** PASS with minor revision recommendations. No factual errors requiring correction. Two minor additions recommended (dividend precision, Pieter Bas source citation).
