## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Review of The Age of Equals (Opening)

**Overall:** I can see the gold, and I can see the horses. But I cannot yet feel the heat of the Khabur plain, or hear the sound of the messenger entering the court, or smell the stables. The opening is architecturally strong but needs more body.

**What works:**
- The gold inventory is physical. Earrings, armlets, a necklace in papyrus-flower links, bolts of fine linen. These are objects I can hold. The specificity grounds the diplomatic abstraction in material reality.
- The horses in the courtyard at the end are excellent: "worked through morning exercises, moving in precise circles, counting the intervals between exertion and rest." I can see this. The motion is specific.
- The journey of the gifts across the Sinai, by donkey and then ox cart, past Megiddo and Qadesh, is a good geographical grounding. The distance becomes physical.

**What doesn't:**
- Where is the temperature? The Khabur triangle in the diplomatic season would be warm but not Egyptian-hot, the steppe wind carrying the smell of dry grass and irrigation channels. I need to feel the air.
- The throne room is described (mud brick, painted walls, blue and red, geometric patterns) but not inhabited. What does it sound like when the messenger delivers the tablet? Is the room silent? Does the court wait? Are there attendants, guards, the sound of horses from the courtyard filtering through the walls?
- Tushratta himself is disembodied. He "considers" the request. Where is he sitting? What does he look like? What is he wearing? The Nuzi archives describe the dress and ornament of the *maryannu* class. Give the king a body.
- The messenger from Egypt is an abstraction. This is a man who has traveled for weeks through the Levantine corridor. He has dust on him. He is tired, or formal, or both. He carries a tablet that will change the relationship between two courts. Let us see him.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening is a mechanism beautifully described. But a mechanism needs a room, and the room needs air, and the air needs a temperature. Before the chapter is written, the sensory architecture of the Mitanni court needs to be built: what does Washukanni feel like, sound like, smell like? The argument will be stronger for having a body to live in.
