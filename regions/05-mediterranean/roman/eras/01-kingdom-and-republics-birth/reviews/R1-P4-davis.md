## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of Roman Era 1 Opening

**Overall:** The opening grounds Roma in its landscape, which is essential. The river, the hills, the salt, the malarial lowlands. This is a city that exists because of where it sits. But I want more of the lived world, more of the body in the place.

**What works:**
- The Forum as "a drained marsh between hills, the ground still soft in places." This is the kind of detail that makes a reader understand Roma was BUILT, not discovered.
- The salt road. Salt is the most underappreciated commodity in ancient history. That it gets named and given a road is correct.
- "River mud and woodsmoke and the faint sourness of the hides curing." Three sensory details that place you at the Tiber in the sixth century BCE.

**What doesn't:**
- I want to smell the Forum during Brutus's funeral. What does a sixth-century-BCE funeral procession smell like? Incense? Woodsmoke? The sweat of a crowd in wool? The sensory grounding is strong for the landscape but thin for the human event.
- The religious dimension is underplayed. The Romani saw their city as existing within a web of divine relationships. The auspices, the augural practice, the templum as a defined sacred space in the sky. The opening mentions the auspices once, in passing. For a Roman, the auspices were not passing. They were the mechanism by which the gods communicated approval for every public act.

**The question I'd push on:**
What did the Romani hear? The Forum was an acoustic space before it was a visual one. The voice of the herald, the crack of the lictor's fasces on the ground, the sound of the crowd at an assembly. Roma was a city of public speech. The voice should carry the acoustic world.
