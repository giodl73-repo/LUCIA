# R2-P1 — Barbara Tuchman | Panel Review, Round 2

**Chapter:** Holy Roman Empire, Era 2 — Der Investiturstreit
**Reviewer:** Barbara Tuchman (P-1)
**Stage:** PANEL-2 (notes review — targeted)

---

The notes address my concern. Benno II of Osnabrück is the right figure for the bishop caught in the middle — a man who navigated the controversy by trying to remain a bishop rather than a partisan, and who was therefore accused by both sides. He needs a scene. The notes promise one but don't sketch it yet.

My concern for the chapter: the five-section structure is front-loaded with exposition (the ceremony, the documents) before reaching the human scenes. Section 1 (the ceremony) and Section 2 (the *Dictatus Papae*) carry the analytical weight; Sections 3 and 4 carry the human weight. The chapter should find a way to load the human element earlier — perhaps beginning Section 1 not with the ceremony in abstract but with a specific ceremony, one specific bishop, one specific moment of investment.

The Ariald figure is strong for Section 1 — he is watching a ceremony in Milan and seeing something different from what the emperor sees. Start Section 1 with his eyes, not with the general description of the ceremony. Let the analysis emerge from his specific perception.

The five-section compression works for pacing, but the notes correctly flag that the intervening forty years between Canossa (1077) and Worms (1122) need to be handled with care. Don't skip too fast. The anti-pope problem is important: when Gregory excommunicated Heinrich again and Heinrich responded by installing Clement III as antipope, this created a genuine schism — two popes simultaneously, each excommunicating the other's adherents. The German faithful faced a real question: which sacraments were valid? Which priests could you confess to? This ground-level confusion is the chapter's most under-explored territory and deserves a paragraph.

The landing — Gregory's last words — is right. "I have loved righteousness and hated iniquity; therefore I die in exile." That is the landing. Don't approach it; arrive at it. The last section should move through the consequences quickly and let the death scene breathe.

*Structure looks sound. Front-load the human element in Section 1 by starting with a specific ceremony through specific eyes (Ariald). Let the Gregory death scene be the chapter's final image.*
