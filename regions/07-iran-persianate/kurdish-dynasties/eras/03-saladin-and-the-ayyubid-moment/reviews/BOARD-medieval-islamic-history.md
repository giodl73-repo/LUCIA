# BOARD — Medieval Islamic History
## Kurdish Dynasties Era 3: Saladin and the Ayyubid Moment
## Board Reviewer: Medieval Islamic History / Crusader Studies

**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

### Scope

This review focuses on factual accuracy of the military, political, and administrative claims; accurate use of primary sources (Baha ad-Din, Imad al-Din, Ibn al-Athir); accuracy of dates and events; and cultural representation of the Ayyubid period.

---

### Factual Assessment

**Hattin (July 4, 1187):** The tactical description is accurate. The water denial strategy, the fire (grass burning, smoke), the location of the Crusader host at Saffuriyya prior to the march — all consistent with Baha ad-Din, Ibn al-Athir, and the Crusader sources (Ernoul, the Itinerarium). The claim that Salah al-Din's scouts monitored wind direction is speculative but plausible and consistent with his documented care for terrain. Flag as educated inference rather than documented fact — but does not require revision.

**The ransom terms at al-Quds (October 1187):** The 10/5/1 dinar ransom rates are documented in both Muslim and Crusader sources. The patriarch Heraclius's personal ransom is confirmed. The release of poorer ransomed prisoners is reported by Imad al-Din. Accurate.

**Shirkuh's death (January 1169):** The sources are ambiguous — "of a fever or of excess" is an honest representation. Some sources give overeating; the medical cause is unverified. The chapter's handling is correct.

**The Fatimid caliphate's end (1171):** The chapter states the caliph died and Salah al-Din did not appoint a successor. Accurate — the last Fatimid caliph al-Adid died September 1171, and Salah al-Din had already shifted the Friday sermon to the Abbasid caliph's name before his death. The "empty chair" image is poetically accurate.

**Arsuf (September 7, 1191):** The description of the Hospitaller countercharge without orders, followed by Richard's full commitment, is consistent with the Itinerarium Regis Ricardi and Ambroise's Estoire de la Guerre Sainte. Accurate.

**Richard's gesture at Beit Nuba:** The covering of the face with a shield is recorded in the Itinerarium. Some historians regard it as legendary; others accept it as consistent with Richard's character in the sources. The chapter's presentation ("The sources record this gesture") is appropriately hedged.

**Treaty of Jaffa terms:** Accurate. The coastal strip from Tyre to Jaffa; Muslim political control of Jerusalem; Christian pilgrim access; the three-year truce. This is well-established.

**Salah al-Din's deathbed (March 1193):** The 47 dirhams and single gold piece figure comes from Baha ad-Din. Confirmed. The Quran passage and the dying word "True" (Sadaqta, "You spoke truth") are in Baha ad-Din. Accurate.

**Al-Adil's reunification:** The timeline given (Egypt by 1202, broader coordination by 1210) is broadly accurate. Al-Adil was confirmed sultan of Egypt in 1200 and extended his control over Syria through the following decade.

**Ain Jalut (September 3, 1260):** Correctly identified as the Mamluk victory over the Mongols. The framing of the Mamluks as "barrack-brothers" with manufactured asabiyyah is a historiographical interpretation consistent with modern scholarship on the Mamluk system (David Ayalon's work). Defensible.

---

### Kurdish Identity Representation

The chapter's core claim — that Salah al-Din's formation was Kurdish in a specific way that shaped his political method — is a legitimate historical interpretation that has scholarly support (Lyons and Jackson, Humphreys). It is not the only interpretation (the Islamic solidarity frame is equally valid and more commonly emphasized), and the chapter acknowledges both without reducing either.

The Rawadiyya clan identification is consistent with the sources. The Dvin highlands connection for the Ayyubid family is documented.

One addition I would welcome (not a required revision): the chapter could note that Salah al-Din spoke Kurdish as a first language, likely alongside Arabic, and that his inner circle included Kurdish-speakers. This would make the Kurdish identity more linguistic and embodied, not just genealogical and strategic.

---

### Source Use

The use of Baha ad-Din as primary source, with acknowledgment that his frame is Islamic and his limitations as a Kurdish source, is exemplary source-asymmetry handling. Ibn al-Athir is referenced implicitly (the "hostile Mosul perspective") but not named. Naming him would strengthen the source section.

---

### Conclusion

Historically accurate. Kurdish representation is defensible and well-argued. One suggested addition (language), one suggested named citation (Ibn al-Athir). Neither requires revision to the chapter text; both can be addressed in SOURCES.md and noted here.

**Board approved.**
