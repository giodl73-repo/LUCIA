# BOARD REVIEW — Enid Schildkrout
## Mangbetu — Era 2: The Expansion Under Tuba

**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

The chapter's treatment of the material culture is accurate within the limits of what can be verified from our work and from Schweinfurth's account.

**On the coiffure:**

The description of the coiffure process — palm-fiber frame, ivory pins, graduated layering on the elongated skull — is consistent with the documented practice. The chapter's description of the skull's grain behaving differently depending on the binding is a reasonable inference from the known anatomy, though we did not document this at that level of specificity. The basic structure of the practice is accurate.

The chapter correctly identifies head-binding as an elite practice — a mark of the Mangbetu aristocracy specifically, not of the general population. This distinction is important and the chapter maintains it throughout.

**On the pluriarc:**

The eight-string configuration, the gourd resonator, the basic playing technique (right hand for melody, left hand for stability and resonance feedback) — consistent with the documented instruments in museum collections and with Schweinfurth's descriptions. The claim that students spent two years on foundational technique before moving to connected phrases is not specifically documented; it is an inference. The chapter does not present this as documented fact but as characteristic of a transmission system of the complexity required. Acceptable.

**On the architecture:**

"Two hundred paces in length" for the audience hall reflects Schweinfurth's measurements of Mbunza's capital, appropriately attributed to his account. The chapter correctly treats this as evidence of what the tradition had produced by the 1870s, not as direct documentation of Tuba's buildings. The inference that Tuba's court would have had similar architectural ambition is reasonable given the continuity of the tradition.

**One correction required:**

The chapter describes the palace as having "interior hung with woven palm-leaf panels that an explorer who arrived two decades later would describe as tapestry." Our work documents the interior palm-leaf decoration extensively, including photographs from later explorers. The comparison to "tapestry" is Schweinfurth's (and it is a useful cross-cultural analogy that the chapter handles appropriately — attributed to him implicitly, not presented as fact). This is fine.

However: the chapter should confirm that the "woven palm-leaf panels" described are specifically what Schildkrout & Keim document as *mbala* (the decorative palm-leaf work on interior surfaces). The term is not in the chapter — for a general audience this may not matter, but for accuracy, the specific material tradition should be noted in figures.md or notes.md.

**Verdict:** Historically accurate within the appropriate limits of the source record. One minor addition recommended (mbala reference in notes/figures). No factual errors that require correction.
