# Sápmi — "The Sami People"

The reindeer migration is our calendar, our economy, and the map of our world. The reindeer know where to go, and we follow — north to the high fells in summer, where the insects cannot reach the herds and the grass is rich, south to the sheltered forests in winter, where the lichen waits under the snow. This movement is not wandering. It is knowledge encoded in generations of observation: which valley opens early, which pass is safe in which conditions, which river crossing requires what season. The people who understand this knowledge are wealthy. The people who do not are dead.

We call ourselves Sami and our land Sápmi — a territory that spans what the maps of southern kingdoms divide into Norway, Sweden, Finland, and the Russian Kola Peninsula. These borders are recent impositions on an older reality. The Sami world follows the reindeer, not the kingdom, and the reindeer do not read maps. Our relationship with the land is not ownership — no one owns the fell or the forest. It is custodianship, earned through generations of presence and competence. The *noaidi* — the Sami spiritual practitioner — holds the relationship between the human community and the beings of the other world, reading the drum to find the lost reindeer, to heal the sick, to negotiate with the powers that control what moves.

Key places and concepts:
- **Sieidi** — sacred sites; rock formations, unusual trees, prominent stones — places where offerings were left and contact with the other world was possible
- **Noaidi's drum (goavddis/runebomme)** — the instrument of Sami spiritual practice; the image map of the cosmos
- **Seita** — the sacred stone or wooden figure; the material presence of the powers
- **The siida** — the fundamental social unit; a group of families sharing seasonal grazing territory

## What Defined Them

**The reindeer as the organizing principle.** Sami society was not organized primarily around agriculture, trade, or military power — it was organized around the reindeer. The shift from hunting wild reindeer to herding semi-domesticated ones (a process completed over several centuries, roughly between 1000 and 1600 CE) reorganized the entire social structure: the *siida* (the cooperative herding group) became the fundamental unit, the seasonal migration route became the defining property, and expertise in reindeer behavior — reading the signs of health, predicting the migration, managing the calving — became the most valued knowledge. A Sami family's wealth was measured in reindeer.

**The noaidi and the drum.** The *noaidi* — the Sami ritual practitioner — operated through the *goavddis* (runebomme, the drum), an instrument decorated with images that formed a map of the Sami cosmos: the upper world, the middle world, and the underworld, inhabited by beings with whom the noaidi could communicate. The noaidi's drum was not merely a musical instrument; it was a navigational tool for spiritual space. By drumming and entering a trance state, the noaidi could send part of his consciousness into the other world to find lost animals, diagnose illness, negotiate with the powers that controlled weather and game, and locate enemies. The Christian missionaries who collected and burned Sami drums understood them correctly as the material center of Sami religious practice.

**The siida — governance without hierarchy.** The *siida* was the fundamental Sami social unit: a group of families, usually related, who cooperated in reindeer herding, fishing, and hunting in a shared territory. It had no permanent leadership structure — decisions were made by consensus among the adult members. The siida was both an economic cooperative and a social safety net: siida members shared resources in difficult seasons, helped each other with herding tasks that required many hands, and defended their collective territory. This non-hierarchical governance system was highly effective for the ecological conditions it operated in and profoundly puzzling to the southern kingdoms that tried to administer it.

**The tribute system and Sami autonomy.** The Sami were not simply the subjects of adjacent kingdoms — they were the objects of multiple tribute claims, which paradoxically gave them a degree of leverage. Norwegian kings, Swedish kings, and the Novgorodian state all claimed tribute from the same Sami communities in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries. The Sami could, and did, play these claims against each other: by paying tribute to multiple parties, they maintained relationships with all of them and could seek protection from one against the extortions of another. The Sami were not passive victims of colonialism; they were experienced negotiators who used their geographical position between multiple powers skillfully.

**The technology of the north.** Sami material culture developed specific technological solutions to the extreme environment: the *pulk* (a boat-shaped sledge pulled by a single reindeer), the *lavvu* (the portable conical tent, ancestor of the tipi, designed to resist Arctic winds), the *komse* (the birch-bark baby carrier that kept infants safe through the migration), and the ski — the Sami ski tradition, going back thousands of years, was the most sophisticated in the world. These technologies were not curiosities; they were the reason people could live above the Arctic Circle. The Norse who encountered the Sami understood this: they borrowed the ski, borrowed the sledge, and paid tribute to the knowledge embedded in Sami material culture.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Beginning Time:** A hunter on the post-glacial Scandinavian tundra, twelve thousand years ago, follows a reindeer trail north. The glacier has retreated within living memory. The land is new — not yet forested, not yet mapped by human practice. The hunter is learning what the land requires. This learning will not stop.

**2 — The Siida Age:** A siida meets in the spring to decide the calving route. The eldest woman in the group has made this journey forty times. She knows which valley floods early and which offers the first green grass. Her knowledge is the group's knowledge. There is no title for what she holds; it does not need a title.

**3 — The Tribute Age:** A Norwegian tax collector arrives at the Sami winter camp demanding the *finneskatten* — the Sami tax in furs and dried fish. A Swedish agent arrived last month demanding the same thing. The Sami elder explains, politely, that both have already been paid. This is not entirely true. It is strategically true.

**4 — The Age of the Noaidi:** A noaidi enters the trance state — the drumming has gone on for an hour; his breath has slowed; his eyes have gone distant. He is looking for the reindeer that the herd has lost. Three families' winter depends on finding them. He sees them, in the place that is not quite this world, in a valley two days' travel north. When he returns, he will tell the hunters where to go. They will find the reindeer. The knowledge held in the drum is real.

**5 — The Christianizing Pressure:** A Swedish Lutheran minister arrives in the Sami winter camp with an interpreter and a mandate to collect the drums. He has been told that the drums are instruments of the devil. He collects eleven drums and burns them. The noaidi watches the drums burn. He does not speak. Some of what the drums held was in the drums. Some of it was never in the drums at all.
