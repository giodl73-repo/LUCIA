# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Panel Round 1 — Opening Review

**Era:** Teke / The Kongo-Teke Interface
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

I can feel the plateau.

This is not a small thing. Central African plateau savanna has a specific weight to it — the laterite road hard underfoot, the grass yellowed at the edges of the dry season, the smell of the forest edge distinct from the open plateau, the distance sound travels differently at altitude than in the basin. The opening gives me the red laterite road, the green-dark forest smell, the clay pots of water at the road's edge. These are real things. I believe I am there.

The moment that places me most completely: the trader's carriers "strung out behind him with the loads wrapped in leaves and bound with vine." This is the forest interior's packaging — you do not have boxes, you do not have sacks, you have the materials the forest provides, wrapped in the way that keeps them cool and clean on a long carry. This detail is so specific it could only come from someone who has actually thought about how goods travel out of the Congo Basin. It grounds the chapter in a physical reality I can inhabit.

The question I would push on:

The Malebo Pool. The Pool is one of the most dramatically physical places in Central Africa — a vast widening of the Congo River, sometimes seventeen kilometers across at its broadest, the currents pulling in different directions, the fishermen's canoes threading between the market platforms, the noise of the market audible before the opposite bank is visible. The chapter gestures at it — "the Pool market to the southwest" — but the chapter must eventually take us there. The Pool is not just a destination; it is a world. The broker's calculation arrives at the Pool as an abstraction in the opening. By the chapter's end, the reader should be able to feel the Pool: the sound of it, the scale of it, the smell of fish and water and a hundred trading communities working the same junction.

The plateau itself also needs more. The Bateke Plateau is high savanna with a specific landscape — the flat-topped hills, the valleys where streams cut down to the Congo basin, the edge where the plateau drops into the forest. The broker walks in this landscape every day. It shapes how he sees the world, how far he can see from high ground, where the roads run and why. The chapter should give me this.

What I notice: the opening is principally the broker in his own mind. This is correct for establishing the voice, but the chapter must also be in the world around him — the sounds of the trading compound, the specific quality of the afternoon light on the plateau, the physical scale of twenty copper ingots stacked against a wall. The mind calculation is the chapter's engine; the body in the world is what makes the engine believable.

The foundation is strong. The chapter needs to build on it sensory room by room.
