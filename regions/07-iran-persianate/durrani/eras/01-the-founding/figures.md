# Figures — The Founding: Ahmad Shah
## Era 1: c. 1747–1772 CE / 1160–1186 AH

---

## Ahmad Shah Durrani (Durrānī) — Durr-i Durrān, the Pearl of Pearls
**c. 1722–1773 CE / c. 1134–1186 AH**

He was probably born in Multan or Herat — the sources disagree and he was not, when he was born, important enough for anyone to record the precise location. His father, Muhammad Zaman Khan, was a Sadozai chief of the Popalzai Pashtuns, dead before Ahmad Khan was old enough to follow him. The absence shaped the son: he came to adulthood without a father's authority behind him and learned early that authority had to be made rather than inherited.

Nader Shah took the Abdali Pashtun contingent into his service, and Ahmad Khan rose through that service to command approximately 10,000 horsemen in Nader Shah's Indian campaigns. These campaigns were formative in a specific way: Ahmad Khan watched Nader Shah dismantle the Mughal empire's prestige in a single afternoon in Delhi in 1739, watched the Peacock Throne be carried west, watched a great sedentary empire prove incapable of defending its accumulated wealth against mobile cavalry. He absorbed the lesson. He also learned Nader Shah's administrative methods, his management of multi-ethnic armies, his use of terror and generosity in alternation to maintain loyalty. He was a student before he was a king.

At the Loya Jirga he was twenty-five, probably the youngest serious candidate. His case rested on three things: Sadozai royal lineage (the bloodline that the Pashtun tradition associated with legitimate leadership among the Popalzai); his control of the Abdali cavalry, which was the only military force in the region already organized and proven; and his personal record in Nader Shah's service. He had demonstrated the leadership qualities Pashtunwali demanded — military competence, physical courage, the capacity to command respect from his peers rather than merely from his subordinates.

He was also a poet. The Persian ghazals that survive from his *divan* are not courtly affectations; they are competent literary productions from a man who understood the classical forms well enough to use them for his own purposes. The meditation on empire and impermanence that runs through his verse is not a young man's pose — it is a commander's understanding of what force can and cannot secure. His poetry suggests a man who knew the difference between what a sword makes possible and what it cannot build.

The couplet used in the chapter (*"The world is dust, and empire is dust, and the name that remains / is the name that the dust could not hold — gone before it was written"*) is a period-consistent composite rendering drawn from the themes and vocabulary of the surviving ghazals rather than a specific attributed text. Ahmad Shah's *divan* is partially preserved; the existing scholarship on his verse (primarily in Persian-language studies) is thin. The composite faithfully represents the philosophical register of his attested poetry without claiming the status of a specific quotation.

He campaigned nine times into the Indian subcontinent in twenty-five years. He held Khorasan, the Punjab, Sindh, and Kashmir at various points of his reign. He chose not to hold Delhi when he could have held it, because he understood the difference between military reach and administrative capacity better than most rulers of his era understood it. He died of cancer in Kandahar in 1772, probably in his early fifties, still wearing the title Durr-i Durrān that the assembly had given him in the year he received a sheaf of green grain as his crown.

The grant of the title *Durrānī* — from *durr*, pearl — replaced the older tribal name Abdālī. This was Ahmad Shah's deliberate act: he renamed his people after the title of their king, making the dynasty's name the people's name, making the people's identity inseparable from the royal house he was founding. The Pashtun linguistic tradition reads this as a kind of covenant: the Pearl of Pearls guaranteed the pearls.

## Hajji Jamal Khan Mohammadzai
**fl. 1747 CE**

What do we know of Hajji Jamal Khan? He sat across the circle from Ahmad Khan at Kandahar. He was the chief of the Mohammadzai branch of the Barakzai — the largest and militarily most powerful of the Durrani tribes, a lineage that could plausibly have put forward its own candidate for leadership of the confederacy. He chose not to. Or, more precisely, he chose to support the Sadozai's royal claim in exchange for what that support would mean in practice: Mohammadzai military commanders holding the empire's key positions while Sadozai princes held the throne.

The chronicles record his acquiescence at the Loya Jirga. They do not record his reasons. Pashtunwali would not have required him to share them; the consensus was the fact, and the reasons were each man's own. But the calculation was not obscure. The Mohammadzai were warriors who understood the ledger of force. The Sadozai had the bloodline and the Abdali cavalry and the holy man's sign. The Mohammadzai had the numbers and the military organization. Between these two, a franchise was possible. Between rivals who each had everything, there was only war.

Hajji Jamal Khan's acquiescence in 1747 planted the seed of what would become the Durrani empire's succession problem. The deal between the Sadozai throne and the Mohammadzai military command held as long as both sides had more to gain from cooperation than from competition. Ahmad Shah held it by being a king worth serving. His successors — less capable, more numerous, more quarrelsome — held it less well. By the early nineteenth century, the Mohammadzai were no longer serving Sadozai kings. They were replacing them. Dost Mohammad Khan Barakzai — a Mohammadzai — would take Kabul in the 1820s. The circle that Hajji Jamal Khan had closed in 1747 reopened on different terms.

We cannot know whether Hajji Jamal Khan saw any of this possibility when he put his hand over his heart at Kandahar. He was a Pashtun khan making a calculation within the frame of Pashtunwali, and Pashtunwali's accounting ran in years and seasons rather than generations. What he saw was: the Sadozai had the cavalry, had the reputation, had the holy man's sign. The Mohammadzai had the power to make the deal stick if they chose to support it. He chose to support it. What came after was after.

## Vishwasrao (Viswasrao)
**c. 1740–1761 CE**

He was the Peshwa's son, the heir to the Maratha Confederacy's political center, sent to Panipat to command the largest military operation the Marathas had yet attempted in northern India. He was approximately eighteen to twenty years old. He rode into the battle near the center of the Maratha line, in the Maratha tradition of commanders who shared the risk with their men rather than directing from a safe distance.

He was killed by a musket ball in the early afternoon. The accounts differ on whether he was leading a charge or reorganizing his position when he was hit. Both versions are consistent with what a young commander trained in Maratha practice would have been doing. The Maratha center broke when he fell. The flanks became untenable. The Maratha army — which had been fighting competently under difficult conditions, its supply routes cut for months, its soldiers hungry — lost the coherence that kept a large force functional under pressure.

Vishwasrao is a figure from the opposing side, included here because his death at Panipat is the moment the battle turned. He is not the chapter's subject but he is one of its load-bearing facts: without understanding that the Maratha political system concentrated its legitimacy in a person, and that person's death in battle had consequences the Durrani political system — which distributed legitimacy across the clan confederation — did not face in the same way, the Panipat outcome is a military fact rather than a historical event. The Durrani confederation could absorb the death of a commander. The Maratha political system, at Panipat, could not.

He is included here with some caution about the limits of what we know. The Maratha sources on Panipat are fragmentary; the Durrani sources are partisan; the British sources (who arrived in India after the battle had already happened and were receiving their information third-hand) are retrospective. What is certain is that he was at Panipat, that he was killed in the battle, and that his death mattered. The rest is reconstruction.

## The Persian Secretary (unnamed)
**fl. 1747–1772 CE**

The Durrani chancery employed Persian secretaries — men trained in the calligraphy and forms of Persian administrative correspondence, capable of composing the diplomatic letters, revenue documents, and royal decrees that the empire required. These men are not named in the chronicles that survive. They were the empire's administrative infrastructure, the layer between the king's decisions and their legible form in the world.

We include the function rather than the person because the function is historically real. The Persianate administrative tradition had professional scribes as a class — men who trained in calligraphy, in Persian rhetoric, in the forms of royal correspondence, in the art of saying what a king needed to say in the way that the receiving court would recognize as authoritative. When Ahmad Shah's letter reached the Ottoman court or the court of Awadh, it would have been read by men trained in the same tradition as the man who had written it. This was the Persianate world's information system: a shared language, a shared set of forms, a shared understanding of what a royal document looked like and meant.

The unnamed secretary is present here as a reminder that empires are made of more than kings. Ahmad Shah's bilingual authority — Pashto in the camp, Persian in the chancery — required people who could sustain the chancery register with professional competence. Those people had faces and hands and a particular way of cutting reed pens to the right angle for nastaʿlīq script. We do not know their names. We know the script survives.
