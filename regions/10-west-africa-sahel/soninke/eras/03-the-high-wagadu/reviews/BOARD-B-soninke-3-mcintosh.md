# B-soninke-3 — Susan Keech McIntosh — Board Review of "The High Wagadu"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Archaeology of the Middle Niger and western Sahel. Material culture of the trans-Saharan trade. Urbanism and state formation in West Africa.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The twin-city structure described by Al-Bakri is consistent with the Kumbi Saleh excavations. The stone buildings excavated at Kumbi Saleh (Thomassey & Mauny, 1951; Berthier, 1997) correspond to the merchant quarter Al-Bakri describes. The royal quarter, described as built of wood and thatch, has not been conclusively identified at the site. The chapter handles this appropriately in the landing section: "The royal quarter, built of wood and thatch within its grove of trees, has left less trace, because wood and thatch return to the earth in ways that stone does not." This is archaeologically honest.
- The chapter correctly notes that the identification of Kumbi Saleh with Al-Bakri's capital is "probable" rather than certain. The scholarly consensus leans toward this identification but it is not definitive. Correct handling.
- The material culture details in the market scene (gold dust on brass scales, salt blocks wrapped in reed matting, leather goods, kola nuts, copper) are all consistent with archaeological finds and Arabic source descriptions for trans-Saharan trade goods. The shea butter detail is consistent with the region's material culture.
- The ecology section is broadly accurate. The Sahel transition zone description (rainfall gradients, vegetation changes from acacia to shea to baobab) is consistent with paleoecological reconstructions for the period, though there is evidence that the Sahel was somewhat wetter during parts of the period in question than it is today.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The physical descriptions ring true to what the archaeological and written records support.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The decision to describe the "archaeological silence" where the royal town stood as "its own kind of evidence" is both honest and evocative. This is exactly the right approach to the absence of material evidence for the sacred quarter.
- The material culture in the market scene is well-grounded and specific.
- The ecological framing (the Sahel as transition zone creating the conditions for intermediary power) is consistent with current understanding.

**What needs correction:**
- "Fragments of painted plaster, glass weights, gold artifacts have surfaced from the sand." The painted plaster and glass weights are attested at Kumbi Saleh. The "gold artifacts" claim should be more cautious. Some gold items have been found, but the Kumbi Saleh excavations have not produced large quantities of gold artifacts. The wealth described by Al-Bakri was primarily in gold dust, not worked gold objects. The sentence implies more gold artifacts than the record supports.
- The ecological description says "fewer than two hundred millimeters" for the northern Sahel. This is consistent with modern measurements but the paleo-rainfall data for the period c. 700-1076 CE suggests somewhat higher rainfall across the Sahel than today, possibly by 50-100mm. The chapter could note that the Sahel may have been marginally wetter during this period, which would have made the agricultural margins more productive and the transition zone somewhat broader.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS. Material culture use is strong. The two corrections are matters of precision. The ecological caveat is worth noting but does not undermine the chapter's argument.
