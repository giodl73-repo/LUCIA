## B-3 — John E. Lavers — Board Review of "The Bornu Empire"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Historian of Islam in Kanem-Bornu, author of "Islam and the State in Kanem-Bornu: Some Thoughts on the Late Saifawa and the Early Kanemi Periods" (1983). Specialist in the relationship between Islamic institutions and Saifawa governance.

**Historical accuracy:**

- The chapter's treatment of the Saifawa sacred kingship as coexisting with Islam rather than being displaced by it is historically correct and represents the scholarly consensus. By the sixteenth century, the two systems had been intertwined for over five hundred years. Ahmad ibn Fartuwa himself embodied this synthesis: an Islamic scholar serving a ruler whose authority rested on both Islamic legitimacy and pre-Islamic sacred kingship.
- The description of Alooma's Maliki legal reforms is consistent with what the sources document. The chapter correctly identifies the reform as a formalization of existing Maliki practice rather than an introduction of a new legal system. The Maliki school had been present in the Lake Chad region for centuries before Alooma.
- Ahmad ibn Fartuwa's role as a Maliki jurist is correctly described. The chronicle reveals a man with legal training applying jurisprudential reasoning to his assessment of the mai's campaigns and policies.
- The hostel in Medina is correctly described and its diplomatic significance is appropriately framed. The construction of a pilgrim hostel in the holy city was an assertion of the Bornu state's place in the Islamic world.
- The Ottoman correspondence is correctly framed within the Islamic political vocabulary: "a communication between Muslim sovereigns, acknowledging a shared framework of obligation and authority." This is the correct reading of the diplomatic relationship.
- The chapter's statement that the Ottomans "recognized the mai as a legitimate ruler" is appropriate. The provision of military assistance and the diplomatic correspondence imply recognition, though the precise terms of the relationship are debated.

**Cultural authenticity:**

- The chapter's treatment of Islam is its greatest strength from my perspective. Islam is presented as the culture's own intellectual and spiritual framework, not as an import or a veneer. "Ahmad took the charge as a scholar takes a commission from God: with precision." This sentence inhabits Ahmad's Islamic worldview without stepping outside it.
- The Maliki legal scene in Section IV effectively shows Islamic jurisprudence as a living practice adapted to local conditions, not as an abstract legal system imposed from outside. The lakeshore dispute, while constructed, represents the kind of case that the Maliki framework would have addressed.
- The phrase "the five centuries of Islamic learning that had become the dynasty's own" is precisely right. This is the correct framing: Islam was not something the Saifawa adopted; it was something they had lived within for longer than most European states had existed.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The sacred kingship-Islam synthesis is handled with appropriate nuance throughout. Neither system is reduced to an instrument of the other.
- The description of the curtain as "the place where they met: the sacred body of the king and the learned authority of the faith, inseparable, reinforcing" is analytically elegant and historically defensible.
- The Ottoman correspondence is placed within the Islamic political vocabulary of mutual recognition between Muslim sovereigns, which is the correct frame.
- Ahmad's dual role as chronicler and jurist is used to excellent effect, showing how Islamic scholarship functioned as governance infrastructure.

**What needs correction:**

- **"Rituals that predated the court's Islam."** This is acceptable as a general claim, but the specific nature and content of the pre-Islamic rituals associated with the sacred kingship are poorly documented. The chapter should not imply that we know more about these pre-Islamic rituals than we do. The sources (including the ethnographic record) provide some evidence of older ritual practices associated with the mai's person, but the specifics are debated. The phrasing is appropriately vague, but the reader should understand that the "older rituals" are partly inferred from later practice rather than documented from the pre-Islamic period.
- **The qadi appointment.** The chapter says Alooma "appoint[ed] qadis in the major towns." While Alooma is associated with legal reform, the specific claim that he appointed qadis across the state's major towns is an extrapolation from the general evidence of his legal reforms. The sources document his emphasis on Islamic law but do not provide a detailed account of the administrative structure of the reform. The claim should be softened.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

The chapter's treatment of Islam in Bornu is the best I have seen in a non-specialist text. The synthesis of sacred kingship and Islamic authority is handled with genuine nuance, and Ahmad ibn Fartuwa is presented as a figure whose Islamic identity is fully inhabited rather than observed from outside. The qadi-appointment claim needs softening, and the pre-Islamic ritual reference should acknowledge the limits of the evidence. With these corrections, the chapter is exemplary in its treatment of this material.
