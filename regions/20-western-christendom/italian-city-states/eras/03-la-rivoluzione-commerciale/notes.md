## 1. Sources

**Primary (Tier 1):**
- *Villani Chronicles* (Giovanni Villani, c. 1280–1348) — Florentine chronicler; eyewitness to commercial and political life; descriptions of the Bardi/Peruzzi banking operations, the Arte della Lana, the guild system
- Francesco Pegolotti, *Libro di mercatanzie et usanze de' paesi* (c. 1340) — the merchant's handbook; trade routes, currency exchange rates, weights and measures across 50+ markets; the most detailed primary source for commercial practice of the era
- Venetian Senate Deliberations (ongoing from 13th c.) — the most complete administrative record in medieval Europe; tracks galley system, trade policy, constitutional decisions
- Doge Andrea Dandolo, *Chronica per extensum descripta* — Venetian constitutional history from the inside
- *Statuta communis Bononie* and comparable commune statutes — the legal texts of self-governance; the commune's own articulation of what it is

**Secondary (Tier 2–3):**
- Lopez, Robert S. *The Commercial Revolution of the Middle Ages, 950–1350* (Cambridge, 1976) — the standard text; Lopez coins the term and traces the mechanisms
- Lane, Frederic C. *Venice: A Maritime Republic* (Johns Hopkins, 1973) — the galley system, the Arsenal, the Venetian state as commercial entity
- Najemy, John M. *A History of Florence 1200–1575* (Blackwell, 2006) — Florentine constitutional history; the guild system as political institution
- Wickham, Chris. *Sleepwalking into a New World* (Princeton, 2015) — commune emergence; how bishops lost power to citizen associations
- Martines, Lauro. *Power and Imagination: City-States in Renaissance Italy* (Knopf, 1979)
- Day, John. *The Medieval Market Economy* (Blackwell, 1987)

## 2. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The bill of exchange as infrastructure of trust — and trust as the commune's political technology applied to commerce.

**Mechanism:** The bill of exchange only works if the network of merchant firms is dense enough, and stable enough, that default is both practically devastating and socially catastrophic. The Florentine banking houses — Bardi, Peruzzi, Frescobaldi, Acciaiuoli — are not just financial companies; they are political actors whose creditworthiness is inseparable from their communal standing. The commune, as a sworn association of free men, has encoded mutual obligation into civic identity. That same structure of mutual obligation is what makes the commercial instruments work across distance. The political form IS the commercial infrastructure.

**Terminal condition:** The engine's vulnerability is identical to its strength: trust distributed through a network of firms collapses if a critical node fails. The Bardi and Peruzzi are building this network in 1200–1350, making it denser and more capable. But they are also building a fragility: by 1340, a single decision by Edward III of England to default on his war loans will bring down the two largest firms in Europe because the network has become too interconnected to survive a major break. This fragility is latent in the era, not yet actualized — the chapter should name it without narrating it.

**Terminal condition type (v1.6 framework):** *Substrate exhaustion* — the trust network's substrate (the creditworthiness of major firms) erodes through concentrated exposure to sovereign borrowers who do not recognize the network's premises as binding. Closest fit: *premise non-recognition* (Edward III does not recognize Florentine credit norms as binding on a king). The chapter should plant this seed in the Era 3 landing, leaving its blooming to Era 4.

## 3. Key Figures

**Berto di Iacopo Frescobaldi** (fl. 1290s) — The opening's anchor figure. Member of the Frescobaldi banking family, one of the "three great firms" of Florentine banking alongside Bardi and Peruzzi. The Frescobaldi operated in England as papal bankers and commercial lenders; Berto is a documented historical figure in English exchequer records. He is not a revolutionary — he is a professional, and his professionalism IS the revolution.

**Giovanni Villani** (c. 1280–1348) — Florentine chronicler and wool merchant; his Chronicle is the most detailed account of Florentine commercial and civic life of the era. He died of the Black Death. His figures on Florentine economic output — 200+ firms, 80,000 florins per day in commercial transactions — are the primary quantitative source for the era's scale. He is also a figure of honest uncertainty: he describes things he cannot fully explain, which makes him a reliable guide.

**Pietro Gradenigo / The Serrata of 1297** — The Venetian Doge who closed the Maggior Consiglio to new families in 1297, creating the hereditary nobility. This moment — the constitutional invention of the Venetian oligarchy — is as important as any commercial instrument. The chapter should place a specific person in the Senate chamber arguing for or against the serrata.

**Andrea Dandolo** (Doge, 1343–1354) — the chronicler-Doge; unusual figure who was both the head of the Venetian state and its historian. His chronicle is simultaneously a primary source and a piece of political self-presentation. He serves the "chronicler-within-chronicle" technique.

**Benedetto Zaccaria** (c. 1248–1307) — Genoese admiral, merchant, and diplomat; operated simultaneously in the Black Sea, the Atlantic, and the Aegean; fought for Genoa, for Castile, for Byzantium; ran the alum monopoly from Phocaea that was one of the era's most lucrative trading privileges. The type: the Genoese merchant who is not a citizen in the Venetian sense but a commercial adventurer whose standing comes from his network, not his city's institutions.

## 4. Narrative Arc

**Section plan:**

**Section 1 — The Parchment (integrated from opening, ~800w):**
Berto in London. The bill of exchange enacted. The widening to the three cities. The world that has been built.

**Section 2 — The Instruments (~900w):**
How the commercial instruments work, shown at method level. The *commenda* contract: a specific Venetian partnership agreement, the investor's calculation, the traveling partner's calculation, the risk distribution. The bill of exchange: what happens at each stage of Piero's journey from London to Florence — who endorses it, who discounts it, what the exchange rate means, what happens if the parchment is lost. Double-entry bookkeeping: a Florentine clerk making entries, the left and right columns, what the balance reveals. These are not innovations described — they are knowledge systems shown operating.

**Section 3 — The Commune (~900w):**
The political form that made the commercial world possible. The communal oath — what a Milanese citizen swore when the commune was founded, what the swearing meant, what it required of him. The *podestà* system: why the commune imported its executive from another city (to prevent factionalism from paralyzing governance). The guild system in Florence: the Arte di Calimala as a political institution, not just a trade association; guild membership as the basis of political participation. This section must be told through a specific civic moment — perhaps a guild council meeting deciding on a trade regulation, or a *podestà* arriving in a new city and taking his oath.

**Section 4 — Venice: La Serenissima (~800w):**
The Venetian state as the most fully realized version of the commune-as-commercial-entity. The serrata of 1297: the closure of the Maggior Consiglio, the invention of hereditary nobility. Why this was a constitutional achievement rather than merely an exclusion. The Arsenal — shown from inside, a specific galley in a specific stage of construction, the division of labor that allowed a galley a day. The Senate deliberations: what a vote in the Venetian Senate looked like, how the decision-making worked.

**Section 5 — Genova and the World (~700w):**
The Genoese model: decentralized, faction-riven, commercially aggressive. Benedetto Zaccaria and the alum trade — the monopoly, the strategic asset, the political manipulation. Caffa on the Black Sea: the Genoese colony at the end of the world, the logic of why it exists, what it connects. The *Casa di San Giorgio* — founded in 1407, just outside this era, but its antecedents visible here: the Genoese habit of incorporating public debt into commercial instruments.

**Section 6 — Landing (~400w):**
The world of 1350 as it stands on the eve of the Black Death and the Bardi-Peruzzi collapse. The network is at its fullest extension — Florentine agents in London, Bruges, Avignon, Constantinople; Venetian galleys on the Alexandria run; Genoese colonies from the Atlantic to the Black Sea. The instruments of trust are everywhere. And the fragility is built in. The chapter does not narrate the collapse — it names what is being built and what it is built on. The jelilu (in Malian terms) is silent here; the equivalent is the Florentine clerk who continues making entries in his ledger, not knowing that the page he is on will be the last full one.

## 5. Panel Pressure Points (anticipated from PANEL-1)

**Tuchman:** The chapter must maintain a single narrative spine across three cities. The spine is the bill-of-exchange-as-trust-infrastructure. Every section must return to this mechanism. The Venice and Genoa sections are not detours — they show variant forms of the same underlying logic.

**Ibn Khaldun:** Name the causal mechanism: why HERE, why NOW. The chapter must explain the relationship between the commune's political form and its commercial achievements. The answer: the commune encodes mutual obligation into civic identity, and that same structure makes commercial credit possible across distances. Show this connection explicitly.

**Achebe:** Each city gets its own civic identity. Florentines are Florentines; Venetians are Venetians; Genoese are Genoese. The chapter must not merge them into "the Italian commercial revolution" and lose the particularity. Find the Venice-specific scene, the Genoa-specific scene.

**Davis:** Show the knowledge systems at method level. At least three instruments shown operating: the *commenda* calculation, the bill-of-exchange endorsement chain, the double-entry balance. The constitutional engineering of the serrata deserves the same depth as the financial instruments.

**Kapuściński:** Three physical spaces, each vivid: (1) the Frescobaldi counting house in London (done in opening); (2) the Venetian Arsenal from inside; (3) a Florentine banking bench on Via Porta Rossa. Each section must have a body in a place.

## 6. Voice

**Register:** Urban/commercial. The counting-house voice: precise, unsentimental, transactional. Dense but not academic. Carries analytical argument in the narrative register. Related to the "Urban and Market Cultures" voice type in the style guide, but more specifically the voice of a culture that has made commerce its theology — not the market as chaos, but the market as organized, documented, certified.

**Rhythm:** Medium-length sentences that accumulate like ledger entries, punctuated by short declarative stops when a principle is established. The voice of a man who knows what things cost and what they are worth, which are not always the same.

**Em-dash target:** 0.

## 8. Open Questions

- The exact mechanism of the bill-of-exchange endorsement chain: how many hands did a typical bill pass through between London and Florence? (Pegolotti's handbook should have detail on this.)
- The *commenda* contract: what was the typical profit split between the sedentary investor and the traveling partner? (Lopez cites roughly 3/4 to investor, 1/4 to traveler — verify.)
- The serrata of 1297: was it contested? Who opposed it? (Lane cites opponents among the lesser nobility — use this for the Senate scene.)
- Benedetto Zaccaria's alum monopoly: what exactly was the contractual arrangement with the Byzantine emperor? (This is the kind of detail that shows the Genoese model at its most distinctive.)
