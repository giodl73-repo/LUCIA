# Board Review — Angela Schottenhammer

**Chapter:** Chinese Overseas Era 1: The Tang-Song Nanyang
**Reviewer:** Angela Schottenhammer (material evidence specialist)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Overall Assessment

The material culture is handled well. The trade goods are correctly identified and their physical properties (camphor sublimation, sandalwood moisture sensitivity, porcelain heat retention) are accurately described. The shibosi system is correctly characterized. The chapter uses the material evidence to do narrative work rather than treating it as background detail.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

1. **Dehua porcelain.** Correctly identified as the primary Quanzhou export ceramic for the Nanyang market. The description "white as pared bone" is accurate for the Dehua white ware that dominates the Song-era Southeast Asian archaeological assemblages. The note that the bowls were "plain, made for eating, not for display" correctly distinguishes the Nanyang export market from the more refined Chinese domestic market.

2. **Copper cash.** The Song court's massive copper coinage and its role in the Nanyang monetary system is correctly described. The detail about the coins with square holes is accurate. The chapter correctly identifies the monetization as an unplanned consequence of Song fiscal policy rather than a deliberate commercial strategy.

3. **The compass.** The note that the magnetic compass supplemented rather than replaced traditional navigation is correct. The compass was in use by Chinese navigators from the eleventh century, but celestial navigation and water-reading remained primary.

4. **Zhao Rugua.** Correctly identified as shibosi at Quanzhou in the 1220s. His Zhufan Zhi is correctly characterized as the most detailed Song-era source on the Nanyang trade.

## What Needs Correction or Clarification

1. **Ceramic specificity.** The chapter mentions only Dehua ware. Quanzhou also exported Longquan celadon (green-glazed ware from Zhejiang) in significant quantities to the Nanyang market. The archaeological record in Southeast Asian port sites typically shows both Dehua white and Longquan green. Adding a reference to the celadon would strengthen the material accuracy.

2. **Junk construction.** The chapter mentions bulkheads and describes the hull as "teak." Song-era Chinese junks were typically built from Chinese fir (Cunninghamia lanceolata) or camphor wood, not teak. Teak was a Southeast Asian timber used in local boat-building. The hull should be described as fir or simply as "timber" without specifying the species.

3. **The shibosi duty rate.** The chapter states "duty paid at the standard rate of one-tenth of value in kind." The duty rate varied over the Song period and across different categories of goods. One-tenth is a reasonable approximation for the middle Song period, but the chapter should acknowledge that rates fluctuated. Adding "roughly" before "one-tenth" would be sufficient.

4. **Watertight compartments.** The chapter mentions "watertight compartments" in Zhao Rugua's description. This is attested in the Quanzhou shipwreck evidence (the Quanzhou Bay ship, excavated 1974), but the attribution to Zhao Rugua specifically should be checked. Zhao describes the ships' size and cargo capacity; the watertight compartment evidence is primarily archaeological.

## Recommendation

The material culture is handled with care, and the chapter's use of goods as narrative carriers (each commodity teaching the reader about the trade through its physical properties) is effective. The corrections are matters of precision. **Approve with minor revisions.**
