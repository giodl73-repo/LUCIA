# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman
## Panel Review: Opening — Canada Era 4, The National Policy and Its Discontents

The opening works as narrative. I say that directly because it is the only thing I care about at this stage, and it is not always the case.

The opening scene earns its reader. The scissors are the right choice. Not a speech by Macdonald, not a map of the CPR's surveyed route, not a bureaucratic preamble — a pair of scissors, cold, against a child's neck. That is the scene. "The braids fell to the floor and lay there like something that had once been living." That sentence will stay with a reader. It is earned, not decorated.

The structure of three movements works. The first movement is the child. The second is the National Policy told as a competing story — the "different kind of story" break is precisely handled. The third returns to the child and opens onto the 2021 reckoning. The arc bends correctly: from the personal, outward to the structural, back to the personal, and then forward into consequence. That is the arc of a book, not a summary.

**Where I would push:** The second movement, the National Policy section, is where the narrative voice becomes expository. "Canada in 1867 was a confederation of four provinces with a design for the west and a problem with the east." This sentence is not wrong, but it is not the voice of the first section. It reads as contextual scaffolding. For the full chapter, this mode must be kept short and returned from quickly. The reader will forgive one expository passage in an opening; they will not forgive a chapter that keeps defaulting to it.

The Quebec problem is introduced efficiently. The tension between English and French Canada needs more than its current two sentences in the full chapter — this is one of the two founding discontents and should carry structural weight alongside the residential schools. The danger is that the chapter becomes a chapter about residential schools with the English-French tension appended. That would be a failure of the "National Policy and Its Discontents" frame: the discontents are multiple, and they speak to each other, and the reader needs to feel that.

**The question I'd push on:** How does the full chapter hold the dual-track structure without one track subordinating the other? The opening manages it; the chapter length will strain it. What is the structural mechanism that lets Qwenqwent's story and the English-French conscription crisis exist in the same chapter without one becoming context for the other?

I will read the chapter when it arrives.
