# R1 — Panel Review: Barbara Tuchman
## Haiti, Era 3: Fè Sa — The Revolution (opening.md)

**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

This opening earns its first sentence. "The night of August 14, 1791, at Bois Caïman, the rain came down like a pronouncement." That is a writer who knows where they are. The date, the place, the weather — not as orientation for the reader, but as the key in a lock. Everything that follows turns on that night.

What works structurally is the three-beat architecture: the ceremony, the colonial context, the structure of the uprising. The ceremony comes first, which is exactly right — this is not a chapter that will explain Vodou and then show it; it inhabits Vodou and then pulls back to let the reader see what that inhabitation meant. The colonial context section (the planter's ledger, the seven-year calculation) arrives at exactly the right moment — after the reader has stood inside the ceremony. They already know which side they are standing on. The ledger lands as horror, not context.

The pull is genuine and specific. "One week later, the north plain was burning." That is Tuchman's kind of transition — the gap between a night in a forest and a continent in flames, held in a single sentence. The reader falls through it.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter's three figures — Boukman, Cécile Fatiman, and the unnamed planter behind the ledger — are present and vivid, but only Cécile Fatiman has a body doing something specific. Boukman prays; his prayer is quoted in fragment. The uprising becomes an army. The chapter is earning the full chapter, but the notes.md should plan for at least three more fully embodied figures: Toussaint, Dessalines, and — I would argue — one ordinary person whose experience threads through the revolution's arc. Not as symbol. As a person.

The ending is clean and carries real weight. "The ancestors had been called. They came." Does not overstate. Does not summarize. Opens onto thirteen years. This is how chapters should end their openings.

**Line that will not leave me:** "What they owned, in 1791, was approximately five hundred thousand enslaved people on the western third of the island the Taíno had called Ayiti." The word "approximately" does all the moral work without being told to. You cannot count exactly. The counting was not done for the purpose of honoring.
