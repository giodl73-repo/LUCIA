# Board Review — Jamaican Music History / Ethnomusicology
**Chapter:** Jamaica Era 5 — The Sound of Freedom
**Reviewer:** Board — Ethnomusicology / Jamaican Popular Music
**Date:** 2026-04-17

I am reviewing for accuracy of the musical history and the ethnomusicological claims about reggae's formal properties.

## Musical History Accuracy

The ska-rocksteady-reggae progression is accurately periodized. Ska as the independence-era music, rocksteady's emergence around 1966–1967 as the rhythm slowed and the bass came up, reggae's consolidation around 1968: this is the standard periodization and it is correct.

The description of the ska and reggae rhythmic emphasis on the upbeat (two and four) is musically accurate. The guitar chop and piano skank on the offbeat is the defining formal characteristic of Jamaican popular music from ska onward. The chapter's claim that this constitutes a formal inversion of Western rhythmic hierarchy is an analytical claim, not purely a factual one, but it is a well-established analytical claim in ethnomusicology (Lloyd Bradley, Michael Veal, and others have made it) and the chapter is on solid ground.

The Studio One history — Coxsone Dodd's studio as the seedbed of Jamaican popular music — is accurate. The list of artists who recorded there (the Wailers, the Heptones, Toots and the Maytals, Alton Ellis) is correct and the studio's historical importance is not overstated.

The Lee Perry/Black Ark section is accurate for what is known. The uncertainty about the studio fire is genuine — accounts differ, Perry himself gave different accounts. The chapter handles this correctly by not resolving what cannot be resolved.

## The "Small Axe" Analysis

The musical analysis of "Small Axe" is accurate: the bass does enter before and below the other instruments as melodic argument. The guitar skank is on the two and four. The horn counterpoint is present. The chapter's description of the instrumental hierarchy — "bass as primary, voice as completion" — is the correct description of how the song is arranged and mixed by Perry.

The "Redemption Song" analysis: the guitar picking pattern in that song does land with upbeat emphasis even in the acoustic arrangement. This is correct.

## The "War" Attribution

The chapter states that "War" is "Selassie's League of Nations speech set to music verbatim." This requires slight precision: "War" (from the album Rastaman Vibration, 1976) is based on a speech Selassie gave to the United Nations General Assembly on October 6, 1963, not the 1936 League of Nations speech (the 1936 speech is "Until the philosophy which holds one race superior..."). The two speeches are both important in the Rastafari tradition but they are different documents. The chapter conflates them. **This requires correction.**

Correction: Change "Haile Selassie's League of Nations speech of June 30, 1936, after the Italian invasion" to a note clarifying that Selassie gave multiple internationally significant speeches, and that "War" is drawn from the 1963 UN General Assembly address, not the 1936 League of Nations address.

## Recommendation

**Approved for FINAL with one factual correction required:** The "War" speech attribution. The League of Nations 1936 speech and the 1963 UN address are both significant and both referenced in the Rastafari tradition, but they are different documents. The locked chapter should note the 1963 UN address as the source for "War." The 1936 speech can be retained as context for Selassie's anti-colonial significance.
