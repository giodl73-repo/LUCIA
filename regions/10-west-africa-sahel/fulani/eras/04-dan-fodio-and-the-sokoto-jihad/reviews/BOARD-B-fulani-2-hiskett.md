## B-fulani-2 — Mervyn Hiskett — Board Review of "Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Dan Fodio's biography, intellectual formation, and writings; the Torodbe scholarly tradition; the pre-jihad period in Gobir.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The Shehu's birthplace (Maratta), his intellectual lineage (Jibril ibn Umar), and his teaching career at Degel are correctly represented.
- The Shehu's age at the time of the jihad (fifty-four) is correct for the conventional birthdate of 1754.
- The characterization of the *Kitab al-Farq* as a juridical document that drew a specific line between Islamic and un-Islamic governance is accurate. The specific violations named (unlawful taxation, syncretism, enslavement of free Muslims) are drawn from the text.
- The Shehu's consultation of al-Maghili and al-Suyuti is accurate. Al-Maghili's writings on Islamic governance in the western Sudan were a direct influence on the Shehu's political theology.
- Yunfa's identity as a former student of the Shehu is correct and well-attested in the sources.
- The Shehu's withdrawal from governance and his continued writing are accurately described. His later treatises do critique the Caliphate's practices.
- The date of the Shehu's death (1817 CE / 1232 AH) is correct. The specific day (20 Jumada al-Thani) is consistent with the primary sources.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The Torodbe scholarly tradition is portrayed with appropriate intellectual seriousness. The chapter treats the *Kitab al-Farq* as a work of legal reasoning, not as a cultural artifact.
- The Shehu's teaching voice ("not loud but carried") is a characterization that, while not directly attested in the sources, is consistent with descriptions of Torodbe scholarly practice.
- The physical details of scholarship (gum-arabic ink, wooden writing boards, reed pens) are accurate to the material culture of Sahelian Islamic scholarship.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The Shehu's intellectual formation is rendered with appropriate depth. The chain from Jibril ibn Umar through the Maliki tradition to the specific arguments of the *Kitab al-Farq* is correct.
- The portrayal of the pre-jihad period as twenty years of teaching and writing before the call to action is accurate.
- The Shehu's reluctance to become a political leader is consistent with the evidence. The chapter handles this without romanticizing it.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter attributes over "a hundred treatises" to the Shehu. The number is generally accepted but should be noted as approximate, since the exact count varies depending on which minor works and letters are included.
- The chapter mentions the Shehu's writings in Fulfulde but does not note that some of his most influential works were in Arabic and that the Fulfulde works tended to reach different audiences. The distinction matters for understanding the network's reach.
- The reference to Jibril ibn Umar studying "in Agadez and in the libraries of the North African scholarly tradition" appears in the figures.md. Jibril's specific educational itinerary is not fully documented; the statement should be slightly hedged.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (3 minor corrections above)
