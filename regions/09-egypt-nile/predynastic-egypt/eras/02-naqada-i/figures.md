# Figures — Naqada I: The Amratian

## The Copper-Worker at Nubt

He sat in a workshop with a reed screen for a wall and a crucible for a hearth, and he turned green stone into copper. That was the work, and the work was his, and he had learned it the way everyone in his world learned the work that was theirs: by watching, by doing, by burning his face over the crucible until the signs of the smelting process lived in his hands and his eyes and the muscles of his mouth.

The record does not give his name. No writing existed at Nubt during Naqada I; the people of the upper valley were still six or seven centuries from the first hieroglyphs. What the record gives is the evidence of his practice: crucible fragments with copper residue, small finished tools in copper, hammerstones for working the metal after smelting, traces of charcoal and slag at settlement sites along the upper valley. He is a composite figure, drawn from the material evidence of early copper-working at Naqada and related sites in the Amratian period. His workshop, his methods, and his relationship to the exchange network are reconstructed from this evidence.

He is not a symbol. He is the best available rendering of a person whose practice connected communities across the upper Nile valley through the movement of a new material. He made copper tools because copper could be made, and he traded them because copper was wanted, and the network that his work sustained was already, by the end of his lifetime, beginning to differentiate the valley's settlements into centers and margins.

## The Desert-Walker

He walked into the Eastern Desert for malachite, the green copper ore that the people of the valley had been using as pigment for generations before anyone learned to smelt it. The route he followed ran east from the Nile through the Wadi Hammamat and its subsidiary wadis to the sandstone formations where malachite occurred in veins. The journey was measured in days, not distance. He carried water for the outward trip and stone for the return.

The desert-walker is a composite figure based on evidence of Eastern Desert exploitation during the Predynastic period: mining sites with hammerstone debris, route markers, temporary camps along the wadis, and the consistent presence of Eastern Desert materials (malachite, various hard stones) at Nile valley settlements. Wilkinson's *Genesis of the Pharaohs* documents the desert-route network and its role in connecting the valley to the mineral resources of the Eastern Desert. The human cost of desert travel, the route knowledge required, and the seasonal constraints on movement are inferred from the physical geography and from ethnographic parallels with later documented desert-crossing practices.

## The Potter

She worked with the fine-grained alluvial clay of the Nile's banks, building vessels by coiling and thinning them to a translucency that defines the Naqada I White Cross-Lined tradition. The painting was done with white lime slip before firing, in patterns that recur across the upper valley: wavy lines, cross-hatching, animal figures (hippopotami, crocodiles), and the upraised-arms figure whose meaning scholars continue to debate.

The potter is a composite figure. Gender attribution follows Wengrow's discussion of craft specialization in Predynastic communities, where pottery production was likely a household-based activity in which women played a central role, though the evidence does not exclude male participation. The techniques described (coiling, wall-thinning, lime-slip painting, open-pit firing with controlled atmosphere for black-top effects) are reconstructed from the pottery itself and from experimental archaeology.

The White Cross-Lined pottery is the type artifact of the Naqada I period. Its distribution across the upper valley is the primary evidence for the cultural coherence of Amratian communities and for the exchange networks that connected them. Patch's *Dawn of Egyptian Art* provides the most accessible visual survey of this tradition.

## The Burial-Maker

She prepared the dead for their journey west. The body was positioned on its left side, knees drawn up, facing west. Red ochre was applied to the skin. Grave goods were selected and placed with deliberation: pottery, palettes, flint blades, combs, and in some cases copper tools and imported materials.

The burial-maker is a composite figure. The mortuary practices described are reconstructed from the excavation records of Naqada I cemeteries, particularly at Naqada itself (Petrie's original excavations and subsequent work), at sites along the upper valley, and at the cemetery at el-Amra (from which the period name "Amratian" derives). The west-facing orientation, the flexed burial position, the use of ochre, and the patterns of grave-good selection are consistent across the Naqada I archaeological record.

The grave-good differentiation described in the chapter, where some burials include imported or high-value materials (copper, obsidian, Red Sea shells) while others contain only local pottery and palettes, is documented in the cemetery analyses of Castillos and in Wengrow's synthesis. This differentiation is the primary archaeological evidence for emerging social hierarchy in the Naqada I period.

## A Note on Composite Figures

All five figures in this chapter are composite constructions. Naqada I predates writing by approximately seven centuries and predates monumental art by at least three. No individual names survive. The figures are rendered as embodied persons performing documented activities in documented settings, and their interior experiences are inferred from the physical evidence of their practices rather than invented for narrative effect. Where the chapter attributes intention or knowledge to a figure, the attribution is grounded in the material record: the copper-worker's knowledge of smelting is evidenced by the smelted copper; the potter's skill is evidenced by the pottery; the burial-maker's cosmological orientation is evidenced by the consistent west-facing position of the dead. The gap between evidence and experience is acknowledged here, in the tradition of the v1.4 Archaeological Figures standard, as an honest limitation of the record.
