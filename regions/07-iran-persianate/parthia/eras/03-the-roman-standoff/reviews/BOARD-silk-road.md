## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

**The coin passage — ACCURATE and well-executed:**
The description of Arsacid coinage — Greek inscription on one face, Iranian royal imagery on the other — is historically accurate and is one of the best pieces of material-culture evidence for the chapter's central argument about the deliberate maintenance of parallel cultural registers. The chapter uses this correctly: not as a symbol but as mechanism, following the rubric's v1.5 Material Object principle. The numismatic evidence supports exactly the interpretation the chapter provides.

**"Philhellenes" on Arsacid coins — ACCURATE:**
The Arsacid coinage does carry the Greek epithet ΦΙΛΕΛΛΗΝΟΣ (Philhellenos) from approximately the reign of Mithridates II onward. The chapter is correct that this was a deliberate political self-presentation, not merely a cultural description. It was addressed to the Hellenized populations of Mesopotamia and western Iran who formed a significant constituency of the empire.

**The Zoroastrian fire temple alongside the Greek theater — NUANCED:**
The chapter's treatment is accurate in substance but could be slightly more precise. The Arsacid relationship to Zoroastrianism was more ambiguous than the Sassanids' later explicit Zoroastrian state religion. The Arsacids maintained fire temples and Zoroastrian ritual continuity but did not impose Zoroastrian practice across the empire in the way the Sassanids would. The chapter's statement that "the Iranian aristocracy expected Iranian religious forms" is accurate. The chapter does not overclaim about the systematic nature of Arsacid religious policy, which is appropriate given that this remains a scholarly question.

**The court cultural brokerage — WELL HANDLED:**
The chapter's description of the Arsacid court as maintaining "two complete cultural registers" is the correct scholarly characterization, and the chapter's explicit rejection of the word "hybrid" (mentioned in the notes.md and reflected in the prose) is scholarly best practice. The Arsacid synthesis was not syncretism — it was deliberate parallel maintenance. The distinction matters and the chapter maintains it.

**The "Two Courts in One City" section — EXCELLENT:**
This is the chapter's strongest cultural-authenticity section. The physical description of Ctesiphon — mud-brick and fired brick, hunting-scene decorations in Parthian style, a portico referencing Hellenistic architectural vocabulary — is consistent with the archaeological evidence and the descriptions in Strabo and later sources. The torchlit theatrical performance and the Greek theatrical tradition in Mesopotamia are historically established. The chapter earns its authority here.

## Silk Road Trade Verification

**Isidore of Charax — VERIFIED:**
The *Stathmoi Parthikoi* is the key source here and the chapter uses it correctly. The road-book is real, and the description of what it contains (distances, town names, customs-points) is accurate. The Board concurs with the BOARD-parthian-history reviewer's note that the ten-percent customs rate is a scholarly inference rather than a directly attested figure. This should be flagged in the production notes.

**Caravanserai description — PLAUSIBLE:**
The specific details (stone-walled courtyards, water cisterns, the goods described) are consistent with archaeological evidence from later Silk Road caravanserais and with what can be inferred from the Parthian period. The cedar-smoked meat is an inference but a reasonable one — dried and smoked meats were standard caravan provisions. The chapter's use of "plausible-reconstruction" for these details is the correct approach; they should be flagged in notes.md as inferred rather than directly attested.

**The specific goods listed — VERIFIED:**
Chinese silk, Indian cotton and pepper, Roman glass and metalwork, Parthian metalwork moving west — all verified in the archaeological and textual record for this period. The chapter accurately represents the bidirectional nature of Silk Road trade.

---

## One Factual Concern

The chapter implies that Ctesiphon's theater district was adjacent to residential and administrative areas in a way that suggests a planned Greek theatrical quarter. The archaeological evidence for Ctesiphon in this era is limited; much of what we know comes from Sassanid-period remains and later Arab-period descriptions. The chapter should not imply too much urban specificity about Ctesiphon in the Arsacid period. The phrase "the theater district" might be too specific. Recommend: "the courtyard of the Arsacid palace, where theatrical performances were held" rather than "the theater district."

---

## Recommendation

The chapter is culturally accurate with one adjustment:
1. **"Theater district":** Revise to "the palace courtyard" or similar less-specific phrasing to avoid implying more urban specificity than the archaeological record supports.

**Status: BOARD APPROVED with one adjustment recommended.**
