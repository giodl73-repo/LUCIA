## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Review of The High Tang (Opening)

**Overall:** I can smell the West Market. That is the test, and the opening passes it. But the opening is strongest in its middle sections and weakest at its edges — the arrival through the Chunming Gate needs more body, and the closing paragraphs lose the ground beneath the reader's feet.

**What works:**
- The West Market paragraph is the opening's sensory anchor. Sesame oil frying, lamb, foreign spice, the drum sounding from the market tower, the gates swinging wide — this is a place with a temperature and a smell, not a concept with a label. The Sogdian cooks making flatbreads staffed with lamb is the kind of detail that carries an entire world in a single image.
- "Rammed earth faced with plaster" — this is the texture of the ward walls. It is specific to Chang'an and to rammed-earth construction. It grounds the avenue in a material reality.
- The avenue "wide enough for twelve carriages abreast" — the reader can feel the scale without being told the city is large. The measurement is human-scaled (carriages, not meters) and lets the body register the space.

**What doesn't:**
- Du Fu arrives through the Chunming Gate "in the autumn" but the autumn is not yet felt. What does autumn feel like in Chang'an? The dust of the Wei River plain, the first cold wind from the northwest, the quality of the light. The opening tells us the season but does not place us in it. The Mali opening, by contrast, opens with the harmattan haze and the smell of dust and shea butter — we feel the season before we are told what it is.
- The final two paragraphs lose sensory ground entirely. "Du Fu could not have known, standing in the West Market in the smell of sesame and lamb and foreign spice" — this returns to the sensory anchor, but it is a reminder, not a new experience. The closing is conceptual, not embodied. "The last years of something" is an idea, not a place.
- The religious diversity section is architecturally specific (the Daqin monastery, the Zoroastrian temple three wards from the palace) but acoustically silent. What did these places sound like? Syriac liturgy, the crackle of a sacred fire, Buddhist bells at dawn — the ear is underused.

**The question I'd push on:**
The full chapter will need to sustain sensory immersion through court politics, rebellion, and flight. The West Market is easy to ground physically — it is a market. But the court, the poetry, the political decisions — how will you keep the reader's body in the room when the content becomes abstract? What does a court audience feel like? What does the sound of an approaching army do to a city? Where is the reader's body during the An Lushan rebellion?
