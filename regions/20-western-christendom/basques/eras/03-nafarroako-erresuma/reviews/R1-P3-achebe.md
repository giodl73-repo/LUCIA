# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel-1 Review: Nafarroako Erresuma Opening

The voice is right. That is the first thing. The opening does not explain the Basques to us — it does not stand above them and describe them in the third person of a museum exhibit. It enters the ridgeline. "A man who knows the ridgeline — who has walked it since childhood, who knows where the stone loosens near the upper lip" — this is not description from outside. This is knowledge from inside, the specific kind of knowledge that only belongs to a people who have lived in a place long enough to know how the stone moves in different seasons.

**Where the voice is strongest:**

"This is not a complaint. It is simply the record." This sentence is the opening's best moment. A Basque telling the story of Roncesvalles would not frame the Chanson de Roland's erasure as an injustice requiring redress. It is a fact about the French. The French wrote their poem and the Basques are not in it. The Basque position is not aggrieved — it is observational. The voice's tone here is the tone of a people who have been misrepresented so many times that accurate recording is simply the practice of living, not a political act. This is authentic.

The *foru* passage is also culturally correct in its approach. "Customs. Usages. The way things are done here." The gloss is provided in the culture's own terms before the political theory is introduced. The chapter does not reach for a Western legal concept to explain the *foru* and then say "but of course it was different." It stays inside the Basque understanding first.

**Where I would push:**

"We are the Pyrenees" — this phrase from the Overview document is stronger than anything in the opening's closing section. The opening reaches toward this claim but does not land it with the same compression. "The Basques did not migrate to the Pyrenees. We are the Pyrenees." The opening tries to say this through several sentences about Euskara surviving the Romans and the Visigoths, but these sentences list what Euskara survived rather than inhabiting what Euskara IS. The chapter needs at least one moment where the language is present on the page — a word, a phrase, a name — not just described as ancient.

The figure of Iñigo Arista is named but not inhabited. "Iñigo Arista, whose name suggests the Basque-Iberian mix the region had always been" — this is observation about a name, not a person. Who was Iñigo Arista in the understanding of the people who followed him? What did they call him? What did the oral tradition say about his holding of Iruñea? A single image drawn from the Basque tradition — not from the Frankish or Arab chronicles that recorded the event from outside — would root the chapter's person-claim. The chapter currently draws its figures from outside records. The full chapter must find a way to show figures from inside.

**The question I'd push on:**

The opening correctly names "the distance between what happened and what the poem said happened" as the founding condition of Basque historical consciousness. But whose telling do we have for what actually happened at Orreaga? The Basques had no contemporary written record. What remains of the Basque oral tradition of Roncesvalles? If the chapter does not engage with this — if it uses the Frankish record and contemporary scholarship to reconstruct "what really happened" — it is still drawing from outside sources and framing the Basque experience through them. The notes.md must address this source question directly.

**Verdict:** The voice is right. The person needs to be filled in. The source question must be answered.
