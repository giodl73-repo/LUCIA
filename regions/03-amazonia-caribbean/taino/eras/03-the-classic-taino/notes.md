# Notes — The Classic Taíno (c. 1200–1492 CE)

**Production Bible — Stage 4**
**Written:** 2026-04-17

---

## 1. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The areíto as the living archive of reciprocity, which is also the political system, which is also the cosmological maintenance mechanism. The areíto holds the community in relationship with the zemís, the ancestors, and each other. Its authority requires performance, and performance requires community, and community requires the cacique's material capacity to sustain the gathering. This is the self-reinforcing loop: the areíto legitimates the cacicazgo, and the cacicazgo's resources allow the areíto to be performed at the quality that legitimates it.

**Mechanism:** The areíto cannot be stored. It must be performed to exist. This means that every performance is also a transmission — the community learns it by doing it — and that transmission requires the community's physical presence in one place, fed and gathered, zemís attended. The cacique who can do this is the cacique whose areítos travel. The areítos that travel are the ones that activate the zemís reliably. The zemís' activation is felt, not measured, and the community knows the difference. This creates an epistemological quality-control system internal to the tradition itself.

**Terminal condition:** The reciprocity loop depends on the material capacity to perform. Epidemic disease removes performers (the people who carry the areítos in their bodies). Enslavement scatters the community that the performance requires. The seizure of zemís disconnects the ceremonial activation that makes the performance meaningful. Each disruption attacks a different node of the same system. The system does not break at once; it degrades node by node, with each degradation making the remaining nodes harder to maintain. By the time the last fluent performer dies without transmission, the archive has not been destroyed — it has become inaccessible. This is the difference that the chapter's landing must honor.

**Non-chronological organizing principle (v1.5):** The chapter will organize around the areíto as both subject and structural principle. The chapter itself is structured like an areíto: an opening invocation (Anacaona at work), movement through the material in sections that build on each other and return to earlier themes, and a closing that brings back the opening image transformed. This is a formal innovation that should be noted at GATE-2.

## 2. Sources

**Primary:**
- Peter Martyr d'Anghiera (Pietro Martire d'Anghiera): *De Orbe Novo Decades* (1511–1530) — eyewitness accounts from the contact period; primary source for Anacaona, Guarionex, the cacicazgos. External source whose biases must be used as evidence about the colonial encounter, not as neutral description.
- Bartolomé de las Casas: *Historia de las Indias* (written 1527–1561, published posthumously) and *Brevísima relación de la destrucción de las Indias* (1542) — key primary source, but ideologically shaped; his defense of the Taíno is also a framing of them, which must be handled carefully.
- Ramón Pané: *Relación acerca de las antigüedades de los indios* (c. 1498) — Columbus's friar who spent years with the Taíno; primary account of zemí traditions, mythological narratives, and ceremonies. Only source that recorded anything from inside the tradition.

**Academic:**
- Irving Rouse: *The Tainos: Rise and Decline of the People Who Greeted Columbus* (1992) — still the foundational archaeological synthesis; essential for chronology and material culture.
- José Juan Arrom: scholarship on Taíno mythology and Ramón Pané — essential for the zemí tradition from Pané's account.
- William F. Keegan: *Taino Indian Myth and Practice* (2007) — integrates archaeology and mythology; the batey and zemí scholarship.
- Lynne Guitar, Pedro Ferbel-Azcarate, Jorge Estevez: collaborative scholarship on Taíno continuity and the "extinction" myth — essential for the landing.
- Roberto Cassá: Hispaniola archaeological and ethnohistorical scholarship.
- Sebastián Robiou Lamarche: Puerto Rico Taíno scholarship, particularly on Borikén.

**Source situation:** This is a classic asymmetric source condition. The primary sources are all external (Spanish) and contact-period or post-contact. Pané is the closest thing to an inside source, and even he is a friar recording what the Taíno told him through translation. The technique required: **outsider-as-structural-evidence** (v1.5 Source Asymmetry Amendment). Las Casas's accounts of what the Spanish destroyed tell us the structure of what existed. Pané's recordings of the mythology tell us the framework of what we cannot see from outside. Peter Martyr's admiring descriptions of the cacicazgos tell us about the political organization at peak. The chapter must make the asymmetry visible as part of the story — not apologize for it.

The Taíno continuity scholarship by Guitar, Ferbel-Azcarate, and Estevez, combined with the recent DNA work (2018 ancient genome study from Cell), provides the evidence base for the landing's insistence on survival.

## 3. Key Figures

**Anacaona** (fl. 1490s–1503): Cacica of Xaragua; poet, composer, political figure. Married Caonabó, cacique of Maguana; after his capture by the Spanish, managed Xaragua alone. Known across Hispaniola for her areítos. Executed by the Spanish governor Nicolás de Ovando in 1503. The chapter's anchor figure.

**Bohechío** (d. c. 1500): Anacaona's brother, paramount cacique of Xaragua before her. Received Bartholomew Columbus in 1496 with a ceremonial welcome that Peter Martyr described in detail — the canoe procession, the women singing, the games. This reception becomes the chapter's first evidence scene (the areíto as diplomatic instrument).

**Guarionex** (fl. 1490s): Cacique of Maguana / La Vega Real; politically powerful, eventually forced to perform Spanish religious rites while maintaining the zemí tradition in private. His situation shows the colonial pressure on the ceremonial system before it breaks.

**Caonabó** (d. 1496): Anacaona's husband; cacique of Maguana at contact; initially hostile to the Spanish; captured by Alonso de Ojeda through deception (offering him "the shackles of the King of Castile" as a gift). His capture leaves Anacaona alone as the leading figure of Xaragua.

**Composite areíto singer:** Following v1.4 Figures Amendment — for the community performers who are the living library, a composite figure drawn from Pané's accounts and the batey ceremony descriptions. She is the elder woman who teaches Anacaona's daughter in the opening. She will appear in the chapter as the embodiment of the tradition's method.

## 4. Narrative Arc / Section Plan

**Section 1 (Opening integrated, ~800 words):** Anacaona composing in her bohío, late afternoon, Xaragua in its evening arrangement. Establishes the areíto as the chapter's structural principle and the voice's register. Introduces the zemí framework, the cacique's function, the batey as ceremonial center.

**Section 2 — The Xaragua World (~900 words):** Xaragua at its height: the conuco system and women's agricultural authority, the batey courts and their function in inter-cacicazgo relations, the cacique's role as reciprocity manager. The 1496 reception of Bartholomew Columbus: use this event not as "contact" but as a demonstration of Xaragua's ceremonial sophistication — Bohechío's reception shows the areíto as diplomatic instrument, the canoe procession as political theater, the women's songs as the form of welcome. Peter Martyr's account is the external source; the Taíno experience of it is the chapter's subject.

**Section 3 — The Areíto at Method Level (~900 words):** The knowledge system shown operating. Drawing from Pané's recordings of Taíno mythology (the cave at Cacibajagua, Deminán and the turtle-woman, the transformation of the dead into zemís): these are not "myths" explained but the content of the areíto library, shown as the living knowledge that the ceremony transmits. This section shows the areíto working at the level of method: the genealogical anchor that locates each community in the cosmos, the specific practices that make zemí activation happen (the tobacco, the cohoba powder, the carved faces that speak when properly approached), the lead singer's technical responsibilities.

**Section 4 — The Political Weave (~900 words):** The cacicazgo system as a political architecture. The five cacicazgos of Hispaniola and their relationships: alliance through marriage (Anacaona's marriage to Caonabó links Xaragua to Maguana), the batey game as a mechanism for managing inter-cacicazgo tension without military conflict, the circulation of areítos as soft power. Kalinago pressure from the eastern islands: the eastern frontier of Borikén and what the sustained Kalinago presence means for Taíno political organization. This section shows the system managing stress.

**Section 5 — The Degradation (~700 words):** The first colonial pressure on the reciprocity system. Not the full contact-period narrative — that belongs to Era 4. This is the Classic era's encounter with what is coming. Columbus's 1492 arrival on the north coast of Cuba, then Hispaniola. Guacanagarí's reception. The seizure of the first zemís (Columbus takes several back to Spain). The year-long gap: the Spanish fort at La Navidad, built from the wreck of the Santa María, garrisoned and then burned by the Taíno when the Spanish soldiers began enslaving women. The system's first stress test, which it fails not because it was weak but because it was not designed for this kind of pressure.

**Section 6 — Landing (~400 words):** Return to Anacaona. Not 1503, not the execution. Return to the bohío, the composition, the phrase that found its landing. The areíto she composed that evening was performed. Some of the people who learned it survived the epidemics by fleeing to the mountains or to other islands. Taíno descendants on Hispaniola, Cuba, and Puerto Rico today carry what did not die. The 2018 ancient genome study confirmed that the people called "extinct" had descendants who were alive to be tested. The living library moved. It carried what it could. The zemís are still listening.

This landing follows Achebe's instruction: not grief for extinction, but the areíto's own logic — you compose for a world that will outlast you. The tradition's own persistence mechanism is the landing.

## 5. Panel Pressure Points (anticipated)

**Tuchman:** The chapter needs a narrative problem that builds across all six sections, equivalent to the opening's "will the phrase land?" Identified: the chapter tracks whether the areíto's logic can hold the community together under pressure that it was not designed to resist. The turn is when the Spanish seize the zemís — not destroy them, SEIZE them — and the community must continue performing without the objects that make the performance work.

**Ibn Khaldun:** The material-to-ceremonial loop must be shown operating explicitly, not implied. Xaragua's resources are its prestige; its prestige generates resources; the crisis comes when the material base is removed. Must show the loop before showing it break.

**Achebe:** The survival question requires the landing to find the areíto's own continuity mechanism. Must not perform grief. Must not sentimentalize survival. The 2018 genome study is factual, not consoling — these people were alive; they had descendants. State it in the chapter's register.

**Davis:** Must show the areíto at method level. What does the cohoba ceremony actually require? What does a lead singer need to know that a participant does not? The knowledge system must be shown from inside its own practice, not admired from outside.

**Kapuściński:** The communal areíto at full performance must be rendered sensorially. The bohío's interior. The batey at ceremony. The chicha. The bodies moving in the same rhythm.

**Cohoba ceremony method level (Davis P2 feedback):**
The practice: Anadenanthera peregrina seeds (cohoba) dried and powdered, taken through forked tubes (tabaco — yes, this is the word Columbus borrowed for tobacco); the cacique uses the duho, undergoes the purging rite first (to empty and open); the inhalation produces a state in which the zemí communicates; the cacique reports to the community. Will be shown at method level in Section 3. This is the individual practitioner's interface complementing the community's collective areíto. Both in the chapter.

**Manioc processing method level (Davis P2 feedback):**
The tipití press (squeezing out prussic acid), the budare (clay griddle for cassava-bread baking), the grater of embedded sharp stones. This is women's knowledge at method level — the transformation of a toxic root into the community's staple is the knowledge that underlies the material base that allows the areíto to be performed. Will be shown in Section 2.

**Cacibajagua emergence narrative (Davis P2 feedback):**
Will be rendered in Section 3 as an areíto performance — not explained but performed on the page, in the areíto voice, as evidence of what the living library contains.

**2018 genome study:** Menéndez et al., *Cell* 2018 — analysis of 1000-year-old genome from a Bahamas cave, matching descent to contemporary Dominican and Puerto Rican populations. Will be cited in figures.md.

- Borikén's distinct political situation: will note in Section 4 as the eastern frontier context, keeping it brief; full treatment belongs to a separate Borikén chapter.

## 7. Voice Notes

**Register: Areíto-Ceremonial.** Cumulative, parallel construction. Period-stops are deliberate and spaced, carrying the weight of a carved line. Comma-chains for accumulation. No em-dashes. The voice repeats and transforms rather than summarizing. Analytical points emerge from the same register as narrative ones — the way an areíto carries knowledge in its rhythm rather than extracting it into a separate explanatory layer.

Test: Read any paragraph in isolation. Does it sound like this culture? Can you hear the ceremony's pace? If the paragraph sounds like academic prose with ornamental Taíno words, the voice has slipped.

**The "she does not know" construction:** Use sparingly but precisely. The opening used it once, and it earned that use. In the chapter, it may appear once more, in the landing — Anacaona performing the completed areíto, not knowing it will travel further than she can imagine.
