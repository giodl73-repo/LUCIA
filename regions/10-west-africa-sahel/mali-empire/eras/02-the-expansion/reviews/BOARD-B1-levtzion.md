# B-1 — Levtzion — Board Review of "When the Empire Reached the Sea"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Political succession and chronology of the Mali Empire from Arabic written sources; trans-Saharan trade economics; the gold-salt exchange system.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The succession sequence (Sundiata → Wali → Khalifa → interregnum → Sakura → Keita restoration → Abu Bakr II → Musa) is broadly consistent with Ibn Khaldun's account, though the sources are notoriously difficult on exact sequence. The chapter handles this well by noting that Ibn Khaldun's sequence does not always match the jelilu's.
- Wali's hajj is correctly noted as preceding Musa's. Ibn Khaldun confirms Wali as the first Manden ruler to make the pilgrimage.
- Khalifa's shooting arrows at his people is from the oral tradition and is carried in multiple jeli accounts. The deposition by the assembly is consistent with the sources.
- Sakura as a jon who became mansa is recorded by Ibn Khaldun. His expansion of the empire's eastern territories is accurate. His death on the return from the hajj is in the sources.
- Abu Bakr II's fleet account is sourced from al-Umari's interview with Musa. The chapter correctly attributes this. The detail of "two hundred canoes" is from al-Umari's account.
- The Wangara goldfield locations (Bambuk along the Senegal/Faleme, Bure along the upper Niger) are correctly placed.
- The trade system (gold-salt-kola triangle, Dyula network, Niger as trade highway, Taghaza salt mines) is accurately described.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The Kurukan Fuga is treated as a genuine constitutional framework rather than a decorative label. The gbara's authority to depose a mansa is consistent with the covenant's terms as carried in the oral tradition.
- The social categories (horon, nyamakalaw, jon) are accurately rendered in their relationships.
- The Wangara's secret-keeping is correctly attributed and framed as economic intelligence rather than cultural exoticism.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The institutional analysis is sound. The chapter's argument that the Manden survived succession crisis through institutional mechanisms rather than individual competence is consistent with the scholarly consensus.
- The Dyula network's role as commercial-cultural expansion agent is accurately described.
- The framing of Abu Bakr's departure as orderly succession rather than crisis is consistent with al-Umari's account.

**What needs correction:**
- Sundiata's death date: the chapter says "around the year 653 of the Hijra (1255 CE)." The Hijra conversion is slightly off: 653 AH corresponds approximately to 1255 CE, which is acceptable, but scholars typically place Sundiata's death in the range of 1255-1260. The chapter's hedging with "around" is appropriate.
- The phrase "the Manden's most far-reaching institution" for the Dyula network is a judgment that some scholars would contest (the jeli tradition has an equal claim to that title). Consider qualifying.
- Minor: "Flat-bottomed boats" on the Niger is reasonable but not specific to this period's evidence. The description is consistent with later ethnographic accounts of Niger River commerce but cannot be confirmed for the thirteenth century specifically. The chapter should be cautious here.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS — Two minor corrections recommended, none critical. The chapter's historical framework is sound.
