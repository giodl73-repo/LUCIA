## Findings

**P2 — Finding 1**

> "This had worked when Shia communities were minorities under Sunni governments: the question of who should rightfully govern was moot, because someone else was governing."

*Type:* Mild outside-view framing. The phrase "the question was moot" applies an outsider's analytical category to describe the community's condition. A member of that community would not say their question about legitimate governance was "moot" — they would say it was deferred, or held in the discipline of patient waiting, or carried as a wound. The word "moot" applies a modern academic dismissal to a question that was existentially central to the tradition.

*Severity:* P2 (implicit framing, not forbidden word).

*Suggested fix:* Replace "the question of who should rightfully govern was moot, because someone else was governing" with language that stays inside: "the question of who should rightfully govern was held in the patience that the tradition had built for exactly this condition — the imam was absent, someone else governed, and the faithful waited."

---

**P3 — Finding 2**

> "The Qizilbash, who believed their king was God, and the scholars, who believed their senior jurist was the imam's legal delegate, had arrived at a working arrangement."

*Type:* Borderline. The phrase "a working arrangement" is a modern diplomatic/bureaucratic formulation that slightly distances the text from the reality it describes. The Qizilbash did not think of their relationship to the scholars as "an arrangement." The scholars did not think of their relationship to the state as "working" in the administrative sense.

*Severity:* P3 (author decides).

*Note:* The phrase does serve the chapter's structural argument (showing the dual-legitimacy system operating as a system), and neither term is on the forbidden list. If the author keeps it, the framing is defensible. If the author replaces it, something like "had found a way to occupy the same state" would be less distancing.
