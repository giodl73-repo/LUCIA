# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Panel Review — Opening: La Violencia

**Round:** 1 (PANEL-1)
**Reviewer:** Ryszard Kapuściński (P-5)
**Chapter:** Colombia, Era 3: La Violencia — opening.md

---

I covered twenty-seven coups and revolutions. I was present for many of them. I know the smell of a city the moment before it decides to burn itself down. It is a specific smell — not smoke yet, but something electrical, a change in the air pressure of human intention. This opening has that smell. It earns it in the first paragraph and maintains it throughout.

"Diesel from the buses, grilled meat from the street carts, rain coming off the mountains." Three smells, three sentences, one street at midday. The opening is fully embodied before the bullet arrives. This is exactly right. You cannot feel the rupture if you have not first felt the normalcy. The Carrera Séptima exists before the shot. It has a temperature, a smell, a pace. The people on it are moving "slowly, warmly, with no particular urgency." Then the shot. Then the crowd turns. Then ten minutes. Then ten years.

The fires visible from the hills — "people in the barrios climbed to their rooftops and watched the city center glow orange" — this is the image I will remember from this opening. Not the shooting, not the politics, not the Castro cameo. The people on the rooftops, watching their city burn orange. This is what revolution looks like from the periphery: you climb to the highest point you can reach, and you watch, and you think something is beginning. You do not know yet that it is not beginning but continuing — that it has always been happening and will continue to happen and that the fires are only the most visible part of an ongoing condition.

The six-region geography of Colombia — the Andean interior, the Caribbean coast, the Pacific coast, the llanos, the Amazon — is not present in the opening. This is both right and slightly problematic. Right, because the opening is Bogotá and should feel like Bogotá — the high altitude, the cold mornings, the government ministries, the particular class of urban Colombians who read the papers and watch from boarding house windows. Problematic, because La Violencia was a terrain phenomenon: it operated differently in the coffee mountains than in the llanos, differently in the Caribbean lowlands than in the Andean valleys. The chapter will need to put the reader's body in those specific terrains.

The boarding house window at the end — García Márquez watching from his room — this is the right image to close with, but only if the chapter delivers what it promises. The man at the window sees the fire. The chapter must take us into the fire. Into Tolima in 1950, into the specific sound of a village being worked over by the pájaros, the hired killers. Into the sound of the radio announcements that told people which areas were currently under which party's control. Into the specific texture of living in a country where the wrong last name in the wrong town was a death sentence.

I want the chapter to smell like those places. The coffee plants in flower. The mud of the Magdalena valley. The gunpowder and the silence after. Colombia has extraordinary sensory specificity — the differences between its ecological zones are as dramatic as the differences between its political ones. The chapter should use this. Violence in the Andes is not the same as violence on the coast.

One specific flag: the opening describes Roa Sierra's fate — beaten to death on the sidewalk — without giving us the sound of it. I understand the restraint. But this opening is not squeamish in other ways. The ten-minute compression is brutal. The 200,000 is stated plainly. If the chapter is going to carry the weight of this period, it must be willing to put the reader inside the physical reality of what happened. Not the politics. The bodies.

**The question I'd push on:** Where are we, physically, when La Violencia is operating at full intensity? Not in Bogotá. In the interior. Give me the specific terrain — the altitude, the vegetation, the sound of the rivers, the layout of the villages — where the killing actually happened. The opening has placed us at the threshold. The chapter must take us through.
