# Notes — Era 1: O Lungo Drom (c. 1000-1300 CE)

## Sources

**Tier 1 — Primary:**
- The Romani language itself: Indo-Aryan core vocabulary, loanword strata (Persian, Armenian, Greek), grammatical features dating separation from the Indian linguistic continuum to c. 10th-11th century.
- Byzantine Greek references: the term "Athinganoi" in Byzantine chronicles, associated with fortune-telling and metalwork. The earliest probable reference: a hagiographic text from Mount Athos (c. 1068) describing Athinganoi as snake-charmers and sorcerers called to deal with wild animals in the imperial park.
- Firdowsi's Shahnameh (c. 1010 CE): the story of the Luri musicians brought from India by the Sassanid king Bahram Gur (5th century). Though the date of the story is much earlier, Firdowsi's retelling is contemporary with the probable departure period and may preserve cultural memory of Indian musicians' westward movement.
- Armenian chronicles: scattered references to traveling metalworkers and musicians passing through the Armenian highlands.

**Tier 2 — Academic:**
- Yaron Matras, *Romani: A Linguistic Introduction* (2002, Cambridge): definitive linguistic analysis of the Indian origin, loanword stratigraphy, and grammatical evidence for dating the departure.
- Ian Hancock, *We Are the Romani People* (2002): insider scholarly account; Hancock is himself Romani and a linguist. Essential for the inside-voice calibration.
- Ralph Lilley Turner, "The Position of Romani in Indo-Aryan" (1926): foundational paper establishing the linguistic link to Central Indo-Aryan languages.
- Angus Fraser, *The Gypsies* (1992, Blackwell): scholarly history of the Roma from Indian origins through European arrival.

**Tier 3 — Scholarly Secondary:**
- Isabel Fonseca, *Bury Me Standing* (1995): ethnographic journalism, useful for sensory detail and contemporary Romani voice.
- Donald Kenrick, *Historical Dictionary of the Gypsies (Romanies)* (2007).

**Source asymmetry note:** The Roma are a people documented primarily by outsiders until the 20th century. The inside voice comes through: (1) the language itself, (2) oral traditions (proverbs, songs, the Romani legal tradition of the kris), (3) modern Romani scholars like Hancock. The chapter must make this asymmetry visible and productive, not apologetic. Technique: language as primary source, outsider accounts as structural evidence about the outsiders' world, not the Roma's character.

## Key Figures

**Named figures are scarce for this era.** The Roma's departure from India and three-century journey to the Balkans produced no named individuals in the surviving record. The chapter must handle this honestly.

Approach (per v1.1 and v1.4 amendments — Honest Limits as Technique, Archaeological Figures):
- **The smith:** A composite figure drawn from the Dom/Romani metalworking tradition. Embodied, physically present, working with specific materials and techniques. Disclosed as composite in figures.md. This figure anchors the craft-knowledge sections.
- **The speaker:** A figure who carries the language. Not named, but embodied through the act of speaking — teaching a child words, negotiating at a market using borrowed vocabulary, telling a story. This figure anchors the linguistic sections.
- **Bahram Gur's musicians (from the Shahnameh):** A literary-legendary figure group that may preserve cultural memory of Indian musicians' migration. Used with honest disclosure that this is legend, not history, but legend that the receiving culture (Persian) found significant enough to record.
- **The Byzantine observer:** The unnamed chronicler at Mount Athos who describes the Athinganoi. Used as outsider-as-structural-evidence: what the observer notices tells us about the observer's categories, not the Roma's character.

## Narrative Arc

**Section 1 — The Word (opening, ~800w):** The Romani word "drom" and the linguistic evidence of Indian origin. The mystery of the departure. The language as the only witness. [Opening already written; will integrate with sensory revision per Kapuscinski/Tuchman feedback.]

**Section 2 — The Forge (~800w):** The Dom caste and the craft-knowledge the travelers carried. Metalworking as portable economy — a skill that makes you welcome wherever you stop. The smith at work: the sound of the hammer, the heat of the portable forge, the specific metals and techniques. The social position of the Dom in Indian caste hierarchy: essential but low-status, mobile by function. Sensory-analytical fusion: the forge's heat IS the economic argument.

**Section 3 — The Persian Road (~800w):** The journey through Persia (11th-12th century). Linguistic evidence of long residence: Persian loanwords clustering around commerce, measurement, authority. The Shahnameh story of Bahram Gur's musicians: legend as cultural memory. The landscape: the Iranian plateau, the caravan routes, the sound of the language changing as it absorbs new words. The structural question (Ibn Khaldun): what held them together? The familia (extended family) and the code of conduct that later becomes formalized as the kris.

**Section 4 — The Mountain Pass (~800w):** Through the Armenian highlands into Byzantine Anatolia. The Armenian loanwords mark this passage. The physical difficulty: mountain roads, winter, the body's experience of altitude and cold. The children in the group learning words their parents did not know as children. The first encounters with Christianity and the Byzantine administrative state. The travelers as they appear to the Byzantines: Athinganoi, outsiders who charm snakes and work metal and read palms.

**Section 5 — The Arrival (~800w):** The Balkans, 13th century. Greek loanwords flood the language: institutional vocabulary, market terms, church terminology. The travelers have been walking for three centuries. They have acquired skills, vocabulary, survival strategies from every place they passed through, but they have kept their core: the family structure, the language's Indo-Aryan heart, the name they use for themselves. Roma. People. The word from India that traveled the whole road without changing.

**Section 6 — Landing (~500w):** The road continues. The Roma are now in Europe, but Europe is not their destination — it is the next stretch of the road. The landing returns to the opening's word, drom, and to the structural engine: the road as the organizing principle of a people's existence. The language carries the journey. The journey carries the people. The sentence has not ended.

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** The portable culture — a complete civilization carried in language, craft, kinship, and music rather than in territory, architecture, or written records. The road is not displacement; it is the fundamental unit of Romani existence.

**Mechanism:** Each place the travelers pass through adds a layer to their culture (loanwords, skills, survival strategies) without replacing the core. The portable culture grows by accumulation, like sedimentary rock, while the core (family structure, Indo-Aryan vocabulary, social code) remains the bedrock. The culture's portability is its survival strategy: you cannot burn a language, you cannot confiscate a skill, you cannot demolish a kinship system.

**Terminal condition:** The engine does not terminate in this era. The portable culture arrives in Europe intact. The terminal condition for the broader Romani story is the Porajmos (Era 5), where the Nazi genocide attempts to destroy the people themselves, not their territory or their institutions, because the Roma have no territory or institutions to destroy. But for Era 1, the chapter ends with arrival, not termination. The engine is still running.

**Culture-specific terminal condition for this era:** The journey's "end" is not a destination but a transformation — the travelers become recognizable as a distinct European presence (the Athinganoi of Byzantine chronicles). The terminal condition is recognition: the moment outsiders begin to name them, which is also the moment persecution becomes possible. Being named by others is the first step toward being controlled by others.

## Panel Pressure Points

1. **Sensory grounding (Kapuscinski):** The opening is cerebral. The chapter must put the reader's body on the road: heat, dust, cold, the sound of the forge, the smell of the camp. Every section needs at least one strong sensory anchor. Section 2 (The Forge) and Section 4 (The Mountain Pass) are the primary sensory sections.

2. **Structural mechanism (Ibn Khaldun):** The engine must be named and operating by Section 3. What held them together? The familia, the code, the shared craft economy, the language itself as bond. The chapter must show this operating, not just assert it.

3. **Voice inhabitation (Achebe):** The voice should carry Romani speech rhythms where possible. Romani proverbs exist: "O drom si lungo" (the road is long), "Sar o cham, sar o balval, sar o brish" (like the sun, like the wind, like the rain — said of things that cannot be stopped). The prose should absorb these rhythms without becoming pastiche.

4. **Knowledge systems (Davis):** Metalworking, horse culture, music, herbal knowledge, fortune-telling as professional expertise. At least two of these must be shown at the method level — HOW they work, not just THAT they existed.

5. **Narrative pull (Tuchman):** The mystery of the departure provides the opening pull. The chapter needs additional pull mechanisms: the question of what held them together (answered in Section 3), the question of how the outside world received them (answered in Sections 4-5), and the question of whether the portable culture will survive Europe (opened in the landing, answered in subsequent eras).

## Open Questions

1. The Shahnameh story of Bahram Gur and the Luri musicians: how much weight can this bear? It is legend, not history, but it may preserve cultural memory of Indian musicians' westward movement. Use with disclosure.
2. The Dom/Roma connection: some scholars dispute whether the modern Roma are descended specifically from the Dom caste. Matras and Hancock support the connection; others are more cautious. Present the range.
3. The dating of the departure: Matras places it c. 1000 CE based on linguistic evidence. Some genetic studies suggest an earlier departure (c. 500-600 CE) with a bottleneck event around 1000 CE. Note the disagreement.
4. The route through Persia: was it a single migration or multiple waves? The linguistic evidence suggests prolonged contact with Persian, but this could mean either extended residence or multiple groups passing through over centuries. Present honestly.
5. The Athinganoi identification: the connection between Byzantine "Athinganoi" and the Roma is probable but not certain. Some scholars argue the Athinganoi were a distinct group. Use with appropriate caveat.
