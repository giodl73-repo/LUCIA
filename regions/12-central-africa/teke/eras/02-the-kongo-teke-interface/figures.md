# Figures — The Kongo-Teke Interface

## The Broker (Composite)

He has no name in the record. This is not an accident or a gap; it is a consequence of the commercial culture the chapter describes. The Teke broker's power rested on information asymmetry — on knowing things the other parties did not know. A man who wrote his name in a ledger had left evidence that could be used against him. A man whose transactions were conducted in the memory of the trade network, in the relationships built over seasons, in the knowledge held in hands and eyes rather than on paper — this man was invisible to exactly the parties he needed to be invisible to. The absence of individual names in the Teke broker record is not a failure of documentation; it is a feature of a commercial culture that had good reasons for not documenting.

Jan Vansina, whose *The Tio Kingdom of the Middle Congo 1880–1892* is the most thorough scholarly reconstruction of Teke political culture, notes that the commercial class maintained their positions across generations through transmitted knowledge rather than fixed social status — any man who could learn the routes and the prices and the relationships could become a broker, and the brokers who survived were the ones who were best at keeping their knowledge proprietary. This makes individual reconstruction difficult and composite construction necessary.

The composite figure in this chapter is built from Vansina's description of the commercial class's characteristics: the training in commodity identification, the network of simultaneous buyer relationships, the practice of gathering market intelligence during hospitality exchanges. The specific details of the copper authentication sequence — the weight, the red-brown surface color, the sound when struck — are reconstructed practices based on general knowledge of how commodity authentication functioned in pre-literate Central African commercial cultures. They are not specifically documented as Teke practices in the scholarly record; they are consistent with the commercial culture Vansina describes and with the general authentication methods documented in neighboring trade systems. The reconstruction is offered as historically plausible inference, not documented fact. The broker's interior voice — transactional, world-weary, emotionally contained — is drawn from Vansina's account of the commercial ethic: a man who showed strong preferences was a man who had given information away, and information was the resource that could not be given away.

The broker's name would not have survived even if his transactions did. This is the tradition the chapter honors by leaving the name empty.

## The Makoko (Title, Not Name)

The Makoko is the title of the Teke paramount chief, not the name of any individual holder. Across the three centuries this era covers, many Makokkos held the position, and the scholarly record does not preserve a reliable sequence of named incumbents for most of this period. The Makoko who appears in this chapter — sitting in the receiving court at Mbe, hearing the Kongo agent's proposal about the plateau station, explaining in quiet language what the plateau's terms were — is a type figure representing the institution of the Makoko's authority rather than a documented individual.

What is documented is the institution itself. The Makoko's authority rested on the concept of the onkani — the land's spiritual essence, understood not as a supernatural force external to the political but as the plateau's actual nature, which the Makoko held in trust for the people who lived on it. This is Vansina's account, and it is supported by comparative evidence from neighboring Central African political theologies. The Makoko did not own the land in a property sense; he was the land's representative in human governance, which is a different claim and a more powerful one. Property can be transferred; representation cannot.

The receiving court scene at Mbe is reconstructed from Vansina's description of the Makoko's compound and receiving practices as recorded in the 1880s period. Whether these practices were identical in the 1600s is not certain; the chapter has used the 1880s documentation as the baseline and projected it backward with appropriate caution, on the grounds that the institutional structure Vansina documents had been in place for several generations by the time his informants described it, and that the structural logic of the Makoko's authority — spiritual rather than military — is consistent with the Teke's position across the entire era.

## The Forest Trader (Composite)

The forest man who arrives with copper at the chapter's opening is representative of the class of traders who moved between the Congo Basin interior and the plateau market. The specific goods — Katanga cross-shaped copper ingots, wrapped in leaves and loaded in wicker frames on carriers' heads — are historically attested. The cross-shaped copper ingot (handa) is documented across the Central African trade network as a form of currency and commodity from at least the sixteenth century; Katanga copper is the well-known source, though the plateau market also received copper from other sources that the brokers could distinguish by quality.

The forest trader's social position is more complex than his functional role suggests. He was not simply a middleman below the Teke broker; he was himself a specialist, a man with knowledge of forest road networks, interior pricing, and the specific relationships with the forest chieftaincies that produced the copper, ivory, and other goods that the plateau market depended on. The hierarchy of the trade ran: forest interior producers, forest traders, Teke plateau brokers, Pool market brokers, coastal merchants, and finally the Atlantic system. Each link had its own leverage and its own knowledge. The forest trader who handed over the ingot without being asked was performing his own assessment of the broker's competence — the gesture communicated "I am a professional; treat me as one."

The forest traders who moved enslaved people to the Pool market in the later part of this era occupy the same structural position in the trade as the traders who moved copper — carrying goods that others had produced, to a market where the price was set by forces several steps removed from the forest. The chapter does not alter its voice when it reaches this point. The forest trader's calculation did not change; the commodity did.

**Cross-reference note (Loango coast parallel):** The Teke were not unique as Central African broker peoples. The Loango coast trade system, running north of the Kongo River mouth and documented in Phyllis Martin's *The External Trade of the Loango Coast 1576–1870*, was itself a significant interior-to-coast brokerage network with its own plateau intermediaries and Pool-equivalent junctions. The Teke operated within a competitive landscape of multiple Central African broker systems, not as an isolated case. Their specific position — the Bateke Plateau, the Malebo Pool, the Kongo-Teke commercial relationship — was distinctive, but the structural logic of the broker position (geographic intermediary, information asymmetry, spiritual-commercial authority over access routes) was a pattern repeated across the Atlantic face of Central Africa.

## Kongo Provincial Agents (Institutional)

The agents of the Manikongo's provincial governors who appear in this chapter are institutional figures rather than named individuals — the human face of a state relationship that the Teke chapter experiences as a permanent pressure from the south. The Kongo kingdom's interest in the interior trade routes is well-documented from the Kongo side: royal correspondence from the sixteenth century (including the remarkable letters of Afonso I) discusses the importance of interior trade goods and the difficulty of controlling the trade routes through territory that the kingdom did not effectively govern.

From the Teke side, the Kongo relationship was managed through the same commercial logic that the plateau market applied to everyone: the Kongo agents could buy at Teke prices or not buy at all. The Kongo attempt to establish a permanent station on the plateau, as described in this chapter, is not a documented specific incident but is consistent with the Kongo state's documented patterns of attempting to extend its commercial reach into interior territories and meeting the Teke's documented pattern of deflecting such attempts through the combination of the Makoko's spiritual authority and commercial leverage.

The specific tactic described — the broker's explanation that the Kongo station would find the forest traders unwilling to engage with it, the roads unknown to Kongo agents, the relationships unavailable to outsiders — is drawn from Vansina's account of how the Teke maintained their commercial monopoly on the plateau routes. It is presented as institutional behavior rather than documented incident.
