# GATE-1 Scorecard — "Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad" Opening

**Scorer:** LUCIA Rubric v1.3
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Scores

| Dimension | Score | Evidence |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| Scene     | 5/5   | The opening drops into a wholly specific moment: a letter arriving in Katsina, carried by a student who walked three days, delivered to a named scholar in a compound near the central market. The first line is pure scene: "The letter arrived in Katsina before the rains." By the second paragraph, the reader has a person (Umaru Dallaji), a place (the compound near the tanned-leather market), and an action (breaking the seal, reading). No abstraction, no preamble. |
| World     | 5/5   | The culture's own categories do all the work. The Hausa sarakuna, the Torodbe scholarly community, the fiqh-based diagnosis of illegitimate governance, the Shehu's hijra, the Maliki jurisprudence, bid'a, the Sharia's standards for taxation. None of these are explained from outside. The reader enters a world where Islamic legal reasoning is the operating system and where the distinction between lawful and unlawful rule is drawn by scholars, not by armies. The green flag carries its own gravity. The pastoral Fulani network is rendered as a political reality: "those families had cattle and those cattle meant wealth that the sarakuna taxed and the scholars said the sarakuna had no right to tax." The world's logic generates the action. |
| Honesty   | 5/5   | No outside judgment anywhere. The jihad is presented entirely within its own theological and legal framework. The sarakuna are not demonized; they are described through the Shehu's legal critique. Dan Fodio is not romanticized; he is a scholar who spent thirty years writing treatises. The flag-holders are not revolutionaries in a modern sense; they are scholars consulting with students and assessing local conditions. The word "revolution" appears once but is immediately qualified by "distributed across a network of books," which reframes it within the scholarly tradition. The Fulani code of munyal is named as the virtue that restrained action, not as anthropological decoration. The culture is so fully itself that no external vantage point is available to the reader. |
| Texture   | 4/5   | Specific and grounded: "tanned leather and ground pepper" in the Katsina market; the rhythmic thud of leather-tanners' mallets over mud-brick walls; the green cloth bundle guarded against the student's chest; the dye pits of Kano where indigo cloth was beaten and folded. The dry scrub of Gobir, the sealed letter in careful Arabic hand. These details anchor the reader in a physical world. Held from a 5 because the middle paragraphs, where the flag distribution is described across multiple cities, become more cartographic than sensory. The texture recedes as the geographic scope widens, and the return to the compound at the close only partially recovers the grounding. |
| Voice     | 5/5   | The prose rhythm is distinctive and sustained: measured, deliberate, building through accretion like a legal argument. Short declarative sentences carry structural weight: "And inside the green cloth bundle was a second flag." "He had raised the flag." "Not yet. But soon." The longer sentences accumulate clauses the way a scholar's treatise accumulates evidence: "a scholar in a distant city who had been reading the same texts, attending the same arguments, arriving at the same conclusions for years." The voice belongs to this story. It is neither lyrical nor martial but precisely what a scholarly revolution would sound like if the prose matched its subject: patient, specific, building toward an inevitable conclusion. |
| Pull      | 5/5   | The opening creates genuine, specific tension. The flag is on the shelf. The students will arrive soon. The leather-tanners are beating hides. The choice has been made but not yet enacted. The reader knows the jihad is coming but does not know what it will look like when it arrives in this city, with this scholar, in this compound. The closing two lines create a pull that is internal to the story and cannot be satisfied without reading further. The momentum is not spectacle; it is the quiet interval between conviction and action, and it is specific to this culture's mode of revolution. |
| **Total** | **29/30** | |

## Overall Assessment

This is a superb opening that succeeds most fully in its world-building and its voice. The scholarly-revolutionary mode is perfectly calibrated: the flag arrives not as a military signal but as the culmination of decades of legal argument. The mechanism of the jihad is made visible in its first moments. The texture is strong but not uniformly sustained across the geographic widening in the middle paragraphs. The pull is earned and specific, the honesty is total, and the voice never wavers.
