## First-Pass Notes (reading straight through)

I read it twice. The first time I did not want to stop at the section breaks. That is the right problem to have.

The opening is structurally confident in a way that surprised me — it uses a refrain ("This is how constitutional precedents are made") as a pivot mechanism rather than a decorative flourish, and it earns the repetition. The de Montfort turn in the third section is the best moment: a man who loses his head for calling a Parliament is made the unwitting constitutional architect. The reader understands immediately that the chapter's subject is not Edward I — it is the gap between what men intend and what they cause.

This is narrative intelligence. The author has identified the story inside the history.

## Where the Arc Lives

Setup: The writ, the Latin phrase, the need.
Complication: The mechanism is older than Edward — de Montfort planted it bleeding in a ditch.
Turn: "No one can un-practice it."
Resolution (anticipated): The Parliament becomes what no one meant it to become.

The arc is clear in the opening and does not need to be stated. The author trusts it. Good.

## What Works

The two-sentence paragraph — "This is how constitutional precedents are made. Not by vision. By need." — is the chapter's thesis delivered in the register of the culture's own legal reasoning. Not analysis grafted onto narrative. Analysis as narrative. This is exactly right.

The de Montfort paragraph is paced correctly. The death ("his body was quartered, his head sent to his enemies") is reported without flinch and without lingering. One sentence. Then the argument. The proportions are controlled.

"The law was not something Edward had invented. It was something he had found — alive, growing, resistant to his own will when he tested it — and had learned to use." This is the chapter's engine stated at the level of character. Edward as discoverer rather than legislator. That is a distinction that will carry the full chapter.

## What Needs Attention

**The statutes passage.** "The Statute of Westminster in 1275, the Statute of Mortmain in 1279, the Statute of Winchester in 1285 — each one taking a custom, a practice, a feudal obligation..." This is a list. It is accurate and it matters, but it reads like a reference section that has not yet been converted into narrative. In the opening, lists lose momentum. The author needs to either pick one statute and render it as scene, or compress the list into a single sentence and move on.

**The burgesses' introduction.** "They sat separately from the great lords, separately from the bishops and abbots, at the far end of the hall where the acoustics were worse and the ceremony less gilded." This is excellent texture — the detail about acoustics is vivid. But the paragraph that follows pivots immediately to abstract constitutional principle. The chapter should spend one more sentence in the hall. What did they look like? What did they carry? Let the reader see them before they become an argument.

**The close.** "The Parliament had been called. It would be called again." I like the economy. But the close is slightly ahead of where the opening has placed us — it states an outcome the reader has not yet been given reason to feel. The opening earns the constitutional argument but not quite the emotional weight of that final repetition. Consider whether the final section needs one more concrete image before the close.

## The Question I'd Push On

The opening establishes beautifully that the Parliament was a transaction — consent for money — that became something else. But what does it feel like to be the knight from Shropshire, riding to Westminster with the county's grievances folded in his saddlebag, who has no idea he is at the founding of parliamentary democracy? The opening gives us Edward's perspective (need-driven, managerial, legally sophisticated) and the constitutional retrospective perspective. The commoner's perspective — the man who consented without knowing what he was establishing — is the missing register. The full chapter will need him.

---

**Overall:** Strong. The arc is there. The voice is found. The statutes list and the close are the two technical problems. The chapter will be excellent if it holds this register at length.
