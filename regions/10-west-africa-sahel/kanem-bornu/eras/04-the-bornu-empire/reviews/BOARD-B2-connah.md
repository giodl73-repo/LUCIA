## B-2 — Graham Connah — Board Review of "The Bornu Empire"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Archaeologist of the Lake Chad region, author of *Three Thousand Years in Africa: Man and His Environment in the Lake Chad Region of Nigeria* (1981). Specialist in the material culture of Ngazargamu, the Sao mound sites, and the paleoecology of Lake Chad.

**Historical accuracy:**

- Ngazargamu's description as "a walled capital" with "compounds built of mud-brick and timber" is consistent with the archaeological evidence. The site's remains show a substantial walled settlement with evidence of mud-brick construction and timber framing.
- The description of wall construction ("mud-brick mixed with chaff and water, pressed into wooden molds, dried in rows under the sun") is plausible for the region's building tradition. The specific details are generic to Sahelian mud-brick construction rather than specific to excavated evidence at Ngazargamu, but they are reasonable.
- The claim that Ngazargamu was "the largest city between the Niger bend and the Nile for its time" is defensible for the period of Alooma's reign, though it depends on how one defines "city" and what estimates one uses for competing settlements.
- The population figure of "perhaps a hundred thousand people" is at the high end of what the archaeological evidence can support. The walled area of Ngazargamu was substantial, but population estimates based on site area are imprecise. **Recommend softening to "tens of thousands" or noting the uncertainty more explicitly.** (Concurs with B-1.)
- The Sao are mentioned correctly as "that older people of the lake basin whose mound-building cities and iron-working traditions predated the Saifawa by centuries." This is consistent with the archaeological record. The mound sites around Lake Chad do show evidence of pre-Saifawa occupation with iron-working and distinctive pottery traditions.
- The oral tradition description of the Sao as "giants and builders of impossible things" is documented in the ethnographic literature and is appropriately framed as oral memory.
- Lake Chad's seasonal fluctuation is correctly described. The lake's behavior ("swelling in the wet season... shrinking in the dry") matches the paleoecological record, though the amplitude of fluctuation varied significantly across centuries.
- The quilted cotton armor is well-attested in the material culture record and in illustrations from the period. The description of it as "layers of cotton compressed and stitched, heavy enough to stop an arrow" is consistent with surviving examples and scholarly descriptions. The claim that horses were also armored is correct.

**Cultural authenticity:**

- The material culture descriptions are appropriate and grounded. The mud-brick construction, the market layout, the timber framing are all consistent with what the archaeology reveals about Sahelian urban settlements.
- The Lake Chad ecology is treated as a dynamic factor shaping political decisions, which is the correct approach. The lake's fluctuation was not background scenery; it directly affected agriculture, pastoralism, fisheries, and the military defensibility of lakeside positions.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The founding of Ngazargamu as a deliberate construction project, not a refugee camp, is consistent with the archaeological evidence of planned urban layout.
- The description of the tributary system's physical manifestation (chiefs arriving with gifts, the chain of intermediaries) gives the political structure material reality.
- The Lake Chad ecology as a legal problem (the fluctuating shore creating disputes) is a sophisticated observation that reflects the real intersection of ecology and governance in the region.
- The decline narrative in Section V is grounded in material evidence: the market shrinking, the compounds emptying, the lake pulling back.

**What needs correction:**

- **Population estimate.** "Perhaps a hundred thousand people" should be softened. "Tens of thousands" is more defensible. (Concurs with B-1.)
- **The Sao mound sites** are mentioned but not located. The mound sites are concentrated around the southern and western shores of Lake Chad. A brief geographic anchor would strengthen the reference.
- **"The walls crumbled to mounds of packed earth that the rains flattened over generations until only the archaeologists could read them."** This is a poetically effective line but somewhat misleading. The remains of Ngazargamu, while degraded, are more legible than "mounds of packed earth" suggests. Wall foundations, compound outlines, and the city plan are recoverable from the archaeological record. The line overstates the degree of erasure.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

The chapter's material culture is well-grounded and the ecological treatment of Lake Chad is a genuine strength. The population estimate needs softening, and the description of Ngazargamu's remains in the final section slightly overstates the degree of archaeological illegibility. With these corrections, the chapter is defensible.
