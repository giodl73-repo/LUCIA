## Baseline Voice

Established in first paragraph: prophetic-migratory register. Medium-to-long gathering sentences that accumulate toward a short declarative. The rhythm of a person describing something they know with absolute certainty and no urgency — the certainty is the point, not the speed. Sparse punctuation; zero em-dashes in the opening confirms the voice relies on the period. Sensory detail embedded in motion, not as landscape description separate from action.

## Voice-Random-Paragraph Test

Three paragraphs selected at random:

**Paragraph 1 (corridor network section):** "This was the corridor's logic: not a road but a living obligation network, maintained by the accumulated weight of what had been given and what was owed. Every community that moved east along it both consumed the network and extended it — using the knowledge deposited by those who had gone before, depositing knowledge for those who would come after."

Voice check: The colon, the accumulating parallel structure ("consumed...extended"), the period landing on the exchange logic. Register is consistent. Identifiable as this chapter's voice. PASS.

**Paragraph 2 (ñe'ë ceremony section):** "What the ceremony did — what it was meant to do, what it accomplished in the body of each participant — was this: it reminded the ñe'ë of its name."

Voice check: The repeated interruption ("what it was meant to do, what it accomplished") is uncharacteristic of the chapter's gathering rhythm — it introduces a stutter where the voice normally flows. This is a P2 finding. The em-dash pair is also the only em-dash pair in the chapter, creating a local anomaly. **Recommended fix:** "What the ceremony did — what it accomplished in the body of each participant — was this" becomes "The ceremony did one thing. It reminded the ñe'ë of its name." The shorter construction is cleaner and more consistent with the prophetic-declarative rhythm.

**Paragraph 3 (ocean arrival section):** "The sound had been present for some time — she understood that now, looking back — but she had heard it as distant rain, as something happening to the forest and not to her."

Voice check: Two em-dashes in a sentence. The voice has established zero em-dashes as its rhythm. These are the second and third em-dashes in the chapter. **Recommended fix:** Break into two sentences: "The sound had been present for some time. She understood that now, looking back: she had heard it as distant rain, as something happening to the forest and not to her." The colon maintains the retrospective quality without the em-dash interruption.

## Full Scan Findings

**Finding 1 — P2**
"What the ceremony did — what it was meant to do, what it accomplished in the body of each participant — was this: it reminded the ñe'ë of its name."
Nature: Em-dash pair; rhythm interruption. The chapter's voice does not use em-dash interruptions elsewhere.
Fix: "The ceremony did one thing. It reminded the ñe'ë of its name. The divine word in each person was always in danger of forgetting itself."

**Finding 2 — P2**
"The sound had been present for some time — she understood that now, looking back — but she had heard it as distant rain..."
Nature: Em-dash pair; second and third em-dashes in the chapter.
Fix: "The sound had been present for some time. She understood that now, looking back: she had heard it as distant rain, as something happening to the forest and not to her."

**Finding 3 — P3**
"By the late twelfth century, Tupinambá communities had established themselves at intervals along the Brazilian coast..."
Nature: The phrase "By the late twelfth century" is a dating construction that reads slightly more academic than the chapter's usual voice (which anchors in sensory/experiential time rather than calendar time). Not a P1 or P2 — the voice recovers immediately in the following clause. Author decides whether to rephrase as "In the generations after the first arrivals..." or similar.
