# Production Bible
## Elam Era 1: The Proto-Elamite and Old Elamite Periods (c. 3200–1500 BCE)

---

## 1. Sources

### Tier 1 — Primary
- Proto-Elamite tablets (thousands held at Louvre, British Museum, Musée du Louvre — corpus partially catalogued at CDLI: cuneiform.library.cornell.edu)
- Linear Elamite inscriptions — including the Gunagi inscription and the inscriptions from Susa; partial
- Old Elamite treaty texts — including the Treaty between Naram-Sin of Akkad and an Elamite ruler (c. 2250 BCE), among the oldest known written treaties
- Elamite royal inscriptions from the sukkalmah period (in Akkadian — the court language before cuneiform Elamite was formalized)
- Mesopotamian sources mentioning Elam: the Sumerian King List (Elam as conqueror after the flood); hymns to Shulgi mentioning Elamite campaigns; the Lament for the Destruction of Ur, c. 2000 BCE

### Tier 2 — Academic
- Potts, D.T. *The Archaeology of Elam: Formation and Transformation of an Ancient Iranian State.* Cambridge University Press, 1999. (Definitive archaeological survey)
- Álvarez-Mon, Javier, Gian Pietro Basello, and Yasmina Wicks, eds. *The Elamite World.* Routledge, 2018. (Comprehensive scholarly volume)
- Dahl, Jacob L. *The Ruling Family of Ur III Umma.* 2007. (On the administrative overlap between Elam and Mesopotamia)
- Englund, Robert K. "The State of Decipherment of Proto-Elamite." 2004. (Definitive assessment of where decipherment stands)

### Tier 3 — Scholarly Secondary
- Pittman, Holly. "Constructing Context: The Gebel el-Arak Knife." 1996. (On proto-Elamite/Mesopotamian contact)
- Carter, Elizabeth and Matthew Stolper. *Elam: Surveys of Political History and Archaeology.* University of California Press, 1984.

### Source asymmetry note
This era has a genuine source-asymmetry challenge: the oldest material (proto-Elamite, c. 3100–2600 BCE) is abundant in quantity but wholly unreadable. The Old Elamite period (c. 2700–1500 BCE) has readable texts but most are in Akkadian (the diplomatic/administrative lingua franca), not in Elamite itself. The sukkalmah period (c. 1850–1500 BCE) is known primarily through Mesopotamian sources — the Elamites' own texts from this period are thin. The chapter should use the **source gap as cultural honesty** technique (v1.5): acknowledge what we cannot read, make the silence part of the story.

## 2. The Structural Engine

**Engine:** The highland-lowland axis — the ecological and political interdependence between Susa (Khuzestan plain, grain surplus, Mesopotamian trade interface) and Anshan (Fars highlands, metals, pastoralists, Iranian plateau connections). The dual kingdom is not a compromise; it is the correct organizational response to the geography. Neither pole is complete without the other.

**Mechanism:** The dual kingdom functions as a redistributive system: grain flows up to the highlands, metals and specialized goods flow down to the plain. The scribal system is the nervous system of this redistribution — the record that keeps track of what moved where and how much. Whoever controls the writing controls the exchange. The sukkalmah ("great regent") title encodes the mediation function: the sukkalmah stands between the two poles.

**Terminal condition:** The sukkalmah system (c. 1850–1500 BCE) appears to end through a legitimacy gap combined with external pressure. By the mid-second millennium BCE, Kassite Babylonia has consolidated the Mesopotamian plain (the Kassites took Babylon c. 1595 BCE, stabilizing after the Hittite raid), and the Mitanni are pressing from the northwest. Elam faces simultaneous commercial-diplomatic competition from a now-unified Babylonian plain and internal succession pressures within the sukkalmah system. The dual-pole structure that had been the engine of stability becomes a liability when both poles face external pressure simultaneously — the highland cannot reinforce the lowland fast enough, and the lowland's connection to the Mesopotamian trade network weakens. The result is not a single dramatic collapse but a decades-long erosion of the sukkalmah's mediating authority, after which Elam reemerges in the Middle Elamite period under a new dynastic form (the Igihalkids, c. 1500 BCE) with a different organizational logic.

**Terminal condition type:** Legitimacy gap — the sukkalmah's mediating authority cannot be sustained when the two poles face different external pressures simultaneously. The redistribution mechanism that made the dual kingdom work also made it fragile: it required continuous successful mediation. When mediation fails, the system loses its legitimating logic.

## 3. Voice Register

**Sedimentary/administrative:** The voice that mirrors both alluvial deposits and a tablet archive growing. Heavy, wet, specific. Accumulative sentences build like account entries, layer by layer. Short declarative theological statements land with the weight of carved pronouncements. The rhythm mirrors the culture that kept meticulous records of the gods' grain and used the same system to manage the entirety of a complex redistribution economy.

**Zero em-dashes.** The sedimentary voice uses the period for weight, the semicolon for accumulation, the colon for specification. Em-dashes break the layering rhythm.

## 4. Key Figures

### The anonymous proto-Elamite scribe (composite figure)
No individual scribes are named from this period — the tablets have not been deciphered. The opening's anonymous scribe is a composite figure drawn from the scribal school tradition visible in the tablets' uniformity (the same signs appearing across hundreds of kilometers implies trained scribal networks). Honest Limits technique: the chapter must acknowledge that we cannot name him, and that the anonymity itself tells us something — that the institution was larger than any individual in it.

### Enmebaragesi (Sumerian king, c. 2600 BCE) — mentioned only as context
The Sumerian King List records that Elam was once "smitten with weapons" and the kingship was brought to Kish. This is the Mesopotamian memory of early Elamite power. We do not have the Elamite side of this account — it is a source asymmetry moment to be handled with honesty.

### The sukkalmah (regent) — generalized figure
The title *sukkalmah* ("great regent") appears in the Old Elamite period alongside *sukkal of Elam and Simashki* (the highland regent) and *sukkal of Susa* (the lowland regent). The system was explicitly tripartite — designed for succession. The sukkalmah of Elam and Simashki was the supreme authority; the other two were subordinate but hereditary. This structure can be shown through a specific sukkalmah: **Atta-hushu** (c. 1945–1905 BCE) is documented in texts — he signed a treaty with the city of Ur and is one of the earliest Old Elamite rulers we can name and date with some confidence.

### Puzur-Inshushinak (c. 2100 BCE)
The governor of Susa who briefly unified Elam during the collapse of the Akkadian Empire and declared independence. He wrote royal inscriptions in both Akkadian and in Linear Elamite — a distinct and still-undeciphered script separate from proto-Elamite — and built extensively at Susa. His reign is a key moment: Elam asserting its own political and scribal identity at the moment of Mesopotamian weakness. His inscriptions are the only substantial Linear Elamite texts we have.

### Kindattu (c. 2004 BCE)
The Elamite ruler who destroyed the Third Dynasty of Ur — the Sumerian empire that had been the dominant power in Mesopotamia. The Lament for the Destruction of Ur names the Elamites and their allies as the destroyers. This is one of the most dramatic events of the era. Kindattu is named in Sumerian sources; the Elamite sources are lost. Handle as source asymmetry: we have the victim's lament, not the conqueror's account.

## 5. Narrative Arc and Section Plan

**Section 1 — The Tablet and the System (opening integrated)**
The anonymous scribe; proto-Elamite as a knowledge system; the highland-lowland axis established. Ends with the silence of the undeciphered script.

**Section 2 — The Dual Kingdom's Logic**
Susa and Anshan as the two poles. The redistribution economy in operation: grain, metals, the goods that travel along the Zagros passes and the Persian Gulf routes. Puzur-Inshushinak asserting Elamite independence c. 2100 BCE — the moment when Elam writes its own royal inscriptions, in its own script, asserting itself as a civilization on its own terms. Linear Elamite: another script, also undeciphered. Two writing systems in one era, both still silent.

**Section 3 — The Destruction of Ur**
Kindattu and the fall of Ur III, c. 2004 BCE. Handle through the Lament for the Destruction of Ur — the Sumerian text is one of the oldest surviving works of literature. The reader hears the victim's voice. The Elamite voice is silent. This is the source asymmetry as narrative technique: we know what Elam did by what the other side said about it. Show this explicitly.

**Section 4 — The Sukkalmah System**
Atta-hushu and the sukkalmah dynasty. The tripartite succession system as institutional design. This was not improvised — it was the Elamite solution to the problem of dual-kingdom succession. Show it operating: the highland regent, the lowland regent, the great regent above both. The system's logic is its attempt to institutionalize the highland-lowland balance.

**Section 5 — The Inshushinak Temple and the God's Logic**
Chogha Zanbil (Dur-Untash) will be built in Era 2, but the theology of Inshushinak — lord of Susa, judge of the dead — shapes this era's administrative system from the start. The grain the scribe counts is the god's grain. The temple is the god's house. The redistribution is not economics; it is theology made administrative. Show the ziggurat of Susa (Inshushinak's own precinct) as the physical form of this theology.

**Section 6 — The Fading of the Sukkalmah (Landing)**
The sukkalmah system erodes. Not a dramatic collapse — the records thin, the royal inscriptions become sparse, the Kassite consolidation of Babylonia changes the plain's commercial logic. The dual kingdom that endured Sumer, endured Akkad, endured Ur III — endures even now, in the sense that it persists as an organizational memory that the Middle Elamite kingdom will reconstitute in new form. Close on the archive at Susa: the tablets are still there, still unread, still recording the grain of a god whose name we know but whose voice, like his scribes', has gone quiet.

## 6. Panel Pressure Points Anticipated

**Tuchman:** The middle section (dual kingdom logic) risks becoming expository. Solution: anchor each section in a specific figure — Puzur-Inshushinak writing in Linear Elamite, Kindattu's army entering Ur, Atta-hushu pressing his seal on the treaty with Ur's representatives. Every structural argument should be carried by a person doing something.

**Ibn Khaldun:** The terminal condition must be made explicit in the text. The erosion of sukkalmah authority is not a mystery — it is a structural process. Name the mechanism: the dual-pole system requires successful mediation; when mediation fails, the legitimating logic fails with it.

**Achebe:** Remove the phrase "scholars believe" and any equivalent from the chapter. When speaking of what the tablets record, speak from within the act of their creation, not from the vantage of modern archaeologists. Exception: at the moments of acknowledged uncertainty (the undeciphered scripts), state the uncertainty directly as the culture's own silence, not as a scholarly inference.

**Davis:** Show the scribal knowledge system at method level. Describe the training — not as background but as a scene: an apprentice learning signs, the teacher's hands guiding the stylus, the first tablet that is correct enough to be archived. This is how you render a knowledge system at the level of HOW IT WORKS.

**Kapuscinski:** Render Chogha Zanbil (or the Susa ziggurat precinct) at the level of physical presence. Scale, mass, the shadow it casts in the morning light. The archive's smell — clay and what else? The incense that never left the temple precinct.

## 7. Open Questions

1. **The proto-Elamite geographic network:** What held the scribal system together across such vast distances? Was there a central training authority at Susa, or did the system self-replicate through trading networks? Current scholarship (Englund) suggests the system spread through the same trade networks that it administered — the script was inseparable from the goods it recorded. Use this: the writing traveled with the goods.

2. **The Linear Elamite decipherment:** As of 2022, a partial decipherment of Linear Elamite has been proposed by François Desset and colleagues. It remains contested, but if correct, it suggests Linear Elamite is related to proto-Elamite and may preserve some of the royal ideology of Puzur-Inshushinak's inscriptions. Note this carefully — it is in motion and the chapter should treat it with appropriate uncertainty.

3. **The sukkalmah's relationship to Mesopotamian diplomatic culture:** The sukkalmah appears to have used Akkadian for diplomatic correspondence and Elamite for internal administration. This bilingualism is not insignificant — it is the Elamite state choosing to interface with the wider world in the world's diplomatic language while maintaining its own for internal use. A parallel to modern situations where languages serve different functional registers.

4. **Inshushinak as judge of the dead:** The theological role of the lord of Susa in Elamite belief has parallels with other Near Eastern underworld judges. This matters for how the redistribution economy was understood from within — what does it mean that the god who receives the grain is also the god who weighs the dead?
