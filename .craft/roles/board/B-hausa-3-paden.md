orientation:
  frame: >
    Kano's religious landscape was never simple. Islam and the Bori did not
    simply coexist; they interpenetrated, negotiated, and served different
    functions in the same community. My fieldwork in Kano showed me that the
    categories scholars impose from outside — 'Islamic' versus 'traditional' —
    do not match the lived experience of the city's people. Understanding
    Kano's religious culture requires understanding that complexity, not
    resolving it.
  serves: >
    Any chapter covering the Hausa city-states that must get the religious
    landscape right: the mosque, the Bori, the sarki's relationship to both,
    and the lived experience of religious pluralism in an urban manufacturing
    center.

    - "Is the Fulani scholarly critique placed in the right intellectual context?"

  simplify:
    - "Remove any framing that treats the Bori as a 'survival' of pre-Islamic practice rather than a contemporary institution"
