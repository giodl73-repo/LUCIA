# Haltamti — The Land of God and Power

We wrote our own name in our own script: *Haltamti* — the land of God and power, the ancient kingdom in the Zagros foothills and the Khuzestan plain that watched the cities of Mesopotamia rise and competed with them, traded with them, raided them, and absorbed their culture while giving them back an older one. We were literate before most of our neighbors, writing in a script that preceded cuneiform in some forms and ran parallel to it in others. Proto-Elamite tablets — the earliest writing from the Iranian plateau — appear around 3100 BCE, almost simultaneously with the earliest Sumerian writing, and no one has yet deciphered them.

The Mesopotamian world called us *Elam* — which comes from a Sumerian word, the outsiders' name — and they remembered us as the enemy who came down from the mountains. Sumerians called us their nemesis; Babylonians spent generations fighting us; the Assyrians attacked Susa and destroyed it. But we endured all of them. The Elamite kingdom outlasted Sumer, outlasted the Akkadian empire, outlasted the Third Dynasty of Ur that we ourselves destroyed in 2004 BCE. For three thousand years we were the eastern wall against which Mesopotamian ambitions broke, and the platform on which a new civilization — the Iranian plateau cultures that culminated in Achaemenid Persia — would eventually build.

## What Defined Them

**The dual kingdom and the highland-lowland axis.** The Elamite state was organized around two poles: Susa on the Khuzestan plain, the lowland administrative center where the scribes wrote and the merchants traded, and Anshan in the Fars highlands, the heartland of Iranian cultural identity. The tension between these two poles — lowland agriculture and upland pastoralism, Mesopotamian cultural influence and Iranian cultural tradition — ran through Elamite history from beginning to end. The royal title "King of Anshan and Susa" encoded this duality: neither pole alone was the kingdom; only together did they constitute it. The Achaemenid Persians, who used the Elamite administrative tradition as their bureaucratic foundation, were themselves from Anshan.

**Inshushinak and Napirisha — the gods of Susa.** The great gods of the Elamite world were Inshushinak, the lord of Susa and judge of the dead, and Napirisha, the great god of the highland. The ziggurat of Chogha Zanbil, built by Untash-Napirisha in the 13th century BCE, was dedicated to both deities and was one of the largest religious structures in the ancient Near East. The Elamite religious world was distinct from Mesopotamian religion while deeply influenced by it: Elamite temples followed Mesopotamian architectural forms, Elamite scribes learned to write Sumerian and Akkadian, but the gods they served and the mythology they maintained were distinctly their own.

**The Elamite script tradition.** Elam produced three distinct writing systems over its history: Proto-Elamite (still undeciphered, from c. 3100 BCE), Linear Elamite (also undeciphered), and Cuneiform Elamite (used for administrative texts in the Mesopotamian-influenced cuneiform tradition). The first Achaemenid royal inscriptions at Persepolis were trilingual — Old Persian, Babylonian, and Elamite — because Elamite was the existing administrative language of the plateau before Old Persian was formalized as a royal script. The Persians did not create their bureaucracy from nothing. They inherited it from Elam.

**The mountain raiders and the plain administrators.** In Mesopotamian memory, the Elamites were above all the people who came down from the mountains: the raiders who sacked Ur, who carried off the stele of Hammurabi (found at Susa, where an Elamite king had brought it as plunder, and now in the Louvre), who disrupted the lowland agricultural order. This memory is one-sided. The Elamite state was sophisticated, literate, and capable of both highland military power and lowland agricultural administration. The raids were real, but so was the centuries-long Elamite kingdom that managed a complex society, maintained diplomatic relations with Mesopotamian powers, and produced the multilingual scribal tradition that would underlie the first Persian empire.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Earliest Writing:** A tablet from Susa, pressed with a reed stylus, records something we cannot yet read. The tokens are organized into a system. The plateau is literate before most of the world knows what literacy is.

**2 — The Middle Elamite Kingdom:** The Elamite army has raided Babylon and brought back Hammurabi's stele as a trophy. It stands now in the courtyard of Inshushinak's temple at Susa, a monument to someone else's law in the city of Elam's god. The king who ordered the raid understood that the stele's presence in Susa was a statement about who was greater.

**3 — The Neo-Elamite Period:** The Assyrian army of Ashurbanipal has sacked Susa. The king's annals will boast of carrying off the Elamite gods. But the Elamite scribal tradition survives in the administrators and scholars who flee east to the plateau. A generation later, the Achaemenid kings will use Elamite as the language of their first great inscriptions. The empire that absorbed Elam will be built on Elamite foundations.
