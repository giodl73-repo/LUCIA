# Board Review — B-1 Irving Rouse
## Era 2: The Island Expansion | Arawak / Lokono

**Reviewer domain:** Caribbean archaeology, Saladoid ceramic sequence
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Overall Assessment

The chapter is historically responsible. The ceramic evidence is presented with appropriate specificity and the migration is characterized correctly as a multi-generational process rather than a founding event. I have a few corrections and a few clarifications, none of which require structural revision.

## Factual Review

**Ceramic claims:**
- "White-on-red painted ware" — correct. This is the most visually distinctive feature of the Saladoid assemblage.
- "Zone-incised crosshatch" — correct and appropriately described. The chapter renders it as a trade-marking convention, which is an interpretive claim I find plausible and consistent with how we understand Saladoid ceramic distribution. I would characterize it as a reasonable inference rather than documented fact, which the figures.md does correctly.
- The chapter does not conflate the painted and incised modes. The rendering focuses on the zone-incised crosshatch for the pottery scene, which is appropriate: the incised decoration is more widely recognized as the Saladoid network marker in the archaeological literature.

**Dating:**
- "c. 500 BCE" for the Trinidad crossings — this is the conventional date. My own sequence work placed the initial Saladoid appearance in Trinidad at approximately 500–400 BCE, and subsequent work has confirmed this range. The chapter's use of "c. 500 BCE" is correct.
- "c. 700 CE" for the era's close — appropriate. By this period the cultural differentiation into what becomes the Taíno tradition is well established.

**Migration process:**
- "Multi-generational and involves cultural adaptation at each island stage" — this characterization, implicit in the chapter's structure, is accurate. I would note that the Saladoid expansion is now understood as considerably more complex than the simple unidirectional movement my early work suggested. The chapter's framing of accumulated household decisions is consistent with current interpretations.

**Archaic populations:**
- The fisher-at-the-reef figure, representing the pre-Saladoid Archaic populations, is handled correctly. The chapter acknowledges their presence without developing their story, and the figures.md explicitly notes the Archaic-Saladoid coexistence evidence. This is the responsible approach.

## Corrections Required

**One factual clarification:** The chapter states that the zone-incised crosshatch appears "consistently enough across sites that archaeologists use it as a diagnostic marker." This is accurate but slightly understates the variability: the crosshatch convention varies in execution across sites and time periods, and later Saladoid assemblages show divergence from the earlier forms. The chapter's use of this variation as an index of cultural differentiation (Waya making the mark on the island with island clay) is actually more nuanced than this sentence suggests. I would not require a revision; the sentence is not wrong. Clarification only.

**No structural corrections required.**

## Board Verdict

**ENDORSE.** The chapter meets the historical accuracy standard for the Saladoid expansion. The ceramic, linguistic, and agricultural evidence is used correctly within the limits of what the sources support. The figures.md transparency about the composite nature of the chapter's characters is appropriate and sufficient.
