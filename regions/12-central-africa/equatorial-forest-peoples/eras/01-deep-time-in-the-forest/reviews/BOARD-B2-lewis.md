## Accuracy of the Ekila Representation

The chapter's representation of the ekila concept is accurate at the level of structural logic: restrictions as the shape of relationships rather than arbitrary rules, the debt-relationship after a successful hunt, the restrictions related to birth and new life. These reflect the documented ekila system.

**One precision flag:** The chapter says "if you have recently given birth, you are in a particular kind of relation with new life, and certain food restrictions protect that relation and what it opens." The word "protect" implies a defensive or precautionary logic that is slightly different from the inside frame. The ekila restrictions around birth are not protecting the relation so much as constituting it — the restriction is what the relation consists of in that period, not what guards it. This is a subtle distinction but an important one. Consider: "certain restrictions come with that relation, because the relation has its own requirements."

**Generalization flag:** The chapter uses ekila (a Mbendjele concept) to describe what Ekali (an Aka figure) carries. The figures.md disclosure is present and appropriate. However, the chapter would benefit from a single word signaling that the relational ethics system Ekali observes is known by different names in different communities — this is mentioned in figures.md but not in the chapter itself. Something as simple as "a web of restrictions and permissions — the Mbendjele call this ekila; among the Aka and Baka, the specific terms differ, but the relational logic holds." This brief acknowledgment would prevent a reader from assuming ekila is a pan-forest-peoples term.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

The overall framing of the relational ethics as the shape of the relationship itself — not rules imposed from outside — is exactly right. The observation that the forest "knows" when restrictions are violated is rendered from inside the worldview, not as superstition to be explained or debunked. This is the correct treatment.

The music section accurately represents the collective nature of Aka/BaYaka polyphony. The absence of a named author for the music is correct and captures something important about how these practices are understood.

**Endorsement:** The chapter represents the forest peoples' relational ethics with accuracy and with genuine understanding of what distinguishes this knowledge system from other epistemological frameworks. The Ekali figure is the chapter's most complex composite, and it is handled with appropriate care.

---

## Recommendation

Accept with two minor revisions: (1) the "protect" language in the birth-restriction passage; (2) a single-sentence acknowledgment that ekila is the Mbendjele term, with an indication that parallel systems exist under different names elsewhere in the forest. Both are small textual adjustments.
