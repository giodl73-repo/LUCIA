# The Angevin Crown — "King and Law"

The argument between the king and the law is the argument that defines us. Henry II built the most sophisticated royal government in twelfth-century Europe — the common law courts, the writs, the itinerant justices, the exchequer — and in doing so created a professional legal class that knew the law better than the king did and eventually used that knowledge against him. John's barons at Runnymede in 1215 were not revolutionaries. They were conservatives: they wanted the king to honor the customs and obligations that Henry I had sworn and that John had systematically violated. What they produced in the process of making him swear again was the first great constitutional document of English history — not because they intended to create constitutional theory, but because the insistence that the king too was bound by law, written down and witnessed and sealed, was a principle that proved infinitely expandable.

The Plantagenets were a dynasty with a continental empire: from the Scottish border to the Pyrenees, the Angevin territories at their height in 1154 covered more of France than the French king himself controlled. This was a curse as well as an inheritance. Henry II, Richard I, and John spent their reigns fighting — against the French king, against their own sons, against the Church. Thomas Becket's murder in Canterbury Cathedral in 1170 demonstrated that even the most capable English king could not simply overpower the institutional Church. The loss of Normandy in 1204 under John demonstrated that a continental empire could not be sustained from England alone. The Plantagenet story is the story of an attempt to rule too much — and the institutional innovations that attempt produced in the attempt.

## What Defined Them

**The common law as a living tradition.** Henry II's legal reforms created something that had not existed before: a uniform royal justice available to free men across the kingdom, administered through standardized writs, trained judges, and a body of decided cases that accumulated into precedent. This was not Roman law — it was not a code written by scholars but a practice built by courts. The common law grew through the decisions of actual cases, and because those decisions were recorded, they could be cited, distinguished, and built upon. The men who made Magna Carta had been trained in this tradition; they knew the law was a real thing, not a royal favor. The common law is the institutional expression of the idea that the king is inside the law, not above it.

**Magna Carta and the charter tradition.** Magna Carta was sealed at Runnymede in 1215 and immediately annulled by the pope at John's request. It was reissued, revised, and confirmed dozens of times before it became the permanent foundation of English constitutional practice. What mattered about Magna Carta was not its specific provisions — most of them addressed the grievances of twelfth-century barons — but the principle it embodied: that the king's government must operate within legal constraints, that certain rights could not be taken without "the lawful judgment of peers or the law of the land," and that these constraints could be put in writing, sealed, and held against the crown. The charter tradition — the idea that government works through written agreements — is England's constitutional contribution to the world.

**The Parliament — counsel becoming institution.** The Plantagenet kings needed money; money required consent; consent required assembly. The Parliament that emerged from Henry III's reign and crystallized under Edward I was not a democratic body — it was an assembly of magnates, clergy, and eventually commons-men who spoke for the shires and boroughs. But the habit of assembling, deliberating, and consenting became over time a constitutional expectation. The principle that taxation required parliamentary approval — established in practice before it was established in theory — was the instrument through which the barons and commons eventually accumulated real constitutional power.

**The French connection — war and culture.** The Plantagenet court spoke French. Its legal records were in Latin and French. Its chivalric culture — the Arthurian romances, the tournament, the idealized figure of the knight — was French. And for three hundred years, English kings fought to retain their French territories, understanding their kingdom as an Anglo-French entity rather than a purely English one. The loss of Normandy in 1204 and the beginning of the Hundred Years War in 1337 both forced the gradual replacement of this dual identity with something more purely English — expressed in the language of the chancery documents, in the use of English in parliament, and eventually in the emergence of English as a literary language in Chaucer and Langland.

**The Church and the crown — Becket's shadow.** The murder of Thomas Becket in Canterbury Cathedral in December 1170 by four of Henry II's knights — acting on what they took to be the king's wish — demonstrated the limits of royal power over the Church. Henry was forced into a public penance at Becket's tomb. Becket was canonized within three years and his shrine became the most visited pilgrimage destination in northern Europe. The conflict between ecclesiastical and royal jurisdiction that the Becket dispute embodied was never fully resolved under the Plantagenets — it would not be resolved until Henry VIII, and then by dissolving the Church rather than reforming it.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Angevin Inheritance:** Henry II receives the briefing on the English legal system from the justiciar who has been running the country during the anarchy. The king takes one look at the chaos of competing jurisdictions — ecclesiastical, baronial, royal — and begins to plan the most ambitious legal reform program in English history. He has no idea it will cost him two of his sons and his friendship with the greatest churchman of the age.

**2 — The Absent King:** John, at Rouen, receives the news that Philip Augustus's forces have taken the castle that was supposed to be impregnable. Château Gaillard — Richard's masterpiece, built in two years to defend Normandy — has fallen in one. John retreats to England and does not come back. He has just lost the continental empire.

**3 — The Long Minority:** Simon de Montfort calls the Parliament of 1265, including knights from the shires and burgesses from the towns — not just barons and bishops. He loses the war and his head. But the Parliament has been called. The precedent exists. No one can uncall it.

**4 — The Model Parliament:** Edward I surveys the realm he has remade — Wales subdued, Scots temporarily quieted, the legal codes systematized — and receives the draft summons for the Parliament of 1295. He signs it. He needs the money. He has no idea that the formula he is about to use — knights of the shire, citizens and burgesses, together with the magnates and clergy — will be called "the model parliament" by historians who consider it the template for representative government.

**5 — The Long War and the Long Crisis:** The peasants are at the gates of London. Wat Tyler is dead, killed by the mayor. Richard II faces the crowd alone, fourteen years old, and tells them their demands will be met. They disperse. He issues the pardons he promised and then revokes them. The rising is over. The world that made it is not.
