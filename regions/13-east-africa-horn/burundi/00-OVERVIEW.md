# Ubwami bwa Burundi — "The Kingdom of Burundi"

Burundi is a small kingdom on the northeastern shore of Lake Tanganyika — one of the deepest lakes in the world, whose waters come from rainfall that fell thousands of years ago and whose fish have evolved in isolation into hundreds of endemic species. The kingdom that grew on its shores was, by the standards of the Great Lakes region, compact: the Mwami of Burundi ruled a territory smaller than Rwanda or Bunyoro, with fewer subject peoples and a more intimate court politics. But what Burundi lacked in territorial scale, it made up in ritual density. The Burundian royal tradition was among the most elaborately ceremonial in the Great Lakes region, with a court culture built around the mwami's person, the sacred cattle, the royal drum (the karyenda, which was the kingdom's soul), and the ganwa princes whose political competition defined Burundian court politics for two centuries.

The ganwa — the princes of the blood royal — were the most powerful political force in the Burundi kingdom, and the most destabilizing. They were the sons of the mwami, and in a royal household that produced many sons, this meant a large and competing class of men with legitimate claims to authority and resources. The ganwa competed for influence through the allocation of provinces, through the favor of the mwami, and through their networks of client relationships among the non-royal population. The political history of Burundi from the eighteenth century onward is largely a history of ganwa factionalism: the Bezi faction (descending from one royal lineage) against the Batare faction (from another), with the non-royal populations navigating between these competing aristocratic claims.

## What Defined Them

**The karyenda and the kingdom's soul.** The karyenda — the sacred royal drum of Burundi — was not simply a musical instrument. It was the embodiment of the kingdom's sovereignty, the medium through which the mwami's connection to the divine order was performed, and the object whose presence or absence signified the legitimacy of rule. The drum could not be touched by ordinary people; it had its own guardians, its own rituals of feeding and care, its own place of honor in the royal enclosure. When Burundian kings were installed or defeated, the fate of the karyenda was a political event of the first order. The drum museum at Gitega, where historical royal drums are now preserved, is one of the most significant cultural institutions in Burundi.

**The ganwa system and its costs.** The ganwa princes were simultaneously the guarantors of the kingdom's continuity — they administered provinces, led armies, and maintained the royal lineage's presence across the territory — and its chronic source of instability. The Bezi-Batare rivalry that dominated Burundian politics from the mid-nineteenth century was not simply personal competition; it was a structural feature of a system in which multiple royal sons competed for limited resources and authority. The colonial German and then Belgian administrations navigated this rivalry by allying with different ganwa factions at different times, which prolonged the rivalry and embedded it more deeply in Burundian political culture.

**Cattle poetry and the aristocratic aesthetic.** Like Rwanda, Burundi's court culture was organized around cattle — their ownership, their movement through the clientship system, their praise poetry. The inyambo cattle of the royal herds were praised in verse; the mwami's gifts of cattle to favored clients were the currency of political relationship. The Burundian tradition of cattle praise poetry (imivyino) and the elaborate court ceremonies around the inyambo were markers of aristocratic identity and royal legitimacy. The aesthetic of the court was one of controlled abundance — the cattle well-tended, the poetry well-crafted, the ceremony well-performed.

**The 1972 genocide and the historical shadow.** Burundi, like Rwanda, carries a history of mass political violence that shapes how its pre-colonial history is read and written. The 1972 killing of approximately 150,000–300,000 Hutu by the Tutsi-dominated Burundian army (and subsequent episodic violence in 1988, 1993, and the civil war of 1993–2005) is not a distant catastrophe; it is the context in which the history of the kingdom must be placed. The pre-colonial Hutu-Tutsi categories in Burundi, as in Rwanda, were more fluid than the colonial-era transformation made them. The political violence of the twentieth century drew on colonial-era rigidifications of these categories; it was not the simple continuation of a pre-colonial ethnic conflict. Both things can be true and both must be acknowledged.

**The modest scale and the deep ritual.** What makes Burundi distinctive within the Great Lakes comparative frame is not its size (small) or its territorial ambition (limited) but the density of its royal ritual and the survival of that ritual tradition into the modern period. The drums, the cattle ceremonies, the ganwa court culture — these were maintained with unusual care and have been documented in greater detail than many larger kingdoms. Smaller scale sometimes produces more legible culture.

## The Chapters

**1 — The First Drum:** The karyenda has been struck for the first time in this new king's reign. The sound carries across the hills. Everyone within hearing distance knows what it means: there is a king, there is a kingdom, the center holds.

**2 — The Inyambo at Court:** The royal cattle are being presented. The court poets are composing their praise verses. The ganwa princes are watching the mwami's face to see which prince's cattle have been praised most fulsome. The cattle ceremony is also a political reading of the mwami's preferences.

**3 — The Bezi-Batare:** Two ganwa princes are competing for the same province. One has the mwami's current favor; the other has the stronger client network. The German station administrator nearby is watching. He has been told to support stability. He is calculating which faction represents stability.

**4 — After the Monarchy:** 1966. The republic has been declared. The karyenda is in storage. A Burundian scholar is interviewing an old ganwa who served three mwami kings. He is trying to record what is about to be lost. He succeeds, partially.
