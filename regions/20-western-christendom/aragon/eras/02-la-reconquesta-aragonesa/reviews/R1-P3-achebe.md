# PANEL-1 — P-3 Chinua Achebe
## R1-P3-achebe.md

**Chapter:** Aragon Era 2 — La Reconquesta Aragonesa
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (opening review)
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe

---

This is a chapter about a Christian king taking a Muslim city. That is a situation in which condescension is the default setting — in which the temptation is to place the Christian in the position of subject and the Muslims in the position of objects, recipients of whatever arrangement the victorious party chooses to bestow. I read this opening looking for that. I did not find it.

What the author has done — and I do not say this is easy — is frame the compact from inside the Aragonese political logic without making that logic the triumphant universal. James needs the Moors to stay. This is not benevolence. It is calculation. The acequia engineers, the silk merchants, the knowledge systems built over generations — these are named as the reason, not as detail. The mudéjar arrangement is not presented as a gift. It is presented as an arrangement that works, from the perspective of a king whose covenant system runs in both directions.

**What is working:**

The opening does not pretend Zayyan's defeat is anything other than defeat. The governor opens the gates because there is no relief from the south. The text does not soften this. What it does — and this is the harder thing — is show James understanding that his power over Valencia is conditional. He can take the city. He cannot take the knowledge that makes the city valuable. That distinction is where the story lives, and the author finds it.

The use of the *Llibre dels Fets* as source is handled correctly. James's own words are used to establish his perspective, not to authorize the chapter's judgment. The weeping at the mosque is reported as James reported it — with the caveat that "tears came before he could stop them, not from piety alone" — which is the author following the primary source rather than using the primary source to endorse a reading.

**What I'd push on:**

The opening risks one omission that the chapter must correct: Zayyan ibn Mardanish. He is named, he has agency — "the last Almohad governor of Valencia held a city of fifty thousand souls and knew there would be no relief from the south" — but that sentence is all he gets. The chapter must give Zayyan more. He is not merely a door opening. He is a man who governed a city, who made decisions about how to protect fifty thousand people, who chose the terms of the capitulació. What was the Almohad world he inhabited? What was his reading of the situation? What did he lose, and what did he preserve?

The mudéjar communities who remain — the farmers in the orchards, the engineers of the acequia systems — they are spoken of collectively, as a population James needs. The chapter must give at least one of them particularity. A named person, a specific occupation, a specific relationship to the land they will continue to farm under new rulers. Without this, the mudéjars are the object of James's calculation rather than the subject of their own continued life in Valencia.

**The question I'd push on:**

Can this chapter be read, fifty years from now, by a scholar who studies the history of Muslim communities under Christian rule in medieval Iberia, and feel that their story was told with dignity — from inside their own understanding of the arrangement, not only from inside James's understanding of it? That is the test I would apply to the chapter.

The opening has earned the right to proceed. The chapter must earn Zayyan's perspective and the mudéjar perspective, not merely James's.
