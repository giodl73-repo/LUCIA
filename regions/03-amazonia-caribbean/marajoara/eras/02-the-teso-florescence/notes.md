# Notes — The Teso Florescence (c. 700–1000 CE)
## Production Bible

---

## 1. Sources

### Tier 1 (Primary)
- Marajoara ceramic assemblages — excavated at Camutins, Teso dos Bichos, and related sites. The objects themselves are primary evidence: their formal properties, their surface treatments, the contexts in which they were found (burial vs. midden vs. platform) are primary data.
- Archaeological stratigraphic sequences at Teso dos Bichos (A. Roosevelt excavations, 1991) — provides chronological and depositional context for the Florescence period.

### Tier 2 (Academic)
- Roosevelt, Anna Curtenius. *Moundbuilders of the Amazon: Geophysical Archaeology on Marajó Island, Brazil*. Academic Press, 1991. The foundational modern study. Provides demographic estimates, ceramic typology, settlement patterns, and dating.
- Schaan, Denise Pahl. "The Camutins Chiefdom: Rise and Development of Social Complexity on Marajó Island, Brazilian Amazon." PhD dissertation, University of Pittsburgh, 2004. The most detailed political-social analysis of the Florescence period specifically.
- Schaan, Denise Pahl. "Into the Labyrinths of Marajoara Pottery." In *Unknown Amazon: Culture in Nature in Ancient Brazil*, ed. Colin McEwan, Cristiana Barreto, and Eduardo Góes Neves. British Museum Press, 2001. The best analysis of the visual language of the ceramic tradition.
- Barreto, Cristiana. "Mounds, Pots, and Ancestors: The Social Life of Marajoara Objects." In *Unknown Amazon*. Addresses the relationship between ceramic production, mortuary practice, and social status.
- Meggers, Betty and Clifford Evans. *Archaeological Investigations at the Mouth of the Amazon*. Bureau of American Ethnology, 1957. Earlier, contested interpretation — important to understand the debate, particularly on population and complexity.

### Tier 3 (Scholarly Secondary)
- McEwan, Colin, Cristiana Barreto, and Eduardo Góes Neves, eds. *Unknown Amazon: Culture in Nature in Ancient Brazil*. British Museum Press, 2001. The most accessible scholarly collection. Multiple essays directly relevant.
- Heckenberger, Michael. *The Ecology of Power: Culture, Place, and Personhood in the Southern Amazon, A.D. 1000–2000*. Routledge, 2005. Relevant for comparative Amazonian complexity; the upper Xingu model illuminates the Marajó case by comparison.

### Tier 4 (Reference)
- Wikipedia: Marajoara culture — for date ranges and basic typology only. Underlying sources are Roosevelt 1991 and Schaan 2004.

## 2. Key Figures

**The urn-maker (unnamed):** The chapter's central figure is a woman who makes burial urns at the peak of the Florescence. She has no name — the Marajoara left no written records, and no names survive in any source. She is not a composite (composite figures require ethnographic analogy, which is not available here). She is an embodied presence: her hands, her tools, her material knowledge, her relationship to the specific urn she is making. Her anonymity is itself historical fact — this is Achebe's question answered in the Swahili mode: anonymity as collective voice.

The novel technique: she is making an urn for her living sister. The urn exists before the death. This is the chapter's central fact and its emotional throughline. Following Tuchman's note, the chapter will trace the urn's spatial journey: from the woman's hands, to the storage place on the teso's high shelf, to the ceremony of burial. This arc is the chapter's spine.

**The specialist class (unnamed):** Archaeological evidence (Schaan 2004) suggests that the most elaborate urns were made by specialists — women with particular training in the visual language — not by generalists. The chapter will acknowledge this: the woman is not a typical Marajoara woman. She is a knowledge-holder. But her knowledge is transmitted within a female lineage system, not through an institution. This distinction is crucial.

## 3. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The ceramic urn as the medium of political legitimacy and cosmological authority.

**Mechanism:** The urn is the object through which the community's claim about death, continuity, and identity is made material. The woman who can make the urn — who controls the visual language, who knows which forms belong to which kind of person, who executes the spiral correctly — holds the authority to define what the community believes about its dead. This is not spiritual in the way that a priest is spiritual: it is technical. The knowledge is in the hands. Losing the hands means losing the cosmology.

**Terminal condition:** The Florescence ends when the teso-construction project — the accumulated communal labor of building the mounds — becomes too costly for the returns it provides. This is not simply environmental: the teso's political logic was that the community proved its cohesion by returning to the same place season after season and adding to the mound. When population stress or ecological shift made this return more costly than staying on smaller dispersed sites, the logic of the teso dissolved. The ceramic tradition continued, but without the spatial concentration that had made the most elaborate urns possible. The terminal condition is: the platform that sustained specialist knowledge loses its reason to be maintained.

**v1.5 connection (Material Object as Structural Principle):** The burial urn IS the Marajoara political theory made physical. Like the Kanem-Bornu curtain or the Norse clinker hull, the urn's properties determine the narrative's logic: it requires specialist knowledge to make, it encodes a specific cosmological claim, it must be made before it is needed, and it endures after the community that made it is dispersed. The urn outlasts everything.

## 4. Narrative Arc

### Section 1 — The Urn-Maker at Work (~800 words, = the opening)
The opening integrates as Section 1. The woman at Camutins, the dry season, the red spiral, the sister still alive. Establish the teso as physical and cosmological center. End with the woman's view from the mound: the flood retreating, the várzea extending, the world below her.

### Section 2 — The Teso as Political Form (~700 words)
How the mound was built and why. The dry-season return as a political act: to come back to the same teso is to claim membership in the community that built it. Chiefly authority organized around controlling the teso — specifically the burial platform and the decisions about who was buried how. The florescence is the period when this system reaches maximum elaboration: the mounds largest, the population densest, the hierarchy most visible in burial differentiation. The communal labor system (who builds, who benefits, who decides) as the political economy of the teso.

### Section 3 — The Visual Language (~700 words)
The ceramic tradition at its peak. The interlocking spiral forms, the zoomorphic elements, the paired-eye motif. Schaan's analysis of the visual language as a coherent symbolic system — not just decorative variety but a grammar with rules. The woman who knows this grammar is not an artist in the modern sense; she is a linguist of the dead, making arguments about continuity in a medium that endures when speech does not. The tanga introduced here: the ceramic pubic covering worn in ceremony and buried with its wearer. Not presented as exotic — presented from within women's ceremonial life as the object that marks female identity at its most specific.

### Section 4 — The Flood Calendar (~600 words)
The várzea ecology in depth. The flood arrives in the wet season and retreats in the dry, and the Marajoara calendar is organized around this pulse. When the water rises: the fish move into the flooded grass, the turtles nest on the mound tops, the community is concentrated on the high ground. When the water retreats: the planting, the fishing of the stranded pools, the long work of construction and ceramic production. The environmental knowledge embedded in this cycle: where the best fishing is at each stage of the flood, how the flood timing varies across decades, what the signs are that the water will peak earlier or later than usual. This is knowledge that lives in the same lineage system as the ceramic tradition — the women who know the clay also know the water.

### Section 5 — The Urn's Journey (~600 words)
Following Tuchman's note: where does the urn go between completion and use? Archaeological evidence suggests that finished burial urns were stored on raised platforms within the teso's main structures — visible, present, waiting. The sister knows the urn is there. This is not a morbid fact but a structural one: the community acknowledges that the person will die before they die, prepares for continuity before continuity is needed. The urn as the community's statement of readiness. When the sister dies — the chapter does not narrate this moment, only approaches it — the ceremony of burial: how the bones are prepared (secondary burial was practiced — bones cleaned and painted, then placed in the urn), how the urn is positioned on the burial platform, who is present, what the act of closing the lid means in the community's terms.

### Section 6 — The Florescence and Its Horizon (~600 words)
The view from the peak. The Camutins complex at maximum elaboration: thirty-plus mounds, the largest ceramic output, the most differentiated burial hierarchy. But the forces that will end the florescence are already present. The cost of maintaining the teso mounds increases as the community grows. The ecological productivity of the várzea varies — some decades of high flood, some of low — and the community's margin of surplus determines how much labor can be diverted to mound construction. The chapter does not narrate the contraction (that is Era 3). It names the horizon: the flood pattern is changing, or the population has exceeded the teso system's carrying capacity, or both. The woman finishing the urn does not know this. The urn will endure longer than the florescence. The chapter ends on this fact, not on tragedy.

## 5. Panel Pressure Points Anticipated

**Tuchman:** The fifth paragraph of the opening (survey of Camutins statistics) will be moved or redistributed into Sections 2 and 4, not presented in the opening block. The woman's view from the mound will be the opening's closing image.

**Ibn Khaldun:** The labor economy of the teso will be addressed in Section 2. The ceramic knowledge system and the labor organization system will be shown as related: the women who hold the ceramic knowledge are also the women whose lineage authority organizes the community's return to the teso each season. The engine is explicitly named in Section 2.

**Achebe:** The tanga will be introduced in Section 3 from within women's ceremonial logic. The woman will not be named. Her presence will be sustained through her hands, her tools, her specific knowledge — the Swahili anonymous-builder technique adapted to a female specialist.

**Davis:** The várzea ecology will be fully developed in Section 4 — not as background but as knowledge system. The flood calendar as a distributed ecological literacy that the community carries. The relationship between ceramic knowledge and environmental knowledge will be made explicit.

**Kapuscinski:** Sound environment of the teso to be added to opening revision. The woman's visual horizon from the mound to be the opening's final image. The texture of the slips — consistency, drying time in dry-season air — to be developed in Section 3.

## 6. Open Questions

1. **Secondary burial practice:** Was secondary burial (defleshing or allowing decomposition, then placing cleaned bones in the urn) consistent across the Florescence, or was it variable? Schaan 2004 addresses this — the chapter will follow her analysis.

2. **Tanga context:** Were tangas worn in life or only placed in burial? The archaeological record has some tangas found in apparent use-contexts and some in burial-only contexts. The chapter will acknowledge this ambiguity.

3. **Chiefdom vs. heterarchy:** Schaan argues for a chiefdom model for the Camutins complex; other scholars favor heterarchy (multiple competing centers without a single hierarchy). The chapter will present the Florescence through the material evidence rather than resolving the theoretical debate — the teso as organizing form, regardless of whether it was led by a chief or by a council.

4. **Voice register:** The opening establishes a ceramic-cosmological register: deliberate, weight-bearing, organized by the logic of material and water. This is related to but distinct from the Monument/Stone voice and the Hydraulic voice. It is perhaps closest to a Sedimentary register — accumulative, specific, patient. The chapter will sustain this throughout.

5. **Em-dash discipline:** The opening uses zero em-dashes. The chapter will maintain this. The ceramic-cosmological voice uses the colon (for the argument embedded in material) and the period (for weight). No em-dashes.

## Voice Note

The voice for this chapter is **Ceramic-Cosmological**: deliberate, weight-bearing, organized by the logic of accumulated material. Sentences accumulate like coil-built clay — each one adding to the structure, each one supported by what came before. The colon is the chapter's punctuation of choice: it names what the material contains. Short declarative sentences carry the theological weight (like the Monument voice) but the accumulation is not toward grandeur — it is toward specificity. "The spiral is an argument." "The teso is the community's body." These are the chapter's mode.

Zero em-dashes. The pause mechanism is the period and the colon.
