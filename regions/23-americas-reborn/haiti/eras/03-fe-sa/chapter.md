# Fè Sa — The Revolution

## Haiti, Era 3: 1791–1804

---

The night of August 14, 1791, at Bois Caïman, the rain came down like a pronouncement.

Boukman Dutty had gathered them in the forest. How many — the sources argue. Hundreds. The north plain's enslaved population had been meeting in clusters for weeks, sending runners, passing word through the cane fields by methods the planters could not read. What the planters read was labor: men and women who were cut, field to factory, with a casualness that made death an accounting entry. What those men and women read was each other — who could be trusted, which bosals had come off the same ship, which Creole-borns knew how to move in the dark.

Boukman was a coachman, which meant he traveled, which meant he knew things. He was also something else, something the Haitian tradition would later call a *houngan*, a Vodou priest, though the categories applied to what happened at Bois Caïman came after the fact, fitted to events that had their own vocabulary in the moment. What he did that night was call to the lwa.

The lwa answered through Cécile Fatiman.

She came into possession the way the spirit enters a person who is ready — not gradually, not politely, but all at once, the lwa riding her like a horse rides water. She danced. She took a machete and cut the throat of a black pig, and the people drank the blood, and the covenant was sealed. This was not theater. This was not a motivational meeting. What was sworn at Bois Caïman was sworn in the language the ancestors understood, in front of witnesses who were not only human. The pact was with the living and with the dead.

Boukman prayed. The prayer has been remembered in fragments — translated, retranslated, disputed, claimed — but what has survived carries its weight across two centuries:

*Listen to the voice of liberty that speaks in the hearts of all of us.*

One week later, the north plain was burning.

---

The planters of Saint-Domingue called themselves colonists, which was a word that meant: we own this. What they owned, in 1791, was approximately five hundred thousand enslaved people on the western third of the island the Taíno had called Ayiti. They owned the cane fields and the coffee mountains and the indigo vats. They owned, on paper, the bodies of the people who worked those fields and mountains and vats. The colony produced forty percent of Europe's sugar and more than half its coffee. It was the most profitable piece of land in the Atlantic world.

It was also, for the people held in it, a calculated killing machine. A planter's ledger from the 1780s survives in which the cost of an enslaved worker's life is calculated against the cost of a replacement from the Guinea coast. The number of years allotted was seven. The planter was not unusual. This was how the math was done across the north plain, the west, the south — a math so ordinary it had its own vocabulary, its own set of professional routines, its own class of overseers trained to keep the extraction at the optimal rate.

The people running that calculation could not read what was building under their feet.

---

What they missed was the structure.

The men and women who came to Bois Caïman did not arrive as an undifferentiated mass. They arrived as a society — broken, brutalized, scattered across a hundred plantations, speaking Fon and Yoruba and Bambara and Kikongo and Creole — but a society nonetheless, with its own hierarchies and networks and ways of transmitting knowledge. The bosals, those who had crossed the ocean and carried African religious and political memory in their bodies, formed one current. The Creole-borns, those born on the island who had learned its terrain and its French law and its social architecture, formed another. And across both ran the religious current: Vodou, which was not a system of belief so much as a system of organization, a way of maintaining collective identity and collective memory under conditions designed to destroy both.

The lwa are not metaphors. They are the ancestors and forces through whom the living communicate with what is larger than the living. Ogu, the warrior lwa, iron and fire and the boundary that cannot be crossed without blood. Ezili, the lwa of love and grief, whose tears the revolution would fill many times over. When Boukman called them and Cécile Fatiman danced, the ceremony was not preparing the people for something political. The ceremony was already political — was political in a register that the French administration, trained to read power in cannon and law code, could not translate.

They would learn.

---

By morning on August 23, the cane fields from the coast to the mountains were burning. The fires could be seen from ships at sea. Cap-Français, the colonial capital, filled with smoke and refugees and the sound of what had been certainty coming apart.

Boukman was captured and killed within weeks. The French displayed his head on a pike at Cap-Français as a message. The message did not travel the way they intended. The uprising did not end. It became an army.

Jean-François and Biassou emerged as commanders in the first months: men who had organized before the night at Bois Caïman, who had been part of the network of meetings and runners and passed word. They fought with machetes and clubs at first, then with captured muskets, then with the muskets they began to requisition from the Spanish on the eastern end of the island, who were happy to see the French colony destabilized. The uprising's military evolution happened faster than the planters could believe, because the planters believed that the people they held in bondage could not organize at this level.

They were wrong about that the way they were wrong about everything.

And then there was Toussaint.

---

François-Dominique Toussaint had been freed from slavery before the revolution — not a common condition, but not unheard of. He was literate, which was a fact the revolution would require of him. He knew French law and French military practice. He had read Caesar and the military theorists of the European Enlightenment. He also knew what it meant to have been enslaved, to have watched the calculation of his body's worth being made by someone who owned a ledger and a signature.

He joined the uprising after Bois Caïman. He organized. He drilled. He taught men and women who had survived the seven-year calculation to hold a line against professional European armies — not by matching European tactics, but by using the terrain the Europeans did not understand: the mountains, the forest paths, the heat that killed white soldiers at a rate that no general could afford. The yellow fever that came off the standing water in the cane fields in August did work that no musket could do.

Toussaint understood something that neither the French nor the Spanish nor, later, the British fully grasped: he was not fighting for France. He was not fighting for Spain. He was fighting for *libète* — freedom as an absolute and non-negotiable fact. When Spain offered freedom in exchange for military service, he took it. When France abolished slavery in 1794 — actually abolished it, in the Convention in Paris, in the aftermath of a revolutionary government that had taken the Declaration of Rights seriously enough to apply it — he switched. He switched because France now served his objective better than Spain did. The group solidarity of the formerly enslaved was not defined by who they were fighting for. It was defined by what they were fighting for.

The British learned this lesson when they tried to take the colony in 1793. They sent thirty thousand men. They lost twenty thousand to yellow fever and to a force that had learned to use every valley and mountain pass on the island. When the British evacuated in 1798, it was the largest British defeat in the Caribbean in the eighteenth century. The officer who negotiated the evacuation, General Thomas Maitland, saluted Toussaint. It was not a courtesy. It was recognition.

---

Toussaint was not a simple figure, and the revolution he commanded was not a simple revolution.

The formerly enslaved people of Saint-Domingue who did the dying — on the plantations, in the forests, in the sieges of Cap-Français and Port-au-Prince and a hundred smaller engagements — understood *libète* as something absolute. They would not go back to the fields for any reason, under any arrangement, by any other name. When Toussaint, after 1798, instituted a system of agricultural labor contracts that tied workers to plantations — because the colony needed revenue, because the coffee and cane had to be harvested by someone, because a republic needs an economy — the contradiction was not invisible. The formerly enslaved noticed. The word they used for the contract system was not the word Toussaint used. They understood that freedom had a cost, and they were not certain they agreed with who was paying it.

This tension ran through the revolution like a seam in rock. Toussaint held it. Dessalines would answer it differently.

The relationship between the formerly enslaved majority and the *gens de couleur* — the free mixed-race population of Saint-Domingue — was its own politics. Some *gens de couleur* owned enslaved people themselves. Some had initially allied with the planters rather than the uprising. Vincent Ogé, a *gens de couleur* leader, had been executed in 1791 for organizing his own uprising — an uprising that demanded rights for free colored people but explicitly excluded the enslaved from his claim. The revolution folded this complexity into itself without resolving it. Alexandre Pétion, *gens de couleur*, became one of the revolution's commanders. The lines were not clean. They were not supposed to be clean. The revolution happened to real people in a world that had given them contradictory inheritances.

---

Toussaint wrote a constitution in 1801. It abolished slavery permanently. It named him governor-general for life. It described Saint-Domingue as a French colony — this was the nominal frame — but left France with no actual power over the island. It was the political theology of the revolution written in law: *libète* constitutionalized. The first constitution in the hemisphere to permanently abolish slavery.

Napoleon read it and sent an army.

General Leclerc arrived with thirty-three thousand men in January 1802. His orders included the restoration of slavery, though he was instructed not to announce this until the island was pacified — Napoleon understood that announcing it beforehand would be strategic suicide. The army was the largest France had ever sent across the Atlantic. It included many of the veterans of the Italian campaign, the best soldiers in Europe.

Yellow fever took them. Not immediately, not all at once, but through the summer of 1802 at a rate that made the arithmetic visible: the army that had come to pacify Saint-Domingue was being consumed by the island's own air, by the standing water in the cane fields, by conditions that the people who had lived there for generations had some measure of resistance to and the newly arrived soldiers did not. By autumn, Leclerc himself was dead. His replacement, Rochambeau, was reduced to offering rewards for the killing of Black people by any means — dogs trained for the purpose, mass drownings — in a campaign of terror that produced not surrender but the opposite.

Toussaint was captured in June 1802 through treachery. He was invited to negotiate. The French guaranteed his safety. He went. He was arrested and shipped to Fort de Joux, a mountain fortress in the Jura, where the cold and the altitude and the deliberate neglect did what battle had not. He died there in April 1803.

His last recorded words, leaving Saint-Domingue: "In overthrowing me, you have done no more than cut down the trunk of the tree of Black liberty in Saint-Domingue. It will spring back from the roots, for they are numerous and deep."

The roots were Dessalines.

---

Jean-Jacques Dessalines had been enslaved on the north plain under brutal conditions. He had worked for an enslaved Black overseer named Dessalines, whose name he took, which was how the naming of enslaved people sometimes worked: you took the name of whoever owned you most recently. He had scars from the branding iron across his body. He had survived the seven-year calculation by surviving.

He was not like Toussaint. He did not read Caesar. He did not negotiate in the vocabulary of the French Enlightenment. He resolved the contradiction between *libète* and the labor contract by burning the plantations. When Rochambeau's campaign of terror made clear that France was not negotiating but eliminating, Dessalines responded in kind. He had watched his people burned and drowned and fed to dogs. He had survived it. He understood what it meant when a party shows you what it is.

By November 1803, at the Battle of Vertières, Dessalines's army — joined by Pétion's forces and commanded by men and women who had fought for twelve years — defeated what remained of the French expedition. Rochambeau surrendered on November 18. The French were allowed to leave.

Dessalines called a congress at Gonaïves. On January 1, 1804 — on New Year's Day, because beginnings matter — he declared independence.

He tore the white from the French tricolor. He kept the blue and the red. He sewed them together: the blue for the *gens de couleur*, the red for the formerly enslaved. One flag. One people. He named the country Ayiti.

Not Saint-Domingue. Not any French name. Ayiti — the Taíno word for the island, the word for "land of mountains," the name that had existed before Columbus, before the plantation, before the seven-year ledger. Dessalines reached past the colony to the land itself and named it as the land had been named. The erasure of Saint-Domingue was the completion of the revolution: not just new rulers but a new name, a new people claiming continuity with the land's own identity before the colonial order had existed.

He proclaimed: "I want the assets of the country to be equitably divided."

The first Black republic in the history of the world was declared on January 1, 1804, in Gonaïves, by a man who still carried the scars of the branding iron.

---

In the early months of 1804, Dessalines ordered the killing of the remaining French white population on the island. He exempted Polish soldiers who had defected to the Haitian side, and Alsatian Germans. He did not exempt the French planters or the French soldiers who had remained.

He knew what he was ordering. He ordered it with knowledge of what the French presence meant as a structural fact: a planter class and its associated population that would, if left in place, provide the base for French reconquest. He had watched Toussaint trust French negotiations and die in a cold cell in the Jura. He would not repeat that calculation. His logic was the logic of a man who had been shown, repeatedly and at the cost of blood, what the French governing position was toward the people of Saint-Domingue.

This is not exoneration. It is the inside of the decision. The revolution would be told, in the years and decades that followed, as a heroic story with a clean moral arc. Dessalines did not give it a clean arc. He gave it a closed door.

---

The hemisphere understood immediately what had happened.

Slaveholders from Virginia to Cuba to Brazil read the news and tightened. The embargo on Haiti was not negotiated; it was assumed. The United States, which had supported the revolution's early years under Adams, reversed course under Jefferson — who owned 130 enslaved people at Monticello and understood exactly what the first Black republic meant for the slaveholding republic he governed. Haiti would not receive diplomatic recognition from the United States until 1862. France recognized it in 1825, in exchange for 150 million francs.

The Black Atlantic world read the news differently. The Haitian Revolution traveled by ship and by word of mouth through the diaspora — to the enslaved people of South Carolina and Jamaica and Brazil, to the free Black communities of Philadelphia and London, to every place where the question of whether *libète* was universal was still an open question.

Haiti had answered it. The answer cost everything and proved everything.

The ancestors had been called. They came.

And what they built — imperfect, contested, costly, surrounded by enemies who would spend the next two centuries making the cost as high as possible — was a republic. Named for the land the Taíno had named. Declared on New Year's Day, 1804, by a man who carried the marks of the world he had just ended on his skin.

*Libète* was not a promise. It was a demonstrated fact.

The world would spend the next two centuries trying to make that demonstration as expensive as possible. Haiti would spend the next two centuries insisting that it had already paid.
