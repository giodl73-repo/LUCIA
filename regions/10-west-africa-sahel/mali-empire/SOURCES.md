# Sources — Mali Empire

## Primary / Oral

- **Sundiata Epic** — oral tradition maintained by Mande jelilu across eight centuries. The foundational narrative of the Manden Kurufaba, carrying the covenant logic, the genealogies, and the institutional memory of the empire. Multiple versions exist; the tradition is living and plural. Djibril Tamsir Niane's transcription (1960) is the standard accessible version. Jan Jansen's fieldwork documents contemporary performances at Kela and Kangaba.

- **Al-Umari, *Masalik al-Absar fi Mamalik al-Amsar*, c. 1342 CE** — the primary written account of Mansa Musa's hajj to Mecca (1324-25). Al-Umari interviewed witnesses in Cairo who had seen Musa's visit firsthand. Contains the most detailed descriptions of the caravan's scale, the gold spending, the meeting with the Egyptian sultan, the recruitment of al-Sahili, and Musa's borrowing on the return journey. Essential for the Great Hajj chapter.

- **Ibn Battuta, *Rihla* (Travels), c. 1355 CE** — first-hand account of travel to Mali in 1352-53, fifteen years after Musa's death. Describes the court, the scholarly culture, the mosques, Mande customs, and conditions of Saharan crossing. Valuable for physical detail but culturally biased by his Moroccan-Islamic perspective. He did not witness Musa's hajj directly.

- **Ibn Khaldun, *Kitab al-Ibar*, late 14th century** — history of the western Sudan including Mali's dynastic succession, the economic impact of Musa's gold spending (the twelve-year gold depression in Egypt), and structural analysis of West African state formation. The source for the self-consuming logic of spectacular redistribution.

- **Catalan Atlas, 1375** — European map depicting Mansa Musa seated on a throne holding a gold nugget, labeled as the richest king in the world. A product of European geographic imagination responding to reports of West African wealth. Useful as evidence of Mali's external reputation, but must not be used as the measure of Mali's significance — the jelilu's tradition is the primary record.

## Academic

- **Levtzion, Nehemia. *Ancient Ghana and Mali*. 1973. Methuen.** — The foundational modern scholarly work on the Mali Empire. Covers political structure, trade routes, economic systems, and chronology. Essential reference for all eras.

- **Gomez, Michael A. *African Dominion: A New History of Empire in Early and Medieval West Africa*. 2018. Princeton University Press.** — The most important recent synthesis. Reframes West African state-building on its own terms, centering African political thought and institutional logic. Essential for avoiding outside-in framing.

- **Niane, Djibril Tamsir. *Sundiata: An Epic of Old Mali*. 1965. Longman.** — The standard written transcription of the Sundiata Epic, based on Niane's work with jeli Mamadou Kouyate of Keyla. The reference text for the genealogical tradition, the Kurukan Fuga, and the founding narrative.

- **Conrad, David C. *Empires of Medieval West Africa: Ghana, Mali, and Songhay*. 2005. Chelsea House.** — Accessible synthesis with strong treatment of oral tradition and Mande social structure. Good on the jeli tradition, the Kurukan Fuga's terms, and the oral tradition's contemporary continuation.

- **Hunwick, John O. *Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire: Al-Sa'di's Ta'rikh al-Sudan down to 1613 and Other Contemporary Documents*. 1999. Brill.** — Detailed history of Timbuktu's intellectual culture, the Sankore mosque, the manuscript traditions, and the scholarly community. Essential for the High Scholarly Period era and for the Great Hajj chapter's treatment of Timbuktu's transformation.

- **Levtzion, Nehemia & Spaulding, Jay (eds.). *Medieval West Africa: Views from Arab Scholars and Merchants*. 2003. Markus Wiener.** — Translations of the key Arabic sources on West Africa, including al-Umari and Ibn Battuta extracts. Provides direct access to the Arabic accounts in English translation.

- **Jansen, Jan. "The Sunjata Epic — The Ultimate Version." *Research in African Literatures* 32, no. 1 (2001).** — Critical analysis of the oral tradition's variation across performances and the politics of transcription. Important for understanding why different jelilu tell different versions and what the variations reveal about the tradition's institutional function.

- **Austen, Ralph A. *Trans-Saharan Africa in World History*. 2010. Oxford University Press.** — The trans-Saharan trade system: its logistics, its political dependencies, the chain of alliances and infrastructure that made crossing possible. Relevant to the Sahara crossing section of the Great Hajj chapter.

## Needed (ACQUISITION)

- Studies on the Mansa Musa hajj's specific economic impact — detailed analysis of the twelve-year gold depression in the Egyptian and Levantine markets
- Recent archaeological work on the Niani capital site — physical detail of the capital for scene-setting
- Timbuktu manuscript studies — specific content of manuscripts from the Musa era and immediately after; the curriculum at Sankore
- Scholarship on Inari Kunate — any sources that name or describe Musa's senior wife beyond the bare mention in al-Umari
- Al-Sahili's architectural legacy — detailed studies of the Djinguereber Mosque's construction techniques and the fusion of Andalusian and Sahelian building traditions
- Recent studies on the Kurukan Fuga's content and its UNESCO recognition process (2009)
- Studies on Mande social categories (horon, nyamakalaw, jon) and the Kurukan Fuga's regulation of them — needed for honest treatment of the jon in the caravan
