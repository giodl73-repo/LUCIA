# Ki-en-gi — The Land of the Noble Lords

We called our land Ki-en-gi — the land of the noble lords, the civilized land as opposed to the lands that surrounded it. Our cities were the first cities: Eridu, the oldest, which the gods founded at the beginning of time; Uruk, the greatest, whose walls Gilgamesh built after his return from the cedar forest; Ur, where the moon god Nanna held court; Nippur, the religious center where Enlil's temple stood and where every king, to be legitimate, had to receive divine recognition. We did not build these cities because we planned to invent civilization. We built them because the flood plain between the two rivers could feed a vast population if — and only if — that population cooperated to manage the water.

The temple was the house of the god who owned the city's land. Not the house of a symbol or a metaphor — the house of the god who literally held title, whose land the people farmed, whose grain the people stored in the temple's magazines, whose administrators kept the accounts. The first writing was the temple's accounting system: the shaped clay tokens and then the pressed and incised tablets that recorded how much barley had come in and how much had gone out. Writing was not invented for literature or law or history. It was invented because a god's estate was complex enough to require record-keeping. Literature and law and history came afterward, because once you have writing, everything else becomes possible.

Key places (Sumerian names):
- **Unug** (Uruk) — the greatest city; Anu's ziggurat; the walls Gilgamesh built; where writing was first used
- **Urim** (Ur) — Nanna's city; the royal cemetery with its death pits; the Third Dynasty that briefly unified the south
- **Nibru** (Nippur) — the religious center; Enlil's temple; where kingship received its divine legitimation
- **Eridug** (Eridu) — the oldest city; Enki's freshwater temple; the primordial city at the edge of the *apsu*

## What Defined Them

**The temple estate as the city's organizing principle.** Each Sumerian city was, in the deepest sense, a god's property. The ziggurat — the stepped temple tower — was the god's mountain, the place where heaven and earth met, where the deity resided when present in the city. Around the ziggurat were the temple's warehouses, workshops, and fields. The temple employed weavers and potters and scribes and farmers, recorded their work and their rations on clay tablets, and redistributed the products of their labor according to the deity's requirements as interpreted by the temple's administrators. This was not a metaphor for the social organization of surplus. It was the social organization of surplus, expressed in the only terms that gave it legitimacy.

**The gift of writing.** Sumerian tradition said that writing — along with kingship, the descent of civilization from heaven — was a gift given to humanity by the gods, specifically by Enki, the god of wisdom and fresh water. What the tradition preserved as myth was historically accurate in outline: writing first appeared at Uruk, in the context of temple accounting, around 3200 BCE. The earliest tablets are records of goods received and disbursed. Within centuries, the system had been extended to record not just quantities but words, sounds, names, stories. The *Enuma Elish* and the *Epic of Gilgamesh* were written in a system that began as a way to track grain.

**Kingship between the gods and men.** Sumerian political theory held that kingship had descended from heaven — that the gods had granted one city at a time the right to hold the *nam-lugal*, the kingship, and that this kingship could be transferred by divine decision to another city. The *Sumerian King List* records this sequence: Eridu, Badtibira, Sippar, Shuruppak — the cities holding kingship in turn before the flood. After the flood, kingship descended again to Kish, then Uruk, then Ur. This was not merely history. It was theology: the claim that political power was legitimate only when it rested on divine grant, and that the gods could and did revoke that grant from unworthy cities and kings.

**Gilgamesh — the hero who asked the wrong question.** The *Epic of Gilgamesh* — the great literary achievement of Sumerian and then Babylonian civilization — is the story of a king who loses his closest companion and then refuses to accept mortality. Gilgamesh travels to the ends of the earth to find the secret of eternal life and fails. The ale-wife Siduri tells him the truth: the life of mortal beings is their proper span; the gods kept immortality for themselves. Gilgamesh returns to Uruk, looks at the walls he built, and understands that the city is his monument. This is not a consolation prize. It is the Sumerian understanding of what human life is for: not to escape mortality, but to build something that outlasts you.

**The lament — literature born from loss.** The great literary form the Sumerians invented alongside the hymn and the myth was the *lament*: the formal, structured mourning of a city destroyed. The Lament for the Destruction of Ur was composed after the Ur III dynasty fell to the Elamites around 2004 BCE, and it speaks in the voice of the city's patron goddess Ningal, who pleaded with Enlil to spare her city and was refused. The theology is unflinching: the gods decided to destroy Ur, and they did, and the goddess herself could not prevent it. This combination of formal beauty and theological honesty — the refusal to pretend that the gods are always kind — is the Sumerian literary voice at its most characteristic.

## The Chapters

**1 — When Kingship Came Down from Heaven:** At Eridu, before the flood, the first temple is built at the edge of the *apsu* — the freshwater abyss below the earth. The god Enki looks up from below and the priest looks down from above, and between them the city begins.

**2 — The Age of the City-Kings:** The armies of Uruk and Kish have met for the third time on the plain between the cities. A scribe at Nippur is recording the hymn to Inanna. Gilgamesh is building his walls. The gods hold counsel about the flood.

**3 — The Akkadian Interlude:** Sargon of Akkad stands before the cities and demands their submission. The Sumerian administrators continue their work in Sumerian even as Akkadian fills the royal inscriptions. The language of the conquered becomes the language of the sacred; the language of the conqueror becomes the language of command.

**4 — The Sumerian Renaissance:** Ur-Nammu's law code is published. For the first time, the punishments for specific crimes are written down and made public. The Ur III bureaucracy produces more clay tablets than any previous administration. The ziggurat at Ur is rebuilt to its final form. This is the height of Sumerian administrative civilization — and it will last a hundred years before the Elamites come.

**5 — The Long Memory:** The Babylonian scribes copy the Gilgamesh tablets in the library at Nineveh. The Sumerian language has not been spoken in conversation for five hundred years, but the scholars who serve the Babylonian gods must learn it to read the sacred texts. A language dead in daily life survives in the academy, and in that survival carries the Sumerian world's understanding of what it means to be human.
