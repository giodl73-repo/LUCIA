# BOARD Review — B-1: John Thornton

**Chapter:** Kongo Kingdom / Era 2: The Portuguese Encounter — Afonso I
**Reviewer:** John Thornton (Specialist in Kongo diplomatic history, Afonso I's correspondence, Atlantic African history)
**Primary works:** *The Kingdom of Kongo: Civil War and Transition* (1983); *Africa and Africans in the Making of the Atlantic World* (1998); primary translator of Afonso I's letters
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Historical Accuracy Assessment

**Letter date and content:** The chapter's use of the 1526 letter is accurate. The Latin quotation (*"And we cannot reckon how great the damage is..."*) is a correct rendering of the letter's content. The chapter correctly identifies October 18, 1526 as the date of the primary slave-trade protest letter. The 1540 dictated letter (eyesight failing) is correctly characterized; the quotation from it is accurate in content if not verbatim.

**Afonso's dates:** The chapter uses "c. 1465" for Afonso's birth and "c. 1543" for his death, and "approximately 1509" for his succession. These are within the range of scholarly consensus, though some scholars prefer "c. 1456" for the birth date. The chapter's hedging ("approximately") is appropriate.

**Henrique's biography:** The chapter says Henrique was born "around 1495," sent to Portugal "as a child of approximately eight," and consecrated bishop in 1518 by Pope Leo X. The 1518 consecration date is correct. The birth date and sending age are estimates — the record is actually quite thin on these specifics, and the chapter should note this more explicitly.

**Minor corrections:**
1. The chapter says Afonso "corresponded with the papacy and at least three European monarchs." This should be "at least two European monarchs" (Portugal and possibly Spain through Portugal). The Vatican correspondence is papal, not monarchical. Small distinction, worth correcting.
2. "Thirty-six communications from the Kongo court during Afonso's reign" — this figure is approximately correct but the actual count varies depending on what counts as a separate communication versus an enclosure. Consider changing to "dozens" for safety.

**The slave trade complexity:** The chapter correctly notes that Afonso engaged in captive trade himself early in his reign. This is accurate and important. Scholars have debated whether Afonso's later protest was sincere or strategic; the chapter correctly declines to adjudicate this, treating it as a structural argument rather than a personal conversion story.

**The committee of inspection:** Correctly described. Afonso did establish this mechanism; it did fail for the reasons the chapter states.

**João III's structural position:** The characterization of João III as unable rather than unwilling to enforce the covenant is historically defensible. Thornton's own work supports this reading — the Crown's legal architecture genuinely did not extend enforcement capacity to the Kongo interior.

---

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter's treatment of the cosmogram-crucifix connection follows MacGaffey's and Fromont's scholarship correctly. The claim that "the cross was older than the Portuguese" is an appropriate rendering of the scholarly consensus on pre-existing Kongo cosmological structures. The kalunga line description is accurate.

The nkisi nkondi description is accurate in essentials. The chapter correctly identifies the nail-figure as a covenant-recording instrument rather than a magical object.

---

## Concerns

**One factual adjustment needed:** The chapter says Afonso's letters are "at least thirty-six communications." The actual published count in Brásio's *Monumenta Missionaria Africana* is somewhat higher (depending on dating), but the attribution and archival location are correct. I recommend "dozens" rather than a specific number to avoid the precision implying scholarly certainty that does not exist.

**One interpretive note:** The chapter says "he was the first sub-Saharan African to hold that office in the Catholic church" regarding Dom Henrique. This is the standard formulation, and it is defensible, though there are some scholars who would debate the boundaries of "sub-Saharan Africa" in this period. The chapter should note that Henrique's title was Bishop of Utica — a titular see, not a residential one — which it does correctly.

---

## Overall Assessment

The chapter is historically accurate in all its significant claims. The minor corrections are editorial rather than substantive. The chapter's characterization of Afonso I as a sophisticated political actor using the Christian covenant as a diplomatic instrument rather than as a naive believer is exactly right and matches the scholarly consensus Thornton's own work helped establish.

**BOARD PASS** with the corrections noted above.
