## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of The Fragmentation (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening treats the First Intermediate Period's knowledge system with respect: the grain-counting, the record-keeping, the canal maintenance are presented as intellectual achievements, not as mere administration. This is a culture that understood its world through measurement and record, and the opening honors that. But I want more of the world itself, the physical Nile valley in a time of low water.

**What works:**
- The grain is not a metaphor. It is the actual substance on which life depends, and Khety's counting of it is presented as an act of governance, not as a literary device. The opening respects the culture's own categories of knowledge: the scribe's record, the granary's inventory, the canal's maintenance.
- The Nilometer reading in cubits is the right kind of detail. It grounds the political crisis in the culture's own measurement system. Seventeen cubits is prosperity. Twelve cubits is crisis. The reader learns the system by seeing it under stress.
- The tomb inscription as political claim is handled without condescension. These inscriptions are primary sources, and the opening uses them as the nomarchs used them: as public statements of legitimacy.

**What doesn't:**
- The valley is absent. I know we are in Upper Egypt, in Asyut, on the west bank or the east bank of the Nile, but I do not feel it. Where is the river? What does a low flood look like? What does the valley look like when the black earth does not come? The opening is rich in political texture but thin in landscape. The Nile valley during a series of low floods would have looked different from the valley during good years: exposed mud flats, shrunken channels, dust where there should be water. This is the sensory ground that the opening needs.
- The canal that Khety maintains is mentioned but not seen. A canal in Upper Egypt is a specific physical thing: dug through specific soil, lined or unlined, requiring specific labor to clear the silt. The maintenance of a canal during low water is a different kind of work than during high water. Show me the canal.

**The question I'd push on:**
What does famine look like along the Nile? The Admonitions of Ipuwer and the autobiographical inscriptions describe hunger, migration, social disorder. The opening correctly avoids dramatizing collapse, but the physical reality of low floods needs to be felt in the landscape, not just measured in cubits. The chapter must find a way to render the ecological crisis sensorially without importing modern catastrophism.
