## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Hammurabi's Babylon Opening

**Overall:** The opening passes the Achebe Test. The law is presented from within: it is cosmic order made visible, not an early attempt at modern legislation. The culture owns its story.

**What works:**
- "The stele did not say: this is my will and I am strong enough to enforce it. The stele said: this is the order of the cosmos." This is the crucial distinction, and the opening makes it cleanly. The law is not presented as the work of a clever ruler anticipating modern jurisprudence. It is presented as the culture understood it: theology published in stone.
- The Akkadian language of the code is quoted directly, not summarized. This gives the culture its own voice. "If a man bring an accusation against a man" is the culture speaking on its own terms, not a modern paraphrase.
- No forbidden words. No "sophisticated legal system." No comparison to modern law. No implicit praise that betrays surprise.

**What doesn't:**
- The merchant at the end is described through his economic activity (silver loans, caravans, tin from Ashur). This is correct but external. What does the merchant think about the stele? Not in modern terms, but in his own: does he trust it? Does the stone carry the god's weight for him, or is it the king's instrument? The chapter body should explore whether the law was experienced as divine order or as royal policy by the people who lived under it.
- "His name is lost." This is honest, but the phrasing is the author's, not the culture's. The Babylonians would not have said "his name is lost." They would have said nothing about him at all, because the archive recorded transactions, not persons. Consider reframing the merchant's anonymity through the culture's own relationship to individual identity.

**The question I'd push on:**
The Amorite identity of the dynasty is mentioned once ("one Amorite family among many") and then disappears. The Amorites were newcomers to the Sumerian-Akkadian cities. They came from the western steppe. They spoke a different language. Hammurabi's own name is Amorite. How does a dynasty of pastoral newcomers claim the theological authority of Marduk's city? This tension between Amorite origin and Babylonian legitimacy should be a thread in the chapter.
