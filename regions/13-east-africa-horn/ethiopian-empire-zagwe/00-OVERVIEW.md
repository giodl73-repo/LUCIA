# The Age of Roha — "Lalibela's Jerusalem"

When the Crusaders held Jerusalem in the twelfth century and Christian pilgrimage to the holy city was possible for those who could make the journey, Ethiopia's Zagwe dynasty built an alternative. At Roha — the highland town they renamed Lalibela — King Lalibela (c. 1181–1221 CE) commissioned a complex of eleven churches hewn directly from the living rock of the Ethiopian plateau. These were not churches built from stone; they were churches cut away from the stone, separated from the plateau on all four sides and from above, with every surface carved — exterior facades, interior columns, ceilings, arches — from the same continuous mass of rock. The result is a sacred complex unlike anything else in the world. The tradition says that Lalibela received the design from God and that angels finished the work at night. The architecture says that the Ethiopian Christian tradition was, in the twelfth century, building the most ambitious sacred space in the world at that moment — and building it as a physical claim that Jerusalem was here.

The Zagwe dynasty that produced Lalibela was not the royal house of Axum; the Zagwe came from the Agaw people of the Ethiopian highlands and held the throne from approximately 900 CE, when the Axumite dynasty faded, until 1270 CE, when the Solomonic dynasty reasserted its claim and displaced them. The Zagwe period is thus a dynasty sandwiched between two periods of Solomonic legitimacy, and this sandwiching has given it less historiographical attention than it deserves. The rock churches of Lalibela — which remain the center of Ethiopian Orthodox pilgrimage to this day, receiving tens of thousands of worshippers at Christmas and Easter — are the Zagwe's permanent argument that their reign was real, sacred, and consequential regardless of what the subsequent dynasty thought about their legitimacy.

## What Defined Them

**The rock-hewn church as theological argument.** Lalibela's churches are not simply religious buildings; they are a theological claim made in the most permanent medium available. To carve a church from living rock — to reveal the sacred space that was always present within the mountain — is a different act from building a church from quarried stones. The churches at Lalibela are revelations, not constructions. The theology that motivated this choice was shaped by the Ethiopian Christian understanding of the sacred as present in creation — the mountain is sacred, and the church is what you find within it. The Zagwe were making an argument about where the sacred dwelt, and they made it in a way that has proved impossible to deny or remove for nine hundred years.

**Jerusalem in the highlands.** Lalibela's design incorporates specific symbolic references to Jerusalem: the Biet Gabriel-Rufael complex representing the palace of Solomon; the Yordanos River (the Jordan) running through the site; the Church of the Holy Sepulchre referenced in the Biet Golgota-Medhane Alem complex. This was deliberate: the Zagwe were building a symbolic Jerusalem in Ethiopia, available to Ethiopian Christians who could not make the pilgrimage to Palestine and asserting that the center of sacred geography was here, in Africa, on the Ethiopian plateau. This theological geography has been maintained: today, hundreds of thousands of Ethiopian Orthodox Christians make pilgrimage to Lalibela, which they call the Jerusalem of Africa.

**The Agaw foundation and the Axumite inheritance.** The Zagwe came from the Agaw people — an ethnolinguistic group predating the Semitic-speaking populations of the northern highlands — and their rule represents a period in which the highland state was led by a non-Semitic dynasty while maintaining the Axumite Christian tradition. This combination — Agaw royal identity with Axumite theological inheritance — produced a distinctive court culture. The Zagwe maintained the Ge'ez liturgical language and the Ethiopian Orthodox theological framework while drawing on Agaw material and ritual traditions. The interaction of these two streams is visible in the distinctiveness of the Lalibela churches, which combine Ethiopian Orthodox theological programs with an architectural tradition that seems to emerge from the rock itself.

**The Solomonic displacement and the Zagwe legacy.** When Yekuno Amlak, claiming Solomonic descent, overthrew the last Zagwe king in 1270 CE, the new dynasty's legitimacy was built partly on the argument that the Zagwe had been illegitimate usurpers. This narrative shaped how the Zagwe were treated in subsequent Ethiopian historiography — marginalized, their achievements attributed to divine intervention rather than human initiative. Modern scholarship has been rebalancing this, recognizing the Zagwe as a sophisticated dynasty that maintained Ethiopian Christian culture through a critical transitional period and produced the most ambitious sacred architecture in the country's history.

## The Chapters

**1 — The First Cut:** The first chisel stroke on the rock at Roha. Whether it was angels who finished the work or a very large and organized workforce, the cut began with a human decision to reveal a church from a mountain. That decision was Lalibela's.

**2 — The Pilgrimage:** An Ethiopian Christian has walked for three weeks to reach Roha. She rounds the corner and sees the churches for the first time — not towers above her but spaces below her, cut into the earth, with priests in white linen at the doorways. This is not what she expected. This is better.

**3 — Biet Giyorgis:** The Church of Saint George — cross-shaped, cut to its full height, its roof at ground level — stands in its rectangular trench. The cross-in-square pattern on its roof is as precise as anything built anywhere in the world at this moment. The chisel work is done. The painting inside has begun.

**4 — The Solomonic Claim:** A monk in the Zagwe court has heard that Yekuno Amlak is asserting a Solomonic lineage. He is writing a response. The argument about who has the right to rule Ethiopia is also an argument about what Ethiopia is. The rock churches have already answered the question they were built to answer, regardless of who wins the dynastic dispute.
