# PANEL-1 — P-3: Chinua Achebe

**Chapter:** Mangbetu — Era 2: The Expansion Under Tuba
**Round:** 1
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

I read this opening looking for the moment where the author's hand becomes visible — the sentence where the text stops being Nasakara's world and starts being an account of Nasakara's world. I did not find it. That is the achievement.

The decision to open with "The woman sitting in the center of the room did not need a crown. Her head was the crown" is the correct decision. It takes the element that is most likely to be rendered as strange — the elongated head — and renders it instead as power, as presence, as self-sufficiency. No explanation. No mediation. The reader enters the Mangbetu court's own aesthetic logic before any other frame can be applied.

**What works:**

"Nasakara understood this without being taught it." This sentence is essential. It is the sentence that places the reader entirely inside the worldview, because it names something that people inside a culture experience all the time — the knowledge that comes not from instruction but from absorption, from being born into a world where certain things are simply obvious. An outside observer always explains the coiffure's meaning. An inside voice says that Nasakara understood it without being taught. That is the difference between description and inhabitation.

The line about the Europeans who would describe the court to the world outside is handled carefully. Schweinfurth is named as the mechanism by which the Mangbetu entered the documentary record, not as the arbiter of whether the court's sophistication was real. The distinction is important and the opening maintains it.

**The question I'd push on:**

The opening's center of gravity is the court aesthetic — the coiffure, the music, the spatial arrangements. This is appropriate. But the full chapter will need to render people who exist beyond the aesthetic regime: the subject populations who were incorporated into the Mangbetu state but who did not practice head-binding, who did not sit in the great hall, who may have had their own relationship to Tuba's expansion that was quite different from what the court presented.

I am not asking for a critique of Mangbetu expansion. I am asking for a fully human account. If the chapter renders only the court's own self-presentation — the beautiful, the ceremonial, the ordered — without any awareness of the people for whom the court was an external imposition, it will have made the same error that court propaganda always makes: presenting the rulers' experience as the totality.

This does not require judgment. The Mangbetu court's aesthetic sophistication and the material conditions of the people who produced the food and labor that sustained it can both be true at the same time, rendered with equal dignity. The notes should address how the chapter will handle this without collapsing into either eulogy or critique.

The voice is correct. Do not lose it.
