# Opening — La Conquista: La Espada y la Cruz

## 1513. The Coast of Tierra Firme.

The notary unrolled the document in the dark.

There was no one to hear it. The village had been empty for three days — the people had gone into the forest when the ships appeared, which was what people did when the ships appeared, because the ships always brought the same thing. Rodrigo de Colmenares, captain, held the torch. The notary read in Spanish, as the law required, into the dark trees and the sound of the sea.

He read that there was one God, and one Pope, and that the Pope had given these lands and their people to the Crown of Castile and Aragon by right of the donation of Alexander VI, which had been made in the year of Our Lord 1493 and was recorded in a papal bull. He read that if the people of these lands did not accept this gift — if they did not come forward, if they did not submit to the Church and to the Crown — then any war made upon them, any death that followed, any enslaving of their persons and their children's persons, would not be the fault of the captain or the soldiers or the Crown of Castile, but their own fault, for having refused the gift.

He read this to the empty air. The notary noted in his record that the Requerimiento had been read. The law was satisfied.

In the morning, the soldiers went into the forest.

---

The document was called the *Requerimiento* — the Requirement. It had been drafted three years earlier, in 1510, by Juan López de Palacios Rubios, a jurist of the Council of Castile who had studied at Salamanca and Bologna. He wrote it in response to a crisis: a Dominican friar named Antonio de Montesinos had stood in the church at Santo Domingo on the fourth Sunday of Advent in 1511 and told the assembled encomenderos that they were in a state of mortal sin. That they had no right to wage war on innocent people. That they were killing people who had done nothing to deserve it. His voice, he said, was the voice crying in the wilderness. The congregation — planters, merchants, officials of the Crown — sat in their best clothes and listened, and when the sermon was done they went to the governor and demanded Montesinos be sent back to Spain.

Palacios Rubios was called upon to solve the problem that Montesinos had named. The problem was theological and legal simultaneously, which in 1510 was the same thing. The Catholic faith held that war required just cause. The Crown of Castile was at war with the people of the Indies, and was conducting that war at great expense and at considerable moral risk to the souls of its soldiers and administrators. It was necessary to establish the just cause. This was not cynicism. Palacios Rubios believed what he wrote. The faith that organized the conquest was the same faith that made the conquest's defenders argue, in writing, before the King, about whether what they were doing was right.

The solution was the Requerimiento. Before hostilities could legally begin, it must be read to the people being conquered. It informed them of the Pope's authority, of the donation, of the requirement to submit. If they submitted, they would be received with love. If they resisted, they had been warned, and the consequences were their own responsibility. The document transferred culpability. It transformed conquest into a conditional offer, and war into a just response to refusal.

It was not always read to no one. Sometimes it was read to people who had been bound in advance of the reading, which was efficient. Sometimes it was read from the deck of a ship, at a distance, to people on the shore. The theologian and humanist Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda, reading accounts of its use, did not find this absurd. The Crown had issued the requirement. The requirement had been issued. That the people being conquered could not understand Spanish, could not read, had not been told what the Pope was or what a papal bull meant or why a man in Rome had the authority to give their lands to a king in Castile — these were complications, but they were not, in the legal framework that Palacios Rubios had built, disqualifying ones. The form had been followed. The just cause was established.

Bartolomé de las Casas, who had himself been an encomendero before he became a Dominican priest, who had watched the conquest of Cuba with his own eyes and then spent fifty years trying to stop it, read the Requerimiento and laughed. He could not help it, he wrote later. He laughed because it was so impossible to take seriously, and then he stopped laughing, because it was very serious indeed.

---

The Requerimiento is the hinge on which this era turns.

Not because it was unusual. Because it was usual. Because it represents something the Spanish Crown actually believed: that the conquest required justification, that justification required form, and that form — legal, theological, written, witnessed — was itself a kind of justice. The same civilization that sent Cortés to Mexico sent Las Casas to argue against Cortés before the Council of the Indies. The same faith that blessed the conquest generated the men who spent their lives testifying against it. The *Brevísima relación de la destruición de las Indias* — Las Casas's account of what the Spanish had done in the Caribbean, in Mexico, in Peru — was addressed to the Crown Prince of Spain. It was not a secret. It was an argument, made within the system, using the system's own moral vocabulary, against what the system was doing.

This is what makes the Spanish conquest unlike simple rapacity. The gap between what the law said and what was done was not hidden. It was inscribed into the documentation. The encomienda system — which gave Spanish settlers the right to the labor of indigenous people in exchange for the obligation to Christianize them — was reformed by the New Laws of 1542 after Las Casas's lobbying. The New Laws were defied by the encomenderos of Peru, who threatened open revolt. The viceroy who attempted to enforce them was killed. The laws were suspended. But they had been written. The debate had been had, in public, in the court of the King of Spain, with both sides present.

The soldiers went into the forest. The notary recorded that the Requerimiento had been read. The Crown of Castile was, by the standards of its own theology, at war with people who had refused the gift. Somewhere in the Archivo General de Indias in Seville, the record of that night still exists.
