# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Panel Review of Opening

**Round:** 1
**Reviewer:** Ryszard Kapuściński (P-5)
**Chapter:** Cuba Era 2 — Las Guerras de Independencia — La Manigua
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I covered revolutions for forty years. I stood in the Addis Ababa Palace while the Emperor's servants described what power smelled like. I was present at the Angolan independence. I know what it feels like when a man rides toward gunfire, and I know what it looks like in prose when someone has understood this from a desk.

This opening is written well, and the discover voice is genuine. But it has a sensory problem, and I want to be precise about it.

The passages that work physically: "the six of them rowing a small boat through the surf toward a beach they could hear but not see" — this is excellent. I am there. I feel the oar in my hands, the darkness, the noise of unseen water. "Cuba was dark." This is the best sentence in the piece. Two words. Absolute. Anyone who has arrived by boat at a coast under military occupation knows what that darkness is. This sentence earns its weight.

The passages that don't work physically: everything that happens in the cigar factory is told at the level of what happened (someone read aloud, the workers voted with their wages) rather than what it felt like to be there. The cigar factory in Ybor City was a specific physical place — the roleros at their tables, the smell of tobacco (a smell that distinguishes itself from any other room in the world), the galería reading aloud from an elevated platform, the particular acoustic of a room where fifty or a hundred people are listening and working at the same time. The class consciousness and the revolutionary consciousness were not separable from the physical experience of that room. I need to smell that room.

Similarly: Dos Ríos. The charge is told in three sentences. Forty meters. Three bullets. He fell. I understand why the author chose compression here — the logic of the death has been established and the death itself is the consequence. But the body in the moment is absent. What did Martí's hands do? Was he shouting? Was he silent? What was the air like at the confluence of two rivers in eastern Cuba in May — the wet heat, the insects, the sound of water? These details are not decoration. They are the proof that this death was real, that it happened to a body, that it was not only an argument but a physical event.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter will need to make the manigua itself physically present — not as a concept but as a place. What does it sound like at night? What does it smell like after rain? What does it feel like to sleep in it, to move through it before dawn, to hear the Spanish cavalry in the distance? The chapter's argument is that the manigua was a republic, and I believe the argument intellectually. But I need to feel the republic to believe it in my body, which is the only place that finally matters.

**What's working:** "Cuba was dark." Keep this. Build from it. The landing ("the question of what it meant would not be finished at all") is right in register — it has earned its abstraction through the concrete argument that precedes it. More of that earning in the full chapter.
