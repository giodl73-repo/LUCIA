# Castilla y León — "The Kingdom of the Castles and the Plain"

The frontier is not a boundary. It is a condition of life. For six hundred years, the kingdoms of Castile and León organized their entire existence around the Reconquista — the re-taking, inch by bloody inch and decade by decade, of the Iberian peninsula from the Muslim kingdoms that had occupied it since 711. Every castle that gave Castile its name was a forward position on a line that moved south, generation by generation: Burgos, then Toledo, then Córdoba, then Seville, then finally Granada in 1492. This was not simply a military campaign. It was the organizing principle of an entire civilization — the reason for noble privilege, the source of the military orders' enormous wealth, the justification for the legal framework that repopulated frontier towns with settlers drawn by the promise of land, freedom, and the charter rights of the *fuero*.

But the frontier was also the place where things mixed that the war itself sought to separate. Toledo, reconquered in 1085, became within a generation the greatest translation center in Europe, where Jewish, Christian, and Muslim scholars working in partnership turned Arabic-language philosophy, mathematics, and astronomy into Latin that could be read from Oxford to Prague. In Castile's cities, Jews served as royal treasurers, physicians, and diplomats; Mudéjar Muslim craftsmen built the churches and palaces of the Christian kings in the style they had always used. The Reconquista's purpose was Christian dominion. Its daily reality was *convivencia* — the uneasy, productive, sometimes violent coexistence of three peoples under one crown.

Key places:
- **Burgos** — the heart of Castilian identity; the cathedral; the tomb of El Cid
- **Toledo** — "the city of three cultures"; the translation schools; the seat of the Primate of Spain
- **Santiago de Compostela** — the apostle's shrine; the greatest pilgrimage destination in western Europe; a Leonese and Castilian possession
- **Las Navas de Tolosa** — the battlefield of 1212 where the combined Christian kingdoms broke Almohad power

## What Defined Them

**The fuero — law as settlement incentive.** The Reconquista needed settlers willing to move into dangerous frontier territory. The *fuero* — the royal charter granted to a reconquered town — offered them an incentive: land, specific rights, tax exemptions, local self-governance, sometimes freedom from serfdom. The men who settled Ávila or Soria or Toledo under their *fuero* were not serfs; they were free settlers with legal standing they would not have had in the north. This created a Castilian social character — direct, legally assertive, proud of local autonomy — that persisted long after the frontier had moved south and the original military need had passed.

**The military orders as institutional frontier.** The Orders of Santiago, Calatrava, and Alcántara were simultaneously religious communities, military forces, and landowners on a scale comparable to the greatest secular lords. They held the vast, depopulated meseta between the Tagus and the Guadalquivir, garrisoning it, settling it, and drawing revenues from the sheep that crossed it on the great drove roads — the *cañadas* — twice a year. The *Mesta*, the shepherds' guild that managed this transhumance economy, was one of the most powerful economic institutions in medieval Europe. Wool — Castilian merino wool — was the financial engine of the Reconquista.

**Convivencia — the productive contradiction.** Castile was officially at war with Islam for four hundred years. It was simultaneously the society in which Christians, Muslims, and Jews interacted more extensively and productively than anywhere else in medieval Europe. Jewish scholars translated Arabic philosophy into Latin for Christian patrons; Muslim craftsmen built Christian churches; Christian kings styled themselves *rex trium religionum* — king of the three religions. This was not equality. Jews and Mudéjars lived under legal restrictions that Christians did not. But it was real coexistence, sustained by mutual usefulness, and it produced a cultural synthesis — in architecture, in philosophy, in music — that was the most sophisticated in the medieval West.

**El Cid and the culture of merit.** Rodrigo Díaz de Vivar — El Cid — was exiled by his own king and proceeded to carve out a principality in Valencia by fighting for whoever would hire him, Christian or Muslim. The *Cantar de mio Cid*, the epic poem about him, is not troubled by this. In the poem El Cid's greatness is measured by his deeds, his loyal men, and his eventual restoration of honor — not by the purity of his cause. The Castilian warrior culture valued effective excellence in a way that transcended simple religious allegiance, and this gave the Reconquista a pragmatic edge that pure ideology could not have sustained.

**The union of crowns and its meaning.** The marriage of Ferdinand of Aragon and Isabella of Castile in 1469 was not the founding of Spain — the two crowns remained legally separate, with separate laws, cortes, and administrations. But it aligned the two most powerful Iberian kingdoms against their remaining enemies: Granada to the south, Portugal to the west, France to the north. In 1492 alone: Granada fell, the Jews were expelled, and Columbus sailed. The Catholic Monarchs understood themselves as the culmination of the Reconquista — the completion of a project begun at Covadonga seven centuries earlier. What they were actually completing, though no one knew it yet, was the medieval period itself.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Kings of Asturias:** Pelayo at Covadonga, 722. The Muslim armies are everywhere. Pelayo has a handful of fighters and a mountain and a story — that this moment is the beginning of the recovery, not the final defeat. The story turns out to be more powerful than the armies. But it will take seven hundred and fifty years to prove it.

**2 — The Expansion:** El Cid rides out of Castile under a sentence of exile, his pennant and his sword and his two hundred knights. He will take Valencia from the Muslims and defend it against them and against his own king. The poem made from this journey is the first great text in the Spanish language.

**3 — Toledo and the Translation:** Gerard of Cremona, an Italian scholar, arrives in Toledo in 1145 to find Ptolemy's *Almagest* and discovers that the Arabic manuscript libraries contain a world of learning unavailable anywhere in Latin Europe. He stays forty years. He translates eighty-seven texts. The university curriculum of medieval Europe is transformed by the work done in a city that changed hands peacefully because both sides needed the market.

**4 — The Great Advance:** Ferdinand III receives the surrender of Córdoba in 1236. The great mosque — once the most beautiful building in the world — will become a cathedral. In the meantime it is empty and Ferdinand walks through it alone for a long moment, trying to understand what he has inherited.

**5 — The Catholic Monarchs:** Isabella receives Columbus on his return in 1493. She is simultaneously finishing a war she has fought her entire adult life, expelling a community that has lived in Castile for seven hundred years, and authorizing a voyage whose consequences she cannot begin to imagine. The medieval world ends and the modern one begins in the same season.
