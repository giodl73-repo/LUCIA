# Sinhalese Kingdoms — Dhammadipa

We are the guardians of Dhammadipa — the island that is the sanctuary of the Theravada dispensation. When the Buddha's teaching was threatened elsewhere in the world, when Nalanda burned and the great Indian sangha was scattered, the Pali canon survived complete in our monasteries, our scholars transmitted it intact, and our ordination lineages remained unbroken. We know who we are: Sinhalese, the people of the Pali chronicles, the heirs of Vijaya who came from India before historical memory and made this island their own, the people whom the Buddha himself visited three times according to the Mahavamsa, blessing this land as the place where his dispensation would be preserved.

Our civilization was built on water management and on the dharma, and the two were not separate. The great irrigation tanks of the north central plain — the Parakrama Samudra that Parakramabahu the Great completed in the twelfth century, a reservoir so vast it was called a sea — were the physical infrastructure of a society whose moral infrastructure was the Theravada sangha. The king who built tanks and who upheld the sangha was performing the same royal duty. The Mahavamsa, our chronicle, measures kings by both: how many tanks they built, how many monks they supported. The two accounts are the same account.

## What Defined Them

**Dhammadipa — the island of the dharma.** The Mahavamsa's claim that the Buddha blessed this island as the future home of his dispensation was not merely religious pride; it was the founding principle of Sinhalese political philosophy. The king who ruled Lanka ruled it as a steward of the dharma. His primary obligation was to uphold the sangha, maintain the Pali canon, and ensure that the Theravada ordination lineages remained intact. This ideology made the Sinhalese kings patrons of scholarship on a vast scale and gave their kingdom a mission larger than mere sovereignty.

**The irrigation civilization.** The north central plain of Sri Lanka, naturally semi-arid, was transformed by an engineering achievement unmatched in the ancient world: a cascade of interconnected irrigation tanks, canals, and sluices that watered thousands of square kilometers. The great tanks — Minneriya, Kaudulla, Parakrama Samudra — stored monsoon water and released it through the dry season, supporting dense rice cultivation. This hydraulic infrastructure required sophisticated engineering, continuous maintenance, and the social organization to coordinate both. It was the material foundation of Sinhalese civilization.

**The Mahavamsa and the historical consciousness.** The Mahavamsa — "The Great Chronicle" — written by the monk Mahanama in the fifth century CE, is one of the oldest continuous historical chronicles in Asia. It combines myth, religious history, and political history into a narrative of the Sinhalese people that begins with Prince Vijaya's arrival from India and continues through the kings' building and military achievements. The chronicle tradition (continued in the Culavamsa) gave the Sinhalese a historical self-consciousness — a sense of their own story — that shaped their political identity for two millennia.

**The tooth relic and sovereignty.** The relic of the Buddha's tooth, brought to Lanka in the fourth century CE, became the palladium of Sinhalese kingship. He who held the tooth relic held the legitimate sovereignty of the island. The tooth passed from capital to capital — Anuradhapura to Polonnaruwa to Kandy — and its custodianship was the most important act of royal stewardship. When the last Kandyan king surrendered to the British in 1815, the tooth was in the temple beside the palace. The British allowed the Kandy priests to continue managing the temple. They understood what it meant.

**Tamil-Sinhalese tension and coexistence.** Tamil communities were present in Sri Lanka from antiquity, and the relationship between the Tamil north and the Sinhalese south was neither one of pure conflict nor pure peace. The Mahavamsa celebrates the Sinhalese king Dutugamunu's defeat of the Tamil king Elara as a dharmic victory, but the chronicle also notes that Elara was a just ruler who upheld the law. The two communities traded, intermarried, and competed for the island's resources across two millennia. The complexity of this relationship, which the British colonial period intensified rather than created, is part of what the Sinhalese chronicle tradition was always negotiating.

## The Chapters

**1 — Anuradhapura Period — Early:** The monk Mahinda arrives at Mihintale. The king Devanampiya Tissa meets him on the hill. The dharma has come to Lanka. The king begins building.

**2 — Classical Anuradhapura:** The great tanks fill with monsoon water. The farmers open the sluices in sequence. The rice grows in the delta. The monks in the Mahavihara copy the Pali texts. This is Dhammadipa at its most ordinary and most complete.

**3 — Polonnaruwa:** Parakramabahu I surveys the Parakrama Samudra. The embankment is fourteen kilometers long. The reservoir holds 134 million cubic meters of water. He looks at what he has built and says: let not a single drop of rain fall into the sea without first being made useful to man. He means it.

**4 — Kotte and Jaffna:** The island is divided. The Sinhalese kings at Kotte watch the Portuguese ships anchor in the harbor. Something has changed. These are not merchants from Arabia. These people have cannon on their ships.

**5 — Kandy:** The last Sinhalese king is captured, not by battle but by betrayal. The British officers enter the Temple of the Tooth respectfully. The Kandyan chiefs sign the convention. The chronicle records: the land of Lanka was surrendered to the English.
