# Key Figures — Bakumatsu Era

## Tōjirō — Harbor Master of Uraga

The chapter opens through Tōjirō's eyes. He is not a historical figure; he is a representative of the system. For thirty years, he has stood at the boundary and enforced it through the simple fact of his presence and the edict behind him. The appearance of Perry's ships is not an external threat that Tōjirō must resist; it is a revelation that the boundary he has been protecting was always conditional on external consent.

His hesitation when Nakajima asks about firing is the moment when the system first recognizes its own fragility. He does not fire — and in not firing, he makes the choice that will end the shogunate. To fire would preserve the fiction of sakoku for a few more years. To negotiate — which is what the shogunate chooses — is to admit that the boundary was always permeable, always contingent on the world respecting it.

Tōjirō embodies the cost of the system's ending: a competent man, skilled in maintaining order through convention, confronting a situation where convention is no longer sufficient.

## Tōkugawa Iesada — The Dying Shogun (1853)

Iesada is the shogun at the moment Perry arrives, but he is already dying. The historical record suggests he was sickly and weak, ill-suited to the decision-making that the Perry crisis demands. He dies within months of the crisis's opening, and the succession struggle that follows paralyzes the shogunate's ability to respond.

Iesada does not appear directly in the chapter, but he is the absent center of the Edo council's debate. His inability to decide is the beginning of the shogunate's collapse. The institution that concentrated power in the shogun's hands discovers that it cannot function when the shogun is incompetent, sickly, or dying.

By the time Iesada dies, it is clear that the shogunate's problem is not merely the Americans. The Americans have simply revealed an institutional vulnerability that was always present: the concentration of authority in a single person, who may or may not be capable.

## Ii Naosuke — Chief Advisor (1854–1860)

Ii becomes the de facto leader of the shogunate after Iesada's death. He is the man who signs the Harris Treaty, who makes the decision to negotiate rather than resist, and who orders the Ansei Purge in an attempt to suppress the growing opposition to the treaty.

Ii represents the attempt to manage the crisis through force and authority. He understands that the Americans are too strong to resist militarily. He negotiates because negotiation is the only option. But his negotiation — visible to the domains as weakness, as the shogunate surrendering to foreign pressure — accelerates the domains' confidence that they can seize power themselves.

His assassination in 1860 is the symbolic breaking point. He is killed by samurai of the Mito domain, which has become a center of opposition to the treaty and the shogunate. His death confirms what everyone already understands: the shogunate can no longer enforce its authority even in Edo, even against its own samurai.

Ii is neither hero nor villain. He is a capable administrator attempting to manage an unmanageable situation. His failure is the shogunate's failure — not a failure of decision-making, but a failure of the system itself to adapt to the situation that Perry's ships have created.

## Tokugawa Yoshinobu — The Capable Shogun (1813–1913)

Yoshinobu appears in the chapter as a marginal figure at first — already understood to be capable, already positioned as the alternative to the sitting shogun. When Iemochi dies in 1864, Yoshinobu is made shogun. But by then, the shogunate's authority is already broken. The domains are armed. The emperor is being repositioned as the center of power. The military has proven itself incapable of suppressing domain rebellion.

Yoshinobu's arc is the inverse of Ii's. Where Ii tried to suppress opposition through force and failed, Yoshinobu recognizes the failure and chooses to restore power to the emperor voluntarily. This is not an act of surrender; it is recognition that the institution has already failed. By stepping aside rather than being overthrown, Yoshinobu preserves some measure of authority and status.

His decision in November 1867 to restore power to the emperor is the decision that ends the Bakumatsu era. It is also the decision that transforms the Meiji oligarchs from rebels into the architects of a new state. They had wanted imperial restoration; Yoshinobu gave it to them. In doing so, he ended the shogunate but preserved his own life and dignity.

Yoshinobu lived into the twentieth century and saw the Japan he had inherited transformed into a military power and then into something more complex. His choice in 1867 was the choice that made that transformation possible.

## The Domains — Satsuma and Choshū

Satsuma and Choshū do not have individual figures in the chapter, but they are present as forces. They are the domains that armed themselves first, sent their samurai to Europe to study, built their own armies, and eventually formed the alliance that would overthrow the shogunate.

They understood something the shogunate seemed not to understand: the answer to Western power was not to resist the West but to adopt Western knowledge. They were the domains that produced the men who would become the Meiji oligarchs.

Saigō Takamori, from Satsuma, becomes the military leader of the new government. Kido Takayoshi, from Choshū, becomes a leading architect of the new state. Ōkubo Toshimichi, from Satsuma, becomes the most powerful figure in the Meiji government.

These men are not yet oligarchs in the Bakumatsu period. They are young samurai whose domains have understood that the transformation of Japan is the only path to survival. By 1868, they would lead that transformation deliberately.

---

## The Imperial Loyalists — Sonnō Jōi (Revere the Emperor, Expel the Barbarians)

The imperial loyalists are the violent response to the shogunate's accommodation with the West. They assassinate foreign diplomats and shogunal moderates, in pursuit of a goal that is politically astute but strategically impossible: the emperor's restoration and the expulsion of the barbarians.

They are not wrong about where authority should reside. They are wrong about whether the barbarians can be expelled. Their violence accelerates the shogunate's collapse, but not in the direction they intended. Instead of expelling the barbarians, they help clear the way for the Meiji transformation — which will eventually turn the barbarians' knowledge into Japan's strength.

As individuals, the imperial loyalists are not named in the chapter. But their movement is the pressure that breaks the shogunate's attempt to manage the crisis peacefully.

---

## Moriyama Takichirô and Iwase Tadanari — The Negotiators

These two men negotiate with the Americans on behalf of the shogunate. They understand what everyone in Edo is debating: the Americans are too strong to resist militarily, and the only option is negotiation.

But negotiation visible to the domains = weakness visible to the domains. Their competence at negotiation is overshadowed by the political cost of the fact of negotiation itself. Moriyama will survive the Bakumatsu and serve in the Meiji government. Iwase will be less fortunate, but both men are representatives of the attempt to manage a crisis through diplomatic means.

They negotiate not because they support the opening of Japan, but because they understand that the alternative — military confrontation — would be catastrophic. Their pragmatism is correct, but it is also politically fatal to the institution they serve.

---

## The Emperor — Absent Center

The emperor does not appear directly in the chapter, but he is the absent center of all the conflicts. The shogunate has concentrated power in Edo and excluded the emperor from decision-making. But the crisis that Perry creates reveals that excluding the emperor from power has made the shogunate vulnerable.

The imperial loyalists want to restore the emperor to authority. The domains increasingly see the emperor as the legitimate center. By 1868, the emperor Meiji (who is fourteen years old) becomes the symbolic center of the new government.

The Meiji Constitution will position the emperor as sacred, sovereign, untouchable — more powerful symbolically than the shogun had ever been. But that power is contingent on the domains allowing it. The Meiji oligarchs are not loyal subjects of the emperor; they are men who have positioned the emperor as the symbol of their own authority.

The absent emperor is the future. The present shogun is the past. The chapter shows the moment when that reversal begins.
