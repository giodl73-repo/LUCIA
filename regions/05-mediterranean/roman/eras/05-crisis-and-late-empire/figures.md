# Figures — The Crisis and the Late Empire (Era 5)

## Diocletian (Gaius Aurelius Valerius Diocletianus) (244-311 CE)

Emperor 284-305. Creator of the Tetrarchy. Reformer of the tax system, army, and provincial administration. Persecutor of Christians. Retired voluntarily.

**What we know:** Diocletian's reforms are documented in Lactantius (*De Mortibus Persecutorum*), the panegyrics, and legal sources. His Price Edict survives in multiple inscriptions. His palace at Split survives as the core of the modern city. His voluntary abdication is unprecedented in Roman imperial history and is attested by multiple sources.

**Sources:** Lactantius, *De Mortibus Persecutorum*; Barnes, *The New Empire of Diocletian and Constantine* (1982); Corcoran, *The Empire of the Tetrarchs* (2000).

## Constantine (Flavius Valerius Constantinus) (272-337 CE)

Emperor 306-337. Founded Constantinople. Favored Christianity. Convened the Council of Nicaea. Unified the empire under sole rule.

**What we know:** Constantine's conversion and its circumstances are extensively debated. Eusebius of Caesarea, his biographer, presents him as divinely guided. Lactantius gives a different account of the vision. The Edict of Milan (313), the Council of Nicaea (325), and the founding of Constantinople (330) are firmly dated events. He was baptized on his deathbed.

**What we cannot know:** The sincerity of his conversion. The chapter presents the decision to favor Christianity from within the Roman political logic (divine patronage as state strategy) without claiming to know Constantine's interior religious experience.

**Sources:** Eusebius, *Vita Constantini*; Lactantius; Barnes, *Constantine and Eusebius* (1981); Drake, *Constantine and the Bishops* (2000).

## Augustine of Hippo (354-430 CE)

Bishop of Hippo Regius in North Africa. Author of the Confessions and the City of God. The most influential Latin theologian.

**What we know:** Augustine's works survive in enormous volume. The Confessions provide an autobiographical account unique in the ancient world. The City of God (written 413-426) responds to the sack of Roma in 410 by reframing the relationship between the Roman state and divine purpose. Augustine was trained in Roman rhetoric, practiced law briefly, taught rhetoric, and converted to Christianity in 386.

**Sources:** Augustine, *Confessiones*, *De Civitate Dei*; Brown, *Augustine of Hippo* (1967; rev. 2000).

## Stilicho (Flavius Stilicho) (359-408 CE)

Magister militum of the western empire. Of Vandal descent. Married to the emperor Theodosius's niece. Defended the western empire against Alaric's Visigoths and Radagaisus's Gothic incursion.

**What we know:** Stilicho is attested in Claudian's panegyrics (favorable), Zosimus (hostile), and Orosius. His execution in 408 on charges of treason, ordered by the emperor Honorius, removed the western empire's most capable military commander at its moment of greatest vulnerability. The sack of Roma in 410 followed within two years.

**Sources:** Claudian, panegyrics; Zosimus, *New History*; O'Flynn, *Generalissimos of the Western Roman Empire* (1983).

## The Christian Convert (composite figure)

A Roman citizen in the 4th century experiencing the transformation from persecution to dominance.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual convert is characterized in the chapter. The Christian experience is presented through the material culture (catacombs, basilicas), the institutional transformation (from house-churches to episcopal administration), and the theological arguments (Augustine's City of God). The three-generation shift (grandmother hiding scriptures, grandson as bishop) is a structural observation, not a biographical claim.

**Sources:** Brown, *The Rise of Western Christendom* (2003); MacMullen, *Christianizing the Roman Empire* (1984).

## A Note on Sources

The late empire is documented from multiple perspectives that often conflict. Eusebius writes as a Christian triumphalist. Ammianus Marcellinus writes as a pagan military historian in the tradition of Tacitus. Orosius writes Christian apologetics. Zosimus writes pagan criticism of the Christian emperors. The Theodosian and Justinianic legal codes provide administrative evidence. The archaeological record shows the transformation of cities, the construction of churches, and the contraction of western urban centers. The chapter draws on all of these while refusing the "decline and fall" framework that Gibbon's influence made dominant.

The "fall of Rome" is not a single event but a historiographic construction. The chapter presents what the sources show: a western empire that fragmented over decades, an eastern empire that continued for another millennium, a legal and linguistic and institutional tradition that persisted through and beyond both, and a religious transformation that was experienced from inside as both crisis and renewal.
