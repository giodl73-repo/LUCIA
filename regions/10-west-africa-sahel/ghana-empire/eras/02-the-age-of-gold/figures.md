# Figures — The Age of Gold

## Disclosure

The Ghana Empire of the Age of Gold (c. 700–1000 CE) presents a fundamental archival challenge: no individual rulers of this period are named with confidence in surviving sources. The Arabic geographers — al-Bakri, Ibn Hawqal — recorded what they learned from merchant informants, and those informants described institutions and ceremonies rather than individuals. The Soninke oral tradition preserves genealogies, but these cannot be reliably synchronized with the textual or archaeological record for the 700–1000 period.

The figures below are composite constructions grounded in attested sources. Where the record is thin, this section says so. This is honest practice for a culture whose historical record was maintained primarily in oral tradition and whose written documentation comes from outsiders who never entered the royal city. Per v1.4 Rubric — Archaeological Figures: a figure does not need a name, but a figure needs a body.

---

## The Merchant from Sijilmasa

He is a composite. No individual Berber or Arab merchant from Sijilmasa is named in the sources for the Age of Gold period, but al-Bakri's account of the Ghana court is built from merchant testimony — men who made the Sijilmasa-to-Kumbi Saleh crossing and reported back on what they saw. The chapter's merchant is drawn from the accumulated testimony of those travelers.

His body — the ache in the spine, the thirst, the cold mathematics of the negotiation — is extrapolated from what is known about the crossing. Forty days of desert at a sitting pace. Water rationed by the skin. Sleep disrupted by the need to maintain the caravan's rhythm. These physical facts are documented in the Arabic geographical literature, and they would have been true of any merchant who made the crossing. The specific merchant in this chapter did not exist as an individual. He existed as a type — documented, real, and very numerous.

His function in the chapter: he discovers the dual-city structure through experience, giving the reader access to the commercial system from inside the act of commerce. He knows the tax rates the way he knows his own bones. He cannot enter the royal city. This limitation is historically correct and narratively productive.

**Source:** Al-Bakri, *Kitab al-Masalik wa'l-Mamalik*, c. 1068 CE. The merchant figures in al-Bakri's account are the basis for this composite. Levtzion, *Ancient Ghana and Mali* (1973), provides the scholarly framework for understanding who these merchants were and what their crossing involved.

## The Wangara Gold-Washer

He has no name in any source. The Wangara gold producers — Soninke and Mande speakers who controlled the alluvial goldfields of Bambuk and Buré — are described in the Arabic geographical sources only from outside: what their trading practices looked like, what goods they accepted, what their silence meant. No Wangara person is individually attested in the written record.

The figure in Section II of the chapter is constructed from what is known about alluvial gold washing in West African forest streams. The technical practice — wading into cold water, working a shallow pan through streambed gravel, tilting to let the lighter material wash away, watching the placer gold settle — is historically attested for this region. The specific sensory details (cold water, burning sun on the shoulders, the weight of gold settling in the pan's bottom) are what those practices would have felt like to the body doing them. This is v1.4 Archaeological Figures applied: not a named individual, but a figure with a body, performing a documented practice in a documented environment.

His function in the chapter: he gives the gold its origin. Without him, the gold is an abstraction — a commodity flowing north. With him, the gold is the product of specific human labor performed in specific physical conditions. The Wangara's deliberate invisibility to the merchant world (the goldfield locations kept secret by policy) means this figure appears only briefly and only from inside his own world. He does not appear as the merchants saw him. He appears as he was.

**Source:** The Wangara are described in al-Bakri and subsequent Arabic geographical sources. The practice of alluvial gold washing in Bambuk and Buré is reconstructed from the geographical literature and from the broader archaeological and historical literature on West African gold production. Mauny, *Tableau géographique de l'Ouest africain au Moyen Age* (1961), provides the geographic framework.

## The Ghana (The Title)

He is not a person in this chapter. He is a title, an institution, a position between the sacred grove and the counting house. Al-Bakri's description is the fullest in the sources: the high cap, the fine cloth, the ten horses in gold-embroidered blankets, the ten pages with golden shields, the dogs on their gold and silver leads, the great drum, the prostration of subjects.

This description dates to al-Bakri's informants' time — likely the mid-eleventh century, at the end or shortly after the Age of Gold period — and is applied to the broader era on the reasonable assumption that the ceremonial patterns were established earlier and maintained with deliberate conservatism. This is a standard assumption in Levtzion's reconstruction and is supported by the material evidence from Kumbi Saleh excavations, which confirm a substantial royal establishment consistent with al-Bakri's description.

The Ghana in this chapter is composite: the institution constructed from al-Bakri's account and from the broader scholarly understanding of sacred kingship in the western Sudan. He represents the office, not an individual. The reader should understand that no individual name could be supplied for the 700–1000 period with confidence.

What the chapter can give him: the logic of the office. The Ghana held the balance because the balance held him. The sacred covenant and the commercial covenant were not separable. A Ghana who converted to Islam would have destroyed one half of the arrangement. A Ghana who expelled the Muslims would have destroyed the other. The men who held this title across three centuries understood this with the precision of men whose survival depended on understanding it.

**Source:** Al-Bakri, *Kitab al-Masalik wa'l-Mamalik*. Levtzion, *Ancient Ghana and Mali* (1973). The sacred kingship framework draws on the broader scholarly literature on divine kingship in West Africa.

## The Priests of the Sacred Grove

They appear in the chapter as a collective presence rather than named individuals. Al-Bakri mentions the sacred grove and the religious practices associated with it; the priests are not individually described in any source.

What is documented: that the grove existed (al-Bakri), that entry was restricted (al-Bakri), that it was maintained (inferred from the ongoing functionality of the covenant system). The specific rites are not documented in writing, because the Arabic writers could not enter and the Soninke who could did not record them. This gap in the written record is not an absence of practice. It is evidence of a knowledge system maintained through different means than writing.

The chapter attributes to the priests what can be reasonably inferred: tending the grove (which required active maintenance — no cutting, protection from the seasonal fires that cleared the surrounding Sahel), performing the rites that renewed the covenant, overseeing the burial ceremonies when a Ghana died. The sensory details of the grove (the darkness under the canopy, the moisture the surrounding laterite has surrendered, the changed register of the insects) are consistent with what a grove of mature, protected trees in the Sahel would actually provide.

**Source:** Al-Bakri on the grove and its restricted access. The broader West African sacred grove tradition provides the comparative framework. Conrad, "The Conquest That Never Was" (1982), provides context for the Soninke oral tradition's relationship to the covenant system.

## Bida the Serpent

Bida is not a historical figure. Bida is a figure in the Soninke oral tradition: the great serpent who maintained the covenant with the Soninke people, ensuring the rains and the gold in exchange for ritual obligation. The legend of Bida's death and the subsequent collapse of Wagadu is the Soninke's own explanation for what happened to their kingdom.

The chapter presents the Bida narrative not as background mythology but as epistemological framework: the Soninke understanding of why the gold came out of the ground (because the earth, through Bida, consented to give it) is not a superstition layered over the economic reality. It is the way the Soninke understood the economic reality. The commercial explanation and the covenant explanation described the same phenomenon at different scales of analysis.

The dating of the Bida legend to the Age of Gold period specifically is uncertain. The chapter places the legend in Section IV as the framework within which the covenant system operated during the peak period, without claiming that the specific narrative about Bida's death belongs to this era. The Soninke told that story looking backward; the chapter presents it looking forward into what the balance required.

**Source:** The Wagadu/Bida legend cycle is documented in later recordings of Soninke oral tradition. Conrad's scholarship on Mande oral traditions provides the framework for understanding the legend's historiographic status. Per Conrad: the oral tradition is a parallel historical record, not myth requiring verification against the Arabic sources.
