# R2-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Review — Notes, Round 2

**Chapter:** Yanomami — Era 1: The Parima World
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The production bible has addressed my primary concern from Round 1: Section 3 (hekura landscape) explicitly commits to showing the knowledge system at the level of method. I want to make sure what this means in practice for the chapter.

Method level, for the Yanomami shamanic tradition, means at minimum:

First, the preparation of yakoana. The Virola resin is not simply dried and inhaled. The preparation involves specific bark-stripping technique, the addition of other plant materials (ash from specific trees, sometimes the seeds of other plants), slow drying over low heat, and grinding. The specific procedure varies by community and by shapori. The chapter should render at least part of this process — not as a recipe but as a skilled act, the way a navigator reading the hull is a skilled act.

Second, the learning of specific hekura. Kopenawa's account of his own initiation in *The Falling Sky* includes extended description of how a shapori learns to host a specific hekura: the fasting, the specific song that invites that hekura, the sensation of the hekura settling, the visual and physical signs that distinguish one hekura from another. The chapter should use this level of specificity for at least two or three hekura — the waterfall's hekura from the opening, and perhaps the hekura of the wind and the jaguar, which Kopenawa discusses extensively.

Third, the diagnostic protocol. What does Aramo actually see when the waterfall's hekura shows him the child's problem? Not "information" — specific information. A color, a movement, a location in the child's body, a sign associated with a particular spirit territory. The outside anthropological literature tends to describe the diagnosis as occurring and then summarize its content. The inside understanding is that the diagnosis is a perception, as specific and verifiable (within the system) as a blood test.

On the tepui cosmology: Marahuaka is Kopenawa's central sacred mountain. The chapter should note that different Yanomami communities may have different cosmological centers, and that the tepui cosmos is locally specific rather than universal. The v1.3 Knowledge System at Method Level amendment specifically warns against "they had a sophisticated tradition" without showing the tradition operating. The tepui section should show a specific shapori in a specific place learning from a specific ancient hekura — not a general description of tepui cosmology.

The source question about Virola species: V. theiodora is the most extensively documented in the ethnobotanical literature (Schultes, 1985, and Lizot's accounts from Venezuelan communities). Use it and note the community variation in figures.md.

**The pressure point I'd watch for:** The chapter risks describing the hekura world at atmospheric level — vivid but general — rather than at method level. The commitment in the notes is correct. The execution will require specificity that makes some readers slightly uncomfortable because it makes the practice legible as a technical system rather than an exotic ritual. That discomfort is correct. Lean into it.
