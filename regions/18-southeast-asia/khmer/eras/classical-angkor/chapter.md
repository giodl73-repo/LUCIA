# Classical Angkor

## I. The Holding of Water

The water came first.

Before the towers, before the carved sandstone, before the kings who called themselves gods, there was the problem of water. The monsoon delivered it in sheets between May and October, drowning the plain around the Tonle Sap in a brown flood that smelled of earth and rotting vegetation and fish. Then the monsoon left, and the plain cracked, and the rice withered, and the people who depended on the rice had a choice: move with the water, or hold it.

They held it.

The first barays were cuts in the earth, long rectangles dug by thousands of hands into the laterite clay north of the great lake. The East Baray, when it was finished, measured seven kilometers by nearly two. The monsoon filled it. The dry months emptied it, slowly, through channels cut into the clay, some lined with sandstone blocks at the outlet points, the blocks fitted without mortar because the weight of the water above pressed them together and the precision of the cut made mortar unnecessary. The water traveled from the baray through the channels into the rice paddies that spread across the plain in a grid that, from the summit of the temple-mountain, must have looked like the squares on a cloth. Each paddy held its portion. Each portion fed its families. The families fed the kingdom, and the kingdom built the barays, and the barays held the rain, and the cycle turned on itself like a wheel that no one could remember starting.

This was not irrigation in the way a modern engineer would use the word. This was the kingdom made visible. The king who ordered the baray dug was the king who controlled the water, and the king who controlled the water controlled the rice, and the king who controlled the rice was the king. The inscriptions at Sdok Kak Thom, carved in sandstone in 1052 CE, describe the founding act of the Angkor state not as a military conquest or a diplomatic treaty but as a ritual. In the year that corresponds to 802 CE, a Brahmin priest named Hiranyadama performed the devaraja ceremony on the summit of Phnom Kulen, the sandstone mountain forty kilometers northeast of the future Angkor. He consecrated Jayavarman II as the chakravartin, the universal sovereign, the god-king whose body was the kingdom and whose temple-mountain was the axis of the world. The ceremony's purpose, the inscription says, was to ensure that the Khmer land would never again be subject to Java. Jayavarman II himself had come from Java, or from a Javanese-influenced court on the mainland, and the declaration was a severance: the Khmer would be their own.

This was a state built on a ritual declaration. The declaration had weight because the Brahmin who spoke it controlled the liturgical technology that connected the king to Shiva, and the connection to Shiva was not metaphor. It was the mechanism by which royal authority worked. The linga that Hiranyadama consecrated on Phnom Kulen was the seat of the god's presence within the kingdom. The king's body was the god's dwelling on earth. The temple-mountain that housed the linga was Mount Meru, the center of the cosmos, and the moat that surrounded the temple was the cosmic ocean, and the kingdom that extended outward from the moat was the world that the god-king protected by the mere fact of his correct consecration.

The Khmer did not borrow this from India the way a student borrows from a teacher. They took what they needed. The Sanskrit language, the Shaiva ritual vocabulary, the cosmological architecture of Meru and the cosmic ocean, the concept of the devaraja itself came through centuries of contact along the maritime trade routes that connected mainland and island Southeast Asia to the subcontinent. But the Khmer made these elements serve Khmer purposes. The devaraja ceremony at Phnom Kulen was not a transplanted Indian ritual. It was a Khmer political technology, spoken in Sanskrit because Sanskrit carried the liturgical authority, but designed to solve a Khmer problem: how to unify the competing polities of the Mekong plain under a single sovereign whose authority could not be challenged by local chiefs, because his authority came not from military dominance or clan prestige but from the gods themselves.

Jayavarman II spent the decades after Phnom Kulen moving his capital across the plain, testing sites, building and rebuilding. He did not build Angkor Wat. He did not build the Bayon. He built the conditions. The ritual technology was in place. The hydraulic infrastructure was beginning. The sandstone quarries at Phnom Kulen were open. The labor system that would move those stones across the plain was being organized. And the water kept coming, and the barays kept holding it, and the rice kept growing.

## II. The Temple-Mountain and the Kingdom Below

Each king built his mountain.

This was not vanity, though vanity sometimes traveled alongside it. The temple-mountain was the devaraja made stone. When a new king took the throne and performed the consecration, he required his own linga, his own sanctuary, his own axis mundi. The temple-mountain his predecessor had built still stood, still held the dead king's divine energy, still received offerings. But the living king needed his own connection to the cosmos, and that connection required a new mountain.

Indravarman I, who ruled in the late ninth century, built the Bakong at Roluos, south of what would become Angkor proper. The Bakong rose in five tiers of sandstone, a stepped pyramid with a central tower that housed the royal linga. Around it he built the Indratataka, the first great baray in the Angkor region, three kilometers long and nearly a kilometer wide. The temple and the reservoir were a pair. The mountain and the water. The axis of the cosmos and the means of feeding the people who gathered around it.

His son Yashovarman I moved the capital north, to the site that would become Angkor, and built the East Baray, larger than anything that had come before, and the temple of Phnom Bakheng on a natural hill that he shaped into a five-tiered pyramid. From the summit of Bakheng, the king could see the baray to the east, the rice paddies spreading in every direction, the line of the Kulen hills to the northeast where the sandstone was cut, the shimmer of the Tonle Sap to the south. The kingdom was visible from the temple. The temple was visible from the kingdom. This was the point.

The inscriptions from this period are in two languages. The Sanskrit inscriptions, composed by court poets trained in Indian literary conventions, praised the king in elaborate verse, connected his genealogy to the gods, and recorded his temple foundations with the weight of cosmic significance. The Old Khmer inscriptions, written in the everyday language of the court and the administration, recorded the practical machinery of the kingdom: which families held which land, which temples received which endowments, how many servants worked the temple estates, what rice they harvested, what cloth they wove, what labor they owed. The two languages operated in parallel. Sanskrit held the sacred. Old Khmer held the real. And the gap between the sacred and the real was the space in which the kingdom actually functioned.

The temple estates were enormous. An inscription from the reign of Rajendravarman II, in the mid-tenth century, lists the endowment of a single temple: thousands of workers, farmers, weavers, dancers, musicians, cooks, rice-huskers, and parasol-bearers. The workers were organized by function and by obligation. They owed their labor not to the king directly but to the god who dwelt in the temple, which meant they owed it to the king who served as the god's regent on earth. The chain of obligation ran from the rice paddy to the temple to the god to the king and back again, a closed circuit in which labor, food, devotion, and authority all flowed through the same channels, and the channels were literal. The water that irrigated the temple's rice came from the baray that the king had built, and the rice that the water grew fed the workers who maintained the temple that housed the god that legitimized the king.

The hydraulic system grew with each reign. By the eleventh century, the network of barays, canals, moats, and distribution channels extended across a landscape that modern LiDAR surveys have revealed to be vastly larger than the temple complexes visible above the forest canopy. The Greater Angkor surveys, begun in the early 2000s, showed a low-density urban landscape stretching across a thousand square kilometers, connected by roads and waterways, with neighborhoods organized around local reservoirs and ponds that fed off the main canal system. The population estimates for this landscape range from three-quarters of a million to over a million people. What the forest hid from view for centuries was not a collection of temples with a city nearby. It was an entire hydraulic world, a landscape engineered to capture, hold, and distribute the monsoon's gift across a plain that would otherwise have cycled between flood and drought.

The labor that built this landscape was not slave labor in the way that word is sometimes casually applied to ancient construction projects. The Old Khmer inscriptions reveal a stratified system of obligation in which different categories of people owed different kinds of service. Some were attached permanently to temple estates. Some owed seasonal labor. Some were war captives whose status was fixed. Others were free farmers whose obligation to the state took the form of corvee labor on the barays and canal works during the dry season, when the fields did not need them. The system was not gentle. But it was legible. The inscriptions recorded who owed what, and the recording was the system's claim to order.

## III. Angkor Wat

In the early twelfth century, Suryavarman II began building.

He had taken the throne through force, defeating a rival claimant and consolidating his authority over a kingdom that had fractured during a period of competing courts. The inscriptions describe a scene at his coronation in which he leapt onto the war elephant of his rival and killed him in single combat, or at least the court poets described it that way, which served the same purpose. A king who had seized power by violence needed legitimacy that violence alone could not provide. He needed a mountain.

The mountain he built was Angkor Wat.

It took approximately thirty years to complete. The sandstone came from Phnom Kulen, fifty kilometers to the northeast, transported on rafts along canals cut for the purpose and on ox-carts along roads that had been leveled and packed. The laterite that formed the core of the structure came from quarries closer to the site. The sandstone was the facing, the visible surface, the skin of the mountain. Beneath it, laterite blocks, heavier and rougher, carried the weight. The entire structure rested on a sand base that had been pounded into the clay substrate of the plain to create a foundation stable enough to support towers that rose sixty-five meters above the surrounding forest.

The plan was the cosmos. Five towers, the central one tallest, representing the five peaks of Mount Meru. A moat two hundred meters wide, representing the cosmic ocean. Three concentric galleries, representing the mountain ranges that in Hindu cosmology ring the central mountain. The whole structure oriented to the west, which was unusual for a Khmer temple and has generated scholarly debate. Some argue the western orientation connected Angkor Wat to Vishnu, the deity to whom Suryavarman dedicated it. Others argue the western orientation marked the temple as funerary, built to house the king's remains. The Khmer themselves left no explanation, and the silence is its own kind of evidence. The temple's orientation was legible to those who built it. That it is not fully legible to us is a measure of what the centuries have carried away.

The bas-reliefs covered over eight hundred meters of gallery wall. The southern gallery depicted the army of Suryavarman II on the march: the king seated on his war elephant, shaded by parasols, his generals beside him, his soldiers arranged by regiment, the Siamese mercenaries distinguished by their clothing and their position in the column, the Khmer soldiers by theirs. This was not mythological art. This was a historical king placing himself on the wall of a cosmic temple, declaring that his military campaigns were continuous with the battles of the gods.

And on the eastern gallery, the Churning of the Ocean of Milk. Devas and asuras pulling the great serpent Vasuki wound around Mount Mandara, churning the cosmic ocean to produce the amrita, the elixir of immortality. The carving ran for forty-nine meters. Ninety-two asuras on one side, eighty-eight devas on the other, their feet braced, their muscles straining, the serpent taut between them, and at the center, Vishnu overseeing the churning, four-armed, calm while the universe convulsed around him. The carving was so precise that the individual fingers of the devas were distinct, and the fish and crocodiles and nagas tumbling in the churning ocean were each separately rendered. The artisans who carved this were not named. The inscriptions recorded the king who ordered the temple and the priests who consecrated it. The hands that held the chisels are unrecoverable.

Suryavarman II did not live to see the bas-reliefs completed. He died during a military campaign against the Dai Viet, the Vietnamese kingdom to the northeast, sometime around 1150 CE. The temple stood. It stands still. It is the largest religious monument on Earth, and it was built not by an empire at the height of its power reaching for ornament, but by a king who needed to make his claim on the cosmos visible in stone, because in the Khmer world, the claim that was not made visible was the claim that did not hold.

## IV. The Kingdom Between the Reliefs

But Angkor was not only its temples.

Zhou Daguan, a Chinese diplomat who visited the city in 1296, wrote an account that is the only surviving eyewitness description of Angkor in something close to its functioning state. Zhou was not Khmer. He measured what he saw against what he knew from Song and Yuan China, and his comparisons reveal as much about him as about Angkor. But he saw what was there, and he wrote it down, and what he wrote is a city.

The houses of the ordinary people, he reported, were thatched, and no one dared use tiles, which were reserved for the temples and the palace. The markets were run by women. Trade was conducted in rice, cloth, and small quantities of gold and silver. Chinese goods were valued: silk, ceramics, copper. The people bathed in the ponds and canals, men and women together, which Zhou noted with the surprise of a man whose own culture did not do this. He described the palace, the golden towers, the parasols that indicated rank, the processions in which the king rode on an elephant and his women marched before him carrying his regalia. He described the festivals, the fires, the performances.

He also described the monks. Buddhist monks in saffron robes, their heads shaved. Brahmins with the sacred thread. The two traditions operating in parallel within the same city, serving different functions in the kingdom's ritual life. The Brahmins performed the consecrations, the royal rituals, the temple rites that connected the king to Shiva or Vishnu. The Buddhist monks offered another path, available to those whose relationship to the sacred did not run through the royal linga.

Zhou's account is evidence, but it is not Khmer evidence. It is the record of an outsider who saw a functioning city and tried to translate it into categories his own court would understand. The Khmer evidence is in the stone. The inscriptions, the bas-reliefs, the temples themselves. The Khmer did not write prose accounts of their daily life. They carved their kings' names and their gods' stories and their temple endowments into sandstone, and the sandstone survived what paper and palm leaf did not.

What the stone tells us is a kingdom organized around the flow of water and the flow of devotion, and the two were not separable. The barays fed the paddies. The paddies fed the people. The people built the temples. The temples housed the gods. The gods legitimized the king. The king ordered the barays dug. The circle was closed, and its medium was water.

## V. The Faces of the Bayon

Jayavarman VII came to power through catastrophe.

In 1177, Cham forces reached the Tonle Sap, crossed the great lake by water, and sacked Angkor. Whether they came entirely by sea or partially overland to the lake, the inscriptions do not fully clarify, but the result was the same: the enemy arrived through the water. The capital fell. The king was killed. The inscriptions describe the event with a brevity that registers the shock: the enemy came by water, through the very channels that sustained the kingdom, and the kingdom that had held the water could not hold the boats.

Jayavarman VII was not yet king. He had spent years away from the capital, in circumstances the inscriptions do not fully clarify. When he returned, he fought the Cham, drove them out, and claimed the throne. He did not merely restore what had been broken. He rebuilt on a different foundation.

He was a Buddhist.

The kings before him had dedicated their temple-mountains to Shiva or to Vishnu. Jayavarman VII dedicated his to the bodhisattva Lokeshvara, the lord who looks down, the compassionate one who hears the cries of the suffering world. He built the Bayon at the center of his new walled city, Angkor Thom, and the Bayon was the supreme expression of a form that may have had precedents at Banteay Chhmar and other sites from his own building program, but that had never been attempted at this scale or density. Instead of a single central tower housing a linga, the Bayon rose in a forest of towers, each tower carved with four enormous faces looking outward in the four cardinal directions. Two hundred and sixteen faces. Serene, smiling faintly, eyes half-closed, lips curved in an expression that has been described as compassion, as detachment, as watchfulness, as the look of a being who sees suffering and has chosen to remain in the world to address it.

Whose face? The scholarly argument has persisted for over a century. The face resembles the known portraits of Jayavarman VII. The face also resembles the conventional representations of Lokeshvara. The Khmer may not have distinguished the question. The devaraja concept had always placed the king's identity within the god's identity. Under Jayavarman VII, the god changed. The living Shiva became the living bodhisattva. The mountain that housed the linga became the mountain that radiated compassion in every direction. The faces looked outward from the center of the city, outward from the center of the kingdom, outward from the center of the cosmos. The king who saw everything. The god who heard everything. The distinction between the two was the point at which the Khmer sacred and the Khmer political became the same thing, and the face that did not resolve into one or the other was the expression of that unity.

Jayavarman VII built more than the Bayon. His inscriptions, which survive in unusual quantity and detail, record a building program that stretched across the empire. He built 102 hospitals. The inscriptions list them by location and by endowment: how many staff, what medicines, what rice. He built 121 rest houses along the roads at intervals of approximately fifteen kilometers, so that travelers and pilgrims crossing the kingdom could stop, rest, and eat. He paved the roads. He built bridges. The Ta Prohm inscription, which describes the endowment of a temple dedicated to his mother, lists the resources of the temple estate: 79,365 people serving the temple, 12,640 of them entitled to eat there daily. The numbers may be inflated. They are also inscribed in stone, which suggests they were meant to be read and remembered, and their specificity is itself a claim.

The Buddhist turn under Jayavarman VII was not a revolution. It was a translation. The devaraja concept persisted. The king remained the axis of the cosmos. The temple-mountain remained the center of the world. But the sacred vocabulary shifted. Where Shaiva Angkor had placed the royal linga at the center of the temple, Buddhist Angkor placed the image of the compassionate bodhisattva. Where the earlier inscriptions had praised the king's ferocity in war and his identity with Shiva the destroyer, Jayavarman VII's inscriptions described a king who suffered with his people. One inscription reads: "He suffered the illness of his subjects more than his own; for it is the suffering of the people that is the suffering of the king, not his own suffering."

This may have been propaganda. It may have been sincere. It may have been both at once, the way that all royal pronouncements in all kingdoms are both at once. What it was, undeniably, was Khmer. The bodhisattva ideal came through Buddhist teaching. The hospital network came through Jayavarman VII's administration. The faces on the Bayon came through Khmer sculptors working in Khmer sandstone on a Khmer mountain. What Indian Buddhism provided was a vocabulary. What the Khmer built with that vocabulary was their own.

## VI. The Stone and the Water

The kingdom's two archives were sandstone and water, and they told different stories about the same truth.

The sandstone held the kings. Every temple inscription named the sovereign, praised his lineage, connected him to the gods, recorded his endowments. The stone was permanent by design. It was cut, dressed, carved, and mortared into structures that were meant to last as long as the cosmos they represented. And the stone has lasted. The inscriptions that tell us about Jayavarman II and Suryavarman II and Jayavarman VII are readable today because they were carved deep enough to survive a thousand years of rain and root and war.

The water held the kingdom. The barays, the canals, the moats, the distribution channels, the local reservoirs that collected runoff and fed it into the paddies during the dry months. The water system was the mechanism by which the Angkor plain supported its population. Without it, the monsoon cycle would have meant abundance for four months and scarcity for eight. With it, the kingdom could grow rice in quantities that sustained a population dense enough to build temples, maintain armies, and project power across mainland Southeast Asia from the Vietnamese coast to the edge of the Burmese kingdoms.

The West Baray, the largest, measured eight kilometers by two and held approximately fifty-three million cubic meters of water. It was the single largest work of premodern hydraulic construction in Southeast Asia. The labor required to dig it, to move the laterite and clay, to build the earthen dykes that contained it, to cut the inlet and outlet channels that connected it to the canal network, would have consumed tens of thousands of workers over years. The Old Khmer inscriptions that record temple endowments list the corvee obligations that fed these projects: farmers owed labor during the dry season, when the fields did not need them, and the kingdom used that labor to extend the hydraulic system that made the fields productive, which made the kingdom that demanded the labor possible.

This was the structural engine of the Angkor state. Not the temples. Not the devaraja ritual. Not the military campaigns. Water. The temples were the kingdom's face. The water was its body. The devaraja was the theology that explained why the king had the right to organize the labor that dug the barays that held the water that grew the rice that fed the people that built the temples. Remove the water and the theology becomes a ceremony performed over an empty plain.

The Angkor state lasted as long as the water held. The barays filled. The channels carried the water. The rice grew. The kingdom endured. And when the balance shifted, when the channels silted and the infrastructure aged and the labor that maintained it was diverted to the unending construction demands of kings like Jayavarman VII, the system did not collapse in a single season. It thinned. The outer channels delivered less. The paddies at the margins failed first. The population at the edges moved closer to the lake, where the natural flood cycle still provided fish and soil. The temples remained. The barays remained. The channels remained. But they held less, and what they held fed fewer. Tree-ring evidence from the region suggests prolonged droughts in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, seasons when the monsoon weakened and the barays did not fill to their former levels. A system designed for surplus met a climate that delivered less, and the kingdom that had been built on abundance found itself governing scarcity with an apparatus that could not adjust.

The Thai kingdom of Ayutthaya rose to the west. The Cham pushed from the east. The kings who followed Jayavarman VII reverted to Shaivism, and many of the Buddhist images that Jayavarman VII had placed in the temples were defaced, chiseled away, replaced with lingas. The iconoclasm was selective and gradual, not a systematic purge: some Buddhist images survived untouched, and the central well of the Bayon itself held a great Buddha image that was later broken apart and would not be reassembled until the twentieth century. The faces of the Bayon remained, because you cannot chisel away two hundred and sixteen faces without destroying the towers themselves, and no one was prepared to destroy the Bayon. But the contest between the Buddhist and the Shaiva factions within the court consumed energy that the kingdom could not spare. The inscriptions grew fewer. The building program slowed, then stopped. The last major Sanskrit inscription at Angkor dates to the late thirteenth century. After that, the stone went quiet.

Zhou Daguan visited in 1296, and what he saw was a city still functioning, still magnificent, still organized around its barays and its temples and its king. But within a century and a half, the capital would move south to Phnom Penh, closer to the Mekong and the trade routes that had shifted away from the inland hydraulic plain toward the coast. Angkor was not abandoned in a day. People continued to live there. Monks tended the temples. The forest grew into the courtyards and over the walls and through the stone, sending roots into the sandstone that split the blocks apart with the patience of centuries.

The water remained. The Tonle Sap still floods each monsoon season, reversing the current of its river, swelling to five times its dry-season extent. The fishermen who work the lake today live in floating villages that rise and fall with the water, and the rhythm of their year is the rhythm that the Angkor kings organized into a kingdom. The barays are still visible. Some still hold water. The West Baray has water in it today, though the channels that once distributed it to the paddies have been silted and overgrown for six hundred years.

The stone stands. The faces of the Bayon still look outward in four directions from every tower, their expression unchanged since the day the sculptors set down their chisels. They look out over a forest that was once a city, over channels that were once the bloodstream of a kingdom, over a plain that was once the most productive rice landscape in Southeast Asia. The face does not resolve. It is the king and the bodhisattva at once, the sovereign and the compassionate one, the one who rules and the one who hears the cries of suffering. The Khmer understood that these were not two things. They were one thing, expressed in stone, looking in every direction, seeing everything, saying nothing, while the water that made the kingdom rose and fell with the monsoon, as it had before the temples, as it has since.
