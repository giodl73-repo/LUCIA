# Mwaant Yav — "The Franchise Kingdom"

The Lunda state — centered on the Kasai River system in what is now the Democratic Republic of Congo — invented, or at least perfected, a model of political expansion that required no permanent army and no ongoing coercion: the franchise. The Mwata Yamvo (Mwaant Yav), the title of the Lunda paramount ruler, held his authority through a system of titled subordinates who carried Lunda political culture to new territories and founded daughter states that acknowledged the Mwaant Yav's ceremonial supremacy while governing themselves independently. Each new state was bound to the center not by force but by the titles, the ceremonies, and the political vocabulary they had received from it. Lunda political culture spread from Angola to Zambia to the eastern Congo — a territory wider than Western Europe — through this franchise mechanism.

The founding myth of the Lunda kingdom connects it to the Luba world: the Lunda queen Lueji married a Luba hunter named Chibinda Ilunga (a son of the Luba hero Kalala Ilunga), and through this marriage the bulopwe — the sacred kingship — was transmitted from Luba to Lunda. The marriage is understood in the tradition as a political event as well as a personal one: Lueji gave Chibinda Ilunga the lukanu, the royal bracelet that embodied Lunda sovereignty, and through this act constituted the new kingdom. That a woman founded the Lunda state by transmitting authority through marriage — and that this act is celebrated rather than erased in the tradition — tells us something important about how the Lunda world understood gender and political authority.

## What Defined Them

**The lukanu as constitutive authority.** The royal bracelet of the Lunda — the lukanu — was more than a symbol; it was the object in which Lunda sovereignty resided. The Mwaant Yav wore the lukanu; to hold the lukanu was to hold the kingdom. The tradition that Lueji transmitted the lukanu to Chibinda Ilunga, and that this act constituted the kingdom, places a portable sacred object at the center of Lunda political theology. This meant that Lunda authority could travel — the titled subordinates who founded daughter states carried Lunda political culture in their ceremonies and their titles, connecting the new state to the center through cultural rather than physical links.

**The franchise mechanism in practice.** When a Lunda titleholder went out to found a new state, he took with him the political vocabulary — the titles, the ceremonies, the political culture — that he had received from the Mwaant Yav. He installed this culture in the new territory by performing the installation ceremonies, establishing the political hierarchy of titles, and creating the ritual relationships between the new state and the center. The daughter state was not a colony or a province; it was an autonomous political entity that acknowledged the Mwaant Yav's ceremonial primacy and maintained cultural connections to the center while governing itself. This is precisely the franchise model: the parent organization transmits the culture and the branding; the franchisee operates independently.

**Lueji and the gender politics of foundation.** The Lunda founding tradition is unusual in the degree to which it acknowledges that sovereignty was originally held by a woman — Lueji — and that the kingdom was constituted through her choice to transmit it. The tradition does not present this as an anomaly; Lueji is the legitimate ruler who chooses her successor and her husband simultaneously. The subsequent history of the Lunda state shows recurring evidence of women's political authority — the Mwaant Yav's mother and the senior queen holding important political roles — that reflects the founding tradition's acknowledgment of female political capacity.

**The copper belt and the trade spine.** The Lunda world's commercial foundation was the Katanga copper belt — the same resource base that underwrote the Luba kingdom to the north. Cross-shaped copper ingots from Katanga circulated as currency across a vast territory, and the Lunda control of, or relationship to, the copper sources gave the kingdom access to the primary prestige metal of south-central Africa. The eastern Lunda state of Kazembe, on Lake Mweru, also developed a substantial ivory and slave trade that connected to the Swahili coast through the interior routes — a connection that Lacerda e Almeida (the Portuguese governor who died trying to cross from the Zambezi to Kazembe in 1798) was attempting to develop for Portuguese commercial interests.

**The Chokwe disruption.** The Chokwe — a people of the Angolan-Congolese border zone who had been incorporated into the Lunda commercial system as hunters and traders — expanded dramatically in the nineteenth century, partly fueled by the Atlantic trade's demand for ivory and beeswax, and eventually invaded and disrupted the Lunda heartland. The Mwaant Yav was expelled from his capital in the 1880s. This disruption, combined with Belgian colonial penetration from the east, ended the Lunda kingdom as a functioning state. The Mwaant Yav title survives today in the Democratic Republic of Congo, maintained as a ceremonial institution.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Marriage:** Lueji hands Chibinda Ilunga the lukanu. She is the legitimate ruler; she has chosen. The political tradition will remember this choice as the founding of a kingdom. She is not erased from the story. She is the story.

**2 — The First Franchise:** A Lunda titleholder is traveling east with his followers. He is going to establish a new state at Lake Mweru. He carries the ceremonies, the titles, the political vocabulary. When he installs himself, the Lunda world grows.

**3 — Kazembe's Capital:** The eastern Lunda kingdom is receiving a Portuguese envoy. The Mwata Kazembe is larger and wealthier than anything the envoy has seen this side of the Zambezi. He is sending goods east to the Swahili coast and west to the Atlantic simultaneously. He is the center of a continental trade arc.

**4 — The Chokwe:** The ivory hunters have become conquerors. The Mwaant Yav has fled his musumba. The Chokwe are in his capital. The lukanu is hidden. Where the kingdom's soul goes when the holder is expelled is a question the tradition will have to answer.
