## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — PANEL-2 Review of Notes

**Overall:** The structural engine is now named and the mechanism is clear: portable culture growing by accumulation while the core persists. This is a genuine mechanism, not a metaphor. But the terminal condition needs sharpening.

**What works:**
- "You cannot burn a language, you cannot confiscate a skill, you cannot demolish a kinship system." This is the engine's logic stated with precision. It explains WHY the Roma survived three centuries on the road: their assets were intangible and therefore indestructible by the normal mechanisms that destroy settled civilizations.
- The section plan gives each section analytical work to do, not just narrative territory to cover. Section 3 (The Persian Road) carries the cohesion question. Section 4 (The Mountain Pass) carries the encounter question. This is structure.

**What I would push on:**
- The terminal condition ("being named by others is the first step toward being controlled by others") is good but needs to be shown operating, not just stated. The chapter should end with the Byzantine naming — the Athinganoi label — and the reader should FEEL that the naming is an act of categorization that makes persecution possible. The Roma existed unnamed for centuries and survived. The name is the cage that European power begins to build around them.
- The engine's mechanism (accumulation without replacement of core) needs a visible test in the chapter. Where does the accumulation threaten to overwhelm the core? When Persian loanwords flood the vocabulary, when Armenian tools replace Indian ones, when Greek market practices replace whatever came before — what keeps the core intact? The answer is probably the kinship system and the language's grammatical structure (which resists borrowing even when vocabulary does not). Show this pressure and this resistance.
