# Figures — La Conquête (Norman Kingdoms, Era 2)
## 1066–1087 CE

---

## William I — King of England, Duke of Normandy
*Born c. 1028, Falaise, Normandy. Died 9 September 1087, Saint-Gervais, Rouen.*

He was called *bastardus* in Latin, *le Bâtard* in French, and *Willelm Bastard* in Old English, and the name followed him all his life without quite settling into epithet — it was too specific, too personal, too directly about his birth and not his deeds. He preferred *Conqueror*, which was a title of achievement rather than origin. The preference is characteristic.

William was the illegitimate son of Robert I, Duke of Normandy, and Herleva, daughter of a tanner in Falaise. Robert recognized the boy and brought him to court, and when Robert died on pilgrimage in 1035, William inherited the duchy at approximately eight years old. Three of his guardians were murdered in the years that followed. He survived by moving often and trusting carefully, and by the time he was old enough to lead men in battle he was already adept at reading who wanted what and why.

He was physically imposing in a way that his contemporaries noted with specificity: large, heavy, with exceptional strength and a voice that carried across a battlefield. He ate and drank moderately by the standards of his court, which was itself a form of discipline. He was faithful to his wife Matilda of Flanders in a period when royal infidelity was so common as to be institutional — another form of discipline, or another form of calculation, or both. The distinction may not have been meaningful to him.

His intellectual gifts were for organization and for long-term planning. He did not forgive easily and did not forget at all, and the men who served him were capable or they were gone. He built things meant to last — castles in stone when timber would have served, documents when memory would have done, surveys when approximation was usual — because he understood that his position in England rested on a foundation that was always thinner than it appeared, and that the only way to compensate was to make everything legible, everything documented, everything provable.

Orderic Vitalis, who did not fully admire him and did not fully condemn him, recorded his deathbed with the specificity that suggests access to real accounts. The king surveyed what he had done and found it unequal — he had taken more than he had a right to, imposed more than was just, destroyed what should not have been destroyed. Whether this was genuine penitence or deathbed convention is not determinable. What is certain: the men around him did not wait to find out. When he died they left for their estates, and his body lay in the heat for much of the day before anyone arranged the burial.

He was interred at his own abbey at Caen, Saint-Étienne, which he had founded. The burial was undignified — the body would not fit the coffin — and the church filled with the smell of decay. He had built for permanence in everything, and his body at the end was what all bodies are. The castle stands. The Domesday Book stands. The man decomposed in his own church on a September afternoon in Normandy, twenty-one years after the field where the ravens had preceded him.

## Lanfranc of Bec — Archbishop of Canterbury
*Born c. 1005, Pavia. Died 28 May 1089, Canterbury.*

He was Italian by birth, trained as a lawyer in northern Italy, and arrived at the monastery of Bec in Normandy sometime around 1042, intending to be a monk and nothing else. Bec under Lanfranc became one of the great schools of Europe. His students included the young Anselm of Aosta, who would succeed him at Canterbury, and a generation of Norman clerics who carried Bec's theological training into the Norman church and, after 1066, into England.

Lanfranc did not want the Canterbury appointment. He had already refused Rouen and had spent his career declining positions that would have made him responsible for more than his monastery. He was sixty-five when William insisted. He came to Canterbury and found an English church that had been doing its own thing for a very long time, with its own liturgical traditions, its own saints, its own relationships to Rome — some of which Lanfranc thought were sound and some of which he thought were irregular. He reorganized what needed reorganizing and left alone what did not need reorganizing, which was the correct approach for a man who had to govern something he had not built.

He was an exceptional administrator and a formidable scholar, and these turned out to be the same qualification: he understood that governance required records, that records required trained scribes, that trained scribes required education, that education required books, and that books required copying, and so the chain went, and so the Canterbury scriptorium became the engine of Norman administrative governance in England. He did not think of it this way. He thought of it as his duty to the Church. The effect was the same.

He and William trusted each other in the way that two men with different skills and the same goal can trust each other: without sentiment, with reliability. When William was in Normandy, Lanfranc governed England. He convened councils, settled disputes, kept the Church's institutional infrastructure operating, and wrote to William in the calm, slightly bureaucratic Latin of a man who has too many things to manage to be dramatic about any of them.

He outlived William by two years and died at Canterbury at approximately eighty-four, which is an extraordinary age for the eleventh century and testifies either to exceptional physical constitution or to the particular kind of survival instinct that produces very good administrators. He was buried in the cathedral he had reorganized, in a tomb that was destroyed in the Reformation.

## Odo of Bayeux — Bishop and Earl of Kent
*Born c. 1036. Died 1097, Palermo.*

He was William's half-brother and the most powerful man in England during the years when William was most often in Normandy, which were frequent. He held the earldom of Kent, which gave him military authority over the southeastern approaches to England. He held the bishopric of Bayeux, which gave him wealth, clergy, and the moral authority of the Church. He was simultaneously a major secular lord and a major ecclesiastical figure, and he managed both roles with energy and, eventually, too much ambition.

He is probably responsible for commissioning the Bayeux Tapestry — an embroidered linen strip approximately 70 meters long, produced at Canterbury in the 1070s, depicting the events leading to Hastings and the battle itself. The Tapestry is a Norman document produced by English hands: the embroiderers at Canterbury who made it were almost certainly English women, working in the craft tradition that English monasteries had developed over centuries. The story they were asked to tell was the story of the Conquest of their own country, from the Norman perspective, in the Norman narrative of justification — Harold the oath-breaker, William the rightful king. The craft tradition was theirs; the story was not.

The Tapestry has its own small English voices, if you know where to look: the lower border where animals behave and misbehave in ways that the main narrative ignores; the faces of the English dead that are drawn with the same care as the faces of the Norman knights; the mysterious figure Ælfgyva in the main narrative, about whom nothing is explained and everything is implied, her name written over her head in Latin letters without context, a woman inserted into a Norman story for reasons that no surviving source explains. The Tapestry records the Conquest; the embroiderers left traces of themselves in it.

Odo was arrested in 1082 on charges that the sources record vaguely: he had ambitions to become pope, or he had exploited his English tenants beyond the customary limits of exploitation, or both, or something else that William kept to himself. He was imprisoned in Normandy, not in England, which may mean the charges involved Normandy or may mean William wanted him away from his English power base. He was released when William was dying, at William's deathbed request, and he outlived his half-brother by a decade, dying in Sicily while on crusade.

## Æthelric — A Domesday Witness
*Composite figure. Active 1086, Essex.*

The Domesday Book records hundreds of English landholders who appeared before the Norman commissioners in 1086 and testified about what they held before the Conquest and what they held now. The record does not name most of them in the way it names the great Norman lords — they appear in aggregate, as "the men of the county say" or "the thegns testify." But their testimony is there, embedded in the assessments, in the details about local customs and field boundaries and mill outputs that only someone who lived in the village could know.

Æthelric is a name from this population of witnesses, drawn from the pattern of Anglo-Saxon landholding that the Essex folios record. He is a composite of several English tenants whose circumstances the Domesday records make visible: men who held small estates before the Conquest and who held them afterward on changed terms, at the will of a Norman sheriff or lord rather than by inherited right, without written documentation of a kind that Norman courts would recognize as authoritative.

He speaks English in a court that writes Latin. His knowledge of his land — the boundaries, the customs, the obligations that have governed his family's tenure for generations — is the raw material from which the Domesday clerks produce a record that no longer requires his presence to be operative. He is the source of the survey's local accuracy and the person the survey most thoroughly replaces: after Domesday, what he knows about his land matters less than what the document says.

The composite technique here is disclosed because LUCIA's honesty discipline requires it: no individual named Æthelric of Writtle is documented in the Domesday Essex folios as a specific named figure in a testimony scene. The scenario is built from the documented patterns of Domesday evidence-gathering and from the social reality of English smallholders in 1086 that the secondary scholarship — Harvey's *Domesday: Book of Judgement* in particular — establishes in detail.

He is representative, not invented. The gap between his English testimony and the Latin document it becomes is not invented either. It is the central event of the Domesday survey, repeated in every village across England in 1086, and it is as historically specific as anything else in this chapter.

## Source Note on Orderic Vitalis

Orderic Vitalis (1075–c.1142) is the primary narrative source for much of this chapter. He was born in England to a Norman father and English mother, sent to the monastery of Saint-Évroul in Normandy at age ten, and spent the rest of his life there writing his *Historia Ecclesiastica* — an enormous chronicle covering Norman history and its ramifications across Europe. He is valuable precisely because of his doubled perspective: he was Norman enough to have access to Norman accounts and English enough to record English experiences and English grief. His account of the Harrying of the North is the fullest we have. His account of William's deathbed speech is the most detailed. He is partisan — his sympathies are broadly Norman — but he is also honest in a way that more purely Norman chronicles are not, and his regrets about the Harrying are genuine regrets, not formulaic piety.

The deathbed speech in particular should be understood as Orderic's characterization, not as a transcript. Medieval historical writing regularly attributed speeches to figures at significant moments — this was a recognized convention, understood to convey what the figure would appropriately have said given what was known of their character and circumstances, not what they actually said word for word. Orderic's William on his deathbed is Orderic's William: shaped by forty years of Norman historical writing, by Orderic's own moral framework, by the conventions of penitential literature. It is not therefore false. It is a characterization, and it is a good one.
