## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of "Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad" opening

**Overall:** The opening does something genuinely difficult: it presents an Islamic reform movement as a knowledge revolution without once implying that the knowledge is less rigorous than its Western academic equivalents. The Torodbe scholarly network is rendered as a genuine intellectual system with its own rigor, its own methods, its own standards of evidence. This is the correct approach. I have two concerns, both about depth.

**What works:**

- The Torodbe scholarly tradition is treated as a genuine intellectual system, not as "religious belief" or "cultural practice." The *Kitab al-Farq* is presented as a work of legal analysis. The scholars are engaged in jurisprudence, not in vague spiritual advocacy. "The line was precise, drawn with the specificity of a jurist who had studied al-Maghili and al-Suyuti and the Maliki scholars of the Sahel for a lifetime." This sentence treats the Shehu's intellectual formation with the same seriousness one would bring to a European jurist's training. No hidden hierarchy.

- The scholarly network as political infrastructure is rendered without condescension. The opening does not marvel that scholars could organize a revolution. It presents the network as self-evidently capable: "The army already existed. It was the network of Torodbe scholars and their students, scattered across every Hausa city-state from Gobir to Zaria, connected by decades of correspondence, shared teachers, shared texts." This is a distributed knowledge system, and the opening treats it as such.

- The flag mechanism is a knowledge-system artifact. Green cloth, the color of the Prophet, sent through a scholarly correspondence network to scholars who had been trained in the same legal tradition. The flag is not a military emblem in the Western sense. It is a juridical commission. The opening conveys this without explaining it, which is exactly right.

- The pastoral Fulani economy is connected to the scholarly critique without being subordinated to it. The cattle, the taxes, the seasonal movement, the sarakuna's extraction. The opening shows that the intellectual argument and the economic grievance are two faces of the same structural reality. This is integrative analysis, not reductive.

**What doesn't:**

- The opening mentions al-Maghili and al-Suyuti as part of the Shehu's scholarly formation but does not engage with the tradition at any depth. What did the Shehu take from al-Maghili? What was al-Maghili's argument about Islamic governance in the western Sudan, and how did the Shehu develop it? The intellectual genealogy is named but not entered. In the full chapter, I would expect at least one passage that shows the Shehu's thought at the level of its actual content, not merely its existence. The Torodbe tradition deserves the same treatment we would give to a passage on Aristotelian logic in a chapter on medieval Paris.

- The pastoral Fulani knowledge system is almost entirely absent. Pulaaku is mentioned in the OVERVIEW but not in the opening. The seasonal migration knowledge, the cattle management expertise, the environmental reading that allowed the Fulani to navigate the Sahel's dry and wet seasons, the ecological intelligence that made the pastoral economy viable. These are knowledge systems as sophisticated as any library, and they are the reason the pastoral Fulani could mobilize so quickly: they were already connected, already moving, already reading the landscape. The opening focuses on the book-based knowledge at the expense of the body-based knowledge. Both must be present.

**The question I'd push on:**

How do you render the Torodbe intellectual tradition with disciplinary depth? The full chapter must at some point show me what a Maliki legal argument looks like from the inside. Not "the Shehu argued that..." but the actual structure of a fatwa, the chain of reasoning from Quran to hadith to scholarly commentary to specific local application. Show me a student in Degel learning to construct a legal opinion. Show me the intellectual method. If we would show a Chinese scholar practicing calligraphy in a Tang chapter, or a Malian student reciting Quranic verses at Sankore, then we must show a Torodbe scholar working through the Maliki chain of evidence. The knowledge system must be rendered as practice, not just as outcome.
