# Muromachi — Flowers in the Storm

We are the Muromachi shogunate — named for the district of Kyoto where Ashikaga Takauji built the shogunal palace — and we governed Japan during the most paradoxical century in its history: the Sengoku period, the Age of Warring States, when political authority fragmented completely and any local lord with sufficient soldiers could declare himself independent. In this period of perpetual war and political catastrophe, Japan produced the cultural achievements that define Japanese civilization to the outside world: Noh theater, the tea ceremony (chado), ink painting (suiboku-ga), ikebana (flower arranging), the Zen garden, the shoin architectural style that became the template for traditional Japanese interiors. The cultural explosion was not despite the chaos; it was through the chaos, by the artists and monks who found, in the compression of uncertainty, the precise attention that great art requires.

We hold the Ashikaga name, but the Ashikaga shogunate's power was always contested and eventually nominal. The Ōnin War (1467–1477) burned Kyoto and began the Sengoku period that no Ashikaga shogun could control. Our greatest cultural moment was the Higashiyama culture of Ashikaga Yoshimasa — the shogun who built the Silver Pavilion (Ginkaku-ji) while Kyoto burned around him, who presided over the development of the tea ceremony and Noh theater, who was, as a ruler, a near-complete failure and, as a cultural patron, perhaps the most influential figure in Japanese aesthetic history.

## What Defined Them

**Noh theater — beauty and death.** Noh was created by the playwright-actor Zeami Motokiyo under the patronage of Ashikaga Yoshimitsu. It is a theater of extreme compression: slow movement, masked actors, chanted texts, the music of the flute and drums. The typical Noh play concerns a ghost — the spirit of a warrior, a woman, a demon — who returns to the living world to perform one more act before being freed. This is not melancholy decoration; it is a precise engagement with the Buddhist understanding of impermanence and the Shinto sense of the spirit world's presence in the visible world. Zeami's theoretical writings on Noh are among the most sophisticated discussions of performance aesthetics in world literature.

**The tea ceremony — wabi-sabi.** The tea ceremony (chado, the way of tea) as formalized by Murata Jukō and later Sen no Rikyū during the Muromachi period was the fullest expression of wabi — the beauty of imperfection, simplicity, and incompleteness. The tea room was small, plain, and slightly rustic; the tea bowl had deliberate irregularities; the garden path was asymmetric; the ceremony itself was a performance of refined simplicity. This aesthetic rejected the gold and lacquer of the aristocratic tradition in favor of patina, imperfection, and the attention to the ordinary moment that Zen meditation cultivated.

**Ink painting (suiboku-ga).** Muromachi ink painting, practiced by monk-painters like Sesshū Tōyō, developed from the Song Chinese ink painting tradition into a distinctly Japanese form: landscape paintings of mountains and water that left large areas of the paper unpainted, allowing the white space itself to carry meaning. Sesshū's winter landscape — a few strokes of ink suggesting snow, a traveler, and a vast empty world — is one of the most austere and powerful images in Japanese art. The Zen principle that what is not said matters as much as what is said became a visual technique.

**The Ōnin War and its consequences.** The Ōnin War (1467–1477) began as a dispute over the shogunal succession and became a ten-year destruction of Kyoto. The two sides — Yamana and Hosokawa factions — used the city as their battlefield. Most of Kyoto's architectural heritage was burned; the population scattered to the provinces. The war ended not in a decisive victory but in exhaustion, and the daimyo who had used it to strengthen their provincial bases simply kept that power. The Sengoku period that followed produced the warlords — Takeda Shingen, Uesugi Kenshin, Oda Nobunaga — who would finally end the Muromachi era.

**The Unkoku and Hasegawa Schools — art in wartime.** The daimyo of the Sengoku period were not culturally barren; they were patrons of the screen paintings (byōbu), folding screens decorated with gold-leaf landscapes and court scenes, that decorated their castle halls. The Kano School, the Unkoku School, and the Hasegawa School produced screens of extraordinary scale and beauty — the visual expression of warlord power and the cultural ambition that warfare generated rather than extinguished.

## The Chapters

**1 — Yoshimitsu:** He has just received the Ming emperor's recognition as "King of Japan" — a diplomatic concession that gave him the tally trade but cost him prestige with those who thought Japan should not be China's tributary. He builds the Golden Pavilion. He watches Zeami perform Noh. He is the most powerful man in Japan and he knows how to use culture to remain so.

**2 — Zeami:** He is writing the theoretical treatise on Noh. He is the greatest theater-maker in Japanese history and he is thinking about what makes a performance transcend entertainment and become art. He writes: the flower blooms in many ways; the actor must find the right flower for the right moment.

**3 — The Ōnin War:** The fires can be seen from the hills surrounding Kyoto. The two armies have been in the city for three years. The great temples and palaces are burning. The monks carry what texts they can. A culture that took five hundred years to build is being unmade in a decade.

**4 — Sen no Rikyū's Tea Room:** The tea room is smaller than a room should be. The guests must bow to enter. The garden path is irregular. The tea bowl was made by a local craftsman and has a crack that has been repaired with gold lacquer. Rikyū serves the tea. This is the most beautiful moment possible.

**5 — Oda Nobunaga:** He expels the last Ashikaga shogun from Kyoto. It takes an afternoon. The shogunate that has existed since 1336 ends with a general's decision not to tolerate it anymore. Nobunaga is already thinking about the next obstacle.
