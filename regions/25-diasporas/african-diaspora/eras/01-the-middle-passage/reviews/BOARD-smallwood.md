# BOARD — Stephanie Smallwood — The Middle Passage

**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Historical Accuracy

The chapter's representation of the trade system is substantially accurate. The embarkation zones (Senegambia, Gold Coast, Bight of Benin, Bight of Biafra, Central Africa) and their associated peoples are correctly identified. The scale figures (12.5 million embarked, ~10.7 million surviving) are consistent with the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database. The seven-year average lifespan for sugar plantation workers is within the range documented for Caribbean and Brazilian plantations (some estimates are lower). The mortality figure of roughly one in six for the crossing is consistent with the database's overall average (~15%), though mortality varied significantly by period, route, and ship.

## Specific Notes

1. **The *São João Baptista*:** This is a documented Portuguese slave vessel. The specific voyage described (Ouidah, 1727, 212 captives) is plausible for the period and route but should be noted as representative in figures.md. **Already disclosed. Satisfactory.**

2. **The forty iron bars:** Iron bars were a standard unit of account in the West African slave trade. The price is plausible for the early eighteenth century at Ouidah. Accurate.

3. **The forced dancing as "exercise":** Documented in multiple ship surgeon accounts, including Falconbridge. The crew's rationale (preventing atrophy, maintaining "cargo" health) is accurately represented. The cat-o'-nine-tails as enforcement is documented.

4. **The speculum oris:** Accurately identified as a forced-feeding instrument used on hunger-striking captives. Documented in the abolitionist literature and in ship inventories.

5. **"Fixed melancholy":** This is the correct period term used by ship surgeons for what we would now understand as severe depression, PTSD, or the decision to die. The chapter's gloss ("they did not have a word for the decision to stop living") is an interpretation but a defensible one.

6. **Revolt frequency:** "Roughly one in ten voyages" is consistent with the scholarship. David Richardson's work on shipboard revolts confirms this range.

7. **Equiano debate:** Handled with appropriate scholarly caution. The chapter presents both possibilities without resolving the debate, and uses the unresolved state productively.

## Concern

The chapter states that the Portuguese Brazil route from the Bight of Benin was "the shortest: five to six weeks if the winds held." This is approximately correct for the direct crossing to Bahia. However, many Brazilian-bound ships from the Bight of Benin first stopped at São Tomé or Príncipe. The chapter could note this intermediate stop, which was itself a significant part of the captive experience, but its omission is not an error of substance. **P3: author decides.**

## Verdict

Historically sound. The chapter handles the trade system accurately and the testimonial record honestly. The composite figure is well-justified by the source record. No factual corrections required.
