# BOARD Review — Frederic C. Lane
## La Rivoluzione Commerciale

**Date:** 2026-04-17
**Reviewer role:** Venetian maritime and commercial history

---

The Venice sections are largely accurate. The serrata, the Arsenal, the galley system, the constitutional structure: the chapter has these right in their essentials.

**Corrections required:**

1. **The Arsenal's founding and the 13th-century capacity claim.** The chapter implies a mature Arsenal organization in the period covered (1200-1350). The Arsenal was founded in 1104, but its major expansion — the *Arsenale Nuovo* — occurred in 1320. The "galley a day" productivity claim is associated with the early 16th century, not the late 13th. For the 1200-1350 period, the Arsenal was an important industrial facility but not yet at the peak productivity the chapter implies. **Recommendation:** Revise to attribute the Arsenal's serial production capacity more carefully: "by the early 14th century, the Arsenal had begun organizing its workforce into the serial production system..." P1 correction — the current text implies a capacity that postdates the era.

2. **The serrata's terms.** The chapter states the serrata restricted membership "to those families who had served in the Council in the previous four years." The actual mechanism was more complex: Gradenigo's reform allowed those who had served in the previous four years to be added to a permanent list, but the reform was implemented through successive acts between 1297 and 1323. The chapter's formulation simplifies a multi-stage constitutional process. **Recommendation:** Add a phrase acknowledging the serrata was implemented over several years rather than in a single vote. P3 — the simplification is understandable in a narrative context; the essential character of the serrata (hereditary closure) is correctly described.

3. **The galley auction mechanism.** The chapter describes the galley system as "state-owned merchant galleys auctioned to syndicates of merchants for specific voyages." This is accurate for the *muda* system, but the chapter does not distinguish between the *muda* (the seasonal convoy system for major routes) and private galley operation. The *muda* was the distinctive Venetian innovation; private galley operation was common to all maritime cities. **Recommendation:** Specify "the *muda* system, the seasonally organized convoy routes" rather than "the galley system" generically. P3.

**What the chapter does well:**

The caulkers' section is the best brief description of Arsenal work I have encountered in narrative history. The pitch, the synchronized mallets, the singing: this is the sensory reality of the Arsenal, and it is placed correctly within the broader argument about the Venetian state as commercial entity. I could find this in no previous narrative account — it is drawn from what the sources allow, rendered at the right level of specificity.

The Doge's constitutional constraints — no gifts from foreign rulers, no departure from the city without permission, letters read by a committee — are all accurate. The Senate's tracking of its fleet operations as "the counting house it was" is the correct framing of Venetian governance.

**Recommendation:** APPROVE with one P1 correction (Arsenal capacity chronology) and two P3 notes. The Venice sections are historically sound.
