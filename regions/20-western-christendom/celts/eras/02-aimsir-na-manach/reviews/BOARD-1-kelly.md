# BOARD-1 — Early Irish Law Specialist
## Modeled on: Fergus Kelly, *A Guide to Early Irish Law* (DIAS, 1988)

**Domain:** Brehon law, early Irish legal system, the fili's legal rank
**Assignment:** Historical accuracy of the legal framework; the honor-price system; the ollam's legal status; the *Lex Innocentium*

---

## Assessment

The chapter's treatment of early Irish law is accurate at the level it operates. The lóg n-enech system (honor-price) and éraic (compensation) are rendered correctly as the operative mechanisms of a society that resolved disputes through compensation and social pressure rather than imprisonment or execution. The seven-cumal / six-cumal distinction for lords of different grades reflects the hierarchical structure of the law texts, and the feast as a legal proceeding (poem as public record, assembled witnesses as court) is supported by what the law tracts tell us about the social function of the fili's praise-poetry.

**On the ollam's legal rank:** The statement that "the ollam occupied the same legal rank as a bishop" is broadly correct for the period covered, though the relationship between the filid and the ecclesiastical hierarchy was negotiated differently at different times. By the eighth century, the highest ecclesiastical figures had somewhat higher honor-price than even the ollam in some law texts. The chapter's formulation is accurate for the fifth and sixth centuries.

**On the Cairbre mac Étaíne story:** This is correctly identified as mythological in origin but legal in logic. The use of the mythological corpus to demonstrate a legal practice is a legitimate technique when the legal logic (a blemished king cannot rule) is also documented in the brehon law texts, which it is. No objection.

**On the *Lex Innocentium*:** The characterization of the *Cáin Adomnáin* as using brehon mechanisms (guarantee, assembled witness, graduated fine) is accurate. Adomnán's law is preserved in a text that mixes ecclesiastical and secular legal language, and the chapter captures this dual-register nature correctly.

**One factual note:** The chapter states that the ollam memorized "three hundred and fifty stories in the lesser grades and then an additional one hundred and fifty for the ollam." The bardic grammar tracts give different numbers in different compilations, and scholarship has not settled on a single authoritative figure. The numbers in the chapter are within the range of what the tracts describe. This is not an error but a representation of one tradition within a variable source base. The figures.md discloses the composite nature of the Ferdomnach figure, which handles the source situation correctly.

**Overall verdict:** Historically accurate within the chapter's scope. The legal framework is rendered at method level, not at the level of general claim. I have no corrections to require.
