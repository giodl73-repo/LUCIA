# BOARD REVIEW — Jan Vansina
## Mangbetu — Era 2: The Expansion Under Tuba

**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

The chapter's handling of the source situation is the first thing I look for in any chapter about a Central African culture from this period, and this chapter handles it correctly.

The central methodological challenge for Tuba's era is as follows: Tuba's reign ended approximately fifteen to twenty years before the first detailed European documentation begins (Schweinfurth 1870). The chapter uses Schweinfurth's account as evidence of what the tradition had produced — not as direct testimony about Tuba's court — and it names this temporal gap explicitly. This is the correct approach.

The political chronology (Nabiembali → Tuba → the rulers of the contact period) reflects the scholarly consensus as established through the combination of oral historical accounts, explorers' documentation, and the later ethnographic record.

**On the oral tradition question:**

The chapter does not claim direct access to Mangbetu oral historical accounts about Tuba's era. This is appropriate — those accounts exist but the scholarly work drawing on them is thin, as the SOURCES.md honestly acknowledges. A chapter that claimed oral-tradition authority it does not have would be worse than a chapter that works carefully from the explorers' accounts while acknowledging the gap.

The composite figures (Nasakara, Amunzika, the musician) are disclosed as composites in figures.md. This is the correct methodology for cultures where the individual record is thin but the class-level record is rich. My own work on oral tradition methodology endorses this approach when it is handled with the transparency this chapter provides.

**On the broader Central African context:**

The chapter situates the Mangbetu state within the northeastern Congo Basin correctly — distinct from the Luba and Lunda systems to the south and west, in a different ecological and geographic zone, with a different mode of political organization. The court-culture model the chapter describes is specific to the Mangbetu; the chapter does not generalize it to other Central African peoples, which would be a historical error.

**One methodological note:**

The chapter's description of the Mangbetu state as reaching its "greatest territorial extent" under Tuba is the scholarly consensus but rests on a thin evidentiary base — primarily later oral tradition accounts and the inference backward from the contact-period documentation. The chapter presents this as established fact; it might more accurately be presented as the scholarly consensus with acknowledged uncertainty. A phrase like "in the accounts collected later, Tuba's reign marked the state's widest reach" would be more precise.

This is a P3 calibration — the existing phrasing is not wrong, it is slightly more confident than the source base strictly warrants. Author decides.

**Verdict:** Methodologically sound. One recommended calibration (territorial extent phrasing). No substantive errors.
