# Khoikhoi — "Men of Men"

The Khoikhoi called themselves "Khoikhoi" — "men of men," or real people, as distinct from "San," the term they used for the foraging communities who did not herd cattle. This distinction — between herder and forager — was the primary social boundary in the pre-colonial Cape world, and it was permeable in both directions: San communities could acquire cattle and become Khoikhoi; Khoikhoi who lost their cattle could revert to foraging and over time merge with San communities. The boundary was economic and social, not racial. The cattle were the difference.

The Khoikhoi brought pastoralism to the Cape approximately two thousand years ago — the oldest herding tradition in southern Africa south of the Limpopo. Their cattle were the fat-tailed sheep and the long-horned Nguni cattle that they had brought south through a migration from the interior, and their social world was organized around the cattle circuit: the seasonal movement of herds between the winter-rainfall Cape coast and the summer-rainfall interior, following the grazing. The kraal — the cattle enclosure at the center of the encampment — was the center of the Khoikhoi social world. The chief's (kaptein's) authority was expressed through his cattle; his relationships with other chiefs were mediated through cattle exchange; his people's loyalty was maintained through cattle distribution. When the Dutch East India Company built its fort at the Cape in 1652, it did so on land that the Khoikhoi had been grazing for two thousand years, and the subsequent history of the Cape is the story of what happened when the settler demand for land and cattle collided with a Khoikhoi world organized around exactly those resources.

## What Defined Them

**Cattle as covenant.** Khoikhoi society was organized around cattle in a way that made cattle the language of political relationship. The kaptein's cattle were the community's wealth; the exchange of cattle between groups — at weddings, in tribute, as compensation for injuries — was the material form of political and social obligation. To give cattle was to make an alliance; to take cattle was to make a claim; to lose cattle was to lose status, community, and eventually identity. The Khoikhoi-Dutch conflict of the 1650s–1660s was, from the Khoikhoi perspective, a series of intolerable cattle thefts that escalated into war. The settlers were not simply building a refreshment station; they were taking the resource that was the foundation of the entire Khoikhoi political economy.

**The clan circuit and seasonal mobility.** The Khoikhoi moved their herds seasonally between winter and summer grazing grounds — a circuit that required knowledge of water sources, grazing quality, and territorial boundaries between clans. The circuit was not random wandering; it was an organized management system for a pastoral economy in a highly variable Mediterranean-type environment. Different clans had rights to different portions of the circuit, and these rights were maintained through relationships — marriage, alliance, trade — between clan leaders. The Dutch fort at the Cape disrupted this circuit almost immediately, claiming for settler agriculture the best grazing land at the Cape's tip.

**The smallpox catastrophe.** In 1713, a smallpox epidemic spread through the Cape Colony from infected laundry from a Dutch East India Company ship and devastated the Khoikhoi population. The epidemic killed an estimated 90% of the Khoikhoi in the southwestern Cape — the communities who had been in most intensive contact with the colonial settlement. Communities that had managed to maintain their cattle and their social organization were reduced to scattered individuals who had no choice but to seek employment on settler farms. The 1713 smallpox epidemic is the single event most responsible for the elimination of the Khoikhoi as an independent social and political entity in the southwestern Cape.

**Eva (Krotoa) and the impossible position.** Eva, also known by her Khoikhoi name Krotoa, was the niece of the Goringhaicona captain Autshumao and served as an interpreter for Jan van Riebeeck's colonial administration from the 1650s. Baptized as "Eva," married to a Dutch surgeon, fluent in Dutch and Portuguese as well as Khoikhoi, she occupied a position between two worlds that ultimately destroyed her — the Dutch colonial society did not accept her as Dutch, and the Khoikhoi world she had left could not fully reabsorb her. Her history is not simply a personal tragedy; it is the prototype for the structural impossibility of the position in which the colonial encounter placed anyone who attempted to bridge it.

**The Griqua and the persistence of identity.** The Griqua — communities formed from the descendants of Khoikhoi, escaped or freed slaves, and some settlers, who established themselves in the interior beyond the colonial frontier in the early nineteenth century — maintained elements of Khoikhoi pastoral culture in a new form. The Griqua leaders (Adam Kok, Waterboer) built small states in the interior, negotiated treaties with the British colonial government, and maintained Khoikhoi claims to land and political recognition. The Griqua's subsequent dispossession — at Griqualand West and Griqualand East, through a combination of settler pressure and colonial manipulation — is one of the less studied but important stories of southern African colonial history.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Cattle Circuit:** A Khoikhoi herd is moving between the winter and summer grazing grounds. The circuit has been followed for fifty generations. The kaptein knows every water source on the route. The cattle know it too.

**2 — The Dutch Ships:** The VOC ships have anchored at Table Bay. Autshumao — the Strandloper captain the Dutch call "Herry" — is going aboard to negotiate. He has been doing this for years, trading cattle for iron. He does not yet know that this time they are staying.

**3 — The First War:** The cattle have been stolen. The kaptein has gathered his warriors. The Dutch fort's cannon are not aimed at him yet, but he knows they exist. He is calculating. The calculation says fight. The cannon say he is wrong. He fights anyway.

**4 — 1713:** The smallpox has reached the clan. Three-quarters of the people are dead in two weeks. The survivors are looking at the cattle — still alive, still needing to be herded — and looking at each other. There are not enough people left to follow the circuit. The circuit is ending.

**5 — Eva's Grave:** Krotoa died in the VOC lodge in 1674, after years of imprisonment and release and imprisonment again. She is buried at the Cape. The stone is unmarked for three hundred years. In 2011, the South African government reinterred her under the Company's Garden, with ceremony. The impossible position she occupied has a name now: the colonial encounter.
