# Panel Review — Round 2 (PANEL-2)
## P-2 Ibn Khaldun | Notes Review

**Era:** The Island Expansion | **Culture:** Arawak / Lokono
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

Reading the production bible, my first-round concern about governance is addressed adequately: Section 4 will treat the lonko as the node-validation mechanism, and the enforcement of reciprocity through network exclusion is named. I am satisfied that the chapter has a causal structure for the network's expansion, not merely a description of it.

What I want to sharpen now is the terminal condition. The notes identify cultural divergence as the era's endpoint — the network produces peoples different enough to require new categories. This is correct historically, and it is theoretically interesting: the expansion mechanism succeeds so thoroughly that it undermines the homogeneity that gave the network its coherence. But the chapter needs to show this divergence at the level of a specific, observable difference, not as a general process. I would suggest: a trade object arriving on the mainland from the islands that the mainland community does not immediately recognize — a ceramic form, a shell ornament, an unfamiliar preparation of food — that marks the moment when the island cousins have become something the mainland network still claims but no longer fully understands. One object, one moment of slight incomprehension, as the chapter's index of divergence.

The matapi as structural engine is well-chosen. It satisfies my theoretical requirement: a mechanism, not just a symbol. The squeeze basket's properties — portable knowledge, deployable in any new environment with locally sourced materials — make it causally responsible for the expansion in a way that "trade networks" as an abstraction does not. I would want the chapter to make this causal link explicit, not through assertion ("the matapi enabled the expansion") but through the specific scene of an island woman making a matapi from materials she found on the island, with knowledge she carried in her hands from the mainland. The made object is the argument.

One remaining concern: the chapter covers approximately 1,200 years (500 BCE to 700 CE). Section 5's "divergence" is treated as occurring in the 400–700 CE window, but the mechanism of divergence — what specifically changes in that period — is not yet specified in the notes. Is it population growth on the islands that makes return migration impractical? Is it the development of island-specific agricultural techniques that the mainland community cannot use? Is it the emergence of the areíto ceremonial tradition as an island-specific cultural institution? The chapter needs a specific driver for divergence, not just a timeframe.
