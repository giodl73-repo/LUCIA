# Figures — Nafarroako Erresuma

## Iñigo Arista (c. 790–851 CE)
### Lord of Pamplona, First King of Navarre

The record does not give us Iñigo Arista's voice. What it gives us is his position: at the hinge point between two empires, neither of which could quite reach him.

The Frankish Annals mention him as a regional lord who resisted Carolingian authority and made trouble on the border. The Umayyad chronicles record him as a sometimes-tributary who paid when it suited him and did not when it did not. He is remembered in both sets of documents as the kind of problem administrators dislike most: not openly hostile enough to justify a full military campaign, and not weak enough to simply ignore.

His name is the first clue. Iñigo is Basque — the Basque form of the name that Castilian would later render as Íñigo or Eneko, which means something like "my little lord" in its archaic Basque root, a diminutive that carried into a proper name the way diminutives do in cultures where family-feeling is embedded in naming. Arista is the surname attached to him by later records — its origin is disputed, possibly Latin or Iberian, possibly a personal name that became a dynastic marker. His name is a mixture. He was a lord in a mixed world.

What is clear is that he managed, for roughly three decades, to maintain the independence of the mountain territory around Pamplona against pressures from both north and south. He did this through an alliance with the Banu Qasi, the Arabicized Iberian family of the Ebro valley who served as the Emirate's border managers and his own family's kin by marriage. The alliance was pragmatic, not ideological. The Banu Qasi were Muslim; Iñigo was Christian in his allegiance; the mountains between them were the real governing fact. What the mountains required, the alliance served.

He did not call himself "King of Navarre." That title came later, applied retrospectively by chronicles that wanted a clean founding moment. What Iñigo was, more precisely, was the first lord of the region to be recognized as a peer rather than a subject by both the Carolingians and the Umayyads simultaneously. That recognition is the definition of sovereignty available to the ninth century.

**What we cannot know:** Iñigo's own understanding of what he was building. The record is thin, filtered entirely through external sources that wrote about him in terms of what he meant to them. Whether he thought of himself as the founder of a kingdom or simply as the current holder of the mountain pass — whether he imagined a dynasty or only a tenure — is beyond what the sources can tell us.

## Sancho III Garcés, "the Great" (c. 992–1035 CE)
### King of Pamplona, "Emperor of Spain"

Sancho III is the easiest Navarrese king to describe because he left documents, and the hardest to assess because the documents are all self-presentation. His chancery produced charters in considerable volume; his donations to monasteries and churches along the Camino de Santiago were recorded; his political correspondence with neighboring kings and with Rome survives in fragments. He is the most documented king in Navarrese history up to his reign, and the documentation tells us he was very good at being seen.

"Emperor of all Spain" was the title his chancery used in the last years of his reign. He did not have an emperor's power — he controlled no more than a quarter of the Iberian peninsula and was dependent on the military assistance of his Castilian and Aragonese kin for any major campaign. What he had was the Camino de Santiago, which gave him the emperor's role in a different sense: the man through whose territory the faithful traveled, whose infrastructure made the journey possible, whose authority guaranteed the road's safety. In the network of Western Christendom, this was a form of power that did not require armies. It required maintenance.

He organized it well. The infrastructure along the Camino through Navarrese territory — bridges, hospices, market charters, the reorganization of Pamplona on the pilgrimage-road logic — was the administrative achievement of his reign. He also conducted successful military campaigns against the *taifa* kingdoms to the south, which supplemented his income and his prestige without fundamentally altering the structure of Iberian politics. The Camino was the achievement. The campaigns were the proof that the achievement was defensible.

His will divided his kingdom among his heirs: García to Navarre proper, Fernando to Castile, Ramiro to Aragon. Later historians would call this a mistake. Sancho likely saw it differently. The *foru* mechanism that his kingdom ran on was fundamentally local — valley-by-valley, province-by-province — and a large centralized state would have required a kind of royal administration that would have violated the covenant structure on which the whole enterprise depended. The division was not an accident or a failure of foresight. It was the logic of the system working as designed.

**What we cannot know:** Whether Sancho was aware of the constitutional incompatibility between the *foru* system and the imperial ambition his chancery projected. The documentation shows both simultaneously, without apparent tension. Either he saw a resolution we cannot see, or he held two contradictory things at once because the present moment required both and the future would have to manage the conflict.

## García Ramírez, "the Restorer" (died 1150 CE)
### King of Navarre 1134–1150

García Ramírez was not elected because anyone expected great things from him. He was elected because Navarre needed a king who was there.

When Alfonso I of Aragon died in 1134 without heirs, leaving Navarre and Aragon as a combined kingdom with no one to inherit it, the Navarrese nobility faced a choice: accept a foreign king imposed by one of the neighboring powers, or choose their own. They chose their own. The assembly of lords gathered and selected García Ramírez — a domestic Navarrese nobleman with a defensible claim — over the alternatives, which included Aragonese, Castilian, and even papal candidates. The choice was an act of the *foru* mechanism at its most explicit: the community's right to determine who governs it, exercised at the kingdom's highest level.

What García Ramírez did with his election was proceed without ceremony to the obligatory swearing. He traveled to each of the Basque provinces in turn and swore the *foru* under its specific terms. He did not negotiate revised terms or seek exemptions. He swore. He understood, or his advisors understood, that the legitimacy of his election depended on the completion of the ritual that the covenant required. An elected king who did not swear the *foru* would not be recognized as king by the people who had elected him.

His reign lasted sixteen years and accomplished the main thing: the restoration held. Navarre did not dissolve back into Aragon or Castile. He passed a kingdom to his heirs. That he passed a smaller and more circumscribed kingdom than the one that had existed under Sancho III the Great is less a criticism of his reign than a description of the structural position Navarre was in — squeezed between two expanding neighbors — after 1035.

**What we cannot know:** The internal experience of a king who owes his throne to an assembly rather than to blood. The documentation gives us his administrative acts and his military campaigns. It does not give us his understanding of the unusual nature of his kingship or what he made of the obligation to travel and swear five times to secure a title he had already been given.

## Otsoa the Bertsolar (composite figure, c. 900–1000 CE)
### Assembly poet, valley of the upper Arga

The bertsolar is the figure for whom the historical record offers the most evidence and the fewest names. The *bertsolaritza* tradition — the practice of improvisational oral poetry in formal competition — is documented from the medieval period onward; individual competitors before the fourteenth century are not named in any surviving record. What we know is the practice: its function in the community, its formal requirements, its role in transmitting legal memory alongside entertainment and cultural identity.

Otsoa is a composite figure drawn from the ethnographic and historical record of what the bertsolar did and how. His name is attested in Basque records of the period — a common enough name, meaning "wolf," one of the animal names that appear frequently in the valley records. His function in the chapter — summarizing an assembly's decision in formal verse, providing the mnemonic form that the community trusted to hold the decision's terms — is documented as a function of the tradition, attributed here to a single individual for narrative clarity.

The disclosure is this: Otsoa did not exist as this chapter describes him. The function existed. The tradition that produced people who performed this function existed. The chapter makes one person do what the tradition did, because narrative requires a body, and the tradition had bodies — specific people in specific valleys who won or lost the verse competitions, who were known by name in their communities, whose verses were remembered long after their names were not.

The *bertsolaritza* tradition is not merely cultural decoration. It is a knowledge-management system. The formal verse requirements — specific meters, rhyme schemes, the obligation to respond to an assigned topic within strict time — create mnemonic structures that increase the probability of accurate recall. A legal finding summarized in the correct meter and rhyme scheme is more reliably transmitted than the same finding in prose, because the formal requirements constrain the variation that memory introduces over time. The tradition knew what it was doing. It had been doing it long enough to know.

**Source note:** Ethnographic and linguistic scholarship on the *bertsolaritza* tradition by Eguzki Urteaga and Joxerra Garzia informs the functional description. The composite figure technique follows the methodology established in the v1.1 amendment to the Figures rubric, with the disclosure made explicit here.
