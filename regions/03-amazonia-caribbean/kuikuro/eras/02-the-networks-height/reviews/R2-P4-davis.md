# Panel Review — R2-P4 — Wade Davis
## Production Bible Review | The Network's Height | Kuikuro Era 2

---

The terra preta section in the production bible is exactly the right call. I am glad it is there. Let me say what I need from it in execution.

Terra preta is one of the great intellectual achievements of the pre-contact Americas and one of the least known. These are soils that were created over centuries through the deliberate incorporation of charcoal (in the biochar form, produced by low-temperature burning), bone ash, fish and animal waste, and organic matter. The result is a dark, rich soil — dramatically different from the surrounding lateritic soils of the Amazon basin — that has maintained its fertility for five hundred years after the civilizations that created it collapsed. The soils are still there. Still more fertile. Still supporting agriculture on the sites of the old garden towns.

This is what I need the chapter to show: not the terra preta as a fact but as a practice. The gardener who is enriching the soil does not think of herself as "building terra preta" any more than a skilled baker thinks of herself as "conducting the Maillard reaction." She knows what the soil needs. She knows what to add, how much, how often. She knows the difference between the old dark earth and the pale soil at the garden's edge where the enrichment has not yet reached. She tends it the way a doctor tends a patient — reading signs, adjusting practice, accumulating knowledge that is never written down but is fully as precise as anything a laboratory would produce.

The chapter must show this knowledge in the gardener's hands. Not explained to the reader — demonstrated through the gardener's practice. What she picks up and examines. What she adds. What she knows by the smell of the soil, the way water moves through it, the density of the manioc roots that come out of it.

The opening quote from Davis's own work on the Wayfinders applies here: "The Amazonian shamans who can identify thousands of plant species and their medicinal interactions have built a pharmacological knowledge system as rigorous as any Western laboratory." The same is true for the terra preta gardeners. Show it.

— Davis
