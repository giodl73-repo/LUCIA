## P-5 — Kapuscinski — PANEL-2 Review of "The Bornu Empire" notes.md

**Focus:** Sensory plan for analytical passages, the physicality of the sacred kingship.

**On the curtain:** The notes identify the curtain as the chapter's central metaphor. Good. But the curtain must be physical before it is metaphorical. What is it made of? Where does it hang? How thick is it? Can the mai see through it? Can the courtiers see a shadow, a silhouette, a movement behind it? When the mai speaks, does the waziri lean close to the curtain to hear? Is the mai's voice muffled, clear, or only audible as a whisper that the waziri then amplifies? The reader must feel the curtain in every section, not as a recurring symbol but as a physical fact of the governance space. A room with a curtain in it is different from a room without one. The acoustics change. The light changes. The power dynamics are spatial, not just political.

**On Section 2 (The Rebuilding):** The founding of Ngazargamu needs mud. Literally. The walls are being built. The mud-brick is being formed: earth mixed with water, sometimes with chaff or animal dung for binding, pressed into wooden molds, dried in the sun. The builders' hands are covered in it. The sun bakes the bricks in rows. The smell of wet earth drying in Sahelian heat is distinctive. The timber being cut and fitted. The first walls rising. The city being born is a physical process and every physical process has a temperature, a texture, and a sound.

**On Section 3 (The Wars):** Battle scenes require bodies. Quilted cotton armor is heavy, perhaps twenty pounds or more, and in Sahelian heat it becomes a portable oven. A cavalryman who has been fighting for an hour in quilted armor is dehydrated, his vision narrowed by sweat, his muscles aching from the weight. The musket reloading sequence is a physical ordeal: pour powder, seat ball, replace ramrod, aim, fire, and the whole process takes perhaps thirty seconds during which you are defenseless. The sound of a cavalry charge on dry savanna ground: hooves on packed earth, the jangling of harness, the shouting. The smoke after a musket volley hangs in still air and blinds the shooters. Every tactical decision in this section should be felt through the body of someone executing it.

**On Section 4 (The Jurist):** A legal proceeding is a physical event. Ahmad sits. The disputants stand or kneel. There is shade or there is not. The paper he consults is rough under his fingers. The ink he uses to write his ruling has a specific color (lamp-black, probably, mixed with gum arabic). The ruling itself is spoken aloud in a specific space: a courtyard, a reception hall, a mosque. The echo matters. The audience matters. Make the law as physical as the war.

**Key demand for the draft:** No section should go more than two paragraphs without a sensory anchor. If the analysis requires three paragraphs, embed the third paragraph's ideas in a physical scene.
