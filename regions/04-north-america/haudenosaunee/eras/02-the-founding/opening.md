# The Founding — Kaianere'kó:wa

The serpents in Atotarho's hair were not still. They moved when he moved, and when he was angry they writhed so that no one could look at him without flinching. He sat in the smoke of his fire at Onondaga, and the sachems who came to speak with him found themselves speaking to the serpents instead of the man, which was part of the problem and part of the point. Atotarho's mind was twisted. The people said this openly. His body was twisted too, bent by a crookedness that was not merely of the spine but of the spirit, a grief so old and so fed by blood that it had become indistinguishable from power. He was the most feared man among the five nations, and no one could bring peace to the land between the lakes while his hair was full of serpents and his mind was full of war.

The Peacemaker had come from the north, across Skaniatariio, the beautiful lake. The Kanien'kehá:ka were the first to hear him. He spoke slowly, as a man speaks who has thought a long time before opening his mouth, and what he said was this: the cycle of killing and mourning and killing again to fill the empty seats in the longhouse had no end inside itself. A woman loses her son. The clan sends warriors to take a captive to replace the son. The captive's clan sends warriors to avenge the taking. The avenging creates new empty seats. The new empty seats require new captives. The blood goes in a circle, and the circle tightens with each turning until every nation is at war with every other nation and the longhouses are full of grief and the fields are neglected because the men are always gone.

This was not an abstraction. The Peacemaker could name the raids. The Kanien'kehá:ka could count their dead.

He said: there is another way. The five nations can extend the rafters of a single longhouse until it shelters all the families. Each nation keeps its own fire, its own council, its own way. But the wars between them end, because the longhouse does not make war on itself. The mothers who have lost sons will find their grief addressed not by the taking of captives but by the ceremony of condolence, in which the tears are wiped from the eyes and the obstruction is removed from the ears and the throat is cleared so that a person can think and hear and speak again. The Peacemaker said: you cannot govern with a mind choked by grief. Before the council speaks, the grief must be acknowledged. Before the law can work, the people must be able to hear.

Aiionwatha understood this before anyone. He had lost his daughters. Some traditions say illness took them. Others say Atotarho's sorcery. What the traditions agree on is that Aiionwatha's grief was so heavy that he left his people and walked into the forest, and that the Peacemaker found him there and combed the grief from his mind the way you would comb the tangles from hair, one strand at a time, speaking the words of condolence that would later become the opening of every Grand Council meeting for centuries.

Together they traveled. Aiionwatha carried the message because the Peacemaker's speech was halting and Aiionwatha's voice could fill a clearing. They went to the Oneida, who listened. They went to the Cayuga, who listened. They went to the Seneca, who listened. Each nation that joined the peace made the peace stronger, because each nation that joined was one fewer nation making war.

But Atotarho would not listen. The Onondaga were the geographic center, and without them the longhouse had no hearth. The Peacemaker and Aiionwatha came to Onondaga and sat across the fire from a man whose rage had knotted his body and whose hair moved with a life of its own, and they did not argue with him. They did not threaten him. They did not offer him power over the other nations.

They offered him the central fire.

The Peacemaker combed the serpents from Atotarho's hair. Strand by strand. The words of condolence, spoken directly to the man who had most resisted them. The grief beneath the rage, acknowledged. The rage beneath the crookedness, named. The crookedness beneath the serpents, straightened. When the combing was finished, Atotarho was not diminished. He was the Keeper of the Central Fire, the first among the Onondaga sachems, and the Grand Council would meet at his nation's territory for as long as the Great Law endured.

The five nations buried their weapons beneath the roots of a great white pine. The Peacemaker planted it at Onondaga and said: this is the Tree of Peace. Its roots spread in the four directions, and anyone who follows the roots to their source will find shelter beneath the branches. An eagle sits at the top, watching. If danger approaches, the eagle will scream, and the nations will hear.

The weapons were under the roots. You could not retrieve them without uprooting the law.
