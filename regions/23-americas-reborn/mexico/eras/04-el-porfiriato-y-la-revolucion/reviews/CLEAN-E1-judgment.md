**Anachronistic morality check:**
- The chapter characterizes Díaz without issuing verdicts: "He was not an evil man in the manner of people who want evil" is inside-voice characterization, not moral judgment. It names what he built and what it did.
- Huerta is named as "a drunk and a murderer" in the chapter text — this is a factual characterization supported by the historical record (Madero's assassination under his orders) rather than an anachronistic moral category. This passes.
- The Decena Trágica is described from the Mexicans' perspective ("smelling of gunpowder and burning wood") without editorial verdict on U.S. Ambassador Wilson's role — it is named and described, not condemned.
- Madero's class-blindness is named as structural ("It was not stupidity — it was class") which is an explanatory frame, not a moral verdict. This passes the Achebe Test.

**Romanticization check:**
- Zapata is not romanticized. His tactical intelligence is noted factually; his decision to go to Chinameca despite warnings is reported without making it heroic tragedy. The corrido tradition's mythologizing is named and distinguished from the chapter's own facticity.
- The soldaderas are not romanticized. María de la O is given a specific role and testimony, not an idealized image.
- The milpa is not romanticized as "timeless traditional farming." It is described as a functioning polyculture and its land-holding system is analyzed as a knowledge form that the Ley de Baldíos refused to recognize.

**No verdicts discipline check:**
- The revolution's costs are stated plainly (one to two million dead in fifteen million population) without authorial weighting toward condemnation or celebration. This is honest uncertainty about the numbers, appropriately hedged.
- The Constitution of 1917 is described as "the most progressive constitutional document of its era" — this is a comparative historical claim that can be documented, not an editorial superlative.

**One minor flag for revision:**
- The phrase "Díaz's inner circle" (Section II, "the men who might have succeeded him... were kept in competition with one another, each dependent on Díaz's favor, none permitted to build an independent base") is analytically sound but could be made slightly more specific. Consider naming one or two of these competing figures (Bernardo Reyes, for instance) to ground the claim. Not a violation — an evidence-density note.
