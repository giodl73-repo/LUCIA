# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman
## Wayúu, Era 3: The Great Cattle Era — PANEL-1 Review

I read this twice. The first time straight through, as a reader. The second time looking for where my attention moved.

The first read held. The opening has what I require from the first lines: a specific person doing a specific thing in a specific place, with a problem already in motion before I've been given any context. "She counts them through the gate. Eleven goats. One gone." That's three sentences. That's a chapter. I want to know about the missing goat. The writer did their job.

What works — and this is structural, not just atmospheric — is that every element in the opening is doing double duty. The mochila teaching isn't just a domestic scene; it's introducing the clan identity system that will carry weight through the whole era. The lost goat isn't just a problem; it's a demonstration of the pütchipü'üi system, which is one of this era's central engines. The wait for rain isn't mood-setting; it's the Guajira's aridity, which shapes everything. The writer planted the chapter's spine inside a scene that reads as pure narrative.

The prose rhythm is right. Short sentences for the physical world, longer accumulative sentences for the logic. "Not by going to recover the goat yourself — that risks turning a lost animal into a confrontation, a confrontation into an insult, an insult into a blood debt." That sequence is the whole pütchipü'üi system explained through its own logic rather than through academic summary. This is craft.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening earns the intimate, domestic scale beautifully. But this is Era 3 — the era when cattle transformed the entire political economy of the Guajira and preserved Wayúu independence for two centuries. That stakes-level story is present only in the penultimate section, and it arrives somewhat abruptly: one section on cattle, one paragraph on Spanish expectations, two sentences on how the Wayúu absorbed the animals. The full chapter will need to build from the intimate to the structural without losing the intimate. The opening works as an opening. The question is whether the notes (and then the chapter) will find a way to make Seyuuya's scale and the peninsula's scale inhabit the same narrative simultaneously. That's the craft challenge ahead.

One minor flag: "The animals moved with the seasonal rains, north toward the Serranía in the wet season, south toward the border lowlands when the water dried." This is strong. But "border lowlands" is slightly abstract — what name did the Wayúu use for the southern territories? The chapter should find the Wayúu's own geographic vocabulary.

**Summary:** Strong opening. Clear narrative pull. The seeds of the chapter's structural engine are planted. The challenge ahead is scale — how to let the intimate and the structural inhabit the same space without the chapter feeling like two different texts stitched together.
