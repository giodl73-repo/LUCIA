## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of Romani Era 1 Opening

**Overall:** This opening does something remarkable: it treats a language as a knowledge system. The Romani vocabulary is not decoration or color; it is the primary evidence, the map, the testimony. This is the linguistic equivalent of what the best LUCIA chapters do with navigation knowledge or ecological practice — it treats a non-written knowledge system with full intellectual seriousness. I am impressed.

**What works:**
- The central conceit — language as the only witness to the departure — is not metaphor. It is literally true, and the opening treats it with the rigor of an archaeological analysis. The loanword stratigraphy paragraph is intellectual engagement at the method level: Persian words older than Armenian, Armenian older than Greek. This is how the knowledge system works, shown at the level of HOW, not just WHAT.
- The listing of Romani words (dej, dad, phen, grast, pani, yag) with their meanings does exactly what the v1.3 amendment demands: it lets the culture's own knowledge system be the evidence. These words are not footnotes; they are primary sources.
- There is no hidden hierarchy. The absence of written records is not treated as a lack, and the culture's mobility is not treated as a failure to settle. "They were not the kind of people for whom chronicles were kept" locates the silence in the record-keeping systems, not in the people.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening engages deeply with the linguistic knowledge system but does not yet engage with the other knowledge systems the Roma carried. Metalworking has its own vocabulary and its own transmitted expertise. Horse culture has a knowledge system — breeding, care, the relationship between horse and road. Music, which is the Roma's most visible contribution to every culture they entered, is a knowledge system of extraordinary sophistication. The chapter will need to engage with these at the same depth it engages with linguistics.
- I want to feel the wonder of this journey more fully. A people who walked from India to Spain over three centuries, carrying their language, their music, their craft, their family structure, their legal system — and arrived intact. That is one of the great human achievements. The opening conveys the intellectual weight of this but not yet its wonder. Wonder is not romanticism; it is the honest recognition that what these people accomplished is extraordinary.

**The question I'd push on:**
The Roma's knowledge systems — metalworking, horse management, music, herbal medicine, fortune-telling (cartomancy and palmistry as professional expertise, not superstition) — are carried in the body and in oral transmission. These are knowledge systems as sophisticated as any laboratory pharmacology or formal music theory. Will the chapter engage with them at that level? Or will music be "atmosphere" and craft be "context"?
