# BOARD Review — Pané Scholar (Arrom/Keegan tradition)
## The Classic Taíno — Mythological and Ceremonial Accuracy

**Reviewer position:** Ramón Pané's Relación, Taíno mythology and ceremonial record
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

**The Cacibajagua narrative:**

The chapter renders the Cacibajagua emergence narrative as follows: the guardian Mácocael fails to return to the cave before dawn and is turned to stone by the sun; those who linger at the threshold are transformed into birds and trees and animals. This is consistent with Pané's account (Chapters I–III of the Relación, in Arrom's critical edition). The specific detail of the sun catching Mácocael at the entrance and the transformation of late-emerging people into birds, trees, and animals is accurately rendered.

One detail: Pané records that the people emerged not from one cave but from two: Cacibajagua ("cave of the jagua fruit") and Amayauna. The greater emergence happened from Cacibajagua, which is the tradition the chapter cites. The chapter's focus on Cacibajagua alone is accurate as the primary emergence site; the omission of Amayauna is a simplification but not an error for a narrative treatment.

The chapter's interpretation of the narrative ("Transformation between states of being is continuous... The passage between states is not a boundary but a permeability") is consistent with how William Keegan and José Juan Arrom have interpreted the mythological corpus. This is not invented interpretation; it reflects the scholarly consensus on the cosmological logic underlying the Taíno mythological system. ACCURATE.

**The zemí framework:**

The chapter distinguishes between the ancestral zemís in the cacique's bohío (the grandmother in the cotton figure, for example) and the community zemís governing local forces. This is consistent with Pané's account, which distinguishes between zemís that speak (those that have been properly activated) and those that do not, and between the personal zemís of caciques and the more general supernatural forces. ACCURATE.

**The cohoba ceremony:**

The chapter describes: Anadenanthera peregrina seeds, dried and ground; the tabaco forked tube; the purging rite; the duho; the altered state in which the zemí communicates; the cacique's subsequent report to the community.

Pané describes the cohoba ceremony in several chapters of the Relación. The substance is identified as cohoba (a hallucinogenic powder); the tube is described. Pané does not use the term Anadenanthera peregrina (he would not know the Linnaean name), but subsequent botanical scholarship has identified the cohoba powder as almost certainly derived from Anadenanthera peregrina seeds. The purging rite is attested in the early Spanish accounts. The duho's use in the consultation is attested in both Pané and the material record. ACCURATE.

One precision note: Pané records that the cacique also prepared an idol (zemí figure) specifically for the cohoba consultation, which would be placed before him during the ceremony. The chapter does not mention this specific element (the preparation of a consultation zemí). This is a minor omission that does not affect the chapter's accuracy but could be added for completeness.

**Pané's own position:**

The chapter notes in figures.md that Pané is "a Spanish friar recording what the Taíno told him, through translation" and that his accounts are "mediated by his interpretive framework and his purposes." This is the correct characterization. The chapter does not use Pané's characterizations as neutral description; it uses the mythological content he recorded as evidence of the tradition's framework while acknowledging the mediation. This is the correct scholarly approach to the source.

**Overall verdict:** The mythological and ceremonial content is accurate, consistent with Pané's Relación as interpreted by Arrom and Keegan. One minor addition recommended (the preparation of a consultation zemí) but not required. No corrections needed.
