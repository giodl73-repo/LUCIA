## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of The Amarna Interlude (Opening)

**Overall:** This is one of the strongest openings I have seen in this project. The choice to anchor the theological revolution in the hands of a stonecutter is inspired narrative craft, and it holds.

**What works:**
- The first two lines ("The stonecutter's name is not recorded. His chisel is.") create immediate tension between the anonymous and the material. The chisel outlasts the man. This is earned, not clever.
- The reversal of the stonecutter's function (maker to destroyer) is the structural engine of the entire opening, and it unfolds without the author announcing it.
- The chain-logic passage (stone-names-gods-order) is the best example of inside-voice theological reasoning I have seen. It reads as the stonecutter's own understanding, not as editorial exposition.
- "Copper on stone sounds the same whether you are carving a name or removing one." This sentence does more analytical work than a paragraph of commentary would.
- The pacing is excellent. Each paragraph builds pressure. The short paragraphs ("Now he had been ordered to destroy them." / "Unless the king was wrong.") land with weight because the surrounding paragraphs are dense.

**What doesn't:**
- The Akhetaten paragraph (line 21) briefly lifts the reader out of the stonecutter's perspective and into an omniscient narrator's overview. "Built in five years, a royal capital that would last less than twenty" is authorial knowledge the stonecutter does not have. The next paragraph addresses this ("The stonecutter did not know that"), but the momentary lift is a crack in the immersion.
- The catalogue of gods (line 25: Amun, Ra, Osiris, Hathor, Thoth) is powerful but risks reading as a list rather than as architecture. Consider whether the "load-bearing pillar" metaphor is doing enough to hold the catalogue inside the narrative voice rather than stepping into reference mode.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening establishes the paradox (the king commands the destruction of cosmic order as an act of cosmic order) but does not yet hint at what comes after. Where does the chapter go from here? The stonecutter's crisis is perfectly set up, but the pull at the end is atmospheric ("held their breath") rather than directional. What is the reader being pulled toward, specifically?
