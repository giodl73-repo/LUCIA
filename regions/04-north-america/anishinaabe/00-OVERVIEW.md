# Anishinaabe — "The Original People"

They call themselves Anishinaabe — "the original people" or "the people from the beginning." The Anishinaabe are a family of related peoples — Ojibwe, Odawa, and Potawatomi are the three most prominent members, sometimes called the Council of Three Fires — who share a language family (Algonquian), a migration story, and a system of spiritual and social organization organized around the doodem (clan) system and the Midewiwin (the Grand Medicine Society). Their world extends from the eastern shore of Lake Huron westward to the Prairies, from the boreal forest in the north to the northern edge of the Corn Belt in the south — a vast arc centered on the Great Lakes (the "Sweetwater Seas") and the rivers that connected them.

The Seven Grandfather Teachings — nizhwaaswi gagiigidowin — are the ethical foundation of Anishinaabe life: nibwaakaawin (wisdom), zaagi'idiwin (love), minaadendamowin (respect), aakode'ewin (bravery), gwayakwaadiziwin (honesty), dabaadendiziwin (humility), and debwewin (truth). These are not commandments but virtues — the qualities that the Creator gave the original people through the Seven Grandfathers (or in some traditions, Seven Grandfather Spirits) so that they would know how to live in right relationship with each other and with all their relations. They were transmitted through the story of the Anishinaabe migration from the eastern sea to the Great Lakes — a journey recorded in birchbark scrolls and maintained in the oral tradition of the Midewiwin. The migration story was simultaneously a historical record, a spiritual teaching, and a map.

Key places:
- **Gichi-gami (Lake Superior)** — "the great sea" — the sacred center of the Anishinaabe world; the largest freshwater lake on earth; the destination of the migration story's final chapter
- **Mooningwanekaaning (Madeline Island)** — "the place of the yellow-shafted flicker"; an island in Lake Superior's Chequamegon Bay; a major Anishinaabe spiritual and political center; the traditional seat of the Midewiwin
- **Mnidoo Mnising (Manitoulin Island)** — "spirit island" or "the island of the great spirit" — the largest freshwater island in the world; a sacred site on the border between Anishinaabe and Haudenosaunee territory
- **Baawitigong (Sault Ste. Marie)** — "the falls" — the rapids connecting Lake Superior and Lake Huron; a major gathering site for the fishery and for trade

## What Defined Them

**The Seven Grandfather Teachings as the governing ethics.** The Seven Grandfather Teachings were not simply a moral code; they were a description of how the world works when it is functioning correctly. Nibwaakaawin (wisdom) was the appropriate application of knowledge for the good of the community. Zaagi'idiwin (love) was the understanding that all beings are related and that the right response to that relationship is care. Debwewin (truth) was speaking from the heart what one knew to be so. The teachings were not external commandments but the natural expression of a being that understood its place in the web of relations. To live the Seven Grandfather Teachings was to live as the original people were made to live.

**The Midewiwin — the Grand Medicine Society.** The Midewiwin (the Grand Medicine Lodge) was the Anishinaabe's primary spiritual and healing institution: a multi-grade initiation society whose members learned the healing practices, the ceremonial knowledge, and the sacred songs that maintained the community's health and relationship with the spiritual world. Initiation into the Midewiwin required years of preparation, the payment of significant goods (the exchange itself was part of the ceremony's logic), and the willingness to undergo a ritual death and rebirth. The knowledge accumulated in the Midewiwin was recorded on birchbark scrolls — one of the few writing systems of eastern North America — and kept by designated keepers whose responsibility was its preservation across generations.

**The doodem system — clan as spiritual lineage.** The Anishinaabe doodem (clan) system organized individuals into totemic groups — each person identified with an animal (the crane, the fish, the bear, the loon, the marten, and others) whose spiritual characteristics were understood to be present in the clan members. The doodem governed marriage (you could not marry within your own doodem), determined social roles (some clans provided leaders, others warriors, others healers), and connected the individual to a trans-community network — any Anishinaabe community you encountered would have members of your doodem, creating an instant basis for kinship obligation and hospitality. The doodem system was the Anishinaabe world's citizenship.

**The birchbark scroll as memory.** The Anishinaabe developed a writing system — birchbark scrolls incised with pictographic records — primarily for the preservation of Midewiwin knowledge and the migration story. The scrolls were not public documents; they were sacred objects, kept by trained keepers and read in ceremonial contexts. The migration story recorded on the scrolls described the seven stopping places of the Anishinaabe's journey from the eastern sea to the Great Lakes, each marked by a prophetic sign and each associated with specific teachings. The scroll was simultaneously a history, a spiritual map, and a proof of the people's relationship with the land they had been given.

**The Great Lakes as the world's Sweetwater Seas.** The Anishinaabe understood the Great Lakes not as bodies of water but as the center of the world — the place where the Creator had placed the people after the migration, the place where the Midewiwin was established, the place where the fish, the wild rice, the maple sugar, and the birchbark that sustained Anishinaabe life were most abundantly available. The lakes were not scenery. They were the world's organizing geography, the axis around which the Anishinaabe world was organized, and their management and their fish runs were the foundation of the Anishinaabe economy and the focus of the treaty rights cases that the Anishinaabe have been winning in US and Canadian courts since the 1970s.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Migration from the East:** The seventh fire is being kindled at the final stopping place on the shore of Gichi-gami. The Anishinaabe have followed the prophets' signs from the eastern sea to here. The birchbark scroll records each stopping place. This is the place the Creator prepared. The community will build here.

**2 — The Midewiwin and the Great Lakes World:** At Mooningwanekaaning, the Midewiwin ceremony is being conducted. A person is lying in the ceremonial lodge, symbolically dead; the medicine men are singing the healing songs that will restore life. The birchbark scroll that records the ceremony's sequence is being held by the keeper who memorized it. This is the community's most important technology: the knowledge that maintains life.

**3 — The French Alliance:** Anishinaabe traders at the French post are exchanging beaver pelts for iron axes and copper pots. The axes are better than stone. The pots are much lighter than birchbark. The epidemic that the French brought without knowing they were bringing it has already killed a third of the community. The trade is not straightforward. Nothing about this encounter is straightforward.

**4 — Pontiac's War and Its Aftermath:** Pontiac of the Odawa is speaking at a council of Anishinaabe and allied nations. He is presenting a wampum belt with a message: the British have replaced the French without the gifts, the ceremonies, or the respect that the French at least performed. The British treat the nations as conquered subjects. The option Pontiac is proposing is war. The council will agree. In 1763, Anishinaabe and allied warriors capture eight of the twelve British posts in the Great Lakes region. The war will end without the decisive victory Pontiac sought, but the Royal Proclamation of 1763 that follows is the British acknowledgment that the indigenous nations' land cannot simply be taken.

**5 — The Long Struggle:** In 1983, the Seventh Circuit rules that the Anishinaabe's 1837 treaty rights to fish and gather in the ceded territory of Wisconsin are still in force. The ruling is greeted with protests from white sport fishermen and with decades of ongoing legal battles. The rights have been upheld. The birchbark scrolls that recorded the migration and the treaty negotiations are being digitized. The language classes at the Ojibwe school are full. The seventh fire is burning.
