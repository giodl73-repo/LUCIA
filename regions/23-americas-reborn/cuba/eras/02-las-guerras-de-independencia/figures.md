# Figures — Las Guerras de Independencia — La Manigua

## José Martí (1853–1895)

He was born in Havana in 1853 to Spanish immigrant parents — his father was a sergeant in the Spanish colonial military — and became the founding conscience of the Cuban nation. The trajectory of his life from Spanish colonial subject to martyr-architect of independence runs through every form of displacement a nineteenth-century political exile could experience: prison, forced labor, deportation, repeated exile, and the final return that he understood, correctly, as the last leg of a journey that would not end with arrival.

The stone quarry. He was sentenced to hard labor at sixteen after his arrest for the treasonous letter, and the image of the young man hauling stone in the Cuban heat — his ankles in shackles, the iron ring that he later described in a poem — was the first layer of a life that would be organized around constraint and the refusal of it. The quarry produced a poem; the exile produced an argument; the argument produced a party; the party produced a war.

In New York he was a journalist as much as a political organizer, and his journalism is the least read and most important part of his work. His dispatches on American life — on the Brooklyn Bridge, on the New York crowd, on the specific character of American power — are the most searching Cuban account of the United States produced in any century. He admired the republic's principles and feared its appetites. He wrote, in the year before his death, that he had spent his life in "the monster" (the United States) and knew it from within. He knew that when Cuba achieved independence the "monster" would be the next challenge. He did not live to confront that challenge. He died knowing it was coming.

The diary. Found in his jacket after Dos Ríos, published in fragments almost immediately, it became the primary text through which Cubans have understood his final weeks. Its register is unlike his political writing: quieter, more personal, more attentive to sensation. The jasmine. The hammock. The specific air of Cuba as a physical country rather than a political concept. The diary is the record of a man discovering the difference between the country he had described and the country he had lived in for thirty-eight days. He was, by the diary's evidence, discovering Cuba as if for the first time — which, in the only sense that mattered, he was.

He did not leave behind a political program detailed enough to have prevented what happened in 1898. He left behind a moral vocabulary — sovereignty, dignity, a Cuba that was "more than white, more than mulato, more than Black" — that every subsequent Cuban political tendency has tried to claim, which is both the measure of his achievement and the limitation of it.

## Antonio Maceo (1845–1896)

He is called the "Bronze Titan," a title that tells you something about how Cuban memory has approached him: at a slight distance, through a monument's vocabulary, with the respect that sometimes replaces the closer engagement that would be more uncomfortable.

The more comfortable Maceo is the great general, the brilliant tactician, the man who marched 1,000 miles across Cuba in three months and proved that the Spanish could not hold the island against a determined insurgency. This Maceo is universally celebrated in Cuba and is easy to celebrate.

The less comfortable Maceo is the man who refused the Pact of Zanjón in 1878 because it did not deliver abolition. Who refused it not out of stubbornness but out of a precise understanding of what the war had actually been for and what a peace without those terms would actually mean. Who went into exile for seventeen years rather than accept a Cuba that was technically reformed but structurally unchanged. Who returned in 1895 and fought not only for independence from Spain but for the specific kind of independence that would make the racial promise real.

He was Afro-Cuban in a society where that designation carried specific legal and social meaning — both the meaning the society imposed and the meaning the Afro-Cuban community had built within and against that imposition. He had grown up in the Afro-Cuban networks of Oriente, had fought alongside both the creole independence movement and the mass of Afro-Cuban soldiers who made up the Liberation Army's backbone, and he understood, with precision, the political economy of each alliance. He was not naive about the creole leadership's intentions. He was pragmatic: the alliance was necessary, and the Afro-Cuban fighters' leverage within the alliance was their military indispensability.

His death in December 1896, ambushed at Punta Brava near Havana, removed from the field the figure most capable of holding the revolution's racial promise to account. The ambush's exact circumstances have been disputed — some Cuban accounts have alleged betrayal, others attribute it to poor operational security. The ambush happened; the cause is uncertain. What is certain: he was dead at fifty-one, eighteen months before the United States arrived to determine the peace, and the peace that emerged would have been contested differently if he had survived to contest it.

## Máximo Gómez (1836–1905)

He was Dominican. He had fought in his own country's independence wars before turning his capacities toward Cuba. He commanded the Liberation Army in both the Ten Years' War and the 1895–1898 war, surviving both and living to see the republic declared on terms he had not fought for.

His relationship with Martí was complicated in the way that relationships between military commanders and political visionaries are always complicated: the visionary understood what the war was for; the commander understood what the war required; the two understandings aligned imperfectly. Gómez gave Martí the order not to ride toward the Spanish column at Dos Ríos. He understood, correctly, that Martí was not militarily useful and was extremely symbolically important, and that the combination made him someone who should be kept alive as long as possible. Martí did not follow the order, and Gómez's account of the day — written afterward, with grief that seems genuine — does not condemn him for it.

He continued. That is what Gómez did. He continued through the death of Martí and the death of Maceo and the reconcentration camps and the American intervention and the Treaty of Paris that excluded Cuba from its own peace negotiations. In 1902 he signed the protocol that ended the American occupation, handing authority to a republic he had fought thirty years to create and which was not, by several important measures, what he had fought for.

There is an account of Gómez at the declaration of the republic, watching the ceremony, silent. Whether the silence was satisfaction or grief is not recorded. He died in 1905 and was buried in Havana.

## The Lectores — Composite Figure

No individual lector (reader) has been named in historical sources as Martí's specific operative in the Ybor City factories, though several names appear in partial records of the period. The figure is reconstructed from what is known about the institution.

The lectores occupied an elevated platform in the center of the work floor. They read for four hours a day, with breaks, in a room where fifty or a hundred workers were rolling cigars at their tables. The choice of what to read was often put to a vote: workers raised their hands for the selection. This made the lector's role partly curatorial — they assembled the reading list — and partly interpretive, since the choice of what to read on a given day was itself a political act.

In the independence period, the Tampa and Ybor City lectores read Patria regularly. They read Martí's speeches. They read dispatches about the war. They read the casualty lists when casualty lists became available. A room of cigar workers listening to casualty lists had a specific quality. Some of the workers would have recognized names. Some of them were relatives.

The lector who read these lists, who read Martí's essays, who read the poetry that Cuban schoolchildren would memorize — this person was a transmission mechanism. The words went from Martí's pen to the exile press to the lector's voice to the workers' ears to the vote that directed their wages to the independence fund. The republic was built in this chain. It is appropriate that the chain runs through a person whose name has not been individually preserved, because the lectores were not individually the point. The transmission was the point.
