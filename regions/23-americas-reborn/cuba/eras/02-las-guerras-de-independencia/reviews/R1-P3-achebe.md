# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Panel Review of Opening

**Round:** 1
**Reviewer:** Chinua Achebe (P-3)
**Chapter:** Cuba Era 2 — Las Guerras de Independencia — La Manigua
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The voice is right. This is the first thing I need to say, because in a chapter about Cuba's independence war — a story that European and North American historians have told many times, usually from outside, usually with Martí as an exotic curiosity or a convenient martyr for a story that really belongs to Theodore Roosevelt and the Spanish-American War — getting the voice right is not a small thing. It is the whole thing.

The opening establishes the manigua as a republic. Not a hiding place. Not a terrain of disadvantage. A republic, with its own laws, its own generals, its own faith. This is the Cuban inside view, and it is correct. The mambises did not experience themselves as guerrillas skulking in the jungle; they experienced themselves as the legitimate government of Cuba operating in the only territory the Spanish had not yet claimed. The text honors this without explaining it to anyone.

The Yoruba gods are handled correctly. "The Yoruba gods the African mambises carried with them from the slave quarters had always known how to live in wilderness." This is good. It does not explain, does not bracket, does not footnote for a skeptical reader. The gods were there. The sentence is declarative. The African dimension of the Cuban independence movement is not a subaltern footnote here; it is structural.

What I want to flag: the word "manigua" gets its Taíno etymology correctly identified — this is right and important, the indigenous layer of Cuban language and landscape deserves this acknowledgment. But there is an opportunity the chapter may not take yet. The African mambises, the Taíno word, and Martí's specific vision of a Cuba where "Cuban is more than white, more than mulato, more than Black" — these three elements are in tension with each other and with the actual social structure of the independence war, which was racially complex in ways that shaped both its conduct and its aftermath. Antonio Maceo, the mulato general who died in the war six months after Martí, was arguably the more formidable military mind and represented a Cuban independence that the United States found more threatening precisely because of his racial identity. The chapter will need to hold the racial complexity of the manigua without resolving it prematurely into Martí's universalist vision. Martí's vision was real and powerful. So was the racial reality of Cuban society that the revolution did not — and in some ways could not — fully transform.

**The question I'd push on:** Does this chapter own the full story, including Antonio Maceo? The risk of a Martí-centered opening is that it follows the tradition of Cuban nationalist memory that centers the white poet-martyr rather than the mulato general who fought for three years and shaped the war's military strategy. Both matter. The chapter should find a way to hold both without reducing either to the other's supporting character.

**What's right:** The voice is right. The inside view is real. The discover register is exactly the correct choice for a story about exile and return, about words that become bodies, about a republic that lived in language before it lived in territory. Do not lose this voice.
