# Han — The Confucian Household of the World

We are the Han, and our name became the name for the Chinese people because we held what the Qin had built and made it livable. The Han empire was the Confucian answer to the Legalist state: not law as the engine of governance but virtue, ritual, and the cultivation of the morally educated official. We created the examination system's prototype — officials recruited through examination rather than birth. We sent Zhang Qian to the west and found the Silk Road. We fought the Xiongnu for a century and a half before we finally defeated them. We adopted Buddhism from India and produced Sima Qian's Shiji — the Records of the Grand Historian — the foundational text of Chinese historical writing.

We call our people Hanren — Han people — and our script Hanzi — Han characters — because the four centuries of Han rule made us the reference point for what Chinese civilization is. The Han model of the empire as a great Confucian household — the Son of Heaven as father to the realm, the officials as elder brothers who govern their territories with the same care a senior family member gives a junior — defined the aspirational model for every dynasty that followed. When a dynasty was strong and virtuous, it approached the Han ideal. When it was corrupt, the Han ideal was the measure of its corruption.

## What Defined Them

**The Confucian state.** Emperor Wu Di's decision to make Confucianism the official philosophy of the Han state — establishing the Imperial Academy (Taixue) to train officials in the Five Classics — was the most consequential intellectual policy decision in Chinese history. It created a ruling class whose shared texts, shared values, and shared sense of obligation to the realm were the same across vast distances. The Confucian official was not merely a bureaucrat; he was a moral actor whose governance was a form of virtue.

**The Silk Road.** Zhang Qian's missions to the west in the second century BCE established diplomatic contact with the states of Central Asia and opened the trade route that would carry silk west and Buddhism east. The Han didn't "create" the Silk Road — trade had moved along those routes earlier — but the Han state organized, protected, and systematically exploited the connections, establishing the first formal diplomatic relationships between China and the Hellenistic world and the Parthian empire.

**The Xiongnu confrontation.** The Han's relationship with the Xiongnu confederation defined its northern frontier for two centuries. The early Han policy of heqin — peace through marriage alliance and tribute — acknowledged the Xiongnu's power while buying peace. Emperor Wu Di's military campaigns finally broke Xiongnu power in the north, but at enormous cost, nearly bankrupting the state. The wall, the cavalry, the silk payments, and the eventual military defeat — this was the cycle that the Han repeated, and that every subsequent Chinese dynasty would repeat with different steppe peoples.

**Sima Qian and Chinese historical consciousness.** The Records of the Grand Historian — Shiji — compiled by Sima Qian in the first century BCE, was the first systematic history of China from the mythological origins through the Han. Sima Qian wrote it after Emperor Wu Di had him castrated as punishment for defending a defeated general. He finished the work in spite of his humiliation, or perhaps because of it. The Shiji established the format for Chinese dynastic history that was followed for two thousand years: annals, chronological tables, treatises on specific subjects, and biographies of notable individuals. History as literature, with a moral purpose.

**Paper and the expansion of literacy.** The Han period saw the invention and initial spread of paper — an innovation that eventually made literacy accessible beyond the expensive bamboo strip and silk alternatives. Paper was the long-term infrastructure of the Chinese literary and administrative culture: without it, the examination system, the historical archive, and the Buddhist textual tradition would have remained elite practices. The Han produced the technology that made the subsequent Chinese civilization possible.

## The Chapters

**1 — Early Han:** Liu Bang, the peasant rebel who became the Gaozu emperor, is reviewing his advisors' proposals for governance. His natural inclination is for the Daoist simplicity of minimal government. His Confucian advisor Chen Ping is explaining why this will not suffice. They are both right.

**2 — Emperor Wu Di:** Zhang Qian returns from the west. He has been gone twelve years. He has been captured twice. He has found the Dayuan — the people who have the "heavenly horses" of Ferghana. He has found something that will become the Silk Road.

**3 — The Silk Road:** A Sogdian merchant enters Chang'an with a caravan from the west. He speaks Sogdian, some Parthian, a little Chinese. He is bringing glass, Mediterranean wine, and lapis lazuli. He is returning with silk, lacquerware, and bronze. He has made this trip eight times.

**4 — Wang Mang:** The usurper has nationalized the land and is redistributing it according to ancient Zhou texts. He has renamed the currency and the government offices. The Yellow River has changed course and is flooding. The peasants are dying. The idea that the classics describe a working society is proving more complicated than he thought.

**5 — Eastern Han:** A monk from Parthia named An Shigao is translating Buddhist sutras into Chinese in Luoyang. He is the first Buddhist translator in China. The texts are strange, and Chinese has no words for many of the concepts. He is inventing the vocabulary that Chinese Buddhism will use.

**6 — The Three Kingdoms:** Cao Cao has just killed the last effective Han emperor's hope. In the south, Liu Bei is building his kingdom. In the east, Sun Quan holds the Yangtze. The Han is over. The story that follows will become the most popular historical novel ever written in Chinese.
