# PANEL-1 Review — P3: Chinua Achebe
## Mexico Era 4 — El Porfiriato y La Revolución

**Lens:** Cultural voice — dignity, authenticity, ownership

---

The opening passes the test I apply to every chapter: could the villages of Morelos read this and feel their story was told from inside? Yes. The milpa — the cornfield, the beans, the squash — is not called a "traditional agricultural system." It is named as what the people were fighting for. The village title deeds locked in the iron box are not curiosities of primitive land tenure; they are the documentary evidence of a claim that the state apparatus tried to erase. The prose understands that the villages had law on their side, older than the Porfiriato's law, and treated that as the relevant fact.

Several things are done well. "The men who owned the sisal plantations of Yucatán, where Maya workers were bound by debt peonage they could never repay, agreed with him" — this sentence locates the Maya workers inside their own situation without sentimentalizing it. "The revolution they were making" — the phrase is Zapata's own framing, not imposed. The Plan de Ayala restored land records to village authorities when towns were taken; this detail demonstrates that the revolutionaries understood what the revolution meant, and the prose trusts that understanding.

What concerns me about the chapter ahead: the Mexican state's own narrative of the Revolution — Zapata as national hero, the murals of Rivera, the rhetoric of mestizaje — tends to absorb indigenous and campesino agency into a national story that ultimately served the PRI. The chapter should be careful not to repeat that absorption. Zapata's politics were specific: he was fighting for village commons, for the ejido as a collective institution, against the privatization of land as such. The Constitution of 1917 acknowledged this. The chapter should tell what the Constitution actually did and did not do, from the perspective of the villages, not from the perspective of the revolutionary state.

There is also the question of the indigenous peoples of Morelos specifically — largely Nahua — and whether the chapter will name them as such rather than treating "campesinos" as an undifferentiated mass. The milpa is a Nahua agricultural form. Anenecuilco is a Nahua village. The documents Zapata recovered are colonial-era records of Nahua land rights. The chapter should carry this specificity.

**The question I'd push on:** When Zapata is killed in 1919 and the Constitution of 1917 is on paper and the villages are still waiting — who speaks for them in 1920? Is there an institutional voice, a community practice, a surviving form of village governance that carries the claim forward? The chapter should not end with Zapata's death as the close of the story; the village claim outlives Zapata.
