# Figures — Girmit: The Indenture System (1838–1917)

## Ramnaresh and Dulari

Composite figures, drawn from the indenture records of the Shahabad district in the 1880s and from the broader documentary record of North Indian girmitiya families. The names are representative; the details of their journey (the failed monsoon, the arkatti's visit, the depot at Calcutta, the ship, the estate in Trinidad) are reconstructed from patterns that appear consistently across the indenture records and the testimonies compiled by Marina Carter in *Voices from Indenture* (1996) and Gaiutra Bahadur in *Coolie Woman* (2013).

Ramnaresh represents the majority of indentured men: agricultural laborers from Bihar and eastern Uttar Pradesh, typically aged between twenty and thirty-five, recruited during periods of agricultural distress. The indenture records preserved his body (height, distinguishing marks, medical fitness) and erased his personhood (no record of his father's name, his village's name, his caste designation in most depot registers).

Dulari represents the women who carried the cultural infrastructure of the girmitiya communities: the religious texts held in memory, the cooking practices that would reconstitute a foodway in exile, the oral traditions that would be transmitted to children born in the colony. Women constituted roughly one-third of the indentured population (the mandated minimum was 40 per 100 men, though this ratio was rarely achieved). Their experience was distinct from the men's: recruited through different networks, subject to different forms of coercion on the plantation, and bearing the additional burden of cultural transmission in conditions designed to prevent it.

The decision to render Ramnaresh and Dulari as composite figures reflects the archival reality: individual girmitiya left fragmentary traces in the colonial record. The indenture registers, ship manifests, and plantation ledgers recorded physical characteristics and contract numbers. They did not record the Sundarkand sung in the hold, the turmeric packed in a rag, or the calculation made in a doorway in Shahabad. These details are drawn from the accumulated testimony of thousands of girmitiya and their descendants, rendered through two figures whose journey is historically typical even if their specific names are not.

## Totaram Sanadhya (c. 1876–1947)

Historical figure. A Brahmin from Firozabad, Uttar Pradesh, indentured in Fiji in 1893. Totaram is the girmitiya who became a witness. His twenty-one years in Fiji encompassed the full arc of the indenture experience: the recruitment (in his case, based on the arkatti's false claim that Fiji was near Calcutta), the ship, the cane fields, the decision to stay, the slow construction of a life, and finally the act of testimony.

His publication, *Fiji Mein Mere Ikkis Varsh* ("My Twenty-One Years in Fiji," 1914, co-authored with Pandit Benarsidas Chaturvedi), was the first sustained firsthand account of indenture conditions written by a girmitiya and published for an Indian audience. The book's power lay in its voice: simple Hindi, descriptive rather than rhetorical, recounting conditions in the tone of a man who had lived them and needed no literary device to make them compelling. The book became evidence in the Indian nationalist campaign to abolish indenture.

Totaram's Brahmin identity is important to his story. He was literate in Hindi and Sanskrit. He had the education to write and the social standing to be heard. Most girmitiya did not. His testimony speaks for himself, but it also speaks for the hundreds of thousands who could not write their own accounts. He understood this and said so.

After the abolition of indenture, Totaram returned to India and continued to advocate for the rights of overseas Indians. His legacy is double: the personal witness, and the political consequence of that witness.

## Gopal Krishna Gokhale (1866–1915)

Historical figure. Indian nationalist leader, member of the Imperial Legislative Council, and the political voice that translated the girmitiya's suffering into parliamentary action. Gokhale never experienced indenture himself. He was a Chitpavan Brahmin from Maharashtra, educated in English, a moderate constitutionalist in the Indian National Congress tradition. His contribution to the girmit story was to take the testimony of the girmitiya and bring it into the institutional space where the British colonial government could be compelled to hear it.

His 1910 resolution before the Imperial Legislative Council called the indenture system "a monstrous system involving almost semi-slavery conditions." The language was precise and parliamentary and devastating. The resolution was advisory and did not bind the colonial government, but it placed the indenture question on the official record and connected the girmitiya's experience to the broader Indian nationalist demand for self-governance. Gokhale's argument was structural: if the British claimed to govern India in the interest of its people, then a system that exported those people under coercion was a contradiction of the governing principle. The argument was not won in 1910. It was won in 1917, when the system was abolished.

Gokhale died in 1915, two years before abolition. He did not see the result of his campaign.

## Kunti (dates uncertain, active c. 1903–1930s)

Historical figure recovered through archival research by Gaiutra Bahadur in *Coolie Woman: The Odyssey of Indenture* (2013). Kunti was Bahadur's great-grandmother, indentured in British Guiana in approximately 1903. She represents the women's experience of the girmit system at its most particular: a woman who navigated the recruitment process alone (or was recruited alone, the circumstances are unclear from the records), who survived the crossing, who worked on a sugar plantation, and who built a life in a colony that had not asked for her and did not ease her way.

Bahadur's recovery of Kunti's story required detective work across three continents and multiple archives. The records were fragmentary. Kunti's name appeared on a ship manifest, in a plantation ledger, in a birth register. The spaces between these entries are the spaces where her actual life occurred: the daily negotiations with overseers, the cooking, the child-rearing, the social alliances that made survival possible.

Kunti's inclusion in this chapter represents a commitment to the women's experience as structurally distinct from the men's, not merely an addendum to it. The women of the girmit system were recruited under different pressures, experienced different forms of exploitation on the plantations, and bore the specific burden of maintaining family and cultural continuity in a system designed to extract labor and nothing else. Kunti survived it. Her great-granddaughter wrote a book about it. The survival and the book are both part of the girmit's legacy.

## The Arkatti

Not a single historical figure but a structural role in the indenture system. The arkatti (the term derives from the Hindi for "recruiter" or "agent") was the human interface between the colonial labor system and the Indian rural population. Arkattis operated on commission: they were paid per head for each recruit who reached the depot and passed the medical examination. Some were former girmitiya who had returned and used their knowledge of the system to recruit others. Some were local men working for sub-agents who reported to the emigration agent in Calcutta. Some were honest. Some were not. The system did not select for honesty. It selected for volume.

The arkatti in this chapter is rendered as a composite because the individual arkatti's identity is less important than the structural function he served. He is the system's salesman, and his omissions are the system's method. He is not a villain. He is a man doing work for which he is paid, and the work requires him to describe a journey he may never have made to a place he may never have seen. The gap between his description and the reality is the gap that 1.5 million people fell through.
