# България — "Bulgaria"

We came from the steppe and we stayed. The Bulgars — a Turkic people who had been ranging the Pontic steppe — crossed the Danube in 679 CE and established the First Bulgarian Empire on the territory of the Byzantine provinces south of the river. We were warriors from the steppe who conquered a largely Slavic population and, over generations, were absorbed into the Slavic language and culture even as we gave the new state its name and its ruling dynasty. Bulgaria is the product of this fusion: steppe warriors and Slavic farmers creating something new, neither quite Turkic nor quite Slavic, and then shaped into an Orthodox Christian civilization by the Byzantine missionaries who brought the Cyrillic alphabet north in the ninth century.

The Cyrillic alphabet is our gift to the Slavic world, and its origins lie in the specific cultural ambition of the First Bulgarian Empire. The missionaries Cyril and Methodius created the Glagolitic alphabet for the Slavic liturgy; their disciples Clement and Naum, working at the Preslav and Ohrid literary schools under the protection of the Bulgarian Tsar Simeon the Great, developed and spread what we now call the Cyrillic script. Simeon — who had been educated in Constantinople, who dreamed of the Bulgarian throne equaling the Byzantine, who called himself *Tsar of Bulgarians and Romans* — understood that an empire needed a literature. The Preslav literary school produced translations and original texts that made Bulgarian the first Slavic literary language. Everything written in Russian, Serbian, Ukrainian, Macedonian, and Belarusian today descends from the decisions made in the Bulgarian literary schools of the ninth century.

Key places:
- **Pliska** — the first Bulgarian capital; the great fortress-palace of the khans; the center of the First Empire before Christianity
- **Preslav** — Simeon's capital; the literary school; the Cyrillic alphabet workshop; a city built to rival Constantinople
- **Tarnovo (Tărnovo)** — the capital of the Second Bulgarian Empire; the Tsarevets fortress on the hill above the Yantra River
- **Ohrid** — the seat of the autocephalous Bulgarian patriarchate; Clement and Naum's school; the earliest Slavic educational institution

## What Defined Them

**The steppe-Slav synthesis.** The First Bulgarian Empire was the product of a remarkable cultural merger. The Bulgars — the Turkic ruling class — gave the state its name, its military organization, its initial ruling dynasty (the Dulo clan descending from Asparuh), and elements of its law code (the *Nominalia* and related texts). The Slavic population gave it its language, its agricultural base, its numerical majority, and its eventual cultural dominance. By the time of Simeon the Great in the early tenth century, Bulgarian had replaced the Bulgar language as the language of the court and the Church. The fusion was completed within three generations.

**Boris's conversion and the church question.** When Tsar Boris I accepted baptism in 864, he faced an immediate political crisis: both Rome and Constantinople wanted his new church under their jurisdiction. Boris played the two against each other with considerable skill, eventually accepting the Constantinople jurisdiction but extracting the maximum possible autonomy for the Bulgarian church. The result — an autocephalous (self-governing) Bulgarian patriarchate, with the Slavic liturgy in the Cyrillic alphabet — gave Bulgaria a church that was simultaneously Orthodox (and therefore aligned with Byzantium culturally) and independent (and therefore not under direct Byzantine ecclesiastical control). This ecclesiastical independence was one of the most important achievements of medieval Bulgarian statesmanship.

**Simeon's golden age and the Preslav school.** Simeon the Great (r. 893–927) was educated in Constantinople's finest schools and returned to Bulgaria determined to make Preslav the equal of the Byzantine capital. The Preslav literary school translated Greek theological and philosophical texts into Church Slavonic, developed the Cyrillic alphabet into a standardized writing system, and produced original Bulgarian literary works. The *Shestodnev* (Hexameron) of John the Exarch, the *Zlatostrui* (Golden Stream) of Tsar Simeon himself, the *Abecedarium Bulgaricum* — these were the products of a deliberate cultural program. When Simeon fought Byzantine armies repeatedly and called himself *Tsar of Bulgarians and Romans*, he was backing a real cultural achievement with military force.

**The Asen dynasty and the Second Empire.** The Byzantine reconquest of Bulgaria in 1018 did not eliminate Bulgarian identity. The Tarnovo uprising of 1185, led by the brothers Asen and Peter, drew on a century and a half of preserved Bulgarian ecclesiastical tradition — the Tarnovo Patriarchate, the Tarnovo literary school — to re-establish an independent Bulgarian state. Ivan Asen II (r. 1218–1241) was the Second Empire's greatest ruler, defeating the Latin Empire at the Battle of Klokotnitsa in 1230 and briefly making Bulgaria the dominant power in the Balkans. The Tarnovo school's literary output — saints' lives, theological texts, the *Manasses Chronicle* — continued the Preslav tradition with Byzantine sophistication.

**The Bogomil heresy — Bulgaria's other tradition.** The Bogomil movement — a dualist religious tradition that rejected the material world as the creation of an evil power, practiced radical asceticism, and rejected the institutional Church — originated in Bulgaria in the tenth century and spread through the Balkans and into France (as Catharism). The Bogomils are the shadow tradition of Bulgarian religious history: suppressed, periodically persecuted, but never fully eradicated. Their cosmological tradition — that the body is a prison and the material church is corrupt — influenced Bulgarian folk religion for centuries and represented a persistent alternative to the official Orthodox synthesis.

## The Chapters

**1 — The First Empire:** Khan Asparuh leads the Bulgar cavalry across the frozen Danube in the winter of 679–680. The Byzantine army cannot follow in the cold. On the southern bank, the Slavic farmers who have been living there watch the horsemen arrive. The Bulgars will rule; the Slavs will farm; within three generations the distinction will be blurred.

**2 — The Baptism:** Boris I receives the Byzantine missionaries and the representatives of Pope Nicholas I in the same year. Both sides want his soul, and Boris knows that his soul is not what is really being negotiated. He wants a patriarch of his own, answerable to him, not to Constantinople or Rome. He gets it, eventually, from Constantinople. The price is the Byzantine rite and the Cyrillic alphabet. He pays it without regret.

**3 — Simeon's Golden Age:** Simeon reads the latest Greek theological text that has been translated into Slavonic in his Preslav scriptorium. He is the most educated man on any Slavic throne. He is also fighting a war with Byzantium for the third time. He considers these facts together: the empire he wants to equal in culture, he must surpass in arms. Neither project is finished when he dies.

**4 — The Decline and the Second Empire:** The Asen brothers stand on a hilltop above the Yantra River in 1185 and dedicate their revolt to Saint Demetrius of Thessaloniki. They are starting a war against the Byzantine Empire with local followers and a lot of conviction. The saints will help. The Cumans they are about to recruit will help more.

**5 — The Second Empire:** The Ottoman army takes Tarnovo in 1393. The Patriarch Euthymius, who has been defending the city, is exiled to a monastery. The Bulgarian patriarchate is extinguished. The Bulgarian tsardom ends. What does not end is the Tarnovo literary tradition — it escapes north to Wallachia and Moldavia, and eventually to Russia, where Bulgarian clerical scholars help Moscow build its Third Rome claim on the same Byzantine inheritance that Bulgaria has been tending for five centuries.
