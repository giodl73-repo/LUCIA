# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman — Opening Review

**Chapter:** Norse/Vikings — Era 2: Vikingalond — The Viking Age Opens
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## First Pass (as a reader)

I kept reading. That is the test, and it passed. The ship-building sequence held me because it was specific: riven strakes, spruce-root lashing, the physics of a hull that flexes. I was not being told about Viking ships. I was watching one being built.

## What Works

The structural decision to open with the ship, not the raid, is exactly right. By the time Lindisfarne appears, I already understand the world that produced the raiders. The captain is a particular man: a bonde with six cows and two prior voyages. He is not "the Vikings." He is one farmer who chose to go west. That specificity is what makes the rest credible.

"A ship that fights the sea breaks. A ship that bends with the sea lives." This is the kind of sentence that earns a reader's trust. It sounds like something these people would actually say.

The Lindisfarne passage works because it is told twice: once from the monks' shore, once from the fjords. Alcuin's horror and the Norse farmer's practicality. Neither is privileged. The reader holds both.

## What Needs Work

**The captain needs a moment of decision.** He "chose to go west," but we do not see the choosing. I want a scene, however brief: the thing assembly, the conversation at the longfire, the moment he looked at his sons and his land and calculated. The decision is where the narrative tension lives. Right now it is stated but not shown.

**The technology passage (paragraph on ship types) is the one place the voice slips toward exposition.** "The knarr for cargo, broad-beamed and deep. The longship for war, shallow-drafted and fast" reads like a catalogue. Can the ship types emerge from action? The knarr loaded, the longship launched? Keep the information but embed it in the story.

## The Question I'd Push On

The captain is unnamed. I understand the historical constraint (we do not have names for the specific men who raided Lindisfarne). But unnamed characters are harder to follow. Can he earn a designation that the reader can hold onto? "The bonde from the Oslofjord" or even a byname? The chapter will need figures the reader can track.
