## B-2 — Michael Gomez — Board Review of The River War

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Author of African Dominion (2018); specialist in West African political history and the relationship between Islam and indigenous political traditions.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The chapter's treatment of the river navy as a distinctive Songhai military innovation is well-supported. No other western Sudanic state maintained a standing river fleet of this kind.
- The characterization of the Sorko and Bozo as the knowledge-base of the navy is consistent with what the chronicles and later ethnographic sources tell us.
- The structural interpretation of the Sunni Ali / scholar tension is accurate and reflects the current scholarly consensus: this was a conflict between two forms of political legitimacy, not simply between piety and impiety.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter correctly centers Songhai political agency. Sunni Ali is not a figure reacting to external forces; he is a state-builder operating within the logic of the middle Niger.
- The treatment of the older Songhai religious traditions (the harey, the Sorko as spiritual intermediaries) alongside Islam is appropriate and avoids the trap of presenting Islam as the "real" religion and indigenous practices as "paganism."
- The scholars are correctly presented as political actors with their own interests, not as passive repositories of knowledge waiting for a patron.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The river as structural engine rather than setting. This is the right analytical framework for Sunni Ali's state.
- The absence of verdict on the scholar-ruler tension. The chapter presents both sides' logic without adjudicating.
- The treatment of Djenne as a commercial and intellectual center in its own right, not just a target of Songhai expansion.

**What needs correction:**
- No factual corrections needed.
- One suggestion: the chapter could note that Sunni Ali's relationship with Islam was not simply "contempt" (as the hostile sources claim) but more likely a strategic ambiguity that allowed him to draw on both Islamic and non-Islamic sources of legitimacy simultaneously. The chapter approaches this in Section V but could be slightly more explicit about the strategic dimension of his religious positioning.

**Verdict:** APPROVED
