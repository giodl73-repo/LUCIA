# O Infante e o Atlântico — The Prince and the Atlantic

## Opening

The chart was wrong. Henrique knew this the way a man knows his own hand — not because he had sailed the coast himself, but because he had talked to enough men who had. Pilots from Genoa and Majorca who had worked the Guinea run. Moorish cartographers whose knowledge of the African coast extended farther south than any Christian king had thought to ask. Arab astronomers whose instruments could fix a latitude at sea with a precision that the old portolans, drawn from memory and rough distance-reckoning, could not match. The chart said the African coast ran south and then southwest, curving out into the open Atlantic and ending somewhere in the equatorial heat. The chart was wrong. The coast ran south, and then — he was becoming more and more certain of this — it curved east.

East meant India.

It was the year 1434 of the Lord, and Henrique, Infante of Portugal, Duke of Viseu, Governor of the Algarve, was forty years old. He had been sending ships south for fifteen years, since the conquest of Ceuta in 1415 had put him on the African shore and shown him that the world beyond his maps was not empty. He had not sailed himself on any of these expeditions. This surprised people who did not understand what he was doing. He was not an explorer in the way that word would later be used. He was a director of exploration — a man who accumulated knowledge the way a treasury accumulates gold: systematically, over years, against the day when you had enough to act.

Sagres was where he worked. The promontory at the southwestern tip of Portugal, Cape St. Vincent, where the Atlantic winds blew in from two directions and the rock fell straight to the sea. He had built his house here, his chapel, his working space. The local fishermen said the winds off that cape were the worst in the known world. They were also, if you understood them, the key to coming back from the south. The northeasterly trade wind could drive you down the African coast in days. The problem — the problem that had stopped every previous attempt to go far south — was that the same wind made return almost impossible. Sailors who had gone far enough south found themselves trapped, unable to beat back against a wind that was blowing them further from home with every tack. Some had never returned at all. Their ships had simply not come back.

The caravel solved this. Not the caravel as it would later be built — the great ocean-crossing vessel of the next generation — but the early caravel that his pilots were developing in the yards at Lagos, twenty miles east along the coast. Lateen-rigged, fore-and-aft rather than square. It could not carry as much cargo. But it could sail into the wind. It could tack. A square-rigged ship was a prisoner of the direction the wind blew. A lateen-rigged caravel could work against it, angle across it, use it. His pilots had been learning the trick of it: go far south, then go west, out past the wind, find the westerlies that blew back toward Europe, and ride those home. The ocean had structure. It had patterns. You had to learn them the way you learned any language — slowly, painfully, one expedition at a time.

In 1434, Gil Eanes had finally rounded Cape Bojador. It seemed like a small thing, one headland beyond another. But Cape Bojador had marked the edge of the known world for a generation of Portuguese pilots, not because anyone knew what lay beyond it, but because everyone feared it. The currents were terrible there. The waters shoaled. The return winds were unreliable. Men had tried and turned back. Henrique had sent Gil Eanes south before, and Gil Eanes had turned back with the others. This time Eanes had gone around. He reported: beyond the cape was more coast. More sea. The world did not end there. He brought back roses from the shore to prove he had landed.

So the work continued. Each year, another degree of latitude. Each pilot who came back brought a fragment of the coast — a headland, an inlet, a river mouth, a beach where strange people had watched the ship from the shore and then disappeared into the trees. Henrique collected all of it. He asked every question. He paid for the answers. He funded the expeditions from his own revenues as Governor of the Algarve and Grand Master of the Order of Christ, the military-religious order that had absorbed the Portuguese Templars and now financed the work of the sea.

He would not live to see the coast round to the east. That proof would come thirty years after his death, when Bartolomeu Dias pushed around the Cape of Good Hope in 1488. But Henrique understood, as clearly as a man can understand a thing he cannot yet prove, that the Indian Ocean was there, that it was reachable, and that the kingdom that reached it first would reorder the world's commerce in its favor.

He began sending ships anyway. The work was the work, whether he would see the end of it or not.
