## Voice-Random-Paragraph Test

Three randomly selected paragraphs, read in isolation:

**Paragraph A (from "The Cosmogram and the Crucifix"):**
"A nkisi is an object charged with the power of a covenant. The most well known to European observers were the nkisi nkondi — iron-nail figures, human in form, their surfaces studded with iron nails and blades, each nail recording a covenant made in the nkisi's presence. The nkisi did not merely symbolize the covenant; it held it. When two parties agreed to something in front of a nkisi nkondi — a boundary, a debt, an obligation — a nail was driven into the figure's body. The nail was the record. The nkisi was the witness. If one party violated the covenant, the nkisi's power turned against them; the community of believers in the nkisi's authority ensured that violations had consequences."

**Voice identification from Paragraph A:** The register is formal, deliberate, precise. Declarative sentences of medium length, building an argument. The vocabulary is legal ("covenant," "obligation," "boundary," "record"). The rhythm is neither steppe nor maritime nor griot — it is the rhythm of a document being read aloud by someone who believes the document is authoritative. The culture is identifiable: a Kongo context, a theological-legal frame.

**Paragraph B (from "The Governor's Calculation"):**
"The calculation worked like this: the Kongo political system held together through tributary relationships. A provincial governor sent tribute to Mbanza Kongo — raffia cloth, copper, ivory, sometimes captives taken in legitimate warfare — and received in return recognition, protection, and access to the goods that the capital controlled. The relationship was reciprocal. It was also asymmetric: the Manikongo had things the provincial governor needed, and the provincial governor had things the Manikongo needed, and this mutual need was the ligament of the kingdom's unity."

**Voice identification from Paragraph B:** Same register. The analytical mode carries through without breaking into textbook. "The ligament of the kingdom's unity" is a figure from within the diplomatic-legal frame — not a metaphor from outside. The paragraph identifies itself, from rhythm alone, as the same chapter.

**Paragraph C (from "The Archive"):**
"He wrote again. He wrote in 1529. He wrote in 1533. He wrote in 1540, by which point he was an old man and the trade had expanded, not contracted, in the years since his first protest."

**Voice identification from Paragraph C:** The repetition structure ("He wrote again. He wrote in 1529. He wrote in 1533.") is the epistolary register under compression — the formal documentary accumulation of a record. The starkness of it is not steppe; it is the starkness of a filing cabinet counting letters. The voice is consistent with Paragraphs A and B.

**Test result: PASS.** Three unrelated paragraphs, read in isolation, all identify the same culture (Kongo, Christian, formal), the same register (theological-legal), the same period (early-middle sixteenth century). The voice has not drifted.

## Register Drift Scan

Reviewing for: textbook voice, modern commentary, passages that sound written for a Western audience rather than from inside the culture.

**Flag 1 — Minor (E-2-01):**
Location: Opening section, paragraph beginning "His strategy was not assimilation."
The paragraph is working hard to preempt a misreading — "not assimilation" signals the author's anxiety about how the material will be received. The paragraph's content is correct and inside the frame, but the opening move ("was not assimilation") reveals awareness of a Western audience who might misread. The content should lead rather than the preemption.
Recommended fix: Open the paragraph on the active construction: "He was using European legal and theological categories to bind the Europeans." Drop the "not assimilation" framing.

**Flag 2 — Minor (E-2-02):**
Location: "The School and the Ship" section, paragraph on Henrique's return.
"What the Kongo court made of him when he returned — a man shaped by Europe, holding a European title, having spent most of his life on the other side of the Atlantic — the record does not say."
This sentence reads as authorial observation from outside — the author telling the reader they are uncertain. The honest-uncertainty principle applies, but the formulation is slightly external. Inside the chapter's register, honest uncertainty would be noted more briefly: "The record is silent on how the court received him."
Recommended fix: Compress. "The record does not say how the court received him."
