# R2-P2 — Ibn Khaldun
## Panel Review — PANEL-2 (notes review)
**Chapter:** Se Dena Tíd — The Danish Time (Anglo-Saxons, Era 4)

---

The production bible has answered my structural concern. The engine is now correctly named as the three-part system — burh, school, text — and the notes understand that these are interlocking, not parallel. Good.

I want to push on the Danelaw's presentation. The notes say "not a concession of defeat but a recognition of a new reality." This is correct but underspecified. The Danelaw is a governance technology: it creates a recognized boundary, which means it creates a border that can be administered. Alfred cannot un-make the Danish settlement, but he can give it a form. A recognized Danelaw is an entity that can sign treaties, take oaths, be sued. An unrecognized Danish occupation is a chaos that cannot be managed. The act of treaty-making is itself a form of governance — it extends the concept of law to cover an adversary, which is something only the party that OWNS the concept of law can do. Alfred owns the concept because he has written the laws in English.

This is the deeper argument: the translation program is not only about literacy. It is about the portability of law. A law in Latin is a law for clerics. A law in English is a law for every man who owes military service, every reeve who collects the gafol, every thegn who administers the king's will in the shires. When Alfred insists that his administrators must be able to read — or must appoint someone who can — he is insisting that governance cannot happen without the governed being able to read the rules. This is a theory of legitimacy, not just a literacy program.

The chapter should name this argument explicitly — not as modern political theory projected backward, but as Alfred's own explicit reasoning from the Preface to the *Pastoral Care*.

**One gap in the notes:** The *witan* — the council of wise men — receives less attention than it deserves. The notes mention it in the overview but not in the production bible. Alfred governed with the witan's counsel and consent. His laws were issued *mid witena geþeahte* — with the counsel of the wise men. This is the structural counterpart to his translation program: the burh is the physical institution, the school is the human institution, the text is the cultural institution, and the witan is the political institution. Without all four, the engine is incomplete.
