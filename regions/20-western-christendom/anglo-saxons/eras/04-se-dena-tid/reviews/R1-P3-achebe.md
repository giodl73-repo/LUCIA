# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Review — PANEL-1
**Chapter:** Se Dena Tíd — The Danish Time (Anglo-Saxons, Era 4)
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (opening review)

---

The voice is right. That is the first and most important thing.

The opening inhabits the Saga-Spare register without straining for it. The sentences land with the weight of the Chronicle: short, deliberate, unwilling to explain more than is necessary. "He had a small company with him. That is all the record says: a small company." That acknowledgment of the source's silence — and the willingness to stop there, not to fill the gap — is correct. It is honest. The author knows what they know and knows what they don't, and does not pretend otherwise.

The culture owns this story. Alfred is not presented as a proto-modern administrator who happened to live in the ninth century. He is a man formed by a specific set of obligations — law and protection — that belong to his world, not ours. When the chapter says "if a king who cannot protect his people is still a king, it is only because the idea of kingship survives the man who holds it," that is Alfred's own framework of legitimacy, not a modern political science gloss. Good.

Guthrum is well-handled. He is not a villain. He is a force, a counterpart, someone with his own purposes who is met on Alfred's terms at the baptism. The naming — "He gave him Æthelstan for his Christian name" — is noted without editorial comment. The reader understands what is happening. That trust in the reader is characteristic of good voice.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening is told entirely from Alfred's perspective — which is appropriate for the hook. But the chapter will need to find the people who are not Alfred: the herdsman's wife, the men on the marsh island, the thegns who came to Egbert's Stone and "were glad to see him." The Chronicle tells their gladness in three words. The chapter should make it felt. An Anglo-Saxon thegn's gladness at seeing his lord return from the marsh is not the gladness of a modern citizen. It is the gladness of a man whose obligations — military service, land tenure, the whole structure of his life — depend on a lord who is still alive to receive them. Make that specific.

Also: the chapter's scope is 793–900. The opening opens in 878. That is correct — this is the hinge. But the chapter must also account for the decades before: Lindisfarne in 793, the Great Heathen Army of 865, the death of Æthelred and Alfred's succession at a moment of maximum crisis. The opening does not need to carry all of this — but the chapter must, and the chapter's voice must remain this precise when it does.

No revision needed at the opening stage.
