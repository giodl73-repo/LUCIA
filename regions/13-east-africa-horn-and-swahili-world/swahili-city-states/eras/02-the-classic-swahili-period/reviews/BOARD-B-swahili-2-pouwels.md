## B-swahili-2 — Randall Pouwels — Board Review of "The Classic Swahili Period"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Islam on the East African coast, the localization of Islamic practice in Bantu-speaking societies, the Shirazi tradition, Islamic commercial law and its interaction with Bantu custom.

**Historical accuracy:**

- The description of Islam's arrival on the coast ("gradually, carried by people who came and stayed") is accurate and reflects the scholarly consensus. Islam was not imposed by conquest or mass migration. It was adopted through commercial contact, intermarriage, and the gradual integration of Muslim traders into coastal Bantu-speaking communities.
- The dual legal system (Islamic law for commercial matters, Bantu custom for kinship and land) is accurately represented and reflects the pattern documented across the Swahili coast.
- The dispute resolution scene is well-constructed and plausible. The application of Islamic commercial contract principles alongside elder-mediated arbitration reflects the observed pattern of legal pluralism in Swahili society.
- The Friday prayer scene is accurate: the congregational prayer, the imam's role, the separate women's section, the social hierarchy visible in seating arrangements.
- The Shirazi genealogy is handled with the correct nuance. The chapter presents it as the dynasty's claim while acknowledging the analytical question, without debunking it from outside. This is the correct approach. The Shirazi tradition is political genealogy, not historical ethnography, and the chapter treats it as such.

**Cultural authenticity:**

- Islam is presented as the coast's own faith, not as an import. This is the most important test for this chapter, and it passes. The key sentence: "Islam had come to the coast the way the monsoon brought everything else: gradually, carried by people who came and stayed." Islam is naturalized, not foreign.
- The integration of Islamic and Bantu practices is presented as organic, not as a collision or a compromise. "They wove the two together" is the correct framing.
- The mosque is presented as a community institution, not as evidence of Arabization. The Friday prayer unifies the town across social divisions. This is accurate.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The phrase "the faith put down roots in the coral soil and adapted, as everything adapted on the coast" is an excellent encapsulation of the localization process. Islam on the Swahili coast is not diminished Islam; it is Swahili Islam.
- The observation that the imam's family "had studied in the Hejaz two generations back and returned with the learning and the authority that the journey conferred" accurately captures the prestige economy of Islamic learning on the coast. The journey to the Hejaz was a credential, and the knowledge brought back was a form of social capital.
- The chronicle tradition is appropriately introduced as the coast's own memory system, writing in Arabic script but recording local history.

**What needs correction:**

- The chapter implies that women prayed "in their own section, separated by a coral-stone screen." While gender separation in prayer is standard in Islamic practice, the archaeological evidence for specific women's prayer spaces in early Swahili mosques is limited. The chapter should soften this slightly: women's prayer arrangements varied across the coast and across periods.
- The phrase "a man's inheritance followed Islamic law for his tradable wealth and Bantu custom for his clan lands" is broadly correct but simplifies a more complex reality. Inheritance practices on the coast varied by locality and by period, and the boundary between Islamic and Bantu domains was negotiated, not fixed. A note acknowledging this variability would improve accuracy.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

Conditions: (1) Soften the women's prayer section — acknowledge variability. (2) Add a note that inheritance practices varied across the coast.
