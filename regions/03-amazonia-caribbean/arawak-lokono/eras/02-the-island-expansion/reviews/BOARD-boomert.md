# Board Review — B-2 Arie Boomert
## Era 2: The Island Expansion | Arawak / Lokono

**Reviewer domain:** Trinidad-Orinoco interface, Caribbean interaction spheres
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Overall Assessment

The chapter's treatment of the Trinidad gateway zone is consistent with my findings. The multi-community complexity of the expansion — the presence of pre-existing Archaic populations, the gradual character of the Saladoid movement, the material adaptation to island conditions — is handled responsibly.

## Factual Review

**Trinidad as gateway:**
- The chapter positions Trinidad as "the first island the delta population reached, close enough to maintain dense connection with the continent." This is accurate. The Trinidad sites (Cedros, Palo Seco, and others in my sequence) show the earliest Saladoid material outside the continental mainland, and the ceramic evidence demonstrates continuous mainland contact rather than a clean break.

**Archaic-Saladoid interaction:**
- The fishing-guide figure who shows Kurima the reef channel is the chapter's image of Archaic-Saladoid knowledge exchange. This framing is good: the archaeological evidence supports sustained coexistence and exchange (the Archaic populations had specialized coastal and marine knowledge the Saladoid arrivals lacked; the Saladoid arrivals had manioc agriculture and ceramic traditions the Archaic populations adopted elements of). The knowledge-exchange framing is consistent with the evidence.

**Matapi technology:**
- The tirite palm (Ischnosiphon arouma) attribution is correct. This is the plant documented in ethnographic accounts of Arawak-family communities using the fiber press. The matapi itself is not archaeologically attested — organic preservation is poor — but the starch residues on ceramic griddles from Saladoid sites confirm manioc processing, and the fiber-press technique is the only plausible method for the large-scale processing the starch evidence implies.

**Conuco mound farming:**
- The chapter mentions the conuco mound system as an island agricultural adaptation in Section 5. This is documented for later periods but may be somewhat anachronistic for the 400–700 CE window. The conuco is well-attested for the Taíno period (post-700 CE) but the evidence for its Saladoid-era development is less certain. I would recommend softening the claim to "a version of the raised-bed mounding system that would become the conuco" — acknowledging the development rather than the established form.

## Corrections Required

**One correction:** The conuco sentence in Section 5. Current: "The island women developed planting techniques suited to this — the conuco mound system, which concentrated organic matter and improved drainage — that differed from continental practice." Recommended revision: "The island women developed planting arrangements that concentrated organic matter in raised mounds and improved drainage — the beginning of what later communities would elaborate into the conuco system — that differed from continental practice."

**No other factual corrections required.**

## Board Verdict

**ENDORSE WITH ONE REQUIRED CORRECTION.** The conuco dating issue is the sole factual problem; the correction is minor and does not affect the chapter's argument. All other claims are within the evidence.
