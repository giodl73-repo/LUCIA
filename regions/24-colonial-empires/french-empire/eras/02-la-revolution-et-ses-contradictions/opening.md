# La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
## Opening — Era 2 (1789–1815 CE)

---

Moreau de Saint-Méry had a system.

In his library in Port-au-Prince, in the year the Estates-General convened in Versailles, he was completing his *Description topographique, physique, civile, politique et historique de la partie française de l'isle Saint-Domingue* — a monument of Enlightenment classification, the most thorough census of a colonial world ever assembled in the French language. He had catalogued the colony's soil types, its rainfall patterns, the varieties of cane grown on the northern plain, the tonnage of sugar loaded at Le Cap-Français each season. He had catalogued its people with the same precision.

There were one hundred and twenty-eight racial categories.

Each required its own designation. Each designation corresponded to a legal status, a set of rights, a set of restrictions, a position in the social architecture that produced the sugar that made Saint-Domingue the wealthiest colony in the history of the Atlantic world. *Sacatra. Griffe. Marabou. Quarteronne. Métis. Mamelouque. Quarteron. Sang-mêlé.* The classifications descended through the fractions with the logic of a mathematical table: an eighth part, a sixteenth, a sixty-fourth. At some denominator small enough that the eye could not see it, a man became white. The law knew where that line was. Moreau de Saint-Méry knew where it was. The line was not nothing. It was everything.

This was the administrative genius of Saint-Domingue: a system so precise in its classifications that it could manage 500,000 enslaved laborers, 30,000 free people of color, and 32,000 white colonists as distinct legal communities with distinct legal fates, held in an equilibrium that produced 40 percent of Europe's sugar and half of its coffee, year after year, without failing. From the vantage of the colonial administration, the system worked. The ledgers balanced. The ships departed full. The numbers added up.

Moreau de Saint-Méry was not a cruel man. He was, by the standards of his world, an educated one — a lawyer, a man of letters, a Fellow of learned societies, a reader of Montesquieu and Raynal. He had no difficulty holding, simultaneously, the proposition that men possessed natural rights and the proposition that the system of Saint-Domingue was rational and necessary. The Revolution assembling in Paris would produce the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen within weeks of his completing his description of the 128 categories. The Rights of Man were universal. The categories were practical. Both could be true, and indeed both had to be true, because the economy of France depended on it.

The contradiction had a geography. It ran between Paris and Port-au-Prince, across 7,000 kilometers of Atlantic Ocean, and across the legal fiction that what happened in Saint-Domingue was a separate species of event from what was being declared in the Salle du Jeu de Paume. Raynal had seen it clearly enough. In his *Histoire des deux Indes*, published in 1770, updated through 1780, he had written a passage that the abbé himself could not quite believe he had written: that the enslaved of the colonies were waiting for a great man to raise the standard of liberty. He wrote it. It was read. Toussaint Louverture, who would eventually become that great man, had read it. He had read Raynal and Montesquieu and the *Épictète* in the edition Moreau de Saint-Méry had catalogued in Port-au-Prince. The literate enslaved and the free men of color read the Enlightenment alongside their enslavers, with more urgency, because the stakes for them were not theoretical.

In August 1791, in the forest of Bois Caïman in the northern province, a ceremony was held. The details are recorded through hostile witnesses and later elaboration, but what followed the ceremony is not in dispute: on the night of August 22, the plantations of the northern plain went up. The fires could be seen from the harbor at Le Cap-Français. The insurrection that became the Haitian Revolution had begun.

The system of 128 categories was about to encounter something no category could hold.

What Moreau de Saint-Méry had built was, in its own terms, a work of Enlightenment reason. Classification was knowledge. Knowledge was administration. Administration was order. The planners and administrators of the French colonial world genuinely believed this — not as an excuse but as a conviction, the same conviction that would produce the *Code Napoléon*, the *Cadastre*, the metric system, the prefectural system that divides France into departments to this day. The Enlightenment project was the rationalization of the world. Saint-Domingue was part of the world. Therefore Saint-Domingue required rationalization.

That the rationalization rested on 500,000 bodies was a cost the ledgers absorbed. The ledgers were very good at absorption.

By 1804, Saint-Domingue would be Haiti — the first Black republic in the Western hemisphere, the first and only successful slave revolution in history. Napoleon would have sent his brother-in-law Leclerc with 60,000 soldiers to prevent it, restored slavery in the colonies he still held, and sold Louisiana to the Americans for reasons of imperial economy that had nothing to do with the liberty he claimed to carry west. Moreau de Saint-Méry, by then living in Philadelphia in exile, his Port-au-Prince library burned, would be writing his memoirs of a world that the categories had failed to hold.

The Declaration of the Rights of Man had not failed. It had, in fact, worked exactly as its words required.

Someone had read it.
