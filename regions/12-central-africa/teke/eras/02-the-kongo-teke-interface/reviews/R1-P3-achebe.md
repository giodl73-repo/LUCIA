# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Round 1 — Opening Review

**Era:** Teke / The Kongo-Teke Interface
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

The Teke own this opening. That is the first thing I will say, and it is the most important.

There is no outsider here. There is no narrator standing above the plateau road watching the broker and thinking "how fascinating, how sophisticated for an interior African commercial network." There is a broker who knows his business, examining copper he knows how to examine, thinking in the terms of a commercial logic he has inherited and practices with competence. The reader enters the Teke world through the broker's perception, and the broker's perception does not contain any category for "what do Europeans think of this." This is exactly right. This is the voice that was required.

The detail that I find most telling: "A man who gloated at the price told the other man the price was wrong." This sentence is the broker speaking in his own mind, in the language of his own commercial ethics, about what competent practice looks like. It is not a translation of an indigenous norm into Western commercial theory. It is the norm in its own register. I am satisfied.

The question I would push on:

The Kongo relationship. The opening positions the Kongo provincial market as one of three destinations for the copper — "the agents of the Manikongo's governors paid in cloth and shell beads." This is accurate, and it is appropriately subordinate to the broker's larger calculation. But the Kongo-Teke relationship across this era is not simply commercial. The Kongo kingdom, at various periods, attempted to politically subordinate the Teke, to impose tribute, to control the plateau roads. The Teke responded not with military force — they had no standing army to speak of — but with the Makoko's spiritual authority and with the commercial leverage of making the roads unusable to anyone the Makoko chose not to serve. This political dimension must be visible in the chapter, and it must be rendered from inside the Teke logic of how political authority works.

The Makoko is not mentioned in the opening. He will need to be present in the chapter, because the broker's ability to operate on the plateau ultimately rests on the Makoko's authority. The broker is not acting in a vacuum. He is acting within a political-spiritual framework that the chapter must honor.

What I am watching for in the chapter: any moment where the text positions Teke commercial sophistication as surprising, remarkable, or in need of Western validation. The Atlantic trade section will be the most dangerous territory — the introduction of European goods and eventually the catastrophic slave demand creates a natural temptation to frame the Teke response as "response to European contact" rather than as the Teke's own strategic decisions within their own framework. The chapter must resist this. The Atlantic trade arrives from the Teke's perspective as a change in what the coastal end wants, not as a civilizational encounter.

The voice is right. The chapter needs to sustain it.
