# BOARD — B-norse-2 — Judith Jesch — Board Review of Vikingalond

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Old Norse language, runic inscriptions, skaldic poetry, Norse maritime culture and terminology.

## Historical Accuracy

**Terminology check:**
- *Bonde* — correctly used as a free farmer/landowner. Accurate.
- *I viking* — correctly used as an activity, not an identity. The chapter's treatment of *vikingr* as describing what a man does, not what he is, reflects the scholarly consensus (Jesch 2001).
- *Thing* — correctly described as a legal assembly of free men. The open-air setting is accurate.
- *Blot* — correctly described as a seasonal sacrifice. The blood-sprinkling is attested in later sources (Snorri, Heimskringla) though the practice in the early Viking Age is reconstructed.
- *Seter* — correctly used for summer mountain pasture.
- *Hov* — used for the place of sacrifice. This is acceptable, though the archaeological evidence for dedicated cult buildings in the early Viking Age is debated.
- *Jatigui* — this term does not appear (it belongs to the Mali chapter). Good.
- *Knarr* — correctly distinguished from the longship as a broader, deeper cargo vessel.
- *Styrbordi* — correctly identified as the origin of "starboard." This is a nice touch of linguistic evidence.
- *Longphort* — correctly used as the Irish term for Norse fortified ship harbors. This is an Irish loanword, not Old Norse, which is appropriate since it describes Norse settlements in Ireland from the Irish perspective.

**Source-type awareness:**
The chapter correctly distinguishes between contemporary sources (Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, Alcuin's letters, Frankish Royal Annals) and later sources (sagas, which are 13th-century writings about earlier events). The chapter does not claim saga evidence as contemporary testimony for 793-850 events. This is important and well-handled.

One concern: the navigation section describes Norse wayfinding in detail (reading clouds, birds, wave patterns, water color). These descriptions are largely drawn from later saga accounts and from analogy with other maritime cultures. The chapter presents them as established practice without flagging the source gap. This is acceptable as narrative technique (the LUCIA method of rendering from inside) but the notes.md or figures.md should acknowledge that specific navigational techniques for the early Viking Age are inferred, not directly attested.

## Cultural Authenticity

The saga voice is well-rendered. Short declarative sentences, understatement, forward motion. "The wind had been fair" is exactly the kind of sentence the saga tradition would produce. The chapter reads like saga prose translated into modern English while preserving the register. This is a genuine achievement.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The distinction between activity (*i viking*) and identity is linguistically correct and culturally important.
- The ship terminology is accurate throughout.
- The treatment of Norse cosmology (Thor, Odin, Freyr) as lived reality rather than "mythology" is appropriate for the inside perspective.
- The runic and skaldic evidence is not over-claimed. The chapter does not attribute specific rune-stones or poems to events where they do not belong.

## What Needs Correction

1. **Minor:** "The word starboard comes from *styrbordi*, the steering side." The Old Norse form is more precisely *stjórnborði* (steering-side). *Styrbordi* is a somewhat simplified rendering. Either form is acceptable for a general readership, but the notes could include the more precise form.

2. **Observation:** The chapter does not use any skaldic verse. This is appropriate for the 793-850 period, since surviving skaldic poetry from this early period is extremely limited. The decision not to invent or misattribute verse is correct.

**Verdict:** APPROVED
