# Panel Review — R2-P1 (Barbara Tuchman)
## La Conquête — Notes Review (PANEL-2)

**Reviewer:** P-1 Barbara Tuchman
**Stage:** PANEL-2 (Notes)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The production bible has solved my main concern from the opening review: each section now has a scene anchor. Lanfranc in the scriptorium. Æthelric before the commissioners. The body at Caen. These are not decorative — they are structural load-bearing moments that will prevent the chapter from becoming a survey of Norman administrative achievements.

The section plan is sound. Six sections, each with its own moment, building from the field at Hastings to the corpse at Caen. The deathbed is the right close: Orderic's speech is genuinely extraordinary, and the detail of the body not fitting the coffin is exactly the kind of specific fact that earns its weight without being staged.

### One Concern Remaining

The Harrying of the North section (Section 3) will be the hardest to write, and the notes are appropriately careful about it: "It cannot be softened. It also cannot be lingered on for spectacle." This is correct guidance, but it does not yet answer the narrative question: whose point of view? The section proposes rendering it through William's own logic — deterrence, governance, the calculation of overwhelming force. This will work for the structural argument. But Orderic Vitalis's account includes something William's logic does not: the sight of the dead. Orderic saw or recorded the aftermath — villages empty, no animals, no people, the roads strewn with the starved. The chapter needs at least one moment of the English reality inside the Harrying, not only William's calculation of it. Otherwise Section 3 becomes a description of a policy, not a description of what the policy did to bodies.

The Æthelric figure in Section 5 is well conceived and correctly flagged as composite. His appearance as a witness rather than a victim is the right framing. The Domesday testimony pattern is historically specific enough that this composite is honest.

### What I Want to See in the Chapter

The narrative momentum between sections must not drop. Each section must end in a way that makes the next section feel necessary. Section 2 (castles) should end with the system in place but the country not yet fully ordered — which pulls into Section 3 (the Harrying, the northern rebellion). Section 3 should end with William having demonstrated the cost of rebellion — which pulls into Section 4 (the Church as the administrative apparatus for a pacified country). Section 4 should end with the administrative infrastructure complete — which makes Section 5 (Domesday) feel like the survey of what has been built. And Section 5 must pull into Section 6 with the weight of what the survey reveals: a country made legible, a king who made it so, and a body that is already failing.

---

**Verdict:** Ready to write. The structural plan is sound, the scenes are anchored, the terminal condition is strong. Execute the chapter.
