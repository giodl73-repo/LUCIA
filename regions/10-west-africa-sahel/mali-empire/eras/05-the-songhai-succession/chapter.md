# The Songhai Succession

## I. The Full Genealogy

The jelilu sang the full genealogy. They sang it in Niani as though nothing had changed.

Bala Faseke's heirs stood before the mansa's court in the heat of the dry season, the year 853 of the Hijra (1449 CE), the mud-brick walls of the audience chamber still cool from the night though the air above the compound had already begun to shimmer. They opened their mouths and the names came out in the order they had always come: Sundiata, who crawled and then walked and then conquered. Wali. Khalifa. Abu Bakr, who sailed and did not return. Musa, who gave everything and came home with knowledge. Sulayman. Maghan. The names of the mansalu who held and the mansalu who lost and the mansalu who held again, one after another, the genealogy reaching forward like a rope pulled taut across two centuries of rule.

The mansa who sat on the raised platform and received the genealogy was not Musa. He was not Sundiata. The jelilu did not say this, because a jeli does not rank the kings he sings. But the court was smaller now. The compounds beyond the palace wall were quieter than they had been in the years when caravans assembled for the Sahara crossing and gold flowed north and scholars flowed south and the Manden Kurufaba stretched from the Atlantic salt flats to the copper mines of Takedda. The tribute that had once come from Timbuktu, from Gao, from the middlemen of the Niger bend, had thinned. Some of it had stopped entirely. A messenger sent to collect what was owed might return with promises, or might return with nothing, or might not return.

The jelilu sang anyway. The genealogy did not contract when the empire did.

Outside Niani, the world the Kurukan Fuga had built was coming apart at its edges. Not from invasion. Not from plague. From a simpler thing: the center could no longer give enough. The Kurukan Fuga had made the mansa through generosity. The covenant bound the tributary states not through garrisons or terror but through redistribution. Gold came in from the provinces, and gold went back out as gifts, as patronage, as the spectacular expenditure that made a mansa worth obeying. When Musa emptied his treasury on the road to Mecca, he was performing the covenant at its highest pitch. But the performance required gold, and the gold required control of the trade routes, and the trade routes required that the periphery remain loyal, and loyalty required gold. The circle had held for two centuries. It was not holding now.

At Gao, seven hundred miles east along the Niger, a different song was being composed. The Songhai people had their own memory, their own river, their own reasons. They had been tributaries of Mali in the generations when Mali's reach was long. Their dia dynasty and then their sonni dynasty had sent what was owed to the mansa because the mansa's power made sending wise. But the power had thinned with the gold, and at Gao the Niger widened into a reach of water that could carry war canoes as easily as trade vessels, and the Songhai had been building both.

Sonni Sulayman Dama sat in Gao and did not send tribute. His son, Sonni Ali, was already listening to the river.

The jelilu in Niani could not sing the Songhai's genealogy. That was not their line. The Kouyate and the Diabate carried the Keita names, the Mande names, the names the Kurukan Fuga had authorized them to carry. What was happening at Gao was outside their commission. They sang what they had always sung, and they sang it in full, and the mansa listened, and the court listened, and the compound walls held back the silence of a capital that had once been louder.

This was not a fall. The jelilu would not use the word. What was happening to the Manden Kurufaba was the covenant's own logic completing a circuit the covenant had no mechanism to reverse. The mansa gave. The tributaries received. But when the giving shrank, the receiving shrank, and the reasons to stay shrank with it. The covenant did not break. It was still being sung, still being invoked, still structuring the relationships in the Mande heartland where the Keita and the Kouyate and the Diabate and the Traore lived among each other as they had since Sundiata's time. What the empire could no longer do was extend the covenant's reach beyond the heartland. The periphery left because the periphery had always been held by generosity, and generosity had a material cost, and the gold routes were shifting and the succession wars were consuming what remained.

At Niani, the jelilu finished the genealogy and began the praise songs. The kora held its low notes. The mansa listened. The dust of the dry season lay over everything, and beyond the walls the road to Gao stretched east along the river, and the river carried sounds the jelilu in Niani could not hear yet.

## II. The Loosening

The edges went first. They went quietly, the way water recedes from a bank: not all at once, but grain by grain, until the line you remember is not the line you see.

Timbuktu, the city of scholars and sand and manuscripts stacked in private libraries where the dust settled on the leather bindings between readings, had answered to the mansa for a century. The mansa's governor sat in the city and the mansa's protection kept the trade routes open and the caravans moving. But by the 1430s the governor's authority depended on a center that could reinforce him, and the center was far upriver and growing farther. The Tuareg of the Saharan edge, the Maghsharen confederation led by their chief Akil ag Amalwal, watched and waited and then did not wait. Around 1433, they moved into Timbuktu. The mansa's governor left or was pushed out. The scholars in their courtyard houses heard the change of authority in the sounds outside their walls: different languages in the market, different names in the Friday prayers, different expectations about who owed what to whom.

The Tuareg hold on Timbuktu was loose, a chieftain's claim over a city that was too large and too cosmopolitan to be governed the way a desert camp is governed. The scholars went on teaching. The manuscript copyists went on copying. The students at the Sankore mosque went on arriving from Morocco and the middle Niger and the Hausa cities to the east, because what Timbuktu offered was not contingent on who collected the taxes. The city's intellectual life was older than any single empire's claim on it and would outlast this claim as it had outlasted others.

But the loss of Timbuktu mattered to the Manden. Not for the city's revenue alone, though that was significant. What mattered was what the loss meant within the covenant's logic. The Kurukan Fuga bound the mansa to protect what the mansa claimed. A mansa who could not hold Timbuktu could not be the mansa the covenant described. The jelilu did not stop singing the genealogy. But the genealogy now named kings whose reach did not match the reach of the kings the genealogy had named before.

Further south and west, the Atlantic coast was beginning to matter in ways the Kurukan Fuga had not anticipated. Portuguese ships appeared along the Senegambian coast in the 1440s and 1450s, probing for trade. They were looking for gold, the same gold that had always flowed north across the Sahara to the Mediterranean markets. What they offered was a new route: the sea. The Wangara goldfields of Bambuk and Bure had fed the trans-Saharan trade for centuries, and the Mali state had taxed the crossing points and the middlemen who controlled them. Now the gold had another direction it could travel. Not north across the sand to Sijilmasa and Tunis and Cairo, but west to the coast and onto ships bound for Lisbon. The shift was gradual, not sudden. The trans-Saharan routes did not collapse; they continued to carry gold and salt and slaves and manuscripts for another century and more. But the monopoly was broken. The Manden could no longer be the only gate through which Wangara gold reached the wider world.

The mansa's options narrowed. The tribute from the east had stopped. The tribute from the north had stopped. The gold routes were branching in directions the covenant had no mechanism to control. In the reception halls of Niani, where Musa's court had once received ambassadors and scholars from across the Islamic world, the ceremonies continued, the kora still played, the jelilu still sang, but the gifts the mansa could distribute were smaller and the visitors who came to receive them were fewer. In the Mande heartland, the social structures held. The horon and the nyamakalaw and the jon continued to live within the Kurukan Fuga's framework. The jeli lineages continued to carry the names. The clans intermarried and quarreled and reconciled according to the covenant's terms. But the empire, the reach that had once made the Manden Kurufaba the largest state in West Africa, was contracting toward its core.

The succession did not help. The mansalu who followed Musa and Sulayman fought each other for the throne with a regularity that consumed the court's attention and its resources. The details of these struggles are harder to see than the details of Musa's hajj. The Arab scholars who had marveled at the gold and the camels did not write much about decades of palace quarrels in a city they could not reach. What the later Tarikh chronicles record, written from Timbuktu's vantage rather than Niani's, is a dynasty turning inward, each succession contested, each contest weakening the center's ability to project power outward. The details are sparse and sometimes contradictory. What is clear is the pattern: the energy that had once flowed outward in gold and patronage and military expedition now flowed inward in factional conflict.

The Kurukan Fuga was a living covenant, spoken and reinterpreted by the jelilu in each generation. But its logic assumed a mansa strong enough to give. When no mansa could give enough, the logic that had held the periphery simply released it.

## III. Sonni Ali and the River

At Gao, the Niger ran wide and slow and brown with the silt of the inland delta, and the smell of the river was the smell of the city: mud and fish and the wet wood of canoe hulls drying in the sun. Fishing boats and trade canoes rode the current, their crews calling to each other across the water in Songhai, and the riverbank markets sold dried fish and millet and the salt that came down from the desert mines at Taghaza in slabs so heavy two men strained to carry one. Gao had been a city before Mali claimed it and would be a city after Mali's claim dissolved, because the river was not a Mande river or a Songhai river. It was the river, and whoever held its banks held the commerce that moved on its water.

Sonni Ali came to power around 1464. The Tarikh al-Sudan, written two centuries later by the Timbuktu scholar al-Sadi, describes him as a man of immense energy who moved with the current and against it, whose war canoes carried soldiers and horses and the ambition of a people who had waited long enough. The scholars of Timbuktu, who wrote that chronicle, did not like him. They called him a tyrant, a persecutor of the learned, a man who kept the old Songhai practices alongside his Islam in ways they considered impure. Their judgment is in the record. It is their judgment, written from inside their world, and it does not have to be the reader's. The Songhai had their own oral historians, the jesere, who would have told the story differently, but the jesere's accounts were not written down in the way the Timbuktu scholars' were, and what survives in the written record is the scholars' version.

What Sonni Ali built was a military state organized around the river. His war canoes were long and narrow, carved from single trunks of the riverine hardwoods, and they carried archers and lancers and sometimes horses, the animals standing wide-eyed in the hull while the paddlers drove the craft upstream against the current with a rhythmic splash that could be heard from the banks before the canoes came into view. The fleet gave him a mobility that land-based armies could not match on the Niger's floodplain. He could move soldiers upstream and downstream faster than any messenger could carry warning on foot, and the river's annual flood turned the inland delta into a labyrinth of channels and temporary lakes that only someone who knew the water could navigate. Sonni Ali knew the water. He used this advantage methodically. In 1468, he moved on Timbuktu. The war canoes came upriver from Gao, and behind them came the cavalry and the foot soldiers along the banks, and the city that had changed hands from Mali to Tuareg now changed hands again. The Maghsharen chief Akil ag Amalwal fled into the desert with his people. The scholars of Timbuktu, some of whom had accommodated the Tuareg and now feared the Songhai, also fled, some to Walata, carrying what manuscripts they could. Others stayed. Timbuktu changed hands, and the manuscripts in the private libraries did not move, and the mosque walls stood as they had stood, and the students who remained continued studying what they had been studying, because the knowledge did not care who collected the city's taxes.

Five years later, Sonni Ali turned to Djenne. The city sat on an island in the inland delta, ringed by floodwaters in the wet season when the Niger rose and turned the surrounding plain into a shallow lake that stretched to the horizon. Its walls had held against multiple armies. The great mud-brick mosque rose above the city, its surface bristling with toron timbers, and the market at its foot traded in gold and salt and cotton and the grain of the delta's rich farmland. Sonni Ali could not storm it. He surrounded it. The siege lasted, the Tarikh al-Sudan says, seven years, seven months, and seven days. The precision is literary, not chronological; the number seven carries weight in the oral and written traditions of the region. What is certain is that the siege was long and that Djenne eventually submitted, around 1473, and that Sonni Ali treated the city with respect once it fell. The Tarikh al-Sudan records that he married into Djenne's elite. He made the city part of his system rather than sacking it. The cities on the river were the empire's arteries, and you do not cut your own arteries.

Sonni Ali's domain now stretched from Timbuktu to Djenne, the two great cities of the Niger bend and the inland delta, connected by the river that was his highway and his advantage. The Songhai held the trade routes that had once fed the Manden's treasury: the salt came south from Taghaza, the gold came north from the Wangara fields, and the taxes on both now flowed to Gao. The territory was not Mali's former territory entire. The Manden heartland, far to the west, remained outside his reach. But the cities that had given Mali its prestige, its trade revenue, its scholarly reputation, were now Songhai. The shift had taken thirty years, roughly the span of a single generation. It had not required a single pitched battle between Songhai and Mali. The Manden Kurufaba had loosened its grip, and the Songhai had closed theirs.

Sonni Ali died in 1492, returning from a campaign. The circumstances of his death are unclear; the sources hint at different things. His son, Sonni Baru, took the throne. The Songhai's story was not finished, and neither was Mali's, and neither was Timbuktu's. The river kept moving.

## IV. Askia Muhammad and the New Order

Sonni Baru lasted months. He inherited his father's throne but not his father's authority, and the generals who had served Sonni Ali did not see in the son what they had followed in the father. Muhammad Ture, one of Sonni Ali's most capable commanders, challenged him at the battle of Anfao in 1493 and won. He took the title Askia, and the Sonni dynasty ended, and the Askia dynasty began, and the Songhai state became something different from what Sonni Ali had built.

Askia Muhammad was a builder of systems. Where Sonni Ali had governed through personal authority and the momentum of the river fleet, Askia Muhammad governed through structure. He divided the empire into provinces and appointed governors to hold them. He established courts of law and reorganized the military and put the taxation of trade on a system that did not depend on one man's energy to function. The Songhai Empire under the Askia dynasty ran on hierarchy where the Manden Kurufaba had run on redistribution.

He went to Mecca. In 1496, three years after seizing power, Askia Muhammad made the hajj with a caravan that crossed the same desert Musa's caravan had crossed a hundred and seventy years before. He traveled with a large entourage and substantial gold, and the comparison to Musa's hajj was inevitable and probably intentional. The Tarikh al-Sudan records that he brought three hundred thousand mithqals of gold for distribution along the route and at the holy cities. The figure may be inflated by the chronicle's conventions, but the intent was clear: this was a ruler performing legitimacy on the same stage Musa had performed it, claiming the same Islamic-imperial prestige for a new dynasty. He sought legitimacy from the centers of Islamic authority. The Abbasid shadow caliph in Cairo, maintained by the Mamluk sultans as a source of religious legitimation, gave him the title of Caliph of the Sudan. The title placed the Songhai ruler within the framework of Islamic political authority in a way the Mande mansalu, whose power rested on the Kurukan Fuga rather than on caliphal appointment, had never sought.

The scholars of Timbuktu welcomed him in ways they had not welcomed Sonni Ali. Askia Muhammad patronized the mosques, funded the scholars, consulted the jurists on matters of Islamic law. He asked the scholars of Sankore to review his administrative decisions against the principles of Maliki jurisprudence, and the scholars did, and this consultation became part of the rhythm of governance: the ruler brought the question, the scholars brought the text, and the answer emerged from the negotiation between power and learning in the courtyard where the afternoon light fell across the open manuscripts.

The Sankore mosque, which had endured the Tuareg interlude and Sonni Ali's reign, now entered another period of active patronage. The private libraries grew. New commentaries were written on texts that had been copied and debated since the Mansa's time, and original treatises on grammar, rhetoric, and the mathematical calculations required by Islamic inheritance law filled the shelves of the scholarly families. A student at Sankore in the late 15th century studied the same Maliki legal texts that students had studied under Mali's patronage, with the same rigor, in the same courtyard, with the sand still drifting into the ink as it had always done. Scholars corresponded with colleagues in Fez and Cairo and the Hejaz, and the caravans carried manuscripts north and south across the desert as they had in Musa's time, because the scholarly tradition was not Mande and not Songhai. It was the tradition, and it attached itself to whatever power would fund the ink and the paper and the teaching.

Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti was born in 1556, near the end of the Askia dynasty's effective rule. He grew up in a household where the manuscripts were part of the furniture, where the smell of old leather and gum-arabic ink was the smell of home. His family, the Aqit, had been prominent scholars in Timbuktu for generations. Their library held manuscripts that spanned all of the city's periods of patronage: texts copied under Mali, acquired under the Tuareg, preserved through Sonni Ali, expanded under the Askia. He became the most prominent scholar Timbuktu produced, writing dozens of works on jurisprudence, biography, and theology. When the Moroccan invasion came in 1591, he was among those the Moroccans arrested and transported to Marrakesh. He spent years in exile, teaching, writing, maintaining the intellectual practice that Timbuktu had cultivated across centuries and empires and changes of patron. He returned to Timbuktu eventually and died there in 1627. His life spanned the Songhai Empire's height and its destruction, and his work survived both.

Askia Muhammad himself did not survive his own dynasty's politics. He went blind in his old age, the man who had ridden to Mecca and reorganized an empire now sitting in his chambers in Gao while his sons circled. They deposed him in 1528, leading him from the palace he had built. He lived on for another decade, blind and powerless, dependent on whichever son held the throne that season, while his sons fought each other for what he had built. The pattern was familiar. It was the same pattern the Manden had lived through. Power concentrated in one man, the man aged or weakened, the sons contested the succession, and the contest consumed the energy that should have held the periphery. Ibn Khaldun would have recognized it. He had described the mechanism centuries earlier, in his own study of how dynasties rise and consume themselves, and the Songhai followed the arc he mapped as faithfully as the Manden had.

## V. The Heartland Endures

The Manden Kurufaba did not end.

The empire contracted. The tribute stopped. Timbuktu answered to Gao, and Djenne answered to Gao, and the great trade cities of the Niger bend looked east for their orders. The mansalu in Niani ruled a territory that shrank across the 15th and 16th centuries to the Mande heartland itself: the upper Niger, the country between Kangaba and Niani, the land where Sundiata had first walked and then run and then conquered. The Keita dynasty continued. The mansalu continued to be named. They were not the rulers Musa had been. Their court was local, their reach was local, their gold was local. But they were mansalu, and the covenant said what a mansa was, and the jelilu sang the genealogy that proved it.

What endured was the structure beneath the empire. The structure that had held the Manden together long before it became an empire, and that would hold the Manden together long after the empire was gone. The Kurukan Fuga had regulated more than tribute and territory. It had regulated who married whom, who practiced which craft, who owed what to whom across the categories of Mande society. The horon and the nyamakalaw and the jon did not need Timbuktu's taxes to know their obligations. The sanankuya, the joking relationships between clans that defused conflict and maintained alliance, continued because the people who practiced them continued and the jelilu who remembered which clans owed which jokes to which other clans continued. The covenant was social before it was imperial, had been social for generations before Sundiata made it the basis of a state. The empire was the covenant's largest expression, not its only one.

In Kangaba, the Diabate lineage kept the ceremony. Every seven years, the roof of the sacred house, the Kamabolon, was renewed. The ceremony brought the Mande clans together to hear the full recitation: the covenant's terms, the obligations of each clan to every other, the marriage rules, the craft designations, the genealogy of the Keita, the history of the founding. The roof was rebuilt with new thatch, and the rebuilding was the renewal of the agreement itself: the clans were still here, the covenant was still spoken, the world Sundiata had made was still being maintained. The ceremony continued through the Songhai period, through the Moroccan aftermath, through the colonial era when the French administrators who controlled the territory did not understand what was happening in the sacred house and did not need to, because the ceremony was not for them.

The manuscripts in Timbuktu survived too. They survived the change from Mali to Songhai, from Songhai to Moroccan occupation after 1591, from Moroccan occupation to the long centuries of diminished but continuous scholarly life. The families who owned the libraries passed them down. The pages yellowed and the ink faded from black to brown and the leather bindings cracked in the dry heat. But the texts were copied when they needed copying, the reed pens moving over new paper in the same careful hand that generations of training had steadied. And the libraries were hidden when they needed hiding, packed into chests and carried to safe places when the city's latest occupiers threatened what the city held. The tradition of preservation persisted because the families persisted and the families understood that the manuscripts were not possessions but obligations.

The Songhai chapter is another people's story, and it will be told in its own time. What belongs to this chapter is the Manden's story, and the Manden's story does not end with the loss of territory. It ends, if it ends at all, with the last jeli who carries the genealogy, and that jeli has not yet been born, because the current ones are still singing.

In the towns and villages of the Mande heartland, the Kouyate and the Diabate and their fellow jeli lineages still perform the Sundiata Epic. The seven-year ceremony at Kangaba still takes place. The names are still sung: Sundiata, Wali, Khalifa, Abu Bakr, Musa, Sulayman, and the mansalu who came after, all of them, the ones who held and the ones who lost, because a jeli does not choose which ancestors to remember. A jeli remembers them all.

The genealogy did not contract when the empire did. The genealogy grew. It absorbed the contraction. It absorbed the Songhai succession and the Moroccan invasion and the colonial period and the independence of modern Mali and the coups and the droughts and the occupation of Timbuktu in 2012, when the families hid the manuscripts again, and the liberation, and the years after. The jelilu carried all of it. They are carrying it now.

A kora holds its low notes in a compound in the Mande heartland. A voice climbs above the strings. The names come out in the order they have always come. The dust of the dry season lies over everything, as it lay over Niani when the last great caravans assembled and as it lay over the road to Gao when the river carried the Songhai to power and as it lies now, in this season, in this year, over the country where the Kurukan Fuga was first spoken. The covenant is still being spoken. The jelilu are still singing. They have not stopped.
