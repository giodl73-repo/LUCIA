# The First Atlantic Contact

The horses came off the ship wrong. They stumbled on the beach sand at the mouth of the Senegal River, legs stiff from weeks in a rolling hold, nostrils wide, flanks dark with sweat, and the smell of them carried on the salt wind before the longboat's keel touched bottom. The Wolof men who had waded into the shallows watched the animals being led down the ramp and said nothing. They had seen horses before. The Saharan trade brought them south from the Berber lands, and a buur's stable was a measure of his authority. But these horses had come from the ocean, and the ocean had never produced horses before.

A Portuguese captain named Lancarote de Freitas commanded the ship. He had sailed from Lagos in the Algarve with a commission to trade, and he did not know the name of the river he had entered.

The Wolof knew more.

They knew the ship had come from the north, around the cape the Berber traders called the end of the known world. They knew the men aboard were not Berbers and not Arabs and not anyone the gewel had a name for yet. They knew the horses were good. A man on the beach walked to the nearest animal and opened its mouth, checking the teeth, running a hand down the front legs, pressing the cannon bone for heat. The horse was young, four or five seasons, and had survived the crossing without the swelling in the joints that killed so many. The Wolof had been managing the southern end of a trade network for generations. They knew exactly what this horse was worth.

The buur of Walo, whose territory controlled the river mouth, sent word inland to the Burba Jolof. A new people had arrived from the sea. They had horses and they wanted to trade. The Burba Jolof sat in the shade of his receiving compound in the Jolof heartland, where the dry-season heat pressed down on the laterite soil and the baobabs stood still in the afternoon air, and he considered what the message meant. He had spent his reign managing the politics of the Saharan trade, balancing the demands of the Berber merchants from the north with the expectations of the coastal buur who wanted a greater share of the exchange. Now the ocean had produced a new partner. He sent men to see.

The gewel would remember what happened next. Njaay Sall, they would say, or others of the Sall lineage who kept the memory of the Burba Jolof's court. The Portuguese said they traded horses and cloth for slaves. The Wolof understood the exchange in their own terms. The people given in trade were jon, war captives taken in the conflicts along the confederacy's borders, men and women whose faces the buur's warriors knew, whose hands had been bound with rope made from baobab fiber. The exchange of jon for horses was a transaction the Wolof had been conducting with Saharan merchants long before any ship appeared on the horizon. What was new was not the nature of the trade. What was new was the direction it faced.

For generations, the wealth of the Jolof Confederacy had moved on a north-south axis: captives and gold going north across the Sahara, salt and horses and copper coming south. Now a second axis opened, running from the inland capital to the Atlantic beaches where the Portuguese ships anchored. The coastal states, Cayor and Walo and Baol, found themselves at the intersection. A buur in Cayor who could trade directly with the Portuguese for horses no longer needed the Burba Jolof to mediate with the Saharan networks for the animals that made a ruler's cavalry, and a ruler's cavalry was what made a ruler.

The Burba Jolof saw this. Within a generation of the first Portuguese ships, he had sent Wolof ambassadors to Lisbon, men who crossed the ocean in the holds of Portuguese vessels and stood in stone halls colder than any room they had known, their robes bright against the grey Portuguese winter. They negotiated terms of trade and alliance that the Burba Jolof understood as recognition of what he already was: the sovereign head of a confederacy that governed its own territory and traded with whom it chose. The Portuguese chronicler Zurara recorded the encounter with the bafflement of a man who had expected subjects and found a court that expected to be received.

The horses, meanwhile, were led up from the beach. The surf broke behind them. They would find their legs, be stabled and fed and trained and ridden by the buur's ceddo warriors in the service of a political order that had existed for a century before the Portuguese arrived and would persist for a century after. But the axis had shifted. The ocean was open. And the calculation the Wolof nobleman had made on the beach that morning, his hand still on the horse's leg, weighing the worth of the animal against the cost of what he gave in exchange, would be made again and again, by many men, across the next hundred years, each time with the terms a little different, each time with the balance a little harder to hold.
