# Plenitudo Potestatis — The Fullness of Power

## Opening

In the ninth year of his pontificate, in the great hall of the Lateran, Lotario de Segni counted what he had made.

Six hundred bishops. Eight hundred abbots and priors. Delegates from every realm of Latin Christendom — from the northern seas where the conversion of the Norse was still recent, from the burning Iberian borderlands where the reconquest still bled, from the crusader cities of the Levant where Latin settlers had learned to sweat in unfamiliar light. The hall held them all and pressed them together, a thousand bodies in November, the heat of massed candles warming the stone, the Latin of the opening prayers rising from a dozen different accents — Flemish, Sicilian, German, the clipped vowels of the English delegates who had traveled for three weeks to be there. They had all come to Rome in the autumn of 1215, in the year called the forty-second of the general chapter of the Apostles, in the one thousand two hundred and fifteenth year from the birth of Christ. When Innocent rose to speak, the hall fell silent. They had come because Innocent III summoned them. That was sufficient.

He had been pope since he was thirty-seven years old. He was fifty-five now, and if the weight of what he held had aged him, the records do not say. What the records say is this: he opened the council with a sermon from Luke. "I have earnestly desired," Christ said at the Last Supper, "to eat this Passover with you before I suffer." Innocent applied the text to himself. He had earnestly desired this council. He had worked for years toward it. And like the Passover that preceded the Passion, this gathering preceded something he could not yet name.

The seventy-one canons he would promulgate before the council closed would shape the life of every Christian soul in the Latin West for the next three centuries. They defined what transubstantiation meant: that the bread and wine of the Mass became, in substance though not in appearance, the body and blood of Christ. They required every Christian who had reached the age of reason to confess their sins to their parish priest at least once a year and receive communion at Easter. They required Jews and Muslims living in Christian lands to wear distinctive clothing. They launched the Fifth Crusade toward Egypt. They created the formal legal category of the heretic and established the procedure by which one was to be identified and handed to the secular arm for punishment. They required cathedral chapters to support schools for educating clergy. They regulated the ordination of priests, the conduct of clergy in taverns, the wearing of red and green vestments, the keeping of concubines.

No council before it had covered so much. No council after it, until the long deliberations at Trent three centuries later, would cover more.

Innocent had not arrived at this moment by accident. He had arrived at it by argument. The argument was ancient, but he had sharpened it to a point that no predecessor had managed. The Bishop of Rome held the plenitudo potestatis — the fullness of power. Not some of the power. Not power within a certain domain. The fullness of it. Secular kings held their authority from God, but they held it through the mediation of the Church, and the Church's highest officer was the pope, and what the pope gave in theory he could withdraw in practice. Emperors were crowned by popes. Kings were anointed by bishops who answered to Rome. The whole architecture of Christian governance rested on this premise, and Innocent had spent his pontificate — fifteen years by the time the council met — building walls and buttresses wherever the structure showed a crack.

He had excommunicated King John of England and placed the entire kingdom of England under interdict, which meant no Mass, no baptism, no burial rite, until John submitted. John submitted. He had done the same to Philip II of France over the matter of a marriage Philip wished to set aside. Philip, after years of resistance, had also submitted. He had sent legates to the Baltic to bring the pagan Livonians and Estonians into the fold. He had recognized Francis of Assisi's order of poor friars and confirmed Dominic of Osma's order of preachers. He had launched a crusade against the Cathars of southern France, the first crusade ever directed against fellow Christians on European soil.

He had also watched the Fourth Crusade sail to Constantinople and sack it. He had condemned the sack. The sack had happened anyway.

This was the texture of what he held: an authority that was real and formal and recognized across the Latin world, an authority that kings bent the knee to, that altered the map of souls; and an authority that, at the moment of its greatest exercise, could not prevent a Christian army from burning a Christian city.

Standing before six hundred bishops, Innocent may have known this. The council's canons, carved in Latin, would outlast the moment. The moment, the fullness of it, would not outlast him.

He died the following year, in Perugia, in July, on his way to negotiate a peace between Genoa and Pisa. The council's work continued without him. As it was designed to do.
