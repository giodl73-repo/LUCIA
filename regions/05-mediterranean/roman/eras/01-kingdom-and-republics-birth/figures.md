# Figures — The Kingdom and the Republic's Birth (Era 1)

## Lucius Junius Brutus (tradition figure)

First consul of the Roman Republic. In the tradition, the man who led the expulsion of Tarquinius Superbus and established the oath against kingship. Died in single combat with the exiled king's son.

**What we know:** Brutus appears in Livy (Ab Urbe Condita, Books 1-2), Dionysius of Halicarnassus, and Plutarch. All sources are centuries later than the events described. The archaeological record confirms a transition from monarchical to non-monarchical government in Roma in the late sixth century BCE, but cannot confirm the individual. The Capitoline Brutus, a bronze portrait bust sometimes identified with him, dates from the 4th-3rd century BCE and may be unrelated.

**What we cannot know:** Whether Brutus existed as an individual. The story as Livy tells it is a moral exemplum: the founder who sacrifices everything (including his own sons, whom he executes for conspiring with the Tarquins) for the res publica. The chapter treats Brutus as the tradition intended: not as a biography but as the argument the Romani made about what their Republic required.

**Sources:** Livy, *Ab Urbe Condita* 1.56-2.7; Dionysius of Halicarnassus, *Roman Antiquities*; Cornell, *The Beginnings of Rome* (1995); Wiseman, *The Myths of Rome* (2004).

## Tarquinius Superbus (tradition figure)

Last king of Roma. In the tradition, the tyrant whose abuses provoked the Republic's founding.

**What we know:** Archaeological evidence suggests the Tarquin dynasty was real and likely had Etruscan connections. The tradition's account of Superbus's tyranny (execution of senators, forced labor, refusal to consult the Senate) follows the pattern of Greek tyranny narratives and may owe something to Greek literary models. The fall of the monarchy in Roma is broadly contemporary with similar transitions in Greek city-states.

**What we cannot know:** The specific acts attributed to him. The story of Lucretia and Sextus Tarquinius follows narrative patterns found in Greek sources and may be a literary construction. The chapter presents both the story and its literary character honestly.

**Sources:** Livy 1.49-60; Cornell (1995); Ogilvie, *A Commentary on Livy Books 1-5* (1965).

## Lucretia (tradition figure)

Roman noblewoman. In the tradition, her violation by Sextus Tarquinius and subsequent suicide were the immediate cause of the Republic's founding.

**What we know:** Lucretia's story is in Livy, Dionysius, and Ovid's Fasti. The narrative has the structure of a founding myth: the violation of the private sphere by the public authority (the king's son) precipitates the revolution. The story functions as a moral argument about honor, the body politic, and the justification for revolution.

**What we cannot know:** Historicity. The story may encode a real political crisis or may be entirely literary. The chapter presents it as the tradition presented it: the argument for the oath.

**Sources:** Livy 1.57-59; Ovid, *Fasti* 2.685-852; Joshel, *The Body Female and the Body Politic: Livy's Lucretia and Verginia* (1992).

## Servius Tullius (tradition figure)

Sixth king of Roma. In the tradition, the reformer who created the census, organized the citizen body into property classes, and built the Servian Wall.

**What we know:** The Servian reforms are the most structurally important legacy of the regal period. The census system they describe (property-based military and political organization) is consistent with what we know of the Republic's later functioning. Archaeological evidence suggests the Servian Wall dates from the early fourth century BCE (after the Gallic sack), not the sixth century, meaning the tradition has attributed a later construction to an earlier king.

**What we cannot know:** Whether Servius was a single individual or an institutional memory crystallized into a name. The census reform may have been a gradual process attributed retrospectively to a founder figure.

**Sources:** Livy 1.42-48; Cornell (1995); Richard, *Les origines de la plebe romaine* (1978).

## The Plebeian Tribune (institutional figure)

The tribunate is presented in the chapter as an institution rather than through a single named tribune. The earliest tribunes' names are preserved in the fasti but their individual actions cannot be reliably reconstructed.

**Honest disclosure:** The chapter describes the tribunate's creation and function through the collective experience of the institution rather than through any individual tribune. This is appropriate for this era: the institution matters more than any individual in the 5th-century context, and the sources do not support detailed characterization of the earliest tribunes.

**Sources:** Livy 2.33 (first secession); Cornell (1995); Raaflaub, ed., *Social Struggles in Archaic Rome* (2005).

## A Note on Sources

The regal period and early Republic present a fundamental source problem. Our main narrative sources (Livy, Dionysius of Halicarnassus) wrote centuries after the events they describe, working from traditions that had been shaped and reshaped by the political needs of intervening generations. The archaeological record (the Regia, the Lapis Niger, the earliest Forum paving, temple foundations) confirms broad outlines but cannot verify specific narratives.

The chapter handles this by treating the tradition as what the Romani made of their own history, not as a documentary record. The stories are real in the sense that they shaped Roman identity and political behavior. Whether they are real in the sense that they happened as described is a different question, and the chapter does not pretend to answer it.
