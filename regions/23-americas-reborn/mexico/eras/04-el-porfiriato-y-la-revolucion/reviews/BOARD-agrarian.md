# BOARD Review — Agrarian and Indigenous Studies
## Mexico Era 4 — El Porfiriato y La Revolución

**Reviewer profile:** Specialist in Mexican agrarian history, indigenous land rights, and Nahua communities of Morelos; expertise in the ejido system's legal history, the milpa polyculture, and the relationship between the Revolution's promises and their implementation.

---

## Historical Accuracy Assessment

**The milpa polyculture:** The characterization of the milpa — maize, beans, squash in intercropped rows — is accurate. The description as a "Nahua polyculture that had fed families here for a thousand years" is supported; the Three Sisters intercropping system (milpa) is documented as pre-Columbian and its specific association with Nahua agricultural practice in Morelos is well-established. The ecological function (soil fertility maintenance, rainfall management, nutritional complementarity) is correctly implied without being over-explained.

**The Ley de Baldíos and communal tenure:** The characterization is accurate and technically correct. The legal mechanism — communal tenure rendered invisible by the law's requirement for individual cadastral registration — is the central argument of Warman's *We Come to Object* and is supported by Womack's work on Morelos land records. The phrase "reclassified community memory as private property" is a precise and apt characterization of what the law did epistemologically.

**The 1607 Anenecuilco land grants:** These are well-documented in the AGN (Archivo General de la Nación). The colonial legal system did record indigenous community land rights under the *fundo legal* and *ejido* provisions of colonial law, which the Ley de Baldíos then effectively overrode. The chapter correctly notes that the colonial system "at least recorded the indigenous communities' rights in its own legal forms" — this is the right historical characterization.

**Article 27 implementation gap:** Correct and important. Actual land redistribution under Article 27 within the era covered by this chapter (to 1920) was minimal. The systematic implementation came under Lázaro Cárdenas (1934–1940), who redistributed approximately 18 million hectares. The chapter correctly states this without projecting Cárdenas's presidency into the 1920 close. The promise of 1917 and the delivery of the 1930s are correctly distinguished.

**The ejido's legal restoration vs. physical restoration:** The chapter makes this distinction clearly: "the ejido was restored as a legal category" and "some land redistribution began under Obregón and accelerated under Calles in the 1920s, but the systematic implementation of Article 27 waited for Lázaro Cárdenas in 1934." This is the correct historical account.

## One Refinement for Consideration

The chapter's characterization of the Yucatán Maya workers as "bound by debt peonage they could never repay" is accurate as a general description of the *enganche* and *tienda de raya* systems. A small addition — noting that the 1915 Constitutionalist decree from General Salvador Alvarado in Yucatán specifically abolished debt peonage there — would demonstrate that the Revolution did address this specific system at the regional level, even while leaving the broader land question unresolved. This is a complexity the chapter could acknowledge without diminishing its central argument.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

The chapter correctly situates the Zapatista movement within the Nahua community's own political tradition rather than imposing external political theory. The village council as the authoritative institution, the title documents as the legal instrument, and the ejido as the social technology being defended — these framings come from the Morelos communities' own understanding of what they were fighting for.

The figures.md treatment of the village council of Anenecuilco as a collective protagonist with institutional continuity before and after Zapata is historically sound and culturally appropriate. The council did precede Zapata; he was elected to it; it continued after his assassination under different leadership.

The corrido as a form of community memory distinct from the state-nationalist appropriation (murals, official iconography) is correctly handled. The distinction between the corrido tradition's Zapata and Rivera's Zapata is historically real and important.

**Board verdict: PASS — one optional refinement noted (Alvarado's Yucatán decree), not mandatory for the chapter's argument**
