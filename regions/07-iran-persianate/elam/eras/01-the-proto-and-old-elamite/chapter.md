# Era 1: The Proto-Elamite and Old Elamite Periods
## c. 3200–1500 BCE

---

The scribe pressed the stylus into the clay while it was still wet. That was the first requirement: wet enough to hold the mark, dry enough not to smear it. The tablet in his left hand was palm-sized, the clay pale and river-damp, pulled from the channel that fed the temple granary. He had been doing this since before his beard came in. His teacher had done it before that. His teacher's teacher had done it before that, back and back through generations in which the marks had multiplied and the system had deepened and what had begun as tokens pressed in soft clay had become something that no single person had designed, something that had grown the way cities grow — one necessity at a time, one sign at a time, until the structure was so large that no one could see all of it at once.

He made the marks. The grain was counted. The god's portion set aside.

Susa in the thirty-first century before the common era was not yet the city it would become — not yet the layered thing of brick and plaster and memory that would outlast a dozen empires — but it was already old. The mound that the city stood on was ancient before the scribe's grandfather was born. People had lived here, on this particular rise above the Khuzestan plain, for so long that the accumulation of their living had raised the ground itself: packed clay floors, collapsed walls, the ash of ten thousand cooking fires pressed down into the earth until the earth was no longer just earth but human time made solid. You built on what came before. That was not a philosophy. It was how construction worked.

The plain stretched west and south, flat and rich, river-watered, given over to barley as far as the eye could follow. To the east and north, visible on clear mornings when the air had not yet thickened with heat, the Zagros lifted. The mountains were not distant scenery. They were the other half of the world. Down from them came the highland peoples with their herds and their own ways of reckoning the world; down from them came the passes that led to the plateau, to Anshan, to the kingdom's other pole. Up into them went the caravans carrying the surplus grain the plain produced, the copper and lapis that came in along the old routes from Elam's eastern connections, the administrative tablets that kept track of what had gone where and how much. The scribe at his clay was part of this flow. His work was the record of it.

He did not think of himself as inventing anything. The signs he pressed into clay had come from his teachers, and his teachers from theirs, and back further than anyone could verify. But signs had proliferated. New goods required new signs. New transactions created new conventions. The counting system had acquired symbols for large quantities because the granary needed them; the granary needed them because the surplus was growing; the surplus was growing because the canal system had extended the reach of irrigation into lands that had been dry before. Necessity accumulated. The scribe recorded the necessity. In doing so he made the necessity legible to people who had not been present, to administrators in other buildings, to future accountants who would open the archive and read what he had pressed into clay.

No one has yet read these tablets.

The proto-Elamite script — the system the scribe was using, in which he had been trained, in which Susa's scribal tradition was keeping the records of a complex redistributive economy operating across hundreds of kilometers of plateau and plain — has not been deciphered. It was in active use from approximately 3200 to 2600 BCE, after which it falls from the administrative record, and left behind more than five thousand tablets from dozens of sites stretching from Susa in the west to the edges of what is now Afghanistan in the east. It is the widest geographically distributed writing system of its era — wider, in its day, than the early Sumerian script that was simultaneously taking shape in the southern Mesopotamian alluvial plain. And the words in it are still silent.

What the tablets hold, even in silence: columns of signs pressed in consistent order, commodities and their quantities arranged in the logic of a trained hand. One sign for grain counted by the unit; a different numerical base for grain measured by volume. Two systems for two kinds of knowing, running simultaneously on the same tablet. Signs that repeat across hundreds of kilometers, pressed by hands that had never met but had trained in the same conventions — which means the conventions were taught, which means there were teachers, which means there were places where teaching happened, which means a class of people whose function was the maintenance of written knowledge had emerged here on the plain and on the plateau before most of the world had decided that marks could outlast memory.

What it meant to them to press these marks: this the tablets do not say, or say in a language we have not yet learned to hear.

What it means that they pressed them at all: that on the Iranian plateau, in the foothills of the Zagros and on the alluvial plain below, a civilization had organized itself around the written word before most of the world knew writing was possible. That the lowland city and the highland kingdom were already, in 3100 BCE, connected by a bureaucratic system sophisticated enough to require a uniform script across vast distances. That the Mesopotamian world to the west, where Sumerian scribes were also pressing clay in this same era, was not the only place on earth where human beings had discovered that a mark could outlast a memory.

The scribe's tablet dried. The grain count was fixed in it.

Somewhere in the archive, in the dark and dry of a storeroom built to hold just this, it waited.

It is still waiting.

## The God Whose Grain It Was

The grain was not the scribe's grain, not the king's grain, not the palace's grain. The grain was Inshushinak's grain.

Inshushinak was the lord of Susa — the city's tutelary god, whose name means "lord of Susa" in a language that is not Sumerian and not Akkadian but something older, something that belongs to the lowland world of the Khuzestan plain before the Mesopotamian civilizations named it from the outside. He was the judge of the dead. What a person carried into his presence at the end of life was what a person had been in it — the measure of their works, the weight of their deeds, assessed by a deity who had watched the city's accumulation for longer than any living person could remember. The grain that the temple managed in his name was not surplus to be distributed at the administration's discretion. It was the god's patrimony. The scribes who counted it were the god's clerks. The sukkalmah who oversaw the entire system was the god's representative to the political world.

This matters for understanding everything else. The Elamite redistribution economy — the system by which grain moved from the Khuzestan plain upward into the Zagros and onto the plateau, and metals and specialized goods moved back down, and everyone received what the network could deliver — was not an economic system that happened to have religious decorations. It was a theological system that had found an economic expression. The temple was the center because the god was the center. The surplus was managed through the temple because the surplus belonged to the god. The scribes trained in the sign system because the god's records required it.

Napirisha was the second great god — the great god of the highland, whose name means "great god" in the Elamite language itself. His domain was the upper country, the Fars plateau, the stone and altitude of Anshan. Where Inshushinak was the judge of the past, Napirisha was the power of the present: the rivers fed by his mountains, the pasturelands sustained by his rain, the ores the highland soils contained. The ziggurat at Chogha Zanbil, built later — in the next era, by Untash-Napirisha — would dedicate its highest point to him alongside Inshushinak, the highland god and the lowland god united in the architecture of a single monument.

Together they organized the Elamite world. Inshushinak held the plain; Napirisha held the mountain. Between them, the kingdom was the distance from one to the other.

The scribal system was the nervous system of this world. It ran on the same routes as the goods it recorded. A tablet pressed at Susa described what had been sent; a tablet at the receiving station described what had arrived; the discrepancy between the two tablets, if any, was the record of loss or theft or miscounting that required adjudication. The sign for a particular commodity was the same at both ends of the route because the scribes at both ends had trained in the same tradition. This uniformity was not imposed from a central authority issuing standardized tablets; it emerged from a shared scribal education that the trading network had propagated along its own channels. The writing traveled with the goods because the writing was about the goods because the writing could only be read by people who had been trained where the goods were produced.

The writing was not alone. Alongside the tablets traveled cylinder seals — small stone cylinders carved with scenes: animals in herding postures, figured in the imagery of the redistribution economy itself. A merchant pressed his seal into the wet clay of a tablet to authenticate it; the impression left behind was his visual signature, the image of his identity in the administrative record. The seals carried the same imagery from Susa to the farthest sites of the proto-Elamite network. Gazelles and cattle and deer organized in careful composition. The same scenes at both ends of the route, pressed in the same tradition by hands that had learned the same visual vocabulary. The scribal system and the glyptic tradition were the same intelligence expressed in two media: one linguistic, one visual, both traveling the same roads, both held in the same archive.

Anshan, on the plateau, was a different world from Susa by every physical measure. The air thinned as the passes climbed into the Zagros. The smells changed: the flat river-mud smell of the Khuzestan plain gave way to the resin of highland trees, the different quality of stone, the cold of elevation that the plain never knew. The light at altitude came harder, more direct, casting shadows that moved differently than the long low shadows of the plain. Anshan's buildings were stone where Susa's were brick. The food was different; the animals were different; the weather was different. A scribe trained in Susa who traveled the passes to deliver tablets would arrive in a physical world that confirmed what the tablets told: that the two poles of the kingdom were genuinely different, each capable of producing what the other could not, each dependent on the other's surplus for its own sufficiency.

The dual kingdom was this dependency made political. The title "king of Anshan and Susa" named both poles because neither pole alone was the kingdom. It was an unusual formula in a world where royal titles usually named a single city and then claimed hegemony over everything else. The Elamite formula was different: it named two cities and implied that the king stood between them, mediating the exchange that made both viable. The king was not the owner of the system. The king was the system's hinge.

## Puzur-Inshushinak and the Moment of Writing

The Akkadian Empire of Sargon of Akkad (c. 2334–2279 BCE) had reached east to the Iranian plateau and brought Elam into its orbit. The Elamites traded with Akkad, sent diplomatic correspondence in Akkadian, allowed Akkadian governors in some regions. The Mesopotamian administrative language became, for a time, the language of the Elamite court's external face. What the internal language of the Elamite administration was during this period — whether proto-Elamite survived as a functional script or had already given way to oral accounting — is not fully clear. The tablets thin. The record goes quiet.

Then the Akkadian Empire collapsed, somewhere between 2154 and 2100 BCE, under pressure that included drought, internal fragmentation, and the pressure of the Gutian peoples from the Zagros highlands. The city-states of Mesopotamia broke free of Akkadian control. In Elam, a governor named Puzur-Inshushinak saw the change and acted on it.

He had been the governor of Susa under Akkadian authority. Now, with Akkad weakening and the Mesopotamian world reorganizing, he declared himself king. Not king of Susa. King of Elam — the old name, the full kingdom, both poles at once. His royal inscriptions are the first we can read that are genuinely royal and genuinely Elamite: not an Akkadian king recording his Elamite campaigns, not a Susa governor recording his local activities in Akkadian, but an Elamite king writing about himself in an Elamite script.

That script was Linear Elamite. Not proto-Elamite — that system had already been in use for centuries and was apparently no longer adequate to the specific function Puzur-Inshushinak wanted. Proto-Elamite was an accounting script: it recorded quantities and commodities. What Puzur-Inshushinak wanted was a prestige script: one capable of recording royal deeds, divine dedications, the language of a king who had decided that his kingdom deserved its own written monument to itself. Linear Elamite was that script. It is also undeciphered. The royal proclamation Puzur-Inshushinak pressed into stone — the monument of his independence, his identity, his break from the Akkadian world — stands now in the Louvre, and the words on it remain silent.

But the act of pressing them was not silent. Puzur-Inshushinak's decision to write in Linear Elamite rather than Akkadian was a statement about whose language the kingdom spoke, whose script the king's deeds would be preserved in, whose scribal tradition was worth perpetuating. He was not inventing a script from nothing — Linear Elamite had antecedents. He was choosing it. In choosing it, he chose Elamite over Akkadian, chose the plateau's own traditions over the plain's borrowed conventions, chose to declare that Haltamti — the land of god and power — wrote itself in its own marks.

The archive at Susa grew. Puzur-Inshushinak built and dedicated. His inscriptions invoked Inshushinak, whose name he carried in his own — Puzur-Inshushinak means "protected by Inshushinak," the god's name embedded in the king's name as a daily reminder of who the ultimate authority was. He expanded the temple precinct. He organized campaigns against the city-states of the Mesopotamian plain, which were reorganizing quickly after Akkad's fall and would soon, under Ur-Nammu of Ur and his successors, reconstitute themselves into the Third Dynasty of Ur — the last Sumerian empire.

This reconstitution was happening fast. Faster than Puzur-Inshushinak's independent Elamite kingdom could accommodate. By the time the Third Dynasty of Ur consolidated its power under Shulgi (c. 2094–2047 BCE), Elam was again within the Mesopotamian empire's orbit — sending tribute, receiving Ur's administrative expectations, integrated once more into the larger system.

The Linear Elamite inscriptions fell quiet. The Akkadian correspondence resumed. But the memory of Puzur-Inshushinak's independent kingdom remained. The scribes who had trained in the Linear Elamite tradition did not disappear; they became the administrators of the next system, carrying a capacity the new rulers had not created and could not easily replace.

## Kindattu and the Lament

The Third Dynasty of Ur lasted from approximately 2112 to 2004 BCE. Its last years were years of pressure: the Amorites pressing from the west, trade routes disrupting, the administrative system that Ur-Nammu and Shulgi had built straining under the weight of its own extent. In those last years, the Elamites came.

They came under a ruler named Kindattu. The Sumerian sources name him in the context of the catastrophe that followed his army. The Lament for the Destruction of Ur is one of the oldest surviving works of sustained literature in the world. It was composed to be performed — not read, but sung, in the empty streets and ruined temple precincts of a city that the Elamites and their Amorite allies had sacked, dismantled, and desecrated.

The text names the gods who had abandoned the city, each divine desertion a separate stanza of grief. "In Ur no one was spared," it says. "The lord turned away from his city." The lamentation was not merely emotional — it was theological. A city destroyed was a city whose god had left it. The god's departure caused the destruction, or the destruction proved the god had left. The two were inseparable in Sumerian understanding. The lament was the city speaking its own abandonment into a form that could outlast the abandonment, that could be remembered and sung again when the rebuilding came, that could ask the gods who had left to return.

Kindattu's army had no Sumerian lament to leave behind. What Elam understood about its own campaign against Ur — why the attack came, what the victory meant, how the destruction was understood from inside the Elamite world — we do not have. The Elamite sources from this period are thin: a few references, a ruler's name, the tablets that recorded the redistribution system continuing its operations through and after the campaign. The grain was still counted. The god's portion was still set aside. The administrative system that the scribe had served persisted through the collapse it had helped to cause.

What the destruction of Ur gave to Elam was this: the removal of the dominant power in the Mesopotamian system, which had for decades been demanding tribute and imposing its administrative conventions on the Elamite court. With Ur gone, the redistribution network that the Elamite kingdom managed could reorganize around Elamite centers rather than Mesopotamian ones. The trade routes that Ur had taxed could be rerouted. The surplus that had been flowing toward Ur could flow elsewhere.

This was not an act of destruction for its own sake. The dual kingdom's engine — the highland-lowland exchange, the redistribution of surplus across the Zagros system — had an interest in the Mesopotamian plain that was commercial and structural, not merely military. Elam needed access to the plain's agricultural surplus, its commercial routes, its downstream connection to the Gulf trade. Ur's dominance had been extractive; its removal was the engine asserting itself against a constraint.

The Lament for the Destruction of Ur was still being recited in Mesopotamia generations later. The city was rebuilt; the lament remembered what had been lost in the rebuilding. Kindattu's name appears in the text as the agent of destruction, embedded in a literature that was never Elamite and never intended to honor him. He has no monument in his own language. His kingdom's understanding of what he did is somewhere in the tablets that cannot be read.

The victim's voice is preserved. The conqueror's is silent.

## The Sukkalmah and the System of Mediation

In the centuries after the destruction of Ur — after the Isin-Larsa period in Mesopotamia, when the city-states competed to fill the vacuum Ur's fall had left — the Elamite kingdom reconstituted itself around a new institutional form. The sukkalmah dynasty.

The title *sukkalmah* means "great regent" or "great messenger" — a title with Mesopotamian roots, adopted and elaborated into something distinctly Elamite. The full structure of the sukkalmah system was tripartite: at the top, the sukkalmah of Elam and Simashki, the supreme authority whose domain was the entire kingdom; below him, the sukkal of Elam and Simashki, his designated heir and second authority; and below that, the sukkal of Susa, who governed the lowland capital directly. The three titles were held simultaneously by three different members of the royal family. Succession moved not from father to son but across the structure: the sukkal of Susa could become sukkal of Elam and Simashki; the sukkal of Elam and Simashki could become sukkalmah. The system was designed so that the highest authority always came from someone who had served in the lower offices — who knew both the highland and the lowland, who had administered Susa before claiming sovereignty over the entire kingdom.

This was not an arrangement that happened by accident. The dual-kingdom's structural logic — neither pole complete without the other, both poles requiring mediation — produced the institutional form that could manage the mediation. The sukkalmah stood between Anshan and Susa not as their master but as their guarantor. His authority derived from his capacity to maintain the exchange. When the exchange functioned, the sukkalmah's legitimacy was self-evident. When it failed, the legitimacy failed with it.

Atta-hushu, sukkalmah in the early nineteenth century BCE, signed a treaty with the city of Ur — a Ur that had reconstituted itself as a city-state after the empire's collapse, still trading, still present on the plain, still worth the formal diplomatic engagement. The treaty was written in Akkadian; the Elamite position in it was that of an equal, not a subject. This was the Old Elamite diplomatic world in operation: the sukkalmah's court wrote in the world's diplomatic language for external correspondence, maintained Elamite administrative conventions internally, and presented Haltamti as a kingdom that engaged with Mesopotamia on its own terms rather than being absorbed by it.

Atta-hushu's archives at Susa would have held hundreds of tablets. The grain counts. The metal inventories. The correspondence with Anshan. The treaty text with Ur. The temple accounts of what Inshushinak was owed and what had been delivered. The record of a kingdom that had organized itself around the written word and was continuing to do so, in the early second millennium BCE, with the same systematic density it had displayed in the thirty-first century when the proto-Elamite system was new.

The scribal tradition that the anonymous scribe of 3100 BCE had served was still in operation, in modified form, a thousand years later. The script had changed — proto-Elamite was gone; Linear Elamite had been used and fallen quiet; cuneiform Elamite, borrowed and adapted from the Akkadian tradition, now served the administration's textual needs. But the function was the same: to record the exchange, to hold the balance, to make legible what had moved from one pole to the other. The archive grew. The kingdom it documented persisted.

What the sukkalmah could not do was hold the system against the pressure of a reorganized Mesopotamian world. The Kassite dynasty, which took control of Babylon around 1595 BCE following a Hittite raid that disrupted the earlier Amorite kingdoms, brought a new stability to the Mesopotamian plain. The Kassites were not interested in the same aggressive extraction that earlier Mesopotamian powers had practiced; they were interested in long-term commercial integration. A unified, commercially stable Babylonia could access highland goods through routes that bypassed the Elamite redistribution network. The engine that had driven Elamite power — control of the exchange between the plain and the plateau — found a competitor that did not need to be defeated militarily because it was being routed around.

The sukkalmah records thin. The royal inscriptions become sparse. The tripartite succession system that had maintained the dual kingdom's mediation for three centuries becomes harder to trace in the texts. What had been the engine of Elamite political coherence — the managed exchange between the highland and the lowland, administered through the scribal tradition that the god's temple had generated — encountered a condition its design had not anticipated: not an external military challenge it could defeat, but an external commercial reorganization it could not control.

The sukkalmah period ends not with a battle but with a fading. The records stop. The titles persist for a time and then stop. Around 1500 BCE, a new dynasty — the Igihalkids — emerges at the head of what we call the Middle Elamite kingdom, with a different organizational logic and a renewed monumentalism that will build Chogha Zanbil and carry Elamite culture to its highest material expression. But that is the next era.

## The Archive That Endured All of Them

The mound at Susa accumulated. Below the Igihalkid building was the sukkalmah building. Below the sukkalmah building was the layer that Puzur-Inshushinak had worked. Below that was the layer of the Akkadian period, when the governor of Susa had been subordinate to a distant empire. Below that were the layers of the proto-Elamite administrators, counting the grain of a god in a script no one has read since the last trained scribe pressed his final tablet and the tradition went quiet.

The mound holds all of it. Compressed, layered, solid. Human time made into ground that later buildings stand on.

The Sumerian empire of Ur III is gone. The Akkadian empire that preceded it is gone. The city-states that Ur III absorbed are gone, reconstituted, gone again. The Amorite kingdoms that followed Ur III's collapse are gone. The Kassites who came after them will be gone. The Assyrian empire that will sack this city in the seventh century BCE will be gone. The Achaemenid empire that will make Susa one of its four capitals will be gone. The Macedonian empire of Alexander that will take the city from the Achaemenids will be gone. All of them. Every power that controlled the Khuzestan plain, from the thirty-first century BCE to the present, has ended or transformed beyond recognition.

Haltamti endured all of them. Not as a single unbroken state — the sukkalmah dynasty fell, the Middle Elamite kingdom would eventually also fall, the Neo-Elamite kingdom that followed would be sacked by Assyria — but as a continuous civilization on a continuous mound in a continuous landscape, maintaining the highland-lowland exchange, the temple economy, the scribal tradition in its various forms, the name in their own language: the land of god and power.

The archive at Susa that held the proto-Elamite tablets — in the dark and dry of its storeroom, in the clay of a script that has not yet given up its words — was built on a mound that was already ancient when the first tablet was pressed. It was buried by later buildings when the city expanded. The tablets are still there, in the clay, waiting.

The scribe did not know his marks would last. He knew only that the clay had to be wet enough to hold the mark and dry enough not to smear it. He pressed the stylus. The grain was counted. The god's portion was set aside.

It was enough.
