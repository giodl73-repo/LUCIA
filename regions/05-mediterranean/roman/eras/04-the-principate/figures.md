# Figures — The Principate (Era 4)

## Augustus (63 BCE-14 CE)

Born Gaius Octavius. Adopted by Caesar as Gaius Julius Caesar Octavianus. Titled Augustus by the Senate in 27 BCE. First princeps. Architect of the settlement that ended the Republic and created the principate.

**What we know:** Augustus is one of the most thoroughly documented rulers of the ancient world. His own Res Gestae survives in multiple copies. Suetonius, Cassius Dio, Tacitus, and Velleius Paterculus provide extensive accounts. His building program is archaeologically attested. The Forum Augustum's layout and decorative program are known from excavation and literary sources.

**What we cannot know:** Whether Augustus genuinely believed the Republic was restored or understood the settlement as a new form of monarchy. The chapter presents the fiction as structurally necessary without psychologizing the man who built it.

**Sources:** Augustus, *Res Gestae*; Suetonius, *Divus Augustus*; Tacitus, *Annales* 1; Cassius Dio 51-56; Galinsky, *Augustan Culture* (1996); Eck, *The Age of Augustus* (2003).

## Virgil (Publius Vergilius Maro) (70-19 BCE)

Poet. Author of the Aeneid, the Eclogues, and the Georgics. The Aeneid is the principate's founding poem, structuring the Roman story from Troy to Augustus through the lens of pietas.

**What we know:** Virgil's works survive complete. His biography is known from Suetonius/Donatus and from the poems themselves. The Aeneid was commissioned (or at least encouraged) by Augustus and Maecenas. Its central theme is pietas: Aeneas carrying his father from Troy is the image of obligation that structures the poem and, through it, the principate's self-understanding.

**Sources:** Virgil, *Aeneid*; Suetonius/Donatus, *Vita Vergilii*; Hardie, *Virgil's Aeneid: Cosmos and Imperium* (1986).

## Marcus Aurelius (121-180 CE)

Emperor 161-180 CE. Stoic philosopher. Author of the Meditations. Last of the Five Good Emperors.

**What we know:** The Meditations survive in Greek. His reign is documented in Cassius Dio and the Historia Augusta (the latter with reliability issues). His Danubian campaigns are depicted on the Column of Marcus Aurelius in Roma. His decision to name Commodus as successor rather than adopt a non-biological heir broke the adoptive succession principle.

**Sources:** Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*; Cassius Dio 71-72; Birley, *Marcus Aurelius* (1987).

## Gaius (the Jurist) (c. 130-180 CE)

Roman jurist. Author of the Institutes, the most important surviving textbook of Roman law from the classical period.

**What we know:** Almost nothing about his life. His Institutes were rediscovered in a palimpsest in Verona in 1816 and remain the clearest exposition of classical Roman law. His categories (persons, things, actions) structured the Justinianic codification and, through it, every civil law system in the world.

**Honest disclosure:** Gaius is presented in the chapter through his work rather than his person, because his person is unknown. This is appropriate: the jurist's anonymity is itself characteristic of the Roman legal tradition, which valued the reasoning over the reasoner.

**Sources:** Gaius, *Institutes*; Honoré, *Gaius* (1962); Nicholas, *An Introduction to Roman Law* (1962).

## The Provincial Citizen (composite figure)

A free person in the empire after 212 CE, living in Gaul or Syria or Africa, experiencing the Pax Romana from outside Roma.

**Honest disclosure:** No individual provincial citizen from the 2nd century is characterized in the chapter. The provincial experience is presented through the infrastructure (roads, baths, forums) and the legal system (citizenship, the ius gentium) rather than through a named individual. This is a structural choice: the chapter's argument is about the system, not about a single life within it.

**Sources:** Woolf, *Becoming Roman* (1998); Mattingly, *Imperialism, Power, and Identity* (2011).

## A Note on Sources

The principate is documented by a range of sources with different perspectives. Tacitus, the most literary of the imperial historians, wrote with a senatorial perspective that valued Republican libertas and was suspicious of imperial power. Suetonius wrote biography organized by character traits rather than chronology. Cassius Dio wrote comprehensive history from a later perspective. The jurists wrote from within the legal system. The Meditations are a unique document: a philosopher-emperor's private notebook. The chapter draws on all of these while maintaining awareness that the senatorial perspective (Tacitus, Cassius Dio) dominates the literary record and may not represent the experience of the provincials, the soldiers, or the enslaved.
