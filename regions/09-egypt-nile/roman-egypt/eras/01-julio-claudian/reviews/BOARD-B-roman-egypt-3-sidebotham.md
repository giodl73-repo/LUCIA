## BOARD — Steven Sidebotham — Review of Julio-Claudian Egypt

**Domain:** Red Sea ports archaeology, India trade, desert route infrastructure

**Overall:** The India trade section is well-grounded in the archaeological and textual evidence. The Berenice pepper residue detail is accurate (we did find peppercorns still identifiable in the storage jars, and yes, the smell was faintly present). The description of the desert crossing, the monsoon timing, and the Periplus merchant are all consistent with what we know.

**Factual accuracy:**
- The desert crossing from Coptos to Berenice: "a hundred miles" is approximate; the actual distance was closer to 258 miles (415 km) for Berenice, or about 95 miles (150 km) for Myos Hormos. Twelve days is the correct transit time for Berenice. The chapter should clarify which port the "hundred miles" refers to, or use the Berenice figure of approximately 250 miles.
- The watering stations are accurately described. We excavated several of these hydreumata along the route and they were indeed fortified posts with wells and garrison quarters.
- Teak from India, coconuts, sapphires, beads from Java: all confirmed in the Berenice excavation record. The Java beads in particular are significant evidence of how far the trade network extended.
- The Muziris Papyrus valuation of approximately seven million sesterces for a single shipment is within the scholarly consensus range. The papyrus is second-century but the trade patterns were established in the first century.
- Pliny's figure of fifty million sesterces to India and fifty million to China and Arabia: these are Pliny's own figures (Natural History 12.84) and are widely cited, though their accuracy is debated. The chapter's attribution to Pliny is correct.
- The twenty-five percent customs duty (tetarte) at Alexandria on Red Sea imports is accurately described.
- The monsoon timing (southwest monsoon, July-September departure) is correct for the direct route to India.

**Corrections needed:**
1. "Across a hundred miles of Eastern Desert": this should be approximately 250 miles (400 km) for the route to Berenice, or specify that the shorter route to Myos Hormos was approximately 95 miles. The current figure understates the difficulty of the crossing.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The Periplus is correctly characterized as a practical merchant's guide rather than a literary text. The quotation about Muziris is accurate.
- The attribution of monsoon knowledge to "Arab and Indian sailors" is correct; the Roman merchants learned the monsoon system from existing maritime traditions, not from independent exploration.
- The statement "Egypt sat at the hinge. It did not control the trade; the Indian and Arab merchants who carried most of the goods controlled it. But Egypt collected the toll" is an accurate summary of the province's role in the trade network.

**Verdict:** One correction needed (desert crossing distance). The India trade section is otherwise grounded in the archaeological and textual evidence.
