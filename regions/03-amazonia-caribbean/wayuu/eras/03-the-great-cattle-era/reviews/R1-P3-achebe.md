# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Wayúu, Era 3: The Great Cattle Era — PANEL-1 Review

I read this with the question I always carry: does this culture own its own story, or has someone borrowed it?

The Wayúu own this opening. Let me say that plainly, because it matters.

Seyuuya is not a demonstration of Wayúu culture. She is a woman counting her goats who happens to be Wayúu, and from the inside of her world — from the way she reasons about the lost goat, from the way she corrects her daughter's thread, from the way she reads the sky — the Wayúu world reveals itself. The writer has not explained the eirruku to the reader. The eirruku appears in Seyuuya's reasoning about her brother Maushi: "he is uterine brother and the one who handles the eirruku's herd negotiations with the neighboring clan." No parenthetical definition follows. No authorial aside steps in to explain what "uterine brother" means and why it matters. The reader either follows the reasoning or slows down and follows it more carefully. That is the correct posture. This is Achebe's method: render the world from inside and trust the reader to enter it.

The same is true of the pütchipü'üi. The word appears without translation. What follows is not a definition — it is a demonstration of the logic: the word-bearer carries the language of obligation between people who cannot speak to each other directly yet. The reader understands what the pütchipü'üi does from watching Seyuuya think through why it is necessary. That is inside-voice narration. That is what I asked for in *Things Fall Apart* when I rendered Okonkwo's world without apology or explanation.

The cattle section is the one I watched most carefully, because this is where borrowed-story risk is highest. The Spanish introduced cattle. The Wayúu adopted them. The temptation is to frame this as: "the Wayúu were resourceful in adapting outside technology to their existing systems." That would be the respectful-outsider's framing — admiring from a distance, crediting the Wayúu with cleverness in the face of colonial influence. The opening does not do this. Instead: "The Wayúu adopted the cattle and the cattle became Wayúu." The voice belongs to the Wayúu. The cattle are not the Spanish cattle that the Wayúu cleverly managed. They are Wayúu cattle now, and the distinction is ontological, not merely rhetorical.

**The question I'd push on:** One sentence gave me pause: "The Spaniards thought this would give them leverage. They were wrong." The voice here — "were wrong" — is correct in its refusal to soften the failure. But the chapter should be careful not to let the Spaniards become the foil by which Wayúu competence is measured. The Wayúu's adaptation of cattle does not need the Spanish miscalculation to be validated. In the full chapter, I want to see the Wayúu's cattle economy described entirely from inside — what it meant to a Wayúu woman, what it meant to an eirruku, what it meant to the pütchipü'üi — without any reference to what the Spanish expected or didn't expect. The Spanish perspective can appear, but only as evidence about the Spanish, not as the contrast that illuminates Wayúu success.

The mochila teaching is exactly right. The mother correcting a single misplaced thread in the fourth row — not with anger, not with extensive instruction, but with observation and silence — is how craft knowledge is transmitted in oral cultures, and it is shown, not explained. "The pattern comes from the mothers, who learned it from their mothers, who learned it from the ones before." That cadence is correct. Do not over-explain the mochila in the full chapter. Let it do its work by being present, not by being analyzed.

**Summary:** This opening earns the Wayúu's ownership of their story. The voice is right. The one risk in the full chapter is the Spanish-as-foil temptation. Watch for it. The Wayúu need no contrast to be themselves.
