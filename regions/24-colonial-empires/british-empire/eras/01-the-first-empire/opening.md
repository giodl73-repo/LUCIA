# The First Empire — Atlantic Commerce and Plantation

## Liverpool, 1750

The manifest was a document of precision.

It was a March morning, cold by the standard of the season, the Mersey running dark and quick between its banks. The men loading the *Thetis* worked in wool jackets that smelled of lanolin and river damp, their breath visible in the air above the quayside, and the iron bars they carried aboard — fourteen hundredweight, each bar roughly the weight of a grown man's arm — rang against the deck planking as they went down. The cotton cloth was packed in oilskin. The kegs of gunpowder were handled with deliberate care and stowed below. The muskets came last, crated, each crate stenciled with the Birmingham manufacturer's mark.

The *Thetis*, registered to the firm of Heywood and Leigh, carried outbound from Liverpool: 4,200 yards of printed cotton cloth, 180 muskets, 72 kegs of gunpowder, 12 gross of knives, 400 gallons of Jamaica rum, and 14 cwt of iron bars. The captain's name was Robert Norris. The intended port of exchange was Anomabo, on the Gold Coast of Africa. The manifest did not say what the *Thetis* intended to carry on the return leg because the return leg was handled by a different ledger, maintained separately in a counting house on Duke Street, kept in a hand so careful and regular that a visitor seeing it for the first time might have taken it for a printer's specimen.

Captain Norris would later testify before a Parliamentary committee that the enslaved people aboard his vessels were cheerful, well-fed, and better off than the laboring poor of England. He said this under oath. He had also, a decade before that testimony, chained two hundred and sixty-seven men, women, and children into the lower deck of a vessel called the *Dobson* for the seven-week crossing to Barbados, and brought two hundred and twenty-one of them alive to market. The remaining forty-six were entered in the accounts as loss.

Both things were true: the ledger, and the crossing. The counting house, and the lower deck. Liverpool understood this. The city had organized its entire material life around the fact of both being simultaneously, continuously true.

---

In 1750, Liverpool was the second-largest slaving port in the world. Its merchants had financed 220 voyages in the previous decade. The triangular trade had paid for the dock at Salthouse, the Exchange building on Water Street, the new streets going up in the north quarter where the merchant families lived. It had paid for the library on Duke Street, established in 1758 on a subscription model — any gentleman of the town could join for a small annual fee and have access to the best new books from London.

William Roscoe had not yet been born. He would be born in 1753, the son of a market gardener in Mount Pleasant. He would become a poet, a banker, a historian, a member of Parliament, and the most important abolitionist in the northwest of England. He would use that library, the one his city built on the proceeds of the trade he opposed. He would write the books that documented what the trade was, in the same rooms, among the same volumes, that the trade had purchased. Liverpool would eventually put up a statue of him.

Olaudah Equiano had not yet been taken. He would be taken in 1756, from what is now southeastern Nigeria, at approximately eleven years old. He would be sold twice in Africa before being loaded onto a slave ship bound for Barbados. He would later buy his own freedom, learn to read and write in the English his owners had intended to confine him, and publish, in 1789, *The Interesting Narrative of the Life of Olaudah Equiano*, which went through nine editions in his lifetime and which the abolitionists distributed in Liverpool, in Manchester, in the towns of the north. He would address the city that had organized its prosperity around his transportation as one of its own subjects. He would write in the language his enslavers spoke, using the arguments they had taught him to value, dismantling the system from inside the vocabulary it had given him.

---

The system worked because everyone inside it understood their part.

The manufacturer in Manchester wove the cotton cloth. The merchant in Liverpool purchased the cloth, loaded it with the muskets and the iron bars, and sent the ship south. The ship's captain ran the exchange on the coast, purchasing enslaved people from African traders who had their own networks, their own wars, their own calculations. The enslaved people crossed the Atlantic in the hold. In Barbados and Jamaica, the planters put them to work in the sugar fields. The sugar came back to Liverpool, was refined, and went to the tables of England. The profit circled back to the merchant, who invested it in another voyage.

The law recognized each transaction as legitimate. The state issued the licences. The Crown had chartered the Royal African Company. Parliament had debated and endorsed the trade's regulation. The Church of England had blessed ships departing for the coast. The slave trade was not something that happened in the dark: it was visible, organized, taxed, and defended in the open language of national interest.

What made Liverpool unusual was not that it participated. All the major ports participated. What made Liverpool unusual was that the contradiction between what its merchants did and what its merchants believed was so compressed into a single block of streets. The Exchange and the counting house. The library and the manifest. The sermon on Sunday and the log of the *Dobson*. The profits that educated Roscoe and the crossings that made Equiano.

The city did not hide the contradiction. It incorporated it. And from inside that incorporation, from within the vocabulary and the libraries and the arguments the system itself had generated, the case against it was slowly, painstakingly being assembled.

The *Thetis* was cleared for departure on the fourteenth of March. The manifest was filed. The provisions were loaded. The crossing to Anomabo would take approximately three weeks.

Captain Norris recorded the date, the tide, and the weather. He did not record what the crossing would cost. That was kept in a different ledger.
