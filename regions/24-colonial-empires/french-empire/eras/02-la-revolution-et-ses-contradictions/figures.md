# Figures — La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
## French Empire Era 2 (1789–1815 CE)

---

## Moreau de Saint-Méry (1750–1819)

Born in Fort-Royal, Martinique, educated in law in Paris. He returned to the Caribbean as a colonial administrator, served on the Conseil Supérieur of Saint-Domingue, became a Fellow of the Académie Royale des Sciences and other learned societies. His intellectual output was prodigious: multiple volumes on the laws of French colonial administration, ethnographic observations of the Caribbean, histories of Saint-Domingue and Spanish Hispaniola. His *Description topographique, physique, civile, politique et historique de la partie française de l'isle Saint-Domingue* (1797) remains the primary colonial-era source for the pre-revolutionary history of the colony.

The 128 racial categories of Saint-Domingue appear in the *Description* with the systematic care of a Linnaean taxonomy: each category named, each legal status specified, each social position mapped. Moreau de Saint-Méry did not invent the categories; they had accumulated through colonial law over the preceding century. He codified them — which is the administrative act that made them visible as a system rather than a collection of local practices.

He was a man of the Enlightenment who accepted the colonial world as a given. He read Montesquieu and Raynal. He sat in the Estates-General as a deputy from Martinique. He fled the Reign of Terror. In Philadelphia, his bookshop at 84 South Front Street was described by visitors as the finest French-language bookshop in North America; his customer records show sales to Thomas Jefferson, James Madison, and Alexander Hamilton. He returned to France under the Consulate, received appointments from Napoleon, and ended his career as an administrator of Parma. He died in 1819, having outlived the world his work described. His memoirs, *Voyage aux États-Unis de l'Amérique, 1793–1798*, were published posthumously.

What the record does not show is any extended reflection on the contradiction between the Enlightenment principles he professed and the colonial system he described with such precision. This may be editorial restraint. More likely it is the characteristic of a man for whom the contradiction was not visible as a contradiction, because within the cognitive framework he inhabited, the two propositions occupied separate categories: one was philosophy, one was administration. The system worked. The ledgers balanced. That the system was built on 500,000 people who had no input into the categories assigned to them was a fact the administrative record absorbed without comment.

## Jean-Baptiste Belley (c. 1746–1805)

Born in Gorée, on the island off the coast of present-day Senegal. Enslaved as a child, transported to Saint-Domingue. He fought in the American Revolutionary War under French command at Savannah in 1779 — the French expeditionary force that joined the American siege, which failed. He returned to Saint-Domingue, purchased his freedom at some point in the 1780s, became a soldier in the colonial militia. In 1793, in the chaos of the insurrection and the shifting political landscape, he was elected by the gens de couleur libres of Saint-Domingue as one of the colony's deputies to the National Convention in Paris.

He arrived in Paris in January 1794 and took his seat in the Convention. He was one of three deputies from Saint-Domingue — one white colonist, one mixed-race man, one African-born Black man — who stood before the Convention on the 4th of February, 1794, when the universal abolition of slavery was voted unanimously. He voted in favor of a measure that, three decades earlier, would have applied to him.

In 1797, he was painted by Anne-Louis Girodet de Roucy-Trioson. The portrait now hangs at the Palace of Versailles. He is depicted in the uniform of a French deputy, wearing the tricolor cockade. He leans against a marble pedestal on which sits a bust of the Abbé Raynal — the philosopher who had written that the enslaved were waiting for a great liberator. Belley is not looking at Raynal. He is looking at the viewer. The pose is relaxed, slightly contrapposto, one hand on the pedestal. It is the pose of a man who is comfortable where he is standing. The portrait is one of the most politically explicit works of the French Revolutionary period: a man born enslaved in Africa, who purchased his freedom, who fought in an American battle, who was elected to the French National Convention, who voted to end slavery, leaning on the text that predicted him.

When Napoleon restored slavery in 1802, Belley was one of the voices in opposition. He was arrested, transported back to France, and imprisoned on Belle-Île, the island off the Brittany coast. He died there in 1805. The cause of death is not recorded in the surviving documents, because the system that classified and recorded such things had classified him, by this point, as a prisoner rather than a deputy. The Girodet portrait remained at Versailles.

## Toussaint Louverture (c. 1743–1803)

The dates of his birth are uncertain because the system that might have recorded them did not initially intend to need them. He was born on the Bréda plantation in the northern plain of Saint-Domingue, enslaved, the son of an African-born man (his father claimed descent from Beninese royalty, a claim that neither Toussaint nor his family had the records to verify). He learned to read, was eventually given a degree of authority over other enslaved workers on the plantation, was permitted to maintain livestock, accumulated small economic resources over years of careful management.

He read Raynal. He read the *Maximes* of Épictète — the Stoic philosopher who had himself been enslaved in Rome, who had written that a man's freedom resided in his will and judgment rather than in his legal status. Whether Toussaint found this consoling or ironic, the record does not say.

After Bois Caïman, he was 48 years old. Most men who make revolutions are younger. He had the particular advantage of a man who had spent decades observing the system he was about to overthrow: he knew the northern plain's roads and trails intimately, knew which plantation managers were competent and which were afraid, knew the distribution of armed resources across the territory. He became a general through the practice of generalship: the guerrilla tactics that used terrain and mobility against a more conventionally equipped enemy, the political negotiations that kept multiple factions in alliance, the administrative capacity that rebuilt the northern plain's economy after the initial destruction.

His 1801 constitution was the act of a man who had read the French Enlightenment and taken it seriously: universal citizenship, abolition of racial categories in law, a state built on the principles the Declaration had stated. The document acknowledged French sovereignty while making it nominal. Napoleon understood this and responded accordingly.

At the Fort de Joux, between August 1802 and April 1803, he wrote letters. The letters cite legal provisions. They request his papers. They ask to be heard through the proper channels. They are the voice of a man who believed, even at the end, that the French administrative system had a mechanism for hearing him — that the rule of law he had read about in Montesquieu was real and would eventually respond. It did not respond. His last surviving letter, written in November 1802, contains the phrase: "I request only justice." The word *justice* appears four times in the letter. The letter received no reply.

He died on April 7, 1803. The temperature at the Fort de Joux that night was recorded by the garrison as three degrees Celsius.

## General Charles Leclerc (1772–1802)

Napoleon's brother-in-law, married to Pauline Bonaparte. Distinguished himself in the Italian campaign under Napoleon's command. Appointed to lead the Saint-Domingue expedition in December 1801, aged 29. He was a capable officer given an impossible assignment, and his dispatches trace a professional trajectory from confidence through bewilderment to exhaustion.

In February 1802: "The black generals are negotiating. I believe the pacification of the island can be achieved within two months."

In April 1802: "I lack the forces to pursue into the mountains. The terrain is unknown to us. The enemy moves without supply lines that I can identify or cut."

In July 1802: "I have lost 14,000 men to disease since January. The reinforcements promised have arrived in insufficient numbers. I request an additional 10,000 troops."

In October 1802: "This campaign cannot be won by military means. The population is against us, the terrain is against us, the climate is against us. I do not know what to recommend."

He died on November 2, 1802, of yellow fever, in Le Cap-Français, which he had renamed Cap-Français again after briefly renaming it Cap-Henri in honor of Henri Christophe's allegiance. He was 30 years old. Pauline Bonaparte accompanied him on the expedition, returned to France after his death, and remarried within the year. Napoleon received the news of Leclerc's death at Saint-Cloud and organized the next expedition without pause.

By the time the French forces finally surrendered and withdrew, in November 1803, approximately 50,000 French soldiers had died in Saint-Domingue, the great majority from yellow fever. This was more than Napoleon lost at any single European battle. The campaign is not prominently featured in the French military historiography of the Napoleonic period. It is, in the accounting of the archive, an expedition that failed, classified and filed.

## Léger-Félicité Sonthonax (1763–1813)

The civil commissioner who, on August 29, 1793, declared the general emancipation of the enslaved in the northern province of Saint-Domingue — eight months before the National Convention voted universal abolition. A Jacobin lawyer from Lyon, educated in Enlightenment principles, sent to Saint-Domingue in 1792 as one of three civil commissioners charged with restoring order and enforcing the limited rights decree for free people of color.

He declared emancipation as a military measure: he needed soldiers, the British were invading from Jamaica, the Spanish were moving from the east, and the only available soldiers were the people he was emancipating. Whether he would have declared emancipation without the military emergency is a question the record cannot answer, because the emergency existed. What the record shows is that he signed the decree, that 10,000 formerly enslaved men joined French forces within a month of its signing, and that these forces, eventually under Toussaint's command, expelled the British by 1798.

Sonthonax was recalled to France in 1793 by the Girondins, before his emancipation decree reached Paris. He returned to Saint-Domingue in 1796 as part of a new commission. He was expelled by Toussaint in 1797 — Toussaint had concluded that Sonthonax was advocating for full independence and was a political liability. Toussaint sent him to France on a ship, which was as close as the two men ever came to a diplomatic incident.

Sonthonax returned to France, entered legal practice, and died in 1813. His role in the emancipation of August 1793 is a point of dispute in the historiography: some accounts center him as the primary actor; others, following C.L.R. James, argue that the enslaved had already effectively liberated themselves and that Sonthonax simply acknowledged a military reality. The debate is, in its way, a reflection of the original contradiction: who is the subject of this story? The administrator who signed the decree, or the people whose liberation it formalized?
