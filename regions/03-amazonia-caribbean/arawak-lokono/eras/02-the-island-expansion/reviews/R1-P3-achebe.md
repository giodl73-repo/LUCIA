# Panel Review — Round 1
## P-3 Chinua Achebe | Opening Review

**Era:** The Island Expansion | **Culture:** Arawak / Lokono
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The voice is right. That is the most important thing I can say, and I say it first because it is the hardest thing to get right and because when it is wrong I say that first instead.

Kurima does not know he is "migrating." He does not know he is "expanding a civilization." He is loading a canoe and thinking about what his uncle told him, and whether he is a river person or an ocean person, and the author does not step outside Kurima's frame to translate these thoughts into anthropological language. This is the discipline that matters. The network is rendered through Kurima's body — the sensation of obligation as weight, the shared rhythm of paddling with a cousin he has paddled with all his life, the smell of the sea changing as the delta recedes. The reader learns the Lokono worldview by inhabiting it, not by being informed about it.

I am particularly attentive to the moment where the author could have written "the people there spoke a related dialect" and instead wrote "spoke the same root of words but with sounds that had shifted, the way a river sounds different at its mouth than at its source." The metaphor is Kurima's. A river person would make that comparison. An outside observer would write "related dialect." This kind of attention — making the language belong to the mind of the person speaking — is what separates a story told from inside from a story told from the doorway.

**My concern:** There is one passage I want to examine: "This was how the Lokono world grew: not through conquest, not through command, but through the accumulated decisions of young men and women who went to see what the islands held and came back knowing." This sentence works — it is accurate and not outside the worldview — but it sits very close to the border of the author making an interpretive point rather than letting the story make it. The sentence is not wrong, but it has the cadence of a summary. It could be cut and the scene would carry the same weight without the author's hand visible. I would consider whether it needs to be said or whether the chapter, by showing, has already said it.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening centers Kurima, who is male. The author mentions "young men and women" in that border passage. The chapter needs to follow through on this: the Lokono network ran through women's knowledge too — the cord his sister braided, the vessels his mother fired. If the chapter follows only male crossings and only male decisions, it has told half the story and presented it as the whole. The network is a gendered system, and women's roles in it — what knowledge they held, what crossings they made, what obligations ran through matrilineal lines — need to be present and specific, not gestured at.
