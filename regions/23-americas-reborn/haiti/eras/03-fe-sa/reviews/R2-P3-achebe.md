# R2 — Targeted Panel Review: Chinua Achebe
## Haiti, Era 3: Fè Sa — Notes (production bible)

**Date:** 2026-04-17
**Focus:** The Dessalines massacre, Bois Caïman source handling, inside voice at maximum

---

The notes correctly identify the highest-stakes Achebe Test moment: the massacre of the French population in early 1804. The note reads: "From inside Dessalines's logic, it is the closing of a door that the French had held open for returning. From outside, it is mass killing. The chapter must render the inside without flinching, without apologizing, and without winking at the reader. Dessalines knew what he was doing. Show him knowing."

This is correct. I want to add one thing: the chapter should not prepare the reader for the massacre by softening Dessalines beforehand. He should not be made charming or sympathetic in advance of the difficult moment, so that the reader has to struggle less when the massacre arrives. The difficulty IS the point. Dessalines is not likeable in the way Toussaint is likeable. He is righteous in a register that the world he lived in — and the world most readers of this chapter will inhabit — found and finds uncomfortable. Show him as he was, with his reasoning intact and his full knowledge of what he was ordering.

The Bois Caïman handling in the notes is good: use French accounts as evidence about the French, not as primary evidence about the ceremony. I want to add: the chapter should acknowledge explicitly that Haitian Vodou tradition has its own account of Bois Caïman, and that account matters more than the French version. This does not require lengthy scholarly apparatus in the chapter itself — it can be done in one sentence that attributes the ceremony's meaning to the tradition that practiced it rather than to the observers who watched it with contempt.

**On the gens de couleur:** The notes flag this as a complication. It is. The chapter should not smooth it over. The initially complex relationship between the formerly enslaved majority and the free mixed-race population (some of whom owned enslaved people themselves) is not a secondary plot; it is a structural fact of the revolution. Alexandre Pétion, who fought for the revolution, and the earlier gens de couleur who allied with the planters — these are inside the story, not outside it. A revolution that began as a race revolution was also always a class revolution; those two registers sometimes aligned and sometimes did not. Render this complexity from inside, without imposing a modern taxonomy on it.

**The name:** The chapter rightly notes that Dessalines named the country Ayiti, reclaiming the Taíno name. This should be handled with care: it is not simply a poetic gesture. It is a specific act of erasure of the colonial name (Saint-Domingue) and a specific act of claiming continuity with the land's pre-colonial identity. The land itself was renamed. That is a political-theological act as significant as the revolution's military conclusion.

**Line from the notes that should become a line in the chapter:** "He was captured through treachery in 1802 (invited to negotiate, then arrested). Died at Fort de Joux in April 1803 in a cold cell in the Jura Mountains." The treachery matters. Not because it makes Toussaint a victim but because it reveals something about the French negotiating position: they were not negotiating. The chapter should show this from inside — Toussaint knew the risks, went anyway, and was betrayed.
