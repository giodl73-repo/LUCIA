## B-1 — Nehemia Levtzion — Board Review of "The Great Hajj"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Political and economic history of medieval West African empires, trans-Saharan trade, and the Arabic source tradition (al-Umari, Ibn Khaldun, Ibn Battuta) that constitutes the primary written record of the Mali Empire. Author of *Ancient Ghana and Mali* (1973), the foundational modern study.

**Historical accuracy:**

- The date of the hajj is given as "724 of the Hijra (1324 CE)." This is broadly correct. The scholarly consensus, based on al-Umari's account (compiled from interviews with Egyptian officials in the 1330s-40s), places Musa's arrival in Cairo in July 1324. The departure from Niani would have been some months earlier. The year 724 AH runs roughly from January 1324 to January 1325 CE. Acceptable.
- The chapter names Musa as "ninth Mansa of the Manden Kurufaba" and "son of Faga Leye." The genealogical position is contested. Al-Umari's informant Ibn Amir Hajib describes Musa's lineage, but the exact ordinal number of his reign depends on how one counts the rulers between Sundiata and Musa — and whether one includes Abu Bakr II, the "voyager king." Calling him "ninth Mansa" is a defensible count but should be understood as one reconstruction among several. Not an error, but a place where scholarly humility would be welcome.
- "Five hundred jon carried gold... each of the five hundred bore a staff of the metal weighing six mithqals." This comes from al-Umari (via Ibn Amir Hajib). The figure of 500 slaves each bearing a gold staff of six mithqals is indeed in the source. However, the chapter should be aware that al-Umari was writing from secondhand accounts gathered years after the event, and these figures carry the conventions of Arabic travel-and-wonder literature. They are the best figures we have, but they are not audit data.
- "A hundred camels loaded with three hundred pounds of gold dust." I cannot locate this specific figure ("three hundred pounds") in al-Umari or Ibn Khaldun. Al-Umari mentions eighty to one hundred camel-loads of gold dust, but the weight per load is not specified precisely in the sources I know. The "three hundred pounds" figure may be an extrapolation or may derive from a secondary source. This needs a check — it risks being a modern author's calculation presented as a primary source claim.
- "The price of gold in Egypt did not recover for twelve years." The chapter attributes this to Ibn Khaldun. Al-Umari's account, via the Egyptian official Ibn Amir Hajib, is actually the primary source for the gold price depression in Cairo. Ibn Khaldun discusses Musa but the "twelve years" figure is more firmly associated with al-Umari's Egyptian informants. The attribution should be corrected or at least made more precise.
- The Mamluk sultan is correctly identified as al-Nasir Muhammad. The diplomatic protocol scene — Musa's refusal to prostrate and the compromise of prostrating "before God" — is drawn from al-Umari and is well established in the literature. Accurately rendered.
- "Musa died around 1337, roughly thirteen years after the hajj." The death date is uncertain. Levtzion and Hopkins place it variously; some scholars argue for as early as 1332, others for 1337. The chapter's "around 1337" is within the range of scholarly estimates but should note the uncertainty more explicitly.
- Al-Sahili is described as "a poet and architect from Granada." The sources (al-Umari, al-Sadi in the *Tarikh al-Sudan*) describe him as Abu Ishaq Ibrahim al-Sahili, from Granada. Calling him a "poet and architect" is defensible from the sources, though his primary identification in al-Umari is as a man of letters and piety. The architectural attribution comes largely from the *Tarikh al-Sudan*, written centuries later. This is acceptable but worth noting the source gap.
- "Musa paid al-Sahili twelve thousand mithqals of gold." This figure appears in the *Tarikh al-Sudan* (al-Sadi, 17th century). It is a late source. The figure may be accurate or may be inflated by the conventions of the later chronicle tradition. The chapter presents it without qualification; a note on the source's distance from the event would strengthen the claim.
- Ibn Battuta's visit is placed "fifteen years after Musa's death." If Musa died around 1337 and Ibn Battuta visited in 1352-53, the interval is approximately 15-16 years. Acceptable. The chapter says he found "Musa's son on the throne." In fact, Ibn Battuta found Mansa Sulayman, who was Musa's brother, not his son. Musa's son Maghan I ruled briefly after Musa's death but was succeeded by Sulayman. **This is a factual error that needs correction.**

**Cultural authenticity:**

- The chapter's central argument — that Musa's gold distribution was not personal extravagance but the fulfillment of a political-religious covenant obligation — is historically sound and represents a significant improvement over popular accounts that treat the hajj as mere spectacle. This framing is consistent with the best scholarship on Mande political culture.
- The economic analysis of gold market disruption is handled well. The mechanism — supply surge depresses price — is correctly described, and the observation that the covenant's logic of redistribution undermined the scarcity that sustained the empire's trade position is a genuinely insightful point that reflects serious engagement with the economic evidence.
- The treatment of Cairo and the Mamluk court avoids the trap of making Musa a naive visitor dazzled by a superior civilization. The diplomatic negotiation scene correctly portrays him as a sovereign navigating between systems of authority. This matches the tone of the Arabic sources, which are impressed by his wealth and piety while occasionally condescending about his customs.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The economic mechanism of the gold market crash is clearly and accurately explained, with appropriate weight given to the consequences.
- The diplomatic encounter with al-Nasir Muhammad is drawn directly from al-Umari and rendered with accuracy and narrative skill.
- The treatment of Timbuktu's scholarly tradition as pre-existing Musa's hajj — "The scholars from Cairo... did not establish this tradition. They entered it." — is historically correct and avoids the common error of attributing Timbuktu's intellectual life solely to Musa's patronage.
- The thread connecting Musa's gold expenditure to his borrowing on the return journey is accurate to al-Umari's account and is used to genuine analytical effect.
- The Sahelian architectural tradition and al-Sahili's contribution are described with appropriate nuance — not a replacement but a "conversation between building traditions." This is good.

**What needs correction:**

- **Ibn Battuta found Musa's brother Sulayman, not his son.** Section V states "He found Musa's son on the throne." This is wrong. Ibn Battuta visited during the reign of Mansa Sulayman (r. c. 1341-1360), Musa's brother. Musa's son Maghan I ruled briefly (c. 1337-1341) but was already dead or deposed by the time of Ibn Battuta's arrival. This must be corrected. (Source: Ibn Battuta, *Rihla*; Levtzion, *Ancient Ghana and Mali*, ch. 5.)
- **The "twelve years" gold depression attribution.** The chapter says "This is what Ibn Khaldun recorded." The twelve-year depression figure is more precisely associated with al-Umari's informants, particularly Ibn Amir Hajib, who was an eyewitness to the aftermath. Ibn Khaldun discusses Musa but the specific twelve-year recovery timeline comes through al-Umari. Recommend correcting to "This is what al-Umari's informants recorded" or citing both sources.
- **"Three hundred pounds of gold dust" on the camels.** This specific figure needs sourcing. If it is an extrapolation, it should be identified as such. If it comes from a specific source, that source should be named. As written, it has the appearance of a primary source claim but may not be one.
- **Musa's death date.** "Musa died around 1337" is within range but the uncertainty is greater than the text suggests. Some scholars place his death as early as 1332. A phrase like "died sometime between 1332 and 1337" would better represent the state of the evidence.
- **Numbering Musa as "ninth Mansa."** This is a defensible reconstruction but not a consensus fact. Consider softening or noting the uncertainty.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

The chapter is historically sound in its major claims and analytically strong in its central argument about the covenant logic of redistribution. The identification of Sulayman as Musa's son is a factual error that must be corrected. The attribution of the twelve-year gold depression to Ibn Khaldun rather than al-Umari should be fixed. The "three hundred pounds" figure needs sourcing or qualification. With these corrections, the chapter is defensible.
