# R1-P4 — Wade Davis

**Chapter:** Naqada I — The Amratian
**Stage:** PANEL-1
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## First Read

This opening treats its people as intelligent. Not as precursors to something greater. Not as a step on the way to the pharaohs. As people who made things, buried their dead with intention, and participated in exchange networks that worked. That is the baseline, and it is met.

## What Works

The knowledge system is present and respected. The discovery of copper smelting is rendered as an act of observation and inference: someone noticed that the green stone, in a hot fire, did something the flint did not. This is an intellectual achievement described at the level of method. The opening does not say "they learned metallurgy." It shows a person noticing a transformation and understanding it. This is the v1.3/v1.5 standard for knowledge-system-as-evidence.

The flint knapping detail is precisely right: "blades of a precision that would not be surpassed in the valley for another thousand years." This sentence treats a non-metallic technology as a peak of achievement rather than as a primitive precursor to metal. The flint IS the achievement. The copper is a new thing, not a better thing.

The burial practice is treated as a knowledge system, not as a custom. The dead are not "honored" (which would be the outsider's soft word). They are provisioned, because they need provisions. The cosmological logic is stated as fact. This is intellectual respect at the right depth.

## What Needs Attention

The opening moves quickly from material to material without pausing to honor the depth of any single craft tradition. The pottery paragraph mentions thin walls and white lime painting but does not engage with what this represents as a knowledge system. Thin-walled pottery requires specific clay preparation, specific firing temperatures, specific techniques of hand-building. The red-on-white painting tradition involves understanding of mineral chemistry. These are not mentioned because the opening is surveying the material repertoire, but the chapter should go deeper into at least one craft at the level of method.

The exchange network is described from the perspective of objects moving, not from the perspective of the people moving them. Who walks into the Eastern Desert for malachite? What do they know that others do not? The route knowledge, the water sources, the seasonal timing of desert travel, the ability to identify ore-bearing rock in a landscape of sandstone. This is a knowledge system, and it is invisible in the opening.

## The Question I'd Push On

Is the copper awl the right anchor object, or should it be the palette? The palette connects cosmetics, mortuary practice, ochre procurement, and social display in a single object. It may be more central to this culture's knowledge system than the copper, which was still marginal at this stage. The chapter should consider which object carries the most intellectual weight for the Naqada I world.
