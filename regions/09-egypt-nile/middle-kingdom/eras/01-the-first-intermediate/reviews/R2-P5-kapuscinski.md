## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Review of Notes (The Fragmentation)

**Focus:** Sensory plan and embodied figures.

**Assessment:** The notes acknowledge the sensory deficit identified in Panel-1 and propose a specific response: Section III ("The Nile's Testimony") as the sensory anchor, plus sensory threading throughout. The question is whether the plan goes far enough.

**What works in the plan:**
- Section III promises to render low floods sensorially: "exposed banks, shallow canals, thinned crops." This is the right instinct. The Nile at low water has a specific look, a specific smell (mudflats drying in the sun), a specific sound (or absence of sound where there should be flowing water).
- Ankhtifi's tomb at Moalla has painted scenes of daily life. The plan mentions this. Use it: the paintings show fishing, fowling, craftwork. These are bodies in a specific place.
- The notes correctly identify that the revised opening needs "the granary itself, the heat, the smell of stored emmer, the dust."

**What needs adding:**
- The Nile valley in low flood is not just "less water." It is a different landscape. The floodplain that should be under water is exposed. The fields that should be black soil are cracked. The irrigation canals that should be full are low or dry. The people who should be building during inundation season have nothing to build because there is no surplus to support construction labor. Show this transformation.
- Ankhtifi needs a body. His inscription suggests a man of physical confidence. His tomb is at Moalla, carved into the cliff above the valley. What does it feel like to climb to that tomb? What is the view from there?
- The Eloquent Peasant travels from the Wadi Natrun oasis to the Nile valley. This is a journey through specific terrain: desert, then the green strip, then the river. His donkeys carry salt, natron, reeds, leopard skins. These are specific objects. Let the reader smell the natron and feel the weight of the salt.

**Recommendation:** Proceed to WRITE with this sensory plan. But treat the landscape as a character, not as a backdrop.
