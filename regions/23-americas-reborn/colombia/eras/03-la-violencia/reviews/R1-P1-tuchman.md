# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman
## Panel Review — Opening: La Violencia

**Round:** 1 (PANEL-1)
**Reviewer:** Barbara Tuchman (P-1)
**Chapter:** Colombia, Era 3: La Violencia — opening.md

---

I read it twice: once as a reader who came to it cold, and once as a craftsman looking for the seams.

The first read was not interrupted. That is the first thing to say. The opening earns your attention in the first sentence and does not release it. "The bullet entered Jorge Eliécer Gaitán's body at 1:05 in the afternoon of April 9, 1948." This is the right kind of opening sentence. It is specific, it is physical, it places you in a moment before you have time to decide whether you want to be placed there. I have been trying to write sentences like this my whole career. It works.

What follows works too, and for the right reasons. The smell of the Carrera Séptima — diesel, grilled meat, rain coming off the mountains — is not decoration. It establishes the ordinary that the shot will rupture. The crowd gathering "slowly, warmly, with no particular urgency" is exactly the kind of period-specific behavioral detail that makes a scene live rather than merely exist. Roa Sierra beaten to death in ten minutes. Gaitán not reaching the hospital. The compression is devastating.

The structure I want to praise most is the "ten minutes / ten years" device. "That took less than ten minutes. What happened next took ten years." It is a journalist's trick at its finest — the kind of thing that looks effortless and is in fact the product of ruthless revision. It earns its own paragraph. It answers the question every reader is about to ask (what does this assassination mean?) without answering it at all. It opens the chapter like a set of double doors.

The Castro cameo is well handled. A twenty-one-year-old Cuban student stepping into "a history that was not his own" — that phrase does enormous work. It gives us Castro without making him the story. It gives us the sense that this is the kind of city where the world's future leaders witness things from hotel doorways. That is an image, not an argument, and it is better for being an image.

Gaitán's characterization holds. I appreciate that the opening does not tell us he was great; it tells us what he said and what his voice sounded like ("physical, something you felt in your chest"). That is the distinction between biography and portraiture. Biography tells you what a person did. Portraiture makes you feel who they were. This opening has achieved portraiture in a paragraph.

The section on La Violencia itself — where the chapter pivots from the Bogotazo to the country-wide pattern — is where my attention briefly flags. "Something more diffuse and more intimate: a landscape of violence in which the state dissolved at the edges" — this is accurate and important analysis, but it is analysis. The previous sections worked by showing. This section tells. It recovers: "the army and police and party militias all moved through the same territory with different flags and different lists" is specific and vivid enough to reground the reader. But the entry into this section is slightly too abstract.

My question for the NOTES stage: what is the single organizing scene — one person, one moment — that the chapter will return to when the analysis threatens to overwhelm the narrative? The Bogotazo is the opening. But the chapter must go into the interior, into Tolima and Boyacá and the coffee towns. Who is the figure who carries us there? Who is the person through whom we experience La Violencia as it actually operated — the farmer whose neighbor turned up dead, the village whose color was wrong in the wrong municipality? The García Márquez close suggests he will be a thread. But García Márquez was a bystander. The chapter needs someone who was not.

**The question I'd push on:** Who carries us into the interior? The opening has given us the spark. The chapter needs a figure who walks us into the fire.
