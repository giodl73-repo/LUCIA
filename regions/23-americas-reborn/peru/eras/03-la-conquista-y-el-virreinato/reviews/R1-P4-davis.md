# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Review — Opening: La Conquista y El Virreinato

What strikes me first in this opening is how it handles the Andean cosmovision — not as a set of beliefs held by people who happened to live in the Andes, but as an internally coherent epistemological system whose terms (ayni, pachakuti, Pachamama, the distinction between Ukhu Pacha and Kay Pacha) define a complete account of causality, obligation, and time. This is the approach I have consistently argued for: every culture's knowledge system is equally sophisticated, and the measure of a historical text is whether it can make that sophistication visible without condescension.

The treatment of ayni is the opening's central anthropological achievement. The author renders reciprocity not as a social custom but as a physical law — "the universe runs on return." This is accurate to Andean thought, and presenting it as physics rather than belief system is the right move. The distinction matters: a belief system can be wrong, superseded, replaced. A physics is the structure of the world. The Andean people who rose with Túpac Amaru II in 1780 were not rising because they had an ideological grievance (though they did). They were rising because the world was structurally broken, because the river was flowing in one direction and not returning, and this was not tolerable the way an unfair tax is not tolerable — it was intolerable the way gravity is intolerable when it fails.

I want to see more of the taki in the chapter's full version. The opening mentions it at the close: "The taki — the song — is also the record." This is important. In Andean culture, musical forms carried political knowledge — the harawi, the taki onqoy (literally "dancing sickness," the 1560s religious resistance movement whose name is itself a song form) — and the relationship between embodied song and political consciousness is a knowledge-system argument, not merely a literary one. The chapter should treat the taki at method level, not as atmospheric decoration: what did a specific taki carry? How did it travel? Who knew it?

The quipu mention at the close is similarly underdeveloped in the opening. The quipu held "what the archive refuses to hold" — this is the right framing. The Spanish colonial archive is partial, external, often hostile. The Andean knowledge system preserved information in forms the archive could not confiscate: in song, in knotted string, in the ritual cycle, in the agricultural calendar. The chapter should identify a specific form of this counter-archival knowledge and show it operating.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening mentions "every sympathetic kuraka" in Micaela Bastidas's correspondence network. The kuraka system — the indigenous intermediary class that mediated between the Andean communities and the colonial administration — is one of the most complex and morally ambiguous institutions of the Viceroyalty. Some kurakas had deeply internalized Spanish legitimacy; others maintained Andean obligations beneath a colonial surface. The chapter should engage with this complexity: how did the rebellion spread through some kuraka networks and not others? What was the knowledge-system basis for loyalty and defection? This is not a social-political question only. It is a question about how Andean epistemology — ayni, reciprocal obligation, the cosmological breach — was unevenly felt across different communities.
