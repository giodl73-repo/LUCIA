# Figures — The Long Struggle: Mohammad Shah and Naser al-Din Shah
## Qajar Era 2 (c. 1834–1896 CE)

---

## Mohammad Shah Qajar (r. 1834–1848)

He was twenty-five when he inherited the throne and thirty-four when he died, and in between he governed a state that had already learned the main lesson of the Qajar era — that the plateau could not support an army capable of fighting Russia or Britain in the field — and did not know what to do with the lesson. Mohammad Shah was not a man of weak character; he was a man whose character expressed itself through religious devotion and personal loyalty rather than through administrative drive. His minister Hajj Mirza Aqasi was his tutor, his confidant, and his guide in the mystical traditions of Sufi Islam, and the two men between them produced a court that was spiritually intense and administratively inert.

The Herat campaign of 1837–1838 is Mohammad Shah's reign in miniature. He marched because Herat was Iranian in culture and in historical claim, and because the Iranian army — at least on land — was capable of besieging it. The campaign was competently executed. The siege was progressing. The British response, when it came from the south, was not military defeat but the demonstration of a kind of power that siege operations on the Afghan border could not address. Mohammad Shah withdrew because the calculation was correct. He had been turned back by sea power while winning by land. This was not a failure of will; it was the structural condition of Iran's position in the world of 1838, and it would be the structural condition for the next hundred years.

He died of gout in 1848. His son was seventeen.

## Mirza Taqi Khan Amir Kabir (1807–1852)

He was born in the village of Hazava, near Arak, to the cook of the Qajar court — a man of low birth who rose through intelligence and the patronage of the Crown Prince Abbas Mirza's reform programs. He watched the Ottoman Tanzimat reforms from the Iranian diplomatic mission in Istanbul, studied Russian military organization through the same channels, and returned to Iran with a clear and precise understanding of what a modern state required.

What he built in four years would have taken another country a generation. The Dar al-Funun was the symbol, but the administrative reforms were the substance: limiting the provincial governors' traditional autonomy, standardizing the tax system, controlling the court's expenditures, building an army that answered to the state rather than to the hereditary officers who had commanded it. He did this while serving as both prime minister and minister of war and acting, in the chaos of the 1848 succession, as the man who held the state together while the new shah found his footing.

His fall was the result of precisely the success he had built. A prime minister who controlled the army, the administration, and the court's finances was a prime minister more powerful than the shah. Naser al-Din Shah was young and learning to rule; the Russian and British embassies found a more efficient administration less amenable to the negotiations through which they managed Iranian affairs; the court factions that Amir Kabir had sidelined had not forgotten being sidelined. The combination was enough.

He was taken to the Bagh-e Fin in Kashan in January 1852 — the Persian pleasure garden of the Safavid era, with its running channels of spring water and old plane trees and the smell of cypress and roses in summer. In January the garden was cold and bare. He was killed in the bath house, in warm water, by men who had come from Tehran. He was forty-two. His wife, the shah's sister, was not told what had happened to her husband until after it was done. She would outlive the shah who had ordered it.

The Dar al-Funun graduated its first students four years after his death. By the 1870s it had produced the generation of Iranian officials who managed the concession system's paperwork. Amir Kabir had meant to produce officials who would not need the concession system at all.

## Naser al-Din Shah Qajar (r. 1848–1896)

He was the central figure of the Qajar era, the man who held the throne for forty-eight years and died at a shrine at the hands of a man who had been in prison for the same political current that had forced the Tobacco concession's cancellation four years before. He came to power at seventeen and learned to rule from a man he would have executed before his twenty-second birthday. He went to Europe three times and wrote about what he saw with the careful attentiveness of a man who understood what he was looking at and also understood what he could not reproduce.

He was not a simple man. His diaries from the European trips show an observer of genuine intelligence: noting the width of the Paris boulevards cut by Haussmann through the old city, understanding that this was not aesthetics but the logic of controlling a population through geography; observing the Manchester mills and the London docks and calculating the differential between what those machines produced and what Iranian artisan workshops produced per worker; watching the military academies at Saint-Cyr and Sandhurst and measuring the distance between their curricula and the Dar al-Funun's improvised Persian-Austrian hybrid. He saw and he understood and he returned to Tehran with the understanding that the gap was real and the resources to close it were not.

The concession system was his solution and his trap. He knew, by the later years of his reign, that the concessions were not building Iran toward independence — they were building a set of foreign dependencies that complicated each other, that required foreign revenue to service, that generated domestic opposition he could manage but not eliminate. He continued because the alternative was a war he could not fight or a bankruptcy he could not survive. He was not wrong, exactly. He was governing in a room where every door led somewhere worse than where he was standing.

After the Tobacco Protest he was more careful. He did not grant the next large concession without consulting the clerical establishment's mood more precisely. He understood that the *marjaiyyat* had demonstrated something that could not be undemonstrated. He was also, by the 1890s, sixty years old and had been governing for most of his adult life, and the energy that had driven the early reign's administrative energy had become something more conserving — watchful, precise, oriented toward maintenance rather than transformation.

He was shot on the first day of the month of Sha'ban in the year 1313 of the Hijra, on the anniversary of his reign. His assassin had been in al-Afghani's circle. The connection was ideological rather than organizational; the shot was one man's decision, not a conspiracy. But the ideological current that had produced the man with the pistol — the current that held the concession system and the Qajar royalty itself to be the problem — would produce the Constitutional Revolution ten years later.

He died in the precinct of the shrine. His body was carried back to Tehran. The dynasty continued.

## Mirza Hasan Shirazi (1815–1896)

He was eighty years old when he issued the tobacco fatwa, living in Samarra in a house above the shrine of the eleventh Imam's father. He had been in Samarra for years — the golden dome of the Askari shrine visible from his study window, the Tigris flowing below the city in the flat light of the Mesopotamian plain. His students came to him from across the Shia world: from Najaf, from Karbala, from Iran and India and Bahrain. He issued legal opinions regularly, as a *marja* does — on questions of inheritance, of commercial practice, of ritual obligation, of the complex situations that Islamic jurisprudence has developed over fourteen centuries of reasoning from the Quran and the traditions of the Prophet and the Imams.

The tobacco ruling was one sentence. Its brevity was not accident. A *marja* of Shirazi's stature did not write polemics. He wrote legal opinions, and a legal opinion on a question of this clarity required precision, not elaboration. The concession harmed the faithful. The concession was therefore forbidden. The logic was complete.

He died the same year as Naser al-Din Shah — 1896 — before the Constitutional Revolution that his tobacco ruling had helped make possible, before the debates about whether the clerical establishment's demonstrated power should be formalized in a constitution or remain independent of constitutional frameworks, before the generation that drew lessons from 1891 had finished drawing them. His authority outlasted his life. The *marjaiyyat* he had embodied did not depend on any single person and did not end when any single person did.

## Mirza Taqi Khan Amir Kabir's Legacy (Absent Figure)

The chapter's most present figure who does not appear in it is Amir Kabir after 1852. He is gone from the reign by the time its central dramas unfold. But the Dar al-Funun graduates who processed the concession paperwork, the officers who commanded the Cossack Brigade that the Russians funded in the 1870s, the journalists who published the reformist press that circulated the ideas that fed the Constitutional Revolution — these were the products of the institutional infrastructure he had started to build. His execution ended his ministry. It did not end his influence. The room he had tried to build continued to be furnished by others, and some of the furniture they added was not what he had designed.

This is noted not because the chapter should labor the point but because the figures section should hold the absence. The Qajar era is full of men who were removed before their project was complete, whose projects continued anyway in forms they would not have recognized, making arguments they might not have endorsed. This is the nature of reform in a state under pressure: you build what you can, someone dismantles it, and what survives is not what you built but the idea that building was possible.
