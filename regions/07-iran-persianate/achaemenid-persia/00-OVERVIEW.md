# Parsa — The Kingdom of the Persians

We are the people of Parsa — the land in the Fars highlands where our tribal ancestors grazed their herds and our first kings built their fire altars. Cyrus came from this land, from the line of the Achaemenids, and in a generation he built the largest empire the world had yet seen — from the Aegean coast to the Indus River, from the Caspian steppe to Egypt. He did it not only by conquest but by the idea that the gods of the conquered peoples were real, their customs were legitimate, and a great king served them rather than destroying them. The cylinder of Cyrus — the clay barrel inscription he placed in the temple of Marduk at Babylon — declares that Marduk himself called Cyrus to restore the city's cults. This was policy, not piety. But the policy worked because it was sincere enough to be maintained.

Our empire — the *Xšaça*, the Kingdom — was the road and the message. The Royal Road from Sardis to Susa, 2,700 kilometers of maintained highway with way-stations every day's ride, carried the empire's orders from the royal court to its twenty-plus *satrapies* in a week where individual travel took three months. The royal scribes who served each satrap wrote in Aramaic — the practical language of the empire's western half — and in the local languages the satraps' subjects used. We did not require our subjects to become Persian. We required them to pay tribute, provide soldiers when called, and not rebel. Within those constraints, they could govern themselves by their own laws, worship their own gods, and use their own languages. This was the Achaemenid imperial bargain, and it held an empire of fifty million people together for two hundred years.

Key places (Persian names):
- **Parsa** (Persepolis) — the ceremonial capital; the Apadana; the tribute processions carved in relief; the royal tombs at Naqsh-e Rostam
- **Pasargadae** — Cyrus's capital; his tomb; the first Achaemenid palace
- **Susa** — the administrative capital for the western empire; Darius's palace
- **Ekbatana** (Hamadan) — the summer capital; originally the Median capital

## What Defined Them

**Ahura Mazda and the rejection of the *daiva*.** The Achaemenid kings declared themselves servants of Ahura Mazda — the Wise Lord — the great deity of the Zoroastrian tradition who stood in cosmic opposition to the *druj*, the Lie, and to the false gods the *daiva*. The royal inscriptions at Persepolis and Behistun open with invocations of Ahura Mazda and attribute the empire's existence to his blessing. This was not simply ceremonial. It reflected a genuine theological framework — Zoroastrian or proto-Zoroastrian — in which the king's obligation was to maintain *asha* (truth, right order) against the forces of the Lie. The kings who followed this framework were, in their own self-understanding, doing cosmic work, not merely political work.

**The *satrapy* system — governing diversity through delegation.** The Achaemenid empire was too large and too diverse to govern through a single uniform administration. The solution was the *satrapy*: a large provincial unit governed by a *satrap* (khshatrapavan — "protector of the kingdom") who was accountable to the king, managed local affairs with considerable autonomy, and delivered tribute and military service on demand. The satrap was typically a Persian or Median noble, often a royal relative, who kept his position through demonstrated loyalty. The imperial postal system, the royal inspectors (the "eyes and ears of the king"), and the regular tribute assessments connected the satrapies to the center without requiring micromanagement.

**Persepolis and the tribute procession.** The relief sculptures of the Apadana at Persepolis — the great audience hall where Darius and Xerxes received tribute — show the twenty-three nations of the empire in procession, each delegation in its own distinctive dress, each carrying the characteristic products of its land. This was not merely decorative. It was a political statement about the nature of the empire: diverse peoples, each in their own form, coming to the Persian king who stood at the center of the world. The king did not require uniformity. He required acknowledgment. And the procession of peoples was the annual re-enactment of that acknowledgment.

**Aramaic as the empire's medium.** The Achaemenid empire governed its vast territory through Aramaic — the *lingua franca* of the ancient Near East that the Assyrian empire had already disseminated — supplemented by local languages where the imperial administrators needed to communicate with local populations. Official documents were written in Aramaic, translated into local languages as needed. The empire's Aramaic correspondence — fragments of which survive in Egypt, in Anatolia, and in Bactria — shows a standardized administrative language operating across an enormous geographic range. The choice of Aramaic rather than Old Persian for administration was practical: Old Persian was the royal ceremonial language, Aramaic was the useful one.

**The road as imperial nervous system.** The Royal Road from Sardis to Susa was the empire's most important infrastructure: 2,700 kilometers of maintained road with way-stations (*paradayadam* — paradises, the source of the English word) at regular intervals where fresh horses waited. A royal messenger using the relay could cover the full road in a week; a traveler on foot or with pack animals needed three months. The road made it possible for the king's orders to reach the western satraps quickly, for tribute to flow reliably, and for military forces to concentrate where needed with reasonable speed. The road was not metaphorically the empire's nervous system. It was literally the mechanism by which imperial decisions became provincial action.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Founding:** Cyrus stands before Marduk's temple in Babylon and participates in the *Akitu* festival. The inscription he places in the temple says that Marduk called him from the mountains to restore the god's city. The priests of Marduk accept this. Every subsequent Achaemenid king will perform this ceremony. The empire is organized around a principle that the gods of every people deserve honor.

**2 — The Age of Darius:** The great inscription at Behistun is being carved into the cliff face above the Royal Road: Darius standing on the prostrate figure of the False King Gaumata, the six great nobles behind him, nineteen captive kings chained before him, Ahura Mazda hovering above in his winged disc. The empire's ideology is being carved into the mountain.

**3 — Xerxes and the Greek Campaign:** The bridge of boats across the Hellespont carries the army. The army is enormous — Herodotus' numbers are exaggerated, but the Persian force was the largest assembled in the ancient world to that point. Thermopylai holds for three days. Athens burns. And then the ships at Salamis and the army at Plataia turn the campaign back. The western limit of the empire is being drawn.

**4 — The Long Peace:** A Persian satrap in Sardis is funding one Greek faction against another. Persian gold is the most important political variable in the Greek world's interminable wars. The empire that could not conquer Greece has found a more efficient way to keep it from unifying against Persia: pay the Greeks to fight each other.

**5 — The Macedonian Conquest:** Darius III rides from one battlefield defeat to another, each time hoping to find a position that negates Alexander's cavalry. At Gaugamela the empire's last great army dissolves. Persepolis is burned — Alexander's generals argue about whether it was deliberate policy or drunken accident. The Achaemenid line ends. The administrative structures, the road, the satrapies, the tradition of governing diverse peoples in their own forms — all of this will be inherited by the successors who follow.
