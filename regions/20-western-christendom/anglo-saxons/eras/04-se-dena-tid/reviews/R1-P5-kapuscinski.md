# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Panel Review — PANEL-1
**Chapter:** Se Dena Tíd — The Danish Time (Anglo-Saxons, Era 4)
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (opening review)

---

The marsh is real. That is what I want to say first.

"The Somerset marshes in February. The reed beds silver with frost at dawn. The paths through the bog known only to the men who lived there, who cut their turf there, who moved their livestock along the dry ridges between the black water and the black water."

I am there. I can feel the cold. I can smell the frost on the reeds. I know the texture of those paths — narrow, muddy at the edges where the frost has not held, firm only in the center where generations of feet have compacted the peat. The image of the two kinds of black water — the standing bog on each side of the dry ridge — gives the landscape a character that is not merely scenic. It is threatening. A man who did not know these paths would be swallowed.

The physical vulnerability of Alfred's position is established through landscape before it is named. He is in a place where a wrong step — in knowledge, in trust, in direction — means death. The marsh does the work that a speech about danger could not.

The hearth scene is also well-done. "His mind was elsewhere." We don't need to be told what he was thinking. The burned bread tells us. The woman's scolding tells us that the social hierarchy has been, temporarily, suspended — she doesn't know he's the king, and even if she did, she'd have something to say about the bread. That detail of social reality in extremity is physical in the best sense: power is not abstract, it is enacted, and when it is not enacted it disappears.

**The question I'd push on:** The chapter needs to maintain sensory grounding when it moves from the marsh to the institutional. The burhs, the schools, the translations — these are ideas in danger of becoming abstractions. What did a burh smell like? Timber and mud, probably, fresh-cut wood and the smoke of a new settlement. What did a scriptorium sound like — the scratch of a reed pen, the murmur of a scholar reading back his translation? What did Alfred's hall at Winchester look like when he was dictating the *Pastoral Care* — what light, what company, what smell from the hearth?

The chapter must carry the same sensory discipline into its institutional sections that the opening carries into the marsh. If it loses the body when it reaches the policy, the reader will feel the loss even if they cannot name it.

One thing I want more of in the chapter: the Danes. They are present in the opening as a force and an adversary, but they had bodies too. What did the Danish camp at Chippenham smell like in the two weeks Alfred's forces sat outside it? What did Guthrum look like standing at the baptismal font? Give me the enemy as a physical presence, not just a geopolitical condition.
