## Manual Pass Findings

**Finding 1 — P3 (borderline)**
Location: Cannibalism section — "This was a cosmological practice, not a nutritional one."

The sentence is technically correct and the chapter is trying to prevent misreading. However, the framing implicitly responds to an imagined outside observer's misreading ("not a nutritional one" answers a question a Tupinambá person would never have asked). The reader inside the Tupí-Guaraní worldview would not need this clarification — the cosmological logic has already been rendered in the preceding paragraph, and the reader who has followed the chapter to this point understands it without the explicit clarification. **Recommendation:** Cut the sentence. The preceding paragraph does the work. (P3 — author decides, but the chapter is stronger without it.)

**Finding 2 — P3 (borderline)**
Location: "Europeans could not see it from inside, and so they saw only the surface of it, which was the eating, and from the eating they built an entire theory of savagery that they carried with them to every coast in the world where they subsequently arrived."

This sentence is in voice but it contains an implicit judgment: "a theory of savagery" judges the European view. This is a judgment from within the Tupí-Guaraní worldview (the Europeans were wrong, and their wrongness had consequences), not a modern editorial judgment. The sentence passes the Achebe Test: a thoughtful Tupinambá person could read this and find it accurate. **Recommendation:** Retain as written. The judgment is the culture's own, not the author's.

**Finding 3 — PASS**
Location: Ritual cannibalism section overall.

The section is handled correctly. It does not linger disproportionately. It is one paragraph in a chapter of many sections. It is framed entirely from inside the cosmological logic. It does not perform shock or disgust. It states what the ceremony was for and what happened. P3 borderline on the "not a nutritional one" sentence (see Finding 1), but the section as a whole passes.
