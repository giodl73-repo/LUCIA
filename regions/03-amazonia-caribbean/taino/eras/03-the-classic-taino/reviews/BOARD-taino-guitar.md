# BOARD Review — Lynne Guitar
## The Classic Taíno — Taíno Continuity

**Reviewer position:** Taíno cultural persistence, survival scholarship, 2018 genome study
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

**The continuity claim:**

The chapter's landing states: "In 2018, scientists at Harvard and the Max Planck Institute analyzed a 1,000-year-old genome recovered from a cave in the Bahamas and compared it to the genomes of contemporary people across the Caribbean. The match was clear. The descendants of the people who greeted Columbus were alive. They were alive in the Dominican Republic. They were alive in Puerto Rico. They were alive on every island that was supposed to have been emptied."

This is consistent with the Menéndez et al. (2018) study published in *Cell*. The study analyzed an approximately 1,000-year-old Lucayan Taíno genome recovered from a cave in the Bahamas (Long Island, Bahamas) and found that contemporary Dominican and Puerto Rican populations carry ancestry that matches this ancient genome, demonstrating genetic continuity. The claim "on every island" is slightly broader than what the study directly tested; the study specifically confirmed continuity to Dominican and Puerto Rican populations. Recommended adjustment: "They were alive in the Dominican Republic and in Puerto Rico. They were alive across the islands that were supposed to have been emptied." This is more precise without weakening the claim.

**The extinction narrative:**

The chapter handles the twentieth-century "extinction" claim correctly: it identifies it as incorrect in a specific way ("wrong about what the Taíno were") and explains why (the Taíno were a network, not a fixed population in a fixed location). This framing is consistent with the approach I have taken in my scholarship. The chapter does not claim that nothing was lost — the disruption is clearly described — but it correctly refuses to equate disruption with extinction.

**The living library as continuity mechanism:**

The chapter's structural engine (the areíto cannot be seized because it has no location except the people who carry it) is the correct mechanism for explaining why continuity was possible despite the material disruption. The areíto's embodied, distributed, non-inscriptional character is exactly what allowed fragments to survive when the conditions for full performance were destroyed. This is consistent with what my scholarship has found about the specific practices and knowledge that persisted.

**One recommendation:**

The chapter would benefit from acknowledging, even briefly, that the continuity was also partial — that some things were lost and some things were not, and that the people who carry Taíno ancestry today do so in a complex relationship with the tradition rather than as its unbroken continuation. The chapter comes close to this in "unmeasured, uncelebrated, continuous," which is good, but a slightly more explicit acknowledgment of the incompleteness of the survival would make the claim more honest without weakening it.

Suggested addition in the landing section, after "the knowledge kept moving": "Not all of it. Some was lost when the last fluent singers died without transmission. But enough moved that the thread held."

**Overall verdict:** The continuity claim is accurate, with one precision adjustment recommended on the geographic scope and one addition recommended for completeness. Both are minor. The chapter's treatment of the extinction/survival question is the most accurate and respectful I have seen in a popular narrative treatment.
