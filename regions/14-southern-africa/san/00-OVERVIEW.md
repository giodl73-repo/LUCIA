# ||Xam, Ju/'hoansi, and Their Kin — "The First People"

The San are not defined by what they lack. They are defined by what they carry: the deepest, most continuously maintained body of ecological knowledge in southern Africa; a religious and artistic tradition expressed in rock paintings that are among the most extraordinary visual documents in human history; a social organization built around sharing, tracking, and the trance dance that is as sophisticated a response to the problem of human community as any political philosophy ever devised; and a claim to this landscape that predates every other claim by sixty thousand years or more. When a Ju/'hoan tracker in the Kalahari reads a spoor — identifying the animal, its age and sex, its gait, how long ago it passed, whether it is injured — from marks in sand that a non-specialist cannot even see, they are doing something that required tens of thousands of years to develop and cannot be replicated by any technology that has yet been invented.

The San are also not a single people. The term "San" covers dozens of linguistically distinct communities across southern Africa: the ||Xam of the Cape, the Ju/'hoansi of the Kalahari, the G/wi, the !Kung, the !Xóõ, the Khoe-speaking groups, and many others. What connects them is the Khoisan language family — with its distinctive click consonants, the most phonemically complex family of languages in the world — and the shared ecological orientation: a knowledge system built on intimate attention to the living world, expressed in rock art, trance ceremony, and the community practices of the trance dance. The San do not call themselves "San" — this is a Nama word meaning "foragers" — or "Bushmen" — this is a colonial derogatory term. Each community has its own name. The Ju/'hoansi call themselves "the real people." The ||Xam called themselves "||Xam-ka-!ei" — the people of the ||Xam land.

- **Tsodilo Hills** (Botswana) — "the mountain of the gods"; the greatest concentration of rock art in southern Africa; UNESCO World Heritage Site
- **Cederberg** (South Africa) — ||Xam rock art sites; the greatest concentration in the Cape

- **!Xaus** — a sacred water source; one of the organizing nodes of Kalahari San territory
- **Dobe** — the community documented in the 1960s–1980s by Richard Lee; the most extensively studied Ju/'hoan group

## What Defined Them

**The trance dance and the doorway to the spiritual world.** The trance dance — the central ceremony of San spiritual and social life — is not entertainment or ritual in a peripheral sense. It is the means by which the healers (n/om-kxaosi, those who carry n/om, the healing energy) enter the spirit world to heal the sick, control the rain, and manage the relationships between the living world and the world of spirits. The rock paintings of the Drakensberg and the Kalahari are almost entirely images of trance experience: the dying posture of the trance dancer as the spirit leaves the body, the therianthropic figures (half-human, half-animal) that represent the transformation experienced in trance, the bleeding from the nose that accompanies deep trance states. These are not decorative images; they are theological documents, painted on stone as a record of spiritual experience and as a means of continuing to access spiritual power in that place.

**The tracking knowledge as epistemology.** San tracking is not simply the ability to follow animals. It is a comprehensive system of ecological knowledge — an understanding of animal behavior, plant phenology, weather, geology, and the relationships between all of these that allows the tracker to predict where an animal will be before they see its sign. The anthropologist Louis Liebenberg has documented the cognitive complexity of San tracking in detail, arguing that it involves the same hypothetico-deductive reasoning as scientific inquiry: the tracker forms hypotheses about animal behavior, tests them against new evidence, revises the hypothesis, and follows the logic to its conclusion. This is a knowledge system developed over tens of thousands of years of direct ecological engagement. It is a knowledge system sophisticated in ways that modern ecological science is only beginning to appreciate.

**The sharing ethic and hxaro.** The San sharing ethic — the obligation to distribute food, water, and other resources across the community — is not simply generosity. It is the social technology that makes a mobile foraging economy work. A community in which individuals hoarded food would face catastrophic inequality in the face of variable environmental conditions; the sharing ethic ensures that good fortune is distributed and bad fortune is shared. The hxaro system — the long-distance exchange of gifts between San communities across hundreds of kilometers — extends this principle across space, creating a network of obligation that provides insurance against local environmental failure. These are sophisticated economic institutions, not simply customs.

**The rock art as living theology.** The rock art of southern Africa — an estimated 20,000 sites containing millions of individual images, created across a period of at least 27,000 years and continuing in some areas into the nineteenth century — is the longest continuous artistic tradition in the world. It is also, in the interpretation developed by David Lewis-Williams and others based on both neuropsychological evidence and direct San testimony about the paintings' meaning, a record of altered states of consciousness experienced in the trance dance. The eland, the most sacred animal in the San cosmology (associated with the creator deity /Kaggen and with the most powerful trance medicine), appears most frequently; the dying posture and the bleeding from the nose appear; the therianthropes appear. The paintings are a theological library, painted in stone, that has survived in the sheltered rock faces of the Drakensberg and the Cederberg for as long as human memory itself.

**The colonial extermination and the survival.** The ||Xam San of the Cape Colony were, by the late nineteenth century, reduced by two centuries of colonial violence — the commandos that killed, enslaved, and drove away San communities whenever they raided settler livestock — to near-extinction as a community. Wilhelm Bleek and Lucy Lloyd's extraordinary archive of ||Xam oral tradition, collected in Cape Town in the 1870s–1880s from ||Xam informants who had been arrested for stock theft, is the primary record of ||Xam language and narrative — a record made at the very moment the community was being destroyed. The |Xam archive at UCT is one of the most significant bodies of African oral tradition ever collected, and it was collected because the colonial system brought its last surviving keepers to Cape Town in chains. This is not irony; it is tragedy.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Oldest Story:** At the Tsodilo Hills, a painter mixes ochre with animal fat and blows it across the rock face through a hollow bone. The eland appears in the stone. This has been happening here for 27,000 years. The painter knows the eland is sacred. She knows why it is sacred. She is doing this correctly.

**2 — The Trance Dance:** The healers are dancing. It is the second hour. The n/om has risen up the spine; the healer is in the doorway between worlds. The community is singing around her, holding the trance, preventing the spirit from taking her too far. This is knowledge. It took years to learn.

**3 — The Tracker's Reading:** The tracker stops at a mark in the sand. He reads: a male eland, slightly lame in the left foreleg, passed here two hours ago, moving east toward the water. He begins following. The hunt will take two days. He already knows where it will end.

**4 — The Commandos:** The colonial militia is moving through the San territory. The family has scattered into the kopjes. The children know the hiding places; their parents have shown them every one. Some will survive. The |Xam speaker who arrives in Cape Town three years from now will be one of them.

**5 — The Living Knowledge:** At Dobe, Ju/'hoan trackers are working with scientists who want to document the tracking knowledge before it is lost. The trackers are patient with the scientists' questions. They find some of the questions odd. The knowledge is not about to be lost; it is being actively used. The scientists are the ones who need the documentation.
