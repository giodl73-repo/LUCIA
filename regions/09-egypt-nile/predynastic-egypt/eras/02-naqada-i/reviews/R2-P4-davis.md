## Assessment

The notes identify two craft knowledge systems for method-level treatment: copper smelting (Section 2) and pottery (Section 3). This is correct. The chapter should render at least one of these at genuine depth.

## Specific Guidance

**Copper smelting at method level:** The transformation of malachite to copper is not simple heating. It requires: (1) recognizing the ore; (2) crushing to appropriate size; (3) mixing with charcoal fuel; (4) achieving temperatures above 1,000 degrees C in a crucible, which requires forced-draft bellowing through reed or bone tubes; (5) recognizing the pour point; (6) understanding the cooling behavior. Each step is a piece of empirical knowledge accumulated across generations. The chapter should show at least steps 3-5 at the level of what the smelter's body is doing. This is not "they had metallurgy." This is a man blowing through a tube until his face is burning and the stone in the crucible begins to change color, and he knows, from his teacher, that when the color shifts from dark to bright he must blow harder, and when the bright becomes liquid he must stop, and the stopping is a judgment call that cannot be written down.

**Pottery at method level:** White cross-lined ware is hand-built (no wheel). The coiling technique requires consistent clay preparation (levigation, temper addition), even wall thickness through hand pressure, and controlled firing in open pits where the atmosphere (oxidizing vs. reducing) determines the final color. The white lime paint is applied after firing. The chapter should show the potter's hands: the roll of the clay coil, the smoothing of the join, the thinning of the wall by pressing from inside and outside simultaneously.

**The palette as overlooked object:** My PANEL-1 question stands. The cosmetic palette may be more central than the copper awl to Naqada I culture. The palette connects eye-paint cosmetics, ochre procurement, mortuary practice (palettes are among the most common grave goods), and the visual-material culture of the period. It becomes the medium for political imagery in Naqada II-III (the ceremonial palettes). Consider whether the palette should receive its own passage.

## Warning

Do not overstate the copper economy. Copper tools are present but rare in Naqada I contexts. The dominant tools are flint. The chapter should be honest about this: copper is a new material, not yet a dominant one. Its significance is that it represents a new kind of knowledge, not that it has replaced the old kind.
