# Figures — The Great Expansion

## Kautai (Composite Navigator)

Kautai is a composite figure. The navigational details attributed to him in this chapter draw from multiple sources: David Lewis's documentation of Polynesian navigation practices in *We, the Navigators* (1972), Thomas Gladwin's *East is a Big Bird* (1970, focused on Carolinian navigation but applicable to the broader tradition), and Ben Finney's experimental voyaging work with the Hokule'a. The name Kautai is a Tongan name, and the figure is placed in the expansion period (c. 1200-1500 CE), but no specific historical navigator of that name is documented in the sources available for this era.

What is documented: that Tongan navigators existed, that they sailed the routes described, that their knowledge encompassed the techniques depicted (swell reading, star navigation, bird observation, cloud interpretation), and that the training was transmitted through long apprenticeships within specialized families. The composite allows the chapter to render the knowledge system at the level of practice rather than at the level of summary. The grandfather teaching scene is drawn from multiple ethnographic accounts of navigational training across Polynesia and Micronesia, where the elder-to-youth transmission through physical proximity and guided observation is consistently attested.

Kautai's interiority is inferred, not documented. What he felt, what he thought, how he experienced the night sky and the swells beneath the hull, the record does not say. What the record does say is how navigators trained and how they practiced, and from these accounts a navigator's experience can be reconstructed with care and with this disclosure.

## The Tu'i Tonga Fefine

The Tu'i Tonga's sister, whose sacred status exceeded even the paramount chief's. Her person was the most tapu in the Tongan system. Her children outranked the Tu'i Tonga's own children, creating a matrilineal thread of highest sacredness running alongside the patrilineal succession of political authority.

The specific Tu'i Tonga Fefine of the expansion period is not named in this chapter because the genealogical traditions do not preserve individual names for the office with the same certainty as they do for the Tu'i Tonga himself. The figure represents the office and its function: presiding over the highest-level exchanges, sanctifying the transfer of fine mats and tabua, embodying the sacred authority that the network transmitted. Her presence in the chapter is structural rather than individual, and this is honest: the record preserves the office more clearly than the officeholders.

## Tu'itatui (The Eleventh Tu'i Tonga)

Oral tradition names Tu'itatui as the Tu'i Tonga who extended Tongan influence most aggressively into Fiji, establishing chiefly lines in the Lau Islands that persisted for centuries. He is also traditionally credited with the construction of the Ha'amonga 'a Maui, the coral limestone trilithon that still stands on Tongatapu.

The dating is uncertain. Genealogical estimates place him in the thirteenth or fourteenth century CE, but the margin of uncertainty spans a century or more. His specific acts, beyond the expansion into Fiji and the monumental construction, are preserved in oral tradition rather than in written or archaeological record. The chapter attributes to his period the broader patterns that the archaeological evidence supports: intensified contact with Fiji, elaboration of the langi at Mu'a, and the network's greatest geographic extent.

## Epeli Hau'ofa (1939-2009)

Tongan-Fijian scholar whose essay "Our Sea of Islands" (1993) reframed the Pacific from the inside. Hau'ofa argued that the European vision of small islands in a vast empty sea was a colonial construction that diminished Pacific peoples' understanding of their own world. The Pacific islanders, he wrote, had always lived in a sea of islands, a connected world where the ocean was road and resource and home, not barrier and void.

Hau'ofa is used in the chapter's landing not as an academic source but as a voice from within the culture articulating what the navigators had always practiced. His framing is the culture's own self-understanding given academic expression, and the chapter treats it as such: not a thesis applied from outside but a statement of what was already known.

## The Etak Concept

The moving-island reference frame described in Section III is documented most richly from the Caroline Islands of Micronesia, particularly through the work of Thomas Gladwin and later researchers working with the master navigator Mau Piailug. The concept, known as etak in the Carolinian tradition, is attributed in this chapter to the broader Polynesian navigational tradition rather than specifically to the Tongan system, because the Tongan-specific documentation is less detailed than the Micronesian. The broader Polynesian tradition shares the same navigational principles, and the reference-frame concept is consistent with what is known about Polynesian navigation in general, but the chapter is explicit that the specific term and its fullest documentation come from Micronesian sources. This is an intellectual honesty choice, not a limitation of the chapter's argument.
