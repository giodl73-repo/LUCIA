# BOARD Review — B-1 Hélène Clastres
## Tupí-Guaraní Era 2: The Atlantic Corridor

**Reviewer role:** Tupí-Guaraní prophetism and Yvy Maraey specialist
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Domain Assessment

The chapter handles the Yvy Maraey tradition with a care I did not expect and that is, frankly, rare in narrative treatments of this material. The crucial decision — to render the Land Without Evil as a fact rather than a belief — is correct, and it is maintained throughout. When Potirava says "I don't know which of these is correct" about whether the land is across the water or further south, the chapter does not editorialize. The uncertainty about the land's location is held inside the tradition's own terms: not "perhaps it doesn't exist" but "people disagree about exactly which direction the fact is." This is precisely the tradition's own epistemological structure.

**On the karai's authority:** The chapter correctly distinguishes the karai from the chief. Tatupeba organizes the feast; Arapoty leads the ceremony. The chapter does not conflate these. It also correctly shows that the karai's authority is persuasive, not coercive — the community prepares, it is not commanded. This is the essential point that most treatments miss. Pierre and I spent years trying to get this distinction clear, and the chapter has it.

**On the ñe'ë:** The treatment of the ñe'ë is largely accurate. The description of the dawn ceremony as "reminding the ñe'ë of its name" is a fair rendering of the tradition's claim, though I would note that the ñe'ë-name relationship is more complex than the chapter indicates — the ñe'ë is the person's divine name, which is different from their social name, and the ceremony's purpose is to maintain the alignment between the divine name and the person who bears it. This distinction is not necessary for the chapter's purposes and I would not require it to be added; I note it for completeness.

**One correction required:** The chapter states that the karai's word could "put an entire community in motion" and describes this as happening within a week of Arapoty's address. The historical record — including the most detailed accounts in the Jesuit sources — suggests that karai migrations typically involve a period of deliberation and preparation measured in months, not days or a week. A week is plausible for the initial *decision* to prepare, but the preparation itself for a community migration of sixty-three people would require longer. I recommend the chapter revise the timeline to indicate weeks of preparation rather than one week, to remain consistent with the documented scale of these events.

**On the coastal tradition:** The chapter's treatment of the coastal Tupinambá's relationship to Yvy Maraey — that they locate it east across the ocean, that this is an adaptation of the inland tradition rather than a contradiction of it — is accurate. Potirava's account of the disagreement between "across the water" and "further south" reflects the actual state of the tradition at the coast, as documented in Léry (1578).

## Summary Judgment

Factually accurate with one timeline correction required. The tradition's cosmological structure is rendered with unusual fidelity. The distinction between karai and chief is correctly maintained. The treatment of the cannibalism ceremony is the most careful I have seen in a non-specialist text. No cultural authenticity concerns.

**Required correction:** Expand the preparation timeline from one week to several weeks.
**Recommended:** Retain the chapter's treatment of Yvy Maraey as fact; this is its most important achievement.
