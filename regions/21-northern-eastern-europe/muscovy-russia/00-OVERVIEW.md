# Москва — Третій Рим / Moskva — Tretiy Rim
# "Moscow — The Third Rome"

We rose from the Mongol shadow. Moscow in the thirteenth century was a minor principality in the forest zone north of the Oka River — younger than Novgorod, smaller than Vladimir, insignificant beside Tver. The Mongol conquest of Kyiv in 1240 destroyed the Kievan world and left the Rus principalities as tributaries of the Golden Horde. What Moscow understood, and what its rivals did not, was that the Horde could be managed — that a prince willing to collect taxes faithfully for the Mongol khans, to serve as an intermediary between the Horde and the other Rus princes, and to use that position to undercut his rivals would accumulate advantages that outlasted the Horde itself. When the Horde fragmented in the fifteenth century, Moscow was the Rus principality best positioned to fill the vacuum.

The claim of the Third Rome — first articulated by the monk Philotheus of Pskov around 1510 — placed Moscow's rise in cosmic context. Rome had fallen to the barbarians. Constantinople, the Second Rome, had fallen to the Ottoman Turks in 1453. Moscow, which had preserved Orthodox Christianity and whose princes had married into the Byzantine imperial family, was now the Third Rome — the final guardian of true Christian civilization. This was not merely ideology; it was a comprehensive theory of history that gave the Muscovite autocracy a divine mandate and its tsar — the word itself derived from *Caesar* — a sacred authority that no prince of the old Rus world had claimed. The Third Rome argument shaped Russian political theology for centuries.

Key places:
- **Moscow (Kremlin)** — the fortified center; rebuilt in Italian Renaissance style by Ivan III; the seat of the metropolitan and the grand prince
- **Vladimir** — the older center of the Vladimir-Suzdal principality; the Dormition Cathedral; the original home of the Vladimir icon
- **Sergiev Posad (Trinity Lavra of St. Sergius)** — Sergius of Radonezh's monastery; the spiritual center of Russian Orthodoxy
- **Novgorod** — absorbed 1478; its independence extinguished; its great bell (symbol of civic freedom) carried to Moscow

## What Defined Them

**Calculated collaboration and the accumulation of power.** The Moscow princes' relationship with the Golden Horde was the foundation of their rise. Ivan I Kalita ("Moneybags") collected taxes for the Horde from other Rus principalities and used the Horde's authority to suppress his rivals. This was collaboration — morally complex, politically shrewd. But the resources it generated allowed Ivan to purchase land, support monasteries, and build institutions that survived the Horde's decline. The Moscow strategy was patient accumulation rather than dramatic confrontation, and it worked because the Horde found a reliable Moscow more useful than an uncertain one.

**Sergius of Radonezh and the monastic revival.** While the princes were building political power, the monasteries were building spiritual authority. Sergius of Radonezh (c. 1314–1392), the hermit who founded the Trinity Monastery north of Moscow, transformed Russian Orthodox spiritual life: his emphasis on inner contemplation, communal monastic life, and personal holiness created a model that spread through hundreds of daughter monasteries across northern Russia. When Dmitry Donskoy sought Sergius's blessing before the battle of Kulikovo in 1380 — the first significant defeat of Mongol-aligned forces by Russian troops — the monk's support gave the military campaign a spiritual legitimation that resonated for centuries. The Moscow state and the Russian Church grew together.

**The Third Rome and the autocratic idea.** The fall of Constantinople in 1453 left Moscow as the only remaining Orthodox Christian state of any significance. The marriage of Ivan III to Sophia Palaiologina, niece of the last Byzantine emperor, allowed the Muscovite princes to claim a biological connection to the Byzantine imperial line. The adoption of the double-headed eagle as the Muscovite emblem, the rebuilding of the Kremlin by Italian architects who gave it a Roman-Byzantine grandeur, and finally the articulation of the Third Rome doctrine: these were the elements of a comprehensive claim to Byzantine succession. The autocratic power of the tsar was not tyranny — it was the divinely ordained form of government appropriate to the guardian of the last true Christian civilization.

**Novgorod and the end of republican Rus.** Novgorod — the great merchant republic of the north, governed by its own assembly (*veche*) and its elected officials — was the alternative to Moscow's model of centralized autocracy. When Ivan III absorbed Novgorod in 1478, he deported its leading families to central Russia, settled Moscow loyalists in their place, and most symbolically, carried away the great bell that hung in the marketplace and had called the citizens to their assembly. The bell was the voice of republican self-governance. Moscow could not allow that voice to remain. The choice between the Moscow model and the Novgorod model — between autocracy and republicanism — was decided in 1478, and the consequences ran forward into the twentieth century.

**The frontier and the Cossacks.** The Russian frontier moved south and east as the Horde fragmented. The steppe that had been the source of the Mongol invasion became, slowly, the zone of Russian expansion. The Cossacks — free warriors of the steppe, serving as the frontier guards of the Russian state in exchange for their independence — were the instrument of this expansion. Ivan IV's conquest of Kazan in 1552 and Astrakhan in 1556 broke the Tatar power on the Volga; the subsequent Yermak Expedition into Siberia (1581–1585) opened the entire Asiatic continent to Russian penetration. The Russia that would stretch to the Pacific was already implicit in the Muscovite momentum of the sixteenth century.

## The Chapters

**1 — Under the Mongol Yoke:** Ivan I Kalita rides to the camp of the Mongol khan to collect his patent as grand prince. He is the most powerful Rus ruler alive, and he is a tributary of the people who destroyed Kyiv. He does not find this humiliating. He finds it useful. The resources he will collect will outlast the power he is currently kneeling before.

**2 — Gathering the Rus Lands:** Dmitry Donskoy receives the blessing of Sergius of Radonezh and rides to the field of Kulikovo. He defeats the Mongol-aligned army of Mamai. It is not yet the end of the Mongol domination — the Horde will raid Moscow two years later. But something has changed. The Rus princes know now that the Mongols can be beaten.

**3 — The Liberation:** Ivan III "stands on the Ugra" in 1480 — the two armies face each other across the river, and neither advances. The Mongol khan eventually withdraws. No battle is fought. Ivan III declares this the end of the Mongol yoke. The declaration is more important than any battle could have been.

**4 — The Crowning as Tsar:** Ivan IV's coronation in 1547 is designed by Metropolitan Macarius to echo the Byzantine imperial coronation. The sixteen-year-old Ivan is anointed with holy oil, given the orb and scepter, and crowned with the Cap of Monomakh — the crown said to have been sent by the Byzantine emperor. He is now not a grand prince but a tsar, a Caesar, a sacred emperor. The weight of this claim will define and eventually crush him.

**5 — Ivan's Terror:** Ivan IV stares at the list of names his *oprichniki* have brought him. Men he trusted, men his father trusted, men who have served the Russian state for thirty years. He adds names. He crosses names out. He adds them back. The autocracy he has inherited from three generations of patient Muscovite power-building is now indistinguishable from paranoia. He cannot tell the difference either.
