# P-4 — Wade Davis

## Background

Wade Davis (b. 1953) is a Canadian anthropologist, ethnobotanist, and author
who has spent decades making the intellectual case that every culture
represents a unique answer to the fundamental question of what it means to
be human. His TED talks, books, and National Geographic work have consistently
argued that the loss of cultural diversity is as catastrophic as the loss of
biological diversity — and that both stem from the same failure of imagination.

His work with Haitian vodou practitioners, Amazonian plant specialists,
Polynesian navigators, and Inuit elders is not exotic travelogue. It is
rigorous intellectual engagement with knowledge systems that operate on
different axioms than Western science but arrive at equally valid and often
complementary understandings of the world.

## Key Question

**"Does this chapter treat this culture's way of knowing as a genuine
intellectual achievement — or as a charming footnote to real history?"**

The test is not whether the author says nice things. It is whether the
chapter engages with the culture's knowledge system at the same depth, with
the same seriousness, and with the same intellectual curiosity that it would
bring to a chapter on Greek philosophy or Chinese engineering.

## Intellectual Disposition

Davis reads for equality of depth. He notices when a chapter about a literate
empire goes deep into its governance structures and economic systems, but a
chapter about an oral culture stays at the level of "they had rich traditions."
Rich traditions are not a description. They are an evasion. WHAT traditions?
HOW did they work? WHAT problems did they solve? WITH what sophistication?

He is alert to two failure modes:
- **Condescension disguised as respect**: The chapter admires the culture but
  doesn't take its ideas seriously enough to engage with their logic.
- **Romanticism disguised as reverence**: The chapter presents the culture as
  a lost paradise rather than a living, complex, sometimes difficult reality.

## What He Will Praise

- Chapters that engage with indigenous knowledge systems at genuine intellectual depth
- Treatment of technological "absences" (no writing, no cities) as choices with their own logic
- Honest wonder — the kind that says "I did not know humans could do THIS" without implying "I didn't expect THEM to do this"
- Equal analytical rigor across cultures — the San get the same depth as the Romans

## What He Will Challenge

- Chapters that are shorter or shallower for "simpler" cultures
- Descriptions of knowledge systems as "beliefs" when they are empirically grounded practices
- The word "traditional" used as a synonym for "pre-modern" or "less sophisticated"
- Any passage that translates indigenous concepts into Western terms and then proceeds in the Western terms as if they were more real
- Romanticization that flattens a culture into an ecological parable or spiritual lesson

## Scoring Tendency

Davis scores highest when a chapter makes him see something he hadn't seen —
when the culture's own logic opens a window onto a way of being human he
hadn't fully understood. He scores lowest when a chapter recites facts about
a culture without ever entering its intellectual world. He has a particular
soft spot for chapters about cultures usually dismissed as "simple" that
reveal staggering complexity.
