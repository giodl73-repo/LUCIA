## Domain Assessment

**Musical accuracy — overall:** Sound. The chapter navigates the contested origins of the tango with appropriate scholarly hedging while maintaining narrative momentum.

**Specific musical notes:**

1. **The tango's African roots:** The chapter's treatment of the candombe connection is accurate and appropriately hedged. The candombe-tango relationship is documented in musicological scholarship (Nketia, Collier, Savigliano) and the chapter correctly notes that the transmission mechanism is more established than the specific claims. The phrasing "carries the candombe's structural logic" is defensible — the 2/4 base, the syncopated phrasing, and the anticipation patterns in early tango recordings show clear structural affinity with candombe forms.

2. **Ángel Villoldo:** The characterization is correct. "El Choclo" (1903) was one of the first documented tango compositions with a known composer. Villoldo's mobility between social worlds is well-established in Simon Collier's work. The chapter correctly notes he was a middle-class performer, not a conventillo figure, which is an important clarification for authenticity.

3. **The bandoneón:** The instrument arrived in Buenos Aires from Germany in the late 1870s through maritime trade, initially associated with small religious communities in the interior (Lutherans) before entering the port culture. Its transition from hymn accompaniment to tango instrument is one of the more remarkable cultural repurposings in music history. The chapter's note that it was "designed for hymns in small churches" is accurate and is a detail worth expanding in the chapter — the specific quality of the bandoneón's sound (the reeds speaking through a leather bellow, each button producing a different note on push and pull) is what made it perfect for the tango's alternation between advance and retreat.

4. **Carlos Gardel, 1917 recording:** Gardel's 1917 Odeón session did produce early tango recordings, though his earlier recordings (including with the duo Gardel-Razzano) predate 1917. The chapter's characterization of him recording at Odeón in 1917 is accurate; this was indeed a significant session.

**The Paris transmission question:** The chapter honestly notes this is underdocumented. The scholarly literature (Savigliano, *Tango and the Political Economy of Passion*, 1995) is the best available source. The Argentine community in Paris was primarily associated with the Beaux-Arts architecture students and diplomatic circles; Savigliano traces the tango's Paris entry through these networks. The chapter's handling ("musicians traveling between Buenos Aires and the French capital") is consistent with available evidence.

**The milonga as social technology:** This characterization is not standard in musicological literature but is not wrong. The tango's bodily communication system — the proposal-and-response structure of the close embrace — has been discussed in ethnomusicological terms by Cynthia Novack and in sociological terms by Julie Taylor (*Paper Tangos*). The chapter's intuition is correct; the framing as "technology" is a useful way to describe what is usually discussed as "technique."

**Required corrections:**

None. The musicological content is sound. The candombe thread is appropriately bounded. The Villoldo characterization is accurate.
