# Yamato — The Kami and the Imperial Line

We are Yamato, the early Japanese state, and we hold the most continuous royal lineage in the world. Our Emperor — the Tennō, the Heavenly Sovereign — is the living descendant of the sun goddess Amaterasu, through the first earthly emperor Jimmu, in an unbroken chain that the tradition traces to the age of the gods (kamiyo) before human time began. This is not mythology that we hold separately from history; it is the framework within which history operates. The Tennō's performance of the correct sacred rites — especially the Daijōsai, the first harvest ceremony of a new emperor — maintains the divine protection over Japan and the proper order between the heavenly and earthly realms.

We are also the state that absorbed Buddhism and Chinese institutional knowledge in the sixth and seventh centuries and used both to build the Ritsuryō state — a centralized imperial governance system modeled on Tang China but distinctly Japanese in its articulation. Prince Shōtoku Taishi, who governed as regent in the early seventh century, composed the Seventeen-Article Constitution (a set of moral injunctions for governance rather than a legal code), negotiated with the Sui dynasty, and embraced Buddhism with a comprehensiveness that made it central to Yamato state ideology. We did not simply receive Buddhism; we made it ours.

## What Defined Them

**The kami world.** The world of the kami — the sacred presences that inhabit nature, objects, ancestors, and exceptional individuals — was not a religion separate from daily life but the fabric of the world itself. Mountains, rivers, trees, and the deceased ancestors were all animate with kami. The Tennō's role was to maintain proper relations with the kami through correct ritual — especially the harvest festivals — ensuring the seasonal renewal of life that the country depended on. Shinto was not named "Shinto" until Buddhism arrived and required a distinction; before that, it was simply the way the world was.

**The kofun — imperial burial mounds.** The great keyhole-shaped burial mounds (kofun) that define the Kofun period are the most visible expression of early Yamato power. The Daisen Kofun (attributed to Emperor Nintoku) is the largest mausoleum by area in the world — larger than the Great Pyramid. These mounds required the organized labor of tens of thousands of people and expressed a conception of royal authority that transcended the individual ruler: the mound was the Tennō's permanent cosmic presence in the landscape.

**Buddhism as state ideology.** The introduction of Buddhism from Baekje Korea in the 538 (or 552) CE created the most productive cultural tension in Japanese history: the kami world and the dharma world, the this-worldly powers of the local sacred and the universal truth of the Buddhist teaching. The conflict between the Mononobe clan (who opposed Buddhism as foreign kami) and the Soga clan (who embraced it) ended with the Soga's victory and the state's adoption of Buddhism. But the two traditions never fully synthesized and never fully separated, producing the distinctive Japanese capacity to hold both simultaneously.

**Prince Shōtoku's vision.** Prince Shōtoku Taishi (574–622 CE), who governed as regent for Empress Suiko, is the founding figure of Japanese Buddhist civilization. His Seventeen-Article Constitution (604 CE) instructed officials in the moral principles of governance; his diplomatic letter to the Sui Emperor famously began "from the Son of Heaven of the land where the sun rises to the Son of Heaven of the land where the sun sets" — asserting equality with the Sui, not subordination. He established four monasteries in the Yamato area, patronized Buddhist scholarship, and personally lectured on Buddhist sutras.

**The Ritsuryō state.** The administrative and legal codes (ritsu for criminal law, ryō for civil administration) promulgated in the late seventh and early eighth centuries modeled the Japanese state on the Tang dynasty's institutional forms: a court bureaucracy with graded ranks, a land distribution system (handen shūju), a conscript army, a national census. This was the most systematic adoption of continental models in Japanese history, and it worked — for a while. The Nara period state was a genuine centralized empire. The pressures that would eventually fragment it were already building.

## The Chapters

**1 — Kofun:** Workers are piling earth for the mound. The calculation is already done: the mound will be 486 meters long. They are burying the Tennō as a cosmic presence in the landscape. Subsequent generations will believe he is still there.

**2 — Buddhism Arrives:** The Baekje king sends a Buddha image and sutras to the Yamato court. The Mononobe clan says: this is a foreign god; if we worship it, our own kami will be angry. The Soga clan says: all the continental states worship this; shall we alone be ignorant? The argument is settled by a plague and a battle. The Soga win.

**3 — Prince Shōtoku:** He is writing the Seventeen-Article Constitution. Article one: harmony is to be valued. He means this. He also means: stop the clan conflicts that are tearing the court apart. Buddhism and Chinese governance theory are his instruments of peace.

**4 — Taika Reform:** The Nakatomi no Kamatari and Prince Naka no Ōe have just killed the Soga chief. The coup is over. The reforms begin. Land is nationalized. Provincial governors are appointed by the court. The Tang model is being implemented. Japan is becoming the Japan that will produce Nara.

**5 — The Great Buddha:** The Tōdai-ji's central hall opens. Inside, the Vairocana Buddha — 15 meters high, the largest bronze casting in the world at this date — sits in the position of universal illumination. Empress Shōmu has given the order: every province must build a provincial temple. Japan is a Buddhist country.
