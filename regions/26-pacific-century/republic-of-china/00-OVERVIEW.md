# 中華民國 — Zhōnghuá Mínguó: The Republic of China

China was the Middle Kingdom. For centuries, the Qing dynasty organized the known world around the assumption that China was its center — the source of civilization, the measure of culture, the state to which all others paid tribute and from which all legitimacy flowed. Then, between 1839 and 1895, Britain, France, Russia, Japan, and the United States each forced China to sign "unequal treaties" — extraterritoriality, treaty ports, trading concessions, the surrender of Hong Kong and Taiwan and the north — that reduced the Qing to a semi-colonial status and destroyed the self-understanding that had organized Chinese civilization for a thousand years. The century of humiliation (百年屈辱 — bainian quru) is not merely a political complaint; it is the emotional substrate of modern Chinese nationalism, the wound around which the Chinese Communist Party has organized its legitimating narrative, and the experience that made May Fourth (1919) possible.

The Republic of China — declared on January 1, 1912, after the Xinhai Revolution that ended 2,000 years of imperial rule — was the first attempt to answer the question: what must China become in order to survive? The May Fourth Movement of 1919 — the student protests following the Paris Peace Conference's decision to award German concessions in Shandong to Japan rather than returning them to China — crystallized the answer that the radical intelligentsia was developing: China must transform itself culturally, not just politically. The Confucian tradition, the classical language, the family system — all of it must be examined and, where necessary, discarded. "Mr. Democracy and Mr. Science" were the slogans. The civil war between the Nationalist Party (Kuomintang, KMT) and the Communist Party that followed was, among other things, a war about which answer to the May Fourth question was correct.

Key places:
- **Shanghai** — the treaty port; the cosmopolitan city where capitalism, communism, liberalism, and imperialism all competed in the 1920s–1930s; the setting for Eileen Chang's novels and the CCP's founding
- **Nanjing** — the KMT capital; the site of the 1937 massacre; the Nanjing Decade (1927–1937) as the Republic's best years
- **Chongqing** — the wartime capital; where the Republic retreated during the Japanese occupation; the alliance between KMT and CCP suspended here during the Anti-Japanese War
- **Taipei** — the capital of Taiwan since 1949; the city from which the Republic of China has governed Taiwan and maintained its claim to represent China for 75 years

## What Defined Them

**The May Fourth Question.** The May Fourth Movement (1919) is the founding moment of modern Chinese intellectual life. The students who gathered in Tiananmen Square to protest the Paris Peace Conference's betrayal were also arguing about a deeper question: what does China need to do to survive and flourish in the modern world? The radical answer — tear down Confucianism, abandon the classical language, adopt science and democracy — produced the New Culture Movement, the founding of the CCP, the generation of writers who reinvented Chinese literature (Lu Xun above all), and the long argument about Chinese identity that is still ongoing. The Republic of China's tragedy is that it asked this question at precisely the moment when Japan's invasion and then civil war made any coherent answer impossible to implement.

**The Taiwan question — the unresolved Chinese Civil War.** The retreat of the Nationalist government to Taiwan in 1949 did not end the Chinese Civil War; it suspended it. The Republic of China still officially claims sovereignty over mainland China (though this claim has become increasingly nominal). The People's Republic still officially claims Taiwan as a province of China. Neither claim is recognized as legitimate by the other side. The Taiwan Strait is one of the world's most dangerous flashpoints. The 23 million people of Taiwan have spent seventy-five years building a democracy, an identity, and a way of life that is increasingly distinct from mainland China while remaining profoundly Chinese in language, culture, and many social values. The question of what Taiwan is — and what it will be — is the most consequential unresolved question in the Pacific geopolitical order.

**The 228 Incident and the White Terror.** On February 28, 1947, a confrontation between Taiwanese civilians and KMT agents in Taipei triggered an island-wide uprising. The KMT response — the massacre of an estimated 18,000–28,000 Taiwanese, including most of the island's intellectual and professional elite — is known as the 228 Incident and is one of the darkest chapters in Taiwan's history. The White Terror that followed (1949–1987, the period of martial law) suppressed all political opposition, imprisoned tens of thousands of suspected communists or independence advocates, and prevented open discussion of the 228 Incident for four decades. The lifting of martial law in 1987, the democratization that followed, and the formal apology issued by President Lee Teng-hui in 1995 are the arc from that founding violence to the democratic society Taiwan has become.

**The Taiwan Miracle — democracy and economic development.** Taiwan's post-war economic development — from one of the poorest territories in Asia in 1945 to one of the wealthiest in the 1990s — is among the most striking achievements of the twentieth century. The agricultural reform of the 1950s (land to the tiller); the export-oriented industrialization strategy; the government's strategic investment in technology (TSMC, founded 1987, now produces more than half the world's leading-edge semiconductor chips); the investment in education — all of these produced a per-capita income that now rivals Japan and Western Europe. The parallel political transformation — from one-party authoritarian rule to the most robust democracy in the Chinese-speaking world — is equally remarkable. Taiwan is the proof that Chinese culture is compatible with democracy. This is not a neutral claim; in the context of mainland China's political system, it is a direct challenge.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Opium Wars:** 1839: the Qing Commissioner Lin Zexu destroys 1,200 tons of British opium in Canton. Britain goes to war over it. The Treaty of Nanjing (1842) that follows opens five treaty ports, cedes Hong Kong, and grants Britain most-favored-nation status. The dynasty that had regarded itself as the center of the world discovers that the world has different ideas. The century of humiliation has begun.

**2 — May Fourth:** May 4, 1919: thousands of students gather in Beijing's Tiananmen Square to protest the Treaty of Versailles's decision to award German concessions in Shandong to Japan. The protest spreads across the country. The students are demanding more than a revision of the peace treaty; they are demanding that China transform itself. "Down with Confucius and Sons" is one slogan. "Mr. Democracy and Mr. Science" is another. The argument about what China must become will not be resolved for a century.

**3 — Shanghai, 1927:** In a cellar on the Rue Molière in the French Concession, a woman named Xiang Jingyu is being interrogated by KMT agents. She is a founder of the Communist Party's women's movement. In the streets above her, KMT forces and the Green Gang are killing thousands of labor organizers and CCP members — the Shanghai Massacre that destroys the first KMT-CCP united front. She will be executed in May. The civil war that will not end until 1949 has effectively begun.

**4 — Chongqing, 1944:** The wartime capital is under Japanese bombardment. Chiang Kai-shek's government is governing from caves carved into the riverbank. In the city, American general "Vinegar Joe" Stilwell is furious at what he sees as Chiang's unwillingness to use the supplies the US is providing to fight the Japanese. Chiang is conserving his forces for the civil war he knows is coming. Both of them are right about what the other is doing. Neither can afford to say so.

**5 — 228:** February 28, 1947: a widow named Lin Jiang-mai is selling cigarettes without a license. KMT agents beat her and fire into the crowd that gathers to protest. Within days, the island is in revolt — fifty years of Japanese colonial rule have produced a Taiwanese population with more education, more economic development, and more political organization than the KMT officials who have arrived to govern them expect. The mainlanders' response is massacre. Taiwan's identity wound will not be openly acknowledged for forty years.

**6 — TSMC:** In a fabrication plant in Hsinchu, Taiwan, machines the size of buildings are producing semiconductor chips at a precision measured in nanometers. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company controls more than half the world's production of the most sophisticated chips — the chips in every iPhone, every AI server, every precision weapons system. The island that could not defend itself militarily against mainland China has made itself strategically indispensable to every country that matters. This is the Taiwan Miracle's latest and most consequential form.
