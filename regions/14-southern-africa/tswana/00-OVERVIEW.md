# Batswana — "The Great Towns"

The Tswana cities were, in the early nineteenth century, among the largest urban settlements in southern Africa — larger than Cape Town, larger than most European provincial cities of the same period. Dithakong, Kuruman, Molepolole, Kanye, Serowe — these were not simply large villages; they were urban centers with populations of 10,000–30,000, organized around a central kgotla (public assembly space) where political decisions were made, disputes adjudicated, and the community's collective life conducted in public view. The European missionaries and travelers who arrived in the early nineteenth century were not expecting cities. They found them anyway.

The Tswana — the Batswana, the people who speak Setswana — are Sotho-Tswana-speaking communities of the southern African highveld and its western extension toward the Kalahari. They were organized in independent chieftaincies (merafe, singular morafe) — the Kwena, the Ngwaketse, the Ngwato, the Tawana, the Kgatla, the Rolong, the Tlhaping — each with its own kgosi (chief) and its own morafe's great town. The towns were not simply population centers; they were the organizing principle of Tswana political life. The kgotla was the parliament, the court, the marketplace, and the public space through which the kgosi maintained accountability to his people and his people maintained their claim on him.

Key places:
- **Dithakong** — the Tlhaping great town; the first major Tswana city encountered by European missionaries
- **Molepolole** — the Kwena capital; one of the largest pre-colonial towns in southern Africa
- **Serowe** — the Ngwato capital; later famous as the birthplace of Seretse Khama
- **Mafikeng / Mafeking** — the contested frontier town; site of the famous Siege of Mafeking (1899–1900)

## What Defined Them

**The kgotla and democratic accountability.** The kgotla — the public assembly at the center of every Tswana town — was the institutional expression of the Tswana political philosophy that the kgosi rules through consultation and consent. Any adult male citizen could speak at the kgotla; the kgosi was expected to listen, to argue, and ultimately to build consensus. The kgosi who ruled autocratically without the kgotla's engagement was violating Tswana political norms, and the community had mechanisms — including the departure of dissatisfied lineages — to express its displeasure. The kgotla tradition is one of the foundations of Botswana's post-independence democratic culture, and the first post-independence president, Seretse Khama, explicitly drew on it.

**Urban scale and the town economy.** The scale of Tswana towns in the early nineteenth century was genuinely extraordinary by the standards of the southern African highveld. The population density, the organized craft production, the planned layout around the kgotla, the integration of cattle kraal and residential area — these were urban systems, not simply large villages. The Tswana town was also an economic center: iron-working, skin-working, brewing, pottery, and long-distance trade in cattle and ostrich feathers were organized around the town market. The European visitors who described these towns in the 1820s were often surprised by their scale; they should not have been surprised that urbanization occurred in Africa without European initiation.

**Khama III and the Christian Tswana state.** Khama III, kgosi of the Ngwato from 1875 to 1923, was one of the most influential African rulers of the late nineteenth century in southern Africa. He converted to Christianity under London Missionary Society influence, banned alcohol and the sale of land to European settlers, and maintained a relatively prosperous and stable Ngwato state in the difficult colonial period. His 1895 mission to London — traveling with two other Tswana chiefs (Sebele I and Bathoen I) to lobby against incorporation into Cecil Rhodes's British South Africa Company — was a remarkable exercise in African diplomatic agency. The three chiefs made their case directly to Joseph Chamberlain (the Colonial Secretary) and to the British public, secured a favorable outcome, and returned home with the Bechuanaland Protectorate's independence from Rhodes's company intact.

**The Bechuanaland Protectorate and the apartheid exemption.** The British Protectorate of Bechuanaland, established in 1885, was never incorporated into the Union of South Africa — partly because of Tswana diplomatic resistance, partly because British officials were reluctant to hand such a large territory to the South African settler state. This decision, maintained through the apartheid period, meant that Bechuanaland/Botswana remained outside the apartheid system and became independent in 1966 under Seretse Khama as one of Africa's more successful post-independence democracies. The kgotla tradition and the Khama dynasty's political culture both contributed to this outcome.

**Setswana and the language of the highveld.** Setswana is one of the most widely spoken languages in southern Africa, used across Botswana, parts of South Africa, Zimbabwe, and Namibia. The London Missionary Society's decision to use Setswana as the primary medium of Christian communication in the region — producing the first Setswana Bible in 1857 — was a consequential choice that gave Setswana literacy a head start over neighboring languages and cemented its role as a regional lingua franca. The literary tradition that emerged — missionary translations, school texts, eventually creative literature — is one of the foundations of Botswana's educational and cultural life.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Great Town:** A European traveler arrives at Dithakong in 1820 and counts the houses. He has been counting for an hour. The city is larger than anything he expected to find. He revises his notes. He will publish an account that European readers will find implausible.

**2 — The Kgotla:** The kgosi of the Kwena is sitting at the kgotla. A dispute about cattle has been brought. Both parties are speaking. The elders are listening. The kgosi will speak last. What he says will be the community's decision. This is not theater; this is governance.

**3 — The Ndebele Raid:** A Ndebele raiding party has come out of the north. The town has been partially destroyed. The great cattle kraal is empty. The Tswana community is regrouping, counting survivors, calculating what can be rebuilt. This has happened before. They begin rebuilding.

**4 — Khama in London:** Khama III is meeting Joseph Chamberlain in Whitehall. He is polite, formal, precise. He has already spoken at a dozen public meetings across Britain; the newspapers are covering his visit. The British public has discovered that the Tswana chiefs are not what colonial accounts have made them seem. The diplomatic pressure is working.

**5 — Independence Day:** 1966. Seretse Khama is inaugurated as the first president of Botswana. He invokes the kgotla. He has been denied this moment for years — the British government and his own uncle tried to prevent his return to Bechuanaland because of his marriage to a white English woman. He is here anyway. The kgotla has spoken.
