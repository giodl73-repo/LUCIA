## B-3 — Jan Jansen — Board Review of "The Songhai Succession"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Mande political culture, the Kurukan Fuga as social charter, social categories in Mande society, the septennial ceremony at Kangaba, and fieldwork-based understanding of how Mande communities understand their own political traditions.

**Mande political philosophy accuracy:**

- The chapter's central analytical framework — the Kurukan Fuga as a redistribution covenant that made the mansa through generosity and that could not sustain the empire when the resources for generosity diminished — is a sound reading of Mande political philosophy. The covenant does indeed bind through redistribution rather than through coercion, and the chapter correctly traces the implications of this mechanism for the empire's contraction.

- The distinction between the covenant as social structure and the covenant as imperial mechanism is important and correctly drawn. The Kurukan Fuga regulated relationships between horon, nyamakalaw, and jon before it regulated relationships between the mansa and tributary states. The chapter's observation that "the covenant was social before it was imperial" is consistent with current scholarly understanding and with what the jeli tradition itself emphasizes.

- The sanankuya are correctly identified and briefly described. The joking relationships between specific clans are indeed a mechanism for defusing conflict and maintaining alliance, and they persist independently of the empire's territorial extent. This is accurate.

- The Kamabolon ceremony description is accurate. The seven-year cycle, the Diabate lineage's custodianship, the roof renewal as covenant renewal — these details are consistent with my published fieldwork on the ceremony at Kangaba and Kela.

- The social categories (horon, nyamakalaw, jon) are present in the chapter and treated as living categories rather than anthropological labels. The chapter does not apologize for them or translate them into modern Western equivalents. This is correct practice.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The refusal to frame the contraction as "fall" or "failure" is not just good narrative practice — it is consistent with how the Mande world understands its own history. The jeli tradition does not carry a narrative of decline. It carries a genealogy that continues. The chapter aligns with the culture's own historiographic framework.

- The observation that the Kurukan Fuga "did not break" during the contraction is analytically precise. The covenant's terms continued to be observed in the Mande heartland. What changed was the scale at which the covenant operated, not the covenant itself.

- The chapter correctly notes that the ceremony at Kangaba continued "through the colonial era when the French administrators who controlled the territory did not understand what was happening in the sacred house and did not need to, because the ceremony was not for them." This is historically accurate and captures the relationship between the colonial administration and Mande political-religious institutions.

**What needs attention:**

- The chapter uses "the Kurukan Fuga had no provision for this" and similar phrasings that risk implying the covenant was a designed system with identifiable design flaws. The Kurukan Fuga is not a constitution in the Western legal sense; it is a covenant that is continually reinterpreted and adapted through performance. The jeli tradition does not treat the Kurukan Fuga as a fixed document that can have "provisions" or lack them. The chapter should be careful not to reify the covenant into a static legal code. The analytical point (the covenant's logic could not sustain imperial scale without resources) is sound; the framing should avoid suggesting the covenant was supposed to anticipate all contingencies.

- The chapter says the mansa "sat on the raised platform." In Mande political culture, the mansa's position in the assembly has specific significance that varies by context. The physical arrangement of the court is not just scenery; it encodes political relationships. If the chapter is going to describe the mansa's physical position, it should be aware that this carries meaning within the Mande framework. Not an error, but a place where deeper specificity would serve the culture.

**Verdict:** APPROVED

The chapter's treatment of Mande political philosophy is accurate and rendered from inside the system. The covenant's logic is correctly understood as operating at two scales (social and imperial), and the contraction is correctly framed as a change in scale rather than a failure of the system. The Kamabolon ceremony and the jeli tradition are handled with the accuracy I would expect from a text that takes the Mande world seriously on its own terms.
