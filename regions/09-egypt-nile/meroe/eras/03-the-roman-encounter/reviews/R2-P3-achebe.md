## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — PANEL-2 Review of Notes

**Focus:** Roman source handling in the section plan

**Assessment:** The twin-source strategy is sound. But the danger is real. Sections 2 and 4 rely heavily on Roman literary sources for their narrative content, and the pull toward Roman framing will be strongest there.

**Specific guidance:**
- In Section 2 (The Strike), the campaign narrative will draw on Strabo. The risk: "Petronius sent a force of..." becomes the chapter's grammar, with the Meroites as the objects of Roman military verbs. Reverse the grammar. The Meroites move south. They take the garrisons. The Romans are the objects of Meroitic military verbs.
- In Section 4 (The Negotiation), the treaty at Samos is known primarily from Roman accounts. The risk: the negotiation is framed as a Roman decision to grant terms, with the kandake as a petitioner. Reverse the frame. The kandake sends envoys because she has achieved her objectives and has no interest in holding Egyptian territory. She is closing a campaign, not seeking mercy.
- The Strabo passage about Amanirenas's lost eye: "andreia" (masculine courage) is Strabo's word. The chapter should note that this is a Roman's attempt to fit a powerful woman into a Roman category. The Meroites had their own category for her: kandake. No Greek translation was adequate.

**One more thing:** The notes mention that Augustus is "present only as the bronze head and as the distant emperor at Samos." This is correct and important. Augustus must not become a character in this chapter. He is an object (the head) and a distant authority (at Samos). The kandake is the character. If Augustus gains interiority, the chapter's center of gravity shifts to Rome.
