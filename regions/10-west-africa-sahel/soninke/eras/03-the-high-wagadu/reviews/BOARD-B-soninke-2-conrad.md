# Board Review — B-soninke-2 (David Conrad)
## The High Wagadu

**Role:** Oral tradition accuracy, Dausi handling, source balance
**Date:** 2026-04-17

## Oral Tradition Review

**The Dausi's structure:** The chapter correctly represents the four-Wagadus cycle — four rises and falls, each associated with a failure of virtue, each potentially reversible because Wagadu is a condition rather than a place. The chapter does not flatten this into a simple decline narrative. It holds the Dausi's own cyclical understanding: the fifth Wagadu is promised, the tradition does not close into defeat.

**The Bida covenant:** The chapter's handling of the Bida legend is accurate in its essentials. The divine serpent in the well, the annual offering, the covenant's guarantee of gold and rain — these are the core elements the Dausi preserves across its variants. The chapter correctly notes that "the Dausi does not give coordinates" for the well — the tradition preserves the structure, not the location.

**Siya Yatabere:** The chapter renders this figure with the appropriate ambiguity the Dausi holds. The tradition does not simply condemn Siya Yatabere — it presents his act as comprehensible, and the chapter follows this: "The tradition holds both: that what he felt was comprehensible, and that what he did was catastrophic." This is the right reading. The Dausi is not a morality tale with a verdict; it is a narrative that holds the complexity.

**The four virtues/failures:** The chapter references the cycle correctly: "Four times founded. Four times destroyed. Each time by a failure within: vanity, falsehood, greed, dissension." The specific virtue associated with this era (dissension, or some versions say greed) is not explicitly named in the chapter; the chapter prefers the general cycle. This is a defensible choice — the variant versions do not agree on which virtue corresponds to which destruction, and the chapter avoids false precision.

**Source balance between Dausi and Al-Bakri:** The twin-source narration technique is well-executed. The Dausi provides the interpretive framework (why the covenant broke, what the breaking meant); Al-Bakri provides the material record (what the court looked like, what the tax rates were, who the king was). Neither source subordinates the other. When they describe the same phenomena from different angles — the annual offering appears in both, the covenant's breaking appears in the Dausi while the Almoravid disruption appears in the written record — the chapter places them as two lenses without claiming one is more reliable than the other.

**The jeli's role:** The chapter identifies the Dausi as "versions that survive in the memory of the jeli who carry it." This is the correct ontology: the tradition is carried, not preserved. It is alive in its keepers, not frozen in a text.

## One Observation

The chapter focuses on the Bida covenant in its High Wagadu period form. The longer genealogy of the Dausi — the earlier Wagadus, the virtues of silence and honor — is not addressed in this chapter, which is appropriate given the chapter's temporal scope (700–1076 CE). The Era 2 chapter (First Wagadu) would carry that material. The chapter correctly scopes to the high period and its terminal condition.

## Corrections Required

None. The handling of the Dausi is accurate, respectful of the tradition's own internal logic, and balanced correctly against the Arabic written record.

## Endorsement

The chapter treats the Dausi as a historiographic source with its own methods and its own relationship to truth. It does not use the oral tradition as decoration for the Arabic record; it uses it as an independent knowledge system that preserves what the Arabic record cannot — the Soninke understanding of their own history. This is the correct approach and the chapter executes it with genuine care.

**Cultural authenticity: ENDORSED.**
