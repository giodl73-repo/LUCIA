# R2-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński (PANEL-2)
## Elam Era 1 | Notes Review

The notes are a plan. Plans do not have smells. My job is to make sure the chapter does.

The notes say: "Render Chogha Zanbil (or the Susa ziggurat precinct) at the level of physical presence. Scale, mass, the shadow it casts in the morning light. The archive's smell — clay and what else?" This is correct as a note to self. But a note saying "put the reader in the physical space" is not the same as doing it. Let me be specific about what the physical reality of Susa in the early second millennium BCE would have offered to the senses.

**Susa in the sukkalmah period:**
- The ziggurat precinct would have smelled of bitumen (used as mortar), river mud (the bricks were made from it), and the specific burnt-wood smell of the fires that fired the bricks. Also: animal sacrifice, incense, and the human smell of the workers — hundreds of them — who maintained the precinct.
- The Khuzestan plain in summer is brutal: 45-50°C possible at peak. The shade of the ziggurat's eastern face in morning would have been one of the few reliefs. Scribes likely worked in the early morning hours when the clay could still be handled without it drying too fast.
- The Karun River, which passes near Susa, floods in spring. The chapter set in this period should feel the relationship between the seasonal flood and the grain accounting — the harvest that follows the flood, the calculation of what is owed to the god from what the flood made possible.

**The Lament for the Destruction of Ur:**
The notes plan to use this text. I want the chapter to quote it in a way that the reader feels where they are when they hear it. The Lament was sung — performed — not read silently. It was a public mourning ritual. The sound of it, in a city that had been destroyed, would have been heard against rubble and ash. If the chapter uses the Lament, it should be felt as sound against the specific physical background of a devastated city. Do not use it as a quotation in an archive. Use it as sound in a ruined street.

**What the chapter must give me that the notes promise but do not yet deliver:**

One moment of genuine physical presence in Anshan — the other pole. We have Susa throughout. But Anshan, in the Fars highlands, is a different physical world: altitude, thinner air, the smell of pine resin that does not exist on the plain, the different quality of light at elevation. The highland and the lowland must feel different, or the dual kingdom is only a political concept rather than an experienced reality.
