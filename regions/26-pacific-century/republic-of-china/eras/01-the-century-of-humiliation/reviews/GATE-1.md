## Notes

**Pass status:** The opening passes GATE-1 comfortably. The scene-setting is masterful; the cultural perspective is genuinely inside. The voice is strong and mostly sustained.

**Voice notes:** The phrase "something had changed in the calculus of the world" is the only moment where the register shifts slightly upward in abstraction. The rest of the opening maintains the careful, observational tone of a scholar-administrator documenting what he has witnessed. This is a single instance and does not break the overall coherence.

**Strengths for the full chapter:**
- The opening establishes the puzzle that will drive the chapter: how a civilization discovered it could not enforce its own rules.
- Lin Zexu is a fully dimensional figure from the start—not a type, but a specific administrator with a method.
- The Qing worldview (tribute, examination, center-of-all-things) is embedded in the narrative without explanation.
- The ending achieves pull through specificity: "the century of humiliation has begun." Not a forecast, but a historical marker the reader now understands.

**Recommended direction for the full chapter:**
- The opening has established the crisis. The chapter should trace the unfolding of that crisis—the subsequent wars, the treaties that compound the damage, the intellectual response.
- Consider whether Lin Zexu's fate (exile, obscurity) will be developed. His removal from power after the treaty is a strong moment and could anchor later reflections.
- The "calculus" shift noted above should be monitored in the full chapter draft—not because it's a problem, but to maintain the opening's sustained register.
