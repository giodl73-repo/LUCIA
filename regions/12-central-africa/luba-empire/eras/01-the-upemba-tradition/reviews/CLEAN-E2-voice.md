**Stage:** CLEAN

---

## Baseline Voice (from opening)

The opening establishes: lacustrine accumulative. Rolling clauses that gather information the way a net gathers fish, broken by short declaratives that land with the weight of a catch pulled aboard. Sensory-grounded. First-person observational — the chapter stands inside the moment, not above it. Sentences run medium-long in the descriptive passages, short and declarative at moments of weight ("She hauled. The net broke the surface in a silver sheet."). No em-dashes in the opening. The rhythm is patient, seasonal, accumulative.

---

## Voice Scan — Full Chapter

**Section 1 (opening integrated):** Voice held throughout. The transition from the individual woman to the landscape description ("Lake Kisale sits in a shallow depression...") maintains the same register — the landscape is described as physical reality felt from within, not as geographical overview. The final line of the section ("She thought of the lake, the net, the furnace, the morning.") is a return to the individual that anchors the register shift back from landscape to person. Clean.

**Section 2 (The Furnace):** Voice held. The smelter's knowledge is rendered in his body — his thumbnail pressing the ore, his tongue wetting the surface, his eyes watching the bloom-color through the tuyere. The analytical passage about the exchange mechanism ("What moved between the plateau and the lakeshore was not simply goods. It was knowledge...") is in the same register as the sensory passages — accumulative, observational, without academic distance. One sentence at slight risk: "He did not know any of this as chemistry. He knew it as what worked and what did not." This is on the edge of explaining what the scene has already shown. Not a violation, but close to E-3 territory.

**Section 3 (What the Graves Know):** Voice held. The shift from narrative (the woman's morning) to archaeological (the burials) is the most technically demanding transition in the chapter, and it succeeds. The graves are described from within the community's perspective, not from the excavation's. "What did the community know about her?" — this question is asked from inside, not from outside. The passage about the lukasa boards introduces a future-looking element without breaking the chapter's temporal register. One minor observation: "the scholars who dug in the centuries beyond that" (in the closing section, not in Section 3) is a forward reference that briefly positions the reader outside the chapter's era; it is handled in the closing and is not a violation.

**Section 4 (The Network):** Voice held. The communal fishing rotation passage is the chapter's most analytical section and the most exposed to voice drift. It holds. The register stays observational and accumulative — the rules are described as practices, not as institutions. "The rules were not written. They were in the memory of the settlement's elders." This is the lacustrine voice carrying analytical weight in its own register. The passage about the copper handa is slightly denser than the surrounding sections but does not shift into academic register.

**Section 5 (What Is Being Made):** Voice held throughout. The long-time-scale section is the most ambitious — covering a thousand years in the chapter's register — and it succeeds by maintaining the seasonal frame (the rain cycle as the chapter's clock) and returning to the individual woman in the closing movement. The analytical observations in this section ("over a thousand years, this rhythm produced something the people living in it could not have seen") are in the chapter's own voice, not in an outside voice.
