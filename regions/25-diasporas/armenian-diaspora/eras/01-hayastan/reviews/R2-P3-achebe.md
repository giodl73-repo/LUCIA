# R2-P3 -- Achebe (Targeted Review of Notes)

**Chapter:** Hayastan -- The Ancient Kingdom
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Assessment of the Production Bible

The notes show cultural ownership throughout. The figures are drawn from Armenian sources. The structural engine belongs to the culture. The voice type is appropriate. I have two targeted concerns.

## Concerns

1. **Gregory the Illuminator.** The notes acknowledge that Agathangelos's account is "heavily legendary" and plan to present Gregory "through Armenian tradition's own voice." Good. But the notes also say the conversion had "geopolitical logic: Rome was moving toward Christianity; Persia was Zoroastrian." This is true, but it is an outside analyst's framing. The Armenian tradition does not understand the conversion as geopolitics. It understands the conversion as Gregory's miracle and Trdat's transformation. The chapter should present the conversion AS the tradition understands it (divine intervention, miraculous healing, the light), and let the geopolitical context emerge from the narrative situation (Armenia positioned between two empires) without the author announcing it as "the real reason."

2. **Tigran and source asymmetry.** The notes correctly identify that Tigran is known primarily through Greek and Roman sources. The planned technique -- "told through a Roman source describing what he saw, using source asymmetry" -- is sound. But the chapter must make the gap feel like a loss, not merely a methodological note. The Armenian Tigran is lost because no Armenian wrote about him in Armenian before the alphabet. This is the engine's NEGATIVE proof: without the script, the king belongs to someone else's history. Use the source asymmetry to reinforce the engine.

## Verdict

The notes are culturally sound. The conversion scene is the critical test. If Gregory is told from within, the chapter passes the Achebe Test. If the author's analysis is visible, it does not.
