## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of The Merchants of Ashur (Opening)

**Overall:** This is a letter that opens a world. The choice to begin with the document itself, in the document's own voice, is the best structural decision in the piece. I kept reading.

**What works:**
- The opening sentence drops the reader directly into a transaction without preamble. No context-setting, no "In the ancient city of Ashur..." The letter arrives, and you are there. This is how you earn attention.
- The paragraph on what the letters contain (para 4) is superb narrative inventory. "Complaints from those wives: you have been gone two harvests now and the children ask for you and the silver you promised has not arrived." That is a scene in a single sentence. The specificity does the work of characterization.
- Lamassatum as the closing figure is a strong choice. A named woman managing the Ashur end of an international trade network, arguing about agents and debts, with her daughter at the loom behind her. The reader wants to know more about her. That is pull.

**What doesn't:**
- The fifth paragraph ("This was Ashur before the empire") risks stepping outside the world to orient the reader in the larger Assyrian story. The references to "flayed skins," "colossal winged bulls," and "lion hunts" belong to a different chapter. The merchant world should be sufficient to itself without needing the later empire as contrast. The reader who picks this up as the first Assyria chapter does not yet know about those things, and the reader who knows about them does not need to be told.
- The second paragraph ("The man who dictated this letter") has a slight throat-clearing quality. "If you pressed him" and the parenthetical dating are author-voice, not world-voice. The opening letter established a register; this paragraph wobbles before the third paragraph recovers.

**The question I'd push on:**
Can the opening sustain itself without the fifth paragraph's forward reference to the empire? The strongest version of this piece would be a world so complete in its commercial intimacy that the reader never thinks about what comes next. The knowledge of what Ashur becomes should be the reader's private burden, not the text's declaration.
