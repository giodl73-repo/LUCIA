## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuscinski — Review of Hammurabi's Babylon Opening

**Overall:** The stone is warm. That is good. But I want more. I want to feel Bab-ilim. The opening gives me the stele and the courtyard, but not the city.

**What works:**
- "Black diorite, taller than a man." The stele is physically present. I can see it.
- "Standing in the open, where the courtyard caught the morning sun and the stone warmed under the hand of anyone who touched it." This is tactile. The reader's hand is on the stone.
- The merchant's trade goods are specific: tin from Ashur, textiles from Babylon, copper from the gulf. These are not generic "trade"; they are the actual commodities of the Old Babylonian period. I can see the bundles.

**What doesn't:**
- The Esagila courtyard is an empty stage. Where are the bricks? (Mud brick, not the stone the reader might imagine.) Where is the ziggurat rising behind the temple? Where is the canal that runs along the precinct wall? Where is the smell of the date palms, the burnt offerings on the altar, the dust rising from the unpaved street outside the gate? Babylon is a city of mud and water and dates and dust and bitumen, and the opening does not yet smell like any of them.
- The karum is mentioned but not experienced. A Babylonian karum is a specific thing: the walled commercial district outside the main city gate, where the trading houses have their own courtyards and the donkey caravans unload and the silver is weighed on balances in full view. The merchant who reads the stele came from this place. I want to feel the transition from the commerce of the karum to the sacredness of the Esagila.
- The heat. The Babylonian plain in summer is 45 degrees and the air does not move and the canal water is warm and green and the shade of the temple wall is the only shade. This heat is not optional. It is the physical reality of every scene set in Bab-ilim.

**The question I'd push on:**
Give me the city. The opening has the stele and the argument and the history. Now give me the mud brick walls, the canal, the date palms, the heat, the donkeys in the karum, the smoke from the temple altar. One paragraph of sensory grounding would transform this from an excellent argument into a place I have visited.
