# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Elam Era 1 | Opening Review

This opening does something I do not take for granted: it treats a knowledge system we cannot read as a knowledge system, not as a relic.

Proto-Elamite writing is one of the world's undeciphered scripts. There is a strong temptation, when writing about such things, to frame the undecipherment as the interesting part — to make the story about our failure to understand, rather than about their success in creating. This opening refuses that temptation. The scribe's script is not a mystery to be solved. It is a working system, adequate to its purposes, operating at a level of sophistication that the opening renders precisely: two numerical bases, trained scribal schools, a geographically distributed uniformity that implies institutional coordination across hundreds of kilometers.

What the opening correctly refuses to do is rank this knowledge system against Sumerian writing. The two systems are contemporaneous, and the opening treats them that way: parallel developments, each adequate to its own world. The Sumerian scribes are mentioned only to establish that the Elamite system was not derivative, not a borrowing, not a lesser version of something the Mesopotamians invented first. That judgment — which would be easy to slip in — is correctly omitted.

**Where I want more:**

The scribal knowledge system is well rendered at the level of *what it produced* — counted grain, recorded transactions, maintained the archive. But v1.5's Knowledge System at Method Level asks for more: show the knowledge system at the level of HOW IT WORKS. What was it like to learn proto-Elamite? What were the stages of scribal training? The opening mentions training for years and scribal schools, but does not put us in the learning. If even one moment — a young apprentice pressing his first tablet, the teacher correcting his marks — were present, the knowledge system would become visible at method level rather than just output level.

This is a note for the full chapter, not a problem with the opening. At 800 words, the opening cannot do everything. But the chapter must show the scribal tradition operating at the level of practice, not just producing its archive.

**The question I'd push on:**

The proto-Elamite script is distributed across a vast geographic range — Susa to what is now Afghanistan. What held that network together? Was there a scribal identity — a guild, a temple affiliation, a shared training tradition — that bound Elamite scribes in different cities to the same sign conventions? Or was the uniformity administrative, maintained by a redistributive center? This is a genuine intellectual question about how knowledge systems sustain coherence across distance, and it is exactly the kind of question the chapter should engage.
