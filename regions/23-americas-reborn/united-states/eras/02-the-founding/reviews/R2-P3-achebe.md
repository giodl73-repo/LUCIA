# Panel Review — R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe (PANEL-2)
## Notes review: The Founding — The Experiment Begins

**Focus: Cultural voice, naming, dignity, the paradox from both sides**

The notes have answered my PANEL-1 question about named figures. Richard Allen is correctly identified as the chapter's counter-voice. Sally Hemings is correctly identified and correctly handled — the acknowledgment of silence as evidence is the right move. Banneker is correctly placed at the close. The chapter has the people it needs.

**What I'm satisfied with:**
The Sally Hemings entry in the figures section is the most important thing the notes get right. "What she thought about this we do not know, because no record of her voice survives. The chapter must honor that silence." This is the discipline. The chapter must not ventriloquize her. It must not invent her interiority. It must not reduce her to a symbol of Jefferson's hypocrisy. She is a person whose life intersected with the founding paradox in the most intimate possible way, and the chapter must place her there and then acknowledge what it cannot know.

The Richard Allen entry is well-developed. The Free African Society in 1787 is the right parallel structure: the same year, the same city, the same principles, applied to a different population by that population itself. The chapter must present this not as resistance to the Convention but as coexistence with it — a parallel founding, happening simultaneously, with the same materials but for people who had not been fully included in the official founding.

**What I'd tighten:**
The notes describe Jefferson as "the founding paradox made flesh" and list his contradictions carefully. But the framing still centers Jefferson as the subject — the paradox is about what Jefferson held in tension. The chapter should also hold Sally Hemings as a subject in her own right, not merely as a mirror of Jefferson's contradiction. What was her situation in Paris? She was legally free there. She was fourteen. She had a choice, and she made one — to return to Virginia. The chapter can describe that choice without knowing what she was thinking. The choice itself is historical fact. The choice is the thing the chapter should render.

The "Other Philadelphia" section needs one more figure: Phyllis Wheatley had died in poverty in 1784, but her poems — a Black enslaved woman writing in the highest neoclassical style about liberty and the revolution — were still being read in 1787. She is not in Philadelphia, but she is part of the period's argument about who could claim the revolution's language.

**The question I'd push on:**
The chapter must not end with Banneker's letter to Jefferson as a moment of "the paradox in miniature" without also ending with what Richard Allen was doing in the same year (1791, when the Banneker letter was written). Allen was organizing the church. He was building the institution. Both are present in 1791: the public appeal to the republic's stated principles (Banneker) and the practical construction of a parallel civic life (Allen). The chapter's close should hold both, not just the letter.
