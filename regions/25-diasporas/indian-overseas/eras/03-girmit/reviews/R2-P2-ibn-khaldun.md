## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — PANEL-2 Review of Notes

**Focus:** System architecture and terminal condition.

**On the structural engine:** The engine is well-defined: contract-as-binding versus cultural reconstitution-as-counter-force. The terminal condition (legitimacy failure) is historically accurate. But the notes plan risks presenting the abolition as a political event rather than a systemic one. Gokhale's resolution and Gandhi's campaign were the visible moments, but the underlying structural failure was economic: by 1910, the indenture system was producing diminishing returns. The sugar price was falling. The cost of maintaining the system (shipping, depot infrastructure, medical inspections, legal apparatus) was rising. The girmitiya's own resistance (work slowdowns, petitions, refusal to re-indenture) raised the cost of enforcement. The political campaign succeeded because the economic logic was already failing. The chapter should show both layers.

**On Section 5 (The Witness):** Totaram Sanadhya is the right figure for the system's self-exposure. But his testimony must be shown as operating within a specific political structure: the Imperial Legislative Council, the Indian National Congress, the anti-indenture committees. The petition is not just a story told; it is a story told to a specific audience through specific institutional channels. The chapter should show the channel, not just the voice.

**On the arkatti commission structure:** The notes mention this as an anticipated pressure point but do not plan to address it directly. I recommend a brief passage in Section 2 or Section 3 that shows the commission structure: the arkatti received a per-head payment for each recruit who reached the depot. This created the incentive for deception. The system's cruelty was not arbitrary; it was structured by payment.

**Recommendation:** Strengthen Section 5 with the economic dimension. The legitimacy failure is not just moral; it is fiscal.
