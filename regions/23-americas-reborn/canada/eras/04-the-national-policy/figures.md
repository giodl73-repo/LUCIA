# Figures — Canada Era 4: The National Policy and Its Discontents

---

## Qwenqwent (composite figure, c. 1913 born)

Qwenqwent is a composite figure drawn from the testimony of Secwépemc and other residential school survivors recorded in the Truth and Reconciliation Commission archives (2015) and the Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples testimony (1996). She is not a named historical individual; she is constructed from documented survivor testimony to provide a specific interior perspective without attributing fictional experience to a named person.

The name "Qwenqwent" is Secwepemctsin. It carries within it the phonological character of the language: the consonant clusters, the vowel patterns that do not correspond to English orthographic conventions, that make the name visually and acoustically strange to an English-trained reader. This strangeness is deliberate. Her name performs, on the page, the same function that her language performs in the culture: it is a system of knowledge with its own internal logic, and it is not translatable without remainder.

The elements drawn from survivor testimony: the cutting of the braids on arrival; the replacement of a name with a number; the prohibition on speaking the home language; the dormitory conditions (overcrowding, institutional food, limited privacy); the whispered language at night; the return home to find oneself estranged in both directions. These are documented patterns in TRC testimony, not individually attributed. The TRC's "Final Report" and "Missing Children and Unmarked Burials" volume are the primary sources.

Her granddaughter, unnamed in the chapter, is similarly composite: the documented pattern of language recovery through family transmission, represented in the Secwépemc Nation's language revitalization programs, which have operated since the 1990s and have produced new fluent speakers in communities where fluency had been near-absent.

## Louis Riel (1844–1885)

Riel is one of the few figures in Canadian history whose story is told differently depending on which language you grew up in. In English Canada of his own era, he was a traitor and a rebel, hanged justly under the law. In French Canada, he was a martyr, executed by an English Protestant government that would never have hanged an Anglo-Protestant in the same circumstances. In Métis memory, he was the president of the provisional government of the Red River, the man who made Manitoba — the only indigenous-led negotiation that produced a lasting constitutional result.

All three framings are evidence about the person holding them. Riel himself, in his trial, refused the frame of rebellion. He offered a different frame: he had been the head of a legitimate government representing a people who had legal rights to the land and political rights to self-determination. He was not asking for pardon; he was asking for recognition. He was denied it, and he was executed.

He was a man of genuine political vision who had periods of mental illness that his contemporaries used to dismiss him. He was a devout Catholic who received prophetic visions that his Church regarded with suspicion. He was the leader of a Métis nation that had a coherent political identity and legal claims, tried under laws that did not recognize those claims as valid. His execution produced the political fracture between English and French Canada that Henri Bourassa would articulate for the next generation. His meaning, in Canadian memory, depends on which Canada you are standing in when you read his trial transcript.

The chapter presents Riel's own words at his trial, which are the clearest expression of his self-understanding available.

## Duncan Campbell Scott (1862–1947)

Scott is the figure who most clearly embodies the era's central paradox: a man of genuine literary cultivation and administrative systematization of cultural destruction who understood himself as doing good.

He wrote poetry. His "The Onondaga Madonna" and "At Gull Lake: August, 1810" have been anthologized as canonical works of Canadian literature. He wrote about Indigenous people with genuine aesthetic interest — which makes him more, not less, disturbing. He could see the beauty of what he was administering out of existence. He saw it as something to be preserved in art while its living form was suppressed.

As deputy superintendent of Indian affairs from 1913 to 1932, Scott administered the residential school system at its height of enrollment and its documented peak of abuse. The government's own inspectors' reports, filed during his tenure, documented the conditions in the schools. Scott knew what the reports contained. He continued to advocate for the system and to expand its reach.

His 1920 statement to the Parliamentary committee is the chapter's clearest evidence of his stated logic: the goal was the elimination of "Indians" as a legal and cultural category, achieved by absorbing them into "the body politic." This was not secret. It was policy, stated publicly, funded by Parliament, administered by his department. The horror of Scott is not that he was hidden; it is that he was visible.

He retired in 1932 and continued writing poetry. His literary reputation persisted well past his death.

## Henri Bourassa (1868–1952)

Bourassa founded *Le Devoir* in 1910 as a voice for French Canadian nationalism independent of both the Conservative and Liberal parties. His grandfather was Louis-Joseph Papineau, who had led the Patriote rebellion of 1837–38. Political opposition was in the family.

Bourassa's nationalism was not separatist in the modern sense — he believed in a French Canadian place within Canada, but a Canada that honored its obligations to both founding European nations equally. His opposition to the Boer War (1899–1902) and to Canadian participation in the First World War was consistent with this logic: if Canada was a sovereign nation, it should decide its own foreign policy; if it was a British colony, it should admit it. The refusal to choose was, in his reading, dishonesty.

His argument against conscription in 1917 was the culmination of his career. He was right that the Military Service Act treated French Canada's young men as available to British imperial purposes without corresponding recognition of French Canadian cultural and political rights. He was wrong to believe that refusing the war was a viable political position in 1917. He lost, but the fracture his loss created has never fully healed.

Bourassa did not concern himself with the Indigenous peoples of Canada. His nationalism was French Canadian nationalism — the claim of two founding nations — which explicitly did not include the nations who had been on the land before either European group arrived. He was, in this respect, entirely typical of his political tradition. His discontent with the National Policy was real. Its limits were also real.

## Nellie McClung (1873–1951)

McClung is the chapter's clearest example of the era's simultaneous tracks. She was a reformer of genuine courage and achievement: a suffragist who was part of the Famous Five that petitioned the Privy Council and won, in 1929, the ruling that women were "persons" under the British North America Act and therefore eligible for Senate appointment. She was a Manitoba MLA. She was a novelist. She was a voice for temperance, for women's labor rights, for the expansion of democratic participation.

She also shared, without apparent conflict, the assimilationist assumptions of her class and time. The "Indian problem" appeared in her writings as a problem to be solved by assimilation — the same logic that Scott operated on, arrived at from a different direction. She did not campaign against the residential school system. She did not see it as inconsistent with her reformist values, because her reformist values were built from a framework that assumed European civilization was the destination.

She is in the chapter not as a villain and not as a hero but as evidence: the progressive reform tradition and the residential school tradition were not opposites. They were simultaneous expressions of the same civilization's self-understanding. Understanding this is part of understanding the era.

McClung moved to Victoria, British Columbia, in her later years. The land on which Victoria sits is the unceded territory of the Lekwungen-speaking peoples. She did not write about this.

## Crowfoot / Isapo-muxika (c. 1830–1890)

Crowfoot was a leader of the Siksika Nation (Blackfoot Confederacy), recognized as one of the most significant diplomatic figures of the treaty era. He was not a chief in the European monarchical sense — Siksika leadership was earned through demonstrated wisdom, warfare skill, and the respect of the community, and it required constant renewal. Crowfoot had earned it.

His speech at Treaty 7 in 1877 is the chapter's evidence of what the Plains nations understood themselves to be agreeing to. He was not naive about power; he had watched the whisky traders and the disease that came with them, and he understood that the NWMP's presence had been, in certain respects, protective. He signed. He understood the signing as a covenant.

The aftermath of Treaty 7 is the measure of what the covenant meant to the government. The land promised for reserves was reduced. The annuities were paid irregularly. The pass system confined Siksika people to the reserve without the agent's written permission. The ceremonies were prohibited. Crowfoot died in 1890. He had lived to see the buffalo gone and the system he had tried to negotiate with revealed in its full character.

He remains, in Siksika memory, a man who did what he could with what was available. The covenant he made was kept by the Siksika in their understanding of what they had agreed to. The other party understood differently.
