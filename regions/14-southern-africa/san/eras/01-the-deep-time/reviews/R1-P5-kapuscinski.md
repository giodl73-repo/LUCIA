## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Review of San, The Deep Time (Opening)

**Overall:** I can feel the fire. I can hear the singing. The bodies are present — /Twi!kxao's sweat, his changed breathing, the women's shoulders touching. This opening has ground. But the ground could be deeper.

**What works:**
- "The song moved between them the way water moves through sand." This is the right simile for this landscape. Water and sand are the two materials that define the Kalahari, and using them to describe the singing anchors the sound in the place. The sensory detail is structural, not decorative.
- /Twi!kxao's body is present throughout. Slick with sweat, breathing short and sharp, hands burning. The trance is not an abstraction — it happens to a body, and the opening keeps that body in front of the reader.
- "He said the scream was not his. It belonged to the sickness, leaving." The sickness has a body too. This detail — the scream belonging to the sickness — is the kind of specific, strange, true detail that puts you in a place no imagination could invent.

**What doesn't:**
- I want the Kalahari. The fire is here, the sand is here, but where is the night sky? The Kalahari night sky is one of the most extraordinary sensory environments on earth — no light pollution, the Milky Way as a physical presence overhead. The trance dance happens under that sky. One line placing the reader under it would deepen the scene immeasurably.
- Temperature is absent. The Kalahari at night in the dry season is cold. The fire is not just ceremonial — it is warmth. The dancers' sweat against cold night air. This tension between the heat of the body and the cold of the desert would add a physical dimension the opening currently lacks.
- Sound beyond the singing. The Kalahari is not silent at night. Insects, distant animals, the sound of feet on dry sand. The singing exists within an acoustic environment, and that environment is missing.

**The question I'd push on:**
The Drakensberg rock art passage (paragraph 6) is currently visual only — the paintings are described as images. But rock shelters have their own sensory world: the cool of the overhang, the smell of rock dust, the echo that changes how voices sound inside them. If the chapter visits the rock art sites, I want to be inside the shelter, not looking at a photograph of it. Place me there.
