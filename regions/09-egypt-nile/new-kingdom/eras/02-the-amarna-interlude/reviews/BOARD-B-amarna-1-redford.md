## BOARD — Donald Redford — Review of The Amarna Interlude

**Overall:** The chapter gets the political dimension right. The economic strangulation of the Amun priesthood is correctly described as a gradual withdrawal of royal support, not a single confiscatory act. The structural fragility of the Aten system is accurately identified. The chapter avoids romanticizing Akhenaten, which is the single most common error in popular treatments of this period.

**Factual accuracy:**

1. **Timeline:** The phased escalation (early co-worship, talatat construction at Karnak, name change, move to Akhetaten) is correctly sequenced. The approximate date of the move (year 5) is consistent with the scholarly consensus. ACCURATE.

2. **Economic dimension:** The description of the Amun priesthood's wealth (agricultural estates, cattle herds, granaries, dependent labor) is correct in its general terms. The specific claim that "by the end of the New Kingdom, the high priests of Amun at Karnak would control more agricultural land than the throne itself" is supported by the Wilbour Papyrus and the Harris Papyrus evidence, though these date to the Twentieth Dynasty. The forward reference is accurate but the chapter should be read as pointing toward a later era's evidence. ACCURATE with caveat.

3. **Amarna Letters:** The representation of Rib-Hadda's correspondence is accurate. The number "more than sixty" letters is correct. The characterization of the archive as showing "a network under strain" is fair. The note that "some replies were certainly sent" is an important scholarly caveat that the chapter handles well.

4. **Talatat:** Correctly described as small standardized blocks. The Arabic etymology is correctly noted as later.

**Cultural authenticity:**

The chapter does not romanticize Akhenaten as a proto-monotheist or spiritual visionary. The Great Hymn is rendered as a theological text within its own context, not as a precursor to Biblical monotheism. The Psalm 104 comparison is correctly omitted. The chapter treats the revolution as a coherent political-theological program, which is the reading the evidence supports.

**Concerns:**

1. The chapter could more explicitly note that Akhenaten's interest in the Aten was not entirely unprecedented: his father Amenhotep III had already elevated solar theology. The chapter mentions the father's building program but not his solar interests. A single sentence noting the father's Aten-adjacent theology would strengthen the phasing.

2. The claim that the empire was "deeply compromised" by the end of the Amarna period is somewhat overstated. Significant Levantine losses occurred, but the empire's core (Nubia, the immediate Sinai buffer) remained intact. The Ramesside reconquest suggests the losses were recoverable, if costly.

**Verdict:** Historically sound. The political reading is correct and well-proportioned within the chapter's overall structure. The two concerns above are minor and do not require revision before locking.
