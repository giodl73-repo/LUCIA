# La Organización Nacional y la Generación del 80
### Argentina, 1853–1916

---

## I. El Conventillo

Buenos Aires, 1895. The building at Calle Defensa 179 holds eleven families and one room that is nobody's room. Italian from Genoa, Spanish from Galicia, a Pole who doesn't speak to anyone, three Afro-Argentine women who have lived in the Barrio Sur since before any of the newcomers arrived and will be here when most of them have moved on. The courtyard is a shared problem: the laundry, the water pump, the smell from the communal latrine. At night, when the heat of the day has lifted slightly and the pump is not being fought over, someone brings a guitar.

This is where it starts. Not in a ballroom. Not in any place the city's newspapers would mention.

The music that comes out of that courtyard is nobody's music yet. It is not what the Italian brought from Naples — his songs had a different sweetness, a different backward pull. It is not what the Galician brought either, nor the African rhythm that surfaces in the bass line without announcing itself. It is the sound of people who came from somewhere else and have not yet become anything together, trying to make an evening bearable. The bandoneón player — a German instrument, originally designed for hymns in small churches — arrived in the port district sometime in the 1870s and nobody remembers exactly when it became the voice of this. But it did. The accordion breathes in and breathes out. The violin enters. Two people who barely know each other stand very close and move.

The name "tango" is already in use by the time these families are crowded into Defensa 179. Where the word comes from is contested: some say it is African, a word for the gathering place where enslaved people danced; others say it comes from the Portuguese; others trace it to the Latin tangere, to touch. Nobody in the conventillo is worrying about etymology. The music is being played. The dance is happening in three feet of shared space because there is no more space than that.

What the Argentine elite understands about this is simple: it is vulgar. The tango is a dance of the arrabal, the city's edge, the immigrant slums where languages pile up and propriety does not reach. The women who dance it are assumed to be of questionable virtue. The men who dance it are laborers, dockhands, people of color, recent arrivals who have not yet learned how Argentina works. The newspapers that serve the oligarchy — that thin upper crust of landowning families who run the country from their estancias and their clubs — do not cover the conventillo music except to condemn it. The country's government, dominated since 1880 by the Partido Autonomista Nacional and the Generación del 80, has very clear ideas about what Argentina should become: educated, European, modern. The oligarchy is building the Teatro Colón. It is inviting Italian opera companies. It is laying out Buenos Aires on a Haussmann grid — the great diagonals, the wide boulevards, the parks named after Libertad and Independencia. There is a vision here, and the tango is not in it.

In 1895, six million Europeans will arrive in Argentina over the next thirty years, the greatest sustained immigration in the country's history. The Generación del 80 planned this immigration, legislated it, paid for it. They needed workers. The Pampa needed to be settled. The railways needed to run. The wheat and cattle economy needed hands. What they did not plan was what six million people would do to the culture when they arrived — the languages they would bring, the music they would make in the courtyards, the way the city would change underneath the government's plans for it.

The tango is happening in the conventillos of La Boca and San Telmo, in the arrabales at the city's edge, in the milonga dance halls that the elite ignores. It is moving outward, slowly, into the middling neighborhoods where clerks and shopkeepers live. It is not yet respectable. It will not be respectable for another fifteen years, and the respectability will not come from Buenos Aires.

It will come from Paris.

## II. La Pampa y el Plan

Before the city, the land.

Julio Argentino Roca crossed Patagonia in the summer of 1879 with six thousand soldiers and a plan that was not complicated: push the Mapuche and Tehuelche peoples off the territory south of the Río Negro and open it for cattle. The Pampa, the vast grassland that stretched from Buenos Aires westward to the Andes and south to Patagonia, was the richest agricultural territory in the southern hemisphere. The European capital that Argentina needed — British above all — was willing to finance railways across it, port infrastructure to ship its beef and wheat, cities to house the workers. But the capital required security of investment, and security of investment required settled land, and settled land in Patagonia required the removal of the peoples who had organized it differently.

Roca did not think of this as removal. He thought of it as civilization. The Generación del 80 understood this as civilization. For the Mapuche and Tehuelche confederacies, it was their land and then it was not.

The Campaña del Desierto of 1879–1884 accomplished what Roca intended. The Mapuche confederacies that had held southern Patagonia through a century of colonial pressure were broken. The Tehuelche were pushed to marginal land or killed in the fighting. An estimated fourteen thousand people were captured or surrendered; thousands more died in combat or displacement. Roca returned to Buenos Aires a national hero. In 1880 he became president of the republic, and the period of his dominance — which lasted through two presidential terms and the careers of his political allies until 1916 — bears his stamp, or rather bears the stamp of what he built.

What he built was a system. The railway network radiated from Buenos Aires across the Pampa like spokes from a hub, following the logic of export: grain and cattle moved toward the port, British manufactured goods moved back. By 1900, Argentina had more railway track per capita than any country in Latin America. The Pampa's beef and wheat were feeding Britain. The profits were being reinvested in Buenos Aires — the Teatro Colón, the widened boulevards, the new residential neighborhoods of Palermo and Recoleta where the estanciero families built their mansions and shopped at the Harrods that opened on Florida Street in 1914.

The financing of all of this was European, and this dependence was not accidental. The Generación del 80 sought European capital the way they sought European immigrants and European architecture: because European was the standard of modernity, and modernity was what Argentina was building. President Miguel Juárez Celman borrowed so heavily against future wheat revenues that when the harvest failed in 1889 and British investors panicked, the peso collapsed. In July 1890, with the government unable to service its foreign debt, the Baring Brothers bank in London — Argentina's primary creditor — faced insolvency. The political crisis that followed forced Juárez Celman to resign; his vice president, Carlos Pellegrini, took over and negotiated a rescheduling of the debt with the help of other European banks.

Pellegrini was, in one of the period's many productive ironies, the son of an Italian immigrant father. His father had arrived in Buenos Aires in the 1840s, had built a career as an engineer, and had married into a creole Argentine family. Carlos was born into the aspiring upper crust, educated as a lawyer, and spent his political life at the absolute center of the Generación del 80's oligarchic establishment. He was not a hypocrite. He was a man who had completed the one possible journey and then defined its end-point as the beginning of the only legitimate society. The next wave of Italian immigrants — the ones arriving at the port of Buenos Aires in steerage in 1895, who would crowd into Calle Defensa — were not his kin in any sense he recognized. They were the labor the plan required.

The land cleared, the railways built, the debt stabilized, the immigration legislated: by 1900 the Generación del 80's system was functioning. What they could not control was the system's output.

## III. La Milonga

On a Friday night in 1900 on the Calle Corrientes, not far from where the boulevard was still being widened, there was a dance hall — one of dozens on Corrientes and the side streets, the kinds of rooms the newspapers did not name. No one recorded who first called rooms like this a milonga — the word had been in use for the dance since the 1880s, borrowed from African-derived musical forms brought to the River Plate generations earlier. La Paloma charged admission. It sold wine and food. The floor was worn smooth. The couples who danced there knew the difference between a bad musician and a good one, and the musicians knew the difference between an appreciative crowd and an indifferent one. It was not a brothel. It was not a church. It was something that had not existed before Buenos Aires created it.

Ángel Villoldo played in rooms like this. Born in 1861, the son of a Buenos Aires family of middling means, he was a composer, lyricist, and performer who moved between the conventillo world and the working-class entertainment circuit with the ease of a man who knew both and belonged to neither entirely. His 1903 composition "El Choclo" — named for an ear of corn, a slang term for something lovable — became one of the first recorded tango pieces. Villoldo recorded in Buenos Aires and in Paris, where the Argentine community's musicians had been finding audiences since the early 1900s. He wrote the lyrics himself. The lyrics were ribald, melancholy, occasionally political, and entirely in the idiom of the Buenos Aires streets — Lunfardo, the slang that had developed in the conventillos out of Italian, Spanish, and African vocabulary rubbing together in the years since the great immigration began.

The tango that Villoldo played was not yet the tango that Paris would receive. It was rawer, faster, closer to the candombe pulse that underlied it. The candombe was an African-derived rhythmic tradition that the Afro-Argentine community of the Barrio Sur had maintained through the nineteenth century in their annual carnival celebrations — the comparsas, the costumed groups that paraded with drums through the streets of Barrio Sur, San Telmo, Palermo. By 1895, this community had been dramatically reduced. The War of the Triple Alliance (1864–1870) had killed Afro-Argentine men disproportionately; they had enlisted or been conscripted at high rates into the Argentine army and had not returned. The yellow fever epidemic of 1871 had devastated the Barrio Sur neighborhoods most densely populated by Afro-Argentines. The arriving European immigrants occupied the spaces they had held. The comparsas still marched, but smaller each decade.

What did not disappear was the rhythm. The candombe's characteristic beat — its syncopated pulse, its interlocking patterns — had already entered the tango's musical DNA before the term "tango" was in wide use. It was there in the way the bandoneón phrased its lines, in the pause before the downbeat, in the particular weight of the bass. When Argentine historians later wrote about the tango's origins, they emphasized the Italian and Spanish musical contributions and mentioned the African one briefly or not at all. The music had absorbed the African contribution so completely that it was possible to hear it and not hear it — to feel the rhythm without knowing where the rhythm came from. The conventillo opening of the Buenos Aires story acknowledged those three Afro-Argentine women and moved on. So did Buenos Aires.

The dance itself was a different matter. What the tango invented, and what no prior dance form in Argentina had systematized, was a set of practices for navigating proximity with a stranger. In three feet of milonga floor space — no more than that — two people who had not met, who might speak different languages, who came from different countries and different worlds, performed a sequence of movements that required complete attention to each other's weight and balance and intention. The lead offered a proposal with their chest; the follow answered with their own. Neither person spoke. The music told them when to move and when to stop. The embrace was close — too close for any ballroom the Argentine elite attended — because the rooms were small and the floors were crowded and there was simply no other way to dance if you were going to dance at all.

This was not romantic in the way the later, internationalized tango was romantic. It was practical. It was a technology for sharing space with people who were different from you, developed by people who had no choice but to share space. The milonga was a laboratory for exactly the social problem the conventillo presented: how do you live alongside someone whose language you don't speak, whose customs you don't know, whose presence you can't escape? You learn to read their body. You learn the grammar of their weight.

## IV. Paris Decides

The tango reached Paris by 1907. The route was not documented with the precision that later historians wished it had been: musicians traveling between Buenos Aires and the French capital, Argentine expatriates in the Latin Quarter, a recording here, a performance there. The Argentine community in Paris was small but present — students, political exiles, a handful of musicians who had found that European audiences paid for novelty. By 1910 the tango was being performed in the cabarets of Montparnasse and Montmartre, and by 1912 it was a European craze.

The French received it as something dangerous and exciting. The Catholic Church issued warnings. The Kaiser of Germany condemned it as an "Argentine national debauch" and forbade it from German military officers' balls. In Berlin, in Vienna, in London, the tango was banned from respectable establishments and danced in all of them anyway. The Pope, Pius X, expressed concern. The King of England's court was reported to be learning it. The Russian aristocracy, which had been dancing the waltz and the mazurka for a century, found in the tango's close embrace a mode of erotic permission that the court's other entertainments did not provide.

What none of these European observers understood was where the tango had come from. For Paris, the tango was Argentine. This was already a simplification: it was from Buenos Aires, from a specific social class in Buenos Aires, from a specific neighborhood geography within that class. But from the distance of the Montparnasse cabarets, it was Argentine, and Argentina was a country that had been exporting beef to Europe for thirty years and could now apparently export culture as well.

When Buenos Aires learned that Paris was dancing the tango, the news traveled through the Argentine newspapers with a particular quality of relief. Not surprise, exactly. The elite had always known, in some formulation, that the tango was being danced in the arrabal. They had known it was being played in rooms they would not enter. What they had not known was what to do with this knowledge. Now Paris had resolved their uncertainty. Paris had pronounced a verdict.

The endorsement was received as genuine rather than as absurd, because within the European-validation loop, genuine and absurd were not distinguishable. If Paris said the tango was art, the tango was art. The Argentine upper class began to dance it at events that would have been unthinkable five years earlier. The young men of the oligarchy, who had been dancing the tango in the arrabales for a decade already without telling their families, now danced it at their families' parties. The women of the oligarchy, for whom the tango had represented something physically and morally disreputable, now attended tango teas in Palermo. The milongas of Corrientes Street received clients who had previously attended only the Colón.

Ángel Villoldo lived to see this. He had already been performing in Paris. He was not a wealthy man — composers of popular music in early twentieth-century Buenos Aires were paid for their recordings and their performances but rarely grew rich from them — but he was a recognized figure, a name. He died in 1919, six years after the Paris recognition had transformed the status of the music he had been playing for thirty years. The music had not changed. The status had.

## V. La Ley Sáenz Peña

In 1912, President Roque Sáenz Peña signed into law the legislation that carried his name: universal male suffrage, secret ballot, mandatory voting. It was a reform he had reached through a calculation that turned out to be wrong.

Sáenz Peña believed that expanding the franchise would consolidate the oligarchic system. The upper and middle classes who had long participated in Argentina's notoriously corrupt electoral arrangements — the machine politics of the Partido Autonomista Nacional, the controlled results, the ballot fraud — would organize themselves into a modern conservative party and win. The immigrants who had arrived and not yet organized politically would either not vote or would vote as their employers and landowners directed them. The Generación del 80's model would continue, only now with the legitimating form of genuine elections.

He was wrong about the immigrants' second generation.

The Unión Cívica Radical had been founded in 1891 in the aftermath of the Baring crisis, when the Generación del 80's model had nearly collapsed under the weight of its own borrowed confidence. Its leader, Hipólito Yrigoyen, was an enigmatic and methodical politician who had spent two decades building a national network of local committees. The Radical party was not a class party in the way the later Peronist movement would be. Its base was the urban middle class: the immigrants and their children who had become clerks, teachers, small-business owners, professionals. They were the Italian son who had left the conventillo and rented a modest apartment in Once or Villa Crespo. They were the Spanish immigrant's daughter who had learned to read in the Argentine school system the Generación del 80 had built. They were the second generation — Argentines who had never lived anywhere else and did not feel that their Argentineness required European validation.

In April 1916, under the Ley Sáenz Peña, Argentina held its first genuinely free presidential election. Hipólito Yrigoyen won.

The Generación del 80 had accomplished something that no other Latin American country had managed at that time: they had organized a mass immigration, built a continent-scale agricultural economy, created a national educational system, and then — under pressure from the forces those achievements had produced — transferred power through a democratic election without military intervention. The transfer was real. Yrigoyen governed until 1922, was reelected in 1928, and was overthrown by a military coup in 1930. The coup of 1930 was not the era's story. It was the next era's beginning.

What the 1916 election revealed was the structural gap in the European-validation loop: it could not produce the political legitimacy needed to govern the country the immigration had made. The six million arrivals and their children were not interested in European validation. They were Argentine. They danced the tango without waiting for Paris to tell them it was theirs. They voted for Yrigoyen without asking London or Madrid what they thought. The loop was not broken by revolution. It was outgrown by demographics.

## VI. El Bandoneón

In a shop on the Calle Florida in 1914, a woman bought a ticket to the Teatro Colón to see a production of Verdi's Aida. The Teatro Colón had opened in its present form in 1908, replacing the original opera house that had stood since 1857. The new building seated 2,500, had acoustics that rivaled any opera house in Europe, and had been built by Italian architects hired by the Buenos Aires government specifically because the city wanted an opera house that would be recognized as world-class. The woman — whose name is not recorded, whose family had been in Buenos Aires for three generations, who was of the landowning class in the way that one is of the landowning class: not by working land but by receiving its income — had subscribed to the season since she was a girl. She had heard Caruso perform here in 1915.

Two blocks away, in a café on Corrientes Street, a young man from Rosario was playing the bandoneón with two other musicians. He had been playing since he was fourteen. His father was a Genoese immigrant who had arrived in the 1890s and had worked in the meat-packing plants of Rosario until he had enough to open a small hardware store. The son had not gone into the hardware business. He played the bandoneón in cafés and milongas and hoped, one day, to record.

These two people would not meet. Their Buenos Aires was not the same city. But the bandoneón's sound — which carried through the open window of the café onto Corrientes Street and was audible, faintly, from the direction of the Colón — was the sound that connected them whether they knew it or not. It was the most Argentine sound in the world, and it had been made by a German instrument designed for a Protestant hymn.

This is what the European-validation loop produced when it was not paying attention: the bandoneón player on Corrientes, playing music that would outlast the Teatro Colón's Verdi productions, outlast the Generación del 80, outlast every political system that Argentina would try and then abandon in the century ahead. The tango endured. The governments changed. The conventillos were demolished and rebuilt. The Pampa continued to export wheat and beef. The loop that required Paris to certify what Buenos Aires already knew gradually loosened, without ever entirely releasing its grip.

In 1917, a year after Yrigoyen's election, a young singer from a poor neighborhood of Buenos Aires walked into the recording studio of the Odeón label and recorded two tangos. His name was Carlos Gardel. His birthplace is contested — Toulouse or Tacuarembó or Buenos Aires itself, depending on the source and the claimant — but he was raised in the conventillo world of Buenos Aires, and his voice carried the melancholy and defiance that the tango had been accumulating for thirty years in the milonga halls of Corrientes and the courtyards of Defensa. He became the greatest tango singer who ever lived, the voice that the entire world recognized as Argentine, and he remained in the memory of the country — after his death in a plane crash in 1935 — as the symbol of something real and irreducible about what Argentina was.

He needed no one's permission.

The tango is a compression of the whole thing, three minutes long. A country that wanted to be Europe so badly that it needed Europe's permission to recognize what it was, and then gradually, generation by generation, learned to recognize it anyway. The bandoneón breathes in. The violin enters. The dancer's heel finds the floor.
