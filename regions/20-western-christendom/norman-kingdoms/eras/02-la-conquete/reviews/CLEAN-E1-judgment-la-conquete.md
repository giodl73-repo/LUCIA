### Anachronistic Morality Scan

**Finding 1 — P2 (Implicit judgment)**
Location: Domesday section
Text: "It was the most complete administrative record of a kingdom's landholding that the medieval world produced."

Violation type: Modern comparative judgment. The statement positions the Domesday Book against a standard (completeness among medieval administrative documents) that no Norman in 1086 would have used. William did not know he was producing the most complete record in the medieval world; he knew he was producing a record of what he owned.

Fix: Reframe through Norman perception. "Nothing like it existed in England before, and nothing like it would appear in France or the Holy Roman Empire in William's lifetime." This keeps the uniqueness claim but grounds it in what was knowable from inside the era.

**Finding 2 — P3 (Borderline)**
Location: Body at Caen section
Text: "Orderic Vitalis, writing forty years later, thought William was a great king who did terrible things. He did not put it in those terms; the moral vocabulary was different."

The parenthetical acknowledgment of different moral vocabulary is admirable, but the phrase "great king who did terrible things" is still a modern moral framing imposed on Orderic's account, not a rendering of Orderic's own categories. Orderic's categories were more complex: William was a man of outstanding capacity who overstepped what God permitted, and who would answer for it at the Last Judgment. That is different from "terrible things" as a modern ethical assessment.

Fix: Render Orderic's actual categories. "Orderic Vitalis, writing forty years later, believed William had been a man of extraordinary capacity who had, in some acts, exceeded what a Christian king ought to do, and that this would be weighed at the end of time. He did not use the word 'terrible.'" Author may choose to defer this one given borderline status.
