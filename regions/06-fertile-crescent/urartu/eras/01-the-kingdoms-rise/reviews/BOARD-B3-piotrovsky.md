# B-3 — Boris Piotrovsky — Board Review of The Kingdom's Rise

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Urartian archaeology, fortress excavation, material culture. Director of excavations at Karmir-Blur and Erebuni. Author of *The Ancient Civilization of Urartu*.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The cyclopean masonry description is accurate for the lower courses at Tushpa. The chapter correctly notes the use of basalt, the absence of mortar in the foundation courses, and the multi-ton weight of individual blocks. The tool-mark description (point chisel grooves, flat chisel on contact surfaces) is consistent with the archaeological record.
- The Erebuni description is correct: citadel with temple, administrative quarters, storerooms. The wall paintings are accurately noted as "fragmentary but recoverable" with geometric and figural designs. The colors (red, blue, white) are consistent with the excavated fragments.
- The storeroom description draws on Karmir-Blur evidence, which is a later site (seventh century BCE). The chapter uses the storeroom evidence illustratively rather than dating it to this era, which is appropriate, but the reader should be aware that the most complete storeroom evidence comes from later fortresses. The organizational principle (centralized storage in the fortress) was established in this founding period, but the scale of evidence is greater for later periods.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The fortress as a total system (citadel, lower town, storerooms, workshops, temple) is correctly described. The Urartian fortress was not just a military installation. It was a complete administrative and economic center. The chapter captures this well.
- The deportee labor is honestly acknowledged.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The materiality of the fortress. The chapter puts you at the wall: the weight of the stone, the precision of the fit, the labor of the cutting. This is how the fortress felt to build, and the archaeological evidence supports the description.
- The storeroom as the mechanism of centralized authority. The surplus held in the king's name, redistributed by the king's officers. The chapter understands that the storeroom is not a detail of architecture but the material basis of power.
- The Erebuni foundation as the culmination of the founding phase.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter's reference to wine storage at Karmir-Blur should note that this evidence comes from a later period (Rusa II, seventh century BCE). The principle of centralized wine storage likely extends to the founding period, but the specific evidence the chapter evokes is anachronistic by about a century.
- The chapter mentions "bronze and iron tools" for canal cutting. For the Sarduri I period (mid-ninth century), iron tools were becoming available but bronze remained the primary material for fine stonework. By Menua's period (early eighth century), iron was more common. The chapter should distinguish between the periods or simply say "copper, bronze, and iron" to cover the full range.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS. Two minor anachronism corrections. The structural description of the fortress system is accurate and well-grounded.
