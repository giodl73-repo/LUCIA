## B-3 — Billie Jean Collins — Board Review of Hittites Era 2

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Hittite religion, ritual practice, and cultural identity; specialist in festival texts and the relationship between religious and political authority.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The KI.LAM festival is correctly described as a major annual ceremony involving a royal procession through the lower city past the Great Temple. The description of artisan workshops (bronze, textiles, stone) in the temple economy is accurate.
- The puruli festival is correctly identified as a spring festival marking the defeat of Illuyanka by the Storm God. The chapter correctly presents this as a ritual reenactment, not merely a mythological narrative.
- The theological logic (king serves gods, gods sustain land, rituals maintain connection, edict ensures legitimate king) is accurately represented and consistent with Hittite religious texts.
- The Storm God of Hatti and the Sun Goddess of Arinna are correctly identified as the principal deities receiving festival offerings.
- The divine witness mechanism in treaties is correctly described: the gods are personal guarantors who take offense at oath-violation.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter treats Hittite religion as operative reality, not as a cultural artifact to be analyzed. The gods punish. The rituals maintain order. The festivals are necessary. This is the correct inside voice for a Hittite chapter.
- The connection between religious legitimacy and political succession is correctly drawn. An illegitimate king's offerings might be refused by the gods, breaking the chain that sustained the land. This is theologically consistent with Hittite religious texts.
- The sensory details of the festival (burnt animal fat, incense, priestly procession) are plausible and grounded in what the ritual texts describe.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The integration of religious and political analysis. The chapter does not treat religion as background to politics. It presents them as inseparable, which is correct for Hittite culture.
- The puruli festival description as "the victory of divine order over chaos reenacted each year so that the year could begin clean" accurately captures the festival's theological function.
- The edict's theological argument (past suffering as proof of divine punishment for oath-breaking) is correctly presented as the Hittites would have understood it.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter mentions Yazılıkaya in the opening overview (00-OVERVIEW.md) but does not discuss it in the chapter itself. Yazılıkaya's rock-cut reliefs were significantly expanded during the period covered by this chapter (or shortly after). A brief mention of the divine assembly carved in stone would reinforce the chapter's argument about the gods as witnesses. Not a factual error; an opportunity.

**Verdict:** APPROVED
