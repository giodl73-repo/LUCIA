# B-1 — Jan Vansina

## Domain Assessment

**Teke Political Culture:** The chapter's treatment of the Makoko's onkani authority is accurate in its main lines. The onkani concept — the land's spirit residing in the paramount chief as the land's representative in human governance — is documented in my ethnographic work and is consistent with comparative evidence from neighboring Central African political theologies. The chapter does not over-spiritualize (treating the onkani as mystical rather than political) or under-spiritualize (treating it as merely rhetorical). It renders the concept as the political technology it was: a claim about what the Makoko is, not just what he has.

The commercial class characterization also holds. The transmission of route knowledge and buyer relationships from father to son (or through other kinship channels), the professional ethic of not revealing information, the practice of gathering intelligence during hospitality exchanges — these are consistent with the Teke commercial culture as documented in the 1880s period and, I believe, extrapolable backward with the caution the chapter shows.

**Historical accuracy concerns:**

1. The chapter states "the Katanga copper had a particular weight to it, a particular red-brown surface color that no local smelting could exactly replicate." The chemical composition of Katanga copper is distinguishable from other Central African copper sources, and this distinction was documented by twentieth-century analysis. Whether the plateau brokers in the sixteenth century distinguished Katanga copper by the specific qualities the chapter names (weight, color, sound) is plausible but not directly attested. The chapter presents this as a reconstructed practice based on general knowledge of how commodity authentication works in pre-literate commercial cultures. This is methodologically defensible but should be acknowledged as inference in the footnotes or figures.

2. The chapter's description of the Pool's physical scale ("seventeen kilometers across at its widest point") is accurate for the Malebo Pool at its broadest seasonal extent.

3. The Portuguese arrival date (1483 at the Congo River mouth) is accurate.

4. The characterization of the 1560s Kongo wars as producing surplus captives that entered the Atlantic trade is consistent with the documentary record, though the specific mechanism (captives the kingdom's own market could not absorb) is one scholar's interpretation of a complex transition. The chapter does not overstate certainty here.

**Recommendation:** Approve with one minor note — add a brief acknowledgment in figures.md that the commodity authentication details (weight, color, sound) are reconstructed practices based on general knowledge of pre-literate commercial authentication rather than specifically documented Teke practices. The reconstruction is sound; the chapter should acknowledge it as inference.

**Overall assessment:** The chapter honors the scholarly record while telling a story the scholarly record has difficulty telling. The treatment of Vansina's own research (used as the primary source) is honest about its temporal limits. I would recognize the Teke commercial culture in these pages.
