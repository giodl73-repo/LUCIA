## BOARD — B-egypt-1 — Mark Lehner — Review of The Fragmentation

**Scope:** Construction logistics, labor organization, administrative systems, workers' conditions. Extended scope for this chapter: the transition from centralized labor systems to provincial administration.

**Assessment:** This chapter does not cover pyramid construction, so my primary expertise is tangential. However, the chapter's treatment of the administrative systems that succeeded the Old Kingdom's centralized labor organization is relevant.

**What is accurate:**
- The description of the Sixth Dynasty nomarchs' transition from royal appointees to hereditary lords is consistent with the archaeological and epigraphic evidence. The provincial tombs at Elephantine, Deir el-Bersha, and Beni Hasan do show the shift described.
- The Eighth Dynasty decrees addressed to nomarchs are real and their tone is correctly characterized: negotiating, not commanding.
- The characterization of the provincial administrative apparatus (grain storage, canal maintenance, militia organization) as the continuation of Old Kingdom administrative practices at the local level is sound.

**What needs checking:**
- Line 21: "by the reckoning of the Turin King List, for more than ninety years." The Turin King List is damaged at this point. The traditional figure is 94 years, but it has been disputed (Hornung proposes 64 years based on a different reading). The chapter's "Whether the number is exact or inflated by a damaged papyrus" handles this appropriately. No change needed.
- The granary scene in Section I is reconstruction, not documentary evidence. Khety's inscription describes grain distribution but does not describe a specific counting scene. This is honest reconstruction from the inscriptional record and is appropriate for narrative purposes, but figures.md should note that the scene is a narrative construction grounded in the inscription's claims, not a documented event.

**Factual errors found:** 0

**Verdict:** Accurate within scope. The administrative transition from centralized to provincial control is correctly rendered. The labor organization that I study at Giza is the system whose breakdown this chapter describes, and the description of that breakdown is structurally sound.
