The production bible has named the structural engine correctly and identified the terminal condition with precision. "Premise non-recognition" is the right term for what happened in 1898 — the revolution performed correctly on its own terms and was overruled by a party operating by different rules. This is analytically sound, and it connects to the v1.6 framework in a way that serves the chapter rather than constraining it.

My concern from the PANEL-1 review has been substantially addressed: the section plan makes explicit that Section 6 will explain *why* the United States was able to determine the outcome. But I want to push the author on one more level of causal depth.

The question is not only that the United States "operated by different rules." The question is WHY the United States was in a position to make those rules binding. Three structural factors made this possible: (1) Cuba's debt. The Liberation Army and the provisional government had no foreign recognition and no access to credit markets; they could not sustain a postwar state without external support. (2) The Spanish-American War ended so quickly (three months) that the United States had not engaged in extended negotiation with the Cuban provisional government; there was no treaty obligation to honor. (3) The United States' strategic interest in Cuba as a naval position in the Caribbean — post-1898, the United States was a Pacific power and needed Atlantic approaches secured. The Platt Amendment was not merely an expression of racism or imperialism (though it was both); it was a strategic calculation.

The chapter's argument will be strongest if it shows, from inside the Cuban experience, the moment when the structural trap became visible — the moment when the Cuban leadership understood that the military victory would not produce the sovereignty it had purchased. When did Gómez realize? When did the provisional government? What did they do with that realization?

**Secondary recommendation:** The section on the reconcentración camps should explain Weyler's logic, not just its consequences. The reconcentración policy was a military doctrine (destroy the insurgency's civilian support base) applied at catastrophic scale. Understanding why Weyler chose it, and why it failed militarily even as it caused mass death, is part of the structural analysis. The chapter should show that the Spanish military understood the insurgency correctly (it depended on civilian support in the manigua zone) and that its chosen method for breaking that support produced exactly the conditions (mass civilian death, American public outrage) that enabled the U.S. intervention.

**The engine is sound.** The section plan will execute it. The note above is additional depth available in Sections 5–6.
