# R2-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Wayúu, Era 3: The Great Cattle Era — PANEL-2 Review (Notes)

The section plan has a sensory gap in Section 4 that needs addressing.

Sections 1, 2, 3, and 5 all have natural sensory anchors: the goats in section 1, the landscape and herds in section 2, the palabrero traveling between encampments in section 3, the mochila and trade goods in section 5. Section 4 (What the Colonial Record Cannot Measure) is the most analytically dense section — the legal asymmetry between matrilineal Wayúu inheritance and Spanish patrilineal property law. This is the section most at risk of floating free of physical ground.

The solution: open section 4 with a colonial document. Not quoted at length — a fragment. The specific wording of a Riohacha administrator trying to record a Wayúu cattle transaction in Spanish legal categories and failing. The document's failure — its blank spaces, its frustrated annotations — is itself sensory evidence. The archive smells of something. The paper has a quality. The handwriting of a frustrated bureaucrat has a rhythm. Before the analysis begins, find the physical object.

Then: after the legal asymmetry is explained, land it in a specific scene. A Spanish merchant arrives at a Wayúu encampment seeking to buy cattle. He deals with the senior male present — the husband, the father. He pays. He leaves. A week later, the cattle's actual owner — the maternal uncle of the eirruku — arrives to ask where the cattle went. Under Wayúu law, the transaction was not valid; the husband had no standing to sell the clan's cattle. Under Spanish law, the sale was complete. What happens next is a pütchipü'üi case. Show this collision — not as a legal argument but as a physical confrontation between two men, two legal systems, and a herd of cattle that is standing in a field being rained on.

The note about the dry well is right — it belongs in the chapter's sensory landscape. Specifically in Section 2 or the transition between Sections 2 and 3. A dry well in the wrong season is a political event: which eirruku controls the only functioning well in a twenty-kilometer radius? That is the origin story of half the cattle disputes the pütchipü'üi handles.

**Summary:** Section 4 needs a physical anchor before the legal analysis and a physical landing after it. The dry well should appear as a political-ecological event, not just a weather fact. The chapter's sensory architecture is otherwise sound.
