# P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of "When the Ancestors Walked" Opening

**Overall:** This is exactly the kind of opening I have been waiting for. It treats the Songline not as a cultural curiosity but as what it is: a navigational, legal, ecological, and cosmological technology of extraordinary sophistication. The opening passes the depth test.

**What works:**
- The Songline-as-infrastructure framing is the breakthrough. "The song was not decoration. It was infrastructure." This single sentence does more to establish intellectual parity than any number of qualifiers about sophistication. A reader who absorbs this sentence will never again think of Songlines as folk music.
- The ecological knowledge is embedded, not listed. The perentie track, the desert oaks, the honey ant galleries, the clay pan's three-month water cycle, the spinifex dew — these are not decorative details. They are data points in an ecological knowledge system, and the opening presents them as such by placing them inside Maku's attention rather than in an anthropological sidebar.
- "A person walking a Songline was walking through the constitution of a continent." This achieves what I call parity of frame — it gives the Songline the same analytical status that Western writing gives to the Magna Carta or the Constitution. Not by comparison, but by using language that carries the same institutional weight.
- The cross-language continuity of the Songline network. The melody holding constant while the words change across language boundaries. This is a detail that reveals genuine systemic sophistication, and it is presented without marvel.

**What doesn't:**
- Fire-stick farming is absent from the opening. This is the other pillar of Aboriginal ecological knowledge, and at 65,000 years of continuous practice it may be the longest-running land management system on Earth. The chapter must give fire the same depth it gives the Songline. The opening need not carry it, but the chapter must.
- The astronomical knowledge system is hinted at ("the slow turning of the stars that told you when the rain would come") but not yet engaged. Aboriginal astronomy is as precise as anything produced in Mesopotamia or Greece. The chapter needs at least one passage that treats it at genuine disciplinary depth.
- The opening risks what I would call the "single-knowledge trap" — presenting the Songline as the central metaphor and letting it absorb all the culture's complexity. The San chapter avoided this through the eland-as-integration-pivot. This chapter needs a similar mechanism that holds multiple knowledge systems together without reducing them to one.

**The question I'd push on:**
Will the chapter engage with Aboriginal knowledge at DISCIPLINARY depth? Not "they knew the stars" but WHICH stars, for WHAT purpose, with WHAT precision? Not "they burned the land" but WHICH country, in WHICH season, in WHICH sequence, and WHY? The Davis test is not whether the chapter respects the knowledge system. It is whether the chapter respects it enough to describe it at the level of method.
