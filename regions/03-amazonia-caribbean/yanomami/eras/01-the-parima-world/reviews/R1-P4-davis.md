# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Review — Opening, Round 1

**Chapter:** Yanomami — Era 1: The Parima World
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

What this opening understands, and what so few accounts of Yanomami life understand, is that the shapori's work is not a "shamanic tradition" in the flattened, tourist-brochure sense. It is a technical practice grounded in a specific epistemology of the cosmos. The hekura are not metaphors. They are not symbols of ecological relationship or expressions of a "spiritual worldview." They are beings, present and operational, with specific jurisdictions and specific capacities. The shapori's skill is a skill — acquired through years of initiation, requiring ongoing maintenance, applied to specific problems. The opening gets this right.

The detail I want to flag for the full chapter: the yakoana's role should not be reduced to "hallucinogenic powder." This is the standard outside gloss, and it carries the unspoken assumption that the Virola resin is producing altered states that generate subjective experiences of hekura who are not really there. This is an outside frame. Inside the Yanomami world, the yakoana opens a channel. The hekura are there. The yakoana allows perception of what is already present. The difference in epistemological framing is enormous, and the chapter should maintain the inside frame without comment on the outside alternative.

Similarly: the opening describes the inner landscape the shapori must maintain — "a clean space, swept and tended, scented with the right plants." This is correct and beautifully grounded. The chapter should develop what this means in practice. What plants? What does neglect look like? What happens when a shapori's inner landscape goes out of condition — do the hekura leave? Become uncontrollable? The knowledge system here is as rigorous as any medical curriculum, and the chapter should show it operating at the level of method, not just describe its existence.

The Parima highlands as a specific ecological context should do more work in the chapter than it does in the opening. The tepuis — those extraordinary table mountains, among the oldest exposed rock formations on Earth, separated from the surrounding forest by their altitude — are cosmologically significant precisely because their isolation makes them genuinely different worlds. The hekura who inhabit the tepuis are ancient and powerful because the tepuis themselves are ancient and isolated. The ecology and the cosmology are the same system.

**The question I'd push on:** Can the chapter show the yakoana knowledge system at the level of method — the specific plants used in preparation, the protocol of calling specific hekura, the bodily signs that indicate a successful calling versus an unsuccessful one — so that the reader understands the shapori's work as a technical discipline rather than a mystical one? Not to demystify it. But to show its rigor, which is the inside understanding.

The opening has planted the right seeds. The chapter's obligation is to let them grow into method, not just atmosphere.

— WD
