## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of The Tang-Song Nanyang (Opening)

**Overall:** The opening passes the test. Lin's world is presented from inside, through his own categories, with his own knowledge as the organizing principle. I would recognize this as a story told with respect. But I have concerns about whose story it is.

**What works:**
- The water-reading is the correct way to open. Lin's knowledge is embodied, practical, accumulated across twenty years. It is not described as "indigenous knowledge" or "traditional navigation." It is simply how the man knows where he is. This is the inside voice working.
- The Hokkien identity is specific. Lin is not generically "Chinese." He is Hokkien, from a specific district, with a specific dialect, and the network he operates within is dialect-specific. This specificity is essential. The Chinese overseas diaspora is not one story; it is many stories organized by dialect, by origin, by generation. The opening correctly begins with one.
- The guanxi passage, despite being somewhat explanatory, does not step outside Lin's world. It presents guanxi as Lin would understand it: not as a concept to be analyzed but as a reality to be navigated. The line "the association remembered debts the way the sea remembered currents: silently, structurally, without forgiveness" is the voice of someone inside the system, not someone describing it.

**What doesn't:**
- The people of the Nanyang are absent. Lin sails through their waters, trades in their ports, but the Malay, Javanese, and Sumatran peoples through whose world he moves are mentioned only as harbor masters and tolling authorities. This is a diasporic opening, and I understand the perspective is Lin's, but if the chapter tells the story of Chinese overseas from inside, it must also tell the story of the relationship honestly. The local peoples are not scenery. They are partners, hosts, sometimes rivals. They have their own stories about the Chinese traders in their ports. The chapter must acknowledge this without stepping outside Lin's perspective, and the opening should plant the seed.
- The women are absent. Lin's world as presented is entirely male: captains, traders, surname associations. Who loads the goods in the warehouse between monsoons? Who manages the household in Quanzhou while Lin is gone? Who negotiates in the Nanyang ports? The record may be thin on women in this period, but the opening should at least leave space for their presence rather than suggesting they do not exist.

**The question I'd push on:**
Does the Nanyang have a voice in this story, or is it only a backdrop? A chapter about the Chinese overseas in Southeast Asia that presents the Nanyang only through Chinese eyes risks reproducing the very dynamic the project is designed to avoid: one people telling another people's story as though they were not in the room. Lin can be the anchor, but the people whose waters he sails through must be present as more than harbor masters.
