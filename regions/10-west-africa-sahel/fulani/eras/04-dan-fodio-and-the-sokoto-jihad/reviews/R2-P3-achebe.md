## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — PANEL-2 Review of Production Bible

**Focus:** Women's presence, cultural ownership, inside-voice discipline

**What the production bible gets right:**

The plan acknowledges the absence of women in the opening and commits to correcting it. Nana Asma'u is named and given a substantial role in Section IV. The structural engine is framed in terms the Torodbe would recognize: the hijra as Islamic political act, the *Kitab al-Farq* as juridical document, the flag as commission.

**What still concerns me:**

1. **Nana Asma'u cannot wait until Section IV.** The production bible places her first substantial appearance in the section on the Shehu's withdrawal. This means the chapter's first three sections present the jihad as an exclusively male intellectual and military undertaking, and then a woman appears once the fighting is over. This reproduces a framing the Torodbe themselves would not recognize. The Shehu's household was a center of learning that included women from the beginning. His wife Maryam and other women of the community were present at the hijra. Nana Asma'u was eleven at the time of the hijra, old enough to be present, old enough to remember. She should appear in Section II, among the community leaving Degel. Not as a featured figure yet, but as a named presence: the Shehu's daughter, already learning, already watching. The chapter should not introduce women only after the action is over.

2. **The slave economy section must be handled with extreme care.** The production bible correctly identifies the tension as internal to the Islamic legal tradition. Good. But the chapter must not become a modern verdict on Sokoto's slave economy delivered in Islamic legal clothing. The Shehu's own categories must be the framework, and the tension must be rendered as a genuine legal and ethical disagreement WITHIN the tradition, not as a shortfall measured against modern abolition. Some of the Shehu's followers genuinely believed the Caliphate's practices were lawful. Others, including the Shehu himself, believed they had gone too far. The disagreement is the story.

3. **The Hausa perspective must not be erased.** The chapter is told from the Torodbe/Fulani perspective, and rightly so. But the Hausa states were not blank opponents. The sarakuna had their own legal and religious scholars who disputed the Shehu's authority to declare them un-Islamic. Some Hausa scholars supported the existing order as compatible with Islam. The chapter should acknowledge this, even briefly, to prevent the Torodbe critique from appearing as the only possible Islamic reading of the situation. Present the Shehu's argument as the one this community held, not as the one Islam required.

**Recommendation:** The production bible should add a note to Section II: "Among the community leaving Degel, name the women who were present. Maryam. The Shehu's daughters. The students. The jihad began as a community's departure, not as an army's march, and the community included its women."
