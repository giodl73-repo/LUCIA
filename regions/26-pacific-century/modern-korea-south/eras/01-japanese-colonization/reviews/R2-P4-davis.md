# Panel Review — R2-P4: Wade Davis (Anthropological Wonder)

**Production Bible: Iljegangjeongi (Japanese Colonization, 1910–1945)**  
**Date: 2026-04-16**

---

## The Question

Will the chapter treat Korean and Japanese epistemologies as equally sophisticated? Will there be hidden assumptions that one way of knowing is superior?

---

## The Assessment

**THE PRODUCTION BIBLE IS CAREFUL AND RESPECTFUL. BUT ONE SECTION NEEDS WORK: "WHAT THE SCHOOLS CANNOT TEACH"**

Davis reads:

> "The section shows the contrast: the official schooling, sterile and Japanese; the secret teaching, rich and Korean."

Davis hears an assumption: **that Japanese ways of knowing are less sophisticated, less real, than Korean ones.**

This is a trap in narratives of colonization. We want to honor the colonized people's knowledge system. But if we do this by *diminishing* the colonizer's knowledge system, we have committed the same error the colonizer committed: we are ranking epistemologies.

Davis wants the chapter to show:

**The Japanese schoolteacher has a sophisticated epistemic system too. She believes in it. She is trying to educate Korean children with genuine care.** She is participating in an evil system, but her *way of knowing* is not stupid or hollow.

The tragedy is that **two equally sophisticated ways of knowing are in collision**. The colonial system requires that one destroys the other. But this does not mean one is better.

The chapter should be able to show: *the Japanese schoolteacher teaching a lesson in Japanese with real pedagogical care, and the Korean haegeum teacher teaching pansori with real artistic care, and both knowing that they cannot coexist under the current system.*

This is more anthropologically complex than the simple contrast of "sterile versus rich."

---

## The Question I'd Push On

**The Tanaka figure (the administrator)**

The production bible says:

> "Tanaka is not evil. He is *systematic*. He genuinely believes he is bringing 'civilization' to Korea."

Davis says: **make sure the chapter shows this conviction, not just names it.**

Show Tanaka at his desk, carefully recording names, seeing himself as building something orderly and good. Show him *believing in the righteousness of what he is doing*. Not because we want the reader to agree with him, but because **understanding how a colonizer sees himself is crucial anthropological work**.

The reader should feel Tanaka's conviction as much as they feel Kim-sa's resistance. Not because Tanaka is right, but because *understanding different ways of seeing the world is what anthropology does*.

---

## The Strong Point

The production bible correctly identifies the comfort women system as part of a larger **knowledge system**:

> "The comfort women system was not ad-hoc; it was bureaucratic."

This is anthropologically sophisticated. The chapter will show that the Japanese didn't invent sexual slavery. They *systematized* it. They made it bureaucratic. They gave it a name. They kept records.

This is the insight Davis values: the *machinery of the system* is visible when you look at how it *knows* — what it documents, what it records, what it erases.

---

## Recommendation

**PASS WITH STRONG GUIDANCE.** The production bible is respectful of Korean knowledge systems. But ensure the chapter is equally respectful of Japanese knowledge systems — not by validating colonialism, but by understanding it. Two epistemologies in collision. Both real. Both sophisticated. One destroying the other by force.

With this anthropological depth, the chapter will be exceptional.
