# The Bantu Encounter

The tracker found the iron at the waterhole before he found the people who had left it.

He was reading the ground, which is what a tracker does first. The sand at the edge of the water was soft where the cattle had been. He had never seen cattle, but he knew at once that what he was looking at was not any animal he knew. The hoof was round. It was heavier than an eland's hoof and lighter than a rhino's. There were many of them, perhaps thirty, moving together, driven from behind by something that walked on two legs and wore sandals whose print he had never seen either.

The people had drunk at the waterhole and moved on. They had left the ashes of a fire, the bones of something they had eaten, and a small thing in the sand that the tracker picked up and turned in his fingers. It was dark, heavier than stone of its size, with an edge that had been worked. He knew stone. He knew the stones of his country that flaked true when a man who knew how to flake them struck at the correct angle. This was not stone. It warmed faster than stone. It had a smell he would remember.

He did not keep it. He set it back where he had found it, because a thing left by people he did not know might belong to those people or might belong to something else, and the tracker was not a man who took things whose owners he could not yet read.

He walked back to the camp with the news.

The elder who listened was called //Kabbo, which is not the name he would have given a stranger, but it is the name the story keeps. //Kabbo listened the way he listened to anyone who brought news, carefully and without haste. The cattle went north. The people with them were tall. The tracks were fresh. The iron was left at the water. //Kabbo nodded at each answer and sat for a long time looking at the fire.

He was not surprised. He had heard, for some years, of people moving down the great valleys from the north, bringing animals that stayed in one place and plants that stayed in one place, a way of living that did not follow the rain but tried to hold it still. The news came in the way all news came to the San: through the network of voices that ran from waterhole to waterhole, carried by people who traveled for marriage or for trade. The news was never hurried and never late, because what the country held it held steadily.

The San's time is not the time of years counted from a starting point. The San's time is the time of the eland, which was here in the beginning and will be here in the end. The eland's fat holds the n/um. The eland moves across the country and the San move with the eland, and in the stories the eland is a person and the person is an eland, and the boundary a stranger might draw between them is a boundary the San have never found reason to draw.

The people with the cattle were in this country now. That was a fact. //Kabbo accepted it the way he accepted a change in the wind. The country was large. There was room for people who held the rain and people who followed it, and the places where the two ways met would be places where certain exchanges could happen.

The exchanges ran in both directions. The San had knowledge the new people did not have. The poison of the chrysomelid beetle larva, prepared in a way that took a lifetime to learn, which could bring down an eland in a day and a man in an hour. The tracking, which was not a skill but a reading of the country that took fifty years to acquire. The knowledge of which pans held rain longest, which tsamma melons could be eaten. The new people had grain. The new people had iron. The new people had cattle whose milk could be drunk. Certain San traded poison for grain. Certain San married into the new villages, and their children grew up with a foot in both worlds.

The old names did not go away. They layered. A place that had been //Gai!kwe in the time of //Kabbo's grandfather was still //Gai!kwe when //Kabbo sat at the fire, and was also now something else in the mouths of the cattle people, and both names were true, because a place in the San telling is not the word spoken for it but the place itself.

What //Kabbo did not say, because it did not need saying, was that a knowledge not spoken into a child is a knowledge lost. The iron was not the danger. The cattle were not the danger. The danger, if there was one, was slower and further off: a world in which the children sat at a fire that was not this fire, and did not learn the names, and did not learn the poison, and did not learn the tracking, because no one had walked with them long enough to teach.

That was a question for a later time. For this evening, the tracker had returned. The camp was whole. The eland was still the eland.

//Kabbo looked at the fire. The children sat behind the women. The women began to sing.
