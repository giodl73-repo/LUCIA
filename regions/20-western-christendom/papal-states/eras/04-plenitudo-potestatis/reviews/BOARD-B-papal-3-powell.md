# BOARD Review — B-papal-3-powell (James Powell)

**Chapter:** Papal States — Plenitudo Potestatis (Era 4)
**Reviewer:** James Powell (Innocent III: Vicar of Christ or Lord of the World?, 1994)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong chapter on Innocent III and the Fourth Lateran Council. The core interpretation — that the council was not improvised but the systematic completion of a project Innocent had been building since his first year — is defensible and represents a reasonable reading of the scholarship. The Fourth Crusade passage is accurate and appropriately nuanced. I have a few corrections and one question about interpretation.

---

## Corrections

**1. Canon count**
As Morris has noted: 71, not 70. Confirmed from my own work on the Council. The discrepancy in the tradition is well-documented; the chapter should use 71.

**2. Unam Sanctam quotation**
The chapter quotes: "Therefore we declare, state, define, and pronounce that it is altogether necessary for salvation for every human creature to be subject to the Roman Pontiff."

This is the standard English translation (cf. Brian Tierney's anthology The Crisis of Church and State, 1964). The Latin runs: "Porro subesse Romano Pontifici omni humanae creaturae declaramus, dicimus, definimus, et pronunciamus omnino esse de necessitate salutis." The translation in the chapter is accurate. Confirmed.

**3. Innocent III's election**
The chapter states Innocent was elected "unanimously, on the first ballot, within a day of his predecessor's death." The sources (Gesta Innocentii Papae III) confirm the unanimous election. The "first ballot" formulation and the timing "within a day" are consistent with the chronicle sources, though the exact mechanisms of the 1198 election are somewhat uncertain since the formal conclave procedure was not yet established (that came with Alexander III's 1179 decree, which required a two-thirds majority but did not specify timing). Acceptable.

---

## Question of Interpretation

**The Fourth Crusade and Innocent's intentions**
The chapter presents Innocent as having "launched" the Fourth Crusade "in 1198, intending a campaign toward the Holy Land via Egypt." This is the standard reading. However, some scholars (notably Jonathan Phillips, The Fourth Crusade and the Sack of Constantinople, 2004) have argued that Innocent's role in the diversion is more complicated than a clean "intended one thing, got another" narrative — that his legate's decisions at various points were not clearly contrary to instructions he had given, and that the diversion to Zara and then to Constantinople involved a gradual slide rather than a clean break. The chapter's account is not wrong, but it is somewhat simpler than the scholarship's most nuanced view.

For a narrative chapter at this level of compression, the simplification is justified. I note it only so the author is aware that the Fourth Crusade is one of the most debated topics in the field.

**The Anagni "slap"**
The chapter describes Boniface as having "offered his neck" but does not mention the "slap of Anagni" — the claim in some chronicle sources that Sciarra Colonna struck Boniface in the face. The chapter's choice to omit this is correct: the slap is probably legendary, appearing in later chronicles but not in the contemporary ones. The chapter's restraint is historically sound.

---

## Confirmations

- The Frederick II material (Innocent crowning him, Frederick failing to keep his promises) is accurate.
- The description of Philip II of France and Ingeborg of Denmark is accurate.
- John of England's submission in 1213 and acknowledgment of England as a papal fief is accurate. Note that John's acknowledgment of England as a papal fief was particularly advantageous for Innocent — it gave him grounds to object to Magna Carta in 1215 as an infringement on the rights of his vassal's realm.
- Boniface VIII becoming pope in 1294 is accurate (he succeeded Celestine V, who resigned in December 1294).
- The Clericis laicos date of 1296 is accurate.

---

## Summary

**Required corrections:** One shared with Morris: canon count (71, not 70). The Unam Sanctam quotation is accurate. The Fourth Crusade simplification is acceptable at this level of compression.

**One bonus note for the chapter:** The mention of Innocent objecting to Magna Carta (1215) could add a dimension to the chapter — Innocent, in the same year as his greatest council, also issued a bull annulling Magna Carta, because it infringed on what he considered the rights of his vassal king. This shows the plenitudo potestatis reaching into English domestic governance at exactly the moment the barons were trying to limit royal power. The irony: the council that required annual confession and defined transubstantiation also tried to cancel the document that became a foundation of English constitutional law. If space permits, this is a substantive addition.
