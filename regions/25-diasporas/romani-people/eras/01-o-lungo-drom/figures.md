# Figures — Era 1: O Lungo Drom (c. 1000-1300 CE)

## A Note on Names

No named individuals from the Romani departure or three-century journey survive in any record. The Roma's own oral tradition preserves no names from this period. The outside sources (Byzantine, Persian, Armenian) that mention travelers who may have been the ancestors of the Roma do not name them as individuals. This chapter therefore uses composite and unnamed figures, drawn from the ethnographic and historical record of Romani life, to anchor the narrative in human experience. Each figure below is clearly identified as composite or anonymous. This is honest uncertainty applied to figures, not invention dressed as history.

## The Smith

**Status:** Composite figure, drawn from the Dom metalworking tradition of northwestern India and the documented practices of Romani smiths across the medieval period.

The smith in this chapter represents the thousands of metalworkers whose portable skill opened the road. The Dom caste in India included hereditary metalworkers whose craft knowledge was transmitted within families across generations. The specific practices described in the chapter (reading metal by color, the bellows apprenticeship, the quench technique, the sonic judgment of the anvil) are documented in ethnographic studies of traditional Indian metalworking and in accounts of Romani smiths in the Balkans and Central Europe through the modern period. The continuity of technique across a thousand years and half a continent is itself evidence of the transmission system's effectiveness.

The smith is not one person. He is the skill made visible in a body. The chapter does not claim to know his thoughts or his name. It claims to know what he did with his hands, because the tradition of what he did with his hands survived.

## The Musician

**Status:** Composite figure, drawn from the Dom musical tradition and the Shahnameh's literary account of Indian musicians in Persia.

The musician in the chapter represents the parallel tradition of portable performance. In the Dom communities of India, musicians and metalworkers often came from the same families or closely allied families, and both occupations shared the same structural position: essential to the community, low in formal status, portable by nature. The specific detail that music and metalwork were twin occupations comes from Ian Hancock's account of the Dom caste system. The Shahnameh's story of Bahram Gur and the Luri musicians is a Persian literary source, not a Romani one, and the chapter treats it accordingly: as evidence of what the Persian world remembered and assumed about Indian musicians, not as evidence of Romani identity or self-understanding.

## The Healer

**Status:** Composite figure, drawn from ethnographic accounts of Romani herbal medicine.

The healer in the chapter represents the women who carried botanical and medicinal knowledge across the journey. The specific claim (that the pharmacological knowledge expanded with each new landscape) is supported by the documented range of Romani herbal practice, which includes plants from multiple bioregions, suggesting accumulated knowledge rather than single-source tradition. The chapter notes honestly that this knowledge was later called sorcery by the settled world. From inside the Romani community, it was transmitted expertise.

## The Byzantine Observer

**Status:** Anonymous figure, based on the hagiographic text from Mount Athos (c. 1068).

The observer who describes the Athinganoi in the Mount Athos text is not named in the original source. The chapter uses this figure as outsider-as-structural-evidence: what the observer notices (snake-charming, animal handling, useful but suspect arts) tells us about Byzantine categories of classification, not about Romani character. The observer sees what his training allows him to see: people whose skills are useful and whose strangeness is alarming. He does not see where they came from or what they carry. The gap between what he records and what existed is the gap the chapter works to illuminate.

## The Speaker

**Status:** Composite figure, representing the transmission of the Romani language.

The speaker appears in the chapter as a mother teaching her daughter to say pani for water, as a child learning Greek words for bread alongside Romani words for home. This figure is not drawn from a specific historical source but from the linguistic fact of transmission: someone taught these words to someone, generation after generation, across three centuries of movement, and the language survived. The speaker is the figure who carried the language, and therefore carried the origin, in her mouth. She is the least visible of the chapter's figures and the most essential.
