## Specific Findings

**Endorsements:**

1. The inner landscape that the shapori must maintain — "a clean space, swept and tended, scented with the right plants" — accurately renders what I have described as the xapiri's house inside the shapori. The maintenance obligation is real and ongoing. Correct.

2. The diagnostic grammar (general survey, narrowing, specific hekura for specific conditions) is consistent with how diagnostic work actually proceeds. It is not a single call but a sequence. Correct.

3. The shortcut-taking shapori whose range narrowed over time — this is a real phenomenon. Shapori who do not maintain the full protocol find that the xapiri become less accessible. The chapter's use of this figure to show the standard of rigor is accurate and well-placed.

4. The tepui hekura as ancient and weighty, with a different quality than forest hekura — correct. The chapter's description ("slower, heavier, with a quality of endurance") matches the phenomenology as I have described it.

5. The hekura of the jaguar for fast-moving sickness, the hekura of the harpy eagle for distance diagnosis, the hekura of the wind for large-area survey — these are documented hekura types with the correct associated capacities. Correct.

**Corrections required:**

1. **Hekura naming conventions:** The chapter uses "hekura of the waterfall," "hekura of the wind," etc. In my testimony and in the literature, xapiri/hekura are more often named by their animal or nature identity first (the waterfall's xapiri, or more specifically the spirit of a particular species of fish or water creature that inhabits that waterfall). The chapter's framing is not incorrect but slightly generalizes. No revision required; note in figures.md that hekura associations are more specific than the chapter's narrative shorthand suggests.

2. **Yakoana preparation:** The chapter mentions "the ash of a specific tree bark" as an additive. This is consistent with documented practice (ash from Elizabetha princeps bark is the most commonly documented additive). The chapter does not specify and this is appropriate at narrative level. No correction needed, but figures.md should note the specific plant.

3. **The reahu timing:** The chapter implies the reahu follows death relatively quickly. In Lizot's documentation and in my own account, the ash is sometimes stored in a gourd for months before the reahu ceremony, which is hosted months after the death and serves as the formal mourning occasion at which guest communities attend. The chapter's reahu section does not specify timing but should not be read as describing an immediate post-cremation ceremony. Consider adding "Months later, when the ash had been prepared and the communities invited" or equivalent framing if the chapter's narrative implies immediacy. Review the reahu section for timing signals.

**Assessment:** Minor clarification on reahu timing may be needed. Endorses the chapter's inside-voice handling of all major Yanomami cosmological and shamanic content.
