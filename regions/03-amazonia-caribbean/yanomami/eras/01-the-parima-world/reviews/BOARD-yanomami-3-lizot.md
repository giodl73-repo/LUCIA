## Overall Assessment

The chapter's texture is correct. The sensory details — the plantain soup, the specific time of day for the yakoana preparation (the chapter says "cool of the morning" for bark-stripping, which is accurate), the evening as the time for the shapori's most sustained work, the quality of the highland forest as distinct from the lowland — these are consistent with what I documented in Venezuelan Yanomami communities over three decades.

## Specific Findings

**Endorsements:**

1. The morning timing for yakoana preparation (bark-stripping when "the sap ran closest to the surface") is consistent with documented Yanomami practice. Correct.

2. The quality distinction between forest hekura (accessible, numerous, with specific jurisdictions) and tepui hekura (ancient, powerful, requiring exceptional shapori) is consistent with Venezuelan highland Yanomami accounts. Correct.

3. The evening as the shapori's primary working time is consistent with my documentation. The shapori works in the late afternoon and evening, when the community is gathered and the day's subsistence activities are complete. Correct.

4. The diagnostic sequence (multiple hekura called in succession, each providing partial information, the shapori narrowing toward a conclusion) is consistent with how shamanic diagnosis proceeds in documented accounts. Correct.

5. The shabono's deterioration cycle (several years, then a move and rebuild) is accurately characterized. The specific years vary (three to eight years depending on location, available materials, insect pressure), and the chapter's "several years" is appropriately general. Correct.

**Corrections required:**

1. **Reahu timing (confirms Kopenawa board review):** In my documentation, the reahu is typically held months after death, not immediately. The ashes are kept in a gourd, often a decorated one, hung in the family's section of the shabono. The reahu ceremony that involves the consumption is a formal event that requires advance invitation of allied communities, preparation of large quantities of food, and a multi-day gathering. The chapter's reahu section does not explicitly claim immediacy but could be read that way. Add timing clarification.

2. **Platano variety:** The chapter mentions "the platano most associated with the dead person's preferences." This is broadly accurate but worth specifying: the most documented practice involves the preparation of a specific cooking banana (not sweet banana) associated with the deceased's garden. "Cooking banana" or "the platano from the dead person's garden" would be more specific without requiring botanical precision.

3. **Children's participation in reahu:** The chapter states "Children who were old enough participated." My documentation suggests children's participation is more graduated — very young children are kept at some distance from the ash consumption, while older children participate progressively. "Children old enough to understand participated" is adequate; no revision required but note in figures.md.

4. **The feast's food:** The chapter mentions "smoked game" as a feast food. This is correct for the Parima highlands, where game is the primary protein at feasts. But feasting also prominently features wild honey (critical prestige food), various cultivated foods (plantains, sweet potatoes, manioc), and occasionally fish if the community is near an adequate stream. The chapter's "best smoked game" plus "plantain soup" is accurate but incomplete. No correction required — the chapter cannot enumerate everything — but worth noting for subsequent era chapters.

## Summary

The chapter's ethnographic texture is accurate for Venezuelan highland Yanomami communities. The reahu timing is the one correction that affects the chapter's text. The other findings are clarifications for figures.md or future chapters.

**Required correction:** Add timing clarification to reahu section (ceremony is held months after death, not immediately following cremation).

The chapter endorses at daily-life accuracy level with one required revision.
