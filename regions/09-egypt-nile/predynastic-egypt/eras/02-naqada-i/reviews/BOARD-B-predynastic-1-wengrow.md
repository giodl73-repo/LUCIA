# BOARD — David Wengrow

**Chapter:** Naqada I — The Amratian
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Assessment

The chapter treats the Predynastic as its own story. This is the fundamental requirement, and it is met. The people of Nubt are not waiting for the pharaohs. They are making things, exchanging them, and burying their dead, and the chapter lets these activities carry the narrative without teleological framing.

## Accuracy

1. **Copper calibration: CORRECT.** The chapter is careful about copper's role: "Copper at this stage was not replacing flint. Flint was still the material that did the serious work." This is accurate for Naqada I. Copper tools are present in the archaeological record but rare. The chapter's treatment of copper as socially significant rather than economically dominant is correct.

2. **Craft specialization and social differentiation: CORRECT.** The chapter's argument that the flood-season labor surplus enabled specialization, and specialization enabled exchange, is consistent with the archaeological evidence and with my own analysis in *The Archaeology of Early Egypt*. The link between specialization and emerging hierarchy is well handled: it is shown through the graves, not asserted through theory.

3. **Grave-good differentiation: CORRECT.** The chapter accurately notes that the differentiation is "not dramatic" but "consistent." This is the right characterization. The Naqada I cemeteries show differential burial wealth, but the scale is modest compared to Naqada II-III. The chapter does not overstate.

4. **Exchange as social relationship: CORRECT.** "The exchange was not commerce. There was no marketplace, no currency, no price." This is the correct framing for Naqada I exchange. The reciprocal-gift model is appropriate.

## Concerns

1. **Hierarchy framing:** The chapter says the network was "building hierarchy" and "creating winners." This is interpretively strong. Some scholars would prefer "differentiation" to "hierarchy" at this stage. The evidence supports emerging inequality in material wealth, but whether this constitutes hierarchy (implying institutionalized power relations) is debated. Recommendation: no change needed, but the chapter should be aware that this is an interpretive choice, not a neutral description.

2. **Settlement size claims:** "Hierakonpolis was becoming the largest settlement in the upper valley." This is defensible for late Naqada I but the evidence is strongest for Naqada II. The chapter could soften to "one of the largest" without losing the argument.

## Verdict: PASS with minor note on Hierakonpolis claim.
