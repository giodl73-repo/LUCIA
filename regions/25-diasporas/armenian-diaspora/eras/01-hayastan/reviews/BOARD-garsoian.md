# BOARD Review -- Nina Garsoian

**Chapter:** Hayastan -- The Ancient Kingdom
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Historical Accuracy

### Arsacid Period
The chapter's treatment of the Arsacid dynasty as a buffer kingdom between Rome and Persia is accurate in broad outline. The 387 partition is correctly identified. The deposition of Artaxias IV in 428 is correctly placed. The characterization of the Arsacid period as "balancing" is appropriate.

**Minor correction:** The chapter states the Arsacid Armenian branch began "from 54 CE." This is the date of Tiridates I's installation by Parthia, which is conventional but should be noted as approximate. The Arsacid presence in Armenia is more complex; the dynasty was established at various points. The chapter should qualify this as "traditionally from 54 CE" or simply say "the first century CE."

### The Conversion
The chapter handles the conversion well by presenting it through Agathangelos's narrative while acknowledging its legendary character. The geopolitical context is correctly identified but kept subordinate to the tradition's own understanding. This is the right balance.

**Note:** The chapter states "the kingdom had declared itself Christian in 301." The 301 date is traditional. Some scholars (e.g., Ananian, Garsoian) have argued for a date closer to 314, after the Edict of Milan. The chapter's use of "by tradition, was 301" in the Khor Virap passage is appropriate. The earlier reference in Section I should also include "by tradition" to maintain consistency.

### The Nakharar System
The description of the nakharar families as holders of hereditary lands with loyalties shifting between cooperation and resistance is accurate. The comparison to "geological persistence" is apt. However, the nakharar system was more formalized than the chapter suggests: specific offices (marzpan, sparapet, hazarapet) were tied to specific families. This level of detail is not necessary for the narrative but should be noted for accuracy.

### Avarayr
The battle and its aftermath are accurately presented. The Treaty of Nvarsak (484) is correctly identified as the resolution. The characterization of Avarayr as a defeat that preserved the faith is consistent with the Armenian tradition and with modern scholarship.

## Factual Corrections Required

1. "From 54 CE in the Armenian branch" -- qualify as approximate or traditional.
2. First mention of 301 in Section I -- add "by tradition" for consistency with the later passage.

## Endorsement

The chapter treats the ancient period with appropriate depth and honesty. The source asymmetry for Tigran is well-handled. The conversion is told from within the tradition without either accepting the legend as history or dismissing it from outside. The nakharar system and the buffer-state dynamics are accurately rendered. I endorse the chapter for publication with the two minor corrections noted above.
