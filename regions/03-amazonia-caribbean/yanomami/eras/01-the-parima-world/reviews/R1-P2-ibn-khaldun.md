# R1-P2 — Ibn Khaldun
## Panel Review — Opening, Round 1

**Chapter:** Yanomami — Era 1: The Parima World
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The scene is well constructed. The question I bring to it is whether the surface reveals the system, and I believe it does — though only partially at this stage, and the chapter will need to deliver more.

What the opening has accomplished is the identification of the structural principle: the shapori's work is the maintenance of cosmic order, and this maintenance is inseparable from the political maintenance of alliance. The child's healing cannot proceed without a visit to the neighboring community's shapori. The spiritual and the diplomatic are a single system. This is a productive engine — it suggests that Yanomami political life operates through a grammar of reciprocal obligation that extends from the human world into the cosmos, and from the cosmos back into the human world. The hekura are not decorative; they are governing.

What the chapter will need to develop is the mechanism of this governance. Several questions I would want answered:

First, what is the structural logic of the shabono as a political unit? The opening establishes that the shabono is the community, that it is constituted by common residence. How does political authority function within it? The shapori's authority here appears to rest on skill and demonstrated capacity — the hekura respond to his maintained inner landscape — but how does this authority interact with the headman (nochile) or other sources of leadership? Is this a system of distributed, skill-based authority, or is there hierarchy that the opening has not yet shown?

Second, the alliance-feasting nexus is gestured at but not yet examined. If the chapter's engine is that alliance maintenance IS cosmic maintenance, the chapter should show the mechanism: how does a feast constitute political obligation? What is exchanged, and what does the exchange obligate? The opening opens this door; the chapter must walk through it.

Third, I note the absence of conflict or tension in the structural picture. A system in which alliance is the constant work implies that alliance can also fail — that communities break, that obligations rupture, that the political cosmos goes out of alignment the way the spiritual cosmos can. The chapter should show the stakes of failure, not just the maintenance of function.

**The question I'd push on:** What is the causal logic connecting the shapori's work to the political survival of the community? The opening implies it; the chapter must make it explicit, through narrative rather than explanation. I want to see an alliance fail, or nearly fail, and understand what that means for the hekura's work.

The opening is sound. It has found the right entry point. The chapter's obligation is to follow the engine to its full articulation.

— IK
