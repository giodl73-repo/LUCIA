# Girmit — The Indenture System (1838–1917)

## The Word That Bound

The rains had failed again. Ramnaresh sat in the doorway of his house, which was not his house. The zamindar owned the house, and the land it stood on, and the mango tree that had dropped its fruit into the dust of the yard, and in a sense the dust itself. Ramnaresh owned what he could carry. After the second failed monsoon, what he could carry did not include enough rice to feed three children through the winter.

The arkatti came in December.

He came on foot from the direction of Arrah, wearing a cotton shirt cleaner than anything in the village, and he sat on the charpoy under the neem tree and accepted water and spoke in Bhojpuri so clean it sounded like a man who had learned it for a purpose. He said there was work. He said the work was across the sea, in a place he called Doonidhad. Trinidad, he meant, but the word had changed shape passing through so many mouths that it no longer sounded like any place on a map. He said five years. He said wages: five rupees a month for a man, four for a woman, food and housing provided, passage paid. He said the word girmit.

Girmit. The word was agreement, the Bhojpuri mouth's way of holding the English word that would hold them. The contract had traveled from London to Calcutta to the sub-depot at Arrah to this doorstep, and at each stage it had shed something. In London it was a legal instrument with clauses and obligations on both parties. By the time it reached Shahabad, it was a word spoken by a man on a charpoy who said five years, five rupees, and the sea.

What the arkatti did not say was as precise as what he said. He did not say that the depot in Calcutta held families for weeks in conditions the colonial surgeon would later describe as requiring improvement. He did not say the ship crossed two oceans in ninety days and that the space allotted to each coolie, the word the British used, was less than the space allotted to a convicted prisoner on a transport vessel. He did not say that the five rupees a month would be docked for sickness, for absence, for refusal, for any reason the overseer chose to name. He did not say that the five years ended in a place ten thousand miles from this doorstep with no money for return passage and no provision for it. He did not say that the housing was a barracks and the food was rationed and the work was sugarcane, which had to be cut by hand in heat that made Bihar's summer feel like a cool morning by comparison.

He said five years. He said wages. He said girmit.

Ramnaresh listened. He was not a fool. He knew that men who promised easy money were not usually offering it. But he also knew that the zamindar's collector would return, and that the moneylender in Arrah charged interest that doubled the debt each season, and that his children would eat in the winter or they would not. He calculated. The calculation was rational and the information was incomplete, and this was the system's design: to arrive at the moment when a rational man had no rational choice but to sign.

He had a wife named Dulari and three children whose names the indenture records do not preserve. The records preserve this: male, age approximately thirty, wife, three dependents, origin Shahabad district, Bihar, registered at the Calcutta depot in January of 1881, assigned to the ship bound for Trinidad, contract number among the hundreds processed that month. The number was the beginning of a new identity. In the colony they would be called by it. Not Ramnaresh. Not the son of his father. A number, assigned at a depot, attached to a contract written in a language he could not read, binding him to conditions he had not been told, for a term that would prove to be the rest of his life.

Dulari packed what could be carried. The brass lota for water. A small murti of Hanuman wrapped in cloth. A handful of turmeric tied in a rag, because turmeric was medicine and spice and the color of auspicious things, and wherever they were going they would need all three. Rice that would not last the journey to Calcutta, let alone the journey after. The Ramcharitmanas, Tulsidas's verses, not a bound book but a knowledge held in the body: she could sing the Sundarkand from memory, and she would sing it on the ship, and her daughters would learn it from her singing, and their children would sing it in a country that had no Ganga and no Ayodhya but would have, because of her, the verses that made those places real.

They walked out of Shahabad in January. The arkatti walked ahead.

## The Depot and the Black Water

The depot at Garden Reach, on the bank of the Hooghly in Calcutta, smelled of too many bodies in too small a space and of the river itself, which carried the effluent of a city of nearly a million people past the barracks windows. Ramnaresh and Dulari arrived after three weeks of walking and two changes of bullock cart, and they entered a system that had been processing human beings for export since 1838.

The medical inspection came first. A colonial surgeon examined each recruit: teeth, eyes, limbs, the capacity for physical labor. The examination was performed in a shed. The women were examined separately, or were supposed to be. The surgeon noted age, height, distinguishing marks. These marks, recorded in English on a form that the recruit could not read, would follow them across the ocean and become, in the colony, the only proof that they were who they said they were. Ramnaresh's form listed a scar on his left forearm. Dulari's listed a mole below her right ear. Their children were counted and noted as dependents, which meant they traveled on the contract but were not contracted, which meant they had no separate legal existence in the system at all.

The arkatti who had recruited them in Shahabad received his commission: a per-head payment for each recruit who reached the depot and passed the medical examination. The amount varied by period and by depot, but in the 1880s it was typically between five and fifteen rupees per head. The arkatti's livelihood depended on volume. Some arkattis were honest men conveying accurate information. Some were not. The system did not require them to be honest. It required them to produce bodies.

They waited at the depot for six weeks. The ship was not ready. The ships were never ready on time, because the scheduling of emigrant vessels depended on the sugar harvest in Trinidad and the availability of tonnage and the colonial government's inspection timetable, and none of these things moved at the speed of a family waiting in a barracks in Calcutta with nothing to do and not enough to eat. Dulari cooked with what the depot provided: rice, dal, a small ration of ghee. The dal was not the dal of Shahabad. It was institutional dal, cooked in quantity, without the tempering of mustard seeds and cumin and the pinch of asafoetida that made it recognizable as food from home. She tempered it herself, using the turmeric she had carried and a few mustard seeds obtained from a woman in the next bunk who had come from Ghazipur. This small act of cooking was the first reconstruction.

The ship, when it came, was called the Doonidhad in the depot records. It was a vessel of approximately 1,200 tons, steam and sail, with space designated for 450 emigrants in the lower deck. The space allotted to each person was roughly six feet by eighteen inches, marked by a chalk line on the wooden platform that served as both bed and living space. Ramnaresh and Dulari and their three children were allocated a family section, which meant a slightly wider platform with a curtain that did not reach the ceiling. The hold smelled of bilge water and the tar used to seal the planking and, within the first week, of vomit and sweat and fear and the particular smell of many people living in a space designed for cargo.

The crossing took eighty-seven days. The girmitiya had a word for it, though they had learned it only recently: kala pani. The black water. For a Hindu from the Gangetic plain, the ocean was not simply a body of water to be crossed. It was a boundary. To cross it was to lose caste, to become ritually impure in a way that could not be recovered by any ceremony available on the other side. The Brahmins in the village had said so. The pandits had warned against it. But the pandits had not been the ones choosing between the kala pani and the zamindar's collector, and Ramnaresh had crossed the boundary anyway, and so had Dulari, and so had the four hundred other people in the hold, and whatever caste meant on the other side of the ocean would have to be something different from what it had meant in Shahabad.

The sea was an unbroken sound. Water against the hull, day and night, for nearly three months. For people who had never been on water larger than the monsoon-swollen Sone River, the sound was disorienting in a way that did not ease. There was no birdsong. No insects. No wind through neem leaves. There was only the creak of timber and the slap of water and the sounds of four hundred people living on top of each other in a space where privacy did not exist and illness spread through the hold like rumor. Dysentery, measles, and the seasickness that flattened people for weeks. The ship's surgeon recorded the deaths: seven on this particular crossing, which was considered an acceptable number. The bodies were lowered over the side. No ghat, no fire, no Ganga. The water took them.

Dulari felt the movement of the ship in her stomach before she understood it in her mind. The floor tilted and the world tilted with it and there was nothing to hold on to and nothing steady. For a woman who had walked barefoot on the flat earth of Bihar all her life, the ocean's motion was a violence done to the body's knowledge of how the world was supposed to behave. It took weeks for the body to accept it. Some bodies never did. The woman in the bunk above Dulari's was sick for forty days straight and arrived in Trinidad ten pounds lighter than she had left Calcutta, with a fear of water that never left her.

But something else was forming. The ship was a furnace of social reorganization. Caste distinctions, which had structured every relationship in the village, became impossible to maintain in a hold where Brahmins and Chamars slept on adjacent platforms and shared the same latrine bucket. The girmitiya began to form new bonds. The word for it was jahaji-bhai: ship-brother. A jahaji-bhai was the person who had suffered the crossing with you. Not blood kin, not caste kin, but kin of the shared ordeal. The jahaji-bhai bond would persist in the colony for generations, a kinship system invented in transit, aboard a ship, in the darkness of a hold. Dulari sang the Sundarkand in the evening when the hatch was opened for air, and the women near her listened, and some of them knew the verses and joined, and this too was a new formation: a mandali, a circle, assembling itself out of shared words in the middle of the black water.

## The Cane

They landed at Port of Spain in April. The air was thick and wet and sweet with the smell of something Ramnaresh could not identify until he saw it: sugarcane, growing in fields that stretched to the horizon in every direction, the stalks three meters tall and dense as a wall.

The barracks at the Doonidhad estate were long wooden buildings divided into rooms roughly ten feet square. Each room held a family or, for single men, four to six occupants. There was a cooking area at the end of each building with a common fire. There was a latrine trench behind the barracks. The overseer, a white man or sometimes a Creole man whose name the records preserve but whose face the girmitiya remembered by his habits (the whistle he blew at five in the morning, the ledger he carried, the way he stood at the edge of the field counting heads), assigned the work.

The work was sugarcane. Cutting it was done with a machete, a tool that most of the Bihar recruits had never held. The cane had to be cut low, near the root, and the leaves had to be stripped. The leaves were edged with silica and cut skin like a blade. By the end of the first week, Ramnaresh's forearms were a grid of shallow cuts that the sugar juice burned and the tropical humidity infected. The work began before dawn and continued until the overseer was satisfied, which was measured not in hours but in tasks: a measured length of cane row to be cut, stripped, and bundled. If the task was not completed, the day was docked. If Ramnaresh was too sick to work, the day was docked. If he refused, the day was docked and a complaint was entered in the ledger, and five complaints could bring a charge before the magistrate, and the magistrate could extend the indenture.

The five rupees a month, when it arrived, arrived diminished. Deductions for rations (whether eaten or not), deductions for housing (the ten-foot room), deductions for tools (the machete that Ramnaresh had not known he would need). What remained was sometimes two rupees. Sometimes less. And two rupees, in Trinidad in 1881, bought very little, and there was nowhere to spend it except the estate shop, which was owned by the planter, and the prices at the estate shop were set by the planter, and the system closed on itself like a fist.

Dulari worked in the fields alongside Ramnaresh. The contract specified lighter tasks for women, but the contract specified many things. On the estate, the overseer assigned work according to his judgment, and his judgment was that women could cut cane. Dulari cut cane. She also cooked, because the rations provided were raw rice and dal and nothing else, and if the food was to be edible she had to cook it, and she cooked it over a fire built from cane trash at the end of the barracks after a day in the field, and the food she made tasted less and less like Shahabad and more like what could be done with what was given, and this too was a form of adaptation that the contract had not imagined.

Resistance took quiet forms. A girmitiya who was docked for absence on Monday would report for work on Tuesday and cut slowly. A group of cutters who were assigned an impossible task would complete it at a pace that made the overseer wait, and waiting cost the planter money, and the planter's accounts were the one language the colonial system respected. In Fiji, organized work stoppages on the estates became common enough that the colonial administration appointed a commission to investigate. The commission found that the girmitiya's complaints were largely justified and recommended improvements. Some improvements were made. Most were not. But the resistance itself, the refusal to be merely the labor unit the contract described, was a form of insistence on personhood that the system could suppress but could not eliminate.

The women's situation on the plantations was particular. The indenture regulations required that every shipload maintain a minimum ratio of women to men, a ratio that was raised over the decades from twenty-five per hundred in the early years to forty per hundred after 1868. Even the higher ratio was never sufficient. On many estates, there were twenty or twenty-five women for every hundred men. The colonial authorities noted this disparity and its consequences: the records from Trinidad, Fiji, and British Guiana all document a pattern of violence against women on the plantations, sometimes by husbands, sometimes by other men. The colonial observers attributed the violence to the gender imbalance and to what they called the moral looseness of Indian women. The girmitiya's reality was less simple. Women on the plantations were subject to coercion from overseers, from drivers, from men with power over their daily conditions. The violence was not the girmitiya's character. It was the system's product: a structure that had taken a social world apart and put nothing coherent in its place, and the people caught inside it suffered the consequences of the disassembly.

## The Mandali

But the disassembly was not the whole story. It was not even the most important story.

In the evenings, after the cane was cut and the overseer had gone and the cooking fire had burned down to coals, people gathered. Not every evening. Not formally. But enough evenings, and consistently enough, that it became a structure.

In Felicity, a village near the Doonidhad estate where some girmitiya had settled after their indenture expired, a group met on Saturday evenings to read the Ramcharitmanas. They had no pandit. The nearest pandit was on another estate, two hours' walk, and he came when he could, which was not often. But they did not need a pandit for the Ramcharitmanas. Tulsidas had written it in Awadhi, the language of the people, not in Sanskrit, the language of the priests. Any literate person could read it. Any person with a good memory could recite it. Dulari could recite the Sundarkand, the book of Hanuman's journey across the ocean to Lanka, and the irony was not lost on the people listening: Hanuman had crossed the sea to rescue Sita, and they had crossed the sea to cut cane, and the verses that described his courage were being sung in a barracks in Trinidad by a woman who had carried them in her body from Bihar.

The mandali was not a temple. It was not an institution. It was a circle of people sitting on the ground, sharing a text that made the world coherent. The Ramcharitmanas contains the whole of the dharmic world: duty, devotion, the relationship between human beings and the divine, the meaning of exile and return. For the girmitiya, the text's story of exile was not metaphor. Rama had been exiled from Ayodhya for fourteen years. They had been exiled from Shahabad, perhaps forever. The text understood exile. It had been written for people who knew what it meant to be far from home, and it was being read by people who were farther from home than Tulsidas had ever imagined possible.

The mandali met. And around the mandali, other structures formed. The holi festival was celebrated, though the calendar had to be estimated because no one had brought a panchang and the seasons in Trinidad did not correspond to the seasons in Bihar. Colored powder was thrown. The cooking developed: the dal and roti of Bihar supplemented with what the island offered, coconut and cassava and peppers that had no name in Bhojpuri but were given names. The curried foods of the girmitiya merged with the flavors of Trinidad, and the fusion, over decades, became its own cuisine. Chutney, the condiment, became chutney, the music: a form that blended the rhythms of Bhojpuri biraha (the songs of separation) with the calypso of the island, creating something that belonged to neither India nor Trinidad alone but to the space the girmitiya had built between them.

The temple came later. The first Hindu temples in Trinidad were built from whatever was available: wood, corrugated iron, packed earth. They were small. They were not Varanasi. But they contained the murti, and the brass bell, and the sandalwood incense, and the prayers, and this was sufficient, because the minimum viable architecture of Hindu worship is not a building but a practice, and the practice had crossed the kala pani intact.

Tamil girmitiya, who arrived from the Madras Presidency in smaller numbers but no less determined, brought their own traditions: the Mariamman temple, the Kavadi festival, the Tamil language that remained distinct from the Bhojpuri majority. In Natal, where a significant Tamil population settled, the fire-walking ceremony persisted. In Fiji, the Hindi that evolved among the girmitiya became its own dialect, Fiji Hindi, drawing on Bhojpuri and Awadhi and the English of the colonial administration and the Fijian of the indigenous population. Each colony produced its own version of the reconstitution, shaped by the specific conditions of the place and the specific people who had been deposited there by the system's indifferent logistics.

## The Witness

Totaram Sanadhya was indentured in Fiji in 1893. He was a Brahmin from Firozabad in Uttar Pradesh, literate in Hindi and Sanskrit, and he had been told by the arkatti that Fiji was a place near Calcutta where good wages were available. It was not near Calcutta. It was five thousand miles from Calcutta, across two oceans, and when Totaram arrived he was put to work in the cane fields like every other girmitiya, regardless of his caste or his learning.

He served his five years. Then he stayed, because the return passage cost money he did not have, and because by the time the five years ended he had married a local-born woman and had begun to teach Hindi to the children of other girmitiya, and because leaving would mean abandoning the community he had helped to build. He stayed for twenty-one years.

In 1914, with the assistance of Pandit Benarsidas Chaturvedi, a journalist and nationalist in India, Totaram published *Fiji Mein Mere Ikkis Varsh*: My Twenty-One Years in Fiji. The book was written in simple Hindi, the Hindi of a man describing what he had seen and done, and what it described was a system that the British colonial administration had presented as humane, regulated, and voluntary.

Totaram described the recruitment: the arkatti's lies, the depot's conditions, the ship's hold. He described the plantation: the hours, the penalties, the overseers who carried whips. He described the treatment of women: the coercion, the assaults, the absence of recourse. He described the magistrate's court, where a girmitiya who complained about conditions was more likely to be sentenced for breach of contract than to receive redress. He described all of this in the voice of a man who had experienced it, and the voice was calm, and the calm was more damaging than outrage, because outrage can be dismissed as emotional but testimony cannot be dismissed when it simply describes, in order, what happened.

The book traveled to India. It reached the Indian National Congress. It reached Gopal Krishna Gokhale, who had been pressing the colonial government on the indenture question for years. Gokhale read Totaram's testimony and brought it to the Imperial Legislative Council. In 1910, he had already moved a resolution calling indenture "a monstrous system involving almost semi-slavery conditions." Now he had evidence in the language of the people it had been done to. The resolution did not pass immediately, because resolutions in the Imperial Legislative Council were advisory, not binding, and the sugar lobby in the Colonial Office was powerful. But the testimony circulated. Gandhi, in South Africa, referenced it. The newspapers in Bombay and Calcutta printed excerpts. The system's internal fiction, that indenture was voluntary labor migration, was being contradicted by the people who had been inside it.

The planters, meanwhile, were finding it harder to make sugar pay. The price was falling. Beet sugar from European farms undercut the cane that the girmitiya cut. The cost of maintaining the system, the ships and depots and surgeons and colonial clerks who supervised the contracts, did not fall with the price. And the girmitiya themselves raised the cost: the slow cutter in Trinidad, the petitioner in Fiji, the man who finished his five years and refused to sign again. Each refusal required the overseer to find another body, and finding another body required another arkatti, another ship, another commission. The system was spending more to coerce labor than the labor was producing in sugar, and this, in the end, was the arithmetic that mattered.

In 1917, the Government of India formally abolished the indenture system. The last ship had sailed the year before. The formal reason was humanitarian: the system was incompatible with the dignity of Indian subjects. The structural reason was that the fiction could no longer be maintained. The contract said voluntary. The testimony said coerced. The economics said unprofitable. The contract collapsed under the weight of the contradiction it had carried since 1838, the contradiction between the word and the thing the word described.

## The Founders

They did not go back. This was the fact that the system had not planned for, because the system had not planned for the girmitiya to become people. The contract imagined labor units: transportable, interchangeable, disposable at the end of the term. The contract had not imagined the mandali, the jahaji-bhai, the temple built from corrugated iron, the chutney music, the Ramayan sung in the barracks, the children born in Trinidad who spoke Bhojpuri and English, the grandchildren who spoke English and Creole, the great-grandchildren who were Trinidadian and Indian and neither and both.

By 1917, there were Indian communities established across a belt of former plantation colonies that stretched from the Caribbean to the Pacific. In Trinidad and British Guiana, Indians constituted approximately forty percent of the population. In Fiji, nearly forty percent. In Mauritius, they were the majority, and they had begun to build the institutions, the Hindi-medium schools, the community organizations, that would shape the island's political life for the next century. In Natal, they were a significant minority whose presence had prompted Gandhi to develop satyagraha. Each colony's story was different: different crops, different relationships with the indigenous population, different paths from barracks to ballot. What they shared was the girmit. In each place, they had arrived as contract labor and become founding populations. They had arrived as numbers in a ledger and become people who named their children and built their temples and planted their gardens and buried their dead in soil that their parents had never seen.

The girmitiya's descendants did not, for the most part, call themselves exiles. The first generation had been exiles. The second generation was something else: people born in a place their parents had not chosen, building a life from the materials available, carrying the Ramcharitmanas and the Bhojpuri and the turmeric and the brass lota into a future that was not India and was not not-India but was something the girmit had made possible without intending to.

Between 1838 and 1917, approximately 1.5 million people were moved under the indenture system from India to the sugar colonies of the British, French, and Dutch empires. The system called them coolies. The system called the arrangement an agreement. The system recorded their bodies and assigned them numbers and docked their wages and extended their terms and did not provide return passage and expected them to disappear when they were no longer useful.

They did not disappear. They built. In Felicity, on a Saturday evening, someone is still singing the Sundarkand. Dulari's voice is in the room, carried forward through a century and a half of voices that learned the verses from other voices. The girmit bound them. The singing carried them. The word was not the whole of what they brought.
