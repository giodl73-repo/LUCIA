## Domain Accuracy — Zoroastrian Texts and Tradition

**The Avestan texts:** The chapter correctly frames the *Avesta* as the canonical Zoroastrian sacred texts and accurately notes that they were compiled into something approaching canonical form under Sassanid patronage. It is important to note, for the historical record, that the *Avesta* as we have it reflects both earlier material (the Gathas, Zarathustra's own compositions) and later additions; the Sassanid period saw the compilation of the *Zand* (Pahlavi commentary on the Avesta) more than the composition of new Avestan material. The chapter does not misrepresent this, but the full complexity of what "canonization" meant could be explored further in a longer treatment.

**The Gathas:** Correctly identified as Zarathustra's own hymns. The Zoroastrian tradition treats the Gathas as the prophet's direct compositions and the foundational revelation. Accurate.

**The cosmological framework:** The asha/druj (truth/lie) opposition is the fundamental dualism of Zoroastrian theology. The chapter's treatment is accurate: asha is cosmic order and truth; druj is the lie, disorder, the cosmic adversary. The equation of the political enemy (Arsacid eclecticism) with the cosmic enemy (druj) is exactly how the Sassanid royal ideology presented itself. This is not the author's interpretive overlay — it is the Sassanid primary record's own logic. The chapter correctly inhabits this.

**The five watches (gehs) of the Zoroastrian day:** Accurate. The Zoroastrian liturgical day is divided into five watches: Hawan (dawn to noon), Rapithwin (noon to mid-afternoon), Uzerin (mid-afternoon to sunset), Aiwisruthrem (sunset to midnight), Ushahin (midnight to dawn). The priests' prayers at each geh are correctly characterized as structural to the maintenance routine.

**The mouth-covering practice:** The *padan* — the cloth covering worn over the mouth and nose during fire rituals — is accurately described. It is prescribed in Zoroastrian priestly practice to prevent the contamination of the sacred fire by breath or saliva. The chapter describes this correctly and in the right spirit: not as exotic ritual but as maintenance procedure within a precise knowledge system.

**The "five kinds of fire" question:** The chapter simplifies to the three great sacred fires, which is appropriate for narrative purposes. The Zoroastrian tradition distinguishes five grades of fire (Bahram being the highest, then Adaran, Atash-e Dadgah, etc.), with the three great fires (Adur Farnbag, Adur Gushnasp, Adur Burzen-Mihr) being the highest-grade Bahram fires endowed with maximum royal legitimacy. The chapter's focus on the three is accurate and appropriately focused.

## The Kartir Inscriptions

The description of Kartir's mystical journey inscription is accurate: the *Arda Wiraz* tradition of afterlife vision is reflected in Kartir's inscription, and the claim to have visited the divine realm is attested in the primary source. The four inscription sites are correctly identified. The titles progression is accurate.

One clarification: Kartir's claim to have "suppressed" non-Zoroastrian communities should be understood in the context of the term he uses in the inscriptions — the verb indicates restriction, reduction of official standing, removal of royal patronage, rather than necessarily physical persecution in all cases. The chapter's phrasing ("curbed") is appropriate.

## Cultural Authenticity — Zoroastrian Worldview

The chapter's most significant achievement from the perspective of this specialist review is the treatment of the fire as a knowledge system rather than a symbol. The phrase "This is not ceremony. This is maintenance" captures something essential: Zoroastrian ritual practice is empirical in its own terms — the fire's condition reflects and affects the cosmic condition; maintenance of the fire is not performed FOR its meaning but because it is the meaning. The chapter understands this.

The treatment of Mani is accurate from the perspective of the Zoroastrian tradition's self-understanding: Mani's theology was not merely competitive but directly challenged the Zoroastrian claim to be the correct and final revelation. Kartir's hostility was not personal; it was doctrinal and institutional. The chapter correctly presents this.

## Summary

**Required corrections:** None. The Zoroastrian framework is represented accurately and with genuine understanding of the tradition from inside.

**Enhancement suggestions:**
1. The *Zand* (Pahlavi commentary) tradition could be briefly mentioned to distinguish the Sassanid contribution to Zoroastrian textual culture from the Avestan texts themselves.
2. The specific Avestan terms (*asha*, *druj*) are used correctly; the chapter could benefit from noting that the Gathas themselves use Old Avestan, while the later Avesta uses Younger Avestan — different registers within the same tradition. This would deepen the "knowledge system at method level" dimension for specialist readers.

Both enhancements are optional. The chapter as written meets the accuracy standard.

**Board Recommendation:** PASS.
