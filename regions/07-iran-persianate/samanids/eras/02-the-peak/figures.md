# Figures — Era 2: The Peak
## Samanids — c. 875–943 CE

---

## Ismail ibn Ahmad al-Samani (c. 842–907 CE)

He was not born to rule an empire, and he did not call what he built one. His grandfather Saman Khuda had been a Zoroastrian nobleman from the Balkh region who converted to Islam and offered his loyalty to the Abbasid caliphate; his father and uncles had been rewarded with provincial governorships in the east. Ismail inherited the governorship of Transoxiana and held it with the patient competence of a man who understood that legitimacy was built over decades, not seized in a single campaign.

The campaign against the Saffarids in 900 CE is where his historical presence becomes sharpest. He sent the captured Saffarid commander to Baghdad in chains — not because he was cruel, but because he understood exactly what the gesture communicated to the caliph: I have solved your eastern problem. The caliph confirmed him. After that, Ismail's correspondence with Baghdad remained formally correct, deferential in tone, punctual in its tribute remittances, and entirely his own in its actual exercise of power.

What distinguished Ismail from the other provincial strongmen who rose and fell in the unstable eastern Islamic world of the ninth century was his administration. He understood that a state is not an army — it is a postal system, a tax register, a court of appeal, a predictable set of rules applied consistently enough that merchants will plan their routes around them and farmers will store surplus rather than hiding it. The *diwan* he built in Bukhara was staffed by Iranian administrative families who had been learning Persian bureaucratic practice since before the Islamic conquest. He kept them. He paid them regularly. He promoted on merit in ways that were unusual enough for the period to be worth remarking on.

He died in 907 CE, sixty-some years old, after an illness contracted on campaign. The mausoleum was already under construction. He had seen to that. A man who commissions his own tomb during his lifetime is not being morbid; he is making a statement about how he expects history to remember him. The building has outlasted every dynasty in the region by more than a thousand years. His estimate of his own permanence was, in the end, conservative.

## Abu Abdallah Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Jayhani (d. c. 920s CE)

The chief secretary served both Ismail Samani and his son Ahmad, which in itself tells you something: he was the kind of administrator that dynasties keep across succession because they cannot afford to lose him. His *Kitab al-Masalik wa'l-Mamalik* — the Book of Roads and Kingdoms — was a geographical treatise in Arabic that organized the known world by its routes and administrative divisions, the kind of document that could be written only by someone who had read every geographical treatise before him and had access to the reports of traveling merchants, diplomats, and military commanders. It was authoritative enough to be cited by geographical writers for two centuries after his death.

His private intellectual life is harder to see, and this is not an accident. Al-Jayhani was a man of the overlap — he occupied the boundary between the Arabic administrative tradition and the Persian cultural inheritance, and he inhabited that boundary productively rather than anxiously. His treatise is in Arabic because his intended audience was international. The conversations he had in his study, in lamplight, were in Persian because the questions he was asking there were Iranian questions that had no Arabic vocabulary.

There is no record of his own poetry, if he wrote any. He may have considered it a form of ostentation he could not afford, or he may simply have understood that his talent was organizational rather than lyrical — he was the man who made the library possible, who managed the correspondence networks that kept the scholarship funded, who provided the stable institutional framework within which Rudaki could be impractical and inspired. The lamp habit — working in firelight regardless of the time of day — is a detail from a later biographical notice, and it is almost certainly the kind of small truth that gets preserved because it reveals character: a man for whom the quality of his thinking mattered more than the conditions of his comfort.

## Abu Abdallah Jafar ibn Muhammad Rudaki (c. 858–940/941 CE)

The first great poet of Classical Persian literature was, depending on which source you trust, blind from birth, blinded by punishment in old age, or simply attributed blindness by a later tradition that associated his inner vision with the iconic blindness of Homer and other canonical poets. The uncertainty matters less than what the tradition made of it: a court poet so gifted that the tradition made of his blindness a kind of hearing — the compensation the gift required.

He came from Rudak, in the Samarkand region — a village, not a city, which suggests that his training in Arabic and Persian poetic forms was acquired through patronage networks rather than institutional education, and which makes his eventual mastery of those forms more remarkable, not less. By the time he appears in the Samanid court record, he is already established: the *Amir al-shu'ara'*, the commander of poets, a title that implies both preeminence and administrative function. He was the court poet, which meant he was also the court's voice on occasions of state.

His method was formal and his sensibility was concrete. The qasida's architecture — *nasib*, *rahil*, *madih* — he inhabited with the ease of a man who does not need to think about the skeleton of a building in order to furnish a room. What he put inside the form was Iranian: the dawn, the fire, the garden in the first warmth after winter, the smell of musk and wine and mountain snow. These were not decorations. They were the vocabulary of a specific emotional world — the world of Iranian literary culture, which had been thinking in these images for centuries before Islam and which had continued thinking in them, quietly, in the oral tradition, while the administrative classes were learning to write in Arabic.

The "Mother of Wine" poem — *Mader-e May* — is one of the few fragments attributable to him with reasonable confidence. It opens in the dawn hour, which in the Islamic day is the time of the *fajr* prayer, and it describes wine rising from sleep and coming with a cup. This is not sacrilege; it is the Persian tradition of the *saqi* poem, in which wine serves as the vehicle for a kind of intoxicated perception of the world that has nothing to do with actual drunkenness. The Sufi poets who came after Rudaki would develop this register into explicit mystical allegory. Rudaki was doing something more elemental: he was demonstrating that the Persian language could carry this kind of double meaning — the surface pleasure and the beneath-surface seriousness — with the same effortlessness that Arabic poetry could carry it.

He lived into old age, long enough to see Nasr ibn Ahmad's reign begin to falter and new powers rising on the northern steppe. The later tradition says he died in poverty, his court position lost when a new generation of patrons had different tastes. This may be true; it may be the convention by which later writers remind audiences that poets are always precarious, whatever their moment of glory. What is not conventional is the survival of his fragments. A thousand years after his death, in the language he helped to make, people were still reading what he had made.

## Nasr ibn Ahmad al-Samani (r. 914–943 CE)

The longest-reigning Samanid amir governed from adolescence to middle age, and the court he presided over was the fullest expression of what the Samanid synthesis could be. He was, by the accounts that survive, genuinely pious — the story of the "Mother of Wine" poem is told as evidence of how unusual it was for him to relent in his religious practice — and genuinely literary, in the sense that he valued poetry as something more than court furniture. The distinction matters. A ruler who tolerates poetry as decoration will fund it; a ruler who understands poetry as statecraft will invest in it.

Nasr's thirty-year reign saw the library grow to its peak collection, the prose translation project produce its first major works, and Rudaki's career reach its fullest extent. The relationship between a patron and a court poet in this tradition was not sentimental; it was transactional in a specific sense — the poet provided the cultural elevation the court needed, the court provided the material support the poet needed, and both parties understood that the transaction served purposes larger than either of them. Nasr understood that a court in Bukhara that patronized the best poets in the Persian-speaking world was a court that commanded the allegiance of the Iranian educated class. Rudaki understood that a patron who actually listened to poems was a patron worth composing for.

The anecdote about Nasr ordering his camp struck and riding south after hearing Rudaki's Bukhara poem is almost certainly embellished. But it persisted in the tradition because it captured something true about how this particular ruler and this particular poet were understood to have related: the poet's voice could reach places that military and administrative argument could not, and a wise ruler knew this and made use of it. The story gives Nasr the quality that later tradition wanted him to have — a ruler responsive to beauty, and therefore a ruler who understood his civilization's highest purposes. Whether the historical Nasr was this man or not, the court he maintained made it possible for others to become so.

## Abu Ali ibn Muhammad Bal'ami (d. 974 CE)

The vizier who commissioned the Persian translation of al-Tabari's history appears in this era as a secondary figure — his major work belongs to Era 3 — but the commission itself falls within the reign of Ahmad ibn Ismail (r. 907–914 CE), Ismail Samani's son, and the intellectual project it represents is the direct consequence of the literary renaissance that Ismail had sponsored.

Bal'ami was a scholar-administrator of the same type as al-Jayhani: a man who understood that cultural policy was infrastructure. The Tabari translation was not a luxury; it was the provision of historical memory to a population whose administrative language had been Arabic for two centuries and whose oral historical memory was in Persian. By translating — and adapting, and expanding — al-Tabari into Persian prose, Bal'ami made it possible for the educated Iranian class to access the Islamic world's most comprehensive historical account in their own language. He also, in the process, helped establish the conventions of Persian prose: the sentence structures, the vocabulary for abstract ideas, the way of handling time and causation in narrative.

The Samanid court produced him, and he produced the tradition. The chain of causation is direct.
