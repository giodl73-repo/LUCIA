# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Review, Round 1 — Opening

**Chapter:** Deep Time in the Forest — Era 1
**Reviewer:** Wade Davis (P-4)
**Stage:** PANEL-1

---

This opening gets the fundamental thing right: the forest peoples are not primitive survivors of an ancient way of life. They are sophisticated intellectuals engaged in a daily, rigorous, adaptive practice of knowing a world of extraordinary complexity. The author's instinct to anchor the opening in the honeyguide relationship is exactly right — not because it is picturesque (though it is), but because it is epistemologically precise. The honeyguide relationship is not a curiosity or a charming habit. It is a formal knowledge system with rules, enforcement mechanisms, and a transmission problem. The opening captures this.

What I want to push on: the opening establishes the depth of ecological knowledge but does not yet convey the epistemological framework. The forest peoples do not simply know facts about the forest — they know the forest through a particular way of knowing that is different in kind, not just degree, from what a botanist does. The Mbuti relationship to the forest is relational and reciprocal: you know the forest by being in right relationship with it. Knowledge and ethics are not separate. This is the point the chapter must develop.

The "molimo" paragraph is the closest the opening comes to this: "the forest is not a backdrop. It is the entity itself." This is good, but it needs more development in the chapter. The molimo ceremony is not just a ritual; it is an epistemological practice — a way of staying in calibrated relationship with the entity that provides knowledge as well as food. When the forest "sleeps" and bad things happen, this is not superstition; it is a diagnostic. Something in the relationship has gone wrong, and the ceremony is the correction.

The chapter must also address the diversity of the forest peoples. The opening uses "Mbuti" but the overview establishes that Baka, Aka, and Mbuti are distinct communities with different practices. The chapter will want to name these differences while honoring what connects them. The risk of treating "forest peoples" as a single cultural unit is the same risk as treating "Asian cultures" as a unit — the diversity is the point, and collapsing it is a form of the very flattening we are trying to avoid.

The specific detail of the honeyguide — "the particular rolling trill that the bird has learned, across generations, to associate with a human who will follow and leave something behind" — is excellent. This is knowledge at the level of method: how it works, specifically, in practice. The chapter should sustain this level of precision throughout.

**The question I'd push on:** What is the epistemological framework that unifies the honeyguide relationship, the polyphonic music, the tracking knowledge, the medicinal plant knowledge? They are not unrelated practices. They share a common way of knowing — relational, reciprocal, embodied, specific. The chapter should articulate this not as a claim but as a demonstration: show all these practices, and let the framework become visible through the showing.
