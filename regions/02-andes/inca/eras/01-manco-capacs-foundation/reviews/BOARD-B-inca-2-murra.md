# BOARD-B-inca-2 — John Murra — Board Review of Manco Cápac's Foundation

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Vertical archipelago theory, Inca economic organization, ayllu structure, mit'a labor system.

## Historical Accuracy

- **Correct:** The vertical archipelago is described as a political-ecological system, not merely as altitude farming. The chapter explicitly states: "The vertical archipelago was not farming at different heights. It was a political-ecological system." This is the essential distinction.
- **Correct:** The ecological zones are accurately described (coast, valley, mid-slope/cloud forest, puna), and the products of each zone are correctly attributed (fish/cotton/guano from coast, maize from valleys, coca from mid-slopes, llamas/alpacas/chuño from puna).
- **Correct:** The ayllu is described as pre-Inca and as the building block the Inca organized through rather than replaced.
- **Correct:** The llama is accurately described as the technology that made the vertical archipelago operational — the thirty-kilogram load capacity is the standard figure.
- **Correct:** The mit'a seed is planted appropriately — communal labor practices at the ayllu level (ayni and minka) are shown as the origin of what will become the state-level mit'a.

## Cultural Authenticity

The chapter avoids the two mistakes I most feared: it does not treat the vertical archipelago as "interesting but primitive" farming, and it does not treat the mit'a as "forced labor with a nice name." The reciprocal logic is correctly described, from chicha ceremonies to kuraka mediation. The economic world of the pre-imperial ayllu is rendered as a functioning system, not as a precursor to something better.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The chuño production process (freezing and sun-drying potatoes on the puna) is described as a technology, which it was — food preservation that enabled long-term storage and long-distance transport.
- The kuraka's role as mediator, not commander, is correctly described.
- The distinction between ayni (household reciprocity) and minka (communal labor) is present.
- The qollqa (storehouse) is mentioned as the mechanism of redistribution.

## What Needs Correction

1. **Waru waru attribution:** The chapter attributes the waru waru (raised-field) system to the Aymara at Titicaca, which is correct as far as it goes, but should note that the Tiwanaku state (which preceded the Inca in the Titicaca region by centuries) developed the waru waru to a level of sophistication that the Inca inherited rather than invented. This prevents the implication that the Inca were building from scratch.

2. **Spondylus significance:** The chapter mentions spondylus as "traded as a sacred material" but understates its importance. Spondylus from the warm waters off Ecuador was arguably the single most valued ritual material in the Andes — more important than gold — and its trade routes running north-south along the coast and into the highlands were among the oldest long-distance exchange networks in the Americas. A phrase strengthening this would be appropriate.

3. **Coca's role:** The chapter mentions coca correctly as the mid-slope product but could strengthen the description of its ritual significance. Coca was not merely "chewed for energy." It was offered to Pachamama at every planting, to the huacas at every ceremony, to the dead at every funeral. Its presence in the mid-altitude zone was one reason that zone was politically critical.

## Verdict: APPROVED WITH CONDITIONS

Minor corrections: strengthen Tiwanaku precedent, spondylus significance, and coca's ritual role. The economic and ecological framework is sound.
