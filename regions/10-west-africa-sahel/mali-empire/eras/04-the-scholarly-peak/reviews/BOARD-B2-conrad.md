# B-2 -- Conrad -- Board Review of "The Scholarly Peak"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Mande oral traditions; the Sundiata epic; the social and political role of the jeli (griot) in Mande society; the relationship between oral and written historical sources.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The jelilu marking Musa's passing "by the reigns that followed rather than by numbered years" is an accurate representation of how the oral tradition operates -- genealogical rather than calendrical reckoning.
- The jelilu at Mansa Sulayman's court "singing the names of the dynasty" is consistent with their documented role as praise-singers and genealogists at the Mande court.
- The reference to the Kurukan Fuga establishing women's right to speak in assembly is consistent with the oral tradition as recorded by multiple scholars, though the specific provisions attributed to the Kurukan Fuga vary by version. The chapter does not over-specify, which is appropriate.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's treatment of the oral tradition is lighter in this era than in Era 3, which is appropriate -- the jelilu are present but not the focus. The scholarly tradition is a written tradition, and the chapter correctly centers the written without denigrating the oral.
- The passage about Ibn Battuta finding the jelilu still singing is an effective bridge between eras that acknowledges the oral tradition's continuity without making it compete with the chapter's written-tradition focus.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The shift from Era 3's jeli-driven voice to Era 4's scholar-driven voice is well-handled. The jelilu appear in their proper role (at court, marking transitions) without being forced into the center of a story that belongs to the manuscript tradition.
- The absence of the jeli from the chapter's landing is the right choice. Era 3 closed with the jelilu singing. Era 4 closes with the manuscripts surviving. Two different preservation mechanisms, each given its era.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter mentions "the intermediary" who relays the mansa's words at court but does not identify this role. In the Mande tradition, this is the jeli's function -- the griot serves as the voice of the ruler, the intermediary between the king and the court. The chapter should identify the intermediary as a jeli or dyeli performing a specific function within the Mande political system. This is not a minor point: the jeli's role as royal spokesperson is one of the most important dimensions of the nyamakalaw's political function.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS. The single correction above is substantive but easily addressed. The chapter's handling of the oral-written relationship is careful and appropriate for this era.
