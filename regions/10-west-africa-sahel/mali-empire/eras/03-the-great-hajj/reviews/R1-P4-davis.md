## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of "The Great Hajj" (Opening)

**Overall:** This is one of the rare openings that lets a non-Western knowledge system carry the narrative on its own terms. The jeli tradition is not explained to the reader as an exotic curiosity — it is enacted, and the reader is inside it before they have time to adopt a spectator's distance. I was moved.

**What works:**

- The opening act belongs to Bala Faseke Kouyate, not to Musa. The chapter begins inside the jeli's function — the singing of names, the genealogical recitation — and treats that function as the event itself, not as preamble to the "real" action. This is a structural choice that communicates intellectual respect more powerfully than any explanatory paragraph could. The jeli is not described; the jeli performs. The reader witnesses the institution from within.

- The line "a jeli does not choose which ancestors to remember. A jeli remembers them all" is the single best sentence in the opening. It communicates the logic of the tradition — its completeness, its refusal to edit for convenience — without translating it into Western archival terminology. The jeli is not presented as "an oral historian" or "a West African equivalent of a chronicler." The jeli is a jeli. The text trusts the reader to understand what that means by showing it in action.

- The Kurukan Fuga is treated as a genuine governing logic, not as a quaint custom. "The Kurukan Fuga demanded generosity. A king who does not give is not a king." This is presented as constitutional reasoning — a framework of obligation that shapes political behavior — and the text does not feel the need to validate it by comparison to Magna Carta or any European equivalent. It simply states the logic and lets it stand.

- The gold is framed through Mande political philosophy, not through European astonishment. "So the gold was not extravagance. The gold was the point." This is the chapter's thesis sentence, and it operates entirely within the Mande intellectual world. The generosity is explained by the covenant, not by a desire to impress foreigners. The foreigners' astonishment comes later, and it arrives as a consequence of Mande logic, not as the reason for the journey. This is the correct hierarchy of explanation.

- The dundun drum is described as speaking "in the tonal language of the Mande" and saying "what words alone cannot." This is not metaphor. The dundun is a surrogate speech instrument in tonal Mande languages — it literally encodes linguistic content. The chapter treats this as fact, not as poetic fancy, which is exactly right.

- The sensory layering — "dust and shea butter and woodsmoke," the harmattan haze, the sagging saddlebags — grounds the scene in physical reality. This is not a mythologized Africa. It is a real place with weather and smells and weight.

**What doesn't:**

- The phrase "The Arab scholars who later recorded this could not agree on the number" introduces the external validation frame too early. The opening has been operating beautifully inside the Mande world — the jeli's song, the covenant's logic, the gold's meaning. Then, at the moment of quantification, the text shifts to Arab scholarly authority as if the numbers only become real when someone writes them down. I understand the historiographical necessity — the written Arabic sources are the reason we have specific figures. But the framing implies that the caravan's scale becomes factual only when external literate observers record it. Consider letting the numbers arrive through the narrative's own voice — "five hundred men carried gold" already does this perfectly two sentences earlier — and saving the historiographical note about conflicting Arab sources for later, or for a footnote. The jeli who sang the departure was also a witness. The chapter should not accidentally suggest that oral witness is less factual than written witness.

- "He was tall. The sources agree on this." This is a small moment, but it pulls the reader out of the scene and into the archive. Which sources? The Arabic ones. The sentence implicitly privileges written testimony over the jeli tradition that is performing the scene. The jelilu certainly had things to say about Musa's physical presence. By citing anonymous "sources" — a word that in historical writing almost always means written texts — the chapter subtly reinstates the hierarchy it has otherwise been so careful to avoid: the written record as the court of final appeal, the oral tradition as atmosphere.

- The European mapmakers in Majorca appear in the penultimate paragraph as the people who will record Musa's significance for posterity: "Mapmakers in Majorca would draw Musa on a throne... the richest king in the world." This is historically accurate. But the chapter never mentions that Musa's significance was also recorded within the Mande oral tradition — that the jelilu carried his hajj forward for centuries as one of the defining acts of Mande history. By ending the chapter's arc of consequence with European cartography and the Egyptian gold market, the text inadvertently suggests that what made the hajj matter was its impact on the wider (read: non-African) world. The hajj mattered to the Manden first. The jelilu's memory of it is the primary record, not the Catalan Atlas.

- The phrase "help him reshape the Manden" when describing why Musa brought scholars feels slightly teleological — as if contact with the broader Islamic world was a modernization project. The OVERVIEW handles this better, noting that Timbuktu's scholars were participants in, not recipients of, Islamic intellectual culture. The opening might find a way to signal that the scholars Musa sought were additions to an existing intellectual tradition, not corrections of a deficit.

**The question I'd push on:**

Whose memory structures this chapter's sense of what is true? The opening begins magnificently inside the jeli tradition — Bala Faseke Kouyate sings, and we are in the Mande world. But by the final paragraphs, the markers of historical truth have shifted to Arab chroniclers, Egyptian gold prices, and Majorcan cartographers. The chapter's emotional center is Mande. Its evidential center drifts Mediterranean. I want the author to ask: can the closing paragraphs find a way to let the jeli tradition — the thirty generations of memory that carried this story to us — stand as the primary frame of significance? The European and Arab sources are important, but they should arrive inside a story whose authority has already been established by the tradition that was there on the morning Musa raised his hand. The jelilu did not need Majorca to know this was the greatest journey ever made from Niani. Let their knowledge be the last word, not the Catalan Atlas.
