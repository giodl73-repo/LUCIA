# Figures: The Roman Standoff
### Parthia / Era 3 — c. 92 BCE–114 CE

---

## The Suren (personal name unverified, fl. 53 BCE)

The second-ranking family of the Arsacid empire — after the Arsacids themselves — held the hereditary right to crown kings and to provide the empire's primary field armies. The man who led the Parthian force at Carrhae is known in Roman sources as "Surena," which most scholars now understand as a transcription of the family name rather than a personal name; his individual name does not survive in verifiable form. Some later Iranian tradition preserves the name Rustaham, but this cannot be confirmed for the Carrhae commander.

What the sources agree on: he was young, perhaps thirty, when he defeated Crassus. Plutarch describes him as tall, heavily perfumed in the Parthian court fashion, and traveling with a retinue of two hundred concubines carried on camels and a household of two hundred servants — the visible wealth of a man whose family was the kingdom's second power. He assembled an army of ten thousand cavalry and managed the logistics of the camel-arrow resupply system that made the Carrhae rotation possible. He understood the battle not as a charge to be won but as a machine to be maintained.

After Carrhae, he was executed by Orodes II. Plutarch says envy was the cause; Dio suggests structural reasons. Both may be true and neither is necessary to understand the act. The Suren family's hereditary significance — their right to crown kings — made any member of that family whose fame exceeded the king's a structural problem. The Suren general's victory had made him the most celebrated man in the empire. The king who had authorized the campaign did not share in the credit the way a victorious Roman general's commander would have; the Arsacid system distributed both power and glory differently. The general had to die. The family endured.

He is one of the ancient world's most precisely documented military figures in terms of what he actually did — the tactical system at Carrhae is described in enough detail by Plutarch to be reconstructed — and one of the most anonymous in terms of who he was. The name the Romans preserved was the family name. The individual disappeared into the family, which was perhaps the appropriate form of commemoration for a man whose family meant more than he did.

## Orodes II (r. 57–37 BCE)

Orodes II secured the Arsacid throne by having his brother Mithridates III killed, after a civil war that lasted several years and demonstrated the noble-house system's tendency to generate competing royal candidates. He then oversaw the defeat of Crassus at Carrhae, the defeat of Antony's invasion, and the maintenance of the Euphrates border through the most intense period of Roman pressure. He was, by the system's own standards, a successful king: the border held, the trade flowed, the noble houses remained in their relationships with the throne.

He ordered the Suren general executed after Carrhae. This is the most revealing single act of his reign because it shows the Arsacid political intelligence operating in its most internal mode: the recognition that the system that protected the empire externally also generated internal threats that required management. The general who was too successful was as dangerous as the general who had failed. The management was efficient and ruthless, and from inside the Arsacid system it was neither cruel nor ungrateful. It was maintenance.

He received the head of Crassus during a theatrical performance and used it in the play. Whether this was improvised theater or deliberate staging is unknown. Either way, the court understood what had happened: Rome had sent its wealthiest man east with forty thousand soldiers and the Suren had sent back a head. The Arsacid world's sense of this achievement was expressed in a Greek theatrical performance because that was the cultural register available to a Hellenized Mesopotamian court on a winter evening. Both the achievement and its expression were the Arsacid empire's own.

He was eventually killed by his son Phraates IV, who had grown impatient. The Arsacid succession pattern — king killed by his own household — was not an Arsacid peculiarity. It was the system's terminal logic operating at the dynastic level: the same mechanism that made great-house loyalty dependent on the king's continuous performance of generosity also made royal-family loyalty dependent on the king's continuous capacity to hold the throne. When he could not, someone replaced him.

## Phraates IV (r. 37–2 BCE)

The king who received and then returned the Roman standards. The negotiation that produced the return in 20 BCE was one of the most sophisticated diplomatic transactions of the ancient world: Phraates understood that Augustus needed the appearance of a Roman military victory over Parthia more than Parthia needed thirty-year-old bronze standards. He gave Augustus the ceremony. He kept the strategic equilibrium.

This was not weakness. It was the Arsacid doctrine applied diplomatically rather than militarily: do not fight on the enemy's terms when you can give the enemy what he needs without surrendering what you require. At Carrhae, the Suren gave Rome a tactical defeat. At the standards return, Phraates gave Rome a diplomatic theater piece. Both achieved the same end: the Euphrates border held, the Parthian position unchanged.

Phraates was also the king who sent his sons to Rome as hostages — a common diplomatic practice, but in Phraates's case apparently chosen partly to neutralize potential rivals. Four of his sons lived in Rome as honored guests of Augustus, which meant four potential Arsacid king-candidates were under Roman custody and unavailable for the domestic succession struggles that the noble-house system generated. This is the Arsacid internal management problem visible from a different angle: the king used Roman hospitality as a tool for managing his own family's dynastic ambitions. Rome and Parthia, in this arrangement, were cooperating to maintain the equilibrium that both understood as preferable to renewed warfare.

He was eventually poisoned by a freed Italian slave woman named Musa, whom Augustus had sent as a gift and who had become Phraates's wife and the mother of his successor. The manner of his death — poisoned by a Roman-origin woman installed in the Arsacid court — was considered remarkable enough to be recorded by multiple sources. Inside the Arsacid system, it was another succession, another transition, the noble houses again evaluating which candidate they would support.

## Isidore of Charax (fl. c. 50 BCE–25 CE)

A geographer who wrote in Greek from the port city of Charax Spasinu, at the head of the Persian Gulf. His *Stathmoi Parthikoi* — Parthian Stations — is a road-book: distances between waypoints along the major Silk Road routes through Parthian territory, with the names of the towns, the customs-posts, and the geographical features that marked the route. It is not a literary work. It is a practical document, the kind of thing a merchant or a government administrator would use to plan a journey.

Isidore is the chapter's witness to the Silk Road because he is the closest thing to an internal source — someone working within the Parthian system, writing from a Parthian-governed city, recording the infrastructure the Arsacids maintained. He was Greek-named (the city of Charax was a Hellenistic foundation) and probably Greek-educated, which meant he was exactly the kind of person who inhabited the Arsacid empire's Hellenized western zone: not Parthian by origin, not purely Roman in orientation, living within the system the Arsacids had built across multiple languages and cultural traditions.

His text survives in a fragmentary form. What it preserves is enough to trace the main route from the Euphrates crossing to the Bactrian frontier: the stations, the distances, the notes about which sections were controlled by which regional authority. It is evidence that someone in the Arsacid empire was keeping track of the road — measuring it, naming it, recording its structure — which means the road was organized, administered, and understood as a system, not merely as a series of individual traders' routes that happened to follow the same general direction.

He does not appear in the chapter as a dramatized figure. He appears as the intelligence behind the Silk Road section: the person who measured the road, whose measurements survive, and who therefore gives us the closest available view of the infrastructure the Arsacid customs revenue was paying to maintain. In figures.md, he is acknowledged as the source. The chapter treats his knowledge as the chapter's knowledge of the road.

## Abgar (name preserved only in Plutarch)

The guide who led Crassus's army from the Euphrates into the Syrian plain. His name appears in Plutarch's *Life of Crassus* as one of the men who advised Crassus's march route. Some scholars identify him with a historical figure named Abgar II of Osroene, a small kingdom east of the Euphrates, whose loyalty the Romans assumed but whose actual affiliations were more complicated. Others treat him as a fictional or composite character created by Plutarch or Plutarch's sources to give the disaster a human agent.

The chapter treats him as the figure Plutarch preserves: a guide from east of the Euphrates, paid by the Romans, who led the army where the Parthian cavalry was waiting. Whether he was acting on Arsacid instructions, on his own calculation, or simply following a well-paid contract and knowing what the outcome would be: the sources do not say. His interiority is not available. What he did is on the record. Why he did it is not.

This uncertainty is itself evidence. Roman sources needed an explanation for Carrhae — how could the best army in the world be destroyed by a force one-quarter its size? The explanation required human agency: someone who knew and who deceived. Abgar performed this function in the narrative. Whether he performed it in fact, and with what understanding of what he was doing, is the kind of question that the available sources cannot answer and that honest historical writing should not pretend to answer.

He disappears from the record before the arrows begin. This is the figures.md's conclusion about him: a presence in the narrative at the moment that matters, gone before the record can locate him further. His route after leaving the Roman encampment is unknown. His fate is unknown. His name survives because the Romans needed a name for what had happened to them, and he was the name they had.
