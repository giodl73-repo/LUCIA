# Figures — The Unification (Era 1)

## Temujin / Chinggis Khan (c. 1162–1227)

Son of Yesugei Baghatur of the Borjigin clan. Born on the steppe near the Onon River. Father poisoned by Tatars when Temujin was approximately nine. Family abandoned by their clan. Rose through alliance, warfare, and institutional innovation to unite the steppe clans. Proclaimed Chinggis Khan at the quriltai of 1206.

**What we know:** Temujin's life is principally documented in the *Secret History of the Mongols* (c. 1240), a Mongol-language text written for a Mongol audience within a generation of the events described. It is supplemented by Rashid al-Din's *Compendium of Chronicles* (c. 1307) and Juvaini's *History of the World-Conqueror* (c. 1260). The core biography is consistent across these sources: the childhood abandonment, the early alliances, the break with Jamukha, the defeat of the Kereyid and Naiman, the proclamation at the Onon. The decimal system, the yasa, and the yam are attested by multiple independent sources.

**What we cannot know:** Temujin's inner life. The Secret History records actions, decisions, and occasional quoted speech, but it is not a psychological portrait. The chapter renders Temujin through his actions and through the system he built, not through invented interiority. His birth year is debated (1155, 1162, and 1167 are all proposed in the scholarship; Ratchnevsky favors 1162). His physical appearance is described in later sources (tall, with "cat's eyes" and a long beard) but these descriptions may be conventional rather than observed.

**Sources:** *The Secret History of the Mongols* (Onon translation, 2001); Rashid al-Din, *Jami al-Tawarikh*; Juvaini, *Tarikh-i Jahangusha*; Ratchnevsky, *Genghis Khan: His Life and Legacy* (1991); Atwood, *Encyclopedia of Mongolia and the Mongol Empire* (2004).

## Hoelun

Temujin's mother. Originally from the Olkhonud clan. Captured by Yesugei from Chiledu of the Merkid (this abduction would later cause the Merkid to attack Temujin's camp and capture Borte in retribution). After Yesugei's death, Hoelun held her family together by foraging along the Onon.

**What we know:** The Secret History presents Hoelun as a central figure of the early narrative. She feeds her children on wild garlic, onions, and fish. She rebukes Temujin for killing Begter, with a specificity of language that suggests the tradition valued her words. She is consistently presented as fierce, resourceful, and morally authoritative. Her voice in the text is one of the few individual voices that emerges with distinct character.

**What we cannot know:** Her own perspective on the events the Secret History records from Temujin's vantage point. Her life after Temujin's rise is not detailed in the sources. The tradition carries her role in the founding story, not her independent biography.

**Sources:** *Secret History* (sections 60-79, Onon); Ratchnevsky (1991), chapter 1.

## Jamukha

Temujin's anda (blood brother). Chief of the Jadaran clan. Represented the traditional model of steppe confederation: personal charisma, kinship alliances, temporary coalitions. His rivalry with Temujin was the central political conflict of the steppe in the 1180s-1200s.

**What we know:** The Secret History devotes extensive attention to Jamukha. The anda bond, the shared childhood, the break over camp placement, the years of intermittent warfare, the final capture and execution are all recorded in detail. The tradition presents Jamukha as a figure of genuine stature whose defeat was consequential. His request for bloodless execution and Temujin's grant of it suggest the Mongol tradition recognized the tragedy of the rivalry.

**What we cannot know:** The Jadaran clan's own account of the rivalry. The Secret History is written from Temujin's lineage's perspective, and Jamukha's motivations are rendered through that lens. His political vision for the steppe, if it differed from simple confederation, is not independently recoverable.

**Honest limit:** Jamukha is the antagonist of the Secret History's narrative, and the text is shaped by the victor's tradition. The chapter presents him as the tradition presents him: a man of ability who represented a different path, not as a villain. His boiling of captives is noted as the steppe's own punishment for oath-breakers, not as unique cruelty.

**Sources:** *Secret History* (sections 116-201, Onon); Ratchnevsky (1991); Atwood (2004), entry on Jamukha.

## Borte

Temujin's first wife. Of the Onggirat clan. Betrothed to Temujin in childhood. Captured by the Merkid early in their marriage and held for months before Temujin, with the help of Toghrul and Jamukha, rescued her.

**What we know:** Borte's capture and rescue is recorded in the Secret History and is one of the formative events of Temujin's early career: it forced his first major military alliance. Her first son, Jochi, was born soon after the rescue, and the question of Jochi's paternity haunted the Mongol succession for generations. The Secret History addresses this obliquely. Borte is described as Temujin's chief counselor in multiple instances, and her advice is presented as influential.

**What we cannot know:** Her experience during the Merkid captivity. The Secret History records the event from Temujin's perspective (the rescue, the reunion), not from hers.

**Sources:** *Secret History* (sections 98-113, Onon); Ratchnevsky (1991); Broadbridge, *Women and the Making of the Mongol Empire* (2018).

## Teb-Tenggeri (Kokochu)

Shaman of the Khongkhotan clan. Proclaimed Temujin as Chinggis Khan at the 1206 quriltai, declaring Tengri's mandate. Later grew dangerously powerful, claiming spiritual authority that rivaled the khan's political authority. Was killed (his back was broken) with Chinggis Khan's tacit approval.

**What we know:** The Secret History records both his role in the 1206 proclamation and his subsequent overreach and death. His story illustrates a structural tension in the Mongol system: the relationship between spiritual authority (the shaman's access to Tengri) and political authority (the khan's claim to rule by Tengri's mandate). The yasa resolved this tension in favor of the political.

**What we cannot know:** Whether Teb-Tenggeri genuinely believed his own claims or was engaged in a political power play. The Secret History presents his overreach as genuine and dangerous.

**Sources:** *Secret History* (sections 244-246, Onon); Ratchnevsky (1991), chapter 5.

## Subotai (c. 1176–1248)

Son of a blacksmith. Became one of the greatest military commanders in history. His career illustrates the merit principle of the decimal system: a man of no lineage who rose to command tumens (units of ten thousand) based solely on demonstrated ability.

**What we know:** Subotai is well-attested in both Mongol and external sources. He led the reconnaissance expedition around the Caspian Sea (1221-1223) that reached the Kipchak steppe and defeated a coalition of Rus princes at the Battle of the Kalka River. He later commanded the European campaign of 1241-1242 that reached Hungary and Poland. His origins as a blacksmith's son are recorded in the Secret History.

**What we cannot know:** His personal relationship with Chinggis Khan beyond what the sources record as military and administrative.

**Sources:** *Secret History*; Rashid al-Din; May, *The Mongol Art of War* (2007); Gabriel, *Subotai the Valiant* (2004).

## A Note on Sources

No composite figures are used in this chapter. All named individuals appear in the *Secret History of the Mongols* and, in most cases, in at least one independent source (Rashid al-Din, Juvaini, or Chinese dynastic histories). The Secret History is the primary source for Temujin's early life, and it is a Mongol text carrying Mongol perspectives. It was composed after Chinggis Khan's death (c. 1240) and reflects the politics of the post-Chinggis succession, but its core biographical narrative is corroborated by independent sources where they overlap. The tradition's perspective shapes the presentation of all figures, and this is acknowledged throughout.

The chapter relies on the Secret History's account of events that no outside source records (the childhood on the Onon, the killing of Begter, the break with Jamukha). These episodes are presented as the Mongol tradition's own account of its founder, not as independently verified fact. The tradition's authority is treated the same way the jelilu's authority is treated in the Mali chapters: as a legitimate form of historical transmission with its own strengths and limitations.
