# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe

**Opening reviewed:** Brazil, Era 2 — A Colônia — Açúcar, Ouro, e Escravos
**Round:** Panel-1

---

The opening passes the test I always apply first: could someone from the culture being described read this and feel their story was told from inside? I believe the answer is yes, and I want to explain why, because it is not automatic.

The axé is presented as fact, not as belief. This is the essential distinction. Many writers who mean well describe African religious traditions from outside — "they believed that," "according to their cosmology," "in their understanding." This opening does not do that. The axé travels in bodies. It arrives in Palmares intact. It is not inventoried. The register is not the anthropologist's register of documentation; it is the community's register of fact. That is an act of authorial respect, and it is not easy to sustain.

The call-and-response insertion works because it is not labeled. It does not say "in the call-and-response tradition of the community." It simply does it. The reader who knows this tradition will recognize it immediately. The reader who does not will feel the form before understanding it — which is the right order.

What I want to examine carefully in the chapter: the Yoruba orixás are named (orixás), and Bantu terminology is invoked (axé from the Yoruba/Fon tradition; the Bantu organization mentioned in the context of Palmares). The chapter will need to hold the internal diversity of the African diaspora in Brazil with precision. The Nagô/Yoruba tradition, which would become dominant in Candomblé, was not the only tradition brought. The Bantu peoples were numerically significant in Palmares specifically. If the chapter conflates these traditions or treats "African" as a uniform category, it will have reproduced a form of the same violence it is trying to resist — erasing internal difference in the name of solidarity.

**The question I'd push on:** Who are the women of Palmares? Zumbi is the face the chapter will carry. But quilombo communities were not organized around a single male leader in the way that the Portuguese reports — and later Brazilian nationalist mythology — have constructed them. The terreiro tradition, which the opening correctly connects to Palmares's legacy, was and is predominantly female-led: the ialorixás, the mae-de-santo. Where are the women in this story? The opening gives us Zumbi. The chapter needs to give us someone else as well.
