# Board Review — Michael Heckenberger
## Upper Xingu Archaeology | The Network's Height | Kuikuro Era 2

---

The chapter's material claims are consistent with the published archaeological record. Specific verifications:

**Town scale and network extent:** The chapter does not assign specific population numbers to Kuhikugu or the ceremony, which is the correct scholarly choice — the population estimates for the pre-contact upper Xingu range widely in the literature (Heckenberger's 2008 paper estimates 50,000+ for the broader network, but single-town estimates remain contested). The chapter's phrase "several hundred" for Kuhikugu's resident population is conservative and defensible.

**Road geometry:** "Runs straight through forest that does not want to be straight" — correct. The mapped roads run in straight lines between plaza centers, with deviations only for major topographic features. The maintenance requirement is accurately characterized.

**Terra preta formation:** The chapter's description of the enrichment process (charcoal, bone, organic waste) is accurate. The 2-meter depth deposits at the oldest Kuhikugu residential areas are real. The characterization of the soil as dramatically different from surrounding lateritic soils is accurate. One minor precision: the chapter says "charcoal from the cooking fires" — more precisely, the terra preta charcoal signature is consistent with low-temperature smoldering rather than high-temperature open fire combustion. This distinction matters for understanding the deliberateness of the practice. Suggest adding "slow-burning" or "smoldering" to the charcoal reference.

**Plaza dimensions:** The chapter does not give specific dimensions, which is appropriate — the plaza archaeology at Kuhikugu supports large gatherings but the chapter's claims stay within what can be supported.

**Kuhikugu as network's largest node:** Accurate per the 2008 Science paper.

**The epidemic passage:** "In twenty years — or thirty, or forty; the timing is not precise" — this honest uncertainty is correct and important. The introduction of epidemic disease into the upper Xingu is archaeologically dated to the 16th century but the specific timing of first contact with epidemic-carrying populations is not precisely established. The chapter's handling of this is exemplary.

**Corrections required:**

1. Minor: "charcoal from the cooking fires" should be "charcoal from slow-burning fires" or "smoldering organic waste" to accurately reflect the biochar formation process.

**Endorsement:** The chapter accurately represents the archaeological record and does not overclaim. It is one of the most archaeologically grounded narrative treatments of the upper Xingu network I have seen outside the scholarly literature. The non-specialist reader will come away with a correct understanding of what the pre-contact upper Xingu was and how it worked.

— M. Heckenberger (board)
