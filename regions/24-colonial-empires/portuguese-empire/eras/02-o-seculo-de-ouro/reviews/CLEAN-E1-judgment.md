### Outside-Lens Violations

**None found.**

The chapter maintains the empire's self-understanding throughout. The Crusader mission is named in the empire's own terms, not as delusion or cynicism. Da Gama's burning of the Miri is rendered as the act of a man completing a calculation within the empire's logic, not as an atrocity judged from outside. The Lusotropicalism treatment is particularly clean: the chapter states Freyre's argument in Freyre's own terms, then presents the evidence (the casado, the Goa Inquisition, the enslaved people of São Tomé) without a verdict. "The Lusotropicalism myth erred not in the observation but in what it concluded from it" — this is the most direct statement in the chapter, and it is a structural observation, not a moral verdict. Acceptable.

### Potential Concerns (Resolved in Text)

The Albuquerque passage on diverting the Nile is handled correctly: "The project was not carried out, partly because it was physically impossible..." The physical impossibility is stated as a fact, not a verdict on Albuquerque's judgment. No correction needed.

The passage on the Goa Inquisition's burning practices — "The people burned at Goa's auto-da-fé were burned the same way they were burned at Seville" — is a comparative statement that could read as outside judgment. However, it appears in the Lusotropicalism section, immediately after Freyre's argument has been stated, and it serves as evidence rather than verdict: the comparison is between two Catholic inquisitions, both using the same practices. It is not an anachronistic moral judgment; it is a structural comparison that the historical record supports. Acceptable as written.
