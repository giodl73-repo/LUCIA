# BOARD REVIEW — Curtis Keim
## Mangbetu — Era 2: The Expansion Under Tuba

**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

My primary concern when reviewing chapters about the Mangbetu court is always the same: does the chapter tell the full story, or does it tell the court's version of its own story? The court version is beautiful and real; it is also partial. A chapter that only renders the aesthetic peak without the labor conditions that sustained it has turned a court culture into a museum exhibit.

This chapter passes that test. Section 3 addresses the subject populations with appropriate specificity — the distinction between Mangbetu noblewomen and women from captured or tributary groups, the different labor relationships, the structure of the incorporated lineages. The chapter does not dwell on this or give it disproportionate space (which would be its own distortion), but it is present.

**One substantive note:**

The chapter describes the expansion under Tuba as involving "military pressure, through negotiated alliance, through marriage." This is accurate as far as it goes. My work establishes that slave raiding was also a mechanism — that the Mangbetu expansion incorporated populations partly through the capture and integration of enslaved women and men into the court's labor system. The chapter's phrasing ("captured or tributary groups") gestures at this without using the specific term.

The chapter does not use the word "slavery" anywhere. Given the LUCIA principles (no outside moral judgment), this is defensible — the chapter describes the material conditions without applying a term that carries modern moral weight the chapter is not positioned to carry. However, the notes.md or figures.md should be more explicit about the term and the scholarly record, for the sake of completeness in the production bible.

**On territorial extent:**

The characterization of Tuba's expansion as the "greatest territorial extent" is consistent with the scholarly consensus. The geographic description in Section 4 (the relationships to the Azande, the Ituri forest peoples, the Great Lakes margin) is accurate. The chapter correctly identifies the Azande as neighbors rather than subjects and the Mbuti as outside the Mangbetu state's governance. These are important distinctions and they are maintained.

**On the Uele valley geography:**

The direction of the trade routes described — northward toward the Azande, southeastward toward the Great Lakes, southward into the forest interior — is consistent with the documented exchange networks. The chapter does not overstate the Mangbetu court's connection to long-distance trade; it correctly characterizes the court as a consumer of tribute rather than a manager of exchange, which is accurate.

**Verdict:** Historically sound. One recommended addition (slavery/captive labor terminology in notes.md for production bible completeness). No factual errors.
