# BOARD — Robert Farris Thompson — The Middle Passage

**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Cultural Accuracy

The chapter's treatment of the African cultural systems that survived the Middle Passage is substantially accurate and demonstrates genuine engagement with the scholarly literature on African aesthetics and philosophy in the Americas.

## Specific Notes

1. **Yoruba orixa system:** The bata drums, the specific rhythms (alujá for Shango, agabi for Ogun, ijexá for Oxum), and the mechanism of spirit possession are accurately described. The description of descent ("the rhythm enters the dancer's body through the feet and the spine and the hips") is consistent with ethnographic accounts of the possession experience. The identification of alujá as the rhythm that calls Shango is correct.

2. **BaKongo cosmogram:** Accurately described. The four-point cosmological diagram, the kalunga as the dividing water, the counterclockwise movement of the ring shout, and the archaeological evidence of cosmograms in slave quarters are all documented. The chapter's connection between the cosmogram and the ring shout is the central argument of my work and is handled correctly. The statement "The cross that the enslaved people drew was not the Christian cross. It was older" is precisely right and represents an important scholarly point.

3. **Fon/Vodou:** The vodunsi terminology is correct. The identification of the lwa (Damballa, Erzulie, Legba, Baron Samedi) as Fon-derived spirits in Haitian Vodou is accurate, though the relationship is more complex than the chapter's compressed treatment suggests. The lwa are a synthesis of Fon, Kongo, and Taino elements. The chapter's description of the Vodou ceremony (peristyle, poteau-mitan, drum rhythms) is accurate.

4. **Bois Caiman:** The chapter handles this appropriately. The Cécile Fatiman tradition is presented as oral tradition, the scholarly debate is acknowledged, and the structural argument (spiritual gathering as political organizing infrastructure) does not depend on the specific details of the ceremony. This is the right approach.

5. **Candomblé syncretism:** The orixas-to-saints correspondences (Shango/Santa Barbara, Yemanjá/Nossa Senhora da Conceição, Ogum/São Jorge) are correct for the Bahian Candomblé tradition. The description of the terreiro and the mãe de santo is accurate.

6. **Mande/griot tradition:** The kora and akonting are correctly identified. The akonting-to-banjo connection is well-documented in the ethnomusicological literature (Shlomo Pestcoe, Daniel Jatta). The griot's function as hereditary oral historian is correctly described.

7. **Igbo Landing:** The Dunbar Creek tradition (1803) is correctly placed and correctly interpreted within the Igbo cosmological framework of the chi. The chapter does not over-determine the event; it presents it as a tradition that carries cosmological weight.

## Concern

The chapter describes the Ifa divination system as using "the sixteen principal odu" and "binary figures cast in palm nuts or cowrie shells." This is essentially correct but compressed. The full Ifa system has 256 odu (16 principal, 240 derived). The casting method involves either ikin (palm nuts) or opele (divination chain), not cowrie shells. Cowrie shells are used in a different, simpler divination method (merindilogun or "casting of sixteen cowries") that is related but distinct. The chapter should either specify ikin/opele or broaden to "cast in palm nuts or on the divination chain." **P2: should fix.**

## Verdict

Culturally accurate across the full range of African traditions represented. The plurality of traditions (Yoruba, Fon, Igbo, BaKongo, Mande) is a significant strength. One factual correction needed (Ifa casting method). The body-archive thesis is well-supported by the cultural evidence the chapter presents.
