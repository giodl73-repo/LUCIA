# Figures — The Lueji-Chibinda Foundation

## Lueji (Rweej)

Her name in the tradition's oldest form is Rweej, though she appears in most secondary sources as Lueji, and the chapter uses the more commonly cited form. Her father is given various names in different versions of the tradition — Yala Mwaku in some accounts, Nkonde in others — which may indicate the oral tradition's layering of separate founding accounts over time, or may reflect a lineage politics in which different communities claim different founding fathers. The tradition is more consistent about Lueji herself than about her parentage.

She is remembered as a ruler, not simply as a bride. This matters to state directly. The tradition does not present her as a transitional figure whose significance lies only in being the bearer of authority until the real ruler arrives. She adjudicated disputes, performed the ceremonies required of the lukanu's holder, maintained the relationships between the living community and the ancestral authority that underwrote Ruund chiefly power. She ruled for a period — however long the sources suggest, probably years — between her father's death and the transmission to Chibinda Ilunga.

What we cannot know: the specifics of her experience. The oral tradition is not interested in interiority in the way that Western biography is; it is interested in action and in the significance of action for the lineage's future. We cannot know what she thought when she first saw Chibinda Ilunga, cannot know whether the recognition she is said to have felt was immediate or developed over time, cannot know what the transmission ceremony meant to her in the moment of performing it. The chapter acknowledges these gaps through the tradition's own practice: it states what the tradition preserves and does not project feelings that the tradition does not record.

Her post-transmission presence: the tradition records that Lueji's lineage continued to hold specific ceremonial and political roles in the Lunda state. The Rukonkesh (the Mwaant Yav's mother) and the Swana Mulunda (the senior queen, whose relationship to the maternal line varied across different periods) held recognized political weight. Whether Lueji herself held one of these roles, or whether the roles developed after her from the precedent she had set, the sources do not specify with certainty. What is clear is that the founding act established the principle of female ceremonial and political authority within the Lunda state's structure, and that this principle was maintained through the state's history.

Her dates: unknown. The founding period is reconstructed as c. 1600-1650 CE from genealogical counting in the oral tradition combined with comparison with neighboring traditions. Lueji's own life dates are not separately calculable from the available evidence.

## Chibinda Ilunga (Tshibinda Ilunga)

His name is given in the sources most commonly as Chibinda Ilunga, sometimes as Tshibinda Ilunga. He is identified as the son of Kalala Ilunga, the Luba founding hero — though in the Luba tradition Kalala Ilunga is himself a figure of the deep past, and the genealogical chain between Kalala Ilunga and the historical Chibinda Ilunga who married Lueji has been compressed and formalized over centuries of retelling. What the tradition preserves is the lineage claim — that he carried the bulopwe — not a verifiable genealogical record.

He was a hunter. This is repeatedly emphasized in the tradition, and it is not incidental. Hunting in the Luba-Lunda world carried ritual and social significance beyond the provision of meat. The hunter's relationship to the animal — the obligation to acknowledge the animal's spirit, the ritual practices that maintained the right relationship between hunter and hunted — placed hunting within a spiritual framework that connected to the political-sacred domain of the bulopwe. A great hunter was not simply someone who provided food. He was someone whose relationship to the non-human world was correctly maintained, which placed him in a category of people who understood authority in a way that others did not.

His arrival in the Ruund country was not political in intention. This is what the tradition preserves: he came hunting, not to rule. The significance of this — that the founding of the Lunda kingdom was not a conquest or a planned political project but a recognition — is part of what the tradition is remembering when it tells the story this way. The kingdom was not imposed from outside. It was constituted from inside, by the person who held the authority to constitute it.

His integration into the Ruund world: the tradition records that he brought Luba hunting practices and Luba cultural elements with him, and that these were not seamlessly absorbed but rather negotiated. Some of Lueji's headmen left because they did not accept the Luba presence in Ruund political life. Some stayed and the integration happened over time. Chibinda Ilunga became the Mwaant Yav — or the precedent for the Mwaant Yav — through a process that took years, not a ceremony that happened once.

His death: undated. He is succeeded within the founding generation, and the succession marks the point where the lukanu becomes fully transmissible in the new Lunda mode — where the bracelet moves again, by design, from holder to holder.

The tradition preserves a statue of Chibinda Ilunga from a later period — a carved wooden figure depicting a hunter holding a staff, wearing the signs of chiefly authority, with an elaborate headdress. This figure, now held in European museum collections, was probably carved several generations after his death, as a ritual object representing his authority rather than as a portrait. It shows a man fully occupying the political and spiritual authority that the tradition ascribes to him: the hunter who became the founding king, rendered in wood by his descendants as an ongoing presence.

## Katema (composite figure)

The headmen who left — those who refused the Chibinda Ilunga marriage and the Lunda synthesis — are collectively documented in the tradition's account of the communities that were founded to the west and south of the Lunda heartland during the founding period. The Imbangala of the western Angolan borderlands, and other communities, carry cultural and political elements traceable to this departure period. Individual named figures among the departing headmen are not consistently preserved in the sources accessible for this chapter.

Katema as used in the chapter is a composite figure, drawn from the tradition's general account of the departures and informed by the social type — the established headman of the previous chief's court who serves loyally but cannot accept the new synthesis. He is not invented; he is a figure whose existence the tradition implies through its account of the departures, given a name from the Lunda-area naming tradition. His presence in the chapter is disclosed here so that the reader understands the compositional technique.

The composite figure technique is acknowledged in LUCIA's rubric (Figures, v1.1 amendment): for oral and archaeological cultures where the record cannot support named individual figures at every dramatic moment, composite figures drawn from the tradition's general account are valid when honestly disclosed in figures.md.

Katema represents something structurally important: he is the first unintended franchisee. By leaving with Ruund political vocabulary and installing it in a new location, he demonstrated that the culture could travel independent of the center. This discovery — made by those who were refusing the synthesis, not by those who were building it — became the foundation of the Lunda expansion strategy in the generations that followed.

## The Lukanu

The lukanu is not a person, but in the Lunda tradition it functions as an agent with its own history and logic. It deserves a separate account here.

The lukanu is a copper bracelet. It has a physical form — a band worn on the wrist — that in some accounts is described as made from copper obtained specifically from the ancestors' territory, worked in ways that would have required specialized metalworking knowledge. In later periods, the lukanu was kept soaked in the medicines of the royal compound, maintained in a specific way that was the responsibility of specific custodians. It was not simply worn; it required ongoing ritual maintenance.

Its political theology: the lukanu's holder held the Lunda state's legitimacy. This was not a metaphor. The state's legitimacy was understood to reside in the object. A Mwaant Yav without the lukanu was not a Mwaant Yav in the full sense. This understanding had practical political implications: when the Mwaant Yav was expelled from the capital by the Chokwe in the 1880s, the question of what had happened to the lukanu — whether it had been taken by the Chokwe, hidden by loyal custodians, or remained with the expelled Mwaant Yav — was the defining question about whether the Lunda state had survived or had ceased to exist. The tradition held that the lukanu had been hidden and preserved, which meant the state had survived even when the capital was occupied.

Its founding significance: Lueji's transmission of the lukanu to Chibinda Ilunga is the founding act of the Lunda state in the tradition's own understanding. Not the marriage, not the political consolidation, not the first franchise departures — the transmission of the bracelet. This is why the object receives such careful attention in the chapter: the narrative is organized around its movement because the Lunda tradition itself organized legitimacy around its movement.

The object survives into the modern era, maintained by the custodians of the Mwaant Yav tradition in the Kapanga region of the Democratic Republic of Congo. The Mwaant Yav title is currently (as of the scholarship available) held as a ceremonial institution. The lukanu continues to be the center of the ceremonies associated with the title.
