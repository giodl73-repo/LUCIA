# Jāhiliyya — The Age of the Arabs Before Islam

We are the people the later tradition called *Jāhiliyya* — the age of unknowing — but this was not our name for ourselves. We called ourselves by our tribe, our household, our lineage: Banu Tamim, Banu Quraysh, the sons of such-and-such ancestor who traced his line back to Ishmael or to Qahtan. The *Jāhiliyya* is the name our own descendants gave to our world in retrospect, looking back from the light of what came after. We did not think of ourselves as living in darkness. We had our gods, our codes, our poets, and our markets. We had the *muruwwa* — the manliness, the honor code — that organized every obligation of life. We had the *qayla*, the noble lineage, and the poet who was the lineage's voice and its weapon, praising those who deserved praise and shaming those who deserved shame in verses that were as much legal instruments as aesthetic achievements.

The Arabian Peninsula that we inhabited was not marginal to the ancient world. Mecca itself was a trading entrepot where caravans from Yemen and from Syria converged, where the shrine of the Kaaba drew pilgrims from across the peninsula, where the Quraysh tribe managed both the religious sanctuary and the commercial infrastructure that made the sanctuary profitable. The peninsula's eastern coasts traded with India and the Persian Gulf world. Yemen's sophisticated states had been exporting frankincense and myrrh to the Mediterranean world for centuries. The Nabataeans had built Petra, one of the ancient world's most remarkable cities, from the caravan trade. We were not waiting to be civilized. We were a complex, literate (in the oral sense), commercially sophisticated society whose gods and poets and honor codes reflected deep engagement with the world as it was.

## What Defined Them

**The poet as the tribe's voice and weapon.** In the pre-Islamic Arabian world, the poet — the *sha'ir* — was not primarily an entertainer. He was the tribe's institutional memory, its public relations officer, its legal advocate, and its weapon in the perpetual competition for honor and reputation that organized tribal life. A *qasida* — the great poem — that praised a tribe's generosity and valor and shamed a rival tribe's cowardice was as effective in the honor economy as a military victory. The *mu'allaqat* — the seven or ten great pre-Islamic odes, legendarily hung in the Kaaba — are the surviving examples of a tradition in which verbal art was a serious instrument of power. The Islamic tradition's reverence for the Quran as the supreme linguistic achievement reflects, among other things, the pre-Islamic world's established conviction that the highest human capacity was mastery of the Arabic word.

**The *muruwwa* — manliness as the comprehensive code.** The pre-Islamic code of *muruwwa* organized every obligation of the noble life: courage in battle, generosity to guests and supplicants, loyalty to the tribe and its allies, vengeance for wrongs done to the household, protection of the weak who came under the household's protection. It was not a written code — it was the accumulated practice of what a man of standing did, enforced by the poet's praise and blame. The man who acted against *muruwwa* — who refused hospitality, who abandoned a companion in battle, who failed to seek vengeance for a murdered kinsman — was not merely socially disgraced. He had failed at the central obligation of human life as the pre-Islamic tradition understood it.

**The Kaaba and the sanctified space.** The Kaaba at Mecca was, before Islam, the sanctuary of a pantheon of tribal gods: al-Lat, al-Uzza, Manat, and many others, whose idols were housed in and around the sacred structure. The Quraysh tribe managed this sanctuary, and the management of the sanctuary — the provision of water and food for pilgrims, the arbitration of disputes during the sacred months, the maintenance of the peace that made the market possible — was the source of Quraysh commercial and political power. The sacred months during which the peninsula's tribes observed a truce were also the months when the great fairs took place, when merchants traveled safely, when poets competed at Ukaz. The sanctuary was the organizational center of the pre-Islamic Arabian economy.

**The caravan and the long-distance network.** Arabian society was organized around movement: the seasonal migrations of the pastoral clans, the caravan routes that connected Yemen's agricultural wealth to Syria and Iraq, the sea-routes that linked the Arabian coast to India and Africa. The Quraysh of Mecca had organized the peninsula's two great caravan seasons — the summer journey to Syria and the winter journey to Yemen — into a commercial system that gave them wealth and influence across the peninsula. This commercial tradition — the management of trade as an organizing social function, the merchant as a figure of prestige rather than marginal status — was part of the world into which Muhammad was born and which shaped his message in ways that have been debated ever since.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Ancient Arabian Kingdoms:** The frankincense caravan leaves Shabwa in the Hadramawt. The camels carry the resin northward, through the Hijaz, toward the Mediterranean markets that have burned Arabian incense in their temples for a thousand years. In Petra, the Nabataean traders are carving their tombs into the rose-red cliff. The peninsula is a crossroads of ancient civilization. It is connected.

**2 — The Tribal Age:** The poet Imru al-Qays opens his great *qasida* at the abandoned campsite: *qifā nabki min dhikrā ḥabībin wa-manzili* — "Stop, let us weep at the memory of a beloved and a dwelling." The poetry competition at Ukaz is drawing the finest verbal artists of the peninsula. The tribes are at war and at truce simultaneously, navigating the honor economy with the same skill that merchants navigate the caravan routes.

**3 — The Last Generation Before Islam:** A merchant's son named Muhammad is approaching forty in Mecca. He has been retreating to the cave on Hira. The Quraysh are prosperous and confident. The Byzantine and Sassanid empires have just exhausted each other in their last great war. Something is assembling in the world that no one in Mecca can yet see.
