# AmaXhosa — "The Frontier and the Faith"

The Xhosa are the people of the eastern Cape — the amaxhosa, a word whose origin is debated but whose identity is clear: the Nguni-speaking communities of the coastal and highland region east of the Fish River, organized through a system of patrilineal chieftaincies in which cattle, seniority, and the approval of the living and dead defined legitimacy. The Xhosa encountered the expanding Cape Colony frontier from the mid-eighteenth century onward, and the nine Cape Frontier Wars fought between 1779 and 1879 constitute one of the longest sustained colonial military conflicts in African history — a century of warfare in which the Xhosa fought to maintain their cattle, their land, and their political sovereignty against a colonial expansion that would not stop.

The Great Cattle Killing of 1856–57 is the most shattering event in Xhosa history. A young girl named Nongqawuse reported a vision from the ancestors: the Xhosa must kill all their cattle and destroy all their grain, and the ancestral dead would rise, drive the British into the sea, and restore the world to Xhosa sovereignty. Approximately 400,000 cattle were killed. Tens of thousands of people died of starvation. The prophecy failed. The British colonial administration used the resulting famine and the Xhosa's catastrophic weakening to seize the last of their land and incorporate the survivors into the labor system of the Cape Colony. The Great Cattle Killing was not madness; it was the response of a people who had exhausted every other option for resisting colonial power and whose entire framework of understanding — the relationship between the living and the dead, the covenant between people and cattle, the power of ancestral intervention — said that this sacrifice was what the situation required.

## What Defined Them

**Cattle as the world's substance.** For the Xhosa, cattle were not simply livestock — they were the material medium of every significant relationship: the lobola paid for a bride; the animals slaughtered for the ancestors; the compensation paid for injuries; the tribute that expressed political loyalty. To be Xhosa in the fullest sense was to participate in a world organized around cattle ownership, cattle exchange, and the spiritual significance of cattle as the bridge between the living and the dead. When the colonial frontier cattle raids and the three-decade land and cattle losses of the early frontier wars stripped the Xhosa of their herds, they were losing the substance of their social world, not just their economic assets. Nongqawuse's cattle killing prophecy makes sense in this context: when you have nothing to lose and the covenant with the ancestors is your only remaining resource, you honor the covenant.

**The amaXhosa chieftaincy system.** The Xhosa were not a single political unit but a family of related chieftaincies — the Gcaleka, Rharhabe, Thembu, Mpondo, Mpondomise, and others — each with its own chief and each tracing descent from common ancestors. Seniority in this lineage determined precedence between chiefs. The system was flexible: new chieftaincies could be founded by a chief's sons; rival chiefs could compete for followers; the political map was not fixed. This flexibility made the Xhosa politically resilient across the century of frontier warfare — there was no single capital to capture, no single king whose defeat would end the war — but it also limited their capacity for unified military response to the colonial advance.

**The missionaries and the literacy transformation.** From the 1820s onward, Scottish and English missionaries established mission stations in Xhosa territory, and the mission stations became the sites of a educational revolution with profound political consequences. The Xhosa who received mission education — the "school people" (amagqoboka, literally "those who have been pierced") — became literate, bilingual, and participants in a Victorian Christian world that the colonial system offered as an alternative identity. The tension between "school people" and "traditional people" (amaqaba) — between Xhosa who adopted Western education and Christianity and Xhosa who maintained traditional practices — was the defining social tension in Xhosa society from the mid-nineteenth century through apartheid and beyond.

**Nongqawuse and the political theology of sacrifice.** The young girl who reported the vision in 1856 — Nongqawuse, niece of the prophet Mhlakaza — is one of the most contested figures in southern African history. Was she a genuine prophet? A manipulated child? A colonial agent? The questions have been argued since the cattle killing itself. What is clear is that her prophecy fell on ground prepared by genuine desperation: the Xhosa had lost eight wars in a century, their cattle were dying of lung-sickness, and the prophetic tradition offered the only remaining framework in which resistance could be conceived. The cattle killing was a political theology of sacrifice — the commitment of the last resource to the covenant with the ancestors in the hope of divine intervention. That it failed does not mean it was irrational; it means the framework in which it was conceived did not have the power its adherents believed it had.

**The literary tradition.** The Xhosa were among the first southern African peoples to develop a written literary tradition in their own language. John Knox Bokwe, Samuel Mqhayi, Walter Rubusana — these nineteenth and early twentieth century Xhosa writers created a literature that engaged with the colonial situation from within Xhosa cultural frameworks. Samuel Mqhayi (1875–1945), known as Imbongi YoMxhosa Wonke (the Bard of the Xhosa People), wrote poetry, biography, and journalism that maintained Xhosa pride and cultural identity through the most difficult decades of colonial dispossession. Nelson Mandela, who was Thembu (a Xhosa chieftaincy), drew on this literary and political tradition throughout his public life.

## The Chapters

**1 — The First War:** 1779. A Xhosa chief is considering the Dutch colonial militia's demand that the Xhosa withdraw east of the Fish River. The river is within his territory. His cattle graze on both sides. He refuses. This refusal will be the first of nine frontier wars.

**2 — The Mission Station:** A Xhosa young man is learning to read. His uncle is watching from across the village boundary with concern. The school people are changing. Whether they are becoming something better or something lost is the question that will define Xhosa politics for the next two centuries.

**3 — Nongqawuse:** The young woman is standing at the river. She says the ancestors have spoken. The cattle must be killed. All of them. Her uncle Mhlakaza believes her. The Gcaleka king Sarhili is listening. The decision is being made.

**4 — The Aftermath:** The cattle are dead. The grain is gone. The ancestors have not come. The British colonial officials are organizing relief — and simultaneously moving dispossessed Xhosa families onto European farms as cheap labor. The prophecy failed. The colonial system succeeds.

**5 — Mqhayi at the Prince of Wales:** 1925. Samuel Mqhayi performs an improvisational praise poem for the Prince of Wales during his visit to Fort Hare. The poem criticizes British imperialism in front of the Prince himself, in isiXhosa, to the delight of the African audience who can understand it. The Prince smiles. He does not know what is being said.
