## B-1 — Trevor Bryce — Board Review of Hittites Era 2

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Hittite political history across all periods; author of the standard political narrative; specialist in Old and Middle Kingdom succession crises.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The succession sequence (Hattusili I > Mursili I > Hantili I > Zidanta I > Ammuna > Huzziya I > Telipinu) is correct and follows the edict's own account.
- The Bilingual Edict of Hattusili I is correctly identified and the quotation ("He is no longer my son") is a reasonable rendering of the text.
- Mursili I's raid on Babylon is correctly placed and its significance appropriately noted. The distance (over 1000 km) is accurate.
- The statue of Marduk: the tradition that the Hittites carried Marduk's statue from Babylon is attested but debated. The chapter presents it as fact. This should be qualified slightly: "according to later tradition" or similar.
- Telipinu's treatment of Huzziya (exile rather than execution) is correctly drawn from the edict text.
- The Kizzuwatna treaty is correctly placed in Telipinu's reign.
- The post-Telipinu succession (Alluwamna, Hantili II, Zidanta II, Huzziya II, Muwatalli I) is correctly listed. The uncertainty about their reigns is honestly acknowledged.
- The dating of Telipinu's reign (c. 1525-1500 BCE in notes.md) falls within the accepted range, though some chronologies place it slightly later.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's treatment of divine punishment as causal reality rather than belief is correct for Hittite worldview. The Hittites did not separate theological and political causation.
- The treaty form is accurately described and its adaptation in the edict is correctly analyzed.
- The panku's role is presented with appropriate caution regarding the limits of our knowledge.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The structural insight that the edict adapts the treaty form is well developed and historically sound.
- The honest handling of the post-Telipinu period, where the record is genuinely thin, is a strength.
- The connection between the edict and the Hittite Laws as parallel exercises in replacing private violence with institutional procedure is a legitimate analytical point.

**What needs correction:**
- The statue of Marduk claim should be qualified. The tradition exists but is not universally accepted as historical.
- The chapter states that Hattusili "named his nephew as heir" and then "stripped him of the succession." The Bilingual Edict suggests a more complex relationship: the nephew was designated but the relationship deteriorated over time. "Named" and "stripped" are accurate shorthand but could imply a cleaner sequence than the sources support. Minor point; no revision required but worth noting.

**Verdict:** APPROVED
