### Outside-Lens Violations — CLEAR with one flag

The chapter approaches the Dessalines massacre passage with care. The language used: "He knew what he was ordering. He ordered it with knowledge of what the French presence meant as a structural fact." This is inside-voice. The sentence "This is not exoneration. It is the inside of the decision." is the most direct engagement with the outside-lens problem in the chapter: it explicitly refuses the verdict while naming the refusal. This is the v1.2 Honesty technique — attributing the refusal to judge to the culture's own logic rather than to editorial restraint.

**Flag (minor):** One sentence approaches the boundary: "He was brilliant and authoritarian. He freed his people from slavery and then instituted labor contracts that chained them to the plantations by a different name." The phrase "chained them to the plantations by a different name" imports a modern critical judgment using the language of chains. It is defensible as the inside voice of the formerly enslaved people who experienced the labor contracts, but it could be read as editorial. 

**Recommendation:** Revise to: "He freed his people from slavery and then instituted labor contracts that kept them on the plantations under different terms." This removes the metaphor that borrows from the slave vocabulary and replaces it with a factual description. The formerly enslaved people's assessment of these contracts — that they were not the freedom they had fought for — is rendered through the composite figure in figures.md and through the final section's observation about smallholding and subsistence farming. That is the right place for that judgment: from inside, carried by the people, not stated by the narrator.

### Romanticization — CLEAR

The chapter does not idealize the revolution. Toussaint's contradictions are held without resolution. The labor contract system is acknowledged. Dessalines's order is shown without softening. The composite figure's unresolved relationship to the republic's vision of freedom is the most honest moment in the chapter.

The Vodou ceremony is rendered from inside the tradition — the lwa are real, the possession is real, the covenant is real — without being made into spiritual spectacle for an external audience. This is the correct treatment.

### Calendar/Dates — CLEAR

Western calendar dates used throughout. This is appropriate: the revolution was operating in dialogue with the French Revolutionary calendar and the common era; using Haitian era names alongside CE dates is correct. The Taíno name for the island (Ayiti) is used throughout alongside Saint-Domingue, with the naming act at independence given full weight.
