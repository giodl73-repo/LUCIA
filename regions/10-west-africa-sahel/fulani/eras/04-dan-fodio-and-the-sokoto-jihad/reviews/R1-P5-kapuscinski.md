## P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Review of "Dan Fodio and the Sokoto Jihad" opening

**Overall:** I can smell the leather. I can hear the mallets. The compound near the market is a real place, and Umaru Dallaji is a man with a letter in his hands, not a name on a page. The opening's sensory foundation is strong but unevenly distributed. The first two paragraphs and the final three put me in Katsina. The middle paragraphs take me everywhere and therefore nowhere.

**What works:**

- "tanned leather and ground pepper hung in the air from dawn to dusk." This is a market I can walk through. Two smells, named with specificity. Not "the smell of the market" but leather and pepper. I know what Katsina smells like because the author knows.

- "the rhythmic thud of the mallets carried over the mud-brick walls." Sound, material, architecture in a single image. The leather-tanners are outside. Umaru is inside. The revolution is being read while the daily work continues. That juxtaposition is felt, not explained.

- The student who carried the letter is embodied: "walked three days through the dry scrub of Gobir and crossed the boundary into Katsina territory without stopping at the sarki's gate." Three days of walking. Dry scrub. The deliberate avoidance of the official gate. I feel the urgency in the walking and the subversion in the route.

- "Green cloth, the color of the Prophet. It was not large." The flag is physical. It has a color and a size. The fact that it was not large is a detail that makes the entire jihad more real. An enormous banner would be spectacle. A small green cloth is a commission that fits in a man's hands. This is how revolutions actually look in their first moments: small, quiet, held against a chest.

- The final image: "He folded the flag and placed it on the shelf beside his books." I see this. The shelf, the books, the green cloth. The physical co-presence of the intellectual tradition and the call to action. The reader sees both on the same shelf, and the chapter's entire argument is in that image.

**What doesn't:**

- The middle section where the flags go to Kano, Zaria, Nupe, Ilorin, Bauchi, Adamawa is a geography lesson, not a scene. The author names cities and says the flags arrived. But what did Kano feel like when the flag arrived? Was it the dry season or the wet? Were the dye pits steaming? What was the sound in the market? The single detail about Kano ("the dye pits where the indigo cloth was beaten and folded") is good, but it is a parenthetical, not an immersion. I need one city besides Katsina where I can feel the flag arriving: the room, the light, the temperature, the scholar's hands.

- The Shehu himself has no body. He is "fifty-four years old" and has been "teaching for more than thirty years." I know his age and his bibliography. I do not know whether he was tall or slight, whether his voice carried or whether people leaned in to hear him, whether his hands were stained with ink, whether the room in Degel where he taught was large or small, hot or shaded. He is the center of this revolution, and I cannot see him. The opening does not need to give him a full physical portrait, but it needs one sensory detail that makes him a man with a body, not a name with a bibliography.

- The dry scrub of Gobir is mentioned, and the mud-brick walls of the compound are present, but the Sahel landscape between the cities is abstract. "A territory larger than France" tells me nothing about what that territory looks, feels, or sounds like. What is the land between Gobir and Katsina? Scrub? Open savanna? Scattered villages? What is the quality of the light in the dry season? What does the harmattan do to the skin, the eyes, the parchment the letter is written on? The geographic scope of the jihad needs a sensory anchor, not a European comparison.

**The question I'd push on:**

Give me the Shehu's body. Not a portrait, not a biography. One physical detail that makes him present. The ink on his fingers. The way he held a pen. The sound of his voice in the teaching compound at Degel. The chapter will return to him many times, and each return will be stronger if the reader carries a single physical impression from the opening. Right now, Umaru Dallaji is more embodied than the man who sent the letter, and that imbalance needs correction.
