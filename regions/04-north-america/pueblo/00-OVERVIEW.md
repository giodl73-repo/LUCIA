# Pueblo — People of the Stone Villages

"Pueblo" is the Spanish word for village — Pueblo de los Indios, village of the Indians, as the Spanish called the multi-story stone and adobe communities they found along the Rio Grande and in the high country of the Colorado Plateau when they arrived in the sixteenth century. The Pueblo peoples — who speak many different languages (Tewa, Tiwa, Towa, Keres, Zuni, Hopi, and others) and do not constitute a single unified people in the political or ethnic sense — share a tradition of stone and adobe multi-story architecture, a kiva-centered ceremonial life, and a relationship with an arid landscape that required, and produced, a social organization based on communal labor, water management, and the maintenance of the ceremonial calendar that kept the community in right relationship with the forces governing the rain.

The kiva — the underground ceremonial chamber reached through a ladder from above — was the center of the Pueblo world. It was simultaneously the place of emergence (in many Pueblo traditions, the people came up from the lower worlds through a sipapu — a hole in the earth — and the kiva's relationship to the underground recapitulated that emergence), the place of political deliberation (men's councils met in the kiva), the place of ceremonial performance (the kachina dances and other ceremonies were planned and rehearsed in the kiva's ritual space), and the place that held the community's relationship with the ancestors and the above. You could not be a Pueblo community without a kiva. The kiva was the community's heart.

Key places:
- **Chaco Canyon** — the great ancestral center (c. 850-1150 CE) in what is now New Mexico; Pueblo Bonito, the largest structure, housed perhaps 700 rooms; the center of a regional road network
- **Mesa Verde** — the cliff dwellings of the Colorado Plateau; Ancestral Puebloan communities built into the canyon cliffs c. 1150-1300 CE before migrating south and east to the Rio Grande
- **Acoma Pueblo (Haak'u)** — "Sky City" — a Keresan-speaking Pueblo on a mesa top 110 meters above the surrounding plain; continuously inhabited for over 1,000 years; one of the oldest continuously inhabited communities in North America
- **Taos Pueblo** — a Tiwa-speaking Pueblo in northern New Mexico; its multi-story adobe structures have been continuously inhabited for approximately 1,000 years; a UNESCO World Heritage Site

## What Defined Them

**The kiva as the world's center and the community's foundation.** The underground ceremonial chamber — reached by ladder, with the sipapu (the emergence hole in the floor) as the world's navel — was not simply a building. It was the conceptual center of the Pueblo world and the physical evidence that the community was in right relationship with the above and below. The kiva's underground position recapitulated the emergence story: the people came up from the lower worlds through the sipapu, and the kiva maintained that connection to the source. The kachina ceremonies, the political deliberations, the winter solstice observances — all of these happened in or around the kiva, because the kiva was where the world's organizing forces were most accessible.

**Dry farming and water management as spiritual practice.** The Pueblo people lived on some of the most arid inhabited land on the continent. The corn, beans, and squash that were the agricultural foundation of Pueblo life required precise knowledge of soil moisture, microclimate, and the timing of planting relative to the last frost and the summer monsoon rains. Water management — the check dams, the irrigation canals, the dry-farming techniques that maximized the use of every drop of rain — was not simply agricultural technology. It was a practice of right relationship with the limited water that the landscape offered. The ceremonies that asked for rain (the kachina dances, the seed blessing ceremonies, the prayers at the spring solstice) were integrated with the agricultural calendar because they were understood to be part of the same practice — maintaining the community's relationship with the forces that controlled the water.

**The kachina and the ancestral presence.** The kachinas — the masked ceremonial figures who appeared in Pueblo plazas during the ceremony season — were not costumes or theatrical performances. They were the presence of the ancestral spirits made manifest in the community's midst. The men who wore the masks were not "pretending" to be kachinas; they were temporarily becoming the vessels through which the ancestral spirits could be present in the physical world. The ceremonies in which the kachinas appeared — the cloud people, the rain bringers, the corn growers — were the community's direct engagement with the forces that governed fertility and survival. The suppression of these ceremonies by the Spanish (prohibited as "devil worship") was the trigger of the Pueblo Revolt of 1680.

**The Pueblo Revolt as the moment the communities acted as one.** On August 10, 1680, Pueblo communities across New Mexico rose simultaneously — Tewa, Tiwa, Towa, Keres, Zuni, Hopi, speaking different languages, governed by different councils, with their own specific histories of Spanish colonization — and drove the Spanish out of the province. The Revolt was organized by Popé (Pó'pay), a San Ildefonso Tewa leader who had been imprisoned and flogged by the Spanish for practicing the forbidden ceremonies. The organizational achievement — coordinating communities that had not acted collectively before, maintaining the plan's secrecy until the moment of action, executing a coordinated uprising across hundreds of miles — was one of the most significant collective acts in North American history. For twelve years, the Pueblo communities governed New Mexico without Spanish authority.

**Permanence as a statement about the land.** The Pueblo communities built to last, in a landscape where the commitment to staying required extraordinary effort. Multi-story stone architecture, maintained and expanded over centuries; water management systems designed for the long term; ceremonial calendars integrated with the agricultural cycle over generations — these were not responses to abundance. They were the choice of a people who had decided that this specific landscape was their world and that they would invest everything required to maintain their relationship with it. Acoma Pueblo has been continuously inhabited for over a thousand years on a mesa top that has no water of its own. The statement that such a choice makes is not about stubbornness. It is about the relationship between identity and place.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Chaco Horizon:** At Pueblo Bonito, in the canyon that channels the winter solstice sun precisely through the great house's alignment, the roads radiate outward to forty or more outlier communities across the Colorado Plateau. This is the most ambitious thing the Pueblo tradition has yet produced. Whether it will last is not the question anyone is asking yet.

**2 — The Mesa Verde Period:** The cliff dwelling is being built in the canyon overhang that makes it invisible from above. Something has changed in the security of the Colorado Plateau world — the communities that could once live on the mesa top are now building in the cliffs. Within a generation, they will move again, south to the Rio Grande. The migrations are not evidence of failure. They are the Pueblo tradition's adaptive intelligence.

**3 — The Rio Grande Consolidation:** The kachina ceremony is being held at the Pueblo's central plaza. The masked figures enter from the north. The corn pollen is being distributed. The prayers for rain are being made in the voices of the ancestral cloud people. This ceremony has been performed at this Pueblo for longer than anyone's memory reaches.

**4 — The Spanish Colony:** The Spanish priest is burning the kachina masks. He is calling them idols. The Pueblo community watches. The ceremony has been performed here for generations. The masks can be remade; the knowledge of the ceremony is not in the objects. The people make a note of what is being done, and begin counting the years.

**5 — The Pueblo Revolt:** It is August 10, 1680. Popé's runners have carried the knotted cord to every Pueblo. Each knot is a day. The cord has been untied to the last knot. The Spanish priests in the missions wake to a morning that is different. By nightfall, twenty-one of the thirty-three priests in New Mexico are dead. The Governor is retreating south with the survivors. The Pueblo world is free.

**6 — The Modern Pueblos:** A Tewa woman at San Ildefonso Pueblo is teaching her granddaughter the songs for the winter solstice ceremony. The songs are in Tewa. The granddaughter speaks English at school and Tewa at home. The kiva is still there. The ceremony has not been interrupted since the Revolt gave it back.
