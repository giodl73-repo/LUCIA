# 中华人民共和国 — Zhōnghuá Rénmín Gònghéguó: The People's Republic of China

"China has stood up." Mao Zedong's proclamation from Tiananmen Square on October 1, 1949, announced not just the founding of a new state but the end of the century of humiliation — the 110 years of treaty ports, unequal treaties, foreign concessions, and Japanese invasion that had reduced China from the world's largest economy to a semi-colonial state. The People's Republic of China was, in this self-understanding, the restoration of Chinese dignity and sovereignty, achieved by the Communist Party that had survived the Long March, organized the peasant revolution, and defeated the Nationalists. The Party's legitimacy was built on this foundation: it ended the humiliation, it unified the country, and it offered a path to the prosperity and power that China's size and civilization history warranted.

The history of the People's Republic since 1949 is a history of extraordinary achievements and extraordinary catastrophes, inseparable from each other. The land reform of the early 1950s, the defeat of US forces in Korea, the development of a nuclear weapons program, the lifting of hundreds of millions out of poverty in the post-1978 reform era, the construction of the world's largest high-speed rail network, the hosting of the 2008 Olympics — these are the achievements the Party narrates. The Great Leap Forward famine (1959–1961, 15–55 million dead — the range reflects the uncertainty; all estimates are catastrophic), the Cultural Revolution's destruction of an entire generation of intellectuals and cultural life, Tiananmen 1989, the suppression of Tibetan and Uyghur identity — these are the catastrophes the Party's narrative prefers to contain. Both are real. They describe the same party in the same country.

Key places:
- **Tiananmen Square** — the center of political symbolism; the Gate of Heavenly Peace; Mao's proclamation (1949); the 1989 crackdown; where the Party's legitimacy is performed and contested
- **Yan'an (Shaanxi)** — the cave city where the CCP based itself after the Long March (1935–1947); the founding myth of the revolution
- **Shenzhen** — the Special Economic Zone established in 1980; the first city to embody Deng Xiaoping's reform era; from a fishing village to a metropolis of 13 million in forty years
- **Xinjiang** — the western region where approximately one million Uyghurs have been held in "vocational training centers" since 2017; the most contested human rights issue in contemporary Chinese governance

## What Defined Them

**The Party's mandate — performance legitimacy.** The Chinese Communist Party derives its legitimacy not from democratic election but from performance: the claim that it has ended the century of humiliation, unified China, and lifted 800 million people out of poverty (the official figure, broadly confirmed by external measures). This performance legitimacy is real — the achievements are real — and it creates a specific political contract: the Party promises continued prosperity and national dignity; the people provide political acquiescence. The contract holds when the economy grows and breaks down when it does not. The Tiananmen protests of 1989 occurred at a moment of economic stress; the COVID-19 protests of 2022 occurred at a moment of economic stress. The performance contract is the key to understanding Chinese political stability and Chinese political crisis simultaneously.

**The Great Leap Famine — the suppressed catastrophe.** The Great Leap Forward famine of 1959–1961 is the largest famine in recorded history. Estimates of mortality range from 15 million (the lowest credible figure) to 55 million (the highest) — the uncertainty reflects the absence of independent Chinese demographic data and the political sensitivity of the question in China. It was caused by the combination of Mao's collectivization policies, the suppression of accurate reporting (local officials who reported the actual harvest would be accused of rightism), and the export of grain for foreign exchange even as people were starving. It was not a natural disaster; it was a policy disaster. The Party's suppression of the historical record of the famine — still largely absent from Chinese school history curricula — is the most consequential single act of historical erasure in the PRC's record.

**1978 and the second revolution.** Deng Xiaoping's decision to open China's economy after Mao's death — the "reform and opening up" (gaige kaifang) that began in 1978 — produced the most rapid economic development in human history. China's GDP grew at approximately 9–10% per year for thirty years. The poverty rate fell from 88% to 11% between 1981 and 2013. China became the world's factory, the world's largest exporter, and eventually the world's largest consumer of virtually every commodity. The model — maintaining Communist Party political authority while allowing market mechanisms to allocate resources — was called "socialism with Chinese characteristics" by the Party and "state capitalism" by its critics. Both descriptions contain truth. What is undeniable is the scale: the economic transformation of the largest country on Earth in a single generation is the central economic event of the late twentieth century.

**Xinjiang and the Uyghur question.** Beginning in 2017, the Chinese state has implemented a campaign of mass detention, cultural suppression, and systematic surveillance targeting the Uyghur Muslim minority of Xinjiang. An estimated one million or more Uyghurs have been held in "vocational training centers" (the official term) or internment camps (the term used by human rights organizations and most foreign governments). Satellite imagery, leaked government documents (the "Xinjiang Papers"), and survivor testimony have documented the system. China characterizes it as counter-terrorism and vocational training; the United States, the EU, and numerous human rights organizations have called it cultural genocide. The competing characterizations represent not merely disagreement about facts but fundamentally different frameworks for the relationship between state authority and minority rights. Both frameworks are held sincerely. The people in the camps have no voice in the argument about them.

## The Chapters

**1 — October 1, 1949:** Mao Zedong stands on the Gate of Heavenly Peace and proclaims the founding of the People's Republic of China. In the square below, hundreds of thousands of people are gathered. "China has stood up," he says. The century of humiliation is officially over. The revolution has won. Millions of people who have lived through warlordism, Japanese occupation, and civil war believe it, and their belief is not wrong about what has been achieved. What comes next is not yet clear.

**2 — The Famine:** A village official in Henan Province, 1959: he knows the harvest has failed. He knows people are dying. He has received orders to report the harvest targets as met and to continue delivering grain to the state. If he reports the truth, he will be accused of rightism and sent to a labor camp. He reports the targets as met. He delivers the grain. The people in his village die of hunger while the state's granaries have food. He is not an exception. He is the system.

**3 — The Cultural Revolution:** 1966: Red Guards are ransacking the home of a professor in Beijing. His books are burned; his manuscripts are burned; the objects that represent the old culture are smashed. The professor will spend the next ten years in a re-education camp in the countryside. His daughter, who joined the Red Guards, will spend the rest of her life trying to understand what she did and why. The Cultural Revolution is not a story about villains. It is a story about a system that made ordinary people its instruments.

**4 — Shenzhen, 1980:** A fishing village of 30,000 people is designated as China's first Special Economic Zone. Foreign investment is allowed. Market prices are permitted. The plan is to test capitalism in a controlled environment while maintaining socialist governance. The experiment works beyond anyone's expectations. By 2020, Shenzhen is a city of 13 million, the headquarters of Huawei and Tencent, and the proof of concept for China's economic model. The fishing village is gone. The city is one of the largest on Earth.

**5 — Tiananmen, June 4, 1989:** Students and workers have been occupying Tiananmen Square for seven weeks, demanding political reform, accountability for corruption, and dialogue with the government. The People's Liberation Army enters Beijing. The number of dead is unknown — the Chinese government has never disclosed it; estimates range from hundreds to thousands. The man standing in front of the tank column on Chang'an Avenue is not killed. His name is unknown. The image is the most recognized photograph of the twentieth century's last decade. In China, it cannot be seen.

**6 — The New Era:** 2022: Xi Jinping is re-elected to a third term as General Secretary, becoming the most powerful Chinese leader since Mao. The constitutional term limits that Deng Xiaoping introduced to prevent another Mao have been removed. The Party is consolidating control over every sector of Chinese society — business, technology, education, religion, culture. Whether this represents China's rise to its historical position as a great civilization, or the beginning of a new cycle of authoritarian excess, is the question that the next generation of Chinese people, and the world they live in, will answer.
