# Songhay — "The River Empire"

The Songhai built their empire from the river. Gao, their capital on the Niger bend, sat at the junction of the river highway and the Saharan trade routes — a position that Sunni Ali Ber, the greatest Songhai military ruler, understood was worth more than any gold mine. When Sunni Ali came to power in 1464, the Mali Empire had been contracting for a century and the Niger bend cities — Timbuktu, Djenné, Gao — were drifting free of any central authority. He spent twenty-eight years campaigning, fighting, building, and reorganizing until the Songhai state controlled the entire middle Niger from Djenné to beyond Gao. He built a professional river navy — the only one in West African history — and used it to project power along a river system that was otherwise impossible to control from the land alone.

The Songhai Empire at its height under Askia Muhammad (1493–1528) was the largest state West Africa has ever seen — a territory of approximately 1.4 million square kilometers, governing diverse peoples under an administrative system that organized the empire into provinces, each with its own governor appointed by the Askia. The scholars of Timbuktu flourished under Askia Muhammad's patronage; the three great mosques of Sankore, Djinguereber, and Sidi Yahia were centers of Islamic scholarship that drew students from across the Muslim world. The Songhai state was not simply a military conquest machine; it was a sophisticated administrative and commercial empire that understood that controlling the Niger bend meant controlling the intellectual and commercial center of West Africa.

## What Defined Them

**The river navy and the politics of water.** The Songhai were the only West African state to build and maintain a professional river navy. The Niger River system was the empire's road, its food supply, and its defensive barrier simultaneously. Sunni Ali built his navy to project force rapidly along river routes that would take a land army weeks to cover, and he maintained it as a standing force, not an emergency assembly. The navy enabled the Songhai to take Timbuktu in 1468 and Djenné in 1473 — sieges of cities that sat on the water and could only be truly subdued by forces that controlled the water. The river was the empire's spine, and the navy was the mechanism by which Songhai held it.

**Timbuktu's scholars and the tension with power.** The Islamic scholars of Timbuktu maintained a relationship with the Songhai rulers that was never entirely comfortable. Sunni Ali was contemptuous of the urban scholars and their pedantry — he was a conqueror from the rural Songhai heartland, and the Muslim clerics of Timbuktu's mosques looked down on him in return. Askia Muhammad, who overthrew Sunni Ali's son and legitimized his coup with a fatwa from the scholars of Timbuktu, was a committed patron of Islamic learning; under him the relationship was productive. The great scholars of the era — Ahmad Baba, Mahmud Kati — were products of this patronage. But the tension between military state-builders and scholarly Islamic institutions runs through Songhai history from beginning to end.

**The administrative reform under the Askias.** Askia Muhammad's contribution to the Songhai state was organizational. He divided the empire into provinces under appointed governors, established a professional army separate from the royal household, regularized tax collection, and created a system of weights and measures for trade. He was also one of the most traveled of West African rulers — his hajj to Mecca in 1496–97 was a state journey on the scale of Mansa Musa's, and he returned with the title of Caliph of the Sudan from the Sharif of Mecca. The Askia system of governance was the most sophisticated administrative apparatus in West Africa at the time, and its collapse during the succession conflicts of the mid-sixteenth century left the empire vulnerable when the Moroccan invasion came.

**The Moroccan invasion and the end.** In 1591, a Moroccan Sa'dian army of four thousand soldiers — many of them Andalusian refugees armed with arquebuses — crossed the Sahara and defeated the Songhai army at Tondibi. The disproportion was not in numbers (the Songhai army was much larger) but in technology: the firearms were decisive against cavalry and infantry with no equivalent weapon. The Moroccan forces captured Timbuktu and Gao, but they could not control a territory of 1.4 million square kilometers. The Songhai state fragmented; the Moroccan pashalik at Timbuktu became its own independent entity; the scholars of Timbuktu — including Ahmad Baba, who was deported to Marrakesh and continued to write there — carried the intellectual tradition into exile and preservation. The libraries survived. The state did not.

**Ahmad Baba and the scholarly tradition's resilience.** Ahmad Baba of Timbuktu (1556–1627) is one of the most important African intellectuals of the early modern period. He wrote more than forty books on Islamic jurisprudence, theology, grammar, and biography. Deported to Marrakesh after the Moroccan invasion, he continued teaching and writing there, producing a biographical dictionary of Maliki scholars that remains a primary source for West African intellectual history. When he was finally allowed to return to Timbuktu in 1608, he resumed teaching. Ahmad Baba did not need the Songhai state to do his work. The intellectual tradition he represented was not the state's property; it was the city's inheritance, carried in the libraries of private scholars' families.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Gao Kings:** At the Niger bend, a chief collects toll from passing boats. He is not yet emperor. His city is not yet the center of the world. But the river bends here, and everything that moves along it passes this point.

**2 — Sunni Ali's War:** The river navy has surrounded Djenné for seven years. The city is on the water and cannot be taken from the land. Sunni Ali waits. He is not a patient man by nature. He has become patient because the city is worth it.

**3 — Askia Muhammad's Hajj:** At Mecca, the Askia receives the title Caliph of the Sudan from the Sharif. He does not know yet whether this will help him govern his empire or simply give his enemies a new argument. He is taking notes regardless.

**4 — The Succession Wars:** An Askia's son has imprisoned his father. This is the third time a son has deposed a father in two generations. The scholars of Timbuktu are writing legal opinions about whether this is legitimate. The army is not waiting for the opinions.

**5 — Tondibi:** The arquebusiers' volley breaks the Songhai cavalry charge. It is over quickly. Ahmad Baba, in his library, hears the news and begins to consider what he can carry and what he cannot. The books are not negotiable.
