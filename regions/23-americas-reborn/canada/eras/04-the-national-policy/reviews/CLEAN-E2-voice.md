# CLEAN — E-2 Voice Keeper
## Canada Era 4: The National Policy and Its Discontents

**Voice audit — Register drift, textbook voice, authorial exposure, random-paragraph test.**

### Voice Register Assessment

The chapter uses the bilingual-embodied register (see Voice Spectrum, Rupture & Diaspora category): sections in the Secwépemc/First Nations interior alternate with sections in the colonial-administrative reported-speech register. The register is productive and appropriate for this era's dual-track structure.

### Random-Paragraph Test

Three paragraphs selected at random:

**Paragraph 1 (Section II):**
"The per-capita funding model made the theory operational and made the outcome worse. Churches received federal payment for each enrolled child. More children meant more money. The incentive was to maximize enrollment regardless of whether the schools could safely care for the children they enrolled."

Voice: Clear, stripped, analytical. The sentences are short and carry weight. The register is appropriate for the administrative machine section — it reads like an inspector's finding, which is the right tone for describing how the machine operated. No drift.

**Paragraph 2 (Section III):**
"The land was not his to sell because it was not property in the sense the law intended; it was relationship. The Niitsitapi were a people organized around mobility, around the buffalo runs and the seasonal movements that the land itself prescribed. You did not own the land; you were in relationship with it, responsible to it, accountable to its patterns."

Voice: The "you" in "You did not own the land" is a direct-address construction that pulls the reader inside the Niitsitapi worldview. This is the right register for this section. Strong.

**Paragraph 3 (Section V):**
"Canada entered the First World War in August 1914 automatically, as a British dominion. There was no Parliamentary vote. Canada was at war because Britain was at war."

Voice: The laconic declarative rhythm here is appropriate. Three sentences, each shorter than the last. The rhythm performs the automatism of the entry. No drift.

### McClung Metacommentary Flag

The three-paragraph McClung passage in Section VI contains this:

"This is not a verdict on McClung's contributions, which were real and lasting. It is an observation about the limits of a reform tradition that could not see what it shared with the system it did not reform. The chapter does not require those limits to cancel the contributions. It requires that both be visible at once, because both were real at once."

This is direct authorial address. "The chapter does not require" and "It requires that both be visible" are the author narrating what the chapter is doing. This is the kind of metacommentary that the Compression Editor would target as explaining what the scene already showed.

**Fix:** Remove the metacommentary and let the juxtaposition do the work. The paragraph before this (ending "without seeing any contradiction between what each was doing") has already made the observation. The three-sentence metacommentary can be cut entirely. The reader has seen both tracks; they do not need to be told that the chapter has shown them both.

### Em-Dash Audit
Chapter: 0 em-dashes. Excellent. The register finds its pauses through the colon (administrative register), the semicolon (treaty/legal passages), and the period (laconic sections). Zero em-dashes is an emergent property of the voice discipline, not an enforced result.

### Additional Voice Observations
- "What the government called settlement" and "what they called civilization" and "what they called paganism": these marked-perspective constructions are used appropriately throughout to distinguish colonial language from neutral description. Consistent.
- The 2021 flash-forward in Section I ("In 2021, ground-penetrating radar will scan...") uses future tense for a past event, which is a specific technique for establishing the oral tradition's prior authority. The tense shift is intentional and controlled. No drift.
