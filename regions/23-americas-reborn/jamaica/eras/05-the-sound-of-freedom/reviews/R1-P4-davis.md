# Panel-1 Review — P-4 Wade Davis
**Era:** Jamaica Era 5 — The Sound of Freedom
**Date:** 2026-04-17

What the opening has understood, which most writing about reggae fails to understand, is that Rastafari is not a style or a sensibility or even a religion in any sense that fits the available Western categories. It is a complete epistemological system. It has its own theory of history (Babylon's rise and destined fall), its own theory of knowledge (the reasoning session, the herb as sacrament-and-instrument), its own theory of the body (the locks as antenna, ital food as purity, the body as the site of a spiritual program), and its own theory of time (the linear European calendar is Babylon's; Jah's time is different, cyclical, the return to Africa is not a future event but an eternal orientation).

The opening handles this correctly. It does not reduce Rastafari to "belief." It does not explain the locks as "a religious symbol." It shows the locks as a practice with its own complete internal logic — Nazarite vow, Samson, Jah, the antenna — and trusts the reader to recognize that this logic is sophisticated whether or not the reader shares its premises.

**What I want to push on:**

The knowledge system here is embodied in a very specific way. The music is not the delivery system for a theology that exists separately. The music IS the theology, in the same way that the eland dance IS the San's knowledge system, or the Tongan navigator's body IS the navigation. You cannot separate the reggae rhythm from the Rastafari claim about what rhythm means. The offbeat is not where Rastafari happens to place its music; the offbeat is the Rastafari argument, made audible.

The full chapter needs to demonstrate this at the level of method — not just assert it, but show it working. The way to do this is through specific songs. "Small Axe." "Redemption Song." "War" (which was Selassie's speech to the United Nations, set to music). These are not examples. They are the primary evidence. The chapter should analyze at least one song at the level of how the music's formal properties enact the theological-political argument, not just what the lyrics say.

**The knowledge system question:**

What is the method of transmission? The reasoning session is named in the opening but not described at method level. How does reasoning work? Who speaks and when? What is the role of silence? What happens when someone speaks falsely — does the circle correct it, or does each man reason for himself and Jah judges? This is the knowledge system operating, and it should appear in the chapter at the level of practice, not description.

The dreadlocks are the most visible external sign of a commitment that takes years to make and cannot be undone (hair once locked cannot be combed out — it must be cut). This irreversibility is itself a theological statement. The chapter should show this, not just state it.

**The question I'd push on:**

What happens to Rastafari when the Emperor dies? Haile Selassie was deposed by a military coup in 1974 and reportedly killed in 1975. How does a theology organized around a living God respond to that God's death? This is the chapter's most important internal crisis and the full chapter must address it with the same respect for the tradition's own reasoning that the opening demonstrates.

**Overall:** A strong opening that correctly identifies the knowledge system. The full chapter must show the system operating at method level.
