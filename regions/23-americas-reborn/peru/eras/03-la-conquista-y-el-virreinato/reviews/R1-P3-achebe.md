# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Review — Opening: La Conquista y El Virreinato

The first test I apply to any chapter in this project is the one that bears my name: could a thoughtful member of this culture read this and feel their story was told with dignity — from inside — regardless of whether they agreed with every detail? 

This opening passes that test. More: it passes it with a quality I find rare — the Andean cosmological framework is not explained to the reader as exotic belief, but inhabited as physics. The sentence "This was not metaphor. This was physics, Andean physics, the deep grammar of how the world worked" is exactly the move that separates inside-voice from outside-voice. The author is not saying "the Andeans believed that the universe runs on return." The author is saying: the universe runs on return. The reader is placed inside the epistemology, not above it.

Micaela Bastidas is rendered with full agency. She is not a supporting figure in her husband's story; she is the one with the plan, the timeline, the Quechua rebuke. The word qonqarqanki — "you are forgetting" — is one of the finest moments in the opening, because it does three things simultaneously: it characterizes Micaela as politically precise and impatient; it characterizes Condorcanqui as a man of deliberation and perhaps doubt; and it names the deepest cultural stake (forgetting who you are is the worst failure) without explaining it. This is inside-voice at its best.

My concern is about the era title and its scope. The opening focuses on 1780 — Túpac Amaru II — but the era is called "La Conquista y El Virreinato," which covers 1533 to 1821: the conquest, the Atahualpa execution, Potosí, the Taki Onqoy movement, and the uprising. The opening justifies this scope by treating the uprising as the era's focal point and the earlier history as depth charges. This is defensible. But the chapter will need to handle the Atahualpa execution and the conquest itself with the same inside-voice discipline. The conquest chapters in many history projects are written from above — the Spanish as subjects, the Andean world as object receiving them. This chapter must resist that entirely.

The mention of the Taki Onqoy in the overview suggests an earlier religious resistance movement that the chapter should address. The taki — mentioned in the closing — is the song-as-record. I would ask: does the chapter's full version carry the Taki Onqoy tradition forward into the Túpac Amaru uprising? The spiritual resistance and the political resistance are not separate streams in the Andean world.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening closes by saying the name Túpac Amaru "has not stopped." This is a forward reference to subsequent revolutionary movements that took the name (the Peruvian guerrilla group MRTA, the Túpac Amaru Revolutionary Movement; and before that, the spirit of the name in independence movements). The chapter will be set within 1533-1821, so it cannot follow the name into the twentieth century. But it should find a way to close that is native to the era — the name traveling, the taki traveling — without importing the anachronism of later movements. Can the chapter's landing stay inside the era while still honoring the opening's promise that the name was already moving?
