# The Late Republic and the Crisis

Tiberius Sempronius Gracchus stood on the Rostra in the summer of 133 BCE and asked the citizens of Roma a question.

The wild beasts of Italia, he said, have their dens. They have a place to sleep, a hole in the earth. The men who fight and die for Roma have nothing. They have the air and the light and nothing else. They wander with their wives and their children, homeless. The generals who command them lie to them when they tell them in battle to fight for their ancestors' tombs, for their household gods. They have no tombs. They have no household gods. They fight and die for the wealth and luxury of others. They are called the masters of the world, and they do not own a clod of earth.

The speech is in Plutarch, who wrote it two centuries later, and the words are Plutarch's, not Gracchus's. But the argument they carry was real. The small farmers who had been the Republic's citizen-soldiers were being driven from their land. The great estates, the latifundia, worked by the enslaved captives of the wars of conquest, were swallowing the ager publicus, the public land that had been distributed to citizens after each new conquest. The wealthy bought or seized what the law said was common, and the common people who had no patrons and no advocates found themselves choosing between selling their labor in Roma and selling their votes to the man who would feed them.

Gracchus proposed a land commission to enforce the existing legal limit on public land holdings and redistribute the excess to landless citizens. The proposal was not revolutionary by the Republic's own standards. The limit he sought to enforce had been law since the Licinian-Sextian rogations of 367 BCE. But the men who held the excess land were senators, and the senators who stood to lose controlled the machinery of the state, and the collision between the tribune's proposal and the Senate's interests would crack the Republic's constitutional structure in a way that could not be repaired.

They killed him in the Forum. A group of senators led by the pontifex maximus Scipio Nasica armed themselves with clubs and chair legs, gathered their clients, and beat Tiberius Gracchus and three hundred of his supporters to death on the Capitoline hill. The bodies were thrown into the Tiber. No trial. No legal proceeding. No precedent in the Republic's five centuries of self-governance for a political murder of this kind. The mos maiorum, which had absorbed every previous crisis through debate, compromise, and institutional adaptation, had reached a question it could not answer within its own rules: what happens when the Senate's interest and the people's interest are irreconcilable?

The Republic had four generations left. In those four generations, every institution the Romani had built to prevent one-man rule would be tested, strained, weaponized, and broken. Not by invaders. Not by catastrophe. By the men the Republic itself had produced, using the tools the Republic itself had given them.
