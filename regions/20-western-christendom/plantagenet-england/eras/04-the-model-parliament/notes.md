# Notes — The Model Parliament
## Production Bible | Era 4, Plantagenet England (1272–1327 CE)

---

## Structural Engine

**Engine:** Consent-for-taxation as self-reinforcing institutional practice.

**Mechanism:** The king needs money for war. Money requires taxation. Taxation of free men requires their consent. Consent requires assembly. Assembly must include those who hold taxable wealth — which, from 1295 onward, means not just barons and bishops but knights of the shires and burgesses of the towns. The act of assembling to consent creates a body of participants who develop expertise in the form, know their rights within it, and have interest in its continuation. Each Parliament that is called makes the next Parliament easier to call — because the participants understand the process, have built relationships within it, and have learned how to use the grievance-presentation mechanism to extract concessions in return for consent. The habit of consent becomes a constitutional custom. Custom, challenged and confirmed, becomes law.

**Terminal condition (v1.6 framework):** *Legitimacy gap* — the engine reaches its crisis when a king (Edward II) attempts to govern without Parliament's consent on financial matters, is deposed by a Parliament-backed coalition, and the deposition itself is legitimized by parliamentary procedure. The engine does not fail. It succeeds too well: Parliament acquires the authority to make and unmake kings. The terminal condition is not the Parliament's dissolution but its apotheosis. The era closes with Edward II removed in 1327 by a procedure that Parliament gives legitimacy to. The engine has become the ground that everything stands on. (Note: this is adjacent to but distinct from the "vindicating" terminal type — here the institution's success creates the crisis for the individual king, not the civilization.)

## Key Sources for This Era

**Primary:**
- *Quod omnes tangit* formula in the summons to the Parliament of 1295 (Maddicott, pp. 153–165)
- Bracton, *De Legibus et Consuetudinibus Angliae* — "the king is under God and the law"; key passages on royal authority and custom
- Year Books (plea rolls of the common law courts) — cases from Edward I's reign
- The Statute of Westminster I (1275) — codifying common law customs in 51 chapters
- The Statute of Winchester (1285) — local policing, watch and ward, assizes of arms
- Roger of Wendover/Matthew Paris as background for preceding period; chronicles thin for Edward I's reign proper
- Flores Historiarum (Westminster continuation) — parliamentary records, 1290s

**Secondary:**
- Maddicott, J.R. *The Origins of the English Parliament, 924–1327* (2010) — the essential work on parliamentary development; distinguishes 1265 from 1295 as constitutive moments
- Prestwich, Michael. *Edward I* (1988) — authoritative biography; detailed on the statutes and the Scottish wars
- Clanchy, M.T. *From Memory to Written Record* (2012) — on the expansion of literate bureaucracy; essential for the chancery's role
- Holt, J.C. *Magna Carta* (1992) — on the charter tradition and its propagation through the thirteenth century

**Named figures available:**
- Edward I (1239–1307) — king; legally sophisticated, personally brutal in war, institutionally innovative
- Robert Burnell (d. 1292) — Edward's chancellor; the man who drafted the Statute of Westminster; architect of the legal edifice
- John de Vescy — a baron who negotiated the parliamentary formula; appears in the records
- Adam de Stratton — a corrupt royal official whose prosecution under Edward's legal machinery illustrates the king's ambivalent relationship to his own law
- Edward II (1284–1327) — the terminal figure; his deposition is the engine's final consequence
- Roger Mortimer — the magnate who effectively deposes Edward II with Isabella of France; Parliament gives him the form

## Narrative Arc

**Section 1 — The Writ** (~800w, opening already written)
Edward I and the Parliament of 1295. The *quod omnes tangit* formula. The chancery clerk, the November cold, the three-estate assembly. The constitutional argument stated in the terms of the culture: need → consent → precedent.

**Section 2 — The Legal Edifice** (~900w)
What Edward built in the first twenty years of his reign. Robert Burnell and the Statute of Westminster I (1275): fifty-one chapters hammering customary law into written statute. The common law circuit judges. The year books — the first systematic recording of decided cases, the knowledge system at method level. Show a specific case: an assize of novel disseisin, argued before an itinerant justice in a county hall, decided by the common law's own procedure. This is where the Bracton quote lives: "the king is under God and the law." Not as a principle stated from above — as a working judge applying it in a county town.

**Section 3 — De Montfort's Shadow** (~800w)
The precedent Edward inherited. The Parliament of 1265 — de Montfort's gamble, its military defeat, its constitutional survival. How Edward, who destroyed de Montfort, used the form de Montfort had invented. The crucial distinction: de Montfort's Parliament was a baronial instrument against the king; Edward's Parliament was a royal instrument for the king. The form was identical. The relationship was inverted. This is the common law's own method of inheritance: the case survives the litigant.

**Section 4 — Wales and Scotland** (~800w)
The wars that drove the parliamentary machine. Wales, 1277–1284: Edward's conquest, the Statute of Rhuddlan, the imposition of English law on a Welsh-speaking people who had their own legal tradition (the Laws of Hywel Dda). The Parliament does not ask the Welsh. Scotland, 1296–1305: the longer, more disastrous war. The Scottish nobility's own constitutional traditions — the Declaration of Arbroath, written in 1320, is the Scottish response to English parliamentary theory. The Parliament of England is being assembled to finance the war against a people who also have claims about law and consent.

**Section 5 — The Machine Learns** (~900w)
The period 1295–1307: Parliament becomes a practice. The knights and burgesses begin to submit common petitions — collective grievances rather than individual county complaints. The Commons starts to emerge as a political body with coherent interests. Specific grievances about purveyance (the royal right to requisition goods and labor), about sheriffs' abuses, about tallage. The king answers petitions because refusing them would break the transaction that makes consent meaningful. The Commons learns that Parliament is a venue where power can be applied.

**Section 6 — The Engine's Consequence** (~600w)
Edward II inherits an institution his father built as a revenue instrument. The magnates use it against him — first in 1311 (the Ordinances, forcing the exile of Edward's favorite Gaveston), then in 1327 when Isabella and Mortimer bring an army and Parliament is assembled to formalize the deposition. The Parliament that Edward I designed to extract consent now gives authority to remove the king who cannot govern within its framework. Landing: the engine does not close — it becomes the ground on which English constitutional history stands. The writ still goes out. The formula still reads: *quod omnes tangit.*

## Voice Plan

**Register:** Legal-public. Declarative sentences that close like rulings. The common law's own cadence: case stated, principle identified, precedent cited. Analysis carried in the same register as narrative.

**Rhythm markers:**
- Short declarative sentences as pivots: "This is how constitutional precedents are made."
- Comma-chains for enumeration (the statutes, the writs, the cases)
- Latin phrases in the original, with immediate English following: the chancery's own bilingual practice
- Zero em-dashes target — the legal-public register finds its pauses in full stops and the colon

**Voice test:** The voice should sound like it is being read aloud in a chancery hall, or argued before an itinerant justice. Not performed — deliberated.

## Figures Notes (for figures.md)

**Edward I** — the chapter's primary figure. Not a constitutional visionary: a pragmatic warrior-king who needed money and knew how the law worked. Physically large, personally violent (he struck a messenger who brought bad news, broke his daughter's crown in a temper), legally sophisticated beyond most of his contemporaries. Bracton-trained in the sense that his judges were Bracton-trained. His legal sophistication was instrumental — he used the law as a tool until the law became the tool's master.

**Robert Burnell** — the architect of the legal edifice, Edward's chancellor for eighteen years. A man of the Church who never became Archbishop of Canterbury because his moral conduct was too notorious (multiple illegitimate children, financial irregularities). A brilliant administrator. The statutes bear his hand more than any other. He is the chapter's hidden figure — the maker of constitutional machinery who appears in none of the traditional constitutional histories.

**The knight of the shire** — unnamed but composite, drawn from the records of county elections and parliamentary returns. He rides in from the provinces. He brings his county's grievances. He has been elected by the county court, which means he has standing in his community. He is not a revolutionary. He is doing his duty within a system that is becoming, without his full knowledge, something larger than itself.

**Edward II** — the terminal figure. Every institution needs the man who breaks it to reveal its strength. Edward II's deposition in 1327 is the machine demonstrating that it now runs independently of any individual king's will. His personal tragedy is real: a man who was not suited to be king of England in this particular moment, surrounded by men who knew how to use the Parliament against him.
