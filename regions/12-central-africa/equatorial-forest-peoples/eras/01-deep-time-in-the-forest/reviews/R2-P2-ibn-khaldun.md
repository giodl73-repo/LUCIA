# R2-P2 — Ibn Khaldun
## Panel Review, Round 2 — Notes

**Chapter:** Deep Time in the Forest — Era 1
**Reviewer:** Ibn Khaldun (P-2)
**Stage:** PANEL-2

---

The production bible addresses my concern from Round 1 in a way that satisfies me. The author has identified the transmission mechanism — "watch, attempt, be corrected by the bird's non-response" — and made it the epistemological argument of the chapter. This is the right move. The knowledge system is verified by the non-human world, not by human authority. This is genuinely different from the verification structures of the knowledge systems I know — the Islamic scholarly tradition, the Roman legal system, the Confucian examination — and the difference matters.

The section plan is sound. The five sections do not narrate a chronology, which is correct given the time span. They demonstrate a worldview through five practices, concentrically expanding from the individual (honeyguide) to the collective (music) to the ethical (ekila) to the structural (farming interface). The arc makes sense as an argument.

My remaining pressure point is Section 5: the farming interface. The notes describe this as "the terms not yet set" — the exchange relationship arrived at but not stabilized. I want the chapter to be honest about the structural asymmetry here without importing modern political-economy language. The farming communities have something the forest peoples need (iron, starch); the forest peoples have something the farming communities need (meat, honey, medicine, labor). The exchange is real. The asymmetry is also real: the farming communities can, and eventually will, use their settled-state advantages to extract more than the exchange requires. The chapter should show the first moment of this negotiation without projecting the eventual exploitation backward onto it. The relationship begins as genuine exchange. Note that it is asymmetric by structure without calling it exploitation. Let the structure speak.

One addition to the structural engine: the notes frame the chapter's terminal condition as the arrival of the farming interface. I would suggest sharpening this: the terminal condition is the moment when the forest knowledge system is first required to operate in relation to a different knowledge system — the farming community's knowledge, which is based on control and cultivation rather than relationship and maintenance. The two systems are not incompatible, but they are in tension. The chapter ends at the beginning of that tension.

**Assessment:** The production bible is ready. The chapter can be written from this foundation. The source asymmetry handling (treating outside observers' accounts as evidence of encounter rather than description of the culture) is exactly right and should produce a strong Evidence score.
