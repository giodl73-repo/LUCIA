# Figures — A Colônia — Açúcar, Ouro, e Escravos

---

## Zumbi dos Palmares (c. 1655–1695)

Born in Palmares. This is the essential fact about him, the one that the colonial sources record without understanding: the last great leader of Palmares did not escape from slavery. He was born into freedom, in the only free state the colony possessed.

He was captured as a child — the records say around the age of seven — and brought to Porto Calvo on the coast, where a Portuguese priest named Father António Melo raised him in the Church. Zumbi learned Portuguese. He learned Latin. He learned to read and write in the language of the colony that was searching for Palmares. At approximately fifteen, he ran. He returned to Palmares by paths the priest could not have followed.

What Zumbi carried back from his years in Porto Calvo was not conversion. He carried the colonizer's language, which is a different kind of knowledge from the colonizer's worldview. He could read the Portuguese military communications that described the expeditions sent against Palmares. He could hear, in the formal Portuguese of official correspondence, what the colonial authorities understood about the quilombo and what they did not. He used this. The knowledge of how the colonizer thought was, in Palmares's hands, military intelligence.

He became the military and political leader of Palmares after the removal of Ganga Zumba — the exact circumstances, whether inheritance or coup, are debated in the sources and probably contained both. He led Palmares through the period of its greatest military pressure: the decade of escalating Portuguese expeditions following the 1678 treaty fracture. He was not killed in battle. He was betrayed. The distinction mattered to those who mourned him, because betrayal is the only way a free people can be delivered to the people who want to own them.

His head was displayed in Recife. November 20, 1695.

November 20 is now the Dia da Consciência Negra in Brazil. The Movimento Negro Unificado established the commemoration in 1978; the federal national holiday was recognized in 2003. The colonial proof of a death became a national day of reckoning. This would not have surprised Zumbi. He understood, from his years with the priest, that the colonizer's records tell a different story from what actually happened. He was right that the story would not end with his head.

## Ganga Zumba (dates uncertain, d. c. 1678–1680)

His title is probably a Bantu royal designation: nganga a nzumbi, something like "great spirit-lord" or "master of the dead." The Portuguese transliteration — Ganga Zumba — stripped the meaning from the words while preserving their sound, which is one way colonial language works.

He built the federation. The ten mocambos of Palmares, spread across forty leagues of the Serra da Barriga, with their separate leaders and their shared purposes, were his political creation. He did not rule by force — a quilombo organized by force cannot last ninety years, because there is nowhere to send the people who refuse. He ruled by the kind of authority that the Bantu political tradition recognized: the paramount lord who held the spiritual genealogy of the community, who adjudicated disputes, who represented the living and the dead to each other.

He signed the treaty of 1678.

The argument for this has been reconstructed from what he said and what he did not say, from the terms he accepted and the terms he rejected. He was not trying to end Palmares. He was trying to preserve what Palmares was capable of preserving. If recognition came at the cost of closing the borders to new runaways, that was a tragic cost — he understood this — but Palmares closed to new runaways still existed, still honored the orixás, still transmitted the initiatory knowledge, still raised children who were free. Palmares destroyed by a Portuguese expedition in 1678 preserved nothing. He calculated.

He may have been wrong. He may have been right about a version of Palmares that Zumbi would not allow to exist. This is the unresolvable argument at the heart of the quilombo's political life.

He died at Cucaú, the coastal settlement to which he moved after signing. Poisoned, the colonial sources say. By whom, they do not record. The colonial sources were interested in whether Palmares was finished. It was not.

He is less remembered in Brazil than Zumbi. November 20 is Zumbi's day, not his. The man who refused compromise is more useful to the national myth than the man who attempted it. This does not mean he was wrong.

## A Quilombola — Composite Figure

She is drawn from the historical record of the seventeenth century Atlantic trade, the ethnographic record of Candomblé initiation, and the documentary record of the enslaved people of the Mina coast who arrived in Pernambuco during the sugar economy's height. She is not a named individual; the records did not preserve the names of the people they are describing. This composite is disclosed here. She represents specifically the Nagô/Yoruba tradition — one of several African traditions present in Palmares. The Bantu peoples who formed much of the quilombo's population carried distinct religious practices (the minkisi system among them), represented by other, unnamed figures in the community. The composite woman does not stand for all of Palmares's African traditions; she stands for one living strand of its transmission.

She came from the Costa da Mina — the term the Portuguese used for the stretch of West African coastline from which Fon, Ewe, and Yoruba peoples were taken in the seventeenth century. She carried specific initiatory knowledge of Xangô, the orixá of thunder and justice and the Yoruba royal lineage. This knowledge had been transmitted to her through her grandmother: the rhythms of his drums, the colors he accepted (red and white), the particular cadence of the chants that opened his attention. The knowledge was procedural, not descriptive. It lived in her muscle memory and her ear.

She was taken, transported, and arrived in Pernambuco with her grandmother's knowledge intact in her body, because her body was the only container the cargo manifest did not inventory. She worked at an engenho during safra. In her third year, she ran.

The knowledge she carried arrived in Palmares. There it joined other knowledge: the Bantu title structures of Palmares's governance, the Tupi agricultural practices that fed the quilombo, the capoeira training that looked like dancing. What she brought was not unchanged by the journey. It adapted. The ceremonies she helped perform in the Serra da Barriga were not identical to the ceremonies of her grandmother's house. The drums were constructed from different materials; the rhythms were held by people of different origins who had learned them through transmission rather than birth. The orixá was present. The orixá does not require sameness. The orixá requires only that the chain of hands be unbroken.

In the history of the terreiro tradition in Brazil, women like her are the primary record. The ialorixás who established the Engenho Velho terreiro in Salvador in the 1830s — Iyá Detá, Iyá Kalá, Iyá Nassô — were carrying a lineage that had passed through hands like hers, through the quilombo years, through the coastal senzalas, through every place the transmission had survived. The babalorixás and ialorixás who now run terreiros in Bahia hold initiatory lineages that trace through these women and through the women before them, back to the ships, back to the coast, back to the hands that first taught a granddaughter how to call the thunder's attention.

She does not have a name in the record. She has descendants in the tradition.

## The Senhor de Engenho — Composite Figure

He is not named here because the chapter does not need to name him. He is drawn from the social history of the Pernambuco and Bahia planter class — the senhores de engenho who ran the sugar economy of the colony from the mid-sixteenth century through the eighteenth, on credit from Lisbon merchants, with enslaved labor they did not own outright but depended on absolutely.

He is Catholic, literate in Portuguese, fluent in the idiom of colonial obligation and Christian paternalism. He does not think of himself as a cruel man. He thinks of himself as a prudent manager of a difficult enterprise, a man whose responsibilities extend to his family, his creditors, his free workers, and — in his own understanding — to the enslaved people in his senzala, who require his guidance and his discipline and his protection from each other.

Gilberto Freyre, writing in 1933, described the relationship between the casa-grande and the senzala as one of intimacy and paternalism — not without cruelty, but not only cruelty. Freyre was right that the relationship was intimate. He was not right enough about what intimacy conducted under conditions of absolute power costs the people without power. The woman at the engenho rollers and the senhor in the great house lived at close range. The closeness was not equality. It was surveillance.

He did not know where Palmares was. His militia commanders had tried twice to find it and returned without success. He understood, at some level, that the people in his senzala knew where it was — that the name passed between them in the way things pass between people who have no privacy except the privacy of a shared language the master does not fully speak. He could not afford to think too clearly about this. He had crops to process, debts to service, and a colonial order to maintain. The colonial order required that certain people could not be free. Palmares was the colony's proof that it was wrong about this. He preferred not to think about Palmares.

He is less interesting than the people in his senzala. This does not mean he was unimportant. He ran the machine.
