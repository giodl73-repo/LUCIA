# P-4 -- Wade Davis -- Review of "The Scholarly Peak" Opening

**Overall:** This is what I have been waiting for. The manuscript tradition treated as what it is: a genuine intellectual achievement, described with the same depth and seriousness any chapter on Oxford or Bologna would receive. No apologetics. No defensiveness. Just the work.

**What works:**
- The opening treats scholarship as physical labor. The lamp, the ink, the pen, the sand that smears wet pages. This is not romanticized knowledge -- it is craft. A man working with his hands and his mind simultaneously, and the text honors both.
- The commentary-on-commentary structure is described with intellectual seriousness. Three layers of interpretation. A cross-desert argument conducted by letter. This is how a living intellectual tradition operates -- through disagreement, response, and counter-response. The opening does not merely assert Timbuktu's scholarly credentials; it shows the mechanism of scholarship at work.
- The ijaza system is rendered as a knowledge technology. Chains of transmission. Licenses to teach specific texts. This is a different epistemic architecture from the European university model, and the opening lets it stand on its own terms without comparison. This is exactly right.
- The students from diverse origins are presented without making diversity the point. They came for the knowledge. That is the point. Their origins are incidental to the intellectual project.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening gives us jurisprudence in detail but mentions astronomy, mathematics, and medicine only in a list: "mathematics for inheritance calculations, astronomy for fixing prayer times and the direction of Mecca, medicine for the obligations of care that the faith required." This is the moment to go deeper. What did the astronomical observations look like? What instruments were used? What mathematical traditions were engaged? The jurisprudence is specific; the other disciplines are gestures. The full chapter must correct this imbalance. If you are going to treat Sankore at the same depth as Oxford, you must treat its astronomy with the same specificity as its law.
- The merchant who carries books -- "a book could be worth more than salt if the right scholar wanted it" -- is a beautiful detail but it sits alone. The economics of the manuscript trade need more texture. Who commissioned copies? What did they cost? How did the book trade intersect with the salt-gold trade? Knowledge has a material economy, and this chapter must make it visible.

**The question I'd push on:**
Timbuktu's scholars were not merely receiving knowledge from Cairo and Fez. They were producing it. Original commentaries, original legal opinions, original astronomical observations. The opening gestures at this with al-Kabari's own gloss, but the full chapter must make the productive dimension of the tradition unmistakable. Timbuktu was not a branch library. It was a center of original intellectual production. Show me what they produced that the rest of the Islamic world had to reckon with.
