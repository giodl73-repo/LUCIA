# P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of "When the Empire Reached the Sea" Opening

**Overall:** The voice is right. The story belongs to the Manden. The jelilu are the first and final authority. I am satisfied.

**What works:**
- The jelilu's epistemological precision: "The jelilu do not say he died. They say he did not come back. There is a difference." This is not commentary on the oral tradition — it IS the oral tradition, rendered in prose that carries its own logic. The distinction between death and not-returning is a knowledge claim, not a literary device. The opening understands this.
- Abu Bakr's Atlantic voyage is presented as the jelilu present it. No modern certainty is imposed. No one says he "discovered" anything or "failed" to reach anywhere. The ocean is open. This is Principle 13 at its finest — honest uncertainty as narrative strength.
- The Kurukan Fuga is treated as constitutional law, not as cultural color. The covenant "said what a mansa must be" — this is the language of a living political system, not an ethnographic exhibit.
- No forbidden words. No outside framing. No "While Europe was..." No "remarkably." The Manden is its own center.

**What doesn't:**
- The phrase "A prudent ruler would have recorded the loss and turned his attention back to the goldfields" introduces a category — prudence — that edges close to outside judgment. Who defines prudent? The sentence works because it immediately establishes that Abu Bakr was not interested in prudence, but I note the risk. In the full chapter, be careful with evaluative adjectives that smuggle in a perspective the Manden did not necessarily share.
- The captain's account uses "something the captain could not name" twice implicitly. The sea's mystery is well-handled, but ensure in the full chapter that the ocean is not rendered as only mystery and loss. The Manden reached the Atlantic as a matter of territorial and commercial expansion. The coast was a place of trade, of fishing communities, of Dyula markets. It was not only the edge of the unknown.

**The question I'd push on:**
How will the succession crises be told from inside? Khalifa's deposition is the most dangerous scene for this opening's promise. The temptation will be to frame Khalifa as a villain or a failure. But the Manden would not have understood him in those terms. He violated the covenant. The assembly acted. This must be told as the system working, not as the system producing a monster. The covenant does not need villains. It needs tests. Keep the voice inside.
