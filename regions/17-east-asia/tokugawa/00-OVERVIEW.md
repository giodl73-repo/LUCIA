# Tokugawa — Sakoku and Its Paradox

We are the Tokugawa shogunate, the rulers who brought peace to Japan after a century of civil war, and the peace we brought was so complete that it became its own kind of problem. Tokugawa Ieyasu unified Japan after the battle of Sekigahara (1600) and established the shogunate that would govern Japan from Edo (modern Tokyo) for 268 years without a major internal war. We governed through the sankin-kōtai system — requiring all daimyo to spend alternate years in Edo and in their home domains, keeping their families in Edo as permanent hostages — and through the Confucian social hierarchy that assigned every person to one of four classes: samurai, farmer, artisan, merchant.

We are also the shogunate of sakoku — the "closed country" policy that restricted contact with the outside world to a single Dutch trading post at Dejima in Nagasaki and maintained strict control over all foreign relationships. But sakoku was never as complete as the myth suggests. The Dutch at Dejima brought European science, medicine, and news into Japan continuously; the Confucian scholars who corresponded with Chinese counterparts across the East China Sea maintained intellectual connections with the continent; the Ryukyu Kingdom provided a channel for contact with China. The closed country was not closed to ideas; it was closed to political threats. And under this controlled stability, Japan accumulated — in art, commerce, literature, and social complexity — the changes that would eventually make the Meiji Restoration inevitable.

## What Defined Them

**The peace of Sekigahara.** Tokugawa Ieyasu's victory at Sekigahara in 1600 ended the Sengoku period — but it had to be maintained. The sankin-kōtai system was the institutional mechanism: the continuous movement of daimyo and their entourages between Edo and their domains, the massive expenditure required by this movement (which kept the daimyo too financially pressed to fund rebellions), and the permanent presence of daimyo families in Edo as hostages. This was governance through exhaustion and financial control, and it worked for two and a half centuries.

**The four-class hierarchy — and those below it.** The Confucian-derived four-class hierarchy (shi-nō-kō-shō: samurai, farmer, artisan, merchant) was not simply social description; it was enforced by sumptuary laws governing clothing, housing, and public conduct. But the hierarchy's theoretical ordering was already contradicted by economic reality: the merchants of Osaka and Edo accumulated wealth that the samurai who theoretically outranked them could only envy, and the cultural vitality of the Edo period came largely from the urban merchant (chōnin) class that the official hierarchy placed at the bottom.

**Edo culture — ukiyo-e and the floating world.** The chōnin culture of Edo produced the defining popular arts of the Tokugawa period: ukiyo-e woodblock prints (Hokusai, Hiroshige, Utamaro), the Kabuki theater, the haiku poetry tradition begun by Matsuo Bashō, the bunraku puppet theater, and the prose fiction of writers like Ihara Saikaku. The "floating world" (ukiyo) — the world of pleasure, beauty, and transience inhabited by the geisha, the kabuki actor, the sumo wrestler — was the subject of an urban visual culture of extraordinary sophistication. These were not elite arts; they were popular arts, produced in large editions of woodblock prints, performed in public theaters.

**Rangaku — Dutch learning.** The Dutch at Dejima were required by the shogunate to produce annual reports (Oranda fūsetsugaki) on world affairs; these reports, translated by Japanese interpreters, gave the shogunate and Japanese intellectuals a continuous if filtered view of the changing world. More significantly, Japanese scholars in Nagasaki and elsewhere developed rangaku (Dutch learning): the study of Western science, medicine, and technology through Dutch books. The rangaku scholars translated medical atlases, studied astronomy and physics, and introduced vaccination. The Tokugawa period's intellectual life was not sealed from Western knowledge; it was accumulating it selectively.

**The crisis of the opening.** When Commodore Matthew Perry arrived in 1853 with four "black ships" — steam-powered warships that could not be ignored — the shogunate's choice was stark: open the country on Western terms or face the kind of military defeat that China had suffered in the Opium War. The shogunate chose partial opening (the Harris Treaty of 1858), but the decision split Japanese society: the Sonnō Jōi ("revere the emperor, expel the barbarians") movement attacked the shogunate for capitulating, while others argued that Japan must learn from the West to survive. The Meiji Restoration of 1868 was the result — the overthrow of the shogunate and the nominal restoration of imperial rule, in the name of modernization.

## The Chapters

**1 — Sekigahara:** Tokugawa Ieyasu surveys the battlefield. It is the fifteenth of October, 1600. The battle lasted six hours. The western coalition has collapsed. He is sixty years old. He has been waiting for this day for forty years. He will spend the next fourteen years making sure it lasts.

**2 — Bashō on the Road:** The poet is walking the Narrow Road to the Deep North. He is sixty years old. He is writing haiku that compress the infinite into seventeen syllables. The ancient pond — a frog jumps in — the sound of water. He is making Japan's aesthetic tradition new with its oldest materials.

**3 — Hokusai's Wave:** The woodblock print is being carved. The Great Wave off Kanagawa — three boats, thirty-six views of Mount Fuji, the white foam that looks like claws. This image will eventually be seen by more people than any other Japanese artwork. The printmaker knows nothing of this. He is working.

**4 — Rangaku:** The physician Sugita Genpaku is dissecting a corpse in Edo in 1771. He has a Dutch medical atlas (Ontleedkundige Tafelen) that shows the internal organs. The dissection corresponds exactly to the Dutch illustration and completely contradicts the Chinese medical texts he trained on. He will spend thirty years translating the Dutch atlas. Japan is learning.

**5 — Perry's Black Ships:** The steam warships are in Edo Bay. The shogunate's officials go out to receive the letter from President Fillmore. They have no ships that could oppose them. The American officer says: open your ports, or we will return next year with more ships. He means it. The shogunate knows it. What comes next will end the Tokugawa era.
