# R1-P4 — Wade Davis — Panel Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Marajoara — Era 2: The Teso Florescence
**Round:** Panel-1
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

What this opening does well — extraordinarily well — is demonstrate that a visual language is a knowledge system. The spiral is not described as art. It is described as an argument. The fish-rib tool is not described as a technique. It is the instrument of a specific intellectual practice. The woman applying the slip is not described as a craftsperson. She is a keeper of a knowledge system that encodes a cosmology in a form that requires no text. This is precisely how ethnobotanists and anthropologists learn to see when they are paying attention: the knowledge is in the practice, and the practice is in the hands.

The teso-as-accumulated-ancestors passage is the most ethnographically alive part of the opening. It captures something that Western ontologies struggle to hold: the landscape as a record of obligation, not of ownership. To walk on the teso is to walk on your grandparents — not metaphorically, but in the specific sense that their refuse built the elevation you stand on. This is not mysticism. This is a different relationship to place: place as the body of community time rather than as a resource with coordinates.

What I want the chapter to develop is the environmental knowledge embedded in this culture. The várzea — the seasonally flooded plain — is one of the most ecologically complex environments on earth, and the Marajoara spent four hundred years learning to live inside its rhythm. The flood calendar the opening names is not simply an agricultural schedule. It is a knowledge system: knowing where the fish run as the water rises, where the turtles nest, how the flood's timing shifts across decades and what that shift means for planting. This knowledge is older than the ceramic tradition and more fundamental. The chapter should give it space.

I am also watching for how the chapter handles the relationship between the woman's knowledge and the community's knowledge. Is the ceramic tradition a specialist's practice or a general women's practice? The answer to this shapes whether we are looking at an expert class or a distributed knowledge system, and that distinction determines how the knowledge is transmitted, lost, and recovered.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening mentions that the most elaborate burial urns are found in what appear to be female burials. What does the burial itself look like? The urn holds the bones — but how are the bones prepared, how is the urn placed, who is present? The burial ceremony is the chapter's climax, and the opening should orient us toward it without narrating it.
