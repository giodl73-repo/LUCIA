# Panel Review — R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Opening: The Founding — The Experiment Begins

**Voice: Chinua Achebe — Cultural voice, dignity, authenticity, ownership**

The opening does something necessary and does it without flinching: it names the enslaved people in the room at the founding without making them props in a drama whose subject is the founders. "Outside the State House, enslaved people serve the delegates their meals." This is a sentence that establishes presence without instrumentalizing it. The people outside are present. They are not there to shame Madison. They are not there to be pitied. They are there because they were there.

What follows is careful: "This is not a detail added later for correction." This is an important act of historical honesty. The opening refuses the framing in which the presence of enslaved people at the founding is something the project of LUCIA has added as a corrective to a purer story. It was always there. The founders knew. The opening knows that the founders knew.

**What works:**
The Achebe Test is substantially passed. The founding paradox is stated from inside: not "these men were hypocrites" (which would be judgment from above) but "the contradiction was not unknown to the people who were building it in" (which is the founders' own understanding, stated plainly). Jefferson's and Madison's own words are used — Madison's "moral and political depravity," Jefferson's position in Paris — without being used to excuse or to condemn.

The closing image is right: "the people who had served the delegates cleared the dishes and went home to whatever home meant for them." The phrase "whatever home meant for them" honors the complexity of what "home" could mean for an enslaved person in 1787 — a place that was not their own, a dwelling that belonged to someone else — without claiming to know what it felt like from the inside. This is honest limitation. It does not perform empathy it cannot deliver. It acknowledges uncertainty while still placing the people in the room.

**What I'd push on:**
The chapter preview ("The Civil War was this argument carried to its military conclusion") concerns me. The founding era chapter must tell the founding era story. The gesture toward later history is appropriate for an opening that situates the era in the larger arc, but there is a risk that the chapter will use the later moral clarity of the Civil War and the Civil Rights Movement as a frame through which to read 1787. The people in Philadelphia in 1787 did not know what would happen in 1861. They knew what they were doing, but they did not know where it led. The chapter must stay inside 1787's own uncertainty, not explain 1787 in light of 1865.

The specific question: does the chapter know, and can it honor, that some of the founders believed the contradiction would resolve itself gradually — that slavery would eventually die out economically without a confrontation? This belief was wrong. But it was held in good faith by some of the people in the room, and it shaped what they were willing to write into the document. The chapter cannot tell this story honestly if it reads 1787 entirely through what we now know about 1861.

**The question I'd push on:**
The opening names Madison and Jefferson but has not yet introduced anyone who carries the full weight of the paradox from within. The enslaved people who served the delegates are present but unnamed. There were people in Philadelphia in 1787 who embodied the contradiction in specific, verifiable ways — Richard Allen, who purchased his own freedom, founded the African Methodist Episcopal Church, and lived through the founding years — who could serve as the counter-voice to Madison's official record. The chapter should not leave the unnamed people unnamed through its full length.
