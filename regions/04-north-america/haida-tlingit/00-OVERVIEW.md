# Haida and Tlingit — The Totem People of the Northwest Coast

They do not have a joint name for themselves — the Haida call themselves Haida ("people"), and the Tlingit call themselves Lingít ("people of the tides" or simply "people"). They are distinct nations with distinct languages (Haida is a language isolate; Tlingit is Na-Dené), distinct territories, and distinct clan structures, but they share a cultural world — the Northwest Coast tradition of salmon, red cedar, potlatch, and the formline art style that is one of the most distinctive visual traditions in human history. They are treated together here because they are the two most prominent examples of a cultural tradition that also includes the Tsimshian, Kwakwaka'wakw, Nuu-chah-nulth, and others — a tradition built on the meeting of the Pacific Ocean, the temperate rainforest, and an abundance of salmon so great that it freed the people who managed it from the obligation of agriculture.

The potlatch — a word from Chinook jargon meaning "to give" — was the institution that organized the Northwest Coast world. A chief who hosted a potlatch gave away enormous quantities of goods to the assembled guests — blankets, copper shields, canoes, food — and in giving demonstrated his wealth, his lineage's prestige, and his right to the names, crests, and ceremonial privileges that were the most important things in the Northwest Coast world. The guests, by accepting the gifts, witnessed the host's claims and acknowledged their legitimacy. Law was made visible through generosity. The potlatch was simultaneously a feast, a ceremony, a legal proceeding, and a performance — the chief's wealth and status enacted in public, witnessed by those whose acknowledgment gave it legal force.

Key places:
- **Haida Gwaii** ("islands of the people") — the archipelago 80 km off the British Columbia coast; the Haida homeland; abundant salmon, halibut, and marine mammals; the forest of red cedar that provided the raw material for everything the Haida built
- **Sitka (Sheet'ká)** — major Tlingit center in southeast Alaska; the site of the 1802 attack by Tlingit warriors on the Russian American Company's fort and the 1804 Russian reconquest
- **Ketchikan** — southern Alaska Tlingit territory; famous for the concentration of surviving totem poles
- **SGang Gwaay (Anthony Island)** — the abandoned Haida village on the southern end of Haida Gwaii; its standing mortuary totem poles are one of the most photographed sites in indigenous North America; now a UNESCO World Heritage Site

## What Defined Them

**The potlatch as visible law.** The potlatch was the Northwest Coast's legal system, its archive, and its theatrical tradition — all in one ceremony. When a chief hosted a potlatch to claim a name, a crest, or a privilege inherited from a deceased relative, the gifts he distributed to the witnesses were not charity. They were payment for witnessing: the guests who accepted the gifts acknowledged the legitimacy of the claim, and their acceptance was the legal record. The larger the potlatch — the more guests, the more gifts — the more authoritative the claim. A claim disputed at a potlatch required a counter-potlatch to refute. The law was not written; it was performed, witnessed, and paid for in blankets and copper.

**The totem pole as genealogical archive.** The carved cedar poles — "totem poles" in the colonial term — were not objects of worship. They were genealogical records, crests made permanent in cedar: the story of a lineage's spiritual encounters, inherited rights, and ancestral connections carved in a sequence of figures that trained community members could read as fluently as written text. The pole carved to honor a deceased chief recorded his lineage, his spiritual helpers, and the key events of his family's history. To destroy a pole was not sacrilege; it was the destruction of a legal record. The Canadian government understood this when it confiscated poles as part of the potlatch prohibition.

**The formline art tradition.** The visual tradition of the Northwest Coast — the formline style, with its characteristic ovoids, U-forms, and split U-forms organized around the depiction of animal and supernatural figures — is one of the most technically sophisticated and visually distinctive art traditions in human history. The rules of formline composition are intricate and the training required to master them is substantial; a formline carver is not simply making a picture but working within a highly structured visual grammar. What appears to the untrained eye as decoration is a dense semantic system: every part of every figure carries meaning — the ovoids that represent joints and eye forms, the form lines that define the primary figure's anatomy, the secondary design elements that fill the spaces between.

**Salmon and cedar as the material foundation.** The Northwest Coast tradition was built on two resources: salmon (which provided the food surplus that freed communities from subsistence agriculture and supported the specialized labor that produced the art and ceremony) and red cedar (which provided the raw material for everything — houses, canoes, tools, containers, clothing, and the totem poles themselves). The relationship with both resources was managed carefully: ceremonies that honored the first salmon of the season, restrictions on harvest methods that maintained the salmon run's sustainability, the selection of specific cedar trees for specific purposes. The abundance was real, but it required management to remain abundance.

**The clan system and the two moieties.** Both Haida and Tlingit social organization was based on matrilineal clans divided into two moieties (Haida: Eagle and Raven; Tlingit: Raven and Eagle/Wolf). Marriage was required to be between moieties — you married someone from the opposite moiety, and your children belonged to your moiety. The moiety system meant that every community was a balanced pairing of two complementary groups, each with its own crests, its own ceremonial roles, and its own obligations to the other. The potlatch was typically given by one moiety to the other — the hosts were from one moiety, the guests and witnesses from the other. The social structure and the ceremonial structure were the same structure.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Cedar and Salmon World:** The master carver is adzing the face of the mortuary pole for the deceased chief. The pole will stand in front of the chief's house and record his lineage's history in a visual language that every adult in the village can read. The chief's children, of the opposite moiety, will witness the raising at the potlatch. The village will eat for four days. The claim will be law.

**2 — The Russian Encounter:** The 1802 Tlingit attack on the Russian fort at Sitka is organized by the Kiks.ádi clan chief and conducted with military precision. The Russian-American Company's fort is destroyed and its garrison killed or captured. The Russians will return in 1804 with a warship. The Tlingit will abandon the Sitka site under naval bombardment. The independence that was maintained for 200 more years has taken its first serious blow.

**3 — The British and American Encounter:** The Haida are expert maritime traders. Their canoes range from Haida Gwaii to Vancouver Island to the Columbia River and back, carrying goods and information across the entire Northwest Coast. They are also dying. The smallpox that travels the same routes as the trade goods has reduced the Haida population by perhaps 90% in three generations. The poles at SGang Gwaay will stand empty because there are not enough people left to live there.

**4 — The Potlatch Prohibition:** The Canadian Indian Act amendment of 1885 makes the potlatch illegal. A Kwakwaka'wakw chief who continues to hold a potlatch is arrested. The ceremonial objects are confiscated and sent to Ottawa and to museums in Europe. The potlatch continues underground, adapted to avoid legal notice. The knowledge is maintained in the people who carry it. It cannot be confiscated.

**5 — The Totem Pole Renaissance:** The prohibition is lifted in 1951. In the decades that follow, the totem pole tradition is revived — not as a museum curiosity but as a living practice, with master carvers teaching apprentices, poles raised at ceremonies attended by thousands, and the formline art tradition finding new media (silkscreen prints, jewelry) that carry the visual grammar into the contemporary world. In 2004, the Canadian Supreme Court rules that the Haida must be consulted about decisions affecting their territory. The law of the potlatch has been translated into constitutional law.
