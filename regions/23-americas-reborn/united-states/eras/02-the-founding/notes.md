# Notes — Production Bible
## United States, Era 2: The Founding — The Experiment Begins (1776–1800)

---

## 1. Sources

### Tier 1 — Primary
- The Declaration of Independence (July 4, 1776) — Jefferson's draft and the final version; key sentences in their exact language
- The Federalist Papers (Hamilton, Madison, Jay, 1787–1788) — especially No. 10 (Madison on factions), No. 51 (Madison on checks and balances), No. 84 (Hamilton against a Bill of Rights), No. 1 (Hamilton on the stakes)
- The Constitution of the United States (1787) — Article I Section 2 (Three-Fifths), Article I Section 9 (slave trade protection), Article VI (supremacy clause)
- James Madison's Convention Notes (written 1787, published posthumously 1840) — the most complete record; Madison's own language about "moral and political depravity"
- The Northwest Ordinance (1787) — the Congress that existed simultaneously prohibited slavery north of the Ohio River while the Convention protected it in the south
- Thomas Jefferson's letters to Madison from Paris, 1787–1789 — on the Bill of Rights, on Shays' Rebellion
- Washington's First Inaugural (1789) — the tone of republican virtue performed
- Hamilton's "Report on Manufactures" (1791) and "Report on a National Bank" (1790) — the economic vision
- Jefferson's Kentucky Resolutions (1798) — states' rights as constitutional doctrine
- Alien and Sedition Acts (1798) — the republic's first self-censorship crisis

### Tier 1 — African American primary voices
- Richard Allen's autobiography — Allen purchased his freedom, was in Philadelphia during the Convention, founded the Free African Society (1787, same year as Convention) and later the African Methodist Episcopal Church (1816)
- Absalom Jones — co-founder of the Free African Society; his 1799 petition to Congress against the slave trade and for freedom of enslaved people brought from abroad
- Prince Hall — Boston; founded the first Black Masonic lodge in America; petitioned the Massachusetts legislature for the abolition of slavery (1777) and for education for Black children (1787)
- Benjamin Banneker's letter to Jefferson (1791) — the free Black Maryland scientist and surveyor who helped design Washington D.C. wrote to Jefferson pointing out the contradiction between his stated principles and his practice; Jefferson's reply

### Tier 2 — Academic
- Gordon Wood, *The Creation of the American Republic, 1776–1787* (1969) — the political thought
- Edmund Morgan, *American Slavery, American Freedom* (1975) — the Virginia paradox; indispensable structural argument
- Eric Foner, *Tom Paine and Revolutionary America* (1976) — the radical democratic strand
- Joseph Ellis, *Founding Brothers* (2000) — the personal and political dynamics among the founders
- David Brion Davis, *The Problem of Slavery in the Age of Revolution* (1975) — the intellectual context
- Annette Gordon-Reed, *The Hemingses of Monticello* (2008) — Jefferson and Sally Hemings; winner of Pulitzer and National Book Award; rigorous scholarship on the most specific embodiment of the paradox
- Gary Nash, *The Unknown American Revolution* (2005) — the founding from below: Black, poor white, and indigenous perspectives
- Pauline Maier, *Ratification* (2010) — the state conventions; what ratification actually looked like in practice

## 2. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The founding paradox as generator of perpetual argument, not as contradiction to be resolved.

**Mechanism:** The Constitution was designed as a machine for managing disagreement, not for resolving it. The founders believed faction was inevitable (Federalist No. 10) and built a system that channeled faction rather than eliminating it. The Three-Fifths Compromise was not a mistake in this machine; it was the machine's initial operating condition. The founding paradox (all men created equal + slavery protected) is not a flaw in the engine — it IS the engine. Every major political conflict in American history from 1787 to the present is, in some form, a negotiation over the terms of this initial settlement.

**Terminal condition (for this era, 1776–1800):** Legitimacy gap. The republic's founding documents generated claims (equality, liberty, consent of the governed) that its founding practices denied (slavery, exclusion of women, dispossession of indigenous peoples). The gap between the claim and the practice was not merely a moral failure; it was a structural instability that the republic's own mechanism — regular elections, constitutional amendment, judicial review — could not contain without periodic crisis. By 1800, the first presidential election crisis (the tie between Jefferson and Burr, resolved in the House) had demonstrated that the constitutional mechanism was more fragile than its architects had supposed. The argument that would eventually require a civil war to adjudicate was already fully visible.

**Material object engine:** The document itself. The Constitution is not metaphorically central; it is literally central to American political life in a way that has no parallel in other democracies. Arguments about what it means are not arguments about history; they are arguments about current authority. The document's physical existence — held at the National Archives, displayed under argon gas, requiring formal petition to reproduce — is itself a form of civic religion. This object is the chapter's material anchor.

## 3. Key Figures

### Madison, James (1751–1836)
The Constitution's primary architect. Thirty-six at the Convention. Had been studying the failures of ancient and modern republics for two years before arriving in Philadelphia — he called this preparation his "literary cargo." Came with the Virginia Plan, the most comprehensive proposal. Wrote the most complete notes of the Convention proceedings. Co-wrote the Federalist Papers with Hamilton and Jay. Served as Jefferson's Secretary of State. Became the fourth president. Owned enslaved people throughout his life; freed none of them in his will (unlike Washington, who freed his). His private letters about slavery are often anguished; his public actions protected the institution consistently. He is the chapter's primary figure because he contains the paradox most completely: the republic's most systematic thinker about liberty was also one of its most determined practitioners of bondage.

### Jefferson, Thomas (1743–1826)
Author of the Declaration. In Paris during the Convention. Writing letters to Madison from the Hôtel de Langeost about the need for a Bill of Rights, about the dangers of too much government power, about the beauties of the French countryside. Simultaneously: enslaved Sally Hemings, who was fourteen when she accompanied his daughter to Paris; their relationship (which modern DNA evidence confirms produced children) was known to Jefferson's contemporaries and was a subject of newspaper attack during his presidency. Jefferson is the founding paradox made flesh: the man who wrote "all men are created equal" owned 175 human beings and fathered children by one of them who never received their freedom from him during his lifetime. He cannot be reduced to either his words or his practice. He is both.

### Hamilton, Alexander (c.1755–1804)
The Constitution's most brilliant defender and its most energetic implementer. Born in the Caribbean, illegitimate, self-made. Saw the Constitution as a framework for building a powerful commercial republic. As the first Secretary of the Treasury, created the national bank, assumed the states' war debts, established the credit of the United States. His vision of America as an industrial and commercial power competed directly with Jefferson's vision of an agrarian republic. The conflict between Hamilton and Jefferson is not merely a policy dispute; it is the first instance of the permanent American argument about what the republic is for. Hamilton is the chapter's counterpoint to Madison: where Madison designed the machine, Hamilton operated it and revealed its contradictions in different ways.

### Washington, George (1732–1799)
The indispensable man, as his biographers call him. Presided over the Convention without speaking much, which gave the proceedings a legitimacy they would not otherwise have had. His agreement to serve as the first president made the executive power credible rather than threatening. In his Farewell Address (1796) warned against "the baneful effects of the spirit of party" — and then watched as parties formed anyway. Washington was a slaveholder who grew more conflicted about slavery as he aged; he arranged, uniquely among the major founders, for his enslaved people to be freed after his wife's death, which Martha Washington did not wait for, freeing them in 1800 because she feared they were calculating when she would die. This detail belongs in the chapter.

### Richard Allen (1760–1831)
Born enslaved in Philadelphia, sold with his family to a Delaware plantation owner, converted to Methodism, purchased his own freedom at twenty years old, returned to Philadelphia. In 1787 — the same year as the Constitutional Convention — he and Absalom Jones founded the Free African Society, the first mutual aid organization in America run by and for Black people. When the Free African Society's members were removed from the gallery of St. George's Methodist Episcopal Church during prayer — pulled from their knees in 1792 — Allen and Jones organized the Bethel African Methodist Episcopal Church, which became the founding congregation of the first independent Black denomination in American history. Allen is the founding paradox from the other side: he is in Philadelphia in 1787, building a parallel civil society under the same sky as the Convention, asserting the same principles of self-governance that Madison was writing into the Constitution, for a population the Constitution was not yet prepared to fully include.

### Sally Hemings (c.1773–1835)
Enslaved at Monticello. Half-sister to Jefferson's late wife Martha Wayles Jefferson (their shared father was John Wayles). Accompanied Jefferson's daughter to Paris in 1787 at age fourteen; was legally free in France, where slavery was not recognized. Chose to return to Virginia with Jefferson in 1789, reportedly after he promised to free their future children, which he eventually did for four of them. She is not a peripheral figure in the founding; she is the most precise embodiment of the founding paradox: the author of the Declaration of Independence, the man who wrote that all men are created equal and are endowed with unalienable rights, held her enslaved. What she thought about this we do not know, because no record of her voice survives. The chapter must honor that silence.

## 4. Narrative Arc — Section Plan

### Section I: The Room (opens with opening.md material)
1787. Philadelphia. The Convention. Madison writing. The windows sealed. The enslaved people serving. The Three-Fifths Compromise being drafted. The founding paradox stated at the level of fact, not argument.

### Section II: The Argument Before the Argument
The period 1776–1787: what the Declaration said, what the Articles of Confederation failed to do, what Shays' Rebellion revealed. The question of whether the revolution was radical (Tom Paine's version: a new beginning for humanity) or conservative (the propertied class's version: security of contract and credit). Both versions were present from the start.

### Section III: The Machine
The Constitution's design: federalism, separation of powers, checks and balances, the Bill of Rights added not from conviction but from political necessity (the Anti-Federalists would not ratify without it). The mechanism described from inside — what its designers thought they were building and why. The Federalist Papers as the most sophisticated defense of republican government ever written, produced in nine months by three men in the middle of a political campaign.

### Section IV: The Other Philadelphia
Richard Allen and Absalom Jones. The Free African Society founded in 1787. The parallel republic: free Black Philadelphians building institutions — churches, schools, mutual aid — under the same sky as the Convention, asserting the same principles, for a population the Convention had not fully included. This section is the chapter's counter-voice. It does not moralize at the founders. It shows what the founding principles looked like when applied consistently.

### Section V: The Argument Begins
The first decade under the Constitution: Hamilton's economic program and Jefferson's resistance; the formation of parties that Washington warned against; the Alien and Sedition Acts (1798) and the first test of whether the republic would suppress political dissent; the election of 1800 and the peaceful transfer of power, which was not obvious in advance. Jefferson's victory as the first test of the machine: does the republic actually change hands through elections? It does. This is not nothing.

### Section VI: What Was Built, What Was Left
The republic by 1800: functioning, fragile, arguing. The founding paradox intact. Benjamin Banneker's letter to Jefferson (1791) — the free Black man who helped design Washington D.C. writing to the Secretary of State to point out the contradiction between his stated principles and his practice. Jefferson's reply: complimentary but noncommittal. The argument in miniature. The chapter closes where it began: the document in the room. The argument that was built into it. The people it did not yet include. What would come next.

## 5. Voice Plan

**Register:** Legal-Public (Forum-Civic), modulated for the founding-paradox hybrid.

The Legal-Public register confirmed for Roman chapters applies here with adjustment: where Rome's legal-public voice is shaped by the Forum's acoustics and the weight of mos maiorum (ancestral custom), the American legal-public voice is shaped by the constitutional convention room, the ratification debates, and the principle of written supremacy. The prose should close like verdicts. The pause mechanism is the period, the colon (for constitutional quotation and enumeration), the semicolon (for balanced clauses in the Hamilton-Madison argumentative mode).

The "founding-paradox hybrid" adjustment: the voice must carry both the republic's founding principles and the founding contradictions in the same register — not alternating between a "freedom" voice and a "slavery" voice, but holding both in the same declarative sentence. "Jefferson wrote X while enslaving Y" is the model sentence: no register shift, no tonal rupture, the paradox stated in a single legal-declarative voice.

Zero em-dashes. The register finds its own pause mechanism.

## 6. Panel Pressure Points (from PANEL-1)

**Tuchman:** The middle passage of the opening (Articles to Constitutional design) is summary rather than scene. The chapter needs to anchor the Constitutional mechanism in a specific moment — a debate, a day, a particular exchange — not just describe what emerged from it. Also: commit to Madison as the primary narrative figure or find the ensemble that will carry the full arc.

**Ibn Khaldun:** The factions need to be visible as social groups with distinct interests, not just positions in a debate. The chapter must explain why the mechanism broke down when it did (i.e., in 1861), not just that it broke down.

**Achebe:** The enslaved people cannot remain unnamed through the chapter. Richard Allen is the answer. Sally Hemings is the answer. The chapter must honor the founding paradox from both sides — not as corrective, but as completeness. Also: stay inside 1787's uncertainty; do not read the founding through the lens of what we know about 1861.

**Davis:** The chapter should show the American founding as a cosmological system: a civic religion with sacred texts, a priesthood of interpretation, a founding myth that is simultaneously a wound. The anxiety of the founders — not just their calculations — is part of the worldview.

**Kapuściński:** Give more physical Philadelphia. The heat. The sealed windows. The smell of the city outside. The dual world: the Convention inside, the Free African Society meeting in a church three blocks away. Both communities in the same city on the same days. The contrast should be felt, not stated.

## 7. Open Questions

1. Sally Hemings's perspective: the chapter must acknowledge the absence of her voice while honoring her presence. The model is the Abu Bakr II technique (open silence): "She did not leave a record. The record left a gap that is itself evidence."

2. The Northwest Ordinance (1787): the Congress under the Articles, simultaneously with the Convention, passed legislation prohibiting slavery north of the Ohio River. This is not a contradiction of the Three-Fifths Compromise; it is the same logic — manage, contain, defer. The chapter should show how both acts were products of the same political calculation.

3. Banneker and Jefferson: the specific epistolary exchange (1791) is one of the most precise embodiments of the founding paradox available. It should be in the chapter.

4. The Bill of Rights: added not from conviction about rights but from political necessity — the Anti-Federalists would not ratify otherwise. Hamilton argued against it (Federalist No. 84: a Bill of Rights is unnecessary and potentially dangerous because it implies the government has powers it doesn't). Madison, who had been against it, wrote it anyway to secure ratification in Virginia. The Bill of Rights is thus itself a product of the founding bargain — written by a man who didn't believe it was necessary to secure ratification from people who did.

5. The peaceful transfer of power in 1801: Jefferson's inauguration after the election of 1800 is one of the period's most important events and belongs in the chapter's close. The republic actually worked — the losing side actually ceded power. This was not inevitable and is not small.
