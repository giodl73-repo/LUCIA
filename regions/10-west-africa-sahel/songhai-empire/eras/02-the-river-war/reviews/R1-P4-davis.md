# R1-P4 — Wade Davis — Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Songhai Empire / Era 2 — The River War
**Round:** 1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

The opening does something important: it presents a river navy as a genuine military innovation without comparing it to anything. It does not say "the only navy in sub-Saharan Africa" as if African naval absence were the norm and this the exception. It says Sunni Ali built something no other ruler in the western Sudan had built, and it describes what it did and why it mattered. This is the right approach.

**What works:**

The river as a knowledge system is implicit. The paddlers knew the river's moods. The fleet moved at the speed of the current. The inland delta was a labyrinth of channels. This is the beginning of treating the Niger as something that required deep knowledge to navigate, not just as a flat highway. The Songhai who crewed these canoes were specialists, men whose relationship with the water was as deep and as learned as the Sanhaja Berbers' relationship with the desert. The comparison to the Sanhaja desert knowledge is well-placed because it is a comparison of parallel expertise, not a hierarchy.

The technological dimension is present: war canoes, standing fleet, professional crews. This is a genuine military technology, and the opening treats it as such without qualification.

**What I would push on:**

The river knowledge system deserves more depth. The opening mentions that the navy "understood the river's moods" but does not show what that knowledge looked like in practice. How did a war-canoe crew read the current? How did they navigate the inland delta's labyrinth of channels? What did the river look like during the flood season versus the dry season, and how did the navy adapt? The Polynesian navigator reads the swell. The San tracker reads the ground. The Songhai paddler reads the river. Each is a knowledge system of equal sophistication. The chapter must show the river-reading at the same depth the Tongan chapter showed swell-reading.

The material culture of the canoes is absent. What were they made of? How were they built? How large were they? Thirty men is specified, but the canoe itself remains abstract. A war canoe of the Niger is as specific an artifact as a birch-bark canoe of the Great Lakes or a Polynesian voyaging vessel. It deserves the same material specificity.

**The question I'd push on:** The river navy is framed as a military tool. Was it also a knowledge tradition? Were there families or clans whose specialty was river warfare? Was the craft of canoe-building a specialized discipline? The chapter should treat the navy not just as an instrument of Sunni Ali's ambition but as a knowledge system in its own right.
