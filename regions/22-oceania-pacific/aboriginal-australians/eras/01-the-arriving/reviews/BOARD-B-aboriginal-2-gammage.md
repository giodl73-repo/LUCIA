# B-aboriginal-2 — Bill Gammage — Board Review of "When the Ancestors Walked"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Fire-stick farming, colonial-era landscape descriptions, Aboriginal environmental management at continental scale.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The fire section accurately describes cool-season burning of spinifex, the spacing of ignitions to control fire fronts, and the mosaic outcome (patches at every stage of regrowth). This is consistent with documented practice.
- The mulga seed timing detail (three years to seed, needs undisturbed ground) is accurate and represents the kind of species-specific knowledge that fire managers carried.
- The observation that the post-burn landscape attracted wallabies is accurate. Fire-attracted game is one of the primary outcomes of mosaic burning.
- The European observers' response (open woodland, park-like landscapes) is well-documented in colonial journals. The chapter correctly identifies this as the product of management, not nature.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter presents fire management as part of the Dreaming's obligations ("Fire was part of the Dreaming's instructions for how the world must be kept alive"). This is consistent with how Aboriginal fire managers describe their practice: burning is caring for country, not merely a technique.
- Ngali as a female fire manager is appropriate. Women's burning is underrepresented in the literature but is well-attested in practice.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The mosaic burning description is the core of my thesis, and the chapter renders it accurately: different patches burned at different times to create a diversity of regrowth stages, the whole landscape shaped into a managed system.
- The link between burning and the Songline system (burning obligations tied to custodianship) is correct and important. It shows fire management as part of the integrated knowledge system, not a standalone technique.
- The observation that the landscape Europeans saw was the PRODUCT of management, not the absence of it. This is the essential insight.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter says Ngali burns spinifex and the fire-dependent plants include "solanum bushes that fruited only after fire" and "native tobacco plants emerging from ash-enriched soil." Both are plausible in Western Desert country but should be noted as illustrative rather than site-specific, since the chapter is using composite figures. The specific plant responses to fire vary significantly by region.
- The chapter could note that fire management was not uniform across the continent. Coastal nations, rainforest nations, and desert nations all used fire differently. The burning described (spinifex, Western Desert) is one variant of a continent-wide practice that took many forms.
- The claim "the entire visible landscape was their work" is defensible for many regions but should be slightly qualified. Some landscapes (deep rainforest, high alpine) were less intensively managed by fire, though even these were influenced by burning regimes on their margins.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (note regional variation; qualify the "entire landscape" claim)
