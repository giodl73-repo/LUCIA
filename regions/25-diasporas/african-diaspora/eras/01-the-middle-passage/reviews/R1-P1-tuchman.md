## P-1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of The Middle Passage (Opening)

**Overall:** This is one of the strongest openings in the manuscript. The first line earns the second. The last line earns a chapter.

**What works:**
- The opening gambit is superb: "He had a name. The ship's log would not record it." Two sentences, and the entire moral architecture of the chapter is established. The tension between the person and the system that refuses to see the person. I would read on from this line alone.
- The scene anchoring is precise: Ouidah, 1727, the *São João Baptista*, forty iron bars. These are not decorative specifics. They are the evidence that this is a real moment in time, not a generalized portrait of suffering.
- The structural surprise of the hold scene. The reader expects horror. The horror is there (seven hundred square feet, two hundred and twelve bodies, the smell of flux). But then someone is singing. The pivot from the physical conditions to the singing is the kind of narrative move that makes a reader sit up. The singing is the argument, not the suffering.
- The final section moves from the specific (one man, one ship) to the general (the twelve million) without losing the thread. The list of cultural survivals (Candomblé, Vodou, the ring shout, the blue notes) is earned by the preceding argument that what survived was what was carried in the body.

**What doesn't:**
- The middle section, where the Yoruba cosmological system is laid out (Shango, the bata drums, the Ifa system, the dead in compound soil), is beautifully done but runs slightly long for an opening. In the chapter, this depth will be essential. In the opening, consider whether three of these four could do the work of four. The reader needs to feel the weight of what he carried, not catalogue it.
- The phrase "purchasing a library" is clever, perhaps too clever. It risks stepping outside the man's own experience to make a point for the reader. The man from Oyo did not think of himself as a library. He thought of himself as a devotee of Shango who had been taken from his compound. The metaphor works from the outside but wobbles from the inside.

**The question I'd push on:**
The chapter must sustain this for 5,000 words. The opening achieves its pull through contrast (ledger vs. living person, hold vs. song, shackle vs. rhythm). Can that contrast sustain a full chapter, or will the chapter need to find additional structural principles? I would want the notes to address this directly.
