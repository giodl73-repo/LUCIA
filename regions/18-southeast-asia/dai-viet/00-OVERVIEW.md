# Đại Việt — Độc Lập — Independence

We are Đại Việt — Great Viet — and our defining act is the assertion of independence from China. We were a Chinese province (Jiaozhi) for a thousand years — 111 BCE to 939 CE — and when Ngô Quyền won the battle of the Bạch Đằng River in 938 CE and declared an end to Chinese rule, we did not simply exchange one administration for another. We began the construction of a Vietnamese civilization that would maintain its independence against every subsequent attempt at reincorporation — including three Mongol invasions (all repelled), the brief Ming reconquest (1407–1427, expelled by Lê Lợi), and the sustained pressure of subsequent dynasties — while building examination systems, Confucian courts, Buddhist monasteries, and literary traditions in the very forms that China had developed, and declaring in those same forms that Vietnam was a civilization in its own right, equal to and distinct from the civilization to its north.

The Proclamation of the Bình Ngô Đại Cáo (1428) — written by Nguyễn Trãi after the expulsion of the Ming — is the founding document of Vietnamese nationhood. It says, in terms borrowed from Chinese rhetorical tradition: "Our country of Đại Việt, from ancient times has had clear cultural distinction / The mountains and rivers of our territory are separate and distinct / The civilization and customs of the North and South are different." This is the paradox of Đại Việt: a civilization that asserted its independence in Chinese literary forms and was none the less independent for that.

## What Defined Them

**Độc lập — independence as identity.** Vietnamese identity was forged in the crucible of resistance to Chinese absorption. The thousand-year experience of Chinese rule did not produce assimilation; it produced a Vietnamese self-consciousness that expressed itself in precisely the terms the Chinese tradition provided — Confucian courts, examination systems, Buddhist monasteries — while insisting on its irreducible distinctness. The Đại Cáo of 1428 is the fullest expression of this paradox: a document written in classical Chinese literary forms asserting that Vietnam is not China.

**The three Bạch Đằng River victories.** Three times in Vietnamese history, the invading fleet was destroyed at the Bạch Đằng River (in 938, 1288, and in related engagements): Ngô Quyền's victory over the Chinese Southern Han (938), Trần Hưng Đạo's victory over the Mongol fleet (1288). The tactic in 1288 — iron-tipped stakes planted in the river bottom at low tide, allowing the Vietnamese fleet to lure the Mongol fleet upriver and then cut off its retreat at ebb tide — is one of the most brilliantly executed ambushes in military history. The river is the national symbol of Vietnamese military genius.

**Nam tiến — the southern advance.** Over eight centuries, Đại Việt expanded southward, absorbing the Cham territories gradually and then the Khmer-held Mekong Delta. This was not simply conquest; it was demographic expansion, as Vietnamese farming communities moved south along the coastal plains, establishing villages that eventually outnumbered the Cham population. The southward advance was driven as much by population pressure and the search for new rice paddies as by state military policy.

**The Trịnh-Nguyễn division.** For 170 years (1627–1786), Vietnam was divided between the Trịnh lords governing the north in the name of the Lê kings and the Nguyễn lords governing the south in their own right. This was not two different countries but one country governed by two rival houses, each acknowledging the nominal Lê sovereignty. The practical division — the Trịnh Wall separating north from south — anticipated the twentieth-century division in its geography. The Tây Sơn rebellion that ended both houses, and the Nguyễn reunification under Gia Long in 1802, created the unified Vietnam that the French then colonized.

**Truyện Kiều and the vernacular tradition.** Nguyễn Du's *Truyện Kiều* (The Tale of Kiều, 1820) — a verse novel in chữ Nôm (the Vietnamese script based on Chinese characters) — is the supreme work of Vietnamese literature and the text every Vietnamese child still memorizes. Its 3,254 verses tell the story of a talented young woman who sacrifices herself for her family and endures fifteen years of suffering before reunion with her love. It is a story about beauty, talent, and the injustice of a world that destroys both; it is also the definitive statement that Vietnamese is a literary language capable of expressing everything that classical Chinese could express.

## The Chapters

**1 — Bạch Đằng, 938 CE:** Ngô Quyền stands at the river at low tide. He can see the tips of the iron stakes. The Southern Han fleet is coming upriver. He waits. The tide turns. He gives the signal. The independence of Vietnam begins in this moment.

**2 — Thăng Long Founded:** Lý Thái Tổ is moving the capital to the new site on the Red River. He names it "the city of the ascending dragon" — because when he arrived, he saw a dragon ascending from the river. He is building not just a city but a permanent statement: here is the center of Vietnam.

**3 — Trần Hưng Đạo:** The Mongol khan has demanded submission. Trần Hưng Đạo's response, recorded in the Hịch tướng sĩ, is addressed to his officers: I eat not in the day and cannot sleep at night, my heart aches and my tears fall, enraged that I cannot yet tear the enemy to pieces. He will win. He has prepared everything.

**4 — Lê Lợi's Liberation:** The Ming occupation has lasted twenty years. Lê Lợi's forces control the countryside. He enters Thăng Long. Nguyễn Trãi writes the Proclamation. The mountains and rivers of our country are separate and distinct. The civilization of the North and South are different. We are Vietnam.

**5 — Truyện Kiều:** Nguyễn Du is writing the final lines. He has spent years on this poem. He is writing in chữ Nôm — Vietnamese script — not in classical Chinese. He is writing the greatest work of Vietnamese literature in the language that Confucian scholars considered inferior. The poem will outlast the dynasty that dismissed it.
