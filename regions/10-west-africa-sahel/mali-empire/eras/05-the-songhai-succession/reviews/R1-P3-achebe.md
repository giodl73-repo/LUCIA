## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of "The Songhai Succession" Opening

**Overall:** This opening passes the Achebe Test more cleanly than any chapter I have reviewed. The No Verdicts discipline is severe and sustained. The Songhai are given their own gravity. The Manden's contraction is presented from inside the covenant's logic without mourning or judgment. I have no fundamental objections.

**What works:**
- "This was not a fall. The jelilu would not use the word." This is the chapter's most important sentence. It does not just avoid the verdict; it names the avoidance and attributes it to the culture's own voice. The jelilu are the authority on how to describe what happened to the Manden. If they do not use the word "fall," neither does the chapter. This is inside voice at its most disciplined.
- "At Gao, seven hundred miles east along the Niger, a different song was being composed." The metaphor is exact: the Songhai have their own song. They are not a disruption in Mali's song; they are a parallel composition. The word "composed" respects the Songhai's agency without aggrandizing it.
- "The jelilu in Niani could not sing the Songhai's genealogy. That was not their line." This is a perfect structural observation rendered in the culture's own terms. The jeli's commission is specific: they carry the names they are authorized to carry. What is outside their commission is not erased; it is simply another lineage's responsibility.
- The jon, the horon, and the nyamakalaw appear as living social categories, not anthropological labels. No glossing, no apologetics.

**What doesn't:**
- Nothing substantive. The No Verdicts discipline holds throughout. I would note only that the phrase "the materials were running out" at the end of the structural passage is very close to a verdict — it implies a material determinism that, if not carefully handled in the full chapter, could shade into retrodiction. The gold did not "run out" in a simple sense; the routes shifted, the competition increased, the monopoly frayed. The full chapter should trace the specific mechanisms rather than implying simple exhaustion.

**The question I'd push on:**
The Songhai's own oral historians, the jesere, are not mentioned. The chapter notes that the jelilu could not sing the Songhai genealogy. But who could? The full chapter should acknowledge, even briefly, that the Songhai had their own tradition of historical memory, and that what we lack is not the memory itself but the written record of it. Silence about the jesere risks implying that the Mande jeli tradition was the only oral-historical institution in the region.
