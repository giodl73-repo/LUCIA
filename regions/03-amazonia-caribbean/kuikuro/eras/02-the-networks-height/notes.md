Tier 1 (archaeological primary).

- Franchetto, Bruna, and Michael Heckenberger, eds. *Os Povos do Alto Xingu: História e Cultura.* UFRJ, 2001. — Edited volume on upper Xingu peoples; Kuikuro contributors. Tier 2.

**Ethnographic and Oral**
- Carneiro, Robert L. "The Kuikuru: A Tropical Forest Village." In *Peoples of the Amazon.* Tier 2.
- Mtraux, Alfred, and Paul Fejos. *Tribes of the Middle and Upper Amazon.* — On ceremony and exchange systems in Amazonian network societies. Tier 3.
- Viveiros de Castro, Eduardo. *From the Enemy's Point of View: Humanity and Divinity in an Amazonian Society.* University of Chicago Press, 1992. — Comparative Amazonian cosmology. Tier 2.
- Gregor, Thomas. *Mehinaku: The Drama of Daily Life in a Brazilian Indian Village.* University of Chicago Press, 1977. — Detailed ethnography of a neighboring Xingu people; plaza life, ceremonial exchange, wrestling. Tier 2.

**Source situation:** The source base is asymmetric in the way that all pre-contact Amazonian scholarship is asymmetric. The archaeological record (Heckenberger's work) is authoritative on material form — town size, road alignment, plaza dimensions, terra preta distribution. The ethnographic record (Gregor, Basso, Carneiro, Franchetto) documents the ceremony, social structure, and knowledge systems of living Xingu peoples, who are the descendants of the era under study. The two sources are complementary but separate: the archaeology sees the bones, the ethnography sees the living body. The chapter should use both — the road's geometry from Heckenberger, the Quarup's structure from ethnography — and acknowledge the asymmetry where the reader needs to feel it.

## 2. Key Figures

**Iauatá** — the road crew leader introduced in the opening. A working figure — not a chief, not a ceremonial specialist, not a notable. Composite, drawn from the social role of the adult Kuikuro man responsible for community maintenance work. His interiority (thinking about the egitsü while working on the road) is the chapter's narrative entry point. He will carry the reader through the arc.

**The anetü** — the hereditary chief of Kuhikugu, the host of this Quarup. Unnamed — the anetü's identity is appropriate to call from the historical/archaeological record, but the record does not preserve individual names at this period. The chapter will follow the rubric's v1.4 amendment: the figure needs a body, not a name. The anetü is characterized through function: the feast-giving, the gift exchange, the public mourning, the obligations of generosity that constitute chiefly legitimacy.

**The song-leader (hüge)** — the specialist in Quarup mourning songs. A named functional role in Kuikuro ceremonial life. The hüge knows the specific songs for the deceased — songs that must be correctly performed for the egitsü to accomplish its work of closing grief. The hüge is a knowledge-at-method-level figure: show the songs being prepared, the specific knowledge of which songs belong to which deceased, what it means to perform them correctly.

**The mourned chief** — the dead man for whom the Quarup is being held. Iauatá's mother's brother's father-in-law (the complex lineage connection from the opening). He represents the network's loss — a major anetü whose death has created a gap in the exchange relationships that his position sustained.

**The terra preta gardener** — an unnamed figure (female, likely — manioc processing is a women's domain in Kuikuro life) who carries the knowledge of the enriched garden soils. Body-without-name, per the archaeological figure model.

## 3. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The Quarup as network-maintenance technology.

**Mechanism:** The upper Xingu network has no overarching political authority — no paramount chief, no unified governance. What holds it together is the cycle of ceremonial obligation: communities host Quarup ceremonies for their dead, and all network communities are obligated to attend. Attendance requires maintained roads. Maintained roads are both infrastructure and announcement. The gift exchange between anetü at the Quarup creates and renews economic relationships. The wrestling matches create and renew the status hierarchies that determine who owes what to whom. The mourning songs performed correctly complete the grief cycle and restore the deceased's relationships to the living. The Quarup is not a ritual that accompanies the network's life. It IS the network's life — the mechanism that transforms autonomous forest towns into a functioning social system.

**Mechanism operating at method level:** Show the gift exchange in motion — the anetü receiving the visiting anetü at the Kuhikugu plaza, the specific goods exchanged (feathers, manioc flour, pottery, woven goods), the obligations incurred by the exchange. Show the hüge preparing the mourning songs — what knowledge does the song-leader carry, what are the consequences of performing incorrectly? Show the terra preta garden — what does the gardener know about the soil that makes the manioc yield sustain the Quarup feast?

**Terminal condition:** The Quarup system is optimized for a world in which all communities maintain roughly equivalent populations. The exchange system requires roughly equivalent capacity to give and to receive. When epidemic disease kills 80–90% of the population in some communities faster than others, the system's balance is destroyed. A community that has lost most of its working-age population cannot maintain its road, cannot host a Quarup, cannot send wrestlers to the matches. The obligations remain, but the capacity to fulfill them has been destroyed. The network does not collapse at once — the survivors attempt to maintain the forms — but the balance of giving and receiving has been broken in ways the system has no mechanism to repair.

**Non-chronological organizing principle (v1.5):** The chapter organizes around the Quarup itself — not chronologically (Era 2 spans 300 years) but through the three-day ceremony. Each chapter section follows a phase of the ceremony, with flashbacks/context embedded in the ceremony's logic. The Quarup is the chapter's organizing center, as the eland is for the San chapter, as the storehouse is for the Swahili chapter. This is the Kuikuro's own center of gravity.

## 4. Section Plan

**Opening (integrated from opening.md):** The road clearing. Iauatá and the work crew, three moons before the Quarup.

**Section 1 — Arrival:** The days before the egitsü. Communities arriving at Kuhikugu along their roads. The plaza filling. The scale of Kuhikugu — the archaeology gives us numbers (this is one of the largest pre-contact sites in the Amazon). The smoke of multiple fires. The sound of the upper Xingu network in assembly.

**Section 2 — The Living Network (terra preta and the garden):** The chapter steps back to ground the Quarup's material foundation. The manioc surplus that feeds the ceremony comes from somewhere — from the terra preta gardens. Show the gardener and the enriched soil. What is terra preta, how is it made, what does it produce, and what does the person who tends it know? This is the chapter's knowledge-at-method-level section for the food system.

**Section 3 — The Gift Exchange:** The anetü of Kuhikugu receiving the visiting anetü in the formal exchange that precedes the ceremony. Specific goods move between specific communities. The exchange is not random generosity — it is a structured renewal of specific inter-community obligations. Show what flows where and why.

**Section 4 — The Egitsü (ceremony proper):** The mourning songs for the dead chief. The hüge performing the correct songs. The sacred log (the quarup log itself) at the ceremony's center. The two days of dancing. The wrestling matches that follow — young men from different communities proving status in a medium that does not produce warfare. The ceremony as the network's annual operating system.

**Section 5 — The Road Home (and the question at the horizon):** The ceremony is complete. The mourning period is closed. The anetü have exchanged. The visitors take their roads home. The network has renewed itself. Three hundred miles to the east, in ways no one at Kuhikugu can see, the first European ships have touched the Caribbean islands. The diseases they carry move through populations with no immunity, faster than news can travel. The chapter does not narrate what is coming — it simply names it as a presence at the edge of the era, the way the Overview's Era 2 hook does: "Twenty years from now, the epidemic... No one at the Quarup knows this yet."

**Landing:** The road still runs straight. The forest is still pressing against it. Someone will clear it next season. Someone will clear it until they cannot. The network endures as long as the roads hold.

## 5. Panel Pressure Points Anticipated

**Tuchman:** The chapter must arrive at the Quarup, not just gesture toward it. She wants us inside the ceremony, not describing it from outside. Section 4 must be vivid and specific — the mourning songs happening, the wrestling happening, the gift exchange happening — not summarized.

**Ibn Khaldun:** The economic foundation of the ceremony. Where does the feast wealth come from? The terra preta section (Section 2) and the gift exchange section (Section 3) address this. The terminal condition must be planted — the network's structural vulnerability to population imbalance should be visible in the system's logic by the end of the chapter.

**Achebe:** Never argue with the denier. The chapter must not say "look how sophisticated this is." It must simply be sophisticated. Every time the temptation arises to underscore the network's organizational achievement, resist it. The Quarup operates. The anetü exchanges. The terra preta produces. The reader understands.

**Davis:** The terra preta section must show the knowledge system at method level — what the gardener knows, how she knows it, what the practice produces. Not "they managed the forest." The specific practice, in a specific person's hands.

**Kapuściński:** The egitsü must be felt. The sound of the mourning songs. The smell of the cooking fires. The feel of the plaza at night with multiple communities present. Sensory specificity in every section — especially Section 4.

## 6. Open Questions

1. What specific goods move in the inter-community gift exchange at the Quarup? Heckenberger and Gregor have details. Need to verify: feathers (harpy eagle, macaw), manioc flour, pottery, woven cotton goods?

2. What are the specific songs in the egitsü? Franchetto's work on Kuikuro oral tradition may have this. If not, the knowledge-at-method-level approach is: show the hüge knowing which songs belong to which deceased, without naming specific songs we cannot verify.

3. Kuhikugu's population estimate. Heckenberger's 2008 paper gives numbers for the larger cluster — need the specific figure. This matters for the Quarup's scale.

4. The terra preta production method — specifically what materials are mixed and how. The charcoal, bone ash, organic waste combination is documented; need the method-level detail for the gardener's practice.

5. How does the anetü's lineage obligation to host work? Is Kuhikugu always the host, or does hosting rotate? This affects whether the chief's death means his community must host the ceremony that mourns him.
