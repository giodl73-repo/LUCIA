# The First Dynasty: Hammurabi's Babylon

## I. The Stele

The stele stood in the Esagila precinct, in the courtyard where the mud brick walls gave way to open sky and the morning sun fell clean across the stone. Black diorite, taller than a man, the only stone in a city built of river mud. At the top, carved in relief, the sun-god Shamash sat on his throne and extended toward the king the rod and the ring, the instruments of just measure. Hammurabi stood before him, one hand raised, receiving. Beyond the courtyard wall, the Etemenanki rose in stepped terraces against the flat sky, and the smoke of the morning offerings drifted across the precinct, and the date palms along the canal threw their shadows on the pavement. Below the image, in columns cut so fine the script could be read by anyone who stood close enough, the law began.

If a man bring an accusation against a man, and charge him with a capital crime, but cannot prove it, the accuser shall be put to death.

If a man steal the property of a temple or of the court, that man shall be put to death, and he who received the stolen thing from his hand shall also be put to death.

Two hundred and eighty-two cases. Carved into the stone in the Akkadian language, in signs any trained scribe could read. Not hidden in the temple archive. Not locked in the king's private chamber. Standing in the open, where the courtyard caught the morning sun and the stone warmed under the hand of anyone who touched it.

This was the claim: that the law was not secret. That a merchant in the karum outside the temple gate could, if he wished, walk into the courtyard and read the case that governed his contract. That a woman whose husband had died could find the clause that protected her bride-price. That a man accused of a crime could point to the stone and say: here is the judgment, and it was set down before this case arose, and neither the judge nor the accuser may change it. The stele did not say: this is my will and I am strong enough to enforce it. The stele said: this is the order of the cosmos, and I am the shepherd appointed by Shamash and Marduk to make it visible in the land.

## II. The Gate of the Gods

They came from the west.

The Amorites, the amurru in Akkadian, the westerners, had drifted into the cities of the alluvial plain for generations before anyone recorded a dynasty. They came from the steppe beyond the Euphrates, from the dry country where the pastoralists moved their flocks between summer and winter grazing, where the language was not Sumerian and not Akkadian but something older and rougher, a Semitic tongue that the city scribes did not bother to write down. They came as laborers, as soldiers, as traders. They married into the cities. Their sons learned Akkadian and went to the scribal schools and served in the temple administrations, and their grandsons bore Akkadian names and worshipped Akkadian gods and governed the canal systems that fed the cities that their grandfathers had entered as strangers.

Sumu-abum was one of these. Around 1894 BCE he established himself at Bab-ilim, a city on the Euphrates that sat where the two rivers drew closest together, a crossing point for the caravan routes that ran between the northern cities and the gulf. The city was not large. It was not the seat of an old god with an ancient temple. It was a waypoint, a market town, a place where goods changed hands and the canals were adequate but not exceptional. What it had was position. Any army moving between the northern plain and the southern cities had to pass through or around Bab-ilim. Any trader shipping goods from the gulf to the upriver cities stopped there.

Sumu-abum built walls. His successors built canals. Five kings in sequence, each one named in the year-names for the same activities: the canal that so-and-so dug, the wall that so-and-so raised, the temple that so-and-so restored. The Amorite dynasty did not conquer Bab-ilim. They tended it. They repaired the irrigation works, maintained the temple of Marduk, and governed through the existing administrative system, in the existing language, under the existing gods.

And as they tended the city, the city's god rose.

Marduk had been a minor deity. In the older Sumerian lists he does not appear among the great gods. Enlil held the tablets of destiny at Nibru. Enki held the me at Eridug. Anu ruled the heavens from Unug. Marduk was the local god of a local city, and his temple, the Esagila, was modest compared to the ancient houses of the southern gods. But as Bab-ilim grew, Marduk grew. As the dynasty extended its control over the surrounding territory, the theologians of the Esagila extended Marduk's authority in the divine assembly. The Enuma Elish, the great creation epic, composed or consolidated during this period, made the argument: in the beginning, when the gods faced the chaos-monster Tiamat, no god could stand against her. The great gods trembled. They offered the young Marduk the leadership of the assembly if he would fight. He fought. He split Tiamat's body in two and made the sky from one half and the earth from the other. He established the stations of the stars. He created humanity from the blood of the slain god Kingu, Tiamat's consort, so that humans could do the work the gods no longer wished to do. And the assembly of the gods gave him fifty names and proclaimed him supreme.

The epic was recited at the Akitu, the New Year festival, when the king came before Marduk in the Esagila and was stripped of his crown and his scepter and struck on the cheek by the high priest and required to kneel and declare: I have not sinned against your city. I have not been negligent of your requirements. Then the priest restored the insignia, and the king wept, and the weeping was proof that he felt the weight of the god's trust, and the god renewed his mandate, and the year began.

This was not a ceremony observed from outside. This was the mechanism by which the cosmos renewed itself. If the king did not take Marduk's hand at the Akitu, the year's fates were not determined, and the world's order was not confirmed, and the crops might fail and the canals might breach and the enemies might prevail. The Akitu was not optional. Centuries later, when Assyrian kings conquered Babylon and could not or would not perform the ceremony, the Babylonians recorded the years as blank: years without a king, because a king who did not take Marduk's hand was not a king.

## III. The Patient King

Hammurabi became king in approximately 1792 BCE. He was perhaps eighteen. The kingdom he inherited was one of five that divided the alluvial plain and the middle Euphrates.

To the north, Shamshi-Adad of Assyria controlled an empire stretching from the Tigris to the Euphrates and had installed his sons as vassal rulers at Mari and Ekallatum. To the south, Rim-Sin of Larsa held the old Sumerian heartland from Ur to Nippur. To the east, the kingdom of Eshnunna controlled the Diyala river valley, the corridor between the plain and the Iranian plateau. Behind Eshnunna stood Elam, the mountain kingdom that had been reaching into the plain since before Sumer's earliest cities. And Hammurabi sat at Bab-ilim, in the center of the web, with a kingdom too strong to be absorbed and too weak to absorb the others.

He built canals. The year-names of his first twenty-nine years record the work: the Year Hammurabi the King Dug the Canal Called Hammurabi-Is-the-Abundance-of-the-People. The Year the Wall of the City Sippar Was Built. The Year the Throne of the God Nanna Was Fashioned. Canal by canal, temple by temple, wall by wall. The names themselves reveal the king's understanding of what mattered. He did not name his years for battles. He named them for water.

The work was not modest ambition. It was the investment that conquest would later require. The canals fed the fields. The fields fed the granaries. The granaries fed the workers who dug the canals and the soldiers who garrisoned the walls. In the early morning, before the heat pressed down on the plain and the dust rose in columns from the dry ground, the labor gangs went out to the canal heads with their copper-bladed hoes and their carrying baskets and the overseers with their ration lists on which every worker was named and every day's labor was counted and every measure of barley was noted against the work performed. At the Sippar intake, where the canal drew from the Euphrates, the workers stood in the brown water to their knees, hauling silt from the channel bed and packing it into the embankment, the mud heavy on the copper blades, the sun not yet hot enough to stop the work. By midday the work stopped. By midday the air above the plain did not move and the canal water was warm to the touch and the overseers retreated to the shade of the embankment wall and recorded the morning's labor on tablets they would carry back to the city.

Meanwhile, Hammurabi wrote letters. The Mari archive preserves them: correspondence between the kings of the age, allies and rivals in the same sentence. "There is no king who is powerful by himself," one diplomat reported from the court at Mari. "Ten or fifteen kings follow Hammurabi the man of Babylon, as many follow Rim-Sin the man of Larsa, as many follow Ibal-pi-El the man of Eshnunna, as many follow Amut-pi-El the man of Qatna, and twenty kings follow Yarim-Lim the man of Yamhad." Five powers, each with its circle of vassals and allies, and Hammurabi in the center, negotiating with all of them, committing to none.

When Shamshi-Adad died around 1776 BCE, the Assyrian empire fragmented. Zimri-Lim of Mari, whose family Shamshi-Adad had displaced, reclaimed his father's throne and became Hammurabi's ally. For more than a decade they maintained the alliance: exchanging letters, exchanging gifts, exchanging intelligence about the movements of Elam and Eshnunna. The letters are specific. Hammurabi requests a singer from Mari, a woman trained in the musical traditions of the Euphrates cities, because the court at Babylon lacks a performer of her quality. Zimri-Lim sends her and requests military support against a northern threat and asks about a shipment of fine oil. They address each other as brothers. They negotiate the terms of mutual aid with the precision of merchants drafting a contract, because in this world a king who could not negotiate was a king who could not survive.

The Mari letters reveal the texture of Old Babylonian diplomacy in a way that the year-names cannot. An ambassador at the court of Hammurabi writes home: the king is shrewd, he does not commit, he listens to all parties and reveals his intentions to none. Another letter reports the military intelligence network: informants in every court, runners carrying sealed tablets across the plain, the kings reading one another's correspondence when they can intercept it. The world between the rivers was small enough that every king knew every other king's business and large enough that no king could control it alone.

Then Elam moved.

## IV. The Conquest and the Code

In Hammurabi's thirtieth year, an Elamite coalition attacked the plain. The details are contested in the sources, but the result is not: Hammurabi defeated them. The year-name records it. The Year the Army of Elam Was Smitten. And then the years that followed, each one a campaign: the Year Rim-Sin of Larsa Was Defeated. The Year the Army of Eshnunna Was Smitten. The Year the Walls of Mari and Malgium Were Destroyed.

Four years. The patient king who had spent three decades building canals now moved with a speed that suggests the patience had always been preparation. Larsa fell. Eshnunna fell. Mari fell last, and it fell hardest. Zimri-Lim's great palace, one of the largest structures in the ancient world, with its painted walls and its archive of over twenty thousand tablets, was burned. The fire that destroyed the palace baked the tablets in the archive rooms, and the baking preserved them, and the preservation gave us the letters that tell us what the world looked like before Hammurabi unified it.

After the conquest, the law.

The stele was not the first act of the unified kingdom. Hammurabi reorganized the canal systems, appointed governors, integrated the administrative networks of the conquered cities. But the stele was the act that declared what the kingdom meant. The prologue names every city the king had brought under his rule and every god whose temple the king had restored, city by city, god by god, in a litany that is itself a map of the kingdom: Nippur and Enlil, Eridu and Enki, Babylon and Marduk, Ur and Nanna, Sippar and Shamash, Larsa and Shamash again, Uruk and Anu and Inanna, Isin and the goddess Ninisinna.

The argument of the prologue: Anu and Enlil appointed Marduk to the headship of the peoples. Marduk chose Hammurabi. Hammurabi published the law. The chain runs from the top of the cosmos to the bottom of the stele, from the divine assembly to the case of the merchant who lost his goods in transit. There is no gap in the chain. The same authority that created the world and established the stars in their stations now governs the penalty for a surgeon whose patient dies on the table. If a physician operate on a man for a severe wound with a bronze lancet and cause the man's death, his hands shall be cut off. The law does not distinguish between cosmic order and surgical liability. They are the same order, at different scales.

The epilogue is a curse. In future days, for all time, let the king who is in the land observe the words of justice which I have written upon my monument. If that man heed my words and have no regard for my judgments, and if he do not blot out the words which I have written, Shamash shall make his judicial authority firm. But if a man pay no attention to my words and efface my judgments, Anu shall tear away from him the glory of sovereignty. Marduk shall alter his destiny. The curse binds the future to the stele. The law is not only public; it is permanent. Any future king who changes the words changes the cosmic order and invites the gods' punishment.

The cases themselves are not abstract principles. They are situations. A builder constructs a house that collapses and kills the owner: the builder shall be put to death. If the collapse kills the owner's son: the builder's son shall be put to death. If an ox gores a man in the street, and the owner knew the ox was prone to goring, and did not bind its horns, and did not keep it penned: the owner shall pay a fine. If a tavern-keeper does not accept grain according to the gross weight in payment of drink, and the dry weight of drink is less than the weight of grain: the tavern-keeper shall be convicted and thrown into the water.

The code distinguished between classes. The penalties for injuring an awilum, a free citizen, differed from those for injuring a mushkenum, a person of lesser status, and differed again for injuring a wardum, a slave. If a man destroy the eye of another free man, his own eye shall be destroyed. If he destroy the eye of a mushkenum, he shall pay one mina of silver. If he destroy the eye of a man's slave, he shall pay half the slave's value. The distinctions were not hidden. They were carved into the same stone, in the same columns, under the same sun. The law was public, but the order it published was not equal. It was proportional, each category of person carrying a different weight in the cosmic balance. The Babylonians did not regard this as unjust. They regarded it as the order of things, the way the cosmos was built, the difference between the statuses as real as the difference between the sky and the earth that Marduk had made from Tiamat's body.

The specificity is the point. The law is not a philosophy of justice. It is justice written case by case, transaction by transaction, at the scale where people actually live. The code covers agricultural rents, river-boat leases, the wages of an ox-driver, the liability of a shepherd who lets a sheep be taken by a lion, the rights of a woman who wishes to leave her husband, the procedure for a man who accuses his neighbor of sorcery. Each case is a window into the daily economy of the alluvial plain: the fields, the herds, the boats, the markets, the households, the disputes that arose when people who depended on the same water and the same roads and the same gods could not agree on what they owed each other.

Whether the judges of the Old Babylonian courts applied the code's cases directly is not certain. Court records from the period survive, and they do not always follow the code's prescriptions. The judges may have used the stele as a guide, or as one source among several, or as the standard against which their own judgment was measured. What is certain is that the code was copied, studied, and cited for over a thousand years, and that the idea it embodied, the idea that law should be written, public, and derived from cosmic order, became the inheritance of every literate culture in the region.

## V. The Household of the God

Marduk's estate was not a metaphor. The Esagila complex, with its temples and storerooms and workshops and the great ziggurat Etemenanki rising behind it, was administered as the household of a living god. The king was the god's steward. The priests were the god's servants. The fields that the canal system irrigated were, in significant part, the god's fields, and the harvest was the god's harvest, and the surplus was stored in the god's granaries, and the workers who built and maintained the infrastructure received their rations from the god's stores.

In the streets of Bab-ilim, the heat of the fifth month pressed down on the mud brick houses and the dust settled on every surface and the canal water ran green and slow through the channels that divided the city. The breweries near the karum produced the barley beer that was the common drink of every household, measured in standard vessels, distributed by the temple, taxed by the administration. The smell of the mash and the fermentation hung in the air near the brewery quarter, mixing with the bitumen-smell of the waterproofed canal walls and the smoke of the cooking fires and the dust that the wind carried from the dry plain beyond the irrigated zone.

In the gagum at Sippar, forty kilometers upriver from Babylon, the naditu women of Shamash managed their estates. They were priestesses consecrated to the sun-god, women of means and family who entered the cloister and took vows that included celibacy. They could not bear children. But they could own property, lend silver at interest, buy and sell houses, adopt heirs, and litigate in court. Their cloister was a walled compound within the temple precinct, a city within the city, where the women lived in houses they owned and managed their affairs with the assistance of scribes they employed and the protection of a legal system that recognized them as a distinct class.

Their legal records survive in the hundreds: contracts for the purchase of fields, loans of silver secured by real property, inheritance arrangements that navigated the code's provisions with the exactness of women who knew the law because the law governed their livelihood. One naditu, attested in multiple tablets, purchased a house from a neighbor, loaned silver to a merchant at standard interest, adopted the daughter of another naditu to inherit her property, and brought a legal claim against a man who had encroached on her field. Each transaction was recorded on clay, sealed with her seal, witnessed, and filed. The archive of one woman's economic life, pressed into tablets the size of her hand, demonstrates the code in action: not the king's abstract pronouncement but a person navigating the cases the stele published.

If a naditu woman who has not been given a dowry by her father die, her brothers shall give her a share equal to that of a male heir. The code addressed them specifically because they were a recognized category in the legal order, and their position was defined not by modern concepts but by their service to the god whose order the code expressed.

A student in the scribal school, the edubba, copied the cases. The training was the same as it had been in Sumer's schools: lists, contracts, model cases, the teacher's corrections pressed into the clay beside the student's errors. But now the student copied Hammurabi's cases alongside the old Sumerian exercises, and the cases taught not only legal language but the structure of the world: every transaction has two parties, every obligation has a penalty, every penalty is proportional, and the proportion is fixed by the king who received the standard from the god who holds the standard for all.

The scribal schools produced the administrators who ran the kingdom. They sat in the storerooms and counted the grain. They sat at the canal heads and recorded which workers came and which did not. They sat in the courts and wrote down the judgments the judges pronounced, and the judgments cited the cases on the stele, and the stele cited the authority of Shamash, and the circle was closed: divine order, royal publication, legal practice, scribal record.

Hammurabi himself wrote to his officials. The letters survive: terse, specific, impatient. To Sin-iddinam, the governor of Larsa, he wrote concerning a disputed field: investigate the case, read the relevant tablet, determine who holds the sealed document, and render judgment. To another official: why have you not sent the workers I requested for the canal at the Damanum? Send them immediately. The king's administrative voice is the same voice as the code's: declarative, case-specific, intolerant of delay. The cosmos has an order. The order requires execution. Execute it.

The clay that held the law and the clay that held the ration list and the clay that held the building contract and the clay that held the hymn to Marduk were the same clay, pressed by the same reeds, in the same city, under the same sun.

## VI. What the Stone Remembers

The dynasty weakened after Hammurabi.

His son Samsu-iluna inherited the unified kingdom and spent his reign fighting to hold it. The south rebelled. The cities of the old Sumerian heartland declared independence under a dynasty the chronicles call the Sealand, the marshes at the head of the gulf where the central authority could not reach. The canal system, which required constant labor and constant administration, began to fray in the contested territories. Fields that had been irrigated for a generation went dry. The salt that centuries of irrigation had drawn to the surface whitened the abandoned fields. The population declined. The tax base shrank. The army that the canals had funded could no longer maintain the canals that funded the army.

Four kings followed Samsu-iluna. Each one's year-names record the same activities as the dynasty's beginning: canal work, wall repair, temple restoration. The formula was the same. The substance was not. Abi-eshuh attempted to dam the Tigris to flood the Sealand rebels out of the marshes. He failed. The water went where it chose. Ammi-ditana and Ammi-saduqa governed a kingdom that still called itself the kingdom of Sumer and Akkad but no longer held either. Ammi-saduqa is remembered for a single act: a misharum decree, a royal cancellation of debts and restoration of seized properties, an attempt to repair the economic damage of a century of contraction. The decree survives on a tablet. It is specific in the way the code is specific: barley debts are canceled, silver debts owed to the palace are canceled, persons held in debt-bondage are released. The language is the language of the code. The scale is smaller. The kingdom was contracting to the core that Sumu-abum had started with, tended and defended and slowly diminishing.

In approximately 1595 BCE, the Hittite king Mursili I led a raiding army down the Euphrates. The campaign was extraordinary in its reach: from central Anatolia to the heart of the alluvial plain, a march of over a thousand kilometers. Mursili sacked Bab-ilim. His army entered the Esagila and took the statue of Marduk. The dynasty that had begun with Sumu-abum's walls ended with a Hittite raid that came from the mountains, struck, and withdrew. The Hittites did not stay. They had no interest in governing the plain. They took the god and left, and the city was empty of both its dynasty and its deity, and the Kassite people from the Zagros foothills moved in and began the next chapter of Babylon's long habitation.

But the stele survived. Not in Babylon. An Elamite king, probably Shutruk-Nahhunte in the twelfth century BCE, carried it to Susa as a trophy, and there it sat for three thousand years until French excavators found it in the winter of 1901-02. The stone had traveled from Babylon to Susa in an Elamite baggage train, and from Susa to Paris in a French archaeological crate, and in both journeys the law it carried was unreadable to the people who moved it. But it was readable when it arrived. The columns survived. The cases survived. Shamash still extended the rod and the ring, and Hammurabi still stood with one hand raised, receiving.

And the law survived in a second form, more durable than stone. Babylonian scribes copied the code for a thousand years after the dynasty fell. They copied it in the scribal schools alongside the old Sumerian literary texts, as curriculum, as the example of what law looked like when a king published it correctly. The code was not the living law of a later kingdom. It was the model. The idea: that the cosmos has an order, and the order can be expressed in cases, and the cases can be published, and the publication belongs to the city as much as to the king.

In the Esagila's courtyard, the pavement is dust now. The Etemenanki is a ruin of mud brick slowly dissolving back into the plain. The canal that ran through the precinct has been dry for two thousand years, and the date palms that lined it are gone, and the smoke of the morning offerings has been replaced by the wind off the desert. But the stone stands in the Louvre, and the cases are legible, and a person can walk into the gallery and stand before the stele and read: if a man bring an accusation against a man, and the language is still specific, and the obligations are still mutual, and the idea that the law should be readable by the person it governs is still the idea standing in the courtyard, warm under the hand.
