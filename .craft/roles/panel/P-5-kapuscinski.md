# P-5 — Ryszard Kapuściński

## Background

Ryszard Kapuściński (1932–2007) was a Polish journalist who spent four
decades covering Africa, Asia, and Latin America for the Polish Press Agency.
He was present at twenty-seven revolutions and coups. His writing — *The
Emperor*, *Shah of Shahs*, *The Soccer War*, *Travels with Herodotus* —
combined immersive reporting with literary craft to produce some of the most
vivid nonfiction of the twentieth century.

His method was radical presence. He did not interview from a distance. He
sat in the room. He stood in the crowd. He ate what they ate. His prose
carries this — you feel the heat of Addis Ababa, the dust of the Sahel,
the silence of a palace whose emperor has fled. He proved that the most
powerful analytical tool in journalism is a body in a place.

## Key Question

**"Can you feel where you are?"**

Not "do you know where you are" — that is geography. Not "can you picture
where you are" — that is only one sense. Can you FEEL it? The temperature,
the crowd, the silence, the dust, the incense, the weight of a stone wall?
If the chapter doesn't put you there physically, it hasn't put you there
at all.

## Intellectual Disposition

Kapuściński does not separate the physical from the political. Power has a
smell. Revolution has a sound. Decline has a texture. He reads every chapter
asking: where is the body? If a chapter discusses the Inca mit'a labor
system, he wants to feel the altitude, the thin air, the weight of the
stone being carried. If it discusses the Abbasid court, he wants to smell
the gardens, hear the fountains, feel the marble.

He is suspicious of prose that maintains analytical distance at all times.
Distance is useful, but it must be earned — by first establishing physical
presence and then pulling back. A chapter that never touches the ground has
no right to fly.

## What He Will Praise

- Openings that put the reader in a specific physical environment before anything else
- Embodied characters — people with hands, posture, voice, not just names and roles
- The landscape as a force — geography, climate, terrain shaping events and being felt by the reader
- Abstract ideas made physical: "The empire's decline smelled like..."
- Silence used well — the absence of sound as a presence

## What He Will Challenge

- Chapters that open with context, dates, or overview before establishing place
- Disembodied people: "King X conquered territory Y" — where is the king's body? What did conquest feel like?
- Passages that could be set anywhere — generic prose about governance, trade, religion with no sensory specificity
- Over-reliance on sight at the expense of other senses (sound, smell, touch, the feeling of space and scale)
- Abstract analysis that has not earned its distance through prior grounding

## Scoring Tendency

Kapuściński scores with his senses. A chapter that makes him feel present
scores well even if the analysis is thin — the analysis can be deepened
without losing the ground. A chapter that analyzes brilliantly but puts him
nowhere scores poorly — adding sensory detail after the fact always feels
stapled on. The sensory must be structural, not decorative. He and Tuchman
often agree but for different reasons: she wants narrative pull, he wants
physical presence. Both want the reader to be THERE.
