## B-san-2 — Pippa Skotnes — Board Review of San, The Deep Time

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** The Bleek and Lloyd archive; ||Xam language and oral tradition; the colonial context of the archive's creation; the relationship between indigenous knowledge and colonial documentation.

**Historical accuracy:**
- /Kabbo is correctly identified as a primary narrator in the Bleek-Lloyd archive. His name ("Dream") is correct. His arrest for stock theft is correct. His desire to return home is directly documented in the archive.
- The description of /Kabbo dictating his stories in Cape Town is accurate. The quote about sitting waiting for the moon to turn back and listening to stories carried by the wind is a faithful rendering of his recorded words (Bleek & Lloyd notebooks, UCT).
- The statement that /Kabbo "never went home" is historically accurate. He died in or near Cape Town.
- The description of ||Xam communities being decimated by commando raids and land seizure is accurate and well within established historical consensus.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The /Kaggen narrative (the eland made from /Kwammang-a's shoe) is accurately drawn from the Bleek-Lloyd archive. The key elements are correct: the shoe, the honey, the eland calf, /Kwammang-a killing the eland, /Kaggen's grief, the blood becoming the first herd.
- The characterization of /Kaggen as "sacred and ridiculous" is exactly right. The ||Xam narratives are often humorous, and /Kaggen is the source of much of that humor. The chapter correctly resists making /Kaggen solemn.
- The chapter correctly attributes the narratives to specific named individuals (/Kabbo) rather than to "the San" collectively. This is important — these are the intellectual achievements of particular people.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The treatment of /Kabbo as a person, not as an informant. His desire to go home, his circumstances, the weight of what he was doing — all rendered with dignity.
- The refusal to treat oral tradition as a lesser form of knowledge. N!aisa telling the /Kaggen story is presented as teaching, not as recitation of "myths."
- The colonial context is present without overwhelming the chapter. /Kabbo's chains are mentioned; they inform but do not define him.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter states /Kabbo "dictated his people's stories to Lucy Lloyd." This should note that both Wilhelm Bleek and Lucy Lloyd were involved in the archive, though Lloyd conducted most of the later interviews. /Kabbo worked primarily with Bleek initially and then with Lloyd.
- /Kabbo's death date is given as 1876. The record is uncertain — he left the Bleek household in 1873 and his subsequent fate is not fully documented. The chapter should soften this to reflect the uncertainty.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (two minor corrections above)
