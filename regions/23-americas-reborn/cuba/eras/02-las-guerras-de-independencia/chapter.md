# Las Guerras de Independencia — La Manigua

The word for the jungle-war is manigua. It is a Taíno word, from the people who were here before the Spanish came, and it means the scrubland, the thicket, the place of thorns and roots and hiding. The mambises, the fighters for Cuban independence, took the manigua as their terrain and their name and their world. They slept in it. They healed in it. They prayed in it, because the Yoruba gods the African mambises carried with them from the slave quarters had always known how to live in wilderness. The manigua was not a retreat. It was a republic. A republic without a capital, without a treasury, without a flag that any other nation would recognize, but a republic nonetheless, operating by its own laws, governed by its own generals, and sustained by a faith that the letter-writers in the exile cities of New York and Tampa and Key West had spent fifteen years putting into words.

José Martí arrived at the manigua on April 11, 1895. He had spent most of his adult life outside of it.

He was forty-two years old. He had been exiled from Cuba for the first time at seventeen, arrested for writing a letter to a classmate who had enlisted with the Spanish forces and asking the classmate if he was ashamed. The letter was treasonous. The Spanish authorities agreed. They sent him to work in a stone quarry, and then they sent him to Spain, and then they sent him back to Havana and exiled him again, and he went to Mexico and Guatemala and Venezuela and finally to New York, where he lived for the better part of fifteen years and wrote everything. He wrote poems that Cuban schoolchildren would memorize for the next hundred years. He wrote essays on the United States that remain the most searching Cuban account of that country ever produced. He wrote the manifesto of the Cuban Revolutionary Party and the letters that held the exile networks together and the articles in Patria, the newspaper he founded, that kept alive among the emigrant workers of Tampa and Key West the conviction that the war would come, the war would come, the war would come.

The war came. Martí called it. He organized it from a desk in a rented room on Front Street in New York and from the floors of cigar factories in Tampa and Ybor City where the lectors read aloud from elevated platforms above the sound of rolling: the specific thick smell of Vuelta Abajo leaf in a room of fifty workers, the hands moving without the workers' eyes, the minds somewhere Martí was taking them. He could speak, and the cigar workers knew it, and they voted with their week's wages because he made them feel that the money in their pockets was, at this particular moment in history, the bridge across ninety miles of water.

He did not want to die in New York.

He had written about this. He had written that the revolution needed him in Cuba, not because he was a general (he was not a general, he had never commanded a cavalry charge), but because without his physical presence in the field, without the proof of his body in the manigua, the revolution would be something the exiles had organized and the fighters had fought, and the split would run through the new nation the way a crack runs through stone, invisible until the moment it opens. He understood that his credibility was not separable from his willingness to die. He understood this the way a mathematician understands a proof: not as an aspiration but as a logical conclusion. The revolution required the poet to fight. The poet fighting required, in all likelihood, the poet dying.

He landed on the southern coast of Oriente province with Máximo Gómez and four others on a moonless night, the six of them rowing a small boat through the surf toward a beach they could hear but not see. Cuba was dark. The Spanish were everywhere. The manigua was close.

For thirty-eight days he rode with the army, learning. He was not a rider. He rode anyway. He wrote in the field when he could, the famous diary that would be found in his jacket after Dos Ríos and published and read and cited and argued over for a hundred years: the jasmine, the hammock, the sensation of Cuba's air on his skin after so many years of exile. He wrote that he loved the country the way you love the thing you have been separated from, which is differently from the way you love the thing you have always had, because the separated lover has been forced to understand what the love is made of.

On May 19, 1895, in the wet heat of eastern Cuba where the Contramaestre runs into the Cauto, the army came upon a Spanish column in the scrub. The scouts returned with their report. Máximo Gómez ordered the men to hold. Martí rode toward the Spanish lines.

He had ridden, perhaps, forty meters, into the open, into the sight of the enemy, into the particular silence before the column understood what it was looking at, when the bullets reached him. Three, the accounts say. He fell from the horse into the red Cuban earth. He was forty-two years old and had not yet seen a battle from its ending.

The manigua took him. The Spanish found the body, identified it, photographed it, and buried it, hoping the burial would settle things.

It did not settle things. The revolution had its martyr. The letters in New York had become blood in Cuban soil, and the question of what that blood required would not be finished for another three years, and the question of what it meant would not be finished at all.

## The Republic in Language

Before the republic existed anywhere else, it existed in the cigar factories.

The lectores, the readers, had been present in Cuban cigar factories since the 1860s, when a tobacco cooperative in Havana began the practice of hiring someone to read aloud while the workers rolled. The logic was simple: cigar rolling required the hands but not the eyes, and a worker who could not look up from the table could still listen. The reading began with newspapers and novels, and in the exile factories of Tampa and Ybor City it became something else: a political practice. The lector's platform was elevated above the work floor so the voice could carry over the rolling sound. The workers voted on what would be read. And after 1892, when Martí founded the Cuban Revolutionary Party in Tampa, what was read was often Martí: his editorials in Patria, his speeches, his letters, his poems.

The cigar factories of Ybor City and West Tampa were the economic engine of the exile revolution. Cuban tobacco workers, most of them skilled, many of them politically educated, some of them veterans of the Ten Years' War, pledged a portion of their weekly wages to the independence fund. The amounts were modest individually: a quarter, sometimes less. Collectively they built the organizational infrastructure that the war required. The Cuban Revolutionary Party had a constitution, a treasurer, a system of clubs across Florida, New York, and Key West. It bought rifles. It chartered ships. It sent agents to the manigua to coordinate with the commanders.

The tobacco workers understood that they were not merely funding a revolution. They were the proof that the revolution was possible, that a free Cuba was not only a dream in a poet's head but a project that working people had decided to invest in with their actual wages. The specific weight of that investment: one Havana cigar sold in an American market for what a Cuban tobacco worker earned in an hour. The money made a round trip from the workers' hands to the exile fund to the island to the manigua fighters who would, if they won, make a Cuba where tobacco workers would live and work as free citizens.

Fernando Ortiz, Cuba's greatest cultural anthropologist, would later describe Cuban culture as the counterpoint between tobacco and sugar: tobacco, the crop of the free laborer, grown in small vegas by skilled farmers, sold in market relationships; sugar, the crop of the enslaved, grown in enormous ingenios by coerced labor, controlled by capital. The independence movement drew disproportionately from the tobacco world. Not accidentally. The tobacco workers had the literacy, the political organization, and the specific experience of being paid for their skill, and therefore the knowledge that there was something worth paying for that was theirs.

Martí wrote for this audience because it was his audience. He had spent time in the Tampa factories, stood on the lector's platform, felt the particular quality of attention in a room where hands are working and minds are waiting. He knew what words did in that room: they traveled. They crossed the ninety miles of water in the minds of the workers and arrived in a Cuba the workers had not seen in years. They made the island real again. They made the war not an abstraction but a destination.

Every issue of Patria was this: a letter made into a republic. A republic that lived in language because language was all the exile community had.

The discover voice, the word that names the road while traveling it, was Martí's native register. He was a poet first, and he knew that naming a thing was a form of creating it. "Nuestra América," Our America, his great 1891 essay, did not describe a reality. It named one into being, a Latin American solidarity that might exist if the people who were supposed to embody it decided to believe in it. The Cuban Revolutionary Party was built the same way: a document, a set of principles, a name, and then gradually, through the lectores and the wage pledges and the speeches, a fact.

## The Manigua as Republic

The first Cuban war of independence, the Ten Years' War of 1868 to 1878, had established what the manigua could sustain and what it could not.

The war had been launched from eastern Cuba, from the tobacco and cattle country of Oriente, by Carlos Manuel de Céspedes, a creole landowner who freed his enslaved people and invited them to join the independence army on the same day he declared the war. This was the founding gesture of the Cuban independence tradition: freedom and independence together, inseparable. The Ten Years' War lasted ten years, killed perhaps 200,000 people, exhausted the Spanish military, and ended in the Pact of Zanjón, a negotiated truce that offered Cuba some political reforms but not independence and not abolition.

Most of the Cuban commanders accepted the Pact. One did not.

Antonio Maceo was thirty-three years old when the war ended, the son of a Venezuelan immigrant father and an Afro-Cuban mother, a man who had risen through the Liberation Army from private to general on the strength of military gifts so evident that his commanders competed to claim his victories as their own. He met the Spanish negotiators at Baraguá in March 1878, heard their terms, and refused them. The meeting is remembered as the Protest of Baraguá. Maceo's argument was precise: the Pact of Zanjón addressed the grievances of the creole landowners who wanted political autonomy. It did not address the grievances of the Afro-Cuban majority of the Liberation Army's fighters, who had been fighting for abolition as much as for independence. A peace that delivered neither could not be accepted.

He refused and went into exile. He would spend the next seventeen years fighting in smaller campaigns, rebuilding the networks, waiting for the conditions to return.

The conditions returned in 1895 because Martí had created them. But the manigua republic that Maceo helped build in those years was not Martí's republic. It was a different kind of institution.

Oriente province, in eastern Cuba, was the deepest root of the independence tradition. It was also the most African part of Cuba. The sugar economy had concentrated its enslaved population there, and after abolition (which Spain finally decreed in 1886, the last country in the Caribbean to do so) the freed population remained in the east, working for wages in conditions not far different from what they had left. The African religious traditions, Lucumí (the Yoruba-descended religious tradition of Cuba), the Abakuá brotherhoods, Palo Monte, were embedded in the social life of eastern Cuba in ways that the Spanish authorities could see but not control. The orixás, Ochún of the rivers, Ogún of the iron and the war, Changó of the lightning and the drum, were not metaphors in the manigua. They were the organizing principle of a spiritual community that the fighters carried with them from their villages into the scrub.

Ogún was the orixá of iron and warriors. In the manigua, where every fighter carried a machete that had been a cane knife and was now a weapon, Ogún was present in the metal. The protection invoked before battle, the prayers, the small objects carried on the body, was not separate from the military preparation. It was part of it. A fighter who went into combat without proper spiritual protection was like a fighter who went without ammunition: underequipped. This was not superstition. It was knowledge, specific and functional, organized through a system that had survived the Middle Passage and the slave quarters and the destruction of the Yoruba city-states that had produced it.

The manigua fighters knew things. They knew which plants could close a wound, the specific medicinal botanicals that Oriente's flora offered, identified and used by the curanderos who moved with the Liberation Army. They knew how to move through dense scrub without sound. They knew how to communicate across the manigua using systems the Spanish could not intercept. They knew which rivers could be crossed in which seasons and which could not. This knowledge was not written down. It was carried in the bodies and the minds of people who had been living in and with this landscape for generations. It was the manigua's own kind of literacy.

Maceo knew this knowledge and trusted it. He was not a romantic about the manigua. He was a general, and generals are pragmatic. But he understood that the Afro-Cuban fighters in his army brought resources the creole commanders sometimes failed to see, not only courage and skill but networks of mutual aid, systems of communication, traditions of resistance that had been functioning since the first enslaved people were brought to Oriente. The manigua republic ran on these resources as surely as it ran on the rifles the exile network shipped in.

## The Proof

Thirty-eight days.

Martí's diary records the specifics with the precision of a man who understood he was writing primary evidence. The date of the landing: April 11, 1895. The beach at Playitas. The six men in the small boat, the surf, the darkness. The way Cuba felt on his skin after fifteen years away, not like a memory but like a fact: the specific humidity of Oriente, the way the heat here is different from the heat of New York or Tampa, wetter, older, carrying the smell of vegetation so dense it produces its own weather.

He writes about the jasmine. He encounters it on his first ride through the interior and writes about the smell with the astonishment of someone rediscovering something they had stopped expecting to find. He had been describing Cuba in words for fifteen years. The jasmine was Cuba in sensation, and the sensation was different from the words.

He writes about the hammock: the specific pleasure of sleeping in the field, which he had never done before. He had spent his adult life in cities, in rented rooms, writing at desks. The hammock between two Cuban trees was something that had not existed in his life until now. He notes it without sentimentality: this is where he sleeps. This is Cuba.

He cannot ride well. He notes this too. Gómez assigned him a horse and he learned as he went, which is to say he learned badly and kept riding. His presence in the field was not contributing military capacity. Every commander knew this. Martí knew this. The presence was the point. The poet's body in the manigua, suffering what the fighters suffered, eating what they ate, sleeping in the same hammock-on-Cuban-earth: this was the proof the exile republic was not only a letter-writing operation.

The diary stops on May 18, 1895. The next day's record would have to be reconstructed from accounts written afterward: Gómez's report, Spanish military dispatches, the accounts of the fighters who were present. What can be said from the evidence: the army was moving through the Oriente interior, the country between the Contramaestre and Cauto rivers, eastern Cuba's wet flat heart. The Spanish had a column in the area. The encounter was not planned.

Gómez ordered the column to avoid engagement. Martí rode toward the Spanish. His reasons are a matter of inference: he had written that the revolution required the proof of his body; perhaps the opportunity for that proof seemed to require acting on the day it appeared. Perhaps he had stopped calculating. Perhaps the thirty-eight days in the field had transformed the proof-as-logic into proof-as-reflex.

He rode into the open, into the sight of the Spanish column, and the column fired. Three bullets. The red earth of Oriente. He died quickly, the accounts say, which is perhaps the kindest thing the accounts offer.

## The War Without Martí

The war did not stop.

Máximo Gómez continued as commander-in-chief, a 59-year-old Dominican who had been fighting for Cuban independence for most of his adult life and who would not stop now because the man who had organized the exile network was dead. Antonio Maceo took the western command. The strategy they had planned, Gómez holding the east while Maceo invaded the west, proceeded on schedule.

Maceo's western invasion of October 1895 is the military achievement of the independence wars. He led a force of 1,500 soldiers on a march across the entire length of Cuba, from the eastern tip of Oriente to Pinar del Río at the western end, covering nearly 1,000 miles of terrain that the Spanish had held for four hundred years. The Spanish scrambled to build defensive fortifications, the trochas, military ditches with barbed wire and blockhouses that cut across the island west to east. Maceo's cavalry went through them. Not around: through. By January 1896 the Liberation Army had demonstrated that the Spanish could not defend the island against a determined insurgency operating with the manigua's knowledge and the eastern fighters' experience.

Spain responded with General Valeriano Weyler, who arrived in Cuba in February 1896 with orders to end the war. Weyler understood the insurgency's logic correctly: the Liberation Army depended on the civilian population in the manigua zones for food, intelligence, recruits, and shelter. Remove the civilian support base and the army loses its supply chain. His method was reconcentración: the forced relocation of the rural population of western Cuba into fortified garrison towns, with their farms burned behind them. The crops were destroyed. The livestock was killed or confiscated. The people were concentrated in conditions with no food, no sanitation, no medicine, and they died in large numbers.

Estimates vary, but the most careful historical accounting suggests that between 170,000 and 300,000 Cubans died in the reconcentration camps between 1896 and 1898, from starvation, from dysentery, from yellow fever. In the camps, the specific cruelty was not violence but deliberate deprivation: the Spanish did not shoot the people they concentrated. They simply provided them with nothing and waited.

Weyler's military logic was sound. His method achieved what he intended: the Liberation Army's western operations became harder to sustain as the civilian infrastructure collapsed. But the images of the reconcentration camps, described in detail by American journalists who traveled to Cuba specifically to cover the crisis, produced in the United States a public outrage that changed the political calculation across the Florida Straits.

Maceo died in December 1896, ambushed at Punta Brava near Havana by a Spanish force. He was fifty-one years old. The war continued without its finest military mind, fought now by commanders who were excellent but who had been working in Maceo's shadow. His death, like Martí's, was mourned as a military loss. It was also the removal, eighteen months before the war's end, of the figure most insistently committed to the racial terms of independence: the man who had refused the Pact of Zanjón because it did not deliver abolition and equality, the man whose very existence as the war's most brilliant general was the argument against every claim that Afro-Cubans could not govern themselves. With Maceo gone, the revolution's racial promise was, for the first time, without its most powerful embodiment.

## The United States Arrives

The USS Maine entered Havana harbor on January 25, 1898. It was a second-class battleship of the United States Navy, dispatched, the American government said, to protect American citizens and property in Cuba during the civil disturbances. The Spanish government accepted its presence with the careful courtesy of a power that could not afford to antagonize the United States and knew it.

On the night of February 15, the Maine exploded. 266 American sailors died. The cause of the explosion has never been definitively established. American naval inquiries in 1976 and a National Geographic investigation in 1998 both concluded that an internal explosion was the likely cause, not an external one. In 1898, the American press did not wait for inquiry. "Remember the Maine, to hell with Spain" was the headline, and the headline became a war cry, and the war cry became policy within three months.

The Spanish-American War lasted from April to August 1898. It was fought in Cuba, Puerto Rico, and the Philippines simultaneously. In Cuba, the decisive military actions involved the United States Army (including Theodore Roosevelt's volunteer cavalry, the Rough Riders, at San Juan Hill) and the United States Navy (which destroyed the Spanish Atlantic Fleet in the Battle of Santiago Bay in July). The Cuban Liberation Army, which had been fighting for three years and had largely broken Spanish control of the countryside, participated in some actions and was excluded from others. When Santiago de Cuba surrendered to American forces in July, the Cuban Liberation Army was not permitted to enter the city. The victory was American; the Cubans were guests at their own liberation.

The Treaty of Paris was signed in December 1898. Spain ceded Cuba, Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines to the United States. Cuba was not a party to the treaty. The Cuban provisional government, the Liberation Army, and the thirty years of Cuban independence organizing had no formal standing in the negotiations that ended the war they had fought. This was not an oversight. The United States had decided that Cuban sovereignty would be a managed condition, not an achieved fact.

Cuba had no foreign recognition and no access to credit markets; the republic-in-formation could not sustain itself without external support, and the United States was positioned to provide that support on its own terms. The Spanish-American War ended so quickly (three months) that the United States had not engaged in extended negotiation with the Cuban provisional government and carried no treaty obligation to honor it. And the United States in 1898 was a Pacific power that had just acquired the Philippines and Guam; Cuba, ninety miles from Florida, was strategically essential to its Caribbean approaches. The Platt Amendment was not merely an expression of imperial appetite. It was the logical expression of a strategic interest that the United States never concealed.

The American military occupation lasted until 1902. In 1901, as a condition of ending the occupation, the United States required Cuba to incorporate into its constitution the Platt Amendment: the reservation to the United States of the right to intervene in Cuban affairs "for the preservation of Cuban independence" and the maintenance of order. The Cubans understood what the amendment said. That Cuban independence was conditional on American approval meant that Cuban independence, as Martí had defined it, full sovereignty, genuine self-determination, had not been achieved. The revolution had performed correctly on its own terms. The key party to the peace had not recognized those terms as binding.

## The Republic That Was Not What It Was For

The Cuban Republic was declared on May 20, 1902. The date was chosen by the United States. The ceremony was held in Havana. The American military governor handed authority to the first Cuban president, Tomás Estrada Palma, who had spent most of the previous years in the United States and was a naturalized American citizen. The new constitution included the Platt Amendment.

In Oriente, in the country where the Contramaestre runs into the Cauto, the ceiba trees were the same. The red earth was the same. The mambises who had survived the war, the Afro-Cuban soldiers who had made up a majority of the Liberation Army and who had believed that the war they fought was for a Cuba without racial hierarchy, returned to their villages. The Liberation Army was demobilized under the American occupation without the land grants or pensions that the soldiers had been promised and had fought expecting; the demobilization left the fighters, disproportionately Afro-Cuban, without the material foundation to participate in the new republic's political economy. Some found land. Many did not. The sugar economy resumed. The American companies moved in, buying land that had been burned in the war at prices that reflected the desperation of people who needed to eat. The racial stratification of Cuban society persisted in the forms that mattered: land ownership, employment, education, political representation.

Martí had written: "Cuban is more than white, more than mulato, more than Black." The manigua republic had, in its own way, lived this. The Liberation Army had been led by a Dominican, a mulato, and a creole, fighting together for a Cuba none of them had been born into legally as free men. Maceo had refused the Pact of Zanjón because that Cuba was not yet real. He had been right to refuse.

What the blood required turned out to be a question the republic itself could not yet answer.

The letters Martí had written in New York had become blood in Cuban soil, and the blood had become a republic, and the republic carried within it the unresolved question of whether the revolution had been for Cuba or for what Cuba might become: the republic Martí described in prose, the republic Maceo refused to accept substitutes for, the republic the tobacco workers in Tampa had funded with one day's wages per week because they believed it was possible.

The word manigua was still there. The Taíno word for the scrubland where the republic had lived in the years when it had no other territory was still in the mouths of the people who had walked through it. The thorns and roots and hiding were still there. Cuba was dark in some ways it had not been dark before, and the question of what that darkness required was not finished at all, and would not be finished for sixty more years, and arguably still is not.
