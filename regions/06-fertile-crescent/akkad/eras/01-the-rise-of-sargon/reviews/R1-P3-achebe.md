# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Panel Review of The Rise of Sargon (Opening)

**Date:** 2026-04-16
**Artifact:** opening.md

## What Works

The culture owns its story. Sargon's birth legend is presented as the culture's own narrative tool, not as a quaint origin myth for a modern audience to smile at. "What matters is what the story told the people who repeated it." This is the correct move. The legend is analyzed from inside its own function.

The divine election by Ishtar is not condescended to. The text does not say "the Akkadians believed" as though belief is a category that requires the writer's permission. It says Ishtar loved him, and the verb is the verb of divine election, and the consequence is power. The cosmology operates as reality within the text. This is what "present from within" requires.

The Sumerian cities are not presented as victims of Akkadian conquest. They are presented as political entities with their own claims (Lugalzagesi held the south, he was en and lugal, he had his own confederation). The conquest is told as a contest between two systems, not as the destruction of one by another.

## What Needs Work

The phrase "the world's first empire" appears once, and while factually conventional, it risks importing a framework from outside. "First" implies a sequence that the culture itself did not experience. Sargon did not know he was "first." He knew he held the four quarters. Consider whether the phrase serves the narrative or the reader's orientation. If the latter, it could be placed more carefully, perhaps parenthetically or in the chapter rather than the opening.

The Sumerian population under Akkadian rule is absent. The opening tells the story of Sargon's rise but does not include a single moment from the perspective of the people he conquered. The Achebe Test does not only protect Akkad. It protects the Sumerians who lived under Akkadian authority. Even a single sentence acknowledging that the thirty-four battles were fought in cities where people lived and worshipped and farmed would prevent the conquest from reading as purely heroic.

## The Question I'd Push On

Whose story is this? Sargon's, or Akkad's? If it is Sargon's, the opening is strong. If it is Akkad's, the opening needs to plant the reality that Akkad was built over living Sumerian cities, and the people in those cities experienced the conquest differently than the conqueror.
