# Kitara — "The Older Kingdom"

Bunyoro-Kitara is the oldest of the interlacustrine kingdoms of the Great Lakes region — the state from which, according to regional oral tradition, all the other Great Lakes kingdoms derived. The Bachwezi — the founding heroes of the Kitara tradition, semi-divine figures of extraordinary power and beauty who established the kingdom and then mysteriously disappeared — are remembered across the entire interlacustrine zone: in Buganda as the builders of the earthworks at Bigo, in Rwanda and Burundi as founding ancestors, in Ankole as the progenitors of the Bahima cattle-owning aristocracy. The Bachwezi are not simply Bunyoro's mythological property; they are a regional memory of an earlier political order that shaped the political culture of the entire Great Lakes zone.

The Bito dynasty that replaced the Bachwezi in Bunyoro — the Nilotic-speaking Luo people who migrated south from what is now South Sudan and established a new ruling dynasty over the existing Bantu-speaking agricultural population — maintained Kitara's identity and continued its expansion. At its height in the sixteenth century, the Bunyoro-Kitara state extended from the Nile to Lake Victoria, from the Ruwenzori Mountains to Lake Edward — a territory far larger than any single Great Lakes state before or since. The subsequent shrinking of this territory, as Buganda expanded at Bunyoro's expense across the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, is the defining geopolitical story of the Great Lakes region in the pre-colonial period.

## What Defined Them

**The Bachwezi and the regional founding mythology.** The Bachwezi tradition is the most widely shared founding mythology in the Great Lakes region. These figures — Ndahura, Mulindwa, Wamara — are remembered as having extraordinary powers: they could travel long distances instantly, they were unusually beautiful, their cattle were perfect. They built the great earthwork at Bigo and other sites, and then they left — some traditions say they went into the earth or into the water, promising to return. Shrines to the Bachwezi remain active in Bunyoro, Buganda, and Ankole, and the mediums who channel Bachwezi spirits are consulted for healing and political guidance. The Bachwezi are not simply history; they are a continuing presence in the spiritual landscape of the Great Lakes region.

**The Bigo earthworks.** The earthwork complex at Bigo bya Mugenyi — in the Katereke area of modern Uganda — is the largest earthwork system in sub-Saharan East Africa. The ditches and banks extend for approximately ten kilometers, enclosing an area of several square kilometers. Archaeological dating places construction in the fifteenth century. Whether the earthworks were built by the Bachwezi (as tradition holds) or by the early Bito dynasty (as archaeological evidence suggests) is a question that the available evidence has not resolved. They were clearly built by organized state labor, under central direction, for a purpose that required massive resource mobilization. They are evidence of a state capable of building infrastructure on a scale that demands acknowledgment.

**The Bito-Bachwezi transition.** The transition from the Bachwezi to the Bito dynasty in Bunyoro is one of the most analyzed questions in Great Lakes historiography. The Bito were Luo-speaking immigrants from the north; the populations they ruled were Bantu-speaking agriculturalists and the Hima cattle-owning class. The transition is remembered in oral tradition as the Bachwezi "departing" and the Bito arriving to continue the kingdom, but historical analysis suggests a more complex process of conquest, incorporation, and legitimation. The Bito maintained the Kitara identity, adopted the political culture of the Bachwezi state, and ruled over the existing social hierarchy without replacing it entirely. This pattern — incoming ruling dynasty adopting the political culture of the people it governs — recurs across the Great Lakes region.

**The Bunyoro-Buganda rivalry.** The long competition between Bunyoro and Buganda for dominance in the Great Lakes region is the defining geopolitical story of the interlacustrine zone. Bunyoro was the older and initially larger kingdom; Buganda was the more flexible, commercially sophisticated, and administratively innovative one. Over the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, Buganda systematically took territory from Bunyoro — including the fertile Bwera and Mwenge counties. When the British arrived, they allied with Buganda, and the Buganda Agreement of 1900 transferred the "lost counties" to Buganda. The subsequent campaign by Bunyoro to reclaim these counties — which succeeded partially in a 1964 referendum — is one of the most persistent political legacies of the colonial period in Uganda.

**The Omukama and the cattle tradition.** The Omukama (king) of Bunyoro maintained a relationship with cattle that reflected the broader Great Lakes aristocratic tradition: cattle were the primary form of wealth and political obligation, and the Omukama's herds were markers of his power. The Hima — the cattle-owning class — maintained a social distinction from the Iru — the agricultural class — that reflected centuries of political differentiation. This structure was not simply economic; it was embedded in the cosmological understanding of the kingdom as a pastoral polity in which cattle were the currency of relationship between the ruler and the ruled.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Bachwezi at Bigo:** The earthwork is being dug. The Bachwezi are directing the labor. The ditches are precise, the banks are measured. This is a project of extraordinary scale for a pre-mechanized state. What they are building will be remembered for five hundred years.

**2 — The Bito Arrival:** The Luo have come south. They have met the existing population. The negotiation is political as much as military: the newcomers speak a different language but will rule in the Kitara tradition. The founding of dynasties is often like this.

**3 — The Long War with Buganda:** A Bunyoro military chief is counting the territory they have lost this generation. The list is long. The Buganda kabaka has a more flexible court, better access to the lake trade, and allies that Bunyoro lacks. The retreat is happening in slow motion.

**4 — The Lost Counties:** A Bunyoro elder in the 1950s is explaining to a British colonial officer why the counties belong to Bunyoro. He has the genealogies, the land histories, the traditions. The colonial officer has the map that was drawn in 1900. Both documents exist. The 1964 referendum will answer the question, partially.
