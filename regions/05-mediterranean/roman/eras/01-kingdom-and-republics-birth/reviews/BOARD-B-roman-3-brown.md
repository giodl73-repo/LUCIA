## B-roman-3 — Peter Brown — Board Review of Roman Era 1

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Late antiquity, the transformation of Roman culture, continuity between classical and medieval institutions.

**Historical accuracy:**
- For this era, my expertise is less directly relevant, but I note that the chapter plants seeds correctly for the later eras. The mos maiorum is presented as a living, adaptable tradition, which is essential for understanding how Christianity would later be absorbed into Roman institutional forms.
- The religious integration into the political system is well handled. This will matter in Era 5 when Christianity transforms that integration.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter correctly avoids presenting the Republic as secular. The Romani would not have understood the distinction between religious and political authority. The augural system and the pontifical calendar are presented as political tools, which they were, but they were also genuinely religious in the Roman understanding. The chapter holds both without reducing one to the other. Good.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The treatment of the mos maiorum as a mechanism for innovation through apparent continuity. This is the key to understanding how Roma would absorb Greek culture, eastern religions, and eventually Christianity without ever admitting it was changing.
- The regal period presented as institutional foundation rather than as primitive prehistory. The kings' contributions are taken seriously. This is correct and important for the book's arc: the institutions that survived the Republic's fall were largely regal in origin (the Senate, the census, the religious calendar).

**What needs correction:**
- Minor: the chapter says Numa "gave Roma its gods." More precisely, Numa organized the worship of gods who were already present. The chapter corrects itself in the next sentence, but the first sentence could be clearer.
- The chapter does not mention the Vestal Virgins. For a chapter on Roman religion's integration with the state, the Vestals are the most visible example: priestesses whose bodies were the state's property, whose virginity guaranteed Roma's survival, whose punishment for violation was burial alive. Their omission is not fatal but is a missed opportunity for sensory and institutional detail.

**Verdict:** APPROVED (minor suggestions above, not blocking)
