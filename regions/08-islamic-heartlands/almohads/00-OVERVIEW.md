# Al-Muwaḥḥidūn — Those Who Affirm Divine Unity

We called ourselves *Al-Muwaḥḥidūn* — those who affirm the divine unity, the *tawhid* that is Islam's most fundamental theological claim. We took this name because we believed the Almoravids had violated *tawhid* by describing God with physical attributes — hands, face, direction — that implied God had a body, and that this theological error was connected to the moral and political failures we saw in Almoravid governance. Ibn Tumart, our founding theologian, had studied with al-Ghazali in Baghdad and returned to the Atlas mountains with a theology of absolute divine transcendence. God has no attributes that can be described in human terms. God cannot be compared to anything in creation. To say otherwise is *shirk* — the unforgivable sin of associating something with God.

We came from the High Atlas — from the Masmuda Berbers who farmed the mountain valleys and grazed the high pastures above the snowline. We were Ibn Tumart's students, and when he declared himself the *Mahdi* — the rightly-guided one whose coming the tradition prophesied — we became his army. We took the Moroccan cities from the Almoravids, then crossed into Al-Andalus, then carried our empire across to Ifriqiya and Libya. At our height we governed the largest Islamic state west of Egypt, a state that stretched from the Atlantic coast to Libya's eastern edge and from the Ebro River to the Saharan fringe. Our capital at Marrakesh and our intellectual center at Seville brought together the greatest Islamic thinkers of the 12th century: Ibn Rushd (Averroes), who wrote the commentaries on Aristotle that the medieval Latin west would receive as the definitive interpretation; Ibn Tufayl, who wrote the first philosophical novel; Ibn Bajja (Avempace), who reconciled Aristotelian philosophy with Islamic thought. The Almohad court was, for a generation, the most intellectually active place in the Islamic world.

## What Defined Them

**Tawhid — absolute divine transcendence.** The Almohad theological program was built on the Ashari and Mutazilite insight that God cannot be described in terms that imply any comparison to created beings. This was not merely abstract theology. It had practical consequences: it forbade the veneration of local saints whose shrines the Almoravids had tolerated; it required the destruction of images and anthropomorphic representations; it condemned the Maliki juristic tradition that the Almoravids had used, because that tradition relied on transmitted opinions that Ibn Tumart considered insufficiently rigorous. The *tawhid* doctrine was the theological foundation of a comprehensive reformation — political, religious, and cultural simultaneously.

**The *Mahdi* claim and the charismatic authority.** Ibn Tumart's declaration that he was the *Mahdi* — the divinely guided figure whose coming the tradition prophesied and who would restore the community to the original purity — was the most radical element of the Almohad founding. It was a claim to a religious authority that transcended the ordinary authority of scholars and rulers. The *Mahdi* did not simply interpret the law; he restored it. This charismatic authority justified the Almohads' willingness to overturn the existing religious and political order entirely, including executing Muslims who refused to accept the reform. It also meant that when the *Mahdi's* successor Abd al-Mumin consolidated power, he had to manage the transition from charismatic to institutional authority — a challenge that every movement built on personal prophecy faces.

**The Almohad philosophical court.** The contradiction of the Almohad movement is that the dynasty which built its program on the rejection of speculative philosophy simultaneously produced the most philosophically sophisticated Islamic court of the 12th century. Ibn Rushd (Averroes) — the great commentator on Aristotle whose works the Latin scholastics would receive as authoritative — served as a court physician and scholar under Almohad patronage. Ibn Tufayl wrote *Hayy ibn Yaqzan* — the philosophical novel about a child raised in isolation who discovers truth through reason alone — for the Almohad court. The explanation for this apparent contradiction is that the Almohad theological program was itself philosophically rigorous: the *tawhid* doctrine required philosophical argument to establish and defend. The court that rejected folk religion and anthropomorphic theology was simultaneously the court most committed to rational theology.

**Las Navas de Tolosa and the western limit.** The Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa (1212 CE) was the decisive military turning point of the Reconquista: a coalition of Castilian, Aragonese, Navarrese, and Portuguese forces, organized as a crusade by Pope Innocent III, defeated the Almohad caliph's army and broke Almohad military dominance in Iberia. The defeat did not immediately end the Almohad empire — the Maghreb provinces held for another two generations — but it ended any prospect of Islamic expansion in Iberia and inaugurated the century of Christian military advance that would reduce Islamic Spain to the kingdom of Granada. Las Navas de Tolosa is the hinge on which the history of Iberia turns.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Mountain Teaching:** Ibn Tumart returns from his Eastern studies to the High Atlas and begins teaching. His method is confrontational: he challenges the Almoravid scholars in the cities, organizes the Masmuda villages in the mountains, and builds the community that will become an army. He declares himself the *Mahdi*. The Almoravid authorities try to arrest him. He retreats to Tinmal and builds his mosque there. The movement has found its mountain base.

**2 — The Empire's Height:** Averroes is writing his commentary on Aristotle's *De Anima* in Marrakesh. The caliph has asked him to make Aristotle understandable; the comment has circulated that the Greek texts are too obscure for most scholars. Averroes works through the evening, building the synthesis that the Latin scholastics will call the Great Commentary. The Almohad empire is at its fullest extent. In Seville, the great minaret is under construction.

**3 — Las Navas de Tolosa:** The Almohad caliph al-Nasir watches the coalition army form on the plain. His military advisers tell him the position is strong. The battle goes badly from the first hour. When the center breaks, the retreat becomes a rout. The Almohad military dominance of Iberia ends on this day. The caliph returns to Marrakesh and never campaigns again. The Almohad empire will survive for fifty more years in the Maghreb, and its intellectual legacy — the Averroes commentaries, the Ibn Tufayl novel, the philosophical tradition — will survive for centuries in both Islamic and Christian thought.
