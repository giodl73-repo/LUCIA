# R2-P2 — Ibn Khaldun — Panel-2 Review (Targeted)

**Focus:** Mechanism of the turn; dhamma as governance system; imperial cohesion

## On the Mechanism of the Turn

The notes identify multiple explanations: Buddhist awakening, third-generation fatigue, administrative pragmatism for a diverse empire. Good. But the chapter must do more than list explanations. It must show how each one maps onto the evidence.

**The Buddhist explanation:** Supported by the Divyavadana and Sri Lankan chronicles (Mahavamsa). Ashoka visits the Kalinga battlefield, sees the dead, converts to Buddhism, becomes a lay devotee and then a great patron. Problem: the edicts do not describe the turn this way. MRE 1 says Ashoka was a Buddhist layman for over two years before becoming serious about his practice. This predates Kalinga. The Buddhist narrative of sudden conversion does not match the edicts' own timeline.

**The pragmatic explanation:** Ashoka's empire governed peoples of many languages and religions. The Arthashastra's coercive model (spies, punishment, deterrence) works in a compact state. At subcontinental scale, it may have been insufficient. Dhamma as a universal ethic that transcended sectarian boundaries could serve as the moral ligature of a diverse empire. Support: the edicts explicitly address multiple sects and insist on mutual respect (RE 12). Ashoka never calls dhamma "Buddhism." He calls it dhamma.

**The generational explanation:** Chandragupta built. Bindusara consolidated. Ashoka inherited. By the third generation, the founding asabiyya has dissipated. The military apparatus is still intact but the social cohesion that made conquest possible has weakened. Dhamma could be the third-generation substitute for founding solidarity: a shared ethic replacing a shared struggle.

The chapter should present all three, let the evidence for each be visible, and refuse to choose. The edicts do not choose. The chapter should not either.

## On Dhamma as System

The notes define dhamma's content but not its mechanism. How did it propagate? The edict network is the infrastructure. The dhamma-mahamatras are the officers. But what did propagation look like on the ground? RE 3-4 describe specific practices: moderation in spending, respect for teachers, abstention from animal sacrifice on certain days. These are not abstract principles. They are behavioral regulations. Dhamma had teeth. Show the teeth.

RE 5 establishes the dhamma-mahamatras and describes their duties: they work among all sects, they visit prisoners, they ensure humane treatment. This is an officer class with no Arthashastra precedent. Ashoka invented them. The chapter should show this as an institutional innovation, not just a moral gesture.

## On the Landing

The structural engine (the stone paradox: power expressed through vulnerability) should drive the landing. The empire fell. The edicts did not. The Arthashastra's state died within fifty years. The dhamma's expression in stone survived twenty-three centuries. This is not irony. It is the paradox's resolution: what endured was not the power but the vulnerability carved into stone. Let this drive the close without stating it explicitly.
