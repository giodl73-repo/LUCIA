# Sources — Swahili City-States

## Tier 1 — Primary / Oral

**Al-Masudi, Muruj al-Dhahab (Meadows of Gold), c. 947 CE**
Arab geographer and traveler who visited the East African coast. Describes the land of the Zanj, trade in ivory and gold, the settlement patterns of the coast. One of the earliest detailed Arabic accounts of the Swahili coast.
*Used in:* Era 1 and Era 2 context. Early trade descriptions, coastal settlement patterns.

**Al-Idrisi, Kitab Nuzhat al-Mushtaq (Book of Roger), 1154 CE**
Compiled for the Norman King Roger II of Sicily. Contains descriptions of East African coastal towns, trade goods, and navigation routes. Draws on earlier sources and contemporary merchant accounts.
*Used in:* Era 2. Descriptions of towns, trade routes, commodities.

**Ibn Battuta, Rihla, c. 1355**
Visited Mogadishu, Mombasa, and Kilwa in 1331. Describes Kilwa as "one of the most beautiful and well-constructed cities in the world." Provides detailed accounts of court customs, food, architecture, and the gold trade.
*Used in:* Era 2 and Era 3. Kilwa's court, architecture, trade. His is the most detailed eyewitness account of a Swahili city in its prime.

**The Kilwa Chronicle (Kitab al-Sulwa fi Akhbar Kilwa)**
A local chronicle of the Kilwa sultanate, surviving in multiple recensions. Traces the ruling dynasty's origins and political succession. The earliest version may date to the 16th century, drawing on oral tradition.
*Used in:* Era 2 and Era 3. Dynastic succession, foundation narratives, political history.

**Chinese sources: Duan Chengshi (9th c.), Zhao Rugua (Zhufan Zhi, 1225), and others**
Chinese records of East African trade, particularly in ivory, ambergris, and aromatics. Zhao Rugua's account describes the land of Cengba (possibly Zanzibar) and its trade goods.
*Used in:* Era 2. Evidence of Chinese-Swahili trade connections before the Ming voyages.

**Swahili oral traditions and poetry**
Utenzi (epic poems), chronicles of town origins, genealogies of ruling lineages. Many collected in the 19th-20th centuries but carrying earlier material.
*Used in:* All eras. Cultural voice, self-understanding, foundation narratives.

## Tier 2 — Academic

**Horton, Mark & Middleton, John. *The Swahili: The Social Landscape of a Mercantile Society*. 2000. Blackwell.**
The standard anthropological-archaeological synthesis. Covers material culture, social organization, trade networks, architecture, and religious practice. Essential for understanding Swahili identity formation.

**Nurse, Derek & Spear, Thomas. *The Swahili: Reconstructing the History and Language of an African Society, 800-1500*. 1985. University of Pennsylvania Press.**
Pioneering work combining linguistic and historical evidence. Demonstrates the Bantu foundations of Swahili culture against the older "Arab colonization" narrative.

**Chittick, Neville. *Kilwa: An Islamic Trading City on the East African Coast*. 2 vols. 1974. British Institute in Eastern Africa.**
The definitive archaeological report on Kilwa Kisiwani. Excavation data for the Great Mosque, Husuni Kubwa, the palace complex, and residential areas. Coin finds, ceramics, trade goods.

**Fleisher, Jeffrey & LaViolette, Adria. "The Changing Power of Swahili Houses, 7th-16th Centuries AD." *Azania* 48, no. 3 (2013).**
Archaeological analysis of domestic architecture and how house form reflects social power. Critical for understanding the coral-stone house as political statement.

**Wynne-Jones, Stephanie. *A Material Culture: Consumption and Materiality on the Coast of Precolonial East Africa*. 2016. Oxford University Press.**
Material culture analysis of Swahili towns. How imported objects were used, displayed, and given local meaning.

**Kusimba, Chapurukha M. *The Rise and Fall of Swahili States*. 1999. AltaMira Press.**
Archaeological perspective on Swahili urbanization, emphasizing African agency and internal dynamics over external influence.

**Pollard, Edward. "The Maritime Landscape of Kilwa Kisiwani and its Region, Tanzania, 11th to 15th Century AD." *Journal of Anthropological Archaeology* 27 (2008).**
Maritime archaeology of Kilwa's harbor and its relationship to the town's commercial power.

## Tier 3 — Scholarly Secondary

**Pouwels, Randall L. *Horn and Crescent: Cultural Change and Traditional Islam on the East African Coast, 800-1900*. 1987. Cambridge University Press.**
Islam's integration into Swahili society. How the coast developed its own form of Islamic practice, distinct from Arabian or Persian models.

**Sheriff, Abdul. *Dhow Cultures of the Indian Ocean: Cosmopolitanism, Commerce and Islam*. 2010. Columbia University Press.**
The dhow trade network as a cultural system. Monsoon rhythms, navigation, commercial relationships across the Indian Ocean.

**LaViolette, Adria. "Swahili Cosmopolitanism in Africa and the Indian Ocean World, AD 600-1500." *Archaeologies* 4, no. 1 (2008).**
Argues for Swahili cosmopolitanism as an internally driven process, not simply a response to external contact.

**Beaujard, Philippe. *The Worlds of the Indian Ocean: A Global History*. 2 vols. 2019. Cambridge University Press.**
Massive synthesis of Indian Ocean trade history. Places the Swahili coast within the broader oceanic system.
