## The Long Struggle — Qajar Era 2
**Specialty:** Russian-British rivalry in Iran, the Great Game in Persian context, Qajar foreign policy
**Key works:** *Russia and Britain in Persia, 1864–1914* (1968)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

## Historical Accuracy Assessment

**On the Great Game framework:**

The chapter correctly identifies Russia and Britain as having distinct and often conflicting interests in Iran, not as a unified "West" or "Europe." Russia's interest was territorial and strategic (southern access to warm water, influence over Azerbaijan and Khorasan); Britain's interest was defensive and commercial (protecting the route to India, controlling the Gulf, preventing Russian expansion). The chapter maintains this distinction throughout, which was explicitly flagged as a requirement in the panel reviews.

**On the concession system's fiscal logic:**

The chapter's description of the court's revenue gap is consistent with the historical record. The *toman* figures used (roughly ten million required, six million available from taxes) are plausible approximations drawn from fiscal estimates. I would note that these figures varied considerably across the reign — the 1880s situation was significantly worse than the 1850s, partly because court expenditures had grown with the European trips and the modernization projects they inspired. The chapter's figures represent the later period of Naser al-Din Shah's reign (1870s-1890s) rather than his full reign, which is appropriate given the chapter's focus.

**On the Reuters Concession:**

The British press quotation is accurate. The chapter correctly notes that the Reuters Concession was cancelled partly under Russian pressure and partly under domestic opposition — both factors were significant, and neither alone would have been sufficient. This is historically accurate.

**On the Cossack Brigade:**

The chapter mentions "a small army trained by Russian-funded Cossack officers." The Persian Cossack Brigade was indeed established with Russian officer training and continued to be officered by Russians through the 1890s. The characterization of it as a check on British influence is one interpretation; it was also simply the most effective military unit in Iran by this period, and the Russians supported it as much for their own influence as against British influence. The chapter's framing is not wrong, just partial.

**On the Anglo-Persian War (1856–1857):**

The chapter omits this conflict entirely. The notes flagged this as a question, and the chapter chose to omit it. From a historical accuracy standpoint, this omission is notable: the 1856 war (Herat's second episode) was a significant event demonstrating the same pattern as the 1837-1838 campaign. Its omission does not create inaccuracies, but it leaves the Herat pattern appearing as a single instance rather than a persistent structural constraint. The chapter would be strengthened by a brief mention.

## Recommended Additions (Optional)

1. The Anglo-Persian War (1856–1857) should be mentioned briefly in the Concession System section as a second instance of the Herat pattern. One sentence is sufficient: "The pattern repeated in 1856, when another Iranian move toward Herat brought British military force to the Gulf coast; the lesson held."

2. The chapter could note that the Cossack Brigade was stationed in Tehran and played a role in the court's internal politics as well as in the external power balance. This is not required for accuracy but would deepen the picture of the military situation.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

The chapter correctly presents the Qajar court's foreign policy as a rational response to structural constraints rather than as corruption or incompetence. The "tactical concession, strategic delay" framing is accurate and was the Qajar strategy as understood by contemporaries on all sides. The chapter does not suggest that Iran should have done otherwise.

**Board verdict: PASS with no required corrections; two optional enhancements noted.**
