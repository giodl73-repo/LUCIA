# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman
## Panel Review — Opening: La Conquista y El Virreinato

This opening does something unusual: it makes the central act the taking of a name. That is not a conventional narrative hook, and it should not work — names are abstract, they don't move — but it works here because the author immediately grounds the name in the body of the hanged corregidor and in the cosmological weight of what "Túpac Amaru" meant to Andean ears. The first line — "The corregidor's body was still warm when José Gabriel Condorcanqui put on the name" — earns its place. It is specific, it is physical, and it creates an immediate question: what name, and why now?

The pacing is well-judged. The opening moves from the immediate scene (the hanging) to the historical depth (the first Túpac Amaru's execution in 1572) to the cosmological frame (pachakuti) to Micaela Bastidas, and then to Potosí. Each section adds one layer — not backstory, but depth charge. The reader does not feel the structure working; they feel the world expanding. That is the craft I most value.

The Micaela Bastidas passage is the opening's strongest moment. The word qonqarqanki — "you are forgetting" — arrives with a weight that the narrative earns rather than claims. The author trusts the Quechua word to do the work. This is right. Explanation would have killed it.

The Potosí section introduces the mercury and the mita in a way that feels neither pedagogical nor decorative. The framing of the mita as ayni-violation — "a river that flowed one direction and did not return" — is the opening's best analytical sentence, because it carries the Andean moral framework without stepping outside it to label it as such.

What I would push on: the closing. The final paragraph ("The corregidor's body was still warm when Condorcanqui put on the name. Everything that followed...") is a recapitulation of the opening's first sentence, which is a structural move I understand — the chapter ends where it began, as pachakuti ends where it begins. But the phrase "two centuries of echoes" is slightly abstract compared to everything that preceded it. The author could earn more weight here. What specifically echoed? The chapter should name at least one resonance more concretely before closing.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening places us at Tungasuca, November 1780, and then immediately goes backward to 1572 and forward to the aftermath of Condorcanqui's capture. The reader is asked to hold three time periods simultaneously before the main scene has fully landed. Does the chapter's full version sustain this temporal complexity, or does it resolve into linear chronology? The pachakuti concept — the world-turning — should be the structural engine of the chapter, not just the opening's frame.
