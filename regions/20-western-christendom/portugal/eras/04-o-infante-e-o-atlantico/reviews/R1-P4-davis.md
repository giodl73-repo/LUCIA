# R1-P4 — Wade Davis — Panel Review Round 1

**Chapter:** Portugal Era 4 — O Infante e o Atlântico
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (opening.md review)
**Reviewer:** P-4 Wade Davis
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

What interests me most in this opening is the knowledge system being described — and whether the chapter will treat it as a genuine intellectual achievement or as a historical precursor to something the modern reader is assumed to already understand.

Henry the Navigator's operation at Sagres was a specific form of knowledge production: empirical, cumulative, tested through physical expedition, organized around the hypothesis that the African coast curved east. This is a research program in the truest sense — not merely accumulating information but testing a model of the world against the world itself. The opening captures this well. The description of how pilots came back with fragments of coast — "a headland, an inlet, a river mouth, a beach where strange people had watched the ship from the shore" — is exactly right. Each expedition adds to the model. The model drives the next expedition.

**What I want the chapter to explore more deeply:** The Moorish cartographers, the Arab astronomers, the Genoese and Majorcan pilots — these are mentioned in the opening as sources Henrique consulted. But the chapter should engage with what they actually knew and how that knowledge was organized. Arab astronomical knowledge in the fifteenth century was not merely "the same as Western astronomy, but from a different tradition." It was epistemically different: organized around different celestial bodies as reference points, calibrated to different latitudes, expressed in instruments (the astrolabe, the kamal) designed around different navigational problems. The chapter should show Portuguese navigation at Sagres not as the triumph of European science but as the synthesis of multiple knowledge traditions — Arabic astronomy, Genoese cartography, Portuguese coastal seamanship, Atlantic wind patterns learned by Madeirean and Azorean fishermen — into something new.

**The question I'd push on:** The "strange people" on the shore who "watched the ship from the shore and then disappeared into the trees" — this is a single, evocative, and troubling phrase. It describes African coastal peoples entirely from the Portuguese ship's perspective, and it risks rendering them as backdrop. The chapter will have more African encounters. Each one should give the African side of the encounter equal presence. The chapter should not restrict itself to the Portuguese ship's viewpoint when describing the first contact moments on the coast.

**On the knowledge system of the Africans encountered:** The Portuguese expeditions along the West African coast were encountering highly sophisticated peoples — Wolof, Mandinka, Senegambian states with their own navigational knowledge, their own trade networks, their own understanding of the Atlantic coast. These knowledge systems are not mentioned in the opening. The chapter need not detail all of them, but it should not treat the African coast as blank space waiting to be mapped by Portuguese knowledge. The coast had already been navigated, traded along, and understood by the people who lived there.

**What I praise:** The opening's treatment of the caravel as a knowledge problem is excellent. The description of how lateen rigging allows beating into the wind — and why this was the solution to the return-trip problem — engages with technical knowledge at the level of HOW IT WORKS, not just that it worked. This is the right approach to the knowledge system. Do the same for the Arabic astronomical instruments.

**Recommendation for the notes:** Plan at least one passage that shows a specific Portuguese navigational method in operation — a latitude calculation, a wind-reading, something that demonstrates the synthesis of Arabic astronomy and Atlantic seamanship at method level rather than describing it in the abstract.
