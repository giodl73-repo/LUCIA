# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński
## Panel-1 Review: Opening, Philippe Auguste et la Grande Expansion

**Round:** 1
**Reviewer:** Ryszard Kapuściński (P-5)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The horse's flanks dark with sweat. This is where the chapter begins in the body, and it is precisely right. Before we know the news, before we understand what Bouvines means, we know the animal — we know how far and how hard the rider has come. A horse's sweat is a clock: it tells you distance, urgency, fear or exultation. The author knew this, and the opening earns its ground.

The Paris celebration is the opening's second sensory anchor: torches and singing through the night, cathedral canons processing, prisoners paraded through the street. I can feel the press of the crowd. I can smell the torches. I can hear the singing that went on through the night without ceasing — which is itself a physical fact, a thing bodies do when they have survived something they were not sure they would survive. The chronicler's "without ceasing" is the kind of detail that puts you there.

What I want more of in the chapter: the physical reality of medieval French governance. The king moving through his kingdom — what did the roads feel like? What did the court in Paris look like, smell like, sound like, when Philip received his vassals? The opening gives us the moment of triumph and the political mechanism. The chapter needs to ground the mechanism in physical reality. When Philip's lawyers enforced feudal obligation against John, this happened in chambers, in courts, in arguments conducted by men in specific rooms. When the trésor des chartes was assembled, clerks were sitting at tables with candles, handling documents that smelled of parchment and ink. History is made in rooms, and I want to feel the rooms.

**The question I'd push on:** The Albigensian Crusade — which will presumably occupy a section of the chapter — happened in a specific landscape: the Languedoc, the dry hills, the walled cities of the Cathars, the heat of southern France. Carcassonne, Béziers, Minerve. The chapter brief tells me this era covers Philip's France tripling in size; some of that tripling was accomplished through what the southerners experienced as fire and siege. I want the chapter to put me in that landscape and not only in the northern French court. The southern sun, the different language, the different culture — Occitan versus French — should be felt even within the Capetian frame. The expansion consumed places with bodies, and the chapter should honor that physical reality.

The opening grounds the chapter well. The chapter's sensory challenge is to sustain this ground across what will be a geographically and institutionally complex era.
