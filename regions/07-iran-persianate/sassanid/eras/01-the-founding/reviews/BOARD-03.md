# Board Review 3 — Sassanid Era 1: The Founding

**Reviewer:** Rika Gyselen (specialist, Sassanid administrative seals, fire temple organization, numismatics; CNRS Paris)
**Chapter:** Sassanid Era 1 — The Founding (c. 224–272 CE)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Domain Accuracy — Administrative, Numismatic, and Material Evidence

**Sassanid coinage:** The chapter correctly notes the transition from Arsacid coinage (with Hellenistic-derived imagery) to Ardashir's coinage, which placed the fire altar on the reverse and the king's portrait on the obverse. This is accurate and well-documented in the numismatic record. Ardashir's early coinage before Hormozdgan already showed this fire-altar reverse type, indicating that the symbolic program was in place before the military victory — the king was asserting the claim before he had won the war to back it. This additional nuance could enrich the opening's merchant figure: the coins he is using reflect a claim that preceded the certainty.

**Administrative seals:** The chapter does not engage with the administrative seal evidence, which is understandable for a narrative chapter. For the specialist record: the Sassanid administrative seal corpus shows the fire temple system functioning as an institutional node — seals from fire temple administrators, from the *mobad* hierarchy, and from royal officials overseeing temple revenues — confirming that the institutionalization described in the chapter had a documented bureaucratic reality.

**The fire at Adur Farnbag:** The chapter states the location is uncertain, which is correct. The location most often proposed is near Kariyan in Fars, though some scholars have argued for a location in Media. The chapter's circumspect treatment is appropriate.

**Roman prisoners and Sassanid building projects:** The band-e Qaysar attribution to Roman prisoner labor is well-established in the Persian tradition and supported by the bridge's structural characteristics (Roman arch technique). The chapter's handling is accurate. The mosaic floors at Bishapur using Roman techniques and craftsmen are also documented in the archaeological record.

**The Ka'ba-ye Zartosht inscription:** The chapter describes it as "70 lines in Persian" — more precisely, the inscription has three columns of text in three languages (Middle Persian, Parthian, Greek), each covering different lengths. The "70 lines" figure may refer to the Middle Persian column. The description in the chapter ("70 lines in Persian, with parallel texts in Parthian and Greek") is a reasonable simplification. Minor note, not a correction.

## Material Evidence Engagement

The chapter's decision to use the rock reliefs as primary evidence — to read them as the Sassanid archive rather than as illustrations — is exactly the right methodological approach. The compositional analysis of Ardashir's investiture relief (the god and king as mirror images, the defeated enemies as parallel structures below) is accurate and reflects current art-historical scholarship on the Naqsh-e Rostam program.

The limestone quality observation ("fine-grained and pale, the kind of stone that holds an edge for centuries") is technically accurate. Fars limestone has been used for fine carving since the Achaemenid period precisely because of its texture; the Persepolis reliefs and the Naqsh-e Rostam carvings share this quality. The chapter's material observation is grounded.

---

## Cultural Authenticity — Material Culture

The Sassanid silver plates are not mentioned in this chapter (they appear in later eras), which is appropriate. The silver plate tradition with its hunting scenes is more characteristic of the mature Sassanid state than the founding era.

The treatment of the Naqsh-e Rostam cliff as an ongoing archive — with Ardashir's relief, Shapur's Roman victory scene, and Kartir's inscription all at the same site — is historically accurate and captures the Sassanid understanding of place as institutional memory. The cliff was not simply chosen for convenience; it was the Achaemenid site of royal legitimacy, and successive Sassanid rulers added to it as a deliberate act of accumulation.

---

## Summary

**Required corrections:** None. The material and administrative evidence is handled accurately within the chapter's narrative scope.

**Enhancement suggestions:**
1. The numismatic point about fire-altar reverses preceding Hormozdgan (Ardashir was making the claim before he won the war) would enrich the merchant figure in the opening and add a material-evidence dimension to the founding's symbolic program.
2. The three reliefs at Naqsh-e Rostam (Ardashir investiture, Shapur victory, Kartir inscription) could be noted in the landing as the cliff's final state — not three separate events but a single accumulated program of Sassanid self-representation.

Both optional. Chapter passes material accuracy review.

**Board Recommendation:** PASS.
