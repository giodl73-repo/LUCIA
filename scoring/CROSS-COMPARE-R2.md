## 1. WORKS EVERYWHERE

### 1.1 Zero Em-Dash Discipline

Every chapter in the sample achieves zero em-dashes. What began as a steppe voice emergent property (Mongol) has become a corpus-wide norm. The technique works because each voice register finds its own pause mechanism: the period (steppe, saga-spare), the comma-chain (maritime, river, urban), the colon (edict-inscriptional), the semicolon (legal-public). The em-dash's parenthetical insertion disrupts every one of these registers. Its absence is now a diagnostic, not a rule: if a writer reaches for an em-dash, the voice has slipped.

**Recommendation:** Promote zero em-dashes from an observation in INNOVATIONS.md to an explicit expectation in the Style Guide's Mechanical Rules. Reframe the current "8 per chapter" limit: "The current limit is 8. The benchmark is 0. Every chapter that has scored 80+ achieved zero. The em-dash's absence is a symptom of voice discipline, not a goal in itself."

### 1.2 Structural Engine as Narrative Spine

All 14 chapters use a culture-specific structural engine to drive progression. The engine types now include: covenant (Mali, Soninke), psychological mechanism (Haudenosaunee), knowledge system (Tang, Tongan), material medium (Benin), system-as-machine (Mongol), resource cycle (Khmer), text-as-territory (Jewish), translation cycle (Abbasid, Tibet), river-control loop (Songhai), intermediary economics (Soninke), distributed network (Fulani), material object (Norse/ship), curtain-as-political-theory (Kanem-Bornu), ecological mechanism (Wolof/tsetse-horse cycle), stone paradox (Maurya), monsoon cycle (Swahili), competitive-specialization (Hausa), and gold-covenant (Ghana).

The engine concept has proven universally applicable. No culture type has resisted it. The range of engine types is now broad enough that no new chapter should need to invent the category; it can find its engine among the existing types or discover a genuinely new one.

**Recommendation:** Add a "Structural Engine Finder" prompt to the Pipeline's NOTES stage (Stage 4). Require the production notes to name the engine, its mechanism, and its terminal condition before the chapter is written. This prevents the chapter from defaulting to chronological narration.

### 1.3 Sensory Ground as Analytical Vehicle

The best chapters do not separate sensory detail from analytical content. The smell of the dye pit IS the economic argument (Hausa). The feel of the hull IS the navigational knowledge system (Norse, Swahili, Tongan). The taste of the river clay IS the state's organizing principle (Songhai). The cold of the butter-lamp room IS the urgency of the translation project (Tibet). The warmth of the laterite rock IS the medium of governance (Maurya).

This goes beyond Style Principle 11 (Sensory Ground), which asks for "at least one sensory anchor per page." The highest-scoring chapters achieve sensory-analytical fusion: the sensory detail does the analytical work. A reader who skipped the analytical passages would still understand the argument from the sensory ones.

**Recommendation:** Amend Style Principle 11 to add: "At its best, sensory detail does not merely ground the narrative. It carries the argument. When the smell of the dye pit tells you about the economic structure, the principle is operating at its highest level."

### 1.4 Landing via Culture's Own Persistence Mechanism

Every chapter in the sample closes with the culture's own persistence mechanism, not with authorial summary. The river still carries (Songhai). The pen still writes (Fulani). The drums still sound (Ghana). The gewel still sings (Wolof). The curtain and the names persist (Kanem-Bornu). The keel waits (Norse). The coral stands (Swahili). The breath did not stop (Tibet). The stone holds (Maurya). The jelilu still sing (Mali). The law still holds (Roman). The machine did not die (Mongol). The pits still work (Hausa). Wagadu waits (Soninke).

The v1.1 Landing amendment is fully validated. No chapter in the corpus has scored well with a summary-style landing. The persistence mechanism is now the only proven technique.

**Recommendation:** No change needed. The amendment is working.

### 1.5 Inside-Voice Honesty

Every chapter handles the No Verdicts principle through the culture's own narrative tradition rather than through editorial restraint. The saga's understatement (Norse). The chronicle's precision (Kanem-Bornu). The gewel's refusal to choose (Wolof). The oral tradition's open silences (Mali, Soninke). The edict's own public accounting (Maurya). The jeli's full memory (Songhai). The scholarly tradition's self-critique (Fulani).

The v1.2 Honesty amendment is validated across all culture types in the sample.

**Recommendation:** No change needed. The amendment is working.

## 2. CULTURE-SPECIFIC

### 2.1 Saga Understatement (Norse only)

The saga-spare voice achieves the No Verdicts discipline through a specific literary technique: saying less than it means. "The wind had been fair" after a monastery sacking. "He took that badly" meaning violence follows. This technique is native to the Norse storytelling tradition and cannot transfer. When the Songhai chapter records Sunni Ali's death ("A man who had lived on the water died on the water. The tradition does not treat this as irony. It treats it as a fact"), this is river-voice factuality, not saga understatement. The registers look similar on the surface but derive from different traditions.

**Recommendation:** Add a Voice Spectrum entry for "Saga-Spare" that specifically notes the understatement mechanism and its non-transferability. Flag it in the NOTES template so writers of other Norse/Nordic chapters can use it, while writers of non-Norse chapters do not imitate it.

### 2.2 Edict-as-Opening (Maurya only)

The Ashoka chapter opens with the primary source text itself (Rock Edict 13) as the chapter's first words. This is the first chapter to open with a direct quotation from the culture's own inscriptional record. The technique works because the Maurya world IS its edicts; the stone IS the voice. This cannot transfer to oral-tradition cultures (whose voice is the performance, not the text) or to cultures whose primary sources are external (Swahili, pre-contact Ghana). It could transfer to other inscriptional cultures: Egyptian, Maya, possibly Sumerian.

**Recommendation:** Add a note in the NOTES template: "For inscriptional cultures, consider opening with the inscription itself. See Maurya chapter for the model."

### 2.3 Distributed Network as Plot (Fulani only)

The Fulani chapter's structural engine (the scholarly correspondence network repurposed for revolution) produces a unique narrative shape: the plot is not centralized but distributed. The flag goes to Katsina, Kano, Zaria, Adamawa, Ilorin simultaneously, and the chapter follows the distribution rather than a single narrative line. This works because the jihad itself was distributed. Attempting this narrative shape for a centralized-power chapter (Mongol, Maurya, Roman) would fracture the narrative. But it could transfer to other distributed-network chapters: early Christianity, the Reformation, possibly the Hansa.

**Recommendation:** Add to Voice Spectrum: "Distributed/Network" register. The narrative follows the network's branching rather than a single line. Best suited to cultures organized by correspondence, ideological affinity, or commercial network rather than by hierarchical command.

### 2.4 Chronicler-Within-the-Chronicle (Kanem-Bornu only, with broader applicability)

Ahmad ibn Fartuwa functions as both anchor figure and primary source. The chapter inhabits the act of its own documentation. This technique is specific to cultures whose richest source IS a single court chronicle (Kanem-Bornu, possibly Yi Jing for Tang Buddhism, Bede for early England). It cannot work for cultures with many sources (Roman, Abbasid) or no written sources (San, Aboriginal).

**Recommendation:** Flag in the NOTES template: "If the culture's primary source is a single named chronicler, consider making the chronicler the anchor figure. See Kanem-Bornu chapter for the model."

### 2.5 Translation Method as Narrative Content (Tibet only)

The Tibet chapter devotes an entire section (IV. The Method) to showing translation at the level of method: how "bodhicitta" becomes "byang chub kyi sems," how the Mahavyutpatti works, how tantric texts require multi-level rendering. This is the Disciplinary Depth innovation applied to a contemplative/intellectual tradition. It works because the culture's defining activity IS translation. Attempting this depth of method-description for a military culture would stall the narrative. But it transfers to any chapter where an intellectual practice is the culture's central act: Talmudic argument, Confucian commentary, Islamic jurisprudence.

**Recommendation:** Already covered by v1.3 Knowledge-System-as-Evidence amendment. No additional change needed.

## 3. FORMAT STRAIN

### 3.1 The ~5000-Word Limit and the Distributed Narrative

The Fulani chapter (Dan Fodio) strains against the word limit. The jihad's distributed character demands coverage of multiple cities (Kano, Zaria, Adamawa, Ilorin, Gobir) and multiple timescales (30 years of teaching, 3 years of fighting, 86 years of aftermath). The chapter handles this by compressing the aftermath (Section V) and the flag-holder campaigns (Section III), but the compression is visible. The Kano campaign gets a paragraph; the Ilorin campaign gets two sentences. A reader familiar with the material would feel the thinning.

**Recommendation:** For chapters covering distributed events across many theaters, allow a word-count flex to 6,000 words without requiring justification. Flag in the NOTES template: "If the era covers simultaneous events in 5+ locations, the chapter may need the upper range."

### 3.2 The 6-Section Structure and the Single-Event Chapter

The Maurya chapter (Ashoka) covers what is essentially one event (Kalinga) and its aftermath (the edicts). The 6-section structure works, but Sections IV and V (The Policy, The Stone) are essentially two views of the same material: what the edicts say and what the edicts look like. The chapter handles this by making Section V a sensory/material account of the physical objects, which is excellent, but a 5-section structure would have been equally natural.

**Recommendation:** No change to the template. The 6-section structure is flexible enough to accommodate this case (the chapter works well). But add a note in the NOTES template: "Sections may be combined or split if the era's arc demands it. The 6-section default is a starting point, not a constraint."

### 3.3 The Landing Section and the Long Aftermath

The Fulani, Norse, and Roman chapters all face the same challenge: the era's story extends well beyond the chapter's primary timeline. The Fulani chapter covers 1804-1903 in its final section. The Norse chapter seeds the Great Heathen Army, Normandy, and Iceland in its landing. The Roman chapter covers 133 BCE to 27 BCE. Each chapter handles this differently, and each solution works, but the challenge is structural: how does a chapter land when the era's consequences extend for decades or centuries?

**Recommendation:** Add to the Pipeline's WRITE stage guidance: "The landing section should close the chapter's arc, not the era's history. If the era's consequences extend beyond the chapter's scope, the landing should acknowledge what is coming without narrating it. The Norse chapter's 'The next generation would bring...' is the model: name the future, do not tell it."

### 3.4 The Evidence Dimension and Archaeological Cultures

The Swahili and Ghana chapters face a shared challenge: the archaeological record provides physical evidence (coral blocks, stone foundations, pottery, coins) but not named individuals. The Swahili chapter solves this with the anonymous builder and the composite Mwana Chambi. The Ghana chapter solves it with the unnamed merchant from Sijilmasa and the generic figures of the audience. Both solutions work, but the Ghana chapter's figures are less vivid because it relies more heavily on Al-Bakri's descriptions (third-hand evidence) and less on the physical experience of the figures themselves.

**Recommendation:** Add to the Figures scoring guidance (The Chronicle): "For archaeological cultures, the Honest Limits amendment (v1.1) permits composite figures. The benchmark is the Swahili chapter's anonymous builder: the figure does not need a name, but the figure needs a body. A figure described only through an outside observer's account is a figure at one remove. A figure described through their own sensory experience is a figure in the room."

## 5. SAHEL INTERNAL REPORT

### 5.1 Voice Collision Assessment

The 8 Sahel chapters (5 in this sample + Mali, Soninke, Hausa from other reads) were tested for voice collision. The critical question: do any two Sahel chapters sound too similar?

**Finding: No collision.** Each chapter sustains a distinct register:

| Chapter | Voice Register | Defining Rhythm |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| Mali (Great Hajj) | Griot cadence | Repetition, genealogy-as-structure, praise-song rhythm |
| Songhai | River | Forward-flowing, gathering clauses, directional stops |
| Fulani | Scholarly-revolutionary | Careful-then-urgent, pen-to-sword, juridical precision |
| Ghana | Trade-intermediary | Observational, merchant's-eye, balance-as-theme |
| Wolof | Diplomatic | Calculative, gewel-framed, axis-shift as organizing metaphor |
| Kanem-Bornu | Chronicler | Ahmad's witnessing eye, curtain-mediated, precision of recording |
| Hausa | Urban-artisanal | Sensory-navigational, guild-structured, competition-as-engine |
| Soninke | Intermediary-sacred | Covenant-framed, ecological, dual-city as structural principle |

The closest pair is **Mali and Wolof**, both of which use a griot/gewel figure as a framing device. But the registers diverge immediately: Mali's jeli is a structural element (the genealogy IS the narrative), while Wolof's gewel is a witness (the gewel observes and records, framing the chapter's arc). Mali's prose has the cadence of performance. Wolof's prose has the cadence of calculation. No reader would confuse the two.

The second closest pair is **Ghana and Soninke**, which share the same culture family (Soninke people) and the same geographic niche (intermediary trade position). The chapters avoid collision by occupying different temporal scales: Ghana is synchronic (a snapshot of the system at its peak), while Soninke is diachronic (the covenant's arc from founding through dispersal). Ghana's voice is exterior and observational (merchant-eye view). Soninke's voice is interior and sacred (covenant-eye view).

**Verdict:** No voice collision in the Sahel corpus. 8 chapters, 8 distinct registers.

### 5.2 Regional Coherence

Does the Sahel feel like a region across 8 chapters? 

**Yes.** Several unifying threads are legible across the corpus:

1. **The river.** The Niger appears in 5 of 8 chapters (Mali, Songhai, Fulani, Ghana indirectly via trade routes, Hausa indirectly via eastern routes). The river's presence gives the region a geographic spine.

2. **The Maliki legal tradition.** Every Sahel chapter references Maliki jurisprudence. This is not repetition; it is a shared institutional framework that manifests differently in each culture: as scholarly infrastructure (Mali, Fulani), as commercial law (Ghana, Hausa, Soninke), as judicial system (Kanem-Bornu), as diplomatic tool (Wolof). The Maliki tradition is the Sahel's connective tissue.

3. **The trans-Saharan trade.** Every chapter is shaped by the north-south trade axis, even the Wolof chapter (which is about the axis shifting from north-south to east-west). The Saharan crossing is the region's defining economic fact.

4. **The scholar-warrior tension.** Mali, Songhai, Fulani, Hausa, and Kanem-Bornu all address the relationship between Islamic scholarship and military/political power. The tension manifests differently in each (in Songhai it is unresolved, in Fulani it produces revolution, in Kanem-Bornu it produces synthesis), but the question is recognizably the same question.

5. **The oral tradition.** The jeli/gewel/Dausi tradition appears as a structural element in Mali, Wolof, and Soninke, and as background in the others. The Sahel's oral traditions give the region a shared narrative architecture.

**Verdict:** Strong regional coherence. The Sahel reads as a region with a shared set of questions (trade, scholarship, governance, ecology) addressed differently by each culture. This is exactly what a well-built region should feel like.

### 5.3 Source Overlap

Are the same Arab geographers doing too much work?

**Al-Bakri** appears in: Ghana (primary source), Soninke (primary source). This is appropriate — Al-Bakri's account is the definitive source for Wagadu/Ghana. No other chapter relies on him.

**Al-Umari** appears in: Mali (Cairo section). This is appropriate — Al-Umari's account is the definitive source for Musa's hajj. No other chapter relies on him.

**Al-Sadi / Tarikh al-Sudan** appears in: Songhai (primary source for Sunni Ali). Used once, appropriately.

**Tarikh al-Fattash** appears in: Songhai (supplementary). Used once, appropriately.

**Ibn Battuta** appears in: Mali (Section V, visitor account). Used once, and explicitly framed as a limited witness ("querulous and opinionated").

**Leo Africanus / al-Hasan ibn Muhammad** appears in: Hausa (Section II, external validation). Used once, briefly.

**Ahmad ibn Fartuwa** is: Kanem-Bornu's own chronicler (inside source, not Arab geographer).

**Finding:** No source overuse. Each chapter uses its own primary sources. The Arab geographers are shared across the region but each appears in at most 2 chapters, and always in the chapter where their testimony is most relevant. The Fulani chapter is notable for using ZERO Arab geographer sources — its evidence base is entirely the Shehu's own treatises. This is the strongest evidence that the Sahel chapters are drawing on distinct source pools.

**Verdict:** No source overlap problem. The Sahel's source diversity is healthy.

## 6. NEW VOICE ASSESSMENT

### 6.1 Saga-Spare (Norse)

**Sustains?** Yes. 4,508 words (estimated from read), zero em-dashes. The voice never breaks into textbook, never rises to lyric, never descends to lecture. The understatement is structural, not decorative.

**Carries analytical weight?** Yes, but differently from other registers. The saga voice carries analysis through implication rather than explication. "The word starboard comes from styrbordi, the steering side. The language remembers what the body knew." This is analytical content delivered through etymology, not through argument. The voice's analytical mode is archaeological: it digs meaning from objects, words, and practices rather than stating it.

**Distinct from existing registers?** Yes. The closest comparator is the steppe voice (Mongol), and the distinction is clear: the steppe voice moves forward relentlessly; the saga voice moves forward but stops to look. The steppe voice does not linger; the saga voice lingers on objects (the keel, the cangue, the rivets). The steppe voice builds through accumulation of action; the saga voice builds through accumulation of material detail. Both are spare. They are spare differently.

**Recommended Voice Spectrum entry:**

> **Saga-Spare (Nordic/Germanic)**
> *Norse, Anglo-Saxon, possibly Icelandic*
>
> Deliberate. Laconic. The prose says less than it means and trusts the reader to hear the rest. Understatement as structural principle: "The wind had been fair" after destruction carries more weight than any adjective. Short declarative default, with proverb-weight sentences spaced carefully. The voice lingers on material objects — the keel, the rivet, the clinker-built hull — because things carry meaning that speech does not. Emotional register: cool. "He took that badly" = violence is coming. Distinct from steppe (more deliberate, stops to look at objects) and from oral tradition (cooler, records rather than performs).

### 6.2 Maritime Accumulative (Swahili)

**Sustains?** Yes. The chapter maintains the rolling, gathering rhythm throughout, with the monsoon cycle serving as both temporal structure and prose rhythm. The clauses accumulate like swells, then break into short declarative stops.

**Carries analytical weight?** Yes, and this is the register's distinctive achievement. The Swahili voice uses the accumulative rhythm to carry trade calculations, information asymmetry, and structural tension. When the prose describes Mwana Chambi calculating the gap between two monsoons, the rhythm of the sentence mirrors the rhythm of the calculation: gathering data, assessing, deciding. The analytical content arrives in the prose's rhythm, not alongside it.

**Distinct from existing registers?** Yes. The Tongan maritime voice (Session 1 benchmark) rolls and crests; the Swahili maritime voice gathers and breaks. The Tongan voice is oceanic (deep swell, long waves); the Swahili voice is tidal (the monsoon's reversal, the harbor's filling and emptying). The distinction maps onto the cultures: the Tongan navigators read the deep ocean; the Swahili traders read the harbor and the hinterland simultaneously.

**Recommended Voice Spectrum entry:**

> **Maritime Accumulative (Trade-Coast)**
> *Swahili coast, possibly Phoenician trade cities, Malay entrepots*
>
> Rolling clauses that gather like monsoon swells, breaking into short declarative sentences like waves landing on a reef. The rhythm is tidal: accumulation, crest, resolution. The voice carries both atmosphere and argument in the same register — trade calculations arrive in the prose's rhythm, not in separate analytical passages. The monsoon's reversal (kaskazi/kusi, import/export, ocean/hinterland) structures the prose's temporal movement. Distinct from oceanic maritime (Tongan/Polynesian), which reads the deep swell; the trade-coast voice reads the harbor, the storehouse, and the two horizons simultaneously.

### 6.3 Philosophical-Devotional (Tibet)

**Sustains?** Yes. The chapter moves at the speed of careful thought throughout. No section breaks the register's contemplative pace, even during the dramatic passages (Yeshe-O's captivity, the crossing of the passes).

**Carries analytical weight?** Yes, and the weight is heavy. The Tibet chapter's analytical content is its most demanding: the mechanics of Sanskrit-to-Tibetan translation, the layered meanings of tantric texts, the political theology of the dharmaraja. All of it is carried in the philosophical-devotional register without the voice ever ascending to lecture. The method section (IV) is the clearest test: it explains a technical process (translation method) in a voice that treats the process as sacred practice. The explanation IS the devotion.

**Distinct from existing registers?** Yes. The closest comparator is the Talmudic/argumentative voice (Jewish Diaspora), and the distinction is fundamental: the Talmudic voice questions, accumulates, and never resolves; the philosophical-devotional voice builds, layer by layer, toward clarity. The Talmudic voice is anxious (will the argument hold?); the Tibetan voice is patient (the meaning will emerge). Both are contemplative. The Talmudic voice contemplates through argument. The Tibetan voice contemplates through attention.

**Recommended Voice Spectrum entry:**

> **Philosophical-Devotional (Contemplative/Translation)**
> *Tibetan Buddhist, possibly Chan/Zen, Sufi, Vedantic*
>
> The prose moves at the speed of careful thought. Long accumulative sentences build meaning clause by clause, the way a translation builds vocabulary term by term. No separation between intellectual and devotional registers: scholarship IS devotion. Silence between sentences mirrors silence between syllables in a translation session. The pace is the defining feature: this voice never rushes because the tradition it serves never rushes. Distinct from argumentative/text-centered (Talmudic), which questions and accumulates; the philosophical-devotional voice builds and clarifies.

### 6.4 Edict-Inscriptional (Maurya)

**Sustains?** Yes, with a notable modulation. The voice opens in the edict's own register (declarative, weighted, carved), modulates through war (kinetic, Arthashastra-precise), through policy (enumerative, accounting-like), through stone (material-descriptive), and lands in an elegiac register that remains inscriptional but carries grief. The modulation is itself a narrative technique: the voice changes because Ashoka changed, and the change is carried in the prose.

**Carries analytical weight?** Exceptionally. The Maurya chapter is the most analytically dense in the sample. It handles the Arthashastra's mandala theory, three competing explanations for the conversion, the institutional innovation of the dhamma-mahamatras, and the material archaeology of the pillars — all within the edict-inscriptional register. The analytical mode is enumerative: it counts, lists, specifies. This mirrors the edicts' own mode (Ashoka counting carcasses, counting medicinal herbs, counting the peoples he addresses by name).

**Distinct from existing registers?** Yes. The closest comparator is the monument/stone voice (Egyptian), and the distinction is clear: the Egyptian voice is gravitational and cyclical (the flood returns, the courses accumulate, ma'at holds); the Maurya voice is declarative and linear (the edict speaks, the policy follows, the stone holds). The Egyptian voice builds upward. The Maurya voice carves outward. Both use stone as medium, but the Egyptian stone is architectural (the pyramid) and the Maurya stone is communicative (the pillar). The difference: a pyramid says "I endure." An edict says "I speak."

**Recommended Voice Spectrum entry:**

> **Edict-Inscriptional (Declarative/Stone)**
> *Maurya, possibly Achaemenid, possibly early Islamic inscription traditions*
>
> Opens with the primary source itself: the inscription IS the voice. Declarative, weighted, carved — each sentence feels placed on the page the way a mason places a chisel mark on stone. Modulates across modes (war/kinetic, policy/enumerative, material/descriptive, landing/elegiac) while maintaining the inscriptional foundation. The analytical mode is accounting: the voice counts, lists, specifies, in the tradition of a king who counted his moral expenditures the way his officers counted revenue. Distinct from monument/stone (Egyptian), which builds upward; the edict voice carves outward, communicating rather than enduring.

## 7. LOWEST SCORE ANALYSIS

### 7.1 Ghana Empire — *The Age of Gold* (Score: 74)

**What holds it back:**

1. **Figures at one remove.** The chapter's figures are generic composites observed through Al-Bakri's third-hand reporting. The merchant from Sijilmasa is vivid in Section I but fades to an archetype by Section V. The Ghana himself is described through Al-Bakri's informants' reports, never through his own sensory experience. Compare with the Swahili chapter's anonymous builder, who is described through the feel of coral under his palms, the ache in his back, the sound of his son's voice from the harbor. The Ghana chapter's figures are seen but not felt. **Figures: 6/10** (vs. benchmark Mali: 9/10).

2. **Progression is lateral, not building.** The chapter describes the system (Section I), then the gold road (II), then the twin cities (III), then the covenant/balance (IV), then the intensification (V), then the landing (VI). But Sections II-IV are essentially three views of the same system from different angles. The chapter does not build the way the Mali chapter builds (departure → crossing → Cairo → return → long memory, each section making the next necessary). The structural engine (intermediary economics sustained by sacred covenant) is identified but does not drive the arc. It describes a state of affairs rather than a process of change. **Progression: 6/10** (vs. benchmark Mali: 9/10).

3. **Voice is competent but not distinctive.** The trade-intermediary register is present (observational, merchant-eye, balance-as-theme), but it does not achieve the inseparability from the culture that the highest-scoring voices achieve. A randomly selected paragraph from the Ghana chapter could, with minor adjustments, belong to the Soninke chapter or even to certain sections of the Mali chapter. The voice lacks a defining rhythmic signature. **Voice: 7/10** (vs. benchmark Mali: 9/10).

4. **Honest uncertainty is handled correctly but not powerfully.** The chapter acknowledges the gap between Al-Bakri's account and the archaeology ("the stone town has been partially excavated... but the royal city has not been conclusively located"). This is competent. But compare with the Soninke chapter's handling of the same material: the Dausi's cyclical framework gives the uncertainty a cultural home. The Ghana chapter's uncertainty is editorial (we note the gap); the Soninke chapter's uncertainty is cultural (the tradition itself holds the gap).

**Is the score fair?** Yes. The chapter is solid, well-researched, and honest, but it lacks the progression, the figure depth, and the voice distinctiveness that separate the 78-82 range from the 85+ range. The score of 74 accurately reflects a chapter that describes a fascinating system without making the system's logic the narrative's engine.
