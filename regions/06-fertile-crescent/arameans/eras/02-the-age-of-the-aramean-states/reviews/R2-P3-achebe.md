# R2-P3 — Achebe — Panel-2 Review of Notes

**Chapter:** Arameans — Era 2: The Age of the Aramean States

The plan acknowledges the Israelite-source asymmetry. Good. But acknowledgment is not enough. The chapter must actively manage the asymmetry throughout, not just note it in Section I.

**Specific concern:** The chapter relies on the Hebrew Bible for the Ben-Hadad/Ahab narrative (Section I), the Hazael stories (Section IV), and the framework of Aramean-Israelite relations throughout. This means the Aramean story is told, for its first half, through the voice of a neighbor who was also an adversary. The Tel Dan Stele (Section IV) provides one Aramean-voice corrective, but it arrives late.

**Recommendation:** From the first paragraph, the author should signal that the Aramean voice is filtered through other peoples' records. Not as an apology. Not as a disclaimer. As a fact that the culture's own situation produced: the Arameans wrote on perishable materials (leather, papyrus), and the perishable has perished. What survives is the stone: the Tel Dan Stele, the Zakkur Stele, the Sfire treaties. And the clay: the Assyrian records. And the parchment of the Hebrew Bible, which survived because the community that preserved it endured. The Aramean voice is not silent. It speaks in Aramaic through the inscriptions. But it speaks less often than the voices of its neighbors, and the chapter should treat this honestly.

**On the Sfire treaties (Section V):** These are critical. They are the most extended Aramean-voice texts from this period: full diplomatic documents in Aramaic, with curse formulas, divine witnesses, and treaty terms. They should carry significant weight in the chapter. They are the Aramean voice speaking in its own language about its own political arrangements. Use them.
