# Bulopwe — "The Sacred Kingship"

The Luba word bulopwe refers to the sacred principle of kingship — not the king as a person but kingship as a power that can be transmitted, that legitimizes authority, and that connects the living ruler to the founding heroes of the Luba world. Bulopwe is not simply hereditary; it is a quality that can be carried and transferred, and the founding myth of the Luba kingdom — the story of the culture-hero Kalala Ilunga, who arrived from the west and brought bulopwe with him — is a story about how legitimate authority is constituted and how it can be renewed. The Luba state was not built simply on military conquest; it was built on the claim to carry bulopwe and on the capacity to transmit that claim to new territories through the installation of new titleholders.

The medium through which the Luba kingdom maintained its history and its political order was not writing but the lukasa — the memory board. The lukasa is a carved and beaded wooden object, held by trained specialists (bana balute), who read the beads' arrangement and color patterns as a coded record of genealogies, territorial agreements, and ritual obligations. This was not a primitive substitute for writing; it was a sophisticated technology for organizing and transmitting political information in a form that required expert interpretation and therefore created a class of specialists whose power derived from their mastery of the code. The Luba governance system was carried in the body and in material culture simultaneously — in the trained minds of the bana balute and in the objects they held.

## What Defined Them

**Bulopwe as transferable sacred authority.** The Luba concept of bulopwe gave the kingdom a mechanism for expansion that did not require military conquest and permanent occupation: send a titleholder who carries bulopwe to a new territory, establish the titleholder's authority through the installation ceremony, and that territory becomes part of the Luba political world through ideology rather than force. When the new titleholder's own children inherit and spread further, the network grows. This was not a franchise in the commercial sense but a political technology for distributing legitimate authority across space. The Luba and Lunda systems share this model, and together they shaped the political organization of an enormous swath of Central Africa.

**The lukasa as governance technology.** The memory board is one of the most distinctive and sophisticated governance technologies in the pre-modern world. Approximately the size of a hand, carved in wood with beads embedded in specific patterns, colored in ways that carry meaning, the lukasa could encode genealogies, territorial agreements, lists of titleholders, and ritual obligations in a form that was simultaneously portable, durable, and secure (requiring expert interpretation). The bana balute who read the lukasa were trained specialists — the keepers of the kingdom's institutional memory. When the Luba king needed to know who had held which title in which territory, the bana balute read it. When a dispute arose about territorial rights, the lukasa was the authoritative record.

**Iron and the Upemba lakeshore economy.** The Luba heartland in the Upemba Depression had two resources that powered the early state: iron ore and fish. The lakeshore communities around Lake Kisale, documented archaeologically from at least 500 CE, developed an economy based on iron production and fishing that generated the surplus wealth which allowed social differentiation — and eventually state formation. The spectacular Iron Age burials at Lake Kisale, with copper jewelry, iron weapons, and elaborate grave goods, document a society with considerable wealth disparity and prestige goods exchange networks long before the Luba kingdom crystallized in its historically known form.

**Katanga copper and the currency system.** The cross-shaped copper ingots — handa — from the Katanga copper belt were a currency that circulated across the entire south-central African interior. They appear in sites from Angola to Zambia, and their distribution maps the commercial network that connected the Luba world to the surrounding region. The Luba control of, or relationship to, the Katanga copper belt gave the kingdom access to a prestige metal that could be fashioned into regalia and traded for other goods. Copper was the medium of political relationships: gifts of copper to allies, copper regalia for titleholders, copper currency for commercial exchange.

**Memory as political power.** The Luba governance system placed an unusually high value on memory — genealogical memory, territorial memory, ritual memory — as the basis of political authority. The bana balute were not simply record-keepers; they were the living embodiment of the state's claim to legitimacy. A Luba titleholder whose genealogy was not known, whose territorial rights were not recorded, whose ritual obligations were not documented, had no firm standing. The lukasa made the claim visible; the bana balute made it audible. This was a state built on the mastery of history, and its destruction by the colonial period's disruption of these transmission systems was the destruction of a governance technology as well as a political order.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Upemba Shore:** A fisher on Lake Kisale brings in a net. At the settlement behind her, the iron furnaces are running. The grave goods in the burial mound are being placed with care. Something is accumulating here that will eventually be called a kingdom.

**2 — Kalala Ilunga:** The culture-hero has arrived from the west. He carries bulopwe. The founding marriage happens — the hero and the local chief's daughter. The kingdom is constituted through this union. The lukasa will record it.

**3 — The Bana Balute:** A specialist holds the memory board. The beads' pattern tells him who held the title in this territory three generations back. The disputants are listening. His reading is the authoritative record. The governance of the kingdom lives in his hands.

**4 — The Ivory Road:** Arab and Swahili traders from the east have reached the Luba heartland. They want ivory. The Luba titleholders control the elephants' territory. The negotiations begin. The trade will change the kingdom more than any military force.

**5 — The Belgian Column:** The Force Publique has entered the Upemba region. The Luba king has fled. The lukasa boards are in a bag. Where they are carried and whether they survive the next decade is a question the colonial record does not fully answer.
