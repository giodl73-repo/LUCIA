# The Americas Reborn

Something new was born in the Americas after 1492. Not simply the colonial project — that is told in Region 24 — but what grew back through it, around it, and eventually against it: new civilizations that were neither the Europe the colonizers brought nor the indigenous worlds they disrupted, but something that had never existed before. The United States did not understand itself as a British colony grown up; it understood itself as a new kind of republic, an experiment in self-governance whose meaning the whole world was watching. Brazil was not Portugal transplanted; it was a society forged from three worlds — indigenous, African, European — whose mixing produced a national identity unlike any other on Earth. Haiti was not a French colony that revolted; it was the first Black republic in history, born from the only successful large-scale slave revolution the Atlantic world produced, and it understood itself as proof that the universal rights declared in Paris were not for Europeans only.

These are the new civilizations — born from the great collision of 1492 and after, shaped by indigenous survival, African displacement, and European ambition, and constituted by the particular ways each place resolved (or refused to resolve) those inheritances into a story about itself. Each nation here has its own founding myth, its own wound, its own insistence on what it means to be itself. The region's coherence is not geography — the Americas are vast — but timing and transformation: every society here was remade, in its own way, by the five centuries following European contact, and every society here is still working out what that making means.

Key features:
- Mississippi-Missouri basin: the river system that organized North American interior settlement and trade before and after colonization
- Caribbean Sea: the first zone of contact; the birthplace of the Atlantic plantation economy; the location of the Haitian Revolution
- Mexico City (Tenochtitlan-Mexico): built directly on the ruins and foundations of the Aztec capital; the palimpsest of conquest and creation in physical form
- Amazon basin: the largest river system on Earth; the home of Brazil's indigenous peoples; the frontier zone where national identity and ecological survival converge
- Rio de la Plata estuary: the gateway to Argentina and Uruguay; the southern cone's axis of Atlantic commerce and immigration
- Andes spine: the geographic and cultural continuity linking Peru to Colombia; the corridor through which Andean indigenous civilization survived and reshaped colonial society

## How They Connect

The Atlantic slave trade is the wire that runs through all of these societies. Between roughly 1500 and 1870, an estimated twelve million Africans were transported to the Americas. Brazil received more than any other country — approximately five million, nearly half the total. Cuba, Haiti, Jamaica, and the United States each received hundreds of thousands. The African presence in the Americas is not a marginal element in these societies' histories; it is a structural fact. Brazilian music, cuisine, religion, and language bear the unmistakable marks of Yoruba, Fon, Bantu, and dozens of other African traditions. Haitian Vodou is an African religious tradition maintained under the most extreme conditions of suppression. Jazz, blues, samba, cumbia, calypso — the most vital popular musical forms the Americas produced are African in their deep structure.

The legacy of indigenous civilizations runs equally deep, though less acknowledged in official national stories. Mexico City sits on Tenochtitlan. Cusco is still there. The Andean agricultural system — the terraces, the potato, quinoa, the coca leaf — persists in Peru and Bolivia regardless of which government claims the land above it. Mexico's official nationalist ideology (mestizaje, the celebration of racial mixture) consciously drew on Aztec symbolism to construct national identity; but the Nahua, Zapotec, Maya, and dozens of other living peoples are not symbols. They are citizens who frequently find the state's claim to their ancestors uncomfortable. The same tension runs through Bolivia, Guatemala, Ecuador, and Peru — countries where indigenous peoples are the demographic majority and the political minority simultaneously.

The revolutions that made these nations connected them to each other and to the wider Atlantic world of ideas. The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) terrified slaveholding societies from Virginia to Cuba to Brazil and inspired every subsequent movement for liberation in the Americas. Simón Bolívar drew on both the American and French Revolutions while fighting Spain; his vision of a unified South America never materialized, but the wars of independence he and others fought created the framework within which every Andean and Caribbean nation still operates. The Cuban Revolution of 1959 reverberated through every country in the region, producing both solidarity movements and CIA-backed counter-insurgencies that shaped Colombia, Chile, Guatemala, and beyond for decades.
