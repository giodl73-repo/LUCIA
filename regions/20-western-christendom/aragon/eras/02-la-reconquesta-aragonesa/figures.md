# Figures — La Reconquesta Aragonesa
## Key People of the Era

---

## James I of Aragon — Jaume I, "el Conqueridor" (1208–1276)

James was born at Montpellier in February 1208, the son of Peter II of Aragon and Maria of Montpellier. His father died at the Battle of Muret in 1213 — fighting against Simon de Montfort's crusade against the Cathar heretics of southern France, in a campaign that had nothing directly to do with the Aragonese expansion into Valencia but everything to do with the complex web of alliances, debts, and obligations that bound the Aragonese-Catalan crown to its southern French territories. James was five years old. He was briefly held by Simon de Montfort — not as a hostage in the brutal sense but as a political pawn — and then transferred to the custody of the Knights Templar, who raised him at their castle of Monzón for the first years of his kingship.

The childhood is not sentimental in the *Llibre dels Fets*. James records it without complaint and without self-pity: the regency council that ran the kingdom while he was a boy, the faction wars among the Aragonese nobility that he had to navigate before he had grown into his own strength, the campaigns he began at thirteen not entirely by choice. What the autobiography conveys — and this is the texture of the man — is a king who learned governance by living inside its mechanisms, including its mechanisms of constraint. He was a king who had been controlled before he was a king who controlled. This produced someone who understood covenant from both sides of it.

His two great military conquests — Majorca in 1229, Valencia in 1238 — were different in character, and the difference was deliberate. Majorca was taken by force, its Muslim population substantially dispersed or enslaved, because Majorca's strategic value was maritime and the existing population was not needed the same way. Valencia was taken by negotiation, its Muslim population largely retained, because Valencia's agricultural value required the people who worked it. James was not a ruler of consistent mercy. He was a ruler of consistent calculation. When mercy and calculation aligned — as they did at Valencia — he was merciful. When they did not, he was not.

The *Llibre dels Fets*, dictated in Catalan in the later years of his reign, is one of the most unusual documents of medieval Europe: a king's autobiography, in the vernacular, covering his own military campaigns, his marriages, his quarrels with his nobles, his piety, and his political reasoning with the directness of someone who had nothing to hide because he had already won. He describes the tears at the mosque of Valencia without embarrassment. He describes his failures — the campaigns that did not succeed, the agreements that fell apart — with the same flat precision. It is a document that reads as honest because its author had no incentive to be dishonest. He was writing for posterity, and he trusted his posterity to understand calculation.

## Zayyan ibn Mardanish (c. 1190–c. 1269)

Zayyan was the last Muslim governor of Valencia and the grandson of Muhammad ibn Mardanish — the Wolf King — who had held Valencia and Murcia against both the Almohads and the advancing Christian kingdoms for a generation before his death in 1172. The family's history gave Zayyan a legitimacy older than Almohad appointment: he was Valencian by lineage and by the particular kind of loyalty that long-rooted families command from a city that has known them for generations.

He governed Valencia under circumstances that made governance a form of dignity. The Almohad empire was in fragmentation after Las Navas de Tolosa (1212). The reinforcements from North Africa did not come because the North African provinces were managing their own succession crises. The Christian advance — Aragonese from the north, Castilian from the west — was systematic and had been underway for decades. Zayyan could not have held Valencia permanently. The question he faced was not whether to resist but what terms he could negotiate for the people in the city, and whether the man across from him would honor them.

What survives of Zayyan in the record — the Aragonese archives, the Arabic chronicles of North African historians who recorded his exile — shows a governor who understood his position and negotiated from it with precision. The *capitulació* of 1238 was not a defeat dressed as a treaty. It was a treaty that limited the defeat's consequences for fifty thousand people who had no role in the military situation. Zayyan accepted safe conduct and departed. He then spent the rest of his life in North Africa — in Tunis, in Ceuta, in the courts of the Hafsid dynasty — still holding the title of king of Valencia, still maintaining the legal fiction of his authority over a city he never saw again, still trying to build coalitions for a return that never came. He died around 1269.

He is the figure the chapter cannot render as scenery. His decision to negotiate, his departure from the shore, his decades of exile — these are the other side of the compact. James I needed the mudéjars to stay. Zayyan chose the terms under which they could. That act of governance, made in conditions of complete defeat, is as significant to the story of Valencia as the siege that preceded it.

## Abu Zayd (c. 1190–c. 1270)

Abu Zayd was the Muslim governor of Valencia's northern territories — the taifa of Valencia's mountainous northern districts — who converted to Christianity in the early 1220s and aligned with the Aragonese advance. His conversion is documented in Aragonese royal records and in the papal letters that processed his baptism. He received his northern lands back as a Christian vassal of Aragon, took a Castilian wife, and spent the rest of his life as a Christian lord of Muslim origin.

Abu Zayd is the figure who makes the binary of the Reconquista complicated. The fall of Valencia was not simply Christian power pressing against Muslim resistance. It was also about which political structures within the Valencian Muslim world could survive by adapting, and which could not. Abu Zayd's calculation — that the Aragonese compact offered more durable protection than the Almohad connection — was correct in his own case. His estates survived. His family survived. His own status as a Valencian lord, though in a changed configuration, survived.

His conversion raises questions that the sources do not fully answer. Was it sincere? The Aragonese records treat it as legitimate. The Muslim chronicles treat it as betrayal. Abu Zayd himself left no written account of his interior state. What is clear is that he continued to manage his northern territories with the same administrative logic he had used before the conversion — the same relationships with the village communities, the same patterns of land management — and that the Aragonese found this continuity useful. He was absorbed into the compact machine in the same way Valencia itself was absorbed: on terms that preserved what was valuable while changing what was politically necessary.

## The Acequia Amin (archetypal, composite figure)

The acequia amin — the water judge of a specific distribution district within Valencia's acequia network — is not a named individual the sources preserve for the weeks immediately following the 1238 surrender. The institution, however, is documented in extraordinary detail by Robert I. Burns's scholarship in *Muslims, Christians and Jews in the Crusader Kingdom of Valencia* (Cambridge, 1984), which traces the records of the Valencia acequia system through the decades following the conquest.

The figure presented in this chapter — the amin making his rounds, checking the channel, recording the distribution in a register with a Catalan notary beside him — is composite: drawn from the documented practices of the acequia amin office as it existed in the mudéjar communities of thirteenth-century Valencia, and from the specific transition records that show the Aragonese notarial system attempting to document the acequia schedules that it did not otherwise understand. The Catalan notary column appears in the archive. The Arabic original appears beside it. The two columns do not always agree.

The Tribunal de las Aguas — the Water Court of Valencia — is the living institution that descends from this. It still convenes, weekly, in the open air in front of Valencia's cathedral. It still hears disputes among the acequia communities of the Valencian huerta. It operates in Valencia's dialect, under procedural rules that trace to the Islamic legal tradition of the acequia amin office. It is one of the oldest continuously functioning courts in Europe, and its roots run directly through the mudéjar communities of 1238 who made the calculation to stay.

The acequia amin is a composite figure because the historical record preserves the institution but not the individual in this specific moment. He is disclosed as composite here so that readers who wish to trace the scholarship know where to find it: Burns (1984) is the foundational text.

## The *Justicia* of Aragon (institutional figure)

The *Justicia* of Aragon — the great judicial officer — is an institutional rather than individual figure in this era. The office was held by several men between 1137 and 1238; the specific *Justicia* who restrained James I in the Rodrigo de Lizana case (referenced in the chapter) is named in the Aragonese royal archive but not with sufficient documentation to reconstruct his individual character fully. He is rendered in the chapter as an institutional actor: someone doing what the office required, in conditions that required courage to do it.

The *Justicia*'s constitutional significance exceeds any individual who held the office. The *manifestación* — the right to interpose the court between royal violence and its object — is documented from the early thirteenth century. Its exercise against James I in the Lizana case is attested. The *Justicia*'s office would reach its fullest development in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, when it became the primary check on Aragonese royal power and the constitutional bulwark that successive kings tried, with varying success, to circumvent. Its abolition by Philip II of Spain in 1591 — after the affair of Antonio Pérez, when the *Justicia* sheltered a fugitive the king wanted — is the terminal condition of the pactisme system rendered in a single political act.

In 1238, the office was still building its precedents. The chapter renders it at the moment of its formation: not yet the famous institution it would become, but already a real constraint on the king who had just ridden into Valencia.
