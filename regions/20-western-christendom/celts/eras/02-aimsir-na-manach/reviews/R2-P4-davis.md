# R2-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel-2 Review: Notes — Aimsir na Manach

The notes correctly identify the knowledge system at method level as the chapter's analytical responsibility. The two places where I want this most carefully executed:

**Section 2 — The fili at method level:** The notes name the seven grades, the twelve years of training, the metrical systems, the satire as legal instrument. This is correct in inventory. But I need the chapter to show the practice operating — not just its outputs. Specifically: the training process itself. How did you learn to hold ten thousand lines? The answer involves the physical practice of recitation, the testing by a senior fili (an ollam), the progressive initiation into higher grades as competence is demonstrated. The bardic schools used darkness: students composed in unlit rooms, lying down, to force the composition entirely into the body's inner ear rather than the visual world. This is the Knowledge System at Method Level I am looking for — the specific physical practice that makes the abstract claim ("they held the culture's entire archive in memory") concrete and verifiable.

**Section 5 — The illuminated manuscript as synthesis:** The notes reference the Book of Kells as the synthesis object — interlace from metalwork, Latin text from the church, verse-structure from the fili. I want this rendered at the level of the illuminator's act. What was the intellectual experience of making the Book of Kells? The pigments came from specific sources: the blue from lapis lazuli (which traveled from Afghanistan via trade routes — the manuscript's very materiality connects to a world beyond Ireland), the red from red lead and kermes, the gold from thin-beaten foil. The craftsperson-monk who worked on the Chi Rho page was not decorating a text — they were making a theological argument in the logic of interlace: that the divine name cannot be traced to a beginning, that every line folds back into every other line, that the Word is as endless and interwoven as creation.

**One concern from the notes:** The open question about Brigid's treatment needs a clearer resolution before writing. The proposed approach — treating the overlap between the Christian saint and the pre-Christian goddess "as the Irish people treated it: as simply true" — is correct in principle. But the chapter needs to handle this at method level too. What was the theological logic by which this overlap was maintained? The Irish church did not have a Council that resolved this. The overlap persisted because the local community maintained both devotions simultaneously, and the church in Ireland was not yet powerful enough to insist on the distinction. This is the inside logic: not syncretism as a theological position but as a practical reality of a decentralized church adapting to an existing sacred landscape.
