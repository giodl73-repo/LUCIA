# Ryukyu — Mannichi — Ten Thousand Generations of Ships

We are the Ryukyu Kingdom — the island arc between Kyushu and Taiwan, the string of islands that bends southwest from Japan toward the Philippines — and our civilization was built on the premise that a small kingdom could prosper by being essential to everyone. We had no iron, no large-scale agriculture, no great natural resources. What we had was position: an archipelago at the crossroads of the East China Sea trade, equidistant from China, Japan, Korea, and Southeast Asia, with harbors that could shelter the ships of all of them. Our motto — carved on the great bell at Shuri Castle — was that Ryukyu is a "blessed land situated in the southern seas" that "is a blessed place where people gather from all over and where fine goods abound." We were the hub of the maritime East Asia trade, and we were proud of it.

We called our policy mannichi — ten thousand generations of ships — and we meant that the Ryukyu Kingdom's survival and prosperity depended on the continuous movement of ships, the continuous cultivation of relationships, the continuous demonstration that we were useful to everyone and threatening to no one. At our height in the fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries, Ryukyuan trading ships visited ports from Malacca to Japan to Korea to Southeast Asia in a trade network that connected goods from every part of the maritime world. Our King of Chūzan sent tribute to the Ming Emperor; we sent courtesy missions to the Japanese shogunate; we maintained relationships with Korea; and our merchants traded in the same ports as the Arab dhows and the Chinese junks.

## What Defined Them

**The tribute trade as diplomacy and commerce.** Ryukyu's relationship with the Ming dynasty — sending tribute missions to Fuzhou approximately twice yearly — was the foundation of the kingdom's commercial prosperity. The tribute relationship gave the Ryukyuan merchants access to Chinese ports and goods (silk, porcelain) on favorable terms; the goods they received in return for their tribute could be traded throughout East and Southeast Asia at profit. The ritual submission of the tribute system was simultaneously a commercial arrangement, and Ryukyu used it more intensively than any other tributary state.

**Shuri Castle and Ryukyuan culture.** Shuri Castle — the royal palace and administrative center — was the architectural expression of a culture that drew from both Chinese and Japanese forms while maintaining its own character. The Ryukyuan arts — the ryūkyūfu dance, the sanshin (a three-stringed instrument), the bingata textile dyeing tradition, the lacquerware that combined Chinese and Japanese techniques — were genuinely original syntheses. The great bell at Shuri, cast in 1458, bears the inscription that describes Ryukyu as a blessed land where all peoples gather. This was not propaganda; it was the kingdom's honest self-understanding.

**The entrepôt trade.** At the peak of Ryukyuan maritime trade (1430–1570), Ryukyuan ships served as the inter-regional middleman that connected China, Japan, Korea, and Southeast Asia in a trade network from which the kingdom extracted profit as broker. They carried Chinese silk and porcelain to Japan; Japanese silver and copper to China; Southeast Asian spices and pepper to Japan and China. No single commodity was Ryukyu's own; the trade itself was the product. This is a sophisticated economic strategy requiring precise knowledge of relative prices, reliable relationships with merchants in every port, and the diplomatic skill to be trusted by all parties.

**The dual subordination.** After the Satsuma invasion of 1609, Ryukyu operated in an extraordinary position: officially a tributary kingdom of the Ming (later Qing) dynasty, simultaneously the possession of the Satsuma domain of Japan (the Shimazu clan). The Ryukyuan kings continued to send tribute to Beijing, continued to maintain the trappings of independent sovereignty, and continued to dress in Ryukyuan court costume when meeting Chinese officials — while in fact implementing Satsuma policies and remitting tax revenue to Japan. This was not simple deception; it was the performance of sovereignty in the only form available to a small state between two large powers.

**The 1879 abolition.** The Meiji government's abolition of the Ryukyu Kingdom and its incorporation into Japan as Okinawa Prefecture was conducted over several years of resistance. The last Ryukyuan king, Shō Tai, was removed to Tokyo. The traditional governance was replaced by Japanese prefectural administration. The Ryukyuan nobles sent appeals to the Qing court asking for intervention; the Qing declined. The kingdom that had survived five centuries of careful diplomacy was ended by the Meiji state's systematic erasure of any alternative sovereignty within Japanese territory.

## The Chapters

**1 — Three Kingdoms:** Three lords control three districts of the main island. The ships still sail. The traders still come. But the kingdom is not yet whole, and the competition among the three wastes resources that unified sovereignty would multiply.

**2 — Shō Hashi Unifies:** He is the first king of all Ryukyu. He moves the capital to Shuri. The first Shō dynasty begins. The golden bell is ordered. The trading policy is systematized. Ten thousand generations of ships.

**3 — Ryukyuan Ships in Malacca:** The ship from Ryukyu arrives in Malacca harbor. The harbor master records it: a ship from Liuqiu (Ryukyu), carrying Chinese porcelain and Japanese copper. The captain asks about the price of pepper. Malacca is where everything meets; Ryukyu is part of everything.

**4 — Satsuma Invades:** The Shimazu forces land on Amami Oshima. They advance to Okinawa. The Ryukyuan forces cannot stop them. The king is taken to Kagoshima. He is returned to Shuri under conditions: Ryukyu will be Satsuma's. The tributary missions to China will continue. The kingdom will perform independence.

**5 — The Last King:** Shō Tai is taken to Tokyo. He is given a Japanese title and a pension. The Ryukyu Kingdom is over. In Okinawa, the people speak Ryukyuan languages (related to but distinct from Japanese), maintain their ancestral rituals (nuru priestess tradition), and remember what they were. The memory does not disappear with the kingdom.
