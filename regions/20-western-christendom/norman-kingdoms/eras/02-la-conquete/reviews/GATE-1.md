## Gate Assessment

**GATE-1 PASSED** — 28/30 exceeds the 24/30 threshold.

### Strengths

- The hook is specific and earns its weight: ravens before men, William's foot-by-foot reckoning of what he has won. The problem is planted in the opening and belongs to William's own calculations, not to a modern framing.
- The "grammar of fire" / castle-as-grammar engine is introduced with real force. The castle appears at the end not as an analogy but as the first structural act — "Always the castle." Sets up the material-object engine without announcing it.
- Voice discipline is high. The deliberate, measured prose with short declarative sentences matches Norman pragmatism without being cold. "England was about to be reorganized from the ground up. The Normans did not know how to do this yet." Two sentences that do enormous work.
- Honesty dimension strong: no romanticization of the conquest, no condemnation, no lingering on the dead for spectacle. The burning of villages is inside Norman logic.

### Concerns to Track

- **World score (4/5):** "The Normans understood the grammar of fire" and "the statements accumulated into a system" verge on authorial analysis. In the chapter, this engine should operate through scenes and people rather than through the author naming the mechanism. The note to the chapter writer: let the castle build the grammar, don't announce that you're building it.
- **Voice score (4/5):** A few sentences in the castle-grammar section feel more like essay than narrative. The chapter will need to sustain the spare-but-inhabited voice across 5,000 words without letting the structural analysis slide into abstraction.
