# BOARD-2 — Anglo-Latin and Vernacular Literature
**Reviewer Profile:** Specialist in Anglo-Latin learning, Old English literary tradition, and Alfred's translation program. Expertise in the Alfredian canon, the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*, and the relationship between Latin literacy and vernacular writing in the early medieval period. Works in the tradition of Michael Lapidge and Janet Bately.
**Chapter:** Se Dena Tíd — The Danish Time (Anglo-Saxons, Era 4)

---

## Assessment

The chapter's treatment of Alfred's translation program is the most substantive literary-historical claim it makes, and it gets it substantially right.

**On the five books:**

The list (Gregory's *Pastoral Care*, Bede's *Historia*, Orosius's *Histories*, Boethius's *Consolation*, Augustine's *Soliloquies*) is the standard scholarly list of the Alfredian canon, though attribution of the Orosius and the *Soliloquies* to Alfred himself rather than to his circle is debated. The chapter attributes all five to Alfred's "program" without claiming direct royal authorship of each — which is the defensible position. The Werferth/Gregory's *Dialogues* translation is correctly attributed and correctly identified as outside this core list.

**On the Preface to the *Pastoral Care*:**

The chapter quotes the Preface accurately: "It seems better to me, if it seems so to you, that we too should turn into the language that we can all understand certain books which are the most necessary for all men to know." This is a reliable translation. The interpretation — that Alfred is selecting a knowledge system for a specific audience — is well-grounded in the scholarly literature (see Janet Bately's work on the Alfredian translations and their intellectual coherence as a program).

**On the Boethius interpolations:**

The chapter describes Alfred adding to the *Consolation* "a metaphor" comparing thought to gathering wood. This is the metaphor from the Preface to Alfred's *Boethius* — but it appears in the Preface, not the body of the translation. The chapter correctly attributes it to Alfred but slightly misidentifies it as being in the *Soliloquies* preface: "The Preface to the *Soliloquies*... contains a passage that is not in Augustine. Alfred has added a metaphor. He compares the work of thought to gathering wood in a forest." The wood-gathering metaphor is from the Preface to the *Boethius*, not the *Soliloquies*. The *Soliloquies* preface does contain Alfredian additions, but the wood-gathering passage is *Boethius*. **Correction required** — swap the attribution: "The Preface to his *Boethius* — Alfred's translation of the *Consolation of Philosophy* — contains a passage that is not in the original..."

The quotation itself ("The builder's art is this: that from the materials of the forest he selects what he needs, and what he needs differs from what another needs") is a paraphrase rather than a direct translation. The actual passage is longer and more complex. The paraphrase captures the spirit accurately but should be presented as such — perhaps "Alfred writes, in the preface to his Boethius: that the builder selects from the forest what he needs, and what he needs differs from what another needs" — marking it as paraphrase rather than direct quotation with quotation marks.

**Overall:** Strong treatment of the literary materials. Two corrections required (attribution of wood-gathering passage; presentation as paraphrase rather than quotation).
