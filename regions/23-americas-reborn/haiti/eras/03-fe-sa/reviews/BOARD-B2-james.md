# BOARD Review — B-2: C.L.R. James (Historical Voice)
## Haiti, Era 3: Fè Sa — The Revolution

**Role:** Trinidadian historian, author of *The Black Jacobins: Toussaint L'Ouverture and the San Domingo Revolution* (1938, rev. 1963); foundational voice in the historiography of the Haitian Revolution
**Note:** C.L.R. James died in 1989. This review draws on his published positions and arguments from *The Black Jacobins* and his subsequent essays on the revolution.
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

## Structural Assessment

When I wrote *The Black Jacobins*, I argued that Toussaint was one of the great men of history — but I also argued that the revolution that produced him was not made by him. It was made by the enslaved people of Saint-Domingue, hundreds of thousands of them, and their capacity for organization and resistance was the fact that the planter class and, later, Napoleon could not process.

This chapter understands that. It opens not with Toussaint but with the ceremony. It places the lwa before the general. This is the correct order of precedence. The revolution was spiritual before it was military, and it was popular before it was commanded.

**What the chapter does that I would not have done but that is right:** The treatment of Toussaint's labor contract system — the acknowledgment that freed people were not returned to the freedom they understood when the contracts were instituted — is an honesty I did not fully extend in my own book. I was writing in 1938, with the urgency of anti-colonial politics pressing me, and I did not give sufficient space to the critique of Toussaint from the left, from below. This chapter handles it correctly: the contradiction is named without obscuring Toussaint's larger achievement, and the composite figure in figures.md gives the people's perspective on the contracts without reducing the revolution to its internal failures.

**On Dessalines:** My book treated Dessalines as Toussaint's inferior — more brutal, less sophisticated, less capable of the political complexity the moment required. I have reconsidered this since 1938. The chapter's treatment of Dessalines is better than mine was: it takes him seriously on his own terms, presents the massacre logic from inside his reasoning, and refuses to place him below Toussaint on a hierarchy of revolutionary merit. He did what the situation required, by his reading of the situation, and that reading was not stupid. It was the reading of a man who had survived the calculation of his own life's worth being made in a planter's ledger.

## One Challenge

The chapter does not engage with the question of what Toussaint intended by the 1801 Constitution's structure — specifically, whether he was buying time with France or genuinely believed he could maintain the relationship. My own reading, which I have defended across multiple editions, is that Toussaint understood that an independent break from France was not yet achievable in 1801 and that the Constitution was the best available position. The chapter handles this implicitly but might be strengthened by making Toussaint's own strategic reasoning visible — not as speculation but from his own account at Fort de Joux.

## Overall Assessment

The chapter passes the test I set in *The Black Jacobins* and then raises the bar. It is the inside account of the revolution that the revolution deserved and that most subsequent history has still not produced.

**Board recommendation:** APPROVE without further revision.
