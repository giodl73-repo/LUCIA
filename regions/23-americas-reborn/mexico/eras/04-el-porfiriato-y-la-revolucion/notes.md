# Notes — Production Bible
## Mexico Era 4: El Porfiriato y La Revolución (1876–1920)

---

## 1. Sources for This Era

### Tier 1 (Primary)
- **Plan de Ayala** (November 28, 1911) — Zapata's manifesto. Named the hacendados and the traitors; demanded return of ejido lands. The foundational text of the Zapatista movement.
- **Constitution of 1917** — Article 27 (ejido and subsoil rights), Article 123 (labor rights). The covenant that defined the revolution's promise and its gap.
- **Anenecuilco village records** — Pre-colonial and colonial-era land titles; the actual documents Zapata recovered. Archival (AGN, Archivo General de la Nación, Ramo Gobernación).
- Corridos of the Revolution — oral/print tradition circulating 1910–1920; "La Valentina," "Adelita," "El Corrido de Emiliano Zapata." These are primary historical evidence of popular memory.

### Tier 2 (Academic)
- **Alan Knight, *The Mexican Revolution* (2 vols., Cambridge, 1986)** — Standard scholarly account. Volume 1 covers the Porfiriato and the revolution's origins; Volume 2 covers the constitutionalist period and the Constitution of 1917.
- **John Womack, *Zapata and the Mexican Revolution* (Knopf, 1969)** — Definitive Zapata study. The Morelos agrarian context is fully developed. Essential for figures, Anenecuilco detail, Plan de Ayala.
- **Friedrich Katz, *The Life and Times of Pancho Villa* (Stanford, 1998)** — Villa counterpart to Womack. Northern revolutionary context.
- **Romana Falcón, *El agrarismo en Veracruz* (1977)** — Regional agrarian movements outside Morelos.
- **Paul Garner, *Porfirio Díaz: Profiles in Power* (Longman, 2001)** — The Porfiriato's political-economic system; the Científicos; railroad concessions.

### Tier 3 (Scholarly Secondary)
- **Octavio Paz, *The Labyrinth of Solitude* (1950)** — The Mexican national-character analysis. The Díaz-as-patriarch argument. Treat critically; Paz often speaks for the urban intellectual rather than the campesino.
- **Anita Brenner, *Idols Behind Altars* (1929)** — Art and the Revolution; Rivera, Orozco, Siqueiros. The muralist program as the state's appropriation of revolutionary imagery.
- **Arturo Warman, *We Come to Object: The Peasants of Morelos and the National State* (1980)** — The Morelos agrarian community perspective, with the distinction between their politics and the national revolutionary narrative.

### Tier 4 (Reference)
- Wikipedia for dates, maps, biographical facts — underlying academic sources preferred.

## 2. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The constitutional covenant — the promise inscribed in the Constitution of 1917 and its systematic non-delivery.

**Mechanism:** The Revolution destroyed the Porfiriato's order but did not redistribute its land. Each successive revolutionary government (Madero, Huerta, Carranza, Obregón) acknowledged the land demands in language while deferring or suppressing their fulfillment in practice. The ejido was restored as a legal category in 1917 but actual land redistribution remained minimal until the 1930s under Cárdenas (outside this era). The villages of Morelos operated as the measure of the covenant's breach: they could name what had been promised, they could name what had not been delivered, and they continued fighting beyond the Revolution's formal end.

**Terminal condition:** "Domestic covenant gap" — the state acknowledges the covenant in writing (Constitution of 1917, Article 27) but does not honor it in practice. Related to v1.6 Type 4 (Premise non-recognition) but distinct: the state that made the promise is the same state that fails to keep it, and the failure is not ignorance of the premises but political calculation (protecting hacienda interests through the new revolutionary elite). Zapata's assassination in 1919 does not close the terminal condition; it confirms it. The villages' claim outlives him.

**Secondary engine:** The Porfiriato's modernization paradox — foreign capital and railroad development produced growth and dispossession simultaneously, so that the "order and progress" that Díaz offered was inseparable from the land theft that made it possible.

## 3. Key Figures

### Emiliano Zapata (1879–1919)
Nahua campesino from Anenecuilco, Morelos. President of the village council at 29. Skilled horseman, known throughout Morelos as a man who could train horses — this is not incidental; it was his social standing before the revolution. He was not poor; he was a ranchero of middling status who had seen the hacienda system absorb his neighbors' land and calculated that it would reach him next. The Plan de Ayala is his clearest political statement. He was not a Marxist, not a liberal, not a nationalist in the Madero sense — he was an agrarian, defending a specific form of communal land tenure that had existed before the Spanish came. Assassinated at Chinameca hacienda, April 10, 1919, by soldiers acting under Carranza's orders. Warned it might be a trap; went anyway.

### Porfirio Díaz (1830–1915)
Zapotec from Oaxaca. Mestizo by identification, indigenous by origin — the irony is heavy and the opening should not editorialize on it. Fought heroically in the Reform War and against the French intervention (Cinco de Mayo, Puebla, 1862). Used his military reputation to seize power in 1876. Reelected continuously through managed elections. His ideology: positivism, "Order and Progress," the belief that Mexico needed a strong state to attract foreign capital and modernize. His instrument: the Científicos (his technocratic advisers, primarily from wealthy creole families). His method: the jefe político system, patronage networks at the state and municipal level that maintained order by absorbing local strongmen into the national hierarchy. Exiled May 1911 on the Ypiranga. Died in Paris, 1915.

### Francisco Madero (1873–1913)
Wealthy landowner from Coahuila. His father owned haciendas. He was not fighting for land — he was fighting for democracy, for effective suffrage, for the end of Díaz's rigged elections. His *La Sucesión Presidencial en 1910* (1908) launched the anti-reelectionist movement. He appealed to the middle class and the provincial elites who felt excluded from Díaz's inner circle. He never understood what Zapata was asking for; he thought the revolution could be constitutional without being agrarian. Assassinated February 22, 1913, in the coup led by Victoriano Huerta, backed by the U.S. ambassador Henry Lane Wilson.

### Pancho Villa (Francisco "Pancho" Villa, born Doroteo Arango, 1878–1923)
Chihuahuan ranchero turned bandit turned revolutionary general. Northern populist. His Division del Norte was the most powerful military force in Mexico in 1914. He was not primarily an agrarian; he was a caudillo whose politics were personal loyalty, military hierarchy, and hatred of the Porfirista elite. His relationship with Zapata was an alliance of convenience — the Aguascalientes Convention (1914) brought them together against Carranza, but their politics were different. Villa's terminal condition: defeated by Obregón at Celaya (1915) through new tactics (entrenchments, machine guns — a Mexican Verdun), his military power broken, reduced to guerrilla raids. Assassinated 1923. His relationship to the constitutional covenant: Villa cared about distributing land to soldiers, not restoring communal land tenure.

### Venustiano Carranza (1859–1920)
Coahuilan landowner and senator. Led the Constitutionalist army against Huerta. Wrote the Constitution of 1917 while fighting Zapata and Villa. The Constitution's agrarian article (27) was inserted over Carranza's resistance by the radical delegates at Querétaro — he was constitutionalizing a revolution he did not fully endorse. He ordered Zapata's assassination. Assassinated himself in 1920.

### The Soldaderas (Las Adelitas)
Women who traveled with the revolutionary armies — some as soldiers (coronelas, capitanas), most as camp followers who cooked, nursed, and maintained the social fabric of army life. The corrido "Adelita" is named for one. Soldadera is not a supporting role in the corrido tradition; she is the revolution's other face. The chapter should give the soldadera a body and a name beyond the corrido archetype.

### The Anenecuilco Village Council
Collective figure. The village elected Zapata to protect the records; the village council is the political unit that preceded and outlasted the individual figures. The chapter should return to them at the close.

## 4. Narrative Arc — Section Plan

### Section 1: The Tierra Caliente (Porfiriato, 1876–1910)
Open with the opening.md's scene (Zapata at Ayoxustla, Plan de Ayala, iron box) — then pull back to show the structural context the opening gestured at. The railroad arrives in Morelos in the 1880s. The sugar haciendas expand, because now the sugar can reach Mexico City in hours rather than days. The hacienda of Hospital needs more cane land; the village of Anenecuilco has cornfields on land it has held since before the Spaniards came. The legal mechanism: the Ley de Baldíos (1883) classifies communally-held land as "unused" because it is not enclosed, individually titled, or producing for the market. The surveying companies arrive. The villages lose.
*Engine at work:* railroad access → commodity demand → hacienda expansion → legal dispossession of ejido.
*Sensory ground:* the smell of sugarcane processing at Hospital, the cane rows taller than a man, the difference between a milpa (small, intercropped, family-scale) and a hacienda's monocrop field.

### Section 2: Order and Progress (Díaz's system)
The Porfiriato was not just Díaz. It was the Científicos advising him, the jefes políticos managing local order, the rurales enforcing it, the foreign investment (British in oil, American in railroads and mines, French in textiles) that gave the system its revenue base. Díaz was Zapotec from Oaxaca and had made himself the servant of a system that dispossessed people like his own. That irony needs one sentence, no more — the prose should not editorialize, just name it.
*Key figure:* Porfirio Díaz. His exile on the Ypiranga is the section's close.
*Sensory ground:* the telegraph line running alongside the railroad; the rurales on horseback outside a village.

### Section 3: The Grito Nuevo (1910–1911)
Madero publishes the Plan de San Luis. Díaz arrests him, releases him, he flees to Texas and calls for revolution. The uprising that follows is not what Madero planned. In Chihuahua, Villa and Pascual Orozco are fighting. In Morelos, Zapata is fighting. Their armies converge on Mexico City from different directions with different demands. Díaz falls. Madero becomes president. Zapata asks for the land. Madero asks for disarmament. The Plan de Ayala is the answer.
*Key figures:* Madero, Zapata, Orozco.
*Sensory ground:* the village of Ayoxustla, November 1911; the twenty-seven signatures; the moment the Plan is read aloud.

### Section 4: La Decena Trágica and the Hydra (1913–1914)
Huerta's coup and Madero's assassination. The revolution fractures: Constitutionalists (Carranza, Obregón) in the north; Zapatistas in the south; Villistas in Chihuahua. The United States intervenes at Veracruz (1914) and then backs Carranza. The Convention of Aguascalientes brings Zapata and Villa together briefly — two revolutionary movements that do not fully understand each other's politics, united by opposition to Carranza. Their joint occupation of Mexico City in December 1914 is the revolution's high-water mark and its last moment of coalition.
*Key figures:* Huerta (as force of reaction), Carranza, Obregón, Villa.
*Sensory ground:* the Constitutionalist army under Obregón uses trench warfare and machine guns at the Battle of Celaya (1915); Villa's cavalry charges are destroyed. The revolution is also changing warfare.

### Section 5: Querétaro (1916–1917)
Carranza convenes the Constitutional Congress at Querétaro. He wants a liberal constitution — effective suffrage, municipal freedoms. The radical delegates — workers, teachers, minor military figures — insert Articles 27 and 123. Article 27 restores the ejido as a legal category, makes communal land inalienable, and declares the subsoil (oil, minerals) national property. Article 123 establishes the eight-hour day, minimum wages, the right to strike. The Constitution of 1917 is more radical than anything its principal author intended.
*Sensory ground:* the Congress chamber at Querétaro; the argument between Carranza's delegates and the radicals; the final text, read aloud.

### Section 6: Chinameca (1918–1920)
Zapata is contained in Morelos. Carranza sends Colonel Jesús Guajardo to stage a defection — offering Zapata the impression that a Constitutionalist commander is joining the Zapatistas. Zapata's advisers warn him. He goes anyway to the hacienda of Chinameca on April 10, 1919. When he rides in, the soldiers fire. He dies in the courtyard.
The villages of Morelos hold. The ejido lands are not returned in 1920. The Constitution's promise is in writing. The fulfillment is deferred.
*Sensory ground:* the hacienda courtyard at Chinameca; the waiting; the shots.

### Section 7: Landing — The Covenant
The chapter closes by returning to the opening image: the iron box, the village records, the claim that has not expired. Anenecuilco in 1920 — what it looks like, who is in the council, what they are still fighting for. The corrido as the surviving form: Zapata's name is still being sung. The Constitution of 1917 is on paper. The covenant is still open.

## 5. Voice — Corrido-Narrative Register

The corrido is Mexico's oral-journalistic tradition: narrative ballads sung by troubadours who carried news of battles, assassinations, and betrayals from village to village. The corrido is not propaganda and not poetry in the Romantic sense — it is reporting in verse, with the named figure as its refrain anchor ("Zapata no se rindió / Zapata no se murió"). Its prose equivalent is: declarative forward motion, third-person facticity building to refrain-weight, the period as cadence-break. No editorializing — the corrido does not judge; it records what everyone knows. The analytical content arrives embedded in the factual sequence.

**Pause mechanism:** the period. Not the em-dash, not the semicolon — the full stop, and then a new sentence that advances the argument.

**Register markers:**
- Named figures as anchors: Zapata, Villa, Madero, Carranza, Obregón — each named when they appear, not "the leader" or "the general."
- The land as grammatical subject: the ejido, the milpa, the tierra — not "agrarian resources."
- Sentence rhythm: medium-length declarative building to a short, weighted close.
- Zero em-dashes (symptom of voice discipline).

**Validation note:** This is a new register candidate — no prior chapter in the Voice Spectrum has validated it. The corrido-narrative is distinct from: Griot-Cadence (first-person authority, West African oral tradition); Oral-Tradition (proverb-weight, Deep Time); Prophetic-Migratory (gathering momentum toward eschatological horizon). The corrido is specific to the Mexican revolutionary tradition and its reportage function. Promotion to Voice Spectrum requires chapter-length validation.

## 6. Panel-1 Pressure Points and Responses

**Tuchman:** Villa's northern populism vs. Zapata's agrarian specificity. Response: Section 4 will give Villa his own political identity — the caudillo model, personal loyalty, military hierarchy — and show at Celaya the moment his military approach breaks. The chapter will hold both by showing them at the Aguascalientes Convention: allied against Carranza, their politics distinct.

**Ibn Khaldun:** The causal mechanism connecting railroad to dispossession. Response: Section 1's engine will trace railroad access → sugar demand → hacienda expansion → Ley de Baldíos application → village dispossession, at the level of mechanism not assertion.

**Achebe:** Indigenous specificity — Nahua people of Morelos, not just "campesinos." Response: Anenecuilco will be named as a Nahua village; the milpa will be connected to Nahua agricultural knowledge; the village council as a surviving form of governance will anchor the close. The corrido tradition's own claim on Zapata's memory (not the muralist-nationalist absorption) will carry the landing.

**Davis:** Epistemological dimension — the knowledge encoded in the communal land system and the village records. Response: Section 1 will show what the Ley de Baldíos actually did: it refused to recognize the village's knowledge-form of land ownership. The iron box will be described at the level of what it contained — not just "land titles" but the specific knowledge (boundary markers, water rights, seasonal use patterns) that the documents encoded.

**Kapuściński:** Heat, smell, body of Zapata on horseback, the texture of Morelos tierra caliente. Response: Section 1 will open with the sugarcane landscape. Zapata will appear as a horseman in Section 3, physically present. Section 6 (Chinameca) will be the chapter's most sensory passage — the hacienda courtyard, the waiting, the shots.

## 7. Open Questions

1. Was the Adelita of the corrido a specific person? Most scholars say the name is composite/generic. The chapter should give a soldadera a specific role without inventing a biography that does not exist.
2. How much land was actually redistributed under Article 27 before 1920 (within this era's scope)? The answer is: very little. Cárdenas (1934–1940) redistributes the majority. The chapter should be honest about this without projecting the 1930s into the 1920 close.
3. The Yucatán dimension: the henequen monoculture and Maya workers. One paragraph in Section 2 is appropriate — enough to show the Porfiriato's dispossession was national in scope, not limited to Morelos.
