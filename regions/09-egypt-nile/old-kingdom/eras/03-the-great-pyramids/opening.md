# The Age of the Great Pyramids

The foreman's name was scratched into the stone before the stone left the ground.

Merer's crew had been hauling since before dawn, since the sky over the eastern desert was still the color of old copper and the plateau of Rostau was a darkness above them broken only by the fires of the night watch. There were forty of them in the phyle, the group the scribes called "Friends of Khufu," and they had been at this work for three months of the four-month rotation, and they would go home when the water dropped and the fields needed them, and they would come back when the flood rose again, because this was what the inundation season was for. The river was high. The fields were under water. There was nothing to plant. There was everything to build.

The stone they moved that morning weighed perhaps two and a half tons. It had come from the quarries at Tura, across the river, floated on barges during the flood when the water reached close to the plateau's edge and the harbor basins filled and the whole logistics of the operation turned on the simple fact that the Nile, every year, without fail, rose and carried and receded and left the black earth behind. The stone was limestone, fine-grained, the white casing stone that would cover the finished surface and catch the first light of Ra each morning and throw it back across the valley so that the pyramid would burn like a second sun on the horizon. Merer's crew had names for the stones the way sailors have names for winds. This one they would wrestle up the ramp and set into the course that the surveyors had marked, and the surveyors' marks were precise to a fraction of a finger's width, because this was not approximate work. This was the work that held the world together.

The pyramid of Khufu was not a monument. The men who built it would not have understood the word. It was the instrument by which the king, when he died, would rejoin the sun. His body would lie in the chamber at the pyramid's heart, preserved, fed by the daily offerings the priests would bring to the mortuary temple at the pyramid's eastern face, and his ka and his ba would rise through the stone along the ascending passage and emerge into the sky and join Ra on the solar barque that crossed the heavens each day and passed through the darkness of the Duat each night and rose again each morning. The pyramid was the ramp. The king was the passenger. The destination was the sun. And if the king did not reach the sun, then ma'at would weaken, and if ma'at weakened, then the flood might not come, and if the flood did not come, then the black earth would not be renewed, and the grain would not grow, and the people would starve, and the order that held the Two Lands together would unravel into isfet, into chaos, into the darkness that pressed at the edges of the created world.

So Merer's crew hauled the stone. Not because they were forced. Because the stone was necessary.

The scribes who tracked the labor left records. Papyrus fragments found at Wadi al-Jarf, on the Red Sea coast, carry the diary of a man named Merer who managed a crew of two hundred men transporting limestone from Tura to Giza in the reign of Khufu. He recorded the work in columns: the day, the destination, the cargo. He did not record wonder. He did not record complaint. He recorded logistics, because the logistics were the point, and the point was the pyramid, and the pyramid was the mechanism by which the cosmos continued to function. The tone of Merer's papyrus is the tone of a man doing sacred work and calling it Tuesday.

On the stones themselves, the crews left their marks. Red ochre, painted in hieratic script on blocks that would be hidden once the next course was laid: "Friends of Khufu." "Drunkards of Menkaure." "The Crew that is the Vigor of the White Crown." These were not the marks of slaves. They were the marks of men who had organized themselves into gangs and named those gangs with the competitive pride of men who haul together and eat together and sleep in the same long barracks at the foot of the plateau and argue about whose phyle moved more stone this week. The archaeological record at the workers' village southeast of the pyramid shows bakeries capable of producing thousands of loaves a day, breweries, a field hospital where broken bones were set and healed, evidence of meat distribution that included beef, which was not common food. These men were fed. They were housed. They were treated when they were hurt. And they wrote their names on the stones.

The pyramid rose. Course by course, season by season, flood by flood. Twenty years, the later sources say, though the precision of the number matters less than the rhythm: the flood came, the crews came, the stone moved, the courses climbed, and each year the structure on the plateau grew closer to the sky. The base covered thirteen acres. The sides were aligned to true north within a margin that modern instruments can barely improve upon. Two million three hundred thousand blocks, give or take, each one quarried and shaped and transported and placed by men whose names, some of them, survive on the stones they laid.

When it was finished, the Great Pyramid of Khufu stood 146 meters above the plateau. It was the tallest structure human beings had built. It would remain the tallest structure human beings had built for three thousand eight hundred years.

Merer's crew did not know this. They knew the flood, and the stone, and the ramp, and the name they had painted on the block before it disappeared into the course. They knew that the work was necessary because the king was the hinge between the human world and the divine, and the pyramid was the hinge between the king and the sun, and the sun was the hinge between today and tomorrow. They knew that ma'at held, because the flood came, because the flood always came.

They hauled the stone. The sun rose. The work continued.
