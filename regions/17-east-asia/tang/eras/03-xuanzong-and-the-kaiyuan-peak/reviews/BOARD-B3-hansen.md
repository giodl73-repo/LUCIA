## B-3 — Valerie Hansen — Board Review of The High Tang

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Silk Road trade networks, Sogdian merchant communities, foreign religions in Tang China, Dunhuang manuscript evidence, material culture of cosmopolitan Chang'an.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The description of Sogdian merchants and Hu restaurants in the West Market is consistent with archaeological and documentary evidence. Sogdian communities in Chang'an are well-documented in tomb epitaphs and the Dunhuang manuscripts.
- The term "nang flatbreads" is correct — this Central Asian bread style is documented in Tang sources under the term hu bing (胡饼, "foreign flatbreads").
- The kaiyuan tongbao description is accurate. The coin was indeed standardized and circulated widely, though calling it a medium that reached "from Guangzhou to the Tarim Basin" is a slight overstatement — in the Tarim oasis cities, silk and local currencies also circulated alongside Tang copper cash.
- The Nestorian (Church of the East) presence in Chang'an is accurately described. The Daqin monastery is documented; the Nestorian Stele (erected 781) confirms the community's presence and its self-presentation as the "Luminous Religion" (景教, Jingjiao). The Syriac liturgy detail is supported by the stele and by manuscript evidence from Dunhuang.
- The Zoroastrian temple in Chang'an is documented in Tang administrative records. The placement "three wards from the imperial palace" is a reasonable approximation based on the archaeological evidence, though the exact ward location is debated.
- The Manichean presence in Chang'an is documented but was smaller and more restricted than the Nestorian or Zoroastrian communities. Grouping them equally may slightly overstate their visibility.
- The statement that "Islam was known, and Arab envoys came and were received" is accurate. The first Arab embassy to Tang China is recorded in 651, and subsequent embassies are documented through the 8th century. A mosque may have existed in Chang'an by the mid-8th century, though the evidence is disputed.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's framing of Chang'an's cosmopolitanism as a product of confidence rather than idealism is consistent with the scholarly consensus. The Tang court's engagement with foreign religions was pragmatic and political, not ideological.
- The description of the West Market as a site of international commerce is accurate, though the chapter should note that the West Market was specifically the more international of the two official markets (the East Market served a more domestic, aristocratic clientele).
- The Silk Road's disruption by the An Lushan rebellion and the Tibetan expansion into the Hexi Corridor is accurately described and is one of the major consequences of the rebellion for China's western connections.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The Sogdian merchants are treated as people with agency and specific origins, not as generic "Silk Road traders." The connection from Samarkand to Chang'an is specific and correct.
- The foreign religions are described in their own terms (the "Luminous Religion," the Zoroastrian sacred fire) rather than simply labeled.
- The kaiyuan tongbao is used as evidence of institutional capacity, not just as a trade detail. This is the right analytical move.

**What needs correction:**
- The Persian glass merchant, the Silla Korean ginseng seller, the Arab frankincense merchant, and the Uyghur horse trader in the West Market passage are individually plausible but presented as simultaneous. This is a composite scene, not a documented moment. The chapter should not present it as something Du Fu specifically encountered. The current phrasing ("In the market, a Persian could sell glass...") uses the conditional appropriately and is acceptable.
- No factual corrections required. The trade and religious details are within the range of current scholarship.

**Verdict:** APPROVED (no corrections required; minor observations noted for the author's awareness)
