# Panel Review — Round 1
## P-1 Barbara Tuchman | Opening Review

**Era:** The Island Expansion | **Culture:** Arawak / Lokono
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The opening works. That is the first thing, and it is not a small thing — more openings than I would like to count fail the most basic test, which is making the reader want the second paragraph. This one earns it. Kurima is loading a canoe, and I want to know what happens when he reaches the island. That pull is genuine, and it is internal to the story, not a manufactured cliffhanger.

What I find particularly successful is the management of the obligation paragraph. The author has taken what could have been an exposition section — "here is how Lokono society works" — and made it physical. Obligation as a "sensation, the way you understand weight by lifting it." That sentence does the work of three paragraphs of anthropological background and does it without removing the reader from Kurima's perspective. I marked it on first read as the chapter's structural pivot point. The reader understands the network before they are told what the network is.

The canoe-paddling passage with the cousin is restrained and effective. No dialogue, no explanation — just the shared rhythm that means two people have paddled together since childhood. That kind of scene requires confidence, the willingness to trust the reader to fill the silence. The author has that confidence here.

**My concern:** The opening is strong but slightly too even. The narrative is building steadily toward the island's appearance, which is the right direction, but the build is almost too smooth. Real crossings have texture — a moment when the current catches the bow wrong, when the wind shifts, when the cousin says something that reveals the stakes differently. The single crisis or complication that threatens the smooth forward motion would make the landing on the island more earned. Right now Kurima faces no resistance, internal or external. His doubt — "he was not yet sure which he was" (river person or ocean person) — is introduced early and then somewhat recedes. That doubt should have one more flare before the island appears. It would deepen the arrival.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening announces that the network grows through "accumulated decisions of young men and women who went to see." But we only see Kurima going. The chapter will need to carry the weight of showing how one young man's decision ramifies into the network's expansion — or the opening has made a promise the chapter can't keep with a single protagonist. How does the chapter move from Kurima's crossing to the civilization-scale migration?
