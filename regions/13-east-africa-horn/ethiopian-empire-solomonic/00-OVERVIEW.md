# Negusa Nagast — "King of Kings"

The Solomonic dynasty of Ethiopia rested on a claim that no other royal house in the world has made with the same combination of theological sophistication and political consequence: descent from the union of King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba. The Kebra Nagast — "The Glory of Kings," written in Ge'ez in the fourteenth century but claiming to transmit a tradition of great antiquity — narrates this founding story: the Queen of Sheba (Makeda in the Ethiopian tradition) visits Solomon in Jerusalem, conceives a son named Menelik, and her son later travels to Jerusalem to meet his father and returns to Ethiopia with the Ark of the Covenant. From that moment, the Ethiopian kings are the guardians of the Ark, their dynasty is the continuation of the Davidic line, and Ethiopia is the chosen nation of the God of Israel. The Negusa Nagast — the King of Kings — is not simply a monarch; he is the living expression of God's covenant with His chosen people.

This claim, maintained without interruption from 1270 CE to 1974 CE — and present in Ethiopian national identity before and after that formal period — shaped every aspect of Ethiopian political culture, foreign relations, and theological self-understanding. The Ethiopian empire resisted the Islamic states of the Horn for centuries on the argument that it was the guardian of a sacred inheritance that could not be surrendered. It resisted Italian colonialism in 1896 at the Battle of Adwa — the only African army to defeat a European colonial force in the colonial era — on the combined grounds of political sovereignty and divine mandate. When Haile Selassie I, the last Solomonic emperor, was deposed by the Derg military junta in 1974, the tradition did not end; it emigrated, in the bodies and faith of the Rastafari movement, to Jamaica, Britain, and beyond.

## What Defined Them

**The Kebra Nagast and the Solomonic claim.** The Kebra Nagast is not simply a royal genealogy; it is a theological manifesto arguing that the center of sacred history has always been Ethiopia. The text rewrites the entire narrative of the Hebrew Bible and the Christian New Testament from an Ethiopian perspective: the God of Israel chose Ethiopia as his true homeland; the Ark was given to Ethiopia because Ethiopia was worthy; the Christian faith arrived in Ethiopia through the Ethiopian eunuch baptized by Philip in Acts 8 — and this is confirmed by the presence of the Ark. The claim is circular in the sense that it is self-referential, but it was held with genuine conviction by Ethiopian Christians for seven centuries and it shaped their political culture more profoundly than any external institution.

**The church-state relationship as the Ethiopian constitution.** The relationship between the Negusa Nagast and the Ethiopian Orthodox Church was the defining constitutional structure of the empire. The church provided the theological legitimacy — the Kebra Nagast, the coronation ceremony, the ongoing ritual of royal sanctification — and the state provided the protection and patronage that allowed the church to maintain its monasteries, its schools, its manuscript tradition, and its missionary activity. When the two were in alignment, the empire was strong. When they conflicted — as in the sixteenth-century debates about the nature of Christ's divine and human natures, which split the Ethiopian church into factions — the political consequences were severe. The church was not the state's instrument; it was the state's partner, with its own institutional power and its own capacity to delegitimize royal authority.

**The Battle of Adwa.** On March 1, 1896, Emperor Menelik II's army — approximately 100,000 fighters — defeated a 17,000-strong Italian expeditionary force at Adwa in the Battle of Adwa. It was the most decisive military defeat of a European colonial army in the colonial era, and it preserved Ethiopia's independence when every other African state had been colonized. Adwa was not luck; it was the consequence of Menelik's political skill in unifying diverse Ethiopian forces, his diplomatic skill in importing modern weapons (including artillery) from France and Russia, and his military skill in choosing the terrain and moment of battle. Ethiopia's independence after Adwa became a symbol for African peoples everywhere under colonial rule. That Haile Selassie invoked this legacy when appealing to the League of Nations after the Italian invasion of 1935–36, and that the League failed to act, is one of the most consequential failures of the international order between the wars.

**The Oromo expansion and the limits of empire.** From the mid-sixteenth century onward, the Oromo people — a pastoral and agropastoral population of the eastern African highlands — expanded north, west, and south in a demographic and military movement that permanently reshaped the Ethiopian highlands' ethnic geography. The Oromo expansion was not simply migration; it was organized under the gada system (an age-grade governance structure) and involved the incorporation of diverse peoples into an Oromo framework. The Solomonic empire and the Oromo world coexisted, competed, and ultimately merged in complicated ways: Oromo generals served at the Gondar court; Oromo queens influenced the succession; the imperial heartland gradually shifted south toward Oromo territories. The modern Ethiopian state is as much an Oromo creation as a Solomonic one, though the Solomonic narrative has historically dominated.

**Haile Selassie and the global legacy.** Haile Selassie I — Ras Tafari Makonnen, crowned emperor in 1930 as the 225th Solomonic monarch — became one of the most internationally recognized leaders of the twentieth century: a symbol of African sovereignty at the League of Nations, the organizer of the Organization of African Unity, a figure of religious veneration to the Rastafari movement in the Caribbean and diaspora who understood him as the returned messiah. His actual governance was more complicated — his feudal land policies, his response to the 1973 famine, his suppression of political reform — but his symbolic position was real and consequential. The Derg's revolution ended the Solomonic empire; the Rastafari tradition carries its theological legacy globally.

## The Chapters

**1 — Yekuno Amlak's Restoration:** 1270. The last Zagwe king has been displaced. A monk in a highland monastery has validated the claim: this man descends from Solomon. The Kebra Nagast is being compiled. The theological charter of the next seven centuries is being written now.

**2 — Ahmed Gran:** The jihad army of Ahmed ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi (called Gran — "the left-handed") has conquered most of the Ethiopian highlands. The emperor is in the mountains with a small force. A Portuguese detachment with firearms is three weeks away.

**3 — Gondar:** Fasiladas has built a castle on the hill above the city. It does not look like an Ethiopian church; it looks like a European castle, with crenellations and towers. Ethiopia has been engaging with Portuguese and other European contacts for a century. The architecture shows it. The theology inside is still entirely Ethiopian.

**4 — Adwa:** Menelik II is watching the Italian column through his field glass. He knows the terrain better than they do. He has 100,000 fighters. He has artillery. He waits for the moment when the Italian command makes its predictable mistake, and then he moves.

**5 — Haile Selassie at the League:** 1936. The emperor of Ethiopia is speaking to the League of Nations in Geneva. He is asking the world's nations to enforce their own covenant against aggression. The answer will be no. The speech is being recorded. It will be played in Jamaica, and in London, and in New York, and the people who hear it will know that the Negusa Nagast spoke and the world failed to listen.
