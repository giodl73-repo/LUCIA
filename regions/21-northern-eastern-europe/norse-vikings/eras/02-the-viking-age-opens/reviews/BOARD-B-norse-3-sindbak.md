# BOARD — B-norse-3 — Soren Sindbak — Board Review of Vikingalond

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Viking-age trade networks, archaeology of Scandinavian trading settlements, material culture of commerce and exchange.

---

## Historical Accuracy

**Trade network assessment:**
- Kaupang as a trading site in the Vestfold region: correct. The Skre excavations (1998-2003) confirm it as a production and trade center active from the late 8th century.
- Birka and Hedeby as major trading centers: correct. Both are archaeologically well-attested for this period.
- Staraya Ladoga as a Scandinavian-connected settlement from the 750s: correct. The dendrochronological dating and the Scandinavian artifact assemblage are well-established.
- The Volga route to Bulgar and the Caspian: correctly described. The route's importance in the early Viking Age is confirmed by the dirham evidence.
- The Dnieper route to Constantinople: correctly identified as the alternative southern route.

**Economic mechanisms:**
- Hack-silver and weight economy: correctly described. The Norse operated a weight-based silver economy in which the origin or form of the silver was irrelevant; only weight mattered. The chapter's description of scales and weights in graves is accurate.
- The "80,000 Islamic silver coins" on Gotland: this figure is in the right order of magnitude. Some estimates run higher (over 100,000 individual coins found across Scandinavia, with Gotland as the single largest concentration). The figure is defensible.
- The portage description: accurate. The Norse portaged ships between river systems, and the ship design (light, flexible hull) enabled this. Archaeological evidence for portage routes is growing.

**Dorestad:**
- The raids on Dorestad (834-837) are correctly dated. The description of Dorestad as "the richest market in northern Europe" is slightly overstated by this period. Dorestad was declining by the 830s, partly due to flooding of the Rhine distributaries. The Norse were raiding a town that was already past its peak. This does not change the narrative significantly, but a minor qualification would be more accurate.

## Cultural Authenticity

The integration of trading and raiding as a single network phenomenon is the chapter's strongest analytical contribution. The sentence "The distinction between east and west, between trader and raider, between viking and merchant, existed in the victims' records but not in the Norse world itself" is an accurate summary of the current scholarly understanding. This is not a new argument, but it is correctly stated and well-placed.

## What the Chapter Gets Right

- The unified network model (western raiding and eastern trading as two expressions of the same maritime culture) is correct.
- The self-reinforcing logic of raiding escalation is economically sound.
- The treatment of the thrall trade as integral to the Norse economy, not a marginal activity, is correct. Human trafficking was a core component of the eastern trade especially.
- The description of the Oseberg burial as containing both western and eastern trade goods is correct and supports the unified network argument.

## What Needs Correction

1. **Minor:** "the silver mines of the Abbasid caliphate" — as noted, the silver was primarily from Central Asian mines (Panjshir, Transoxiana). Suggest: "the silver of the Abbasid lands" or "the Abbasid silver economy."

2. **Minor:** Dorestad's characterization as "the richest market in northern Europe" should be qualified for the 830s. By that date, the site was already experiencing flooding and decline. Perhaps: "the great Frankish trading town on the Rhine, still one of the wealthiest markets in northern Europe though its best years had passed."

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (2 minor corrections)
