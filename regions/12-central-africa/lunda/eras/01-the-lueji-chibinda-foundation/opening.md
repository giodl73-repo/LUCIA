# The Lueji-Chibinda Foundation

She held the bracelet in both hands.

It was not large. A band of copper worked in the old way, hammered and twisted until it held its shape, worn smooth along the inner curve where it had rested against her father's wrist and her grandfather's before him and back through every Ruund chief who had held the authority of this country. It smelled of skin and of the river, of the red laterite soil of the Kasai watershed where the headwaters ran in braided channels through the savanna grass. In the dry season, when the grass stood yellow and stiff and the animals moved toward the remaining water, the bracelet seemed to hold that smell too — the particular dryness of a land that would be green again in four months and was not green now. The lukanu did not look like much, if you did not know what it was. If you knew, you could not look at anything else.

Lueji knew what it was. She had watched her father wear it for as long as she could remember, watched the way lesser men's eyes moved toward it when he spoke and then moved away, deferential, the acknowledgment happening below the level of words. She had held it once before, when he was very ill and could not rise, and she had set it down again when he recovered. Now she held it because there was no one else to hold it.

Her brothers were gone. This is how the story tells it: that Chinguli and Chinyama were away, or that they had quarreled with their father before his death, or that their temperament was wrong for rule — the tradition carries several reasons, which may mean the reasons were all partially true. What matters in the story is not why they were absent but that they were absent, and she was present, and the lukanu was in her hands.

She wore it on her wrist as her father had worn it. She heard cases. In the mornings, when the smoke from the cooking fires still hung over the compound, men came to her with disputes over cattle and inheritance and the rights of neighbors at the river's edge, and she sat and listened and spoke the judgment that carried the authority of the lukanu and not merely of a woman who held it. She performed the rituals required of the one who wore it: the ceremonies that connected the living authority of the chief to the world of the ancestors who had held this authority before. She ruled. The tradition does not present this as unusual. The lukanu had come to her through her blood, through the line of chiefs, and she held it because she was the one who could.

Then the hunter arrived.

It was at the edge of the dry season's last month, when the rivers had shrunk to their lowest and the grass was brittle underfoot and the animals were thinner than they would be after the rains. A man came to the compound from the east — from across the boundary of Ruund country, from the direction of the Luba lands. He was lean and dark and he moved the way men move who have been on foot for many days, with the particular economy of a body that has learned to cover distance without spending itself. He carried a bow and the tools of a hunter. He had come to trade for provisions, or to ask to hunt in Ruund country, or perhaps simply because the tracking had taken him this far — the tradition is not precise about his intention, only about his arrival.

His name was Chibinda Ilunga. He was the son of Kalala Ilunga, the founding hero of the Luba, and through that lineage he carried what the Luba world called the bulopwe — the sacred principle of kingship, the quality of legitimate authority that the Luba understood as descending from the first king and living in those of his blood. This was not a credential in the way a title is a credential. It was a recognized quality. The people of the region knew what bulopwe looked like, could recognize it in a man's bearing, in the way he conducted himself when tested, in the particular kind of calm that comes from certainty about one's own authority. Chibinda Ilunga had not come to the Ruund country to rule. He had come hunting. But the bulopwe came with him.

Lueji saw it.

The tradition does not elaborate on the moment of recognition — the oral record is careful here, preserving the outcome without staging the scene in detail. What it preserves is this: that she recognized in him the bulopwe, and that she understood what two authorities, held together, could constitute. She had the territorial-ancestral authority of the Ruund in the bracelet on her wrist. He had the sacred-spiritual authority of the Luba in his blood. Neither form alone was sufficient for what she had in mind.

She invited him to stay. He stayed. They married. And then Lueji did the thing the tradition has remembered across four centuries and through the entire span of the Lunda empire that followed: she removed the lukanu from her own wrist and placed it on his.

This is not presented as a moment of subordination. This is the founding act. The tradition knows the difference. Lueji is not surrendering authority to Chibinda Ilunga. She is constituting the kingdom — she, with her legitimate right to hold the lukanu, is the one with the power to transfer it, and she is choosing to transfer it, and the choosing is the act of sovereignty. A woman who is merely a wife does not found a kingdom. A woman who holds the lukanu and decides what it means does.

She did not disappear from the story when the bracelet left her wrist. Her lineage would continue to hold the Ruund claim that underwrote the Lunda state's legitimacy. The Mwaant Yav — the title that Chibinda Ilunga's successors would bear — held his authority not simply from the Luba sacred lineage but from the Ruund territorial authority that Lueji's transmission had grafted onto it. The women of her blood would hold ceremonial and political roles in the Lunda state for as long as the state existed. She founded the kingdom. She remained part of its constitution.

What the bracelet moved was not the woman's authority but the kingdom's center. The Kasai watershed below the dry-season stars. The braided rivers waiting for the rain. The empire that would reach from Angola to Zambia to the eastern Congo had not yet been built, but its logic had been established: authority travels in objects, and objects move on human wrists, and the world grows where a woman chooses it should.

The bracelet moved from her wrist to his.

The kingdom began.
