# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Targeted Review of Production Bible

## Focus: Cultural Voice and Inside Perspective

The notes understand the voice requirements. But I want to flag three risks that are not yet addressed.

## Risk 1: The Enslaved-Labor Section

Section 3 will describe Amina's campaigns as securing the supply of enslaved people for the urban economy. This is the chapter's most delicate passage. The temptation will be to either moralize (stepping outside the system to judge it) or to sanitize (avoiding the specific details of human extraction). Both fail the Achebe Test.

The correct approach: present the system as the system understood itself. The Hausa city-states operated within a framework where enslaved labor was an economic and social category with its own structure — not a monolithic condition. Some enslaved people worked in the dye pits. Some were domestic. Some became soldiers. Some were ransomed. Some were traded south to north as commodities. The system had gradations, and the people within it understood those gradations. Present them. Do not flatten the system into a single moral category, and do not excuse it by describing the gradations. Let the system speak, and let the reader judge.

## Risk 2: The Bori Section

The notes correctly identify the risk of framing Bori as a "pre-Islamic survival." But there is a subtler risk: framing the mosque-Bori relationship as a tension that needs to be resolved. For the Hausa city-states in this era, the coexistence WAS the resolution. The sarki patronized both. The population moved between them. The question "how did they reconcile Islam and Bori?" is an outsider's question. The inside answer is: they did not need to reconcile them, because they were not experienced as contradictory by most people most of the time. The chapter must inhabit that lived experience, not the theological discomfort that outsiders (including the Fulani reformers) would later impose on it.

## Risk 3: Leo Africanus

The notes plan to use Leo Africanus in Section 2 as "a character passing through, a witness." Good. But Leo was not a neutral observer. He was a North African Muslim man of letters captured by Christian pirates, converted (or not), working for the Pope, writing an account of Africa for a European audience. His descriptions of Kano and Katsina are valuable for physical detail but carry his particular cultural position. When he describes Kano's leather and cloth trade, he is describing what a North African merchant would notice and value. When he describes the king's court, he is measuring it against courts he knew.

If Leo appears, his position must be visible. He is not confirming the Hausa cities' importance — they do not need his confirmation. He is a man passing through, seeing what his training prepared him to see. Frame his descriptions as what THEY ARE: one man's observations, culturally situated, useful for detail, not authoritative about meaning.

## The Dyer's Knowledge

I pressed this point in Panel-1 and the notes address it, but I want to be specific: the moment of expertise I am asking for is not a dramatic decision. It is a quiet moment of judgment — the dyer assessing the fermentation by smell or color or feel, the knowledge that distinguishes a master from an apprentice. This is intellectual labor. The chapter must treat it as such. The dyer is not a worker. He is a specialist whose knowledge was worth guarding behind guild walls. Show the knowledge, and the guild walls make sense.
