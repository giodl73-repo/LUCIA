# Era 2: The Peak
## Samanids — Ismail Samani and His Successors (c. 875–943 CE)

---

The poet had been standing outside the secretary's room for the better part of an hour when the door finally opened and the secretary came out carrying a lamp, even though it was the middle of the afternoon. The lamp was a habit. The secretary, whose name was Abu Abdallah al-Jayhani, was the kind of man who thought better in the presence of flames.

"Come in," he said, not looking at the poet. "Sit."

The room smelled of paper and sesame oil and the particular sourness of a man who had been working since before sunrise. The table was dense with documents: petitions from Nishapur, grain tallies from the Merv district, a letter from the Abbasid caliph's vizier in Baghdad that had been unfolded and refolded so many times it was beginning to separate along its creases. Al-Jayhani moved the letter without looking at it and set the lamp down in its place.

"You want to know about the stipend," al-Jayhani said.

The poet — his name was Rudaki, or at least that was what they called him in Samarkand, where he had come from, and he was said to be blind, though the way he navigated the room you would not have guessed it — said that was not why he had come. He had come because of a word.

Al-Jayhani looked at him.

"The amir," Rudaki said, "asked me last month to compose a poem for the occasion of his receiving the caliphate's acknowledgment of his authority. I wrote it in Arabic. He thanked me. Then he asked me what the word would be in Persian."

A silence.

"What word?" al-Jayhani said.

"Sovereignty," said Rudaki. "He wanted to know what sovereignty was in Persian."

The answer, of course, was *farr*. The luminous divine grace that settled on kings who had earned it, that left kings who had not — visible, felt, the organizing principle of Iranian kingship across a thousand years of Zoroastrian theology and pre-Islamic epic. The word was not difficult to find. Any educated Persian knew it. But the amir of Khorasan and Transoxiana, Ismail ibn Ahmad al-Samani — who had recently destroyed the Saffarid rival armies on the eastern steppe, who had just received from Baghdad the formal recognition that made him the undisputed master of the Iranian east — had been raised in a world where governance was written in Arabic and theological argument was conducted in Arabic and the highest compliment you could pay a poet was that his Arabic did not sound like a foreigner's. The question was not about vocabulary. It was about something else.

Al-Jayhani understood this. He was the author of a great geographical treatise written in Arabic. He was also, privately, a man who could recite from memory whole sections of the *Khudaynamak*, the ancient Iranian book of kings, in the Middle Persian that his grandfather had spoken. He had spent his life in the overlap between worlds.

"Tell me what you wrote," he said.

Rudaki told him.

Al-Jayhani listened with his eyes closed and the lamp sending its thin column of smoke toward the ceiling. The Arabic was precise and accomplished, the kind of Arabic that a court official in Baghdad would have recognized as cultivated. It named the amir's conquests. It invoked the blessings of God. It compared Ismail Samani to the great caliphs, which was the correct comparison to make if you were writing for the caliph's representative.

"And what would you write," al-Jayhani said, "if you were writing for us?"

Bukhara in the year 279 of the Hijra — 892 CE — was a city in the process of deciding what it was. The markets ran from the Friday mosque to the eastern gate, half a day's walk through stalls selling silk from Khotan and lapis from Badakhshan and paper — the Samarkand workshops had been making paper for a century and a half by then, and paper had changed the world more than any conquering army. The mud-brick walls had been rebuilt with fired brick in the last decade; the amir's architects were finishing the mausoleum that would hold Ismail Samani's remains and that would stand, seven centuries later, when the Mongols had reduced everything around it to dust. The mosques were full. The madrasas were full. The market in books — manuscripts in Arabic, newly, carefully, expensively made — was flourishing as it had not in any city east of Baghdad.

And in al-Jayhani's study, a blind poet from Samarkand was being asked what he would write if he were writing for them.

## The Conqueror's Confirmation

Ismail Samani had not expected to be what he became. His family were governors, appointed administrators — the Samanid brothers had been given their provinces by the Abbasid caliph's representative as a reward for loyalty during an earlier round of regional politics, and the expectation was that they would govern capably, remit taxes, maintain order, and remain firmly within the orbit of Baghdad. For half a century, this is what the Samanids did.

Then the Saffarid dynasty — a rival power from Sistan, rough soldiers who had built an empire on military charisma and conquest — moved into Khorasan. Ismail Samani met their army at the Oxus River in 900 CE. The battle was not long. The Saffarid commander, Amr ibn al-Layth, was captured and sent in chains to Baghdad — a gift from Ismail to the caliph, a demonstration of what the Samanid governor of the east could accomplish. The caliph received the prisoner and confirmed Ismail's authority over both Khorasan and Transoxiana. It was the first time a Samanid governor had held both.

In Baghdad's account, this was the caliph rewarding a loyal subordinate. In Bukhara's account, it was the moment the Samanid emirate became something the Abbasid caliphate could not revoke. The army that had destroyed the Saffarids was Ismail's army. The eastern provinces that generated the silk-road tax revenues were under Ismail's administration. The caliph in Baghdad, who was dealing with his own civil wars and financial crises, needed the eastern revenues more than he needed the theoretical purity of direct control.

What Ismail built after 900 CE was not an empire — the word would be anachronistic, and the Samanids never used it — but an administration of a quality that the eastern Islamic world had not seen since the Abbasids at their height. The postal network ran from Bukhara west to Nishapur and south to Balkh; a letter from the amir's chancellery in Bukhara could reach Nishapur in eight days during the summer. The tax registers were in order. The military was professionalized: infantry from the Iranian settled population, cavalry recruited from the Turkic peoples of the steppe north of the Oxus, organized under Iranian commanders who had learned their trade from Abbasid military manuals written in Arabic. The *diwan* — the administrative bureau — functioned with a regularity that subsequent dynasties would cite as a model for centuries.

In the autumn of 906 CE, Ismail Samani was dying. He had contracted an illness during a campaign on the northern steppe frontier, and by the time he was carried back to Bukhara it was clear he would not recover. His son Ahmad had already been designated his successor. The Samanid state would continue without interruption.

He died in 907 CE. His mausoleum — already under construction; he had ordered it built during his lifetime, which was itself a statement about how he understood his place in history — was completed by his successors. It still stands in what is now the city of Bukhara, in the country called Uzbekistan. The baked brick is the color of old honey. The geometric patterns on the exterior walls shift in the light as the sun moves, so that at different times of day different patterns become visible, as if the building is showing different faces to different viewers. Inside, the lantern dome sends a single column of light down into the chamber, and the walls absorb it without giving much back. It is a building that keeps its own counsel.

## The Library

The palace library in Bukhara was not a room. It was a project.

It had begun under Ismail Samani and grown under his successors, and by the middle of the tenth century it had achieved a reputation that drew scholars from every corner of the Islamic world. When Ibn Sina — Abu Ali al-Husayn ibn Abdallah ibn Sina, who would become known in the Latin West as Avicenna, who would write the *Canon of Medicine* and the *Book of Healing* and nearly two hundred other works — came to Bukhara as a young man around 999 CE and was given permission to use the palace library, he described finding there books he had never seen before and would not see again. He read his way through the library in what he called a frenzy of discovery, writing afterward that he had understood by the time he left that there was no knowledge that had not already been thought.

What was in the library? Arabic manuscripts: philosophical texts translated from Greek during the Abbasid translation movement of the eighth and ninth centuries, including Aristotle's logical works and Galen's medical treatises; Islamic theological and legal works; geographical treatises; astronomical tables; collections of poetry in Arabic from the Arabian, Iraqi, and Iranian traditions. And increasingly, Persian manuscripts: poetry by Rudaki and his predecessors and contemporaries; translations of Arabic philosophical and historical works into Persian prose; the emerging genre of Persian *adab* — the literature of cultivation and courtly manners.

The library was assembled book by book, which meant copy by copy. This is where the paper economy mattered. Papyrus, which the Abbasid chancery still used for some documents, was expensive, fragile, and difficult to produce far from the Nile delta. Parchment was more durable but required slaughtered animals, which made it scarce and costly. Samarkand paper — a hundred and fifty years old by Ismail's reign, its technology imported from China by Arab armies and refined by Sogdian craftsmen in the Zarafshan valley — was different. It was made from linen rags and hemp and sometimes cotton, pulled through wooden frames and dried in the sun, then burnished with smooth stones until the surface could hold ink without bleeding. It was lighter than parchment, cheaper to produce, and in a dry climate like Transoxiana it was nearly as durable. It could be folded without cracking. It could be cut to any size. A book that would have required a hundred sheep to produce in parchment could be produced in paper for the cost of the copyist's labor.

Cheaper paper meant more copies. More copies meant more books in private hands. More books in private hands meant that scholars could own their own libraries rather than traveling to institutional collections. And it meant that the Bukhara palace library — which could afford to pay for copies of anything it wanted — could accumulate manuscripts at a rate impossible before paper.

The translation project that the Samanid vizier Bal'ami commissioned during the reign of Ahmad ibn Ismail (r. 907–914 CE) was the library's most significant single intellectual investment: a complete translation of al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk*, the History of Prophets and Kings — the largest Arabic historical work of the period, running to tens of thousands of pages — into Persian prose. Al-Tabari himself was still alive in Baghdad when this commission was given. Bal'ami did not simply translate; he adapted, condensed, and added material relevant to the eastern Iranian world that al-Tabari had not included. The result was the first major work of Persian prose in the new Islamic era: a history of the world from creation to the present, available in the language that most of the educated population of Khorasan actually thought in.

With the Bal'ami translation, the Persian literary revival had moved beyond poetry into prose. It was now possible to conduct scholarly argument, preserve historical memory, and transmit technical knowledge in Persian. Arabic remained the language of theology, of jurisprudence, of the international Islamic scholarly community. But Persian had become, for the first time since the Arab conquest, capable of carrying the full weight of a civilization's intellectual life.

## The Poet's Method

Rudaki performed.

This needs to be said directly, because the assumption that poetry is primarily a written art — something you read in a room, silently, from a page — belongs to a world that did not yet fully exist. In the Samanid court, poetry was occasion-specific, voice-carried, and public. A qasida was composed for a moment: a military victory, a royal birthday, the arrival of a foreign ambassador, the end of a fast. The poet composed and then stood and spoke the poem aloud, and the court listened, and the assessment was immediate — this is good, this fails, this deserves a robe of honor or a cold reception.

The qasida's structure was not arbitrary. The *nasib* — the amatory prelude — opened the poem in the register of longing and memory, often describing a lost beloved or an abandoned campsite at dawn. This was conventional, and the convention mattered: it established the poet's emotional range before the political argument began. The *rahil*, or journey section, carried the poet from the world of private feeling into the world of action and travel. The *madih*, the praise section, was where the amir or patron was eulogized — but by this point in the poem the audience had been moved through a full emotional range, and the praise arrived not as flattery but as the natural conclusion of a journey. The form itself was an argument for the legitimacy of the ruler.

In Arabic, these formal moves had been developed over three centuries. The great Arabian poets of the early Islamic period — Jarir, al-Farazdaq, al-Akhtal — had worked the qasida's conventions to a high finish, and the Abbasid poets of Baghdad had layered onto it the refinements of urban courtly culture. Rudaki inherited all of this. He had studied the Arabic masters; his Arabic was good enough to perform at the Abbasid court if he had been sent there.

But when he wrote in Persian, something different happened. The Arabic qasida's imagery was drawn from the Arabian landscape: the campsite in the desert, the she-camel of the journey, the rain cloud over the empty plain. Rudaki's Persian qasidas filled the same formal containers with different contents: the steppe grass of Central Asia, the rose gardens of Khorasan, the snowmelt streams of the mountains east of Bukhara, the fire — the perpetual fire, the *atash*, which carried in its Persian context not just light but the memory of Zoroastrian theology without ever having to say so. You did not need to explain the fire to a Persian audience. The fire was already there.

The poem known as "Agar an Turk-i Shirazi" — the fragment that begins "If that Turk of Shiraz would take my heart in hand" — is probably not Rudaki's, though it was attributed to him for generations. What survives of his actual work is fragmentary: a few hundred verses, scattered references in later anthologies, the biographical accounts in Narshakhi and the tenth-century anthologist al-Tha'alibi. The fragments are enough to show what he was doing. The line "the Mother of Wine rose from the deep sleep, took the cup, and came in the morning" — from the poem known as *Mader-e May*, "Mother of Wine" — manages in its seven words of Persian to carry the Arabic wine-poem tradition, the Iranian rose-garden imagery, and the memory of the dawn prayer time all at once, without explaining any of them. A line that requires three glosses to understand is still, in the hearing, a single moment.

There is a story — probably embellished, possibly invented — that the amir Nasr ibn Ahmad, who was devoutly pious and had been campaigning in the north for years, refused to return to Bukhara. His generals wanted to go home. His courtiers wanted to go home. Finally they sent for Rudaki, who composed and performed a poem about Bukhara's beauty — the Oxus running blue and silver, the towers visible from a distance, the city waiting like a beloved — and the amir broke camp and rode south the same day.

The story is almost certainly elaboration. But it is the kind of story a court tells about itself: that the poet's voice could move armies, that the right words at the right moment were more compelling than strategic argument, that the amir who listened to poetry was also the amir who acted wisely. This is not naivety. It is a theory of how knowledge travels — through form, through beauty, through a sound that lands in the body before it reaches the mind.

## Two Languages, One World

The Samanid court used two languages for the same reason a scholar uses two different books: because they contained different things.

Arabic was the language of the Quran, which meant it was the language of God's direct speech to humanity. It was also the language of the great Islamic jurisprudential tradition, of theology, of the philosophical translations from Greek that the Abbasid court had commissioned. To work in Arabic was to be connected to the entire Islamic scholarly world — from Cordoba to Samarkand, scholars who had never met each other could read each other's work. The *ulama* — the scholarly class, the interpreters of Islamic law — wrote, argued, and judged in Arabic. When Ismail Samani wanted to signal to the caliph in Baghdad that the Samanid court was a proper Islamic court, he did it in Arabic. When al-Jayhani wrote his geographical treatise — *Kitab al-Masalik wa'l-Mamalik*, the Book of Roads and Kingdoms — he wrote it in Arabic, because he was writing for the international scholarly community.

Persian was the language of the Iranian world's memory. It was the language in which the *Khudaynamak* — the old book of Iranian kings — had been preserved in its Middle Persian form, and it was the language into which the oral traditions of the eastern Iranian steppe had been accumulating for centuries. When Rudaki wrote in Persian, he was writing in the language of the *nasib*, the pre-dawn love lyric, the fire and the rose garden, the *farr* — the concepts that had no Arabic equivalents because they described an Iranian political and cosmological reality that Arabic had never needed to name. To work in Persian was to be connected to a different kind of knowing: not the analytical tradition of Aristotle filtered through Arabic translation, but the mythological-historical tradition of Iranian kingship filtered through centuries of oral transmission and now, for the first time, being set in durable written form.

These were not competing knowledge systems. The Samanid synthesis held them simultaneously: you could cite al-Tabari in Arabic and recite Rudaki in Persian and attend Friday prayers and have the *Khudaynamak* read aloud in the evening and not feel that any of these activities contradicted the others. The Arabic philosophical tradition worked by argument: it classified, distinguished, reasoned from premise to conclusion. The Persian literary tradition worked by image: it accumulated specific, concrete particulars — the fire, the rose, the snow on the mountain pass — and let the accumulation build a felt understanding that argument could not quite reach. Both were ways of knowing. The Samanids needed both because the world they governed contained both.

One word marked the boundary: *farr*. The Arabic word *mulk* meant kingship in its political sense — authority, territory, sovereignty in the administrative meaning. But it did not carry what *farr* carried: the idea that legitimate rulership was a divine quality that descended on certain rulers and departed from others, visible in their success, absent from those who lost it, transferable in principle but not by force. The Arabic tradition had concepts of divine blessing, of God's favor on righteous rulers — but these were relational, between God and the individual ruler. *Farr* was something else: cosmological, built into the structure of the world, available to kings who were worthy of it and taken back when they were not. When Ismail Samani asked about the Persian word for sovereignty, he was asking whether there was a framework within which his authority could be understood as more than the caliph's appointment — whether the Iranian world had its own account of why he deserved to rule.

The answer was yes. The chapter was writing itself.

## The Mausoleum

In Bukhara, in the garden that the Samanids planted east of the old city walls, a building was going up that had no precedent in the Islamic world.

It was a mausoleum: a tomb building for Ismail Samani, commissioned during his lifetime, completed by his successors in approximately 943 CE. This alone was unusual — Islamic practice was generally skeptical of elaborate tomb buildings for individuals, on the grounds that such monuments risked elevating humans to a prominence that belonged only to God. The great mosque was the proper monument; the grave should be simple. And yet here, at the height of Samanid power, was a building of extraordinary refinement, built to hold the amir's remains in permanent architectural honor.

The builders solved the theological problem by making the building a statement of craft rather than of personal grandeur. Its walls are made of fired brick — kiln-baked, harder and more regular than sun-dried mud brick, the product of a level of artisanal investment that placed it immediately in a different category from ordinary construction. The bricks are laid in patterns: a basket weave on one section, engaged pilasters on another, a band of interlocking geometric forms running around the dome's base. These patterns create texture that changes with the angle of light, so that the building in the morning looks different from the building at noon, and the building at noon looks different from the building at dusk. It is as if the craftsmen understood that a building meant to stand for centuries would be seen in many lights, and gave it something different to show in each one.

Inside, the dome is a hemisphere supported by four squinches that carry the round dome on a square base — a structural problem that Sassanid architecture had worked out centuries earlier, and that Islamic architecture was inheriting and elaborating. The dome's octagonal lantern sends shafts of light down into the chamber. The chamber is quiet in a specific way: the sound inside is absorbed rather than reflected, as if the brick itself is listening.

The building speaks in two architectural vocabularies simultaneously. The dome and squinch system came from the Sassanid tradition — you can trace its lineage directly to the fire temples of pre-Islamic Iran. The geometric brick patterning came from the Islamic tradition that was developing new ornamental vocabularies as it moved away from figural representation. The Samanid mausoleum did not choose between them. It was both: an Islamic building in its Islamic ornament, an Iranian building in its structural logic, a Samanid building in its refusal to apologize for the combination.

The Mongols came in 1220 CE and destroyed most of Bukhara. The mausoleum survived because someone buried it under a mound of earth before the army arrived, and the Mongols did not know it was there. When it was uncovered centuries later, the brickwork was intact. The *farr* of the building, if one could speak this way, had held.

## The Word the City Was Saying

What Rudaki wrote, in the years that followed his conversation with al-Jayhani, was the beginning of something.

The *qasidas* were the first great poetry in New Persian — the language that had been forming for two centuries in the shadow of Arabic and now emerged into something recognizable, whole, able to carry the weight of feeling and argument and praise. He wrote, eventually, thousands of verses. Most are lost. What survives is enough to see what he was doing: making the Persian language the equal of Arabic not by replacing Arabic, not by rejecting the Islamic intellectual tradition that Arabic carried, but by claiming that the Iranian world had its own voice, its own way of knowing, its own accumulation of the real.

The Samanid court at Bukhara in the years of Nasr ibn Ahmad's long reign (914–943 CE) was the most intellectually productive place in the medieval Islamic world east of Baghdad, and arguably more productive than Baghdad in the specific register of new synthesis rather than accumulated refinement. The library was growing. Persian prose was developing alongside Persian poetry. Scholars were moving between Bukhara and Nishapur and Baghdad and back, carrying manuscripts in both directions.

The court itself was changing. The Turkic soldiers who had been recruited from the steppe north of the Oxus were rising through the military hierarchy, becoming commanders, becoming indispensable. Their loyalty was to the Samanid court that paid and promoted them; their cultural allegiance was to the Persianate world the court embodied; their political ambitions were, for now, constrained by the hierarchies the Samanids maintained. For now.

What the Samanids could not have known — though perhaps al-Jayhani, with his lamp and his habit of working in firelight, might have sensed it — was that the tradition they were building would long outlast them. The Ghaznavid commanders who would displace the Samanids in 999 CE, fewer than sixty years from this moment, would themselves become patrons of Persian poetry, maintaining the form if not the dynasty. The Seljuks who came after the Ghaznavids would use the Samanid administrative framework as their model. Ibn Sina, who learned to think in the Bukhara library, would write his books in Arabic and Persian and carry the Samanid synthesis with him wherever he went.

The *farr* did not belong to a dynasty. It had never belonged to a dynasty. It settled on rulers who deserved it and moved when they no longer did. The Samanids had not created it. They had found a language — two languages — in which it could be spoken.

In al-Jayhani's study, the lamp burned. Outside, the city of Bukhara was deciding what it was. It had already decided; it just had not said so yet.

*Farr*, Rudaki told him finally. The word is *farr*. The light that falls on kings who deserve it.

Ismail Samani turned the word over in his mind. Outside, the city was doing the same. And the language that held the word was writing itself down, book by book, poem by poem, translation by translation, into a permanence the dynasty would not live to see but had already, in this season of lamp-smoke and paper, made inevitable.
