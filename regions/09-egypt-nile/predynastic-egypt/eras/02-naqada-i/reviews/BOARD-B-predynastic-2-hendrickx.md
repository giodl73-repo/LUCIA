# BOARD — Stan Hendrickx

**Chapter:** Naqada I — The Amratian
**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Archaeological Accuracy

1. **White Cross-Lined Ware description: ACCURATE.** The chapter describes coiling technique, wall-thinning, lime-slip painting, and the principal iconographic motifs (hippos, crocodiles, wavy lines, upraised-arms figure). The description of the black-top firing technique (inverting in reducing atmosphere) is correct.

2. **Dates: ACCEPTABLE.** c. 3900-3500 BCE for Naqada I is consistent with current radiocarbon calibrations, though some scholars place the beginning slightly earlier (c. 4000 BCE). The range is defensible.

3. **Geographic scope: CORRECT.** The chapter correctly centers Naqada I in the upper valley. The Lower Egyptian / delta cultures (Buto-Maadian) are absent, which is appropriate since the Naqada I cultural horizon is an Upper Egyptian phenomenon.

4. **Eastern Desert procurement: PLAUSIBLE.** The malachite extraction from wadis of the Eastern Desert is consistent with evidence from survey and excavation at sites in the Wadi Hammamat area and further east. The description of the desert-walker's journey is plausible though necessarily reconstructed.

5. **Obsidian sourcing: CORRECTLY HEDGED.** "Sources that may have been as distant as the southern highlands." This is the appropriate level of uncertainty. Ethiopian highland sourcing is probable for some obsidian found in Upper Egyptian contexts but not all pieces have been sourced definitively.

## Concerns

1. **Pottery distribution range:** "It appeared at sites thirty and fifty and eighty kilometers away." These specific distances should be verifiable against the published distribution maps. The general claim of wide distribution is correct.

2. **Copper smelting at Naqada I sites:** The evidence for on-site copper smelting at Naqada itself during the Amratian period is thin. Crucible fragments and small finished objects are documented, but the scale and location of smelting during Naqada I specifically (as opposed to Naqada II) requires caution. The chapter's description of small-scale crucible smelting is plausible but should not be read as fully documented for this specific site in this specific period.

3. **Reed boats:** The depiction of boats on White Cross-Lined pottery is correct. However, the interpretation of specific features (sail vs. standard) is debated. The chapter appropriately uses "what may have been" language.

## Verdict: PASS. No factual errors requiring correction. Minor cautions on copper smelting specificity and Hierakonpolis size.
