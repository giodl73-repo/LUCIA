# La Violencia — Opening

## El Bogotazo

The bullet entered Jorge Eliécer Gaitán's body at 1:05 in the afternoon of April 9, 1948.

He was walking out of his law office on the Carrera Séptima — the main artery of Bogotá, the wide boulevard where government ministries stood alongside bookshops and shoeshine stands and vendors of chicha, the fermented corn drink that the city's respectable classes had spent a decade trying to ban. The city had that midday smell: diesel from the buses, grilled meat from the street carts, rain coming off the mountains. Gaitán had just finished a meeting. He was going to lunch.

He never arrived.

The shooter, a young man named Juan Roa Sierra, fired three times. Gaitán fell. The crowd on the Carrera Séptima — the vendors, the office workers, the passersby who had gathered the way passersby gather in any Latin American city at midday, slowly, warmly, with no particular urgency — turned. They saw what had happened. Someone grabbed Roa Sierra. The crowd grew. By the time anyone thought to take Gaitán to a hospital, the crowd had already beaten Roa Sierra to death on the sidewalk.

That took less than ten minutes.

What happened next took ten years.

The news spread through Bogotá the way news spread then: mouth to mouth, down the block, into the cafés and government offices, up the Carrera Séptima to the Plaza de Bolívar and out from there in every direction. In a room at the Hotel Granada, a twenty-one-year-old Cuban student named Fidel Castro was preparing for a youth congress. He walked downstairs and stepped into a history that was not his own. He watched the city come apart and later called it "spontaneous revolution" — meaning it as praise. Colombians would not have used that word.

Gaitán had been the one Colombian politician who spoke to the poor in language they recognized as their own. Not because he was poor — he had a law degree from Rome, a suit, an office on the Carrera Séptima — but because he said the thing everyone in the cafés and the market stalls and the barrio houses already knew: that Colombia was divided between the país político and the país nacional, the political country and the real country, and that only one of them actually governed. He said it from the stages of public plazas, in a baritone that people described as physical, something you felt in your chest. He said it in suits that were too good for the barrios he visited and too simple for the oligarchs he attacked. Colombia had not heard a voice like his before.

After the shot, the real country came into the streets.

By nightfall, the center of Bogotá was burning. Rioters had torn apart the trolleys and used the iron rails as weapons. They had broken into the armories and distributed the rifles inside. They had set fire to the offices of the Conservative Party newspapers. They had stormed the prisons and freed whoever was inside. The fires were visible from the hills that ring Bogotá, and people in the barrios climbed to their rooftops and watched the city center glow orange and thought, some of them, that the revolution had finally arrived.

It had not. Or rather: what arrived was something for which "revolution" was the wrong word. Revolutions have programs. What spread from Bogotá into the valleys and mountains of the Colombian interior over the following years was older and stranger than any program — it was grief converted into habit, personal enmity raised to the level of policy, local scores settled under the cover of national conflict.

The Liberals and the Conservatives had been killing each other in Colombia for a hundred years. They had fought nine major civil wars since independence. The parties meant different things in theory — the Church, federalism, the role of the state — and in practice meant almost nothing at all, since every village had its color the way every village has its local saints, inherited rather than chosen, defended because ancestors had defended it, written into the landscape so deep that a farmer might not be able to say what the party believed but could tell you which families were Liberal and which were Conservative and what each owed the other. The political was personal. The personal was lethal.

Gaitán's death was the match.

What it ignited was not a war with front lines and armies. It was something more diffuse and more intimate: a landscape of violence in which the state dissolved at the edges, where the army and police and party militias all moved through the same territory with different flags and different lists, and where ordinary people — farmers in Tolima and Boyacá, market vendors in the coffee towns of Antioquia, the families that had always voted Liberal in a Conservative municipality or Conservative in a Liberal one — found themselves on the wrong side of a line no one had drawn but everyone could feel.

Two hundred thousand people would die before it ended.

The period came to be called La Violencia. Not a revolution. Not a war. La Violencia. The noun. As though violence itself had become a condition of life, like altitude or rain — something the country lived inside, the way the Andes live inside their own cloud cover, hidden and enormous and simply there.

A young man from Aracataca, on the Caribbean coast, absorbed all of this. He was twenty at the time of the Bogotazo, a law student in Bogotá who had watched the city burn from his boarding house window. He would spend the next twenty years trying to find a language adequate to what he had seen. Not a language of explanation — Colombia had plenty of those, political essays and commissions and white papers that laid out the causes and proposed the solutions and accomplished nothing. Something else: a language in which the fantastic and the murderous could sit side by side without either canceling the other out, because that was how it actually felt to live inside that country, in that century, in that particular arrangement of geography and history and grief.

García Márquez found it. But first, the dying.
