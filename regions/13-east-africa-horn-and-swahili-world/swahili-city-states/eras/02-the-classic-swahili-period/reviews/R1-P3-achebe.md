## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of "The Classic Swahili Period" opening

**Overall:** This opening does something difficult and does it well: it presents a synthesis civilization from inside, without making the synthesis itself the spectacle. The old man building with coral does not think of himself as synthesizing two cultures. He is building a mosque. The world he inhabits is simply his world. That is exactly right. But there are moments where the author's hand becomes visible, and those moments must be cut.

**What works:**

- The old man is the right entry figure. He is not a sultan, not a merchant, not a scholar. He is a builder, and building is the most literal expression of what this civilization does: it takes what the sea gives and makes it into something that can stand. He does not narrate. He does not explain. He works, and the world reveals itself through his work. This is how you tell a culture's story from inside.

- "This was how you built on the coast. You built with what the sea gave you." The pronoun "you" is exactly right. It is not "they built" — which would create distance — but "you built," which pulls the reader inside the practice. The reader becomes someone who knows this. The reader becomes coastal.

- The dhows are "sewn, not nailed" — and the explanation that follows comes from the sailors' own knowledge, not from a narrator explaining boat construction to a Western audience. "The sailors who built them trusted the flex the way the old man trusted the coral: it held because it gave." This is a culture explaining itself to itself. No one in this passage is surprised by anything. The knowledge is assumed. The reader must enter it or be left behind. That is the correct relationship.

- The coin passage handles a potentially dangerous moment well. The old man "did not think of this as innovation. He thought of it as what the town had become." The word "innovation" is flagged and immediately rejected — the narrator refuses to apply a modern category to a change the old man experiences as simply how things are now. This is the inside voice holding steady under pressure.

- The gold from "the mines south of the Zambezi, passed hand to hand through networks of exchange that reached from the stone-built settlements of the plateau to the coral-built harbor of Kilwa" — the trade is described through its African chain of custody, not through its arrival from outside. The gold moves from African mine to African coast. The ocean trade is what happens next, but the inland trade comes first. This is the correct ordering.

**What doesn't:**

- "And into this rhythm, into the coral and the commerce and the call to prayer that sounded from the mosque the old man was building, the Swahili world made itself. Not Bantu alone, though the language was Bantu and the kinship was Bantu and the governance of the land followed Bantu custom. Not Arab, though the faith was Islam and the script was Arabic and the merchant families traced their genealogies to Shiraz and the Gulf. Something made at the seam. Something that belonged to neither shore of the ocean but to the water between them." This paragraph is the author speaking. It is well-written and it is accurate, but it is an analysis delivered from above the world, not from inside it. The old man does not think of himself as "something made at the seam." He is Swahili. That word contains the synthesis without needing to explain it. Every preceding paragraph already showed the synthesis through material detail. This paragraph explains what was already shown. Cut it, or rewrite it from inside — perhaps through the old man's own experience of what his world contains.

- "the blue-and-white porcelain that the merchants said came from a country so far east that even the sailors of Cambay had never been there" — this is a good detail and the perspective is correct (the distance measured from the coast), but "the merchants said" introduces a slight narrative distance. Who are "the merchants"? The old man would know them by name, by family, by the harbor they berthed in. Make the knowledge specific. Not "merchants said" but a specific exchange, a specific person.

- The opening does not yet establish whose oral tradition carries this world. The Mali chapters had the jelilu. What is the Swahili equivalent? The utenzi poets? The town chroniclers? The genealogists who trace the ruling lineages? If the chapter will rely on oral tradition as one of its sources of authority, the opening should establish the tradition's presence. Not as a formal introduction, but as a voice in the world — someone who remembers, someone who tells.

**The question I'd push on:**

Whose story is this, specifically? The old man is well-drawn, but he is anonymous and generic enough to represent any coastal builder. The chapter will need to decide whose perspective governs each section. A sultan? A merchant? A woman who manages the household economy between monsoon seasons? A sailor? The opening's anonymity works as a doorway, but the chapter must walk through it into specific lives. And the culture's own voice of memory — its own tradition of self-narration — must be present, or the chapter will be told by no one, which is the same as being told by the author. The Swahili coast had chroniclers, poets, genealogists. Let one of them speak.
