# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## Panel Review — Notes: Toledo y la Traducción (PANEL-2)

**Round:** 2 (PANEL-2)
**Reviewer:** P-3 Chinua Achebe
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The production bible has taken my concern seriously: Abraham ibn Daud's *Sefer ha-Qabbalah* is named as a primary source from inside the Jewish scholarly tradition, the Arabic intellectual tradition is to be treated as an achievement in its own right, and the anonymous translators are acknowledged as a category whose erasure from the Latin record is itself historical evidence.

This is the correct approach. I want to be precise about why.

The risk in this chapter is not condescension — the opening showed no condescension. The risk is invisible privilege: a chapter that, without malice, organizes its perspective around the point of view of the person who has a biography (Gerard, Gundissalinus) while the people without biographies remain as backdrop. This is not a moral failure; it is a structural one, and structural failures are harder to catch than moral ones precisely because they feel natural.

**What I want to see in the chapter:**

Section 3 — "What the Libraries Held" — must do two things simultaneously: (1) characterize the Arabic-language intellectual tradition as an achievement in its own right, and (2) show this from the inside — not from the perspective of Gerard encountering it but from the perspective of what those scholars were actually doing when they wrote. Avicenna did not write the *Qanun* so that Gerard could translate it. He wrote it to solve problems in Arabic-language medicine that his predecessors had not solved. The chapter should show what those problems were.

Section 4 — "The Workshop" — needs at least one moment that shows the Jewish intellectual tradition from inside Abraham ibn Daud's own perspective. The *Sefer ha-Qabbalah* is a chronicle of the Jewish scholarly tradition in Toledo — Ibn Daud is not a participant in the translation school as a Christian patron would understand it; he is a scholar maintaining a continuous Hebrew intellectual tradition that the translation work intersects. His perspective on what is happening in Toledo is different from Gerard's perspective. The chapter should hold both.

**What I would still push on:**

The notes propose using Abubacer's unclear identity as "honest limits as technique." This is defensible. But the execution requires care: the chapter should not acknowledge the uncertainty in a way that implies the unnamed scholars matter less because we cannot name them. The uncertainty about Abubacer's identity is the record's failure, not the person's. The chapter should be clear about that distinction.

**Summary:** The structural plan addresses the ownership question. The execution in Sections 3 and 4 must show the Arabic and Jewish intellectual traditions from inside their own concerns, not merely as sources that Gerard's story happened to require.
