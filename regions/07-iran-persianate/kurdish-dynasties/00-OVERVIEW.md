# Kurd — The Mountain Lords

We have lived in the mountains between empires since before the mountains had the names the empires gave them. The Zagros range, the Taurus, the uplands of what the outside world calls Kurdistan — these are our world, the highlands where the winters are hard and the passes are defensible and the lowland states have always found it easier to negotiate with us than to subjugate us. We called ourselves Kurd, and the mountain lords who organized our political life through the medieval and early modern periods — the Marwanids around Diyarbakir, the Shaddadids in the Caucasus, the Ayyubid dynasty that Saladin founded and that briefly ruled Egypt and Syria — did so by balancing the competing pressures of Ottoman, Safavid, and Mesopotamian power with the same skill their predecessors had used to balance Byzantine, Abbasid, and Seljuk pressure.

We are not a single political unit, and we have rarely wanted to be. The highland community, the valley, the lord who controls the pass — these are the organizing units of Kurdish political life, and the great Kurdish dynasties that achieved prominence across the medieval world did so by extending the mountain-lord pattern into a larger frame rather than by creating a unified Kurdish state. Saladin al-Ayyubi — the most famous Kurd in world history — organized the Sunni Muslim world against the Crusaders, reconquered Jerusalem, negotiated a peace with Richard I of England, and never ruled Kurdistan as a distinct entity. He was the lord of Egypt and Syria who happened to be Kurd by birth and formation, and who carried the Kurdish instinct for pragmatic coalition into his statecraft.

## What Defined Them

**The mountain as the organizing principle.** Kurdish political and social organization was shaped by the highland geography in the most direct way: the valley was the unit, the pass was the strategic asset, the winter pasture and the summer upland grazing were the economic basis, and the lord who controlled the pass and the seasonal movements was the natural authority. This was not underdevelopment. It was adaptation to a specific environment that lowland agricultural empires consistently found difficult to absorb: too fragmented to conquer comprehensively, too economically marginal to be worth the cost of permanent occupation, and too militarily capable in their home terrain to suppress without disproportionate effort.

**The Kurdish lord as the frontier manager.** The great empires that surrounded the Kurdish highlands — Byzantine, Abbasid, Seljuk, Ottoman, Safavid — found it more efficient to manage the Kurdish lords as frontier auxiliaries than to absorb the highlands directly. The Kurdish lords collected taxes from their valleys, provided soldiers when required, enforced the pass-roads' security, and maintained the borders between competing empires in their particular sector. In return, they received recognition of their authority, occasionally titles and appointments from the imperial court, and the freedom to manage their valleys according to their own customs. This arrangement was stable as long as the empires were strong enough to be worth accommodating and weak enough to need Kurdish cooperation.

**Saladin — the Kurdish lord as world-historical figure.** Salah al-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub — Saladin — was born in Tikrit in 1137 CE to a Kurdish family that had risen in Zengid service. He reconquered Jerusalem from the Crusaders in 1187 CE, organized the Sunni Muslim world of Egypt and Syria under his authority, and negotiated the Treaty of Jaffa with Richard I — allowing Christian pilgrimage access to Jerusalem while maintaining Muslim political control. The chivalric reputation he acquired in both Muslim and Christian tradition came from his policy of restraint after the reconquest (in deliberate contrast to the Crusaders' behavior when they took the city in 1099). He was neither a Kurdish nationalist nor a pan-Islamic idealist. He was a pragmatic military and political organizer who used Islam as his legitimizing framework and Kurdish military networks as his operational base.

**Islam in Kurdish form.** Kurdish communities adopted Islam in the 7th–8th centuries CE, and the faith became central to Kurdish identity — but it was Islam held in a distinctly Kurdish register. Sufi orders, particularly the Naqshbandi and Qadiri tariqas, became deeply embedded in Kurdish social life, with the shaykh (the Sufi master) functioning as a social and political authority alongside the clan chief. The Kurdistan frontier between Shia Iran and Sunni Ottoman Turkey was a zone where both traditions coexisted, and where local religious practice often preserved pre-Islamic elements — the cult of saints at mountain shrines, seasonal festivals tied to the agricultural calendar — within an Islamic framework. Kurdish Islam was orthodox in its commitments and local in its expression.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Mountain Confederacies:** A valley lord in the Zagros foothills is negotiating with the Abbasid governor about the pass-road taxes. The negotiation has been going on for three generations. The lord's great-grandfather negotiated the same arrangement with the Sassanid governor. The mountains have different rulers, but the mountains' logic does not change.

**2 — The Medieval Kurdish Dynasties:** The Marwanid lord at Diyarbakir is writing his patronage letters in Arabic to the scholars at his court. His city's black basalt walls have been standing for centuries and will stand for centuries more. He governs the largest Kurdish polity of his age and nobody outside the highland region quite knows what to do with him.

**3 — Saladin and the Ayyubid Moment:** Saladin stands on the walls of Jerusalem after the Crusader garrison has left. His decision not to massacre the Christian population — in deliberate contrast to what happened when the Crusaders took the city — will be the moment that defines his reputation across the medieval world. He is organizing the reconquest from a Kurdish military base, but what he is building is something larger.

**4 — The Ottoman-Safavid Frontier:** The Ottoman and Safavid ambassadors are drawing a border through Kurdistan that their respective bureaucracies will need to administer. The Kurdish lords of the region know that neither empire fully controls the territory they are dividing on paper. The border will remain porous, the lords will play both sides, and Kurdistan will remain a frontier rather than a province for another four centuries.

**5 — The Modern Encounter:** A Kurdish intellectual in Istanbul is reading the newspapers about nationalism and wondering what the same ideas would mean for a people divided between three empires. The answer to that question will dominate the next century of Kurdish history, in ways he cannot yet see.
