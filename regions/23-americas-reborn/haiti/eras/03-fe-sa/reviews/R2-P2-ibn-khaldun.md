# R2 — Targeted Panel Review: Ibn Khaldun
## Haiti, Era 3: Fè Sa — Notes (production bible)

**Date:** 2026-04-17
**Focus:** Structural engine, causal mechanism, the three simultaneous wars

---

The notes name the engine correctly: *libète* as absolute and non-negotiable. The mechanism — every attempt to qualify freedom generates proportionally larger assertion of it — is a genuine structural observation, not just a rhetorical claim. The 1794 abolition, Toussaint's response, the 1802 betrayal, Dessalines's response: this is a system with legible logic.

What I want the chapter to do with Section 3 (Three Wars at Once): show the reader WHY Toussaint's 1794 switch made sense in terms of the structural logic, not just narrate that it happened. Here is the mechanism: Toussaint was fighting for an objective (permanent abolition of slavery), not for a nationality. When Spain offered freedom-in-exchange-for-military-service, he took that offer. When France actually abolished slavery (permanently, not as a bargain), he switched, because France now served his objective better than Spain. This is asabiyyah operating at a sophisticated level: the group solidarity is not defined by ethnicity or nationality but by a shared claim on liberty. The chapter should make this visible.

The terminal condition (Vindicating) is correct and important. January 1, 1804 proves the engine worked — the revolution succeeded — and immediately reveals what the next test is. Era 4 (the indemnity) is the logical consequence of Era 3's success: the world acknowledges that libète worked by charging Haiti for it. The chapter's landing should place the reader at the threshold of that next test without crossing into Era 4's territory.

**One pressure point for the full chapter:** The massacre of the French population in early 1804 should be analyzed structurally, not only narrated. From Dessalines's perspective: what was the structural logic? The answer is that he was closing an internal faction — the planter class and its allied French population — that would, if left in place, provide a bridgehead for French reconquest. This is the same logic Cromwell used in Ireland, the same logic various revolutionary governments have used when they believed a fifth column threatened the revolution's survival. The chapter should show this logic FROM INSIDE without endorsing it. Ibn Khaldun does not endorse; he explains.

**What the notes get right:** The note on source asymmetry is exactly right and will be important for the chapter's credibility. The Bois Caïman ceremony is filtered through French accounts designed to discredit. The chapter that makes this visible — that uses the French account's biases as evidence about the French, not about the ceremony — will score well on Evidence.
