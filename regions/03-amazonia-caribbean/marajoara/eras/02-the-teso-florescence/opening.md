# Opening — The Teso Florescence (c. 700–1000 CE)

The woman works in the early dry season, when the flood has pulled back from the teso's base and the air carries the smell of exposed mud and the small fish left stranded in the grass. The clay is ready. She prepared it herself: the grog mixed in from broken vessels, the paste kneaded until it held the shape her hands pressed into it without cracking. Now the urn stands finished on the mat before her — the coils built up over many days, the vessel fired and cooled — and she is painting the final layer.

The slip is red. She applies it with a tool made from the rib of a fish, drawing the line in a single continuous motion around the vessel's shoulder. The spiral begins wide at the top and pulls inward, turning on itself, crossing and rejoining. It is not decoration. She does not think of it as decoration. The spiral is an argument about what happens when the body is finished — how the person inside the living form is released into a different kind of continuity. Her grandmother painted the same form. Her grandmother's grandmother before that. The tradition of the spiral is older than any name she knows for it, and what it says has never needed translation because it has never been spoken aloud. It is carried in the motion of the rib across the clay.

The person who will be buried in this urn is still alive.

She is that woman's sister, and she is healthy now, working the fish weirs in the flooded channels along the teso's western edge. But the urn is already made. It is already her. The Camutins teso complex holds urns that were prepared for people who lived for many seasons after the clay was fired, urns that waited on the high shelves of the burial houses until the moment they were needed. This is not morbid. It is the opposite of morbid. To make the urn before the person dies is to say: you are not an ending. You are something the community has already prepared a form for.

The teso itself says this. The mound on which the woman works is five meters above the floodplain — high enough to remain above water at the peak of the rainy season, when the várzea stretches from horizon to horizon and the island becomes an archipelago of earthen hills. The teso did not rise from the ground. The community built it. Over generations, they returned to this same elevated place each dry season and deposited the residue of their living: broken vessels, fishbones, the ash of fires, the packed debris of houses built and dismantled and built again. What the woman walks on is her ancestors, rendered into earth. The teso is the community's body, accumulated season by season, and to live on it is to live inside the record of everyone who lived on it before.

The woman sets down the fish-rib tool. The red spiral is complete on this face. She turns the vessel — it is heavy, a grown woman could fit inside it — and finds the position for the black line that will cross the red. The black slip comes from a different preparation, a different material knowledge. She mixes it herself, knows the proportions by touch. The line she will make will cross the spiral at an angle, creating the intersection that marks the vessel as belonging to this complex, this teso, this way of understanding what the urn is for.

From where she sits, she can see the várzea extending in every direction below the teso's edge. The flood has pulled back in the last days, and the exposed plain is the color of river mud — dark, fertile, seething with the small life left behind by the water. The channels between the mounds are passable again on foot. The paths are firm. Out along the western edge, she can hear the sounds of the fish weirs — the knock of wood on wood, voices calling across the water. Her sister is out there. The smoke from the cooking fires on the neighboring teso is rising straight in the still morning air.

The flood will come again. It always comes. The várzea will return to the places it retreated from, the paths between tesos will disappear under brown water, the fish will move back into the flooded grass. When the water rises, the teso rises with it — or rather, the teso stays and the world around it submerges. This is what it means to live on an island that floods: the built ground becomes the horizon, and the accumulated past is what holds the living above the water.

The urn will be ready before her sister needs it.
