# BOARD-1 — Marco Palacios
## Domain Expert Review — La Violencia

**Reviewer:** Marco Palacios (Simón Bolívar University, Bogotá; *Between Legitimacy and Violence: A History of Colombia, 1875–2002*, Duke University Press, 2006)
**Expertise:** Colombian political history; the twentieth-century Liberal-Conservative conflict; state formation and political violence in Latin America
**Date:** 2026-04-17

## Factual Assessment

The chapter's treatment of the Bogotazo is accurate. The 1:05 PM timestamp is documented. Juan Roa Sierra's identity as shooter is confirmed; his motivations remain disputed in the historiography (the chapter correctly avoids attributing motivation). Gaitán's trajectory — law degree in Rome, the país político / país nacional framework, the silent march of February 1948 — is correctly represented.

One factual clarification: the chapter refers to the silent march as occurring on "February 7, 1948" and having "100,000 people." The date is correct (February 7, 1948). The crowd estimate of 100,000 is the standard estimate from contemporary accounts and is used in the historiography (including my own work), though it is by nature an approximation. The chapter handles this correctly by presenting it as a crowd of 100,000 without editorial qualification, which reflects the standard treatment in the field.

The pájaros and chulavitas are correctly distinguished. The chulavita was the police-based organized unit; the pájaro was the freelance hired killer operating in the coffee zones. The chapter conflates them slightly: "Armed groups, called chulavitas... Later these became the pájaros." The historical sequence is correct (the chulavita system preceded the pájaros as an organized phenomenon), but the relationship between them was not strictly sequential — they operated simultaneously in different zones during different periods. This is a minor historiographic nuance that the chapter's narrative form handles adequately; noting it for completeness.

The "colonización antioqueña" explanation for smallholder coffee culture in Tolima, the Quindío, and Caldas is accurate and represents the standard historical explanation. The chapter correctly connects this to the intensity of violence in those zones. This is the argument developed in Forrest Hylton's work and consistent with the geographic analysis in the CNMH's *¡Basta Ya!* report.

The Rojas Pinilla section is accurate. The 1953 coup, the amnesty program, and the eventual reassertion of party control are correctly represented. The National Front, negotiated at Sitges in 1957 and implemented in 1958, is correctly characterized: the alternation of Liberal and Conservative presidents for sixteen years, the equal division of all government appointments.

The FARC founding in Marquetalia, Tolima, in 1964, with founders who included former Liberal guerrillas from La Violencia, is historically accurate. The chapter's treatment — naming the founding without narrating it — is the correct editorial choice for a chapter about La Violencia rather than the FARC.

## Cultural Representation Assessment

The chapter correctly identifies La Violencia as a period during which violence operated through party affiliation at the village level, not through ideological conviction. This is the fundamental historical point that distinguishes La Violencia from either a revolutionary conflict or a purely criminal phenomenon. The chapter earns this point through the Líbano, Tolima example and the Rosa composite, rather than through assertion.

The composite figure disclosure in figures.md is appropriate and transparent. The CNMH testimonial archive is the correct source for this kind of composite, and the chapter's handling matches the field's own practice in truth-commission documentation.

## Historical Accuracy Concerns

No significant historical inaccuracies found. The chapter represents the state of the field (Palacios 2006, Sánchez and Meertens 2001, Hylton 2006, CNMH 2013) accurately and without distortion.

One interpretive observation: the chapter's framing of the National Front as a "legitimacy gap" resolution is consistent with the political-science literature on elite pacts but does not engage with the debate about whether the National Front itself constituted a form of ongoing violence by exclusion. This is a legitimate historiographic debate (Paul Oquist's 1980 work argues for the National Front's structural contribution to subsequent violence). The chapter's choice to leave this implicit in the landing rather than explicit is an editorial decision, not a factual error.

## Recommendation

Chapter approved for FINAL with no required corrections. The one minor clarification on chulavita/pájaro periodization is noted but does not require revision given the chapter's narrative form.
