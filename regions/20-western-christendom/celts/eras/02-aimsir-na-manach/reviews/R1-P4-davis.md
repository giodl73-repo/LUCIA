# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel-1 Review: Opening — Aimsir na Manach

The opening does something rare with an oral tradition: it renders it at method level. Ferdomnach is not described as someone who "memorizes" or "performs" — those words belong to the outside observer who reduces a complex epistemic practice to a simpler category. Instead, the opening shows the fili working: composing in "the upper registers of his mind," tracking syllable counts and end-sounds and internal rhymes, reading seating arrangements as social data, calculating éraic as the operational logic for the order of praise. This is a knowledge system shown in operation, not described in summary.

The twelve years of bardic training, the ten thousand lines of genealogy and law and mythology carried without ink — this is treated as what it is: a technology. The line "What he held in his memory was not merely poetry. It was the archive" does the work precisely. It does not romanticize (the fili is not a mystical keeper of sacred wisdom) and it does not diminish (the memory is not a lesser substitute for writing). It is accurate: the archive was the memory.

I want the chapter to go further into this. What did it feel like to hold ten thousand lines? What was the physical practice of maintaining that knowledge — the daily recitation, the testing by senior filid, the penalty for a poem incorrectly performed? The oral tradition has a body. The archive has a body. I want to be inside that body in the full chapter.

The monastery's knowledge system also needs this treatment. Tírechán copying is rendered with physical specificity — the cost of the vellum, the hours from Lauds to Vespers. But I want to know more about what copying meant to the monks not just as preservation but as intellectual practice. Lectio divina, the slow reading that was also a form of prayer, the marginalia that monks added to manuscripts as their minds wandered — these are the method level of monastic knowledge. The chapter should show the monastery's epistemic practice with the same depth it shows the fili's.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening says the two systems produced "unlike anything the world elsewhere was making." I want this to be shown through the knowledge system itself — not just through the beautiful objects (the Book of Kells, the Ardagh Chalice) but through the specific intellectual acts that made those objects possible. What did a monk who was also trained in the fili tradition think? How did that dual training show up in the way he composed, organized, illuminated? That is the wonder I am looking for.
