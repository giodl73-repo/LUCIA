# Gojoseon — The Land Where the Bear-Woman's Son Was King

We are Gojoseon — Old Joseon — the first Korean state, and our origins are cosmic before they are historical. Dangun Wanggeom, our founder, was the son of Hwanin (the Lord of Heaven) and a bear-woman: a she-bear who endured a hundred days in darkness, eating only mugwort and garlic, to become human, and who then bore the son who became our king. We tell this story because it is true: not in the way that a historical chronicle is true, but in the way that a people's understanding of what they are is true. We are the people born from the covenant between the heavenly and the earthly, the transformation through endurance, the emergence from darkness into sovereignty.

The historical Gojoseon, centered on the Liaodong Peninsula and the northern Korean peninsula, was a confederation of clan communities that maintained its independence against Han Chinese pressure for centuries. We were not simply absorbed; we were conquered only after a sustained war (109–108 BCE) that required four Han armies and internal treachery to complete. The Han established four commanderies in our territory, and the resistance to those commanderies — which lasted for decades and never fully succeeded — was the first expression of the Korean determination to be governed by Koreans that would define the peninsula's history for two thousand years.

## What Defined Them

**The Dangun origin myth.** The story of Dangun is not merely a creation myth; it is a statement about what the Korean people believe they are. The bear's endurance — a hundred days in darkness, eating only the prescribed ritual foods — is the pattern of Korean perseverance. The divine father and the transformed-from-animal mother represent the union of heavenly authority and earthly persistence. The story has been told continuously for three thousand years, and every subsequent Korean dynasty traced its legitimacy to the moment when Dangun Wanggeom descended from the heavenly realm to rule the people of Asadal.

**Bronze culture and the daggers.** Gojoseon's material culture is defined by the slender-bladed Korean bronze dagger (비파형 동검, lute-shaped bronze dagger) found across the northern Korean peninsula and Liaodong. This distinctive form, different from Chinese bronze weapons of the same period, marks the extent of the Gojoseon cultural sphere. The bronze daggers were not merely weapons; they were prestige objects that encoded rank and identity in a culture organized around warrior chieftains.

**Resistance to the Han.** The Han conquest required sustained effort: four armies, internal treachery (the assassination of the last Gojoseon king by his own officials), and years of active resistance to the commandery system that followed. The Lelang commandery survived for centuries, but Korean communities around it maintained their identity and gradually absorbed or expelled the Chinese presence. The resistance to the Han commanderies was not simply a political episode; it was the formative experience of Korean national identity as something that defines itself against Chinese absorption.

## The Chapters

**1 — Dangun:** A bear and a tiger are given mugwort and garlic and told to endure a hundred days in a cave without sunlight. The tiger leaves after twenty days. The bear endures. She emerges as a woman. She becomes the mother of the first king of Joseon. This is how a people comes to be.

**2 — Historical Gojoseon:** The bronze dagger is placed in the chieftain's grave. His lineage will be known by this blade. The style of its casting identifies who made it and what world they inhabited. Three thousand years later, archaeologists will find it and know where Gojoseon was.

**3 — Wiman Joseon:** A Chinese exile named Wiman arrives from the Han. He says he will serve the king. He builds his power at the border. He deposes the king. He rules Gojoseon as a powerful buffer state between Han China and the peninsula. He is the Korean state's most effective ruler and most controversial figure.

**4 — The Han Conquest:** The Han armies cross the Yalu. They expect a quick victory. They get a siege. The Gojoseon court argues about whether to resist or surrender. The resistance faction wins the argument but loses the war when the surrender faction assassinates the last king. The commanderies are established. The resistance begins immediately.
