# Հայ Ժողովուրդ — Hay Zhoghovurd: The Armenian People

We remember because they want us to forget. The Armenian diaspora is organized around an act of memory that the perpetrating state has spent a century denying: the systematic mass murder of the Armenian population of the Ottoman Empire, primarily in 1915–1923, which killed between 600,000 and 1.5 million people (estimates vary; the question of exact numbers is itself disputed as part of the denial politics) and ended the 3,000-year Armenian presence in historic Armenia. The survivors and their descendants — scattered across Syria, Lebanon, France, the United States, Argentina, and dozens of other countries — carried with them the obligation of witness: to keep saying "Meds Yeghern" (the Great Crime), to keep naming what was done, to insist on recognition against official Turkish denial, to ensure that the dead were not erased twice, first from life and then from history.

The Armenian civilization that the genocide almost destroyed was ancient. Armenians have a continuous recorded presence in the region of what is now eastern Turkey, Armenia, Azerbaijan, and northern Iran for at least 2,600 years — since the Urartian kingdom (c. 860–585 BCE) whose culture and territory partly overlapped with the later Armenian state. The Armenian Apostolic Church, founded by tradition in 301 CE when Armenia became the first kingdom to adopt Christianity as a state religion, is one of the oldest continuous Christian institutions on Earth. The Armenian alphabet, invented by the monk Mesrop Mashtots around 405 CE, is one of the most elegant and most durable script inventions in history, and it remains in use today both in Armenia and in diaspora communities worldwide. The civilization that was attacked in 1915 was not a remnant or a fragment; it was a living, old, and sophisticated world.

## What Defined Them

**Meds Yeghern and the obligation of memory.** Every year on April 24 — Genocide Remembrance Day — Armenians across the world mark the anniversary of the arrest of the Armenian intellectual and community leaders of Constantinople in 1915, the event that signaled the beginning of the systematic destruction. The date is the axis around which diaspora Armenian identity organizes. The political campaign for recognition — to have the genocide named as such by states, by international bodies, by the United Nations — is not merely symbolic: it is the insistence that the murder of a people cannot be retrospectively legalized by the murderer's denial. The states that have recognized the genocide (France, Germany, the United States in 2021, and others) have taken a position in this argument. Turkey's denial is the ongoing wound.

**The Church as nation.** During the centuries when Armenians had no state, the Armenian Apostolic Church was the institution through which Armenian national identity was preserved and transmitted. The Catholicos (the head of the Church) was the national leader in the absence of a king or president. The Church's administrative structure, its educational system, and its liturgy in the Armenian language maintained a community that the political conditions of the region would otherwise have dissolved. This relationship between Church and nation is not merely historical; it is still felt in diaspora communities where the church building is the community center and the church calendar is the community calendar.

**The alphabet as civilization.** The Armenian alphabet, created around 405 CE by the monk Mesrop Mashtots (with the support of the Catholicos Sahak the Great) specifically to translate the Bible into Armenian, is one of the most successful script creations in history: it accurately represents Armenian phonology, it has been in continuous use for 1,600 years without significant modification, and it remains the primary marker of Armenian cultural identity. A diaspora Armenian who cannot speak Armenian will still often recognize the script. The first words written in the alphabet — from the Book of Proverbs: "To know wisdom and instruction; to perceive the words of understanding" — set the tone for a civilization that has organized itself around text and learning.

**Resilience as national character.** The Armenian diaspora has built extraordinary communities in extraordinary adversity: churches, schools, newspapers, political organizations, cultural societies, and eventually businesses and professional networks in the Middle East, Europe, and the Americas. The Armenian-American community's integration into American professional life — the Kardashian family is the most famous, but also William Saroyan, Kirk Kerkorian, Alex Manoogian (founder of Masco Corporation), and many others — is one example of the pattern. The Lebanese-Armenian community produced scholars, writers, and political figures of influence in Lebanese national life. The pattern is consistent: a community that arrived with nothing but its skills and its memory rebuilt itself in each new location, generation by generation.

## The Chapters

**1 — Mashtots:** Around 405 CE, the monk Mesrop Mashtots sits down to create a script for a language that has been spoken for centuries without one. He begins with the sounds of Armenian, not the shapes of Greek or Aramaic letters. The result is 36 characters (later 38) that fit Armenian phonology precisely. The first sentence written in the new script is from the Bible. The civilization that follows is built on this foundation.

**2 — The Millet:** Under Ottoman rule, the Armenian community exists as a millet — a self-governing religious community with the right to manage its own internal affairs under the authority of the Patriarch of Constantinople. The system is not equality, but it is survival: schools, churches, newspapers, community organizations. In the nineteenth century, the Armenian middle class grows; Armenian merchants, doctors, lawyers, and bankers play important roles in Ottoman economic life. The millet system creates the conditions for survival and, eventually, for the envy and resentment that the Young Turks will weaponize.

**3 — The Hamidian Massacres:** 1894–1896: the Ottoman state, under Sultan Abdulhamid II, organizes massacres of Armenian communities across Anatolia. The European powers express concern and do nothing. Between 100,000 and 300,000 Armenians are killed. The Young Turks who overthrow Abdulhamid in 1908 speak of reform and equality. The Armenian community hopes. The hope is not fulfilled.

**4 — The Death March:** April 1915: Armenian families in Erzurum are told to gather their possessions. They are being relocated, the gendarmes say, for their own protection. The convoys that form will walk into the Syrian desert. Many will walk until they fall. The wells along the route are poisoned. The rivers fill with bodies. Of the estimated 2 million Armenians in Anatolia at the start of 1915, approximately 600,000 to 1.5 million will be dead by 1923.

**5 — Aleppo, 1921:** A survivor arrives in Aleppo with nothing: no house, no land, no documents, no money. The Syrian Arab community provides initial relief. Other Armenian survivors are already organizing: a church is being established, a school is being planned, a newspaper will start publishing within the year. Within a generation, the Aleppo Armenian community will be one of the most economically successful ethnic communities in the Arab world. The pattern repeats: arrive with nothing, build.

**6 — Tsitsernakaberd:** April 24, 2021: U.S. President Joe Biden becomes the first sitting American president to formally recognize the Armenian Genocide. Turkey protests. Armenia and its diaspora receive the recognition as a long-delayed justice. The Tsitsernakaberd memorial in Yerevan — the eternal flame, the wall of names — continues to burn. The names on the wall are still being added as survivors' families submit documentation. The work of witness is not finished.
