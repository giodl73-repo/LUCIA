## Domain Assessment

### The Parliament of 1295 as "Model Parliament"

The chapter correctly notes that the term "Model Parliament" is a later historians' designation, not a contemporary one. The chapter does not describe the 1295 Parliament as "the model" without quotation — it uses the formulation from Edward I's perspective that he did not know the formula "will be called 'the model parliament' by historians." This is historically accurate and appropriately skeptical of the Whig teleology.

The chapter's argument for what made 1295 constitutionally generative — the unified three-estate consent — is supported by my own work (Maddicott, pp. 153–165). However, I would add one qualification that the chapter should consider: the significance of 1295 was not fully recognized even by contemporaries. Subsequent parliaments did not consistently replicate the three-estate configuration. It was the pattern of the century — multiple parliaments with varying configurations, each building on the last — rather than the single 1295 Parliament that created the constitutional custom. The chapter understands this (it says "called nine times between 1295 and 1307") but might state more explicitly that the Model Parliament is a pattern rather than a singular event.

### On the Common Petition

The chapter's treatment of the emergence of the common petition (Section IV) is historically accurate. The transition from individual county grievances to collective commons petitions was gradual, occurring primarily in the parliaments of the 1290s and 1300s. The chapter correctly identifies this as the moment the commons became a political body rather than an aggregate of individuals.

**One addition:** The chapter could note that the common petitions were not initially drafted by the commons themselves but were often taken from multiple individual petitions and consolidated by clerks of Parliament — which means the institution of the commons' collective voice was partly an administrative creation, not simply a political one. This fits the chapter's own argument about how institutions are built by accumulating practices.

### De Montfort's Parliament

The characterization of the 1265 Parliament as an instrument against the king, repurposed by Edward I as an instrument for the king, is the correct analytical distinction. My own work (Maddicott, pp. 126–130) makes the same argument. The chapter's formulation — "the form was identical; the relationship was inverted" — is an apt summary.

### The Claim About Parliamentary Legitimacy and Edward II's Deposition

The chapter's analysis of Edward II's deposition (1327) is appropriately nuanced. It correctly observes that Parliament gave "form" to the deposition without providing authority — Mortimer had the army, Isabella had the coalition, Parliament gave the legitimacy. This distinction (form vs. authority) is the crucial one and the chapter makes it clearly.

**One caution:** The chapter states "It was not clear that a Parliament had the authority to depose a king, and no one wanted to establish that precedent too nakedly." This is correct as far as it goes, but should note that the procedure actually used was a renunciation — Edward II renounced the crown voluntarily (under considerable pressure) — rather than a formal parliamentary deposition. The Parliament then accepted the renunciation and acclaimed Edward III. The chapter's narrative implies this without stating it precisely.

---

## Recommended Revisions

1. **1295 as pattern rather than singular event:** Add one sentence in Section IV noting that the Model Parliament's constitutional significance lay in the repeated pattern of the following decade, not in the 1295 Parliament alone.
2. **Edward II's renunciation:** Specify that the 1327 procedure was a renunciation accepted by Parliament, not a parliamentary deposition per se.
3. **Common petition origins:** Optional addition about the administrative consolidation of individual petitions into common petitions.

---

## Board Verdict

**Historical accuracy:** High. Two clarifications recommended (renunciation vs. deposition; pattern vs. singular event).
**Source alignment:** The chapter's thesis is consistent with the best current scholarship on parliamentary origins.
**Cultural representation:** The Plantagenet constitutional world is represented with sophistication.

**Cleared for FINAL with two recommended clarifications.**
