# Plenitudo Potestatis — The Fullness of Power
## Papal States — Era 4 (1122–1303 CE)

---

In the ninth year of his pontificate, in the great hall of the Lateran, Lotario de Segni counted what he had made.

Six hundred bishops. Eight hundred abbots and priors. Delegates from every realm of Latin Christendom: from the northern seas where the conversion of the Norse was still recent, from the burning Iberian borderlands where the reconquest still bled, from the crusader cities of the Levant where Latin settlers had learned to sweat in unfamiliar light. The hall held them all and pressed them together, a thousand bodies in November, the heat of massed candles warming the stone, the Latin of the opening prayers rising from a dozen different accents, Flemish and Sicilian and German and the clipped vowels of the English delegates who had traveled for three weeks to be there. They had all come to Rome in the autumn of 1215, in the year called the forty-second of the general chapter of the Apostles, in the one thousand two hundred and fifteenth year from the birth of Christ. When Innocent rose to speak, the hall fell silent. They had come because Innocent III summoned them. That was sufficient.

He had been pope since he was thirty-seven years old. He was fifty-five now, and if the weight of what he held had aged him, the records do not say. What the records say is this: he opened the council with a sermon from Luke. "I have earnestly desired," Christ said at the Last Supper, "to eat this Passover with you before I suffer." Innocent applied the text to himself. He had earnestly desired this council. He had worked for years toward it. And like the Passover that preceded the Passion, this gathering preceded something he could not yet name.

The seventy-one canons he would promulgate before the council closed would shape the life of every Christian soul in the Latin West for the next three centuries. They defined what transubstantiation meant: that the bread and wine of the Mass became, in substance though not in appearance, the body and blood of Christ. They required every Christian who had reached the age of reason to confess their sins to their parish priest at least once a year and receive communion at Easter. They required Jews and Muslims living in Christian lands to wear distinctive clothing. They launched the Fifth Crusade toward Egypt. They created the formal legal category of the heretic and established the procedure by which one was to be identified and handed to the secular arm for punishment. They required cathedral chapters to support schools for educating clergy. They regulated the ordination of priests, the conduct of clergy in taverns, the wearing of red and green vestments, the keeping of concubines.

No council before it had covered so much. No council after it, until the long deliberations at Trent three centuries later, would cover more.

Innocent had not arrived at this moment by accident. He had arrived at it by argument. The argument was ancient, but he had sharpened it to a point that no predecessor had managed. The Bishop of Rome held the plenitudo potestatis: the fullness of power. Not some of the power. Not power within a certain domain. The fullness of it.

---

## I. The Settlement and Its Ambiguity (1122)

The era begins not with Innocent but with a treaty. In 1122, at Worms, the Holy Roman Emperor Henry V and Pope Calixtus II signed a concordat that ended the Investiture Controversy. The controversy had run for half a century, during which popes and emperors had excommunicated each other, deposed each other's candidates, and, in the famous winter of 1077, forced the emperor Henry IV to stand barefoot in the snow at Canossa for three days before the pope would lift the sentence that had freed his subjects from their oaths.

The Concordat of Worms appeared to settle the question by dividing it. The emperor would invest bishops with temporal authority, symbolized by the scepter. The pope would invest them with spiritual authority, symbolized by the ring and staff.

The canonists who worked through its implications, over the following decades, clarified what the settlement had actually done. If there were two kinds of investiture, temporal and spiritual, and if the bishop's temporal authority derived from his spiritual office, then who determined whether a candidate was spiritually qualified? The pope. The emperor could bestow lands and jurisdiction with a scepter, but the person receiving them had to first receive the ring and staff from Rome. The settlement had conceded the emperor's right to invest; it had simultaneously established that the person being invested was already the pope's appointee.

No emperor failed to understand this. Every subsequent conflict between pope and emperor in the twelfth century was a fight over what the Worms settlement actually meant: the long struggle between Frederick Barbarossa and Alexander III, the equally long struggle between Frederick II and Gregory IX and Innocent IV. The papacy read the settlement as establishing primacy. The emperors read it as establishing partnership. They were not both right.

The struggle also demonstrated something the papacy had not originally needed to consider: that the claim of universal spiritual authority required constant active enforcement. An authority that was never challenged could maintain itself at low cost. An authority challenged repeatedly and in multiple jurisdictions had to be defended, and defense required not only theology but also lawyers, legates, and the financial apparatus to send them. The Curia that developed in the twelfth century was partly the expression of the papacy's growing confidence in its authority; it was also the administrative response to the fact that authority, in order to remain real, had to be continuously asserted. The machinery of assertion became inseparable from the authority it was meant to express. Each new crisis that the papacy resolved through legal argument required more lawyers. Each new legal precedent generated more cases. The revenue that funded the lawyers came from the fees charged by the courts. The courts generated the precedents that required more courts. The machine built itself, one dispute at a time, across the entire breadth of Latin Christendom.

## II. The Canonists and the Architecture of Law (1140–1180)

Around 1140, in Bologna, a monk named Gratian completed a work he called the Concordia Discordantium Canonum: the Harmony of Discordant Canons. The title is the argument. The canons of the Church were not discordant. They appeared discordant. The apparent contradictions could be resolved if one understood how to read them.

Gratian's method was the distinctio. Consider two canons that seemed to contradict each other. A canon from an early council permitted a slave to marry without his master's consent. A decretal from a later pope addressed the required formalities for a public marriage contract. Were these incompatible? Not if one understood that they governed different domains. The early canon rested on the principle that consent to marriage is a spiritual act: the soul belongs to God, not to the master, and the master's authority over the body does not extend to the soul's free choice. The later decretal governed the public, external formalities of contracting a marriage among free persons. They did not conflict because they addressed different forums. The apparent contradiction dissolved when the question behind each canon was properly identified. They were answering different questions.

One question Gratian addressed directly: could a marriage contracted in ignorance of an impediment be valid? Suppose a man married a woman he did not know was his first cousin. The impediment of consanguinity existed at the time of marriage. Was the marriage valid? Gratian worked through the prior authorities. Some canons focused on the impediment itself: it existed, therefore the marriage was invalid. Others focused on consent: the parties had freely consented, and consent was the essence of marriage. Gratian's resolution: the marriage was invalid because of the impediment, but the contracting parties had committed no sin, because they had acted in good faith. Two outcomes, not one. An annulment without a moral condemnation. The legal question and the penitential question were distinct.

This was not hair-splitting. It was the creation of a legal system sophisticated enough to govern the actual complexity of human life. The same marriage might be canonically invalid and spiritually blameless. The same act might be heresy in one diocese and orthodox in another, depending on which council's canon applied, until Rome's decretal established which canon governed. Before Gratian, the local bishop decided most questions by precedent and judgment. After Gratian, every question had a method of resolution, and the method pointed toward Rome.

By the time Alexander III reorganized the Curia in the 1160s and 1170s, papal letters were the primary mechanism by which the law developed. A bishop in Scotland asked the pope whether a marriage was valid. The pope's letter back, called a decretal, resolved that specific case, and the resolution then became the rule for all similar cases. Rome accumulated authority one letter at a time. The bishops who corresponded with the Curia were simultaneously making appeals and generating precedent. They were, without necessarily intending it, building the papacy's universal jurisdiction one resolved dispute at a time.

The canon law that emerged from this process was real law. It governed marriage and divorce, inheritance and testamentary bequests, the legal status of clergy and laypeople, the definition of heresy and the procedure for prosecuting it. Across all of Latin Christendom, from the Irish monasteries to the crusader states, from the Iberian frontier to the Elbe River, the same body of law applied. A dispute in Castile could be appealed to the same court that heard a dispute in Hungary. The universality was not metaphorical. It was juridical.

This was the architecture. Innocent III walked into it thirty years later, identified every structural weakness, and set about reinforcing each one.

## III. The Height of the Claim (1198–1215)

Lotario de Segni had studied theology at Paris and law at Bologna. He was the most thoroughly educated man the College of Cardinals elected pope in the twelfth century, and he was elected unanimously, on the first ballot, within a day of his predecessor's death. He was thirty-seven. The cardinals who voted for him knew exactly what they were choosing.

He understood the plenitudo potestatis not as a claim to be asserted but as a logical consequence of the structure he had studied. The bishop of Rome was the successor of Peter, to whom Christ had given the keys of the kingdom. The keys meant this: what you bind on earth is bound in heaven, what you loose on earth is loosed in heaven. This was not a metaphor for general goodwill. It was a specific grant of jurisdiction over souls. The pope who excommunicated a Christian had bound that person, in the eyes of heaven, outside the community of the saved. Until that sentence was lifted, until the pope loosed it, the person remained bound. Not figuratively. Bound.

The secular consequence followed directly. A king's subjects owed him loyalty because the Church recognized their oaths as binding. A king whose subjects had been freed from their oaths by papal excommunication had no subjects. He had a territory. He had soldiers who served for wages. He did not have a kingdom in the sense that gave him a right to rule.

John of England found this out. In 1205, Innocent refused to accept John's candidate for Archbishop of Canterbury and instead appointed Stephen Langton. John refused to accept Langton. Innocent placed England under interdict: no Mass, no sacraments, no Christian burial, no bells. For six years, the churches of England stood silent. In 1209, Innocent excommunicated John personally. John's barons began to look at him differently. His rivals in France began to look at him as a man whose throne might legitimately be seized. In 1213, John submitted. He accepted Langton, paid compensation to the Church, and acknowledged England as a papal fief, a move that gave Innocent the right to intervene in English affairs as an overlord, not merely as a spiritual authority.

The acknowledgment of England as a papal fief had a consequence Innocent did not seek but could not refuse. In 1215, the same year as the council, John's barons forced him to seal Magna Carta, which placed formal limits on royal power. Innocent, as John's overlord, annulled the charter by papal bull, declaring it null, void, unjust, and iniquitous. The barons ignored the annulment. The charter remained in force. The plenitudo potestatis, extended into English domestic politics, reached the precise limit where the claims of ecclesiastical overlordship met the claims of baronial custom, and found that neither pope nor king could simply pronounce the other away.

Philip II of France received the same treatment over a different matter. Philip had put aside his queen, Ingeborg of Denmark, for reasons that canon law did not recognize as grounds for annulment. Innocent's legate placed Philip's lands under interdict. Philip's court argued. Philip submitted. What is notable is not that Philip submitted, since submission was the expected outcome eventually, but that the mechanism worked without a single papal soldier entering France.

Frederick II of Germany was more complicated. Innocent crowned him emperor in exchange for substantial concessions: promises about Sicily, promises about the German church, promises about a crusade. Frederick kept almost none of them. Innocent threatened and cajoled and negotiated. Frederick did not submit in any meaningful sense during Innocent's lifetime. The papacy's authority over the German emperor was always the most contested part of its universal claim.

The Fourth Crusade is where the engine revealed its limits most clearly. Innocent launched it in 1198, intending a campaign toward the Holy Land via Egypt. The crusaders, without the resources to pay the Venetians who were transporting them, were diverted first to the Christian city of Zara on the Dalmatian coast, which they sacked. Then to Constantinople, where a dynastic dispute within the Byzantine imperial family offered an opportunity the Venetian commercial interests could not resist. In April 1204, the crusaders took the city. They sacked it for three days.

Innocent's letters condemning the diversion of the crusade were in transit when Constantinople fell. His letter condemning the sack arrived when the sack was finished. He wrote to the crusade leaders that they had, through their conduct, made it more difficult to recover the Holy Land; that they had given the Eastern church more reason to resist reconciliation with Rome; that they had disgraced the crusade's purpose. He then, cautiously, accepted the Latin Empire that the crusaders established in Constantinople as a fact, and worked to ensure that the Latin ecclesiastical hierarchy installed there acknowledged his authority.

The fullness of power could pronounce. It could condemn. It could, afterward, recognize and incorporate. It could not undo what soldiers had done in three days.

The Fourth Lateran Council in November 1215 was Innocent's answer to this. If the crusade had escaped his control, the council would demonstrate what his authority could accomplish when exercised directly. The seventy-one canons were a complete legislative act: theology, sacramental practice, clerical discipline, the legal procedure for heresy, the regulation of religious orders, the status of Jews and Muslims in Christian society, the preparation for a new crusade. Every canon required something of someone. The requirement of annual confession required the parish priest to hear every adult in his parish. The parish priest in northern France who now had this obligation, a man whose name no chronicle records, who had served his village for twenty years without this specific demand on his time and his discretion, found himself by the council's decree the final point of contact in a system that reached from the hall in the Lateran to the smallest farmstead in Christendom. He was to hear what they had done. He was to keep it secret. He was to impose penance. He was to deny communion at Easter to those who had not come. His bishop would certify compliance. The bishop's certification would make its way, eventually, to the records of the Curia.

This was the machine at full operation. This was the fullness of power expressed not through excommunication and interdict but through annual repetition, in every parish, in every household, in every soul that had reached the age of reason.

The canon on confession also stated the penalty for any priest who revealed what he had heard. He was to be deposed from his priestly office and confined to a strict monastery to do penance for the rest of his life. There was no appeal. The council understood that a system requiring annual disclosure of private sin would collapse if the disclosure flowed in both directions. The priest who received confession became a sealed vessel. The seal was canonical, formal, and absolute. What entered the confessional stayed there, until it reached the hearing of the God to whom the whole apparatus was ultimately directed.

He had designed much of it. The Fourth Lateran Council was not an improvised response to particular crises; it was the systematic completion of a project he had been building since his first year as pope. The canons on confession, on transubstantiation, on heresy, on the regulation of clergy: they formed a single coherent system in which the authority of the Bishop of Rome reached every dimension of Christian life simultaneously. The theology of the Eucharist and the law of confession and the procedure for identifying heretics and the regulation of clerical dress were not separate matters. They were aspects of a single claim: that the spiritual governance of Christendom was unified, that its unity was real and juridical, and that its head was the pope.

## IV. The Machine Continues (1216–1260)

Innocent died in July 1216, in Perugia, on his way to negotiate a peace between Genoa and Pisa. He had been ill. The heat of an Italian summer, the road north from Rome, the practical diplomatic necessity of keeping two merchant cities from destroying each other's fleets: these were the conditions of his death. Not a throne room, not a moment of ceremony. The road.

The machine continued without him.

Gregory IX, who became pope in 1227, was Innocent's nephew. He had watched, as a young cardinal, how the system worked. He now formalized two aspects of it that Innocent had left partially developed.

The first was the Inquisition. Heresy, now defined by the Fourth Lateran canons as a specific legal category, required a specific legal procedure. The procedure Gregory established in 1231 and 1232 was not arbitrary. It was methodical. An inquisitor arrived in a territory and announced a period of grace: anyone who confessed voluntarily and abjured their heresy would receive a lighter penance. After the grace period, the inquisitor began formal proceedings. The accused was questioned under oath. The witnesses against them were not named, since the inquisitor feared reprisals, but their testimony was recorded and could be examined for consistency. If the accused confessed, they abjured their heresy and received their penance. If they refused to confess despite the evidence, and if the inquisitor found them guilty, they were "relaxed to the secular arm," that is, handed to the civil authorities for punishment, which could mean execution.

The procedure rested on the distinction between the Church's spiritual authority and the secular power's temporal authority. The inquisitor did not execute. The inquisitor judged and handed over. The secular authority executed. The distinction was canonical and precise, and it did not diminish what happened to the person handed over. But it was the distinction that made the Inquisition a papal institution rather than a royal one: the Church adjudicated the spiritual question; the state acted on the temporal consequence.

The second thing Gregory formalized was the Decretals. Innocent III had issued hundreds of decretals, the letters that resolved legal questions and thereby created precedent. Gregory commissioned Raymond of Penafort, a Dominican lawyer, to compile and organize them. Raymond finished in 1234. The result was a second body of canon law, following and extending Gratian's Decretum, carrying the papacy's legislative activity into the next generation. Each new pope who issued decretals added to the system. The system grew.

The mendicant orders, the Franciscans and Dominicans both officially recognized by Innocent III in the years just before the council, became the papacy's mobile agents. They were not bound to a specific monastery or diocese. They traveled. They preached. They heard confessions, a privilege granted by papal authority overriding the usual requirement to confess to one's parish priest. They staffed the Inquisition tribunals. Francis of Assisi had wanted voluntary poverty outside all institutional form; he ended up leading an order with a formal rule, papal recognition, and legal privileges. The institution had absorbed the reform impulse and given it structure. The structure made it more useful to the papacy and, according to some of Francis's early followers, less faithful to what Francis had originally intended. Both things were true simultaneously.

Gregory IX also fought bitterly with Frederick II for most of his pontificate. He excommunicated Frederick twice. Frederick twice ignored the sentence and then submitted on terms that were largely his own. The German emperors were the crack in the system that would not seal.

## V. The Terminal Condition: Boniface VIII at Anagni (1294–1303)

Benedetto Caetani became Boniface VIII in 1294. He had been a canon lawyer. He had served as a papal legate and a cardinal. He understood the system as well as anyone alive. When he became pope, he understood also what the system was facing.

Philip IV of France needed revenue. Wars cost money. Philip taxed his clergy. The clergy appealed to Rome. Boniface issued a papal bull in 1296, Clericis laicos, prohibiting clergy from paying taxes to secular rulers without papal consent, and prohibiting secular rulers from demanding such taxes. Philip responded by prohibiting the export of gold and silver from France, a measure that immediately cut off a significant portion of papal revenue, since French clergy could no longer send their contributions to Rome. Boniface backed down.

The conflict continued over other issues. Philip arrested a French bishop on a charge of treason. Bishops, Boniface held, were not subject to secular criminal jurisdiction without papal permission. Philip rejected this. In 1302, Boniface issued the bull Unam Sanctam. It was the fullest formulation of papal supremacy ever promulgated. "There is one holy catholic and apostolic church," it began, "outside of which there is neither salvation nor remission of sins." The church had two swords: the spiritual and the material. The material sword was wielded by secular power but at the direction and with the permission of the Church. "Therefore we declare, state, define, and pronounce that it is altogether necessary for salvation for every human creature to be subject to the Roman Pontiff."

Every human creature. The fullness of power, stated without qualification.

Philip IV convened an assembly and charged Boniface with heresy, simony, and a list of other crimes. He demanded a general council to judge the pope. Boniface threatened to excommunicate Philip. Philip's agent Guillaume de Nogaret, with the support of the Colonna family, a Roman noble family that had conducted a running feud with Boniface for years, arrived at Anagni on September 7, 1303.

Boniface was at his summer palace in his birthplace. He was in his mid-seventies. He knew they were coming; there had been warnings; and he did not flee. He dressed in his full pontifical vestments and waited in the great hall. The soldiers came in. Sciarra Colonna, who led the Italian contingent, reportedly wanted to kill him on the spot. Nogaret, whose legal training told him that killing a pope would produce consequences no one could manage, restrained this. Boniface reportedly offered his neck. He told them to proceed with what they had come to do.

They held him for two days. The town of Anagni rose on Sunday morning and freed him. The soldiers left. Boniface was escorted back to Rome by the cardinals who came for him.

He died thirty-five days later, in October 1303.

The bull Unam Sanctam remained in force. Philip's assembly's charges had produced no council, no trial, no verdict. The Anagni incident was not, in any formal sense, a defeat of the papacy's legal claims. Boniface had stated the fullness of power in its most extreme form, been physically seized, and died. The claims remained on the books. No one retracted them.

But something had been demonstrated that was not legal and not theological. The claim that every human creature's salvation required submission to the Bishop of Rome had met a king who found that claim inconvenient, and the king had sent soldiers, and the soldiers had walked into the room. The legitimacy machine, the machine that had worked on John of England through interdict and on Philip II of France through the same mechanism and on a hundred smaller lords through the threat of excommunication, had met in Philip IV of France a party who had decided not to pay the debt. The machine had no instrument for this eventuality. It could issue bulls. It could condemn. It could threaten. Philip's soldiers were already in the room.

What followed was Avignon. The papacy, under a French pope elected within a year of Boniface's death, moved its seat to southern France and remained there for seventy years, under the de facto influence of the French crown. The fullness of power relocated from Rome to a city the French king could see from his own territory. The council's canons still governed annual confession in every parish in Christendom. Canon law still adjudicated marriages and inheritances from Scotland to Jerusalem. The Inquisition still proceeded. The machine Innocent III had operated from the Lateran still ran. Its operator had changed address.

The council's work continued without him. As it was designed to do.

## Closing

The years between 1122 and 1303 are the papacy at the full development of its theory and the full exposure of its limitation. The Concordat of Worms established the distinction on which the papacy built its universal claim. Gratian gave the claim a legal system that could operate at the level of every disputed marriage and contested inheritance in Latin Christendom. Innocent III extended the system to its furthest reach: kings submitting, councils legislating, a parish priest in every village now obligated by law to hear the annual confession of every adult soul in his care. Gregory IX institutionalized what Innocent had improvised. Boniface VIII stated the conclusion of the argument with absolute clarity and died in a room where soldiers stood.

The argument had been correct, within its own premises. The premises required the cooperation of parties who found the premises useful. When the premises became inconvenient, the parties revised their understanding of what the premises required.

Philip IV of France, who sent soldiers to Anagni, also engaged theologians and canon lawyers who argued, in published treatises, that the pope's authority was limited to spiritual matters and that secular rulers were not subordinate to Rome in temporal affairs. The arguments were not novel; bishops and lay scholars had made versions of them for generations. The papacy had answered such arguments before by asserting that no general council could override the authority of Peter's successor. In 1302, Philip IV's lawyers added an army to the argument. The *Unam Sanctam* was answered not just by soldiers but by treatises.

Canon law continued. The Curia continued. The annual confession continued. The institution that had held the fullness of power did not dissolve. It adapted. The fullness, as a claim, remained in force on the books. As a living political reality, it had peaked at the Fourth Lateran Council in November 1215, in a hall full of bishops, before the silence that fell when Innocent rose to speak.
