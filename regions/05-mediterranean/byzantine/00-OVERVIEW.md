# Rhomaion — The Romans of the East

We called ourselves Rhomaion — Romans. Not because we could not see that our capital was on the Bosphorus rather than the Tiber, not because we forgot that a western court had fallen in 476 CE, but because Rome was not a place. Rome was an order: the Christian Roman order, the empire of law and Augustus and the Senate and the emperor who governed as God's viceroy on earth. We were that order's continuation, and we knew it, and for eleven centuries we maintained it against every pressure that came from every direction — the Persian campaigns, the Arab conquests, the Bulgarian wars, the Crusaders who sacked our capital in 1204, the Ottoman advance that finally ended us in 1453.

From our city of Konstantinopolis — the city built on the Bosphorus that controlled the only passage between the Mediterranean and the Black Sea, protected on three sides by water — we governed what Rome had left us and then rebuilt from what we had. At our height under Justinian in the sixth century, we had reconquered North Africa and Italy and southern Spain. We had codified Roman law into the *Corpus Juris Civilis* that every law school in Europe would study for a thousand years. We had built the Hagia Sophia, whose dome was the largest in the world for nine centuries. We governed in Greek, worshipped in Greek, argued in Greek — but we were *Romaioi*, Romans, and that identity was not negotiable.

## What Defined Them

**The emperor as God's viceroy.** The Byzantine understanding of imperial authority was theological as much as political: the emperor was the earthly image of the heavenly king, appointed by God to rule the Christian people as God rules the cosmos. This was not merely ceremonial language. It had institutional consequences: the emperor presided over church councils, appointed patriarchs, and legislated on matters of doctrine. The relationship between imperial and ecclesiastical authority was never fully resolved — the Iconoclast controversy tore the empire apart for over a century — but the framework was always that they should be unified, because the Christian empire was a single organism whose health was God's concern.

**The law of Justinian.** The *Corpus Juris Civilis* — the body of civil law that Justinian's legal commission assembled from centuries of Roman juristic writing — was the most comprehensive legal codification the ancient world produced. It organized Roman law into the *Institutiones* (an introductory textbook), the *Digesta* (the jurists' writings), the *Codex* (the imperial legislation), and the *Novellae* (Justinian's new laws). When western Europe rediscovered this corpus in the 11th century at Bologna, it became the foundation of European legal education and eventually of most European legal systems. The Byzantines maintained and transmitted the Roman legal tradition through the centuries when the west had lost it.

**The city on the Bosphorus.** Konstantinopolis was not simply the empire's capital. It was the empire's most important strategic asset. Situated at the only passage between the Mediterranean and the Black Sea, protected on two sides by water and on the third by the Theodosian Walls — the most sophisticated defensive fortification of the ancient and medieval world — the city was virtually impregnable to any army that lacked a fleet. For a thousand years, every assault on the city failed. The Arabs besieged it twice and failed. The Bulgarians besieged it and failed. Only when the Fourth Crusade betrayed its nominal purpose and attacked from the sea did the walls finally fall — and even then the city was recovered within sixty years.

**Greek as the language of civilization.** Byzantine culture was conducted in Greek — not the *koine* of the New Testament but the classical Attic Greek that educated Byzantines learned as a second literary language alongside their spoken medieval Greek. This attachment to classical Greek language was not archaism for its own sake. It was the maintenance of a continuous cultural tradition that connected the Byzantines to Homer and Plato and Aristotle and Thucydides — to the whole heritage of classical thought that the Byzantines preserved, copied, and transmitted to the Renaissance west. The manuscript tradition of most surviving Greek classical literature passes through Byzantine scriptoria.

**The Hippodrome as the political theater.** The Hippodrome of Konstantinopolis — the vast chariot-racing stadium adjacent to the palace — was the place where the emperor displayed his power and the people expressed their will. The chariot teams, identified by color (Blues, Greens, Reds, Whites), were the organizing framework of popular political identity in the Byzantine city. When the factions rioted — as they did in the Nika Riot of 532 CE, which nearly destroyed Justinian's reign — it was in the Hippodrome that the crisis played out. The emperor addressed the people from the imperial box. The relationship between ruler and ruled was, in some meaningful sense, enacted there.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Founding and Justinian's Reconquest:** Constantine stands at the Bosphorus and marks the city's boundaries with his spear. Three centuries later, Justinian sits in the Hippodrome after the Nika Riot has been suppressed and begins planning the Hagia Sophia. The dome that will stand for nine centuries as the largest in the world is being designed by a mathematician.

**2 — The Age of Crisis and Survival:** The Arab fleet is in the Sea of Marmara. The city deploys Greek fire — the naval weapon that no one else can replicate — and the fleet burns. The empire has lost Egypt and Syria and will never get them back, but the city holds. The empire remakes itself around what it still has.

**3 — The Macedonian Renaissance:** The emperor Basil I — a peasant from Macedonia who rose to the throne by killing his predecessor — begins a dynasty that will last two centuries. Byzantine missionaries carry Orthodox Christianity and the Cyrillic alphabet to the Slavic world. The empire's cultural radiation is, in some ways, wider than its political reach.

**4 — The Decline and the Crusades:** The army of the Fourth Crusade, diverted from its stated purpose by Venetian commercial interests, sails into the Golden Horn and sacks the Christian capital of the Christian world. Byzantines who survive the sack will spend the rest of their lives trying to understand how this was allowed to happen.

**5 — The Restoration and the Long End:** Constantine XI Palaiologos puts on his armor and walks to the wall as the Ottoman guns open fire in the final assault. He is last seen fighting in the breach. The city falls on May 29, 1453. Mehmed II rides to the Hagia Sophia and orders the minarets built. The Christian Roman order, eleven centuries after Constantine built his city, ends on that morning.
