# Íslendingar — "The People of Iceland"

We came to this island because we refused. Refused the centralization of Harald Fairhair, who was making Norway too small for men who had always governed themselves. Refused to give up the custom of the *Ting* — the assembly where disputes were argued, law was stated, and no man was required to bow. We took our high-seat pillars and threw them into the sea as we approached an unknown shore, letting the gods choose the landing place, and we built a society on an island with no king, no standing army, and no coercive authority except what the assembly could produce. For almost four centuries — from the first settlement in 874 to the submission to Norway in 1262 — Iceland was governed by the *Althing*: the world's first national parliamentary assembly.

The saga is our form. Not the heroic epic of the continent, not the chronicle of kings, but the saga — the prose narrative of farmers and their feuds, of women who outlived several husbands and managed their own estates, of ships and voyages and the Iceland of three generations ago when the land was being shaped by human decision. The sagas are not written down until the thirteenth century, but they preserve the memory of the ninth and tenth centuries with extraordinary texture: the names of farms, the genealogies, the specific phrases of the *Althing* proceedings. They are the form a society without a king invented to remember itself.

Key places:
- **Þingvellir (Thingvellir)** — the great rift valley where the Althing met; the lava plain between the tectonic plates; the founding place of Icelandic identity
- **Skálholt** — the first bishopric, founded 1056; the center of ecclesiastical and intellectual life
- **Reykholt** — Snorri Sturluson's farm; where the *Prose Edda* and *Heimskringla* were written
- **Hólar** — the northern bishopric; the second intellectual center

## What Defined Them

**The Althing — governance without a state.** The Althing, founded at Þingvellir in 930 CE, met for two weeks each June on the great lava plain at the edge of the rift valley. It was not a parliament in the modern sense — it had no permanent bureaucracy, no enforcement mechanism, and no army. It was a meeting of the thirty-six chieftains (*goðar*) and their followers, where cases were argued before the Law Council (*lögrétta*), settlements were negotiated, and the *lögsögumaðr* (law speaker) recited from memory one-third of the law code each year (the entire code every three years). What held it together was the recognition that the alternative to lawful settlement was feud — and feud in a country with only 50,000 people and no external enemies was ultimately self-defeating.

**The saga as the form of self-knowledge.** The sagas — *Njáls saga*, *Egils saga*, *Laxdæla saga*, *Eyrbyggja saga* and fifty others — are the Icelandic literary achievement that has no peer in medieval vernacular literature for psychological depth and narrative sophistication. They are written in the thirteenth century but set in the ninth and tenth; they preserve genuine historical memory alongside narrative invention. What is remarkable about them is their perspective: they are told from the inside, without moral editorializing, presenting characters who are simultaneously sympathetic and terrible, events that are simultaneously inevitable and contingent. The saga form assumes that the reader can judge without being told how to judge. This is a literary democracy.

**The goðorð — chieftaincy as contract.** The Icelandic chieftain (*goði*) held his position through a chieftaincy (*goðorð*) that was, uniquely, transferable — it could be bought, sold, divided, and inherited. The chieftain did not hold authority over a territory; he held a relationship with a group of followers (*þingmenn*) who had chosen to attach themselves to him. A follower who was dissatisfied could switch to another chieftain. This created a competitive market for leadership: chieftains who failed to protect their followers, settle their disputes successfully, or represent their interests at the Althing lost followers to rivals. The goðorð system is a constitutional structure built from individual choice rather than geographic jurisdiction.

**The law speaker's memory.** The *lögsögumaðr* — the law speaker — held what was perhaps the most important office in the Icelandic Commonwealth. He was elected for a three-year term and was required to recite the law from memory at the Althing, one-third per year. He had no coercive power. He could not enforce anything. But he was the authoritative source of what the law said, and in a society without written law, that authority was enormous. The law speaker who misremembered or distorted the law could be corrected by others present — but the correction required knowledge, and knowledge required training. The law speaker's role was therefore both political and educational.

**The horizon as the Icelandic condition.** Iceland is an island in the North Atlantic, visible from no other land. Its settlers brought Norwegian culture to a place without any of Norway's constraints — no king, no aristocracy with established positions, no church, no neighboring peoples with whom to wage territorial war. What they built in this vacancy was original: a society organized around law, poetry, farming, and the sea. And when that society became crowded with its own internal conflicts, some of them went further — Greenland, Vinland, the end of the known world. The horizon was always the Icelandic answer to what was wrong with the available options.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Settlement:** Ingólfr Arnarson watches his high-seat pillars drift ashore at the steaming bay. The land is empty — only a few Irish monks, who leave when the Norse arrive. He will spend the rest of his life farming a place that has no name yet. He names it for the steam. The steam is still there.

**2 — The Commonwealth:** The Althing meets at Þingvellir for the first time. The law speaker climbs the Law Rock and recites the oath. There is no king present because there is no king. The men assembled understand that what they are doing is unusual. They do it anyway, and come back next year, and the year after.

**3 — The Taking of Christianity:** At the Althing of 1000, the assembled Icelanders are on the verge of civil war between the Christians and the pagans. The law speaker Þorgeirr Ljósvetningagoði withdraws under his cloak for a day and a night. When he emerges, he has a decision: Iceland will be Christian, in public. What people do privately is their own business. Both sides accept this. The compromise holds for a generation.

**4 — The Age of the Sturlungs:** Snorri Sturluson — the greatest writer Iceland ever produced — is assassinated at Reykholt in 1241 by agents of the Norwegian king he has been playing against his domestic rivals. He runs into his cellar, crying "Do not strike!" They strike. The man who preserved Norse mythology for the world dies in a cellar, the casualty of a political miscalculation.

**5 — The Norwegian Period:** The sagas are being written down. The Commonwealth is over; the violence of the Sturlung age ended it. But the writers working in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries are preserving the memory of a different Iceland — the Iceland of the settlement, of the Althing at its peak, of men and women who governed themselves without a king. They are writing a lament in the form of a story.
