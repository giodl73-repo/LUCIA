## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of The High Tang (Opening)

**Overall:** The voice is largely right. Du Fu is not a specimen — he is a young man in a city, and the city is his world, not ours to admire. But there are moments where the prose steps back to frame the scene for a reader who stands outside, and those moments need attention.

**What works:**
- The opening belongs to its world. The Kaiyuan reign year leads; the Common Era date follows in parentheses. The keju is named before it is described. Chang'an is a place before it is a concept. These are the correct instincts.
- Du Fu's examination failure is presented without modern sympathy. "He would try again. He would try again for the next ten years, and fail each time, and the failure would shape everything he wrote." This is stated as fact within the world's logic, not as a comment for the reader's benefit. The keju was the path. He did not succeed on the path. The prose does not grieve for him; it observes.
- The religions in Chang'an are presented as part of the city's functioning, not as exhibits of tolerance. The Nestorians wore Tang robes. The Zoroastrian temple was three wards from the palace. These are spatial facts, not moral arguments.

**What doesn't:**
- "The architecture of a confidence so immense that it could welcome any god without fearing for its own" — this sentence is beautifully constructed but it is the author speaking, not the world speaking. A person in Chang'an in Kaiyuan 23 would not have described their city's religious diversity as "confidence." They would have simply lived in it. The sentence analyzes from a slight distance.
- The final paragraph — "who did not yet know they were living in the last years of something" — is the author's voice, not Du Fu's and not Chang'an's. It is the voice of someone who knows what comes next. The sentence is effective as narrative craft, but it is an outside perspective superimposed on an inside moment.

**The question I'd push on:**
The full chapter will cover An Lushan's rebellion. The temptation will be to narrate the rebellion as the loss of something beautiful — the golden age destroyed. But whose golden age? Du Fu's? The emperor's? The Sogdian merchant's? The conscripted farmer's? The voice must stay inside, and "inside" means inside the specific person whose experience is being rendered, not inside a generalized nostalgia for a civilization at its peak. Whose loss are you writing?
