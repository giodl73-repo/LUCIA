# The Crisis and the Late Empire

## I. The Crisis

The emperor Diocletian stood in the palace at Nicomedia in the year 1057 of Roma's founding (303 CE) and decided that the empire was too large for one man to hold.

He had reason to know. The century between the assassination of Commodus in 192 and Diocletian's accession in 284 had tested every load-bearing wall the principate possessed. More than fifty men claimed the purple in those ninety years, most violently, most briefly. The frontiers broke in the 250s: the Persian king Shapur I captured the emperor Valerian alive in 260, a humiliation no Roman precedent could process, and the captured emperor was displayed at the Persian court and may have been used as a mounting block for the king's horse. In the same decade, the Palmyrene queen Zenobia carved an independent state from the eastern provinces, and Germanic peoples crossed the Rhine and Danube and raided as far south as Athens.

The plague arrived in the 250s. The disease, probably smallpox, killed perhaps a quarter of the empire's population in some regions. Cities that had thrived under the Pax Romana contracted. Tax receipts fell because the people who paid taxes were dead or fled. The army's manpower shrank, and the army's demands on the surviving population grew, because the frontier required soldiers and the soldiers required pay and the pay came from the taxes and the taxes came from fewer people. The coinage was debased to cover the shortfall: the silver denarius that Augustus had minted with confidence was, by the 270s, a copper coin with a thin wash of silver that fooled no one. Prices rose. The economy contracted. The roads that had connected the empire's markets were less safe, and the goods that traveled on them cost more.

Diocletian's response was systematic. He divided the empire into a Tetrarchy: two senior emperors (Augusti) and two junior emperors (Caesares), one pair governing the west, the other the east. He doubled the number of provinces and created a new administrative layer, the diocese, between the province and the center. He reformed the tax system, tying tax assessment to regular censuses that counted heads and land together. He reformed the army, enlarging it and creating a mobile field army (the comitatenses) separate from the frontier garrison forces (the limitanei). He fixed prices with an edict that specified the maximum price for every commodity from wheat to fish sauce to lawyers' fees. The price edict failed, because the economy did not cooperate with legislation, but the attempt revealed the scale of what Diocletian was trying to do: hold together a system that was coming apart at every joint.

He also launched the last great persecution of the Christians. In 303, edicts ordered churches destroyed, sacred books burned, clergy imprisoned, and citizens required to sacrifice to the traditional gods. The persecution was severe in the east, less so in the west where the Caesar Constantius Chlorus enforced it half-heartedly. The Christians, who had grown from a marginal sect to perhaps ten percent of the empire's population, endured the persecution as they had endured earlier ones: some recanted, some hid, some died, and the communities that survived were hardened by the experience.

Diocletian retired in 305. He walked out of the palace at Nicomedia, laid down the purple, and went to his palace at Split on the Dalmatian coast, where he grew cabbages and refused to return to power when the system he had designed began to collapse. He was the first emperor since Augustus to leave office alive and voluntarily, and when his former colleagues asked him to resume the purple, he reportedly told them that if they could see the cabbages he had planted, they would not ask.

## II. Constantine

The Tetrarchy lasted less than two years after Diocletian's abdication before it dissolved into civil war.

The war was fought between the sons and favorites of the retiring Augusti, men who held armies and claimed the title their fathers or patrons had held, and the war consumed the empire's attention from 306 to 324. When it ended, one man stood: Flavius Valerius Constantinus, the son of Constantius Chlorus, proclaimed Augustus by his father's troops at Eboracum in Britannia and victor over every rival the next eighteen years produced.

Constantine's decision regarding Christianity was the hinge of the Roman world. In 312, before the Battle of the Milvian Bridge against his rival Maxentius, Constantine reportedly experienced a vision. The sources differ on what he saw. Lactantius says he dreamed of the chi-rho monogram on his soldiers' shields. Eusebius says he saw a cross of light in the sky with the words "by this sign, conquer." What is certain is that after the victory, Constantine favored the Christian community with legal privileges, financial support, and political access. The Edict of Milan in 313, jointly issued with his co-emperor Licinius, granted Christians (and all others) freedom of worship and ordered the return of confiscated property.

Constantine was not, in this decision, rejecting the Roman tradition. He was extending it. The Roman system had always integrated divine support into political authority. The augures had read the gods' will. The emperor had held the office of pontifex maximus. The divine relationship was structural, not merely sentimental: the state's success depended on the gods' favor, and the rituals that maintained the favor were political acts. Constantine shifted the divine relationship from the traditional gods to the Christian God, and the shift was made through the same political logic: the God who had given him victory at the Milvian Bridge was the God whose favor the state would now maintain.

The Council of Nicaea in 325, which Constantine convened and over which he presided, was an act of the Roman state as much as an act of the church. The council produced the Nicene Creed, the statement of orthodox belief that defined the boundary between acceptable and unacceptable Christian theology. Constantine's interest was not theological. It was political: a church divided by doctrinal disputes was a church that could not serve as a stabilizing institution, and Constantine needed a stabilizing institution. The council was called in the same spirit the Senate had once been consulted: to reach a consensus that the state could enforce. The bishops who attended, traveling on the imperial roads, housed at imperial expense, debating in the imperial palace, were participating in an institution that looked, from the outside, remarkably like a senatorial assembly with a different vocabulary.

Constantine built a new capital. In 330, he dedicated Constantinople, the city he had built on the site of the Greek colony Byzantion at the Bosphorus, the strait connecting the Mediterranean to the Black Sea. The location was strategic: closer to the eastern frontier where the Persian threat required permanent attention, closer to the wealthy eastern provinces that paid the bulk of the empire's taxes, positioned at the intersection of European and Asian trade routes. The city was designed as a new Roma: a Senate, a Forum, an imperial palace, seven hills, a grain supply shipped from Egypt. The old Roma remained the empire's symbolic center, but the operational center had moved east, and the move acknowledged what the third-century crisis had revealed: the empire's weight had shifted.

## III. The Two Romes

By the mid-fourth century, the empire operated as two administrative units that shared a legal system, a military structure, and an emperor who was, in theory, singular and indivisible.

The eastern half was wealthier. The provinces of Syria, Egypt, Asia Minor, and Greece produced more tax revenue, sustained larger populations, and maintained more active urban economies than the western provinces. Constantinople grew rapidly, attracting population from across the eastern Mediterranean, its markets carrying goods from the Silk Road that arrived through Persia, its harbor hosting the grain fleet that fed the city as Ostia had fed Roma. The eastern cities, built on Greek foundations, continued to function as centers of commerce and intellectual life. The schools of Athens, the law school of Berytus, the library of Alexandria, the theological faculties of Antioch and Constantinople maintained traditions of learning that had survived the third-century crisis with less disruption than their western counterparts.

The western half was poorer, less urbanized, and more vulnerable. The cities of Gaul, Iberia, and Britannia had contracted during the third century. The villa economy of the western countryside, where the large estates of senatorial families produced grain and wine and oil, depended on enslaved or bound labor and on markets that were increasingly local rather than imperial. The Rhine and Danube frontiers required constant military attention, and the armies that defended them were increasingly recruited from the very peoples they were supposed to defend against: Franks, Goths, Alamanni, Vandals, men who served Roma because Roma paid them and would serve whoever paid them next.

The distinction between Roman and non-Roman was dissolving at the frontier. Germanic leaders served as Roman generals. Roman generals employed Germanic warriors. The foederati, the federate troops bound by treaty to serve the emperor in exchange for land and subsidies, were organized under their own chiefs and fought by their own methods, and the distinction between a Roman army with Germanic soldiers and a Germanic army in Roman service was increasingly difficult to draw. Stilicho, the general who defended the western empire in the 390s and 400s, was himself of Vandal descent, married to the emperor Theodosius's niece, commanding an army that included Goths, Huns, and Alans alongside Roman regulars. The empire was not being invaded by outsiders. It was being transformed by people who were already inside.

## IV. The Christian World

To understand what Christianity meant inside the Roman experience, begin with the cemetery.

Before Constantine, the Christian dead were buried in catacombs outside Roma's walls, in underground galleries cut from the soft tufa beneath the Via Appia and the Via Salaria. The galleries were narrow, the niches in the walls close together, and the air smelled of damp stone and the oil of the lamps that lit the passages for the living who came to honor the dead. The walls were painted: the Good Shepherd carrying a lamb, the fish that was the acrostic of Christ's name in Greek, the orantes, the figures with arms raised in prayer. The paintings were simple, the colors the earth pigments that were cheapest, because the Christians of the second and third centuries were not, for the most part, wealthy. They were freedmen and women, artisans, soldiers, merchants, people whose position in the Roman social hierarchy was modest and whose membership in the Christian community offered something the hierarchy did not: a community of equals before God, regardless of census class.

After Constantine, the Christians built churches. The basilica, the Roman architectural form used for law courts and commercial exchanges, was adapted for worship: the long nave, the apse at one end, the columns along the sides, the scale that could hold a congregation. The Basilica of St. Peter, which Constantine began on the Vatican hill over what the tradition held was the apostle's tomb, was one of the largest buildings in Roma. The church replaced the catacomb, and the replacement was not merely architectural. The community that had met in private houses and underground galleries now gathered in buildings that dominated the urban skyline, funded by imperial patronage, staffed by a clergy that was increasingly organized on the model of the Roman administrative system: bishops governing dioceses, metropolitans overseeing provinces, the bishop of Roma claiming primacy.

The shift from persecution to dominance took three generations. A grandmother who had hidden her copy of the scriptures during Diocletian's persecution could have lived to see her grandson appointed bishop by an emperor who considered the Christian God the empire's patron. The speed of the transformation was disorienting, and the Christian community struggled with what it meant. The theologians debated: was the emperor's favor a blessing or a temptation? Was the church's new wealth a tool for good works or a corruption of the radical equality the catacombs had maintained? Was the God who had sustained the martyrs the same God who now blessed the armies?

Augustine of Hippo, writing in North Africa in the early fifth century, gave the most enduring answer. The City of God, written in response to the Visigothic sack of Roma in 410, argued that the Roman state was not and had never been the vehicle of divine purpose. The earthly city, the city of human ambition and power, was transient by nature. The city of God, the community of the faithful, existed within the earthly city but was not defined by it. Roma's fall, if fall it was, did not affect the city of God, because the city of God was not built on the Tiber or the Bosphorus or any other river. It was built on faith.

Augustine was a Roman citizen, educated in the Roman rhetorical tradition, writing in Latin, using the categories of Roman law and Greek philosophy to argue that the Roman state's ultimate significance was limited. The argument was Roman in its method and un-Roman in its conclusion. The pietas that had organized Roman life for a millennium, the obligation owed to the state and the ancestors, was reframed: the obligation was owed to God, and the state's claim was subordinate. The transformation was not the replacement of one religion by another. It was the replacement of one framework of obligation by another, carried out within the Roman legal and institutional system by men and women who were themselves products of that system.

## V. The Western Fragmentation

In the fifth century, the western provinces ceased to be governed from a single center.

The process was not a single event and should not be told as one. There was no morning when the citizens of Gaul or Iberia or Britannia woke up and discovered they were no longer Roman. What happened was a transfer, gradual in some places and sudden in others, of effective military and political authority from the imperial administration to the leaders of the Germanic communities that had been settling within the empire's borders for generations.

The Visigoths, who had entered the empire in 376 fleeing the Huns, and who had defeated a Roman army and killed the emperor Valens at Adrianople in 378, were settled in Aquitaine in the 410s as foederati. Their king Alaric had sacked Roma in 410, an event that shocked the Mediterranean world because the city had not been taken by a foreign enemy in eight centuries. But Alaric was not a foreign enemy in the simple sense. He was a Roman officer who had served the empire and who wanted a formal position within it. His sack of Roma was a negotiating tactic, not a civilizational confrontation. The Visigoths wanted land, legal status, and recognition. The empire eventually gave them Aquitaine and, later, Iberia.

The Vandals crossed through Gaul and Iberia to North Africa, where they established a kingdom centered on Carthago, the city Roma had destroyed six centuries earlier and rebuilt as a Roman colony. The Vandal seizure of Africa in the 430s cut Roma's grain supply and forced the western empire to depend on Italian and Sicilian production, which could not sustain the city's population at its previous level.

The Franks settled in northern Gaul. The Burgundians settled in southeastern Gaul. The Angles, Saxons, and Jutes crossed to Britannia, where the Roman garrison had been withdrawn in 410 and where the remaining Romano-British population was left to organize its own defense. Each settlement was different. Some were negotiated. Some were violent. Some involved the near-total replacement of the existing power structure. Others involved the Germanic leaders ruling through existing Roman administrative forms, maintaining the law, the roads, the tax system, the church, because the infrastructure was useful and the expertise to run it was Roman.

The last western emperor, Romulus Augustulus, was deposed by Odoacer, a Germanic general, in 476 CE. The event is in the sources, but its significance at the time was less than later historians would make it. Odoacer did not destroy the empire. He sent the imperial insignia to Constantinople and asked the eastern emperor to recognize him as the legitimate ruler of Italy. The eastern emperor, in theory, had always been the senior partner. The western throne was, by 476, an office without the power to justify its existence, and its abolition was administrative as much as revolutionary.

In Constantinople, the empire continued. The eastern emperor ruled from the Bosphorus, commanding armies, collecting taxes, administering law, maintaining the infrastructure of governance that Augustus had built and Constantine had transferred. The Senate in Constantinople met. The chariot races in the Hippodrome drew crowds of eighty thousand. The theological debates continued in the councils. The law was studied at Berytus and applied in every court. Justinian, who became emperor in 527, would reconquer parts of the west, build the Hagia Sophia, and codify the entire body of Roman law into the Corpus Iuris Civilis, the Body of Civil Law, which organized everything the jurists had written from the Twelve Tables forward into a systematic code that would be rediscovered in the west in the eleventh century and become the foundation of every civil law system in the modern world.

## VI. What Held

The Forum Romanum stood between the hills.

By the sixth century, the space that had been the Republic's acoustic bowl and the principate's marble showcase was a different place. Some of the buildings were still maintained; others had been converted to churches; others were quarried for their stone by a population that had shrunk to a fraction of the principate's million. The Tiber still flooded, but the drainage system that had kept the Forum dry was no longer maintained, and the ground level was rising with the accumulated debris of centuries. Cattle grazed in the space that had held Brutus's funeral procession and Gracchus's speech and the proscription lists and the augures reading the sky. The Romani who lived in the shrunken city of the sixth century still called themselves Romani, still considered themselves the inheritors of the tradition, still maintained the churches that now occupied the spaces the temples had held.

What had fallen was not Roma. What had fallen was the western empire's ability to govern its provinces from a single center. The eastern empire had not fallen. The law had not fallen. The language had not fallen: Latin persisted in the west as the language of the church, of law, of learning, and would evolve into the Romance languages that carry its structure today. The roads had not fallen: they were still there, degraded and less maintained but still the arteries along which people and goods and ideas moved. The aqueducts functioned in some places and not in others. The baths closed where there was no one to maintain them. The cities contracted where the population could not sustain them. But the thing that the Romani had built over a thousand years of political engineering, the thing that began with the oath and the machinery and the Twelve Tables and the alliance system and the roads and the law and the principate's fiction and the Christian framework that replaced the fiction, this thing did not end in 476 or in any other year.

It transformed. The bishop of Roma, who had inherited the title pontifex maximus from the emperor, now claimed authority over the western church with the same insistence that the Roman Senate had once claimed authority over the res publica. The law of Justinian, carried in manuscripts from Constantinople to Ravenna to the monasteries of the west, preserved the jurists' work until the eleventh century, when the law school at Bologna rediscovered the Digest and the Institutes and the Code, and the rediscovery remade European law on Roman foundations. The Latin that Cicero had written and Virgil had sung and the jurists had used to define the categories of obligation was still being read and written and spoken in every monastery and every episcopal court and every university that the medieval church would build, and the reading was not antiquarian. The Latin was alive, and it carried with it the categories of thought that the Romani had forged: persona, res, actio, ius, lex, the vocabulary of obligation that the medieval and modern world would inherit without always knowing where it came from.

The geese on the Capitoline are gone. The augures no longer read the sky. The consuls no longer serve their year. The tribune no longer stands in the Forum and says veto. The Forum itself is an archaeological site, the column stumps rising from the ground level that has risen three meters above the pavement where Brutus's body passed. But the law holds. In every country whose legal system descends from the Roman tradition, and that is most of the countries on earth, the categories Gaius wrote in his Institutes still structure the argument: persons, things, actions. The obligations the Twelve Tables made public, the jurisprudence the principate's scholars refined, the codification Justinian's commission assembled in Constantinople, these survive, not as monuments but as working tools, applied every day in courts that do not display the fasces or feed the geese but that owe their categories, their procedures, and their assumption that the law is a public thing, a res publica, to the people who built on the hills above the Tiber and swore that no man would hold permanent power over the public thing, and who kept the oath for five centuries, and who spent the next five centuries learning what happened when the oath gave way, and whose law, the most durable thing they built, is still being read, still being applied, still being argued in the Forum's descendants around the world, because the law does not require the geese, and the law does not require the augures, and the law does not require the marble. The law requires what the Twelve Tables required: that the rules be written, that the writing be public, that the public can read them. The Romani built this. The marble crumbled. The law held.
