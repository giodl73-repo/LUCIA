# The Merchants of Ashur

The letter says: I have sent you the tin. Fourteen minas, wrapped in leather, loaded on the lead donkey. The cloth you asked for is packed in the second load, forty bolts of the fine Akkadian weave, the kind the palace at Kanesh prefers. Tell Amur-Ishtar that if he does not settle his account by the time the caravan returns, I will take the matter to the karum assembly, and he knows what that means. Also: your daughter is well. She has begun learning the loom. Send word when you can.

The man who dictated this letter lived in the city of Ashur, on the west bank of the Tigris, in a house of mud brick that backed onto a narrow lane near the temple of the god who shared the city's name. The river below the cliff caught the morning light and threw it against the temple wall. In summer the heat pressed down on the city like a hand, and the lanes between the houses were narrow enough that a man could touch both walls, and the shade they made was the only relief between the river and the workshops where the weavers worked with the doors open because the looms needed air. He reckoned the year by the eponym official then serving: the year of Shu-Laban, the year of Ili-bani (c. 1900 BCE). He was a merchant. His particular name is lost among the thousands recovered from the archives, and the letter at the top of this page is assembled from phrases that recur across dozens of tablets, the common language of Ashur's merchant correspondence. But the voice is real. It sounds like the voices of Imdilum, of Pushu-ken, of Shalim-Ashur and Lamassatum and dozens of others whose letters have survived because they wrote on clay, and because clay, unlike papyrus, does not burn.

The letters traveled by donkey. Caravans left Ashur loaded with tin from the east and textiles woven in the city, crossed the steppe through territory that was not always friendly, climbed into the Anatolian highlands, and arrived weeks later at the colonies the Ashur merchants called karum: the trading post at the gate of a foreign city, where Ashur's sons lived and traded under an arrangement with the local ruler that gave them the right to buy and sell and adjudicate their own disputes, so long as they paid the taxes and did not cheat on the customs. The greatest of these was Karum Kanesh, in the heart of Anatolia, where a quarter of clay tablets from the entire ancient Near East was found buried in the burned ruins of merchant houses, still legible, still sharp, still carrying the voices of people who had been dead for nearly four thousand years.

They wrote about everything. The price of tin in Kanesh versus the price in Ashur. The quality of the wool. The dishonesty of a particular business partner. The weather on the mountain passes. Instructions to wives who managed the Ashur end of the trade while their husbands spent years in Anatolia. Complaints from those wives: you have been gone two harvests now and the children ask for you and the silver you promised has not arrived. Requests from fathers to sons: the textiles you sent last time were poor quality and I could not sell them at the agreed price, do better. Legal disputes filed with the karum assembly, which functioned as a court of merchant law, with judges elected from among the traders themselves, following precedents they recorded on clay and stored in the karum's archive room.

Ashur was a city of the road. Not large, not powerful in the way of Babylon to the south or the kingdoms that rose and fell on the plain. Its wealth was movement: tin carried from the mountains of the east, textiles woven in its workshops, loaded onto donkey caravans and walked across six weeks of steppe and highland to the markets where Anatolian rulers paid in silver and copper. The city lived on the difference between the price of tin where it was plentiful and the price of tin where it was scarce, and on the willingness of its merchants to carry that difference in leather sacks on the backs of animals across territory where the only law was the agreement you had made with the people who controlled the road.

The god Ashur presided over this city. He was the patron of the journey, the protector of the road, the one whose blessing a merchant invoked before the caravan departed and whose temple received a portion of the profit when the merchant came home. The city's ruler bore the title of *rubā'um*, something between prince and steward, and his authority coexisted with the karum assembly, with the city elders, with the temple administration. No single power commanded Ashur. The merchants governed their trade. The assembly governed their disputes. The god governed the obligations that held the system together: the oath sworn before Ashur's name that made a contract binding, the curse invoked against the man who broke it.

Lamassatum, writing to her husband Pushu-ken at Kanesh, argued about debts and textile shipments and the behavior of agents who had not done as they were told. She managed the Ashur end of the trade from her house on the lane near the temple: the looms, the weavers, the inventory of cloth ready for the next caravan, the accounts that told her which agents had paid and which had not. She knew what she knew. The price of tin. The reliability of donkey drivers. The date the next caravan was due. And the sound of her daughter practicing at the loom in the room behind her while she dictated words onto wet clay that would hold its shape long after the city, the temple, the lane, and the loom had all returned to dust.
