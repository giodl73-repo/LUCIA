# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Ghana Empire / Era 2: The Age of Gold
**Round:** 1

## What Works

The opening tells the story from inside the merchant's experience of Kumbi Saleh, not from above it. The merchant does not marvel at what he sees. He does not explain it for a reader who has never been there. He sees it in two parts "because that is what it was." That sentence carries the right weight: the dual structure is self-evident to those who live within it. It does not require explanation. It requires only arrival.

The sacred grove is handled well. "Dark with trees that no one cut, where the priests maintained the rites that bound the Soninke to the land and the land to the sky." This is the culture's own language for what the grove means. No one explains it as "an animist belief system" or "a traditional religious practice." It is simply the rites that bound the Soninke to the land. That is how the culture would say it. That is how it should be said.

The absence of any comparison to another civilization is correct and consistent. The opening does not tell us that Kumbi Saleh was "as large as" any European city or "as wealthy as" any other place. It is what it is, measured in its own terms: twelve mosques, ten horses, gold and silver collars, and a drum made from a single long piece of hollowed wood. The specificity is its own authority.

"This was how a people showed their king that the covenant between the throne and the land still held." This sentence passes the Achebe Test. A Soninke elder could read it and recognize the description. It does not sentimentalize or exoticize the ceremony. It states what the ceremony meant to those who performed it.

## What Needs Work

The merchant is from Sijilmasa. He is an outsider to the Soninke world, seeing it through the eyes of a Muslim trader from the Moroccan south. This is a legitimate viewpoint for an opening that is about the dual-city structure as experienced by someone who inhabits one half of it. But the chapter must not remain in this viewpoint. The Soninke world of the royal city, the sacred grove, the Ghana's own court, must eventually be told from inside that world, not from the merchant quarter looking across one mile of ground.

The phrase "the realest thing in Kumbi Saleh was the arrangement itself" hovers at the edge of authorial commentary. The merchant from Sijilmasa would not use the word "realest" in this comparative sense. He would say the arrangement was how things worked, or how things had always worked. The superlative implies a judgment about what matters most, and that judgment belongs to the author, not to the merchant. Consider whether this sentence can be grounded more firmly in the merchant's own perspective.

The opening does not yet give us a Soninke voice. Every perspective in this piece belongs to the Muslim merchant or to al-Bakri (also an outsider, writing from Cordoba). For an opening, this is acceptable. For a chapter, it would be a failure. The Soninke themselves must speak before the chapter is finished.

## The Question I Would Push On

Whose story is this? The merchant's? The Ghana's? The system's? The opening suggests the story belongs to the system, with the merchant as its witness. That can work, but it risks making the Soninke into the backdrop for a trade narrative. The Ghana Empire's story, told from inside, is not primarily a story about trade. It is a story about sacred kingship, about the covenant between a ruler and the forces that made the gold appear and the rain fall and the enemies stay distant. Trade is how the outside world experienced this kingdom. The covenant is how the Soninke experienced it. Both must be present, but the Soninke perspective must not be the lesser voice.

## Verdict

The opening passes the Achebe Test. No forbidden words, no outside judgment, no hidden hierarchy. The dual-city structure is presented on its own terms. The risk lies ahead: if the full chapter remains in the merchant's perspective, it tells the story of a trade system, not of a people. The Soninke must own their chapter by the time the writing is done.
