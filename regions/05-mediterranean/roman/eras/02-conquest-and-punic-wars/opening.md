# The Conquest of Italy and the Punic Wars

The consul Gaius Terentius Varro rode back to Roma after Cannae, and the Senate went out to meet him at the gates and thanked him for not having despaired of the res publica.

This is the detail that defines the Roman Republic. Not the victory. Not the genius of the commander. The response to catastrophe. On an August day in 216 BCE, on the flat ground near the river Aufidus in Apulia, Hannibal Barca had destroyed the largest army the Republic had ever assembled. Polybius, who wrote the account a generation later and who had access to Roman sources, puts the dead at seventy thousand. The number may be inflated, but the scale is not in dispute. The consul Lucius Aemilius Paullus was dead. Eighty senators were dead. Equestrians, centurions, the men who formed the Republic's military and political backbone, lay in windrows on a field that Polybius says the ground could not absorb. The birds came. The smell carried for miles. The survivors stumbled south and east, and some of them did not stop until they reached the coast.

In Roma, the news arrived and the city did not fall. The Senate sat in its chamber and refused to discuss terms. Hannibal sent an envoy to negotiate the ransom of prisoners, and the Senate sent him back with the message that Roma did not ransom captured soldiers, because the Republic could not afford to teach its citizens that surrender was an option. The city mobilized boys and old men. It armed slaves, promising freedom in exchange for service. It borrowed from the treasury of the temples. It melted the gold of the matrons' jewelry for coin. And it waited, and it did not negotiate, and when the consul Varro returned from the worst defeat in Roman military history, the Senate thanked him, because he had not given up.

The thanking of Varro is not a story about Roman virtue. It is a story about a political system that could absorb a catastrophe and keep functioning. The Romani did not survive Cannae because they were braver than other people. They survived because the machinery held. The Senate's collective authority gave Roma a decision-making body that did not depend on any single leader's survival. The census-based army gave Roma a reserve of manpower that no other Mediterranean power could match. The alliance system that bound the Italian communities to Roma gave Roma access to soldiers from every corner of the peninsula. When one army was destroyed, the system produced another. When one consul fell, the system produced another. The res publica was a machine for absorbing loss and continuing, and Cannae was the test that proved it.

The test had been three centuries in the making. Between the founding of the Republic and the day Hannibal stood at Cannae, Roma had absorbed the Italian peninsula, fought the Samnites in the Apennine mountains for fifty years, defeated the Greek general Pyrrhus who brought his elephants across the Adriatic, and collided with Carthago across the water in a war for Sicily that lasted twenty-three years. Each conflict had forced the machinery to adapt. The alliance system grew. The legionary army evolved from the Servian phalanx to the manipular formation that gave each unit of sixty to a hundred and twenty men the ability to fight independently. The roads were built, first to supply the armies, then to bind the territory they conquered. And the question that hung over every Roman generation had been tested again and again: could the machinery that governed a city govern a world?

At Cannae, the question was still open. After Cannae, the Romani would answer it.
