# The Founding — Ardashir and Shapur I (c. 224–272 CE)

## Opening

The mason does not begin at the bottom of the cliff. He begins at the summit, where the Achaemenid tombs already stand — Darius, Xerxes, Artaxerxes — their facades carved into the vertical limestone face of Naqsh-e Rostam four centuries before Ardashir was born. The mason studies the height of those doorways, the proportions of the columns, the angle of light in the late afternoon when the shadows lengthen and the relief carvings speak most clearly to anyone on the road below. Then he descends. Ardashir's investiture scene will be carved at the base of that cliff, where a man on horseback is the natural scale, where the composition can be read by any rider passing on the road from Persepolis.

This is not modesty. It is a statement.

Above: the Achaemenid kings, their tombs cut into the mountain, their bodies sealed in silence.

Below: Ardashir, king of kings — *shāhanshāh* — receiving the ring of kingship from the hand of Ahura Mazda himself. The god rides a horse to the left. Ardashir rides a horse to the right. Between them, on the ground, crushed beneath the hooves of Ardashir's mount, lies Artabanus IV, the last Arsacid, the last king of the Parthian line. Beneath Ahura Mazda's horse lies Ahriman — the Lie, the dark twin, the cosmic enemy of truth. The two pairs mirror each other exactly: the divine defeating evil above, the king defeating the old order below. The message does not require translation. The world has been restored to its proper alignment.

On the road below, a merchant from Shiraz reins his mule and tilts his head back. The cliff face catches the afternoon light. He does not need to read the Avestan inscription to understand what he is seeing. He has seen Parthian coins — the small silver drachms that passed through his hands every market day, some bearing the face of a Hellenistic god alongside the Arsacid king's portrait, the Greek lettering worn smooth from handling. Those coins are gone now, melted or buried. The new coinage shows Ardashir on one face and a fire altar on the other. No Greek gods. The altar burns. The merchant understands the change in the metal of the coins he carries. He sees the same change carved, permanent, into the mountain above him.

What the mason carves in stone, Ardashir has already carved in history. Two years earlier, at the Battle of Hormozdgan in 224 CE, the last Arsacid king died — some sources say in single combat, the old Iranian form, the king's body proof of his dynasty's exhaustion. Ardashir was not a great lord overthrowing a great lord. He was a local temple custodian from Istakhr in Fars, from the heartland province where the Achaemenid kings had built Persepolis, where the sacred fire of Adur Farnbag had burned without interruption for longer than anyone could remember. He was the grandson of Sasan, who had been the keeper of that fire. He understood what the fire meant.

The Arsacids had not been careless stewards. For nearly five centuries they had held the plateau against Rome, maintained trade along the Silk Road, kept some version of the Iranian world intact. But they had done it without the old religious clarity. The Parthian court had been eclectic — Greek gods on their coins, Zoroastrian fires in their territories, local practices left largely undisturbed. This was practical. It was also, from inside the Zoroastrian tradition, a kind of abdication. The sacred fires needed not merely tolerance but active defense. The *druj* — the Lie — was always pressing from the edges of the ordered world. A king who did not actively fight it was not a neutral party. The druj does not wait for kings who will not fight it.

Ardashir had grown up inside that tradition. His father Papak had controlled Istakhr by holding the office of the fire temple. Power and sacred duty had the same shape in Fars. When Ardashir began his expansion — first against neighboring lords in Persis, then against the Parthian provincial governors, then against the Arsacid dynasty itself — he was not merely taking territory. He was restoring the proper order of things. The Avestan hymns described a world in which truth and falsehood were locked in permanent combat, in which the righteous king was Ahura Mazda's earthly champion. Ardashir intended to be that champion without qualification.

The rock relief at Naqsh-e Rostam announces this. But there is a second element that the stone cannot quite capture: the sacred fire burning at Istakhr behind him, tended by priests whose function is now elevated from local custodians to the spiritual infrastructure of an empire. When Ardashir formalizes the Zoroastrian clergy — giving the *magi* a defined hierarchy, a relationship to the royal court, revenues from royal endowment — he is not creating religion. He is building it into the bones of the state. The fire does not merely burn. It certifies. It validates. It connects the king to a cosmic order that no military defeat can fully erase.

This is what the Arsacids lacked. This is what Ardashir is building now.

His son Shapur will continue the work — will defeat three Roman emperors, will bring back 70,000 Roman prisoners to build his cities, will have his own investiture scene carved at Naqsh-e Rostam: the Roman emperor Valerian kneeling before him, the emperor Philip the Arab beside him in submission. The dynasty's claim carved in stone again, higher up the cliff this time, closer to the Achaemenid tombs, as if the generations are climbing.

The mason works in the afternoon light. The limestone takes the chisel cleanly in Fars, fine-grained and pale, the kind of stone that holds an edge for centuries. In the composition before him, the ring passes from the hand of the god to the hand of the king. The horses are carved with their legs extended at the same angle, mirror images of each other, the divine and the royal in perfect visual rhyme. Below the horses, the defeated — one divine enemy, one human one — lie with their faces turned away.

The scene will last longer than any written account. It will outlast the dynasty it celebrates. It will outlast the religion that gave it meaning. Travelers will ride past it on the road from Persepolis, and if they know what they are looking at, they will understand: here is the moment the world was put in order. Here is the king who put it there. Here is the god who gave him the ring.

The mason finishes his work and descends to the road. The cliff face holds the light for another hour after sunset.
