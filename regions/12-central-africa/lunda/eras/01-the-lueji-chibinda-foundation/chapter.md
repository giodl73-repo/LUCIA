# The Lueji-Chibinda Foundation

## I. The Holding

She held the bracelet in both hands.

It was not large. A band of copper worked in the old way, hammered and twisted until it held its shape, worn smooth along the inner curve where it had rested against her father's wrist and her grandfather's before him and back through every Ruund chief who had held the authority of this country. It smelled of skin and of the river, of the red laterite soil of the Kasai watershed where the headwaters ran in braided channels through the savanna grass. In the dry season, when the grass stood yellow and stiff and the animals moved toward the remaining water, the bracelet seemed to hold that smell too: the particular dryness of a land that would be green again in four months and was not green now. The lukanu did not look like much, if you did not know what it was. If you knew, you could not look at anything else.

Lueji knew what it was. She had watched her father wear it for as long as she could remember, watched the way lesser men's eyes moved toward it when he spoke and then moved away, deferential, the acknowledgment happening below the level of words. She had held it once before, when he was very ill and could not rise, and she had set it down again when he recovered. Now she held it because there was no one else to hold it.

Her brothers were gone. This is how the story tells it: that Chinguli and Chinyama were away, or that they had quarreled with their father before his death, or that their temperament was wrong for rule, and the tradition carries several reasons, which may mean the reasons were all partially true, or may mean the tradition is being careful about a lineage dispute that had living consequences long after the founding. The oral record of kingdoms does not always select one truth. It holds the field of possible truths open, because the people who carry it must live among the descendants of everyone in the story.

She wore it on her wrist as her father had worn it. She heard cases. In the mornings, when the smoke from the cooking fires still hung over the compound and the air was cool enough that you could see your breath before the sun cleared the trees to the east, men came to her with disputes over cattle and inheritance and the rights of neighbors at the river's edge. They stood before her in the shade of the musumba's outer enclosure, the woven-grass barrier that set the chief's space apart from the ordinary ground of the village, and she listened and spoke the judgment that carried the authority of the lukanu and not merely of a woman who held it.

One case from this period stays in the memory the tradition preserves, though the name of the disputing parties changes across its tellings. Two men, brothers by blood, had divided their father's herd according to the elder's right, and the younger refused the division, claiming the elder had taken the two best breeding animals. The matter had gone to a village headman and then beyond him, and it arrived at Lueji's court because each man was willing to submit to the lukanu's judgment but not to any lesser authority. She heard them both. She asked about the animals individually, not the herd as a whole but each animal's parentage, temperament, history. She required the elder to return one animal and the younger to acknowledge the elder's right in everything else. Both men accepted.

She performed the rituals required of the one who wore the lukanu: the ceremonies that connected the living authority of the chief to the world of the ancestors who had held this authority before. The specifics of those ceremonies are carried in restricted knowledge; they belong to the custodians of the tradition and are not disclosed in the record that outsiders may read. What the record preserves is the fact of the obligation: the lukanu's wearer was not free to simply rule. She was required to maintain the relationship between the living and the ancestral that made rule legitimate.

She ruled. The tradition does not present this as unusual. The lukanu had come to her through her blood, through the line of chiefs, and she held it because she was the one who could.

## II. The Hunter

It was at the edge of the dry season's last month, when the rivers had shrunk to their lowest and the grass was brittle underfoot and the animals were thinner than they would be after the rains. A man came to the compound from the east, from across the boundary of Ruund country, from the direction of the Luba lands. He was lean and dark and he moved the way men move who have been on foot for many days, with the particular economy of a body that has learned to cover distance without spending itself. He carried a bow strung from a wood the Ruund people recognized as coming from forests further east, and a quiver, and the tools of someone who had been living from what the land provided. He had followed game across the boundary between Luba and Ruund territory; how far the tracking had taken him, he could not have said precisely. The land does not post its borders.

He asked to hunt in the area. He asked for provisions. He was Luba, and the Ruund people understood what that meant in ways that require some care to explain.

The Luba world to the northeast of the Ruund had developed a principle of sacred kingship, the bulopwe, that understood political authority as descending from a founding hero, Kalala Ilunga, who had arrived in the Upemba region from somewhere else and married into the existing fishing and ironworking communities there. The bulopwe was not a rank conferred by ceremony alone, though ceremony was part of it. It was a quality understood to be present in those of the founding lineage: a particular kind of political and spiritual authority that made certain men fit to rule in ways that others were not. The Luba recognized it in their own kings; neighboring peoples who had commercial and cultural contact with the Luba world recognized it too, understood it as a real thing even when they did not share the Luba cosmological framework that explained it.

This man was Chibinda Ilunga, son of Kalala Ilunga. He carried the bulopwe in his blood.

When the Ruund people of Lueji's compound saw him, they saw a Luba man with a hunter's tools and a hunter's bearing. They also saw, those who knew how to look, a man from the bulopwe lineage. These were not two separate things they were seeing. The bulopwe was visible. It expressed itself in how he held himself when challenged or questioned, in the calm of someone who knows what he is, in the quality of attention he gave to the country he moved through. The Luba had a way of saying this: the bulopwe man reads the country the way the country reads the bulopwe man. What he sees, he sees truly. What is true about him, the country sees.

He did not come to rule. He came hunting. But the quality he carried came with him into the Ruund compound, and Lueji saw it.

The tradition is sparing about the moment of recognition. It preserves the outcome without staging the scene. What it holds is this: the bulopwe was present in a man who was not yet ruling, who had not come with political intention, who had arrived following animals and requiring only provisions and permission. The lukanu in her hands and the bulopwe in his blood were two forms of authority that the tradition had not yet put together. Putting them together would make something neither could be alone.

She invited him to stay. He stayed.

## III. The Recognition

He hunted in the Ruund country. He was skilled with the bow in the way that some hunters are skilled, the skill deep enough that it no longer looks like effort; the animal steps into the place where the arrow will be, rather than the arrow finding the animal where it stands. He brought meat back to the compound. He taught what he knew to those who asked. He was Luba in his practices: the ritual obligations he performed before a kill, the acknowledgment he made to the animal's spirit, the way he read the grass and the water and the wind not as obstacles but as language. The Ruund hunters learned from this. The Luba hunting knowledge and the Ruund territorial knowledge were not the same thing, and each recognized something in the other.

He and Lueji married. The tradition does not dwell on the courtship in the Western sense of courtship; the emphasis is not on personal feeling but on political recognition. Two people who held two forms of authority had recognized each other. The marriage was how that recognition was made formal and binding and transmissible to the children who would come after.

Not everyone in the compound accepted this.

Katema was among those who had served Lueji's father and then her. He was old enough to have attended the previous chief's court when the decisions being made there were about cattle disputes between headmen and trade negotiations with neighbors, when the lukanu's authority was primarily local and its legitimacy was primarily Ruund. He had understood the lukanu's meaning within that frame. A Luba hunter was not part of that frame. However skilled, however calm, however clearly something of consequence was in him, Chibinda Ilunga was not Ruund. He was not from the lineage of those who had held the authority before. Katema had opinions about this.

He was not alone. Some of Lueji's headmen shared the conviction that the lukanu should not leave the Ruund lineage's hands in this way, that whatever the bulopwe was, it did not belong in the same frame as Ruund ancestral authority, that the marriage was a category error dressed as a founding. They said so, in the way that headmen speak: not in direct opposition to the ruling chief but in accumulated hesitation, in pointed questions about ceremony and precedent, in a gradual withdrawal of the enthusiastic participation that had characterized their service before.

Eventually, some left.

Katema was among those who eventually chose to take his following and go. He went west, taking with him the Ruund political vocabulary: the titles, the ceremonies, the way of organizing a community around a chief's authority, that he had carried for decades. Somewhere in the territory to the west and south, he stopped, and he installed himself, and the political culture he had learned in Lueji's compound became the political culture of the community he founded. He was not the last to do this. The departures continued through the period of Chibinda Ilunga's consolidation, each departing figure carrying the vocabulary outward, each one seeding a new community that acknowledged the Ruund cultural inheritance even while rejecting the Lunda synthesis taking shape at the center. Some of these communities would become associated, in the scholarship's accounting of western Angolan history, with the Imbangala and other peoples of the region; the connections are debated, but the westward movement of Ruund political culture through these departures is documented.

The departures were not a loss. Katema had left with what he had learned at the center: the titles, the ceremonies, the knowledge of what authority was supposed to look like when it was correctly organized. Somewhere to the west, that knowledge had become a community. It had traveled in him. The lukanu had stayed in the musumba, and the political culture it had constituted had gone west in a man who refused it. This was what the authority could do.

The generations that followed would discover how far it could go.

## IV. The Transmission

The lukanu left Lueji's wrist and went to Chibinda Ilunga's.

The ceremony of transmission is not preserved in full detail in the sources that have come to us. The oral tradition, which is where the Lunda record lives, preserves the fact and the meaning of the transmission without preserving the specific words or gestures that constituted the ceremony. What can be reconstructed from the object's logic and from the ceremony's purpose: it was formal, it was witnessed, and it was designed so that the authority passed completely.

The witnesses mattered. When Lueji removed the lukanu from her wrist, she did so before the people of the compound, before the headmen who had stayed and those who were still deciding whether to stay, before whatever assembly the tradition required for a transmission of this significance. An authority that passes in secret passes partially. An authority that passes before witnesses passes fully. The witnesses carry the transmission in their memory. They can testify to what they saw. Their testimony is what makes the new holder's claim uncontestable.

He held out his wrist. She placed the bracelet on it. He wore it.

The moment of wearing was the moment of constitution. Not of his becoming the Lunda king, for that process took a generation, but of the new synthesis becoming possible. The lukanu that had held the Ruund ancestral-territorial authority now sat against the wrist of a man who carried the Luba sacred-spiritual authority in his blood. The two forms of authority were in contact. What they could produce together was, at this moment, only a possibility. But the possibility was now real.

Lueji did not disappear from the story when the bracelet left her wrist.

The tradition is careful about this. Her lineage, the maternal line of Ruund chiefs from which she descended, remained the legitimating source of the lukanu's Ruund authority. The Mwaant Yav's power was not simply the bulopwe transplanted into a new location. It was the bulopwe held by a person whose right to hold the lukanu derived from a woman's deliberate choice, and that woman's choice derived from her legitimate standing in the Ruund lineage. Remove her from the chain and the chain breaks.

In practice, this meant that the women of Lueji's lineage held specific political and ceremonial roles in the Lunda state for as long as the state existed. The Rukonkesh, the Mwaant Yav's mother, held a politically significant position that was not merely honorific. The Swana Mulunda, a senior queen whose exact relationship to the Ruund maternal line varies in the tradition's accounts, held another. The ceremonies of installation that made a new Mwaant Yav required the participation of specific female lineage-holders, because without their participation the transmission was incomplete. The lukanu's authority required the Ruund maternal line to pass it. The Ruund maternal line continued to exist and to matter precisely because the tradition understood that Lueji's transmission had been the founding act.

She had founded the kingdom. She remained part of its constitution.

The bulopwe and the lukanu together: what the synthesis produced was an authority that was neither simply Luba nor simply Ruund but was recognized by the surrounding peoples as something new. A Mwaant Yav could send a titleholder to found a new community in territory far from the Kasai watershed, and that titleholder could install himself and perform the installation ceremonies and create a political structure that acknowledged the Mwaant Yav's ceremonial supremacy, and all of this would work, across hundreds of kilometers and multiple generations, because the original synthesis was legitimate. Because Lueji had held the lukanu and chosen.

## V. The Consolidation

Chibinda Ilunga wore the lukanu through the dry seasons and the wet seasons of his first years of rule, and the Kasai watershed slowly organized itself around the new center he and Lueji had established.

The musumba, the capital compound, was a place unlike either a Luba village or a Ruund village, because it had to hold both within it. It was built from the materials of the savanna: wooden-frame structures with thatch, the walls sometimes reinforced with packed clay, the whole compound organized around a central enclosure that was the Mwaant Yav's space and was not the space of ordinary men. The barrier that separated the Mwaant Yav's enclosure from the rest of the compound was not simply physical; in the ceremonial practice that developed from this period, it would become the visible form of the spiritual boundary between the holder of the lukanu and those who did not hold it. You could stand at the edge of this barrier and feel the difference in the air, or at least you felt it if you understood what the boundary meant.

The musumba was not meant to be permanent. This was not a failure of ambition but a principle. The Lunda capital could move when it needed to move: when the resources of an area were depleted, when political circumstances required a new position in the landscape, when the spiritual logic of the Mwaant Yav's authority suggested relocation. A capital that could not be moved was a capital that was hostage to its location. The Lunda state would build and occupy and eventually leave musumbas across a wide territory over the centuries of its existence. Each musumba was a complete expression of the Lunda political world for the time it was occupied.

From the path that approached the musumba from the east, you would hear it before you saw it. The sounds of a large community at work: the percussion of smiths, the voices of women at grinding and cooking, children, goats, the occasional low note of cattle in the pens at the compound's edge. Then the smell: woodsmoke, the particular smell of an inhabited place, the traces of the ceremonies that had been performed in the Mwaant Yav's enclosure the previous evening. Then the compound itself, the barrier visible, the guards who stood at the entrance with their authority derived from the Mwaant Yav's authority and clearly understood.

From this compound, in Chibinda Ilunga's time, the first tentative extensions of Lunda political culture began.

A headman who had stayed through the period of Katema's departure went east, to establish a new community in territory where Ruund people had sometimes hunted but not settled permanently. He carried with him a title, not the Mwaant Yav's title but a subordinate title granted from the Mwaant Yav's authority, and the ceremonies appropriate to that title, and the knowledge of what political organization was supposed to look like. When he arrived in the new territory and established himself, he established it as a Lunda-affiliated community. The people who lived nearby could see that. The ceremonies were recognizable. The title placed him within the structure that had its apex in the musumba. He was a franchise, though that word would not have been the word anyone used.

The copper underwrote the network.

The Katanga copper belt lay to the southeast of the Lunda heartland, within the reach of the expanding Luba-Lunda commercial world. The cross-shaped copper ingots, handa, that moved through south-central Africa were not simply currency, though they functioned as currency in the sense that their value was agreed upon across a wide territory and they could be exchanged for other goods with that value accepted. They were also prestige objects, and they were also measures of relationship between communities. When a community in the franchise network sent copper to the musumba, it was affirming its position in the hierarchy. When the musumba sent copper outward, as gifts, as payment, as marks of favor, it was extending its authority through the material network.

A handa in the hand is heavier than you expect. The cross shape means that the weight is not in a single lump but distributed across the four arms, and carrying it requires a different grip than carrying a simple bar. The copper is red-brown, oxidized to something darker on the surface with a reddish brightness where the surface is freshly exposed. It smells metallic, the way all copper smells, but with something underneath from the smelting: a hint of the charcoal and the clay of the furnace that made it. To exchange a handa was to move a significant object between hands. The exchange was always visible, always witnessed. You could not quietly slip a handa to someone.

Chibinda Ilunga died in the first generation of Lunda rule. The succession passed, through a process the tradition records in compressed genealogical terms, to the line that he and Lueji had established. The lukanu moved again, not by conquest, not by force, but by the same deliberate transmission that had been the founding act. The authority that Lueji had held and chosen to pass continued to pass, generation to generation, through the mechanism she had constituted.

The headmen who had left, Katema and the others, had established their communities to the west and south. These communities knew their origin. They carried the Ruund political vocabulary. They did not bow to the Mwaant Yav's day-to-day authority, but they acknowledged, in the ceremonies they performed and the titles they used, that the center of their political culture was in the Kasai watershed. The franchise was not designed. It was discovered, in the act of people carrying a culture with them when they left the place where they had learned it.

The world would grow from this discovery. The next era would see it grow deliberately.

## VI. Landing

The lukanu moved from Chibinda Ilunga's wrist to the wrist of his successor, and from that wrist in time to another, each transmission witnessed, each one an act of constitution.

The Kasai watershed ran its braided channels through the savanna as it had always run, the rivers swelling with the wet season's rain and shrinking back in the dry months when the grass turned yellow and the animals moved toward the water. The musumba sat at the center of the network, the compound smelling of smoke and copper and the particular human smell of authority gathered in one place. From it, in the dry season when travel was easier, men went out with their titles and their ceremonies. They were going to places where no Lunda community yet existed. They would stop. They would build. They would perform the installation ceremonies that declared: this authority is grounded in the same source as the authority at the center, because I carry from the center the titles and the ceremonies that say so.

The lukanu stayed in the musumba. But what it had made, the political culture it had constituted when Lueji placed it on Chibinda Ilunga's wrist, that could travel in any wrist that received a title from the center.

She had held the bracelet in both hands, in the dry season, in the compound that would become the musumba. She had understood what putting the two authorities together would make. She had chosen.

The bracelet, worn smooth along the inner curve from generations of wrists, moved into the Lunda future that she had constituted. It smelled of skin and copper and the ancestors whose authority it accumulated. It was not large. If you knew what it was, you could not look at anything else.

The kingdom was made. The franchise was its nature. The choosing was its soul.
