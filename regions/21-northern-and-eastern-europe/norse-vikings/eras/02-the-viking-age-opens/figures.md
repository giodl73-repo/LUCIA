# Figures — Vikingalond: The Viking Age Opens (Era 2)

## The Bonde (composite figure)

A free farmer from the Oslofjord district. Unnamed. Represents the typical early Viking-age raider: a landholding bonde, not a landless warrior or a professional pirate. He has a farm, a household, a place in the thing assembly, and the social standing of a man who has gone i viking before and come back with silver.

**What we know:** The archaeological record from Vestfold (the district around the Oslofjord) confirms the material world described in the chapter: longhouses with central hearths, byre-end animal housing, small-scale farming supplemented by maritime activity. Ship burials from the region (Oseberg, Gokstad, Tune) attest to the centrality of ships in the social and spiritual life of the Vestfold Norse. The Kaupang trading site, excavated from 1998 onward, confirms a settlement where farming, trade, and maritime activity coexisted. The bonde is drawn from this material.

**What we cannot know:** No individual raider from the Lindisfarne expedition is named in any source. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle and Alcuin's letters describe the attack but not the attackers. The bonde is a composite figure, grounded in the archaeological evidence of Vestfold farm-life and the later saga tradition's depiction of the free farmer who went i viking seasonally. His specific thoughts, words, and biography are not recoverable.

**Honest limit:** The chapter uses the bonde as a viewpoint figure to render the Norse world from inside. He is not a named historical individual but a type: the free farmer-turned-seasonal-raider who, according to the archaeological and textual evidence, was the most common participant in early Viking-age expeditions. His composite nature is disclosed here.

**Sources:** Kaupang excavations (Dagfinn Skre, ed., *Kaupang in Skiringssal*, 2007); Vestfold farm archaeology (Myhre, 1998); ship burials (Brink & Price, 2008); Price, *Children of Ash and Elm* (2020), ch. 1-4.

## Alcuin of York (c. 735-804)

A Northumbrian scholar serving at the court of Charlemagne in Aachen. His letters, written in the immediate aftermath of the Lindisfarne raid (793), are the primary written response to the event. He interpreted the raid as divine punishment for the sins of the English.

**What we know:** Alcuin is one of the best-documented intellectuals of the early medieval period. His letters are preserved and dated. His description of Lindisfarne ("Never before has such terror appeared in Britain") is a genuine contemporary reaction, written within months of the event. He was not present at the raid but was in correspondence with the Northumbrian court and church.

**What we cannot know:** Whether his theological interpretation of the raid reflected popular understanding or only clerical framing. His letters are not neutral reports. They are arguments, written to make a case about divine judgment and moral reform.

**Use in the chapter:** Alcuin appears as the outside voice that recorded the raid from the English side. His perspective is clearly marked as exterior to the Norse worldview. He is not a protagonist. He is a witness, and his witness is used to establish what the raid looked like from the receiving end, before the text returns to the Norse perspective.

**Sources:** Alcuin, *Letters* (ed. Allott, 1974; ed. Dummler in MGH); Garrison, "The English and the Irish at the Court of Charlemagne" (1998).

## Charlemagne (c. 742-814)

King of the Franks and Emperor of the Romans. Referenced in the chapter in the context of his coastal defense efforts against Norse raids on the Frankish coast (799-810s). His wars against the Saxons (772-804) disrupted the Frisian trading network that had previously connected Scandinavia to Continental markets, which may have contributed to the redirection of Norse maritime activity from trading to raiding.

**What we know:** The Frankish Royal Annals document Charlemagne's responses to Norse raids, including the construction of coastal defenses and the order to build a fleet. His frustration at the inability to defend the coast against seaborne attackers is attested.

**Use in the chapter:** Charlemagne appears as a figure the Norse encountered from outside, the ruler whose coastline they raided. He is not rendered from inside. His presence establishes the scale of the problem the Norse posed: the most powerful ruler in Western Europe could not stop men in boats.

**Sources:** *Annales Regni Francorum* (ed. Kurze, 1895); Einhard, *Vita Karoli Magni*; Nelson, *King and Emperor: A New Life of Charlemagne* (2019).

## The Oseberg Woman (d. c. 834)

An unnamed woman of high status buried in a ship at Oseberg, in the Vestfold district. The burial is the richest ship burial of the Viking Age, containing a complete ship, a cart, four sledges, beds, textiles, kitchen implements, and the remains of horses, dogs, and oxen. A second woman was buried with her.

**What we know:** The burial was excavated in 1904. Dendrochronology dates the ship's construction to c. 820 and the burial to c. 834. The woman's identity is unknown. The quality and quantity of the grave goods indicate exceptional status. The textiles are among the finest surviving examples of Scandinavian weaving from this period.

**What we cannot know:** Her name, her role (queen? priestess? both?), the nature of her authority. The second woman's relationship to her (servant? companion? sacrifice?) is debated.

**Use in the chapter:** Referenced to illustrate the ship as grave as well as transport, and the material culture of the Vestfold elite. Not narrated as a character.

**Sources:** Christensen et al., *Osebergfunnet* (1992); Price, *Children of Ash and Elm* (2020), ch. 14; Brink & Price (2008).

## Ibn Rustah (fl. early 10th century)

Arab geographer whose *Kitab al-A'laq an-Nafisa* (Book of Precious Records) describes the Rus traders encountered on the Volga route. His account documents the goods they carried (furs, slaves, amber, honey) and their customs, providing an outside ethnographic perspective on the Norse eastern expansion.

**What we know:** Ibn Rustah compiled his work in Isfahan in the early tenth century, drawing on the accounts of earlier travelers and merchants. His description of the Rus, while later than the period covered in this chapter (793-850), reflects trade patterns that archaeology confirms were established by the mid-eighth century.

**Use in the chapter:** Referenced as a documentary source for the eastern trade routes. His observations are used as evidence, not as narrative voice. The chapter notes that he is an outside observer.

**Sources:** Ibn Rustah, *Kitab al-A'laq an-Nafisa* (ed. de Goeje, 1892); Montgomery, "Ibn Rustah's Account of the Rus" in Brink & Price (2008).
