## Manual Pass — Implicit Judgment, Hidden Hierarchy, Anachronistic Morality

**Finding 1 — P3 (borderline)**
Location: Section "The Calculus Under Pressure"
Quoted text: "He understood more than most Europeans who came to the Lesser Antilles. But not enough to see what the word was doing."

Violation type: Implicit hierarchical ranking with modern vantage point.
Explanation: "More than most Europeans" establishes a comparative ranking from an outside position. "Not enough to see what the word was doing" is the author's judgment on Breton's comprehension. Both sentences are written from a position above the scene, where the author knows something Breton does not. This is controlled irony, which may be appropriate for the chronicler-within-chronicle technique, but it should be checked: is the author's superiority to Breton necessary, or can Breton's limits be rendered through his own words and actions rather than the author's assessment?

**Recommended fix:** Let Breton's dictionary entry itself show his limit. If the text can quote or paraphrase his actual note — "I do not know the precise meaning" — the limit is demonstrated from inside, through Breton's own words, rather than assessed from above by the author.

---

**Finding 2 — P3 (borderline)**
Location: Section "Two Knowledges Meet at St. Vincent"
Quoted text: "the particular stubbornness of people who had already survived one kind of elimination."

Violation type: Anachronistic characterization; potential sentimentalization.
Explanation: "Stubbornness" is a characterization applied from outside. "Survived one kind of elimination" frames the Middle Passage in terms of the aftermath rather than the experience, which is appropriate, but "stubbornness" as a characteristic of the Africans risks romanticizing resilience as a personality trait rather than as a structural fact about people who had survived a specific set of conditions. The sentence is trying to honor the Africans' survival without access to their own worldview (which the chapter cannot provide — they are secondary figures here). The risk is that "stubbornness" flattens what was likely a complex range of responses to an extreme situation.

**Recommended fix:** Replace "stubbornness" with a more specific formulation — what specifically did these African communities bring that was operational? Not a character trait, but a practice or a capacity. "Knowledge of what European slavery looked like from inside" is already in the notes and is more specific than "stubbornness."

**Finding 3 — P2 (must fix)**
Location: Section "The Demographic Wall" / "Calculus Under Pressure"
Quoted text: "The Kalinago had no way to see what was happening to them at the scale it was happening."

Violation type: Author's superior vantage point asserted over the culture.
Explanation: "No way to see" is the author's judgment about the Kalinago's epistemic limits. Even if factually accurate (the Kalinago did not have pan-Caribbean demographic monitoring), stating it from above creates an implicit hierarchy: the author knows something the Kalinago could not know, and the sentence announces this gap. The effect is subtle but present: the reader is positioned above the Kalinago for one moment, looking down at their lack of aggregate knowledge.

**Recommended fix:** Reframe from inside the experience rather than from above the limitation. "Each island knew its own dead" already does this work — that sentence is written from inside. The next sentence ("The inter-island news networks that transmitted tactical information so efficiently could not transmit the aggregate picture") could be tightened to remove the comparison between what the network could and could not do, and simply show what happened as the Kalinago experienced it: each island's news arrived, but the total picture was not visible because no one was standing outside all the islands at once. (And: the author should not be standing outside all the islands either.)
