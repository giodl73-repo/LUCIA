# Notes — Naqada I: The Amratian

## Production Bible

### Structural Engine

**Engine:** Material exchange creating networks before political structure.
**Mechanism:** Craft specialization (enabled by the flood-season labor surplus) produces objects that travel beyond the community. Each exchanged object extends the network. Specialization deepens because exchange rewards it. The network grows because the materials are geographically distributed (copper and malachite in the Eastern Desert, obsidian from the south, shells from the Red Sea, fine flint from specific outcrops) and no single community has all of them.
**Terminal condition:** The network grows too large for reciprocity. Unequal access to prestige materials (copper, obsidian, imported shells) creates differentiation. The graves at the end of Naqada I are already unequal. Some have copper and imported goods; many do not. The network that began as exchange between equals is producing hierarchy as its emergent property. This hierarchy will, in Naqada II, become visible in architecture, settlement size, and proto-royal imagery. The engine consumes itself: the network that required no central authority begins to generate one.

### Sources

**Archaeological (Tier 1):**
- Petrie's Naqada excavation sequence (1895): foundational ceramic chronology. SD (Sequence Dating) system for Naqada I = SD 30-38.
- Hierakonpolis Painted Tomb 100: late Naqada I / early Naqada II; earliest monumental wall painting in Egypt. Scenes of boats, hunting, combat.
- Cemetery T at Naqada: elite burials with differential grave goods indicating emerging hierarchy.
- Wadi Hammamat inscriptions and rock art: evidence of desert route usage.
- Eastern Desert surveys: evidence of copper smelting sites, malachite extraction.

**Academic (Tier 2):**
- Wengrow, *The Archaeology of Early Egypt* (2006): social transformations, craft specialization, and the formation of complex societies.
- Wilkinson, *Genesis of the Pharaohs* (2003): desert origins hypothesis, trade route development.
- Hendrickx & Vermeersch, "Prehistory" in *Oxford History of Ancient Egypt* (2000): period survey with ceramic chronology.
- Patch (ed.), *Dawn of Egyptian Art* (2011): visual culture, cross-lined pottery, figurines.
- Adams, *Ancient Nekhen* (Hierakonpolis publications): settlement patterns, elite precinct evidence.

**Needed for chapter:**
- Specific data on grave good differentiation at Cemetery T (Baumgartel, Castillos databases).
- Lithic specialists on bifacial flint knife technique (Holmes 1989 on Predynastic lithics).
- Obsidian sourcing studies confirming Ethiopian highland origins (Bavay et al.).
- Desert route archaeology (Darnell, Hendrickx) for wadi travel evidence.

### Key Figures

This is an archaeological culture. No named individuals survive. The chapter must use composite/embodied anonymous figures per v1.4 Archaeological Figures standard:

1. **The copper-worker at Nubt** (opening anchor): Composite figure drawn from workshop evidence at Naqada and related sites. Represents the craft-specialist whose work connects communities through material exchange. His body, his hands, his workshop are the chapter's sensory anchor.

2. **The desert-walker**: Figure who carries malachite from the Eastern Desert wadis back to the valley. Represents the human link in the exchange network. Knowledge system: route-finding, water sources, seasonal timing of desert travel, identification of ore-bearing rock.

3. **The potter**: Figure who shapes the white cross-lined vessels. Represents the most diagnostic craft tradition of Naqada I. Knowledge system: clay preparation, firing temperature control, lime-paint application, iconographic tradition.

4. **The burial-maker**: Figure who prepares and provisions the dead. Represents the mortuary knowledge system. Knowledge system: body positioning, grave good selection, ochre application, the cosmological logic of west-facing orientation.

5. **A figure at Hierakonpolis**: Represents the emerging settlement differentiation. Hierakonpolis is already larger than other communities by late Naqada I.

All figures disclosed as composites in figures.md.

### Narrative Arc — Section Plan

**Section 1: The Workshop (opening integration, ~800w)**
The copper-worker at Nubt. The awl. The material world of making. Sensory anchor: the workshop (smell of charcoal, feel of copper, sound of the bellows). Integrate panel feedback: add smell, add the sound. Establish the network question: the same object from the same ore, downriver.

**Section 2: The Green Stone (~700w)**
The Eastern Desert and the desert-walker. How malachite was procured. The wadis as knowledge corridors. The cosmetic use of malachite ground on palettes (eye paint) as the original relationship with the green stone. The moment when pigment becomes metal. Copper smelting at method level: the crucible, the temperatures, the charcoal, the bellows tube, the transformation. Knowledge system at method level (v1.3/v1.5 standard). The desert-walker's body: heat, thirst, sandstone, the weight of ore in a pack.

**Section 3: The Clay (~700w)**
The potter and the white cross-lined ware. The clay preparation, the coiling technique, the thin walls, the firing in open pits. The iconographic program: hippos, crocodiles, the figure with upraised arms. What these images mean from inside the culture's world. The pottery as the Naqada I diagnostic artifact, the thing that identifies this community across hundreds of kilometers. The potter's body: hands in clay, the heat of the kiln.

**Section 4: The Flood Calendar (~600w)**
The flood as the structural precondition for specialization. When the water rises, the fields are covered. The surplus labor of the inundation season is the engine of craft production. This is not "leisure" but structured time: the flood creates the window in which copper is smelted, pots are made, flint is knapped, graves are prepared. Ground this in the craftsman's experience of the seasons. The rhythm of the year.

**Section 5: The Dead (~700w)**
The burial-maker prepares a grave. The body positioned on its left side, facing west. The grave goods selected. The ochre applied. The cosmological logic: the dead continue, the sun goes west, the provisions are real. The emerging hierarchy visible in the graves: some have copper and obsidian, some have only pottery, some have almost nothing. The network's effects made visible in death. The cemeteries as the archive of a society beginning to differentiate.

**Section 6: The Network (~700w)**
Pulling back to the full picture. The copper from the east, the obsidian from the south, the shells from the Red Sea, the flint from specific outcrops. Mapping the exchange system not as trade routes on a map but as relationships between people who carry things. The network has no center because no one controls it yet. But the communities with best access to materials are growing. Hierakonpolis is already larger. Nubt sits at the junction of the Nile and the wadi route to the Eastern Desert. Location begins to matter.

**Section 7: Landing (~300w)**
Return to the copper-worker. The flood is coming. The network is growing. The graves are beginning to differentiate. No one has named any of this. No one will for a long time. But the awl in his hand has already traveled further than he knows. The coals he blows on are heating ore that came from a place three days' walk east, and the tool he makes will end up in a grave with someone whose community is connected to his by the simple fact of shared material. The network is forming. The landing should feel like the closing of a circle that is about to become a spiral.

### Panel Pressure Points Anticipated

1. **Tuchman** will push on narrative pull through the middle sections. The flood and network sections risk becoming expository. Solution: anchor each section in a specific figure's physical experience.

2. **Ibn Khaldun** will push on the terminal condition. The chapter must show the hierarchy emerging, not just assert it. Solution: the grave goods differential in Section 5. Some graves rich, some bare. The network produces inequality.

3. **Achebe** will watch for moments where the archaeological frame intrudes on the cultural interior. The chapter must never say "the archaeological evidence suggests." It must say what the people did and let the evidence support it silently. Solution: maintain strict inside voice. All archaeological knowledge delivered through embodied scenes.

4. **Davis** will push on craft knowledge at method level. The chapter must show at least one craft (copper smelting or pottery-making) at the level of how it works, not what it produces. Solution: Section 2 (copper at method level) and Section 3 (pottery at method level).

5. **Kapuscinski** will push on sensory consistency across all sections. The workshop is grounded. The desert must be grounded. The flood must be grounded. The burial must be grounded. Solution: every section has a body in a place.

### Open Questions

1. Was obsidian during Naqada I definitively from Ethiopian highland sources, or are Anatolian sources also possible? (Sourcing studies vary. Use "may have been as distant as" language.)

2. How much copper was actually produced during Naqada I? Some scholars argue copper was rare and largely symbolic until Naqada II. The chapter should not overstate the copper economy. The awl is a real object but copper tools are not common in Naqada I graves.

3. The white cross-lined pottery: is the upraised-arms figure a goddess, a mourner, a dancer? The literature is divided. The chapter should present the ambiguity honestly.

4. The Painted Tomb at Hierakonpolis (Tomb 100): usually dated to late Naqada I or early Naqada II. If it falls in Naqada II, it should be mentioned only as a forward gesture, not as a Naqada I event.

5. Relationship with Nubian A-Group communities to the south: contemporary, interacting, but distinct. The chapter should acknowledge them without making them a subplot.
