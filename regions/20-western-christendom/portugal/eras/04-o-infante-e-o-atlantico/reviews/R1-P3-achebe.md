# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Panel Review Round 1

**Chapter:** Portugal Era 4 — O Infante e o Atlântico
**Stage:** PANEL-1 (opening.md review)
**Reviewer:** P-3 Chinua Achebe
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I have a particular and necessary interest in this chapter, because Portugal's story in this era is inseparable from the beginning of the Atlantic slave trade — which is, for many people who descend from that trade, the defining event of this period, not the navigational achievement. The opening knows this. It has chosen not to put it in the opening. Let me say what I think about that choice.

The opening tells the story from inside Henry's worldview — which is the project's rule, and the rule is right. Henry did not think of himself as the founder of the Atlantic slave trade. He thought of himself as a crusader, a man of God, an organizer of knowledge, a director of a program that would bring the Gospel to the peoples of Africa and eventually find the road to India. This is his worldview. This opening captures it faithfully.

But the opening's silence on the enslaved Africans is not neutral. It is a choice, and the choice has consequences. When the chapter is read — and it will be read by people who know what the caravels were carrying — the chapter's relationship to its own shadow will determine whether this is honest history or history that papers over what it prefers not to see.

**The Achebe Test, applied directly:** Could a thoughtful Wolof scholar, a descendant of one of the people taken from the Senegambian coast in 1441, read this chapter and feel their story was told with dignity — from inside — regardless of whether they agreed with every detail?

The answer depends entirely on how the chapter handles what the opening has deferred. The opening is well-crafted and honest within its scope. The chapter must expand the scope.

**What the chapter must do:**

First, the 1441 moment — when Nuno Tristão's men brought the first enslaved Africans to Lagos — must be rendered with the same specificity and presence that the rounding of Cape Bojador receives. It is not enough to note it. The chapter must put the reader on both sides of the caravel: the navigator's perspective AND the perspective of the people on the coast who saw the ship, and did not yet know what seeing it would mean.

Second, the chapter must not frame the slave trade as the "shadow" of discovery in a way that makes it secondary to the primary story. The trade was not a shadow. From the perspective of the Africans involved, it was the entire story. The chapter needs to find a way to hold both truths simultaneously — Portugal's navigational program and the human catastrophe that program initiated — without privileging one over the other.

Third, the voice. The opening has found a voice that belongs to the research-program — measured, accumulative, admirably disciplined. That voice will need to stretch when it encounters the human cost. It cannot remain calm and measured when describing the 1441 crossing. The voice should not break — that would be a register shift — but it must carry more weight at that moment. The way to do this without breaking the register is through specificity: not "enslaved Africans" in the abstract but particular people, named where the record allows, described where it does not.

**The question I'd push on:** Zurara's *Crónica de Guiné* is the primary source for the early slave trade. Zurara himself felt the weight of what he was witnessing — his description of the separation of families at the Lagos market in 1444 is one of the earliest expressions of abolitionist unease in European literature. The chapter should engage with Zurara not just as a source but as a figure: a man who recorded what he saw, who could not quite look away, who described the grief and then went on recording. That is an honest figure for this moment — someone who neither condemns nor ignores, but witnesses. The chapter could use Zurara's own divided witness as a structural hinge.

Proceed. But the chapter's debt to this opening is substantial.
