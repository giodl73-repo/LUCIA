**Overall:** The treatment of the Meroitic script and language is accurate and honest. The state of decipherment is correctly represented. The script is neither romanticized as a mystery nor dismissed as a failure.

**Accuracy confirmed:**
- The script is correctly described as alphabetic with twenty-three signs.
- The distinction between phonetic readability and semantic opacity is correctly stated.
- The development from Egyptian hieroglyphic and demotic forms is correctly described.
- The Eastern Sudanic hypothesis is mentioned as a proposal ("recent work by Claude Rilly and others has proposed connections"), not as established fact. This is the correct representation of the current state.
- The observation that the script represents a "leap from hieroglyphic to alphabetic within the same cultural sphere" is a fair characterization of the adaptation process.
- The note about the absence of a sufficiently long bilingual inscription is correct.

**Corrections needed:**
1. The chapter refers to "twenty-three signs representing consonants and vowels." The Meroitic script has twenty-three signs, but the precise count depends on how one classifies certain signs. The standard count is twenty-three in the alphabetic (cursive) form, but the hieroglyphic form has additional variants. The chapter's statement is correct for the cursive script, which was the primary form in use during the Roman encounter period. **Severity: None.** The statement is accurate for the context.

2. The characterization of the Meroitic language as belonging "to no known language family with certainty" is slightly overstated given the progress of the past decade. Consider: "The Meroitic language's classification is debated, with the strongest current hypothesis placing it within the Nilo-Saharan phylum, specifically the Northern Eastern Sudanic branch." However, this is a matter of scholarly caution: the hypothesis is strong but not yet universally accepted. The chapter's phrasing ("proposed connections") is defensible. **Severity: None.** The phrasing is cautious but not inaccurate.

**Cultural authenticity assessment:** The chapter's treatment of the script honors the culture's intellectual achievement without overstating what we know. The sentence "That we cannot yet read them fully is a measure of how thoroughly Meroitic stood apart from the languages that survived around it, not a measure of what the script failed to accomplish" is the correct framing. The script's untranslatability is presented as a fact about the historical record, not as a deficiency of the culture. Approved.
