## BOARD — B2 (Bahadur) — Review of Girmit Chapter

**Domain:** Human experience, women's experience, dignity and agency

**Overall assessment:** The chapter does what the best indenture scholarship tries to do: it tells the system's story through the people inside it. Dulari is the chapter's strongest creation. She is not a symbol of "the Indian woman." She is a person who packs turmeric, sings the Sundarkand, cooks dal with mustard seeds obtained from a woman from Ghazipur, and cuts cane. Her specificity is her dignity.

**What the chapter gets right about the women's experience:**
- The colonial framing of plantation violence (attributing it to "the moral looseness of Indian women") is correctly identified as a colonial projection, and the structural explanation (gender imbalance, disrupted kinship, coercion from overseers) is provided without excusing the violence.
- Dulari's double labor (cane in the field, cooking in the barracks) is rendered as fact, not complaint. The chapter does not sentimentalize her. It shows what she did.
- The women's cultural role (the singing, the mandali, the food) is shown as active construction, not passive tradition-keeping. Dulari sings because she chooses to sing, not because she is performing "cultural preservation."

**What could be stronger:**
- Kunti is named in the figures.md but does not appear in the chapter text. The notes.md planned to include her story in the plantation section. Her absence is a gap. Even a brief passage anchoring one specific moment of a woman's experience in British Guiana would strengthen the chapter's claim to represent the women's experience across colonies. Not every colony has to be shown, but the chapter currently renders the women's experience only through Trinidad/Dulari.
- The passage on violence against women is careful and accurate, but it is written in aggregate ("the records document a pattern"). A single specific incident, rendered through one woman's experience, would make the passage land harder. The colonial records contain such incidents. Bahadur's *Coolie Woman* names several.

**Verdict:** The chapter treats the girmitiya with dignity. Dulari is a genuine figure, not a type. The women's experience is structurally integrated, not appended. The gap is Kunti, whose absence weakens the chapter's geographic range on the women's experience.
