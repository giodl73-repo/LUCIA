## Notes

This is a rare clean sweep. The opening earns it by doing something precise: it uses Abgar — a character who acts with full agency but whose interiority is honestly marked as unknown — to frame the Parthian strategic doctrine not as a military curiosity but as a political philosophy. "The Parthian shot was not a parting blow. It was a statement about terrain." This is the structural engine announced in the opening, which is exactly what the pipeline asks for.

The Euripides scene is the highest-risk passage — it uses a famous anecdote (Crassus's head at a theatrical performance) that has been widely doubted by scholars. It does not claim certainty. "The story went" carries the appropriate epistemic weight. The opening earns its license to use the story.

One technical note for the chapter writer: the sources on Surena (the Suren general) are primarily Plutarch's *Life of Crassus* and Cassius Dio — both Roman, both reconstructed at remove. The chapter will need to handle this source asymmetry honestly. The opening's treatment of Abgar (a character of unclear historical provenance in Plutarch) models exactly the right approach: use the figure, mark the limits, let the action do the work.

The voice register: this is not the steppe register (too sparse, too forward-only), not the court register (too layered). This is something new and correct for Parthia — a border-state voice, the voice of an empire that holds by strategic withdrawal, prose that advances by appearing to retreat. Each paragraph gives something and then qualifies or redirects. This is the Parthian shot as voice register. Flag for the Voice Keeper.
