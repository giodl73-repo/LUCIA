# R2-P3 — Chinua Achebe
## PANEL-2: Notes Review — Canada Era 4

The notes have addressed my core concern: the instruction to frame the National Policy's language as a perspective, not a neutral description. "What the government called settlement" rather than "settlement." This is the right move. The chapter inhabits the Secwépemc interior as its default and enters the colonial administrative frame as reported speech.

The two-track structure with Qwenqwent returning in sections 1, 6, and 7 is correct. She must not disappear. A chapter that introduces a child, turns to systems analysis for 3,000 words, and then returns to the child in the landing will lose the Achebe Test in the middle. Each section must have a human being holding the sensory ground.

**My remaining concern:** Section 5 (Two Conscriptions) risks becoming the English-French track at the expense of the Indigenous track. The conscription crisis is important — but whose crisis is it? The Indigenous veterans promised land grants who were excluded from receiving them are part of this section's material. Their experience of the war and the broken promise must be in Section 5, not only the English-French conscription argument. Henri Bourassa's nationalism and Crowfoot's grandsons enlisting: these must be in the same section, parallel, not hierarchical.

**The McClung question:** The notes are right to flag the complexity here. The chapter must hold McClung's genuine contribution and her assimilationist assumptions simultaneously. The technique is: show her winning what she won, in her own voice, in the register of her own moral self-understanding — and then show what her moral self-understanding could not see. No verdict. The reader sees both.

**On Qwenqwent's granddaughter in the landing:** This is the right structural choice if it is done carefully. She must not be deployed as a symbol of resilience — "despite everything, they survived" is the tourist frame, not the inside frame. She speaks Secwepemctsin because someone carried it in their body for fifty years. The language is there because people refused to let it not be there. That is different from resilience. That is insistence. The chapter's landing should carry the insistence, not the survival-against-odds.
