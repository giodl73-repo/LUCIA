## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Notes (Targeted)

**Focus:** Cultural ownership and female presence.

**What works:**
- The notes correctly identify Tushratta as the anchor figure and commit to showing him as an active negotiator, not a passive recipient. Good.
- Section 4 (The Hurrian World Beneath) is structurally placed to prevent the chapter from reducing Mitanni to its ruling class. The placement matters: after the *maryannu* military section, before the princess's journey, so that the reader understands the cultural ground before the diplomatic act.

**What needs strengthening:**
- Tadukhipa must have interiority, even if constructed carefully from what we know and what we honestly cannot know. The notes list her as a figure but describe her only in terms of her diplomatic function. What did a Mitanni princess's world look like? The Nuzi archives document women's legal rights in Mitanni-period society. Use this. She is not just a figure in transit; she is a person leaving one world for another.
- Gilukhipa's 317 attendants represent a female court-in-transit. These women carried Mitanni culture to Egypt. What did they bring? Dress, cosmetics, religious practice, language, cooking, music? The chapter should render at least a glimpse of this world.
- The Shaushka statue's dispatch to Egypt is correctly noted but under-developed. A king sending his goddess to heal a foreign pharaoh is an extraordinary act of diplomatic-religious faith. It reveals the interpenetration of religion and diplomacy in a way that no political analysis alone can.
