## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of Julio-Claudian Egypt Opening

**Overall:** The opening handles a difficult challenge well: telling the story of a colonized province without either celebrating the colonizer or reducing the colonized to victims. The priests are not passive. Augustus is not a villain. The tension is structural, not moral. This is honest work.

**What works:**
- The priests' perspective is the opening's moral center, and it is rendered from inside. "They had been recording the behavior of foreign rulers for a very long time." This is not victimhood. It is institutional patience. The priests are professionals managing the latest in a long line of rulers, and that framing gives Egypt its own agency without romanticizing it.
- "Pharaohs came and went. The rituals continued. The river rose." This is the Egyptian voice speaking, not the author summarizing. The sentence rhythm belongs to a culture that thinks in cycles, not in events. This passes the test.
- The opening does not step outside to judge Augustus's refusal to see the bull. It presents the refusal and lets the Egyptian response carry the weight. No verdict. No "this shows the insensitivity of Roman rulers." Just the act and the response.
- "They had been feeding foreign capitals before Rome was a city." This is the culture speaking about itself with quiet authority. It does not compare Egypt favorably to Rome; it simply states a fact from Egypt's own chronological perspective.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening's voice is predominantly Roman in its perspective for the first three paragraphs. Augustus acts; the text follows him. The Egyptian counter-voice appears in paragraph 2 and the end of paragraph 4, but the grain system and India trade paragraphs are described from the perspective of what Egypt produced FOR Rome, not from the perspective of how Egyptians understood their own economic life. "Egypt was too productive to be trusted to the political class" is a Roman thought. What is the Egyptian thought?
- "The Egyptians who worked the land" is a collective that risks becoming scenery. Who are these people? Do they have names in the papyri? They do. The Oxyrhynchus papyri are full of named individuals: farmers petitioning for justice, women managing property, priests complaining about tax assessments. The opening should use at least one.
- The word "province" appears as if it is a neutral term. It is not. It is a Roman category. What did the Egyptians call the arrangement they lived under?

**The question I'd push on:**
Whose story is this chapter telling? The opening currently tells it from a position equidistant between Rome and Egypt. That is honest and defensible. But the OVERVIEW says the voice should be "discover" and the tension between imperial extraction and local continuity. If the chapter is to be told from inside Egypt's experience of Roman rule, the Egyptian voice needs to be the dominant register by the end of the opening, not just the counterpoint. Can the chapter find its way fully inside the Egyptian perspective without losing the Roman administrative machinery that makes the era legible?
