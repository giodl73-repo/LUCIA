# Het Nederlandse Imperium — De Onderneming als Staat

The Dutch empire did not disguise its purpose. Where the Portuguese wrapped commerce in crusade and the Spanish wrapped it in theology, the Dutch made commerce the purpose and were unapologetic about it. The Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie — the VOC, the Dutch East India Company, founded in 1602 — was the first publicly traded joint-stock corporation, the first multinational company, and the first commercial entity to operate with the powers of a state: the right to wage war, conclude treaties, establish colonies, and administer justice in its own name. The VOC was not a tool of the Dutch state; it was, in significant respects, the Dutch state's extension into the world, a commercial enterprise organized with military efficiency and governed by the shareholders' demand for return on investment.

This is the Dutch empire's distinctive contribution to the history of imperialism: the corporate model. The VOC separated the management of imperial operations from the state, distributed the financial risk and the financial reward among shareholders, and created an administrative apparatus whose primary accountability was to its investors rather than to a king or a church. The VOC's annual dividends — which averaged 18% over 200 years — were the envy of Europe and the model for the British East India Company, which replicated the structure for the conquest of India. The Dutch empire was not merely Dutch; it was the template for the corporate colonialism that would shape the world's political economy for the next three centuries.

- **Amsterdam** — the financial center of the seventeenth-century world; the VOC headquarters; Rembrandt and Vermeer; the Golden Age built on trade

## What Defined Them

**The VOC as template.** The VOC's organizational innovation — a joint-stock company with state powers, operated by professional managers on behalf of shareholders, accountable to profit rather than to political or religious authority — was the most consequential institutional invention of the early modern period. It separated ownership from management, distributed risk across thousands of shareholders, and created the financial instruments (transferable shares, bonds, options) that form the foundation of modern capital markets. The Amsterdam Exchange Bank (1609) and the Amsterdam Stock Exchange (the world's first) were created to serve the VOC's capital needs and became the model for every subsequent financial center. The Dutch didn't just build an empire; they invented the financial infrastructure of capitalism.

**The Cultuurstelsel — Java as a plantation.** Governor-General Johannes van den Bosch's Cultivation System (1830–1870) required each Javanese village to devote 20% of its agricultural land (or 66 days of labor per year) to producing export crops — coffee, sugar, indigo, tea — for the Dutch state. The system generated enormous revenues: at its peak, Java's forced-crop revenues funded approximately one-third of the Dutch national budget, including the construction of the Dutch railway system. The cost was famine — the Javanese famines of the 1840s and 1850s killed hundreds of thousands. Multatuli's novel *Max Havelaar* (1860), a Dutch official's devastating exposé of the system, is one of the most important anti-colonial documents in European literature, written by a Dutchman about Dutch practice.

**Deshima and the mediation of modernity.** The Dutch trading post on the artificial island of Deshima in Nagasaki harbor was the only point of European contact with Japan during the Tokugawa period (1641–1854). The Dutch, who accepted the conditions Japan imposed (confinement to the island, restrictions on religious practice, annual tribute to the Shogunate), maintained this contact while the Spanish and Portuguese were expelled for proselytizing. The consequence was that all European science and technology that reached Japan in this period — medicine, physics, astronomy, military technology — came through Dutch intermediaries. Rangaku ("Dutch learning") became the Japanese term for Western science. The Dutch were the window through which Japan saw the scientific revolution.

**Apartheid's Dutch roots.** The Cape Colony, established in 1652 as a way-station for the India route, developed a racial hierarchy that would eventually become the foundation of the apartheid system. The Dutch settlers (Boers) who moved inland to escape VOC control developed a Calvinist theology of racial difference and a society built on the labor of enslaved Africans and Asians. When the British took the Cape Colony, the Boers moved further inland. The racial architecture that the Dutch settlers built in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries — the legal distinction between settler and indigenous, the systematic dispossession of land, the labor coercion of non-white populations — is the direct ancestor of the apartheid state established by their descendants in 1948.

## The Chapters

**1 — The First Auction:** In 1602, the shares of the newly founded VOC go on sale in Amsterdam. Merchants, tradespeople, and wealthy citizens buy them. For the first time in history, ordinary people own shares in a colonial trading enterprise — and the enterprise's success or failure will affect them directly. The first shareholder meeting discusses the spice routes. The first dividend will come from Javanese nutmeg, purchased with the proceeds of violence the shareholders in Amsterdam will never see.

**2 — Batavia:** Jan Pieterszoon Coen, the VOC's most effective and most brutal governor, founds the city of Batavia on the ruins of Jayakarta in 1619 and immediately begins the systematic destruction of competing Javanese trading networks. His correspondence to the VOC directors in Amsterdam is precise about means and ends: the spice trade monopoly requires force; force requires the willingness to use it. "There's nothing in the world that gives a better right than power and force added to right," he writes. The VOC adopts this as operational doctrine.

**3 — Max Havelaar:** Eduard Douwes Dekker, writing as "Multatuli," publishes *Max Havelaar* in 1860. It is a Dutch novel about Dutch colonial administration in Java, and it describes what the Cultuurstelsel has done to the Javanese peasantry. It causes a sensation in the Netherlands and helps end the Cultuurstelsel. It does not cause immediate Javanese independence — that will take another eighty-nine years — but it introduces the concept of colonial injustice into the Dutch political conversation.

**4 — Deshima:** A Dutch physician named Engelbert Kaempfer is living on the artificial island of Deshima in 1690. He is permitted to travel to Edo (Tokyo) once a year to present the VOC's tribute to the Shogun. On these journeys, he observes Japan with a naturalist's precision. His *History of Japan* (1727) is the first comprehensive account of the country available to European readers. The Dutch are the keyhole through which Europe watches Japan, and through which Japan watches Europe.

**5 — The Indonesian Revolution:** August 17, 1945: two days after Japan's surrender, Sukarno and Hatta proclaim Indonesian independence. The Dutch spend the next four years trying to reimpose colonial rule, conducting what they called "police actions" and what the Indonesian nationalists called a war of independence. The United States pressures the Netherlands to recognize Indonesian independence in 1949. The colony that produced the VOC's greatest revenues is gone.

**6 — The Acknowledgment:** December 19, 2022: the Dutch Prime Minister Mark Rutte formally apologizes for the Netherlands' role in the Atlantic slave trade — 550,000 enslaved people transported over 250 years, the trade organized primarily by the VOC and WIC. It is the first such formal acknowledgment by a Dutch head of government. The colonial past, the Dutch government says, has "shaped our nation." The work of reckoning with what that means has begun.
