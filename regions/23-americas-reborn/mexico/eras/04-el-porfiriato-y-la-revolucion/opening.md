# El Porfiriato y La Revolución
## Era 4 — 1876–1920 CE

---

The Plan de Ayala said what the villages had been saying for thirty years without anyone writing it down.

Emiliano Zapata carried it out of the sierra of Morelos in November 1911, signed by twenty-seven men at the village of Ayoxustla. It was not a declaration of war. It was a declaration of what had already happened: that the haciendas had swallowed the communal lands, that the courts had legitimized the swallowing, that the ejidos — the village commons held since before the Spaniards came — had been surveyed into private property by men with instruments who arrived with legal papers and left with land. The Plan said: restore what was taken. It named the hacendados. It named the lawyers. It named Porfirio Díaz, still clinging to power in Mexico City, as the source of the rot. And then it named Zapata as the commander who would take the land back.

He was thirty-one years old. He had watched the hacienda of Hospital absorb the fields of Anenecuilco, a Nahua village in the tierra caliente of Morelos where sugarcane grew tall enough to hide a man on horseback and the smell of the mill came down the valley when the wind turned. The village records — title documents going back to 1607, maps drawn in the colonial era showing which land belonged to which community — had been locked in an iron box and buried in the church floor when it became clear that anyone holding indigenous title deeds was in danger. Zapata knew where the box was buried. He had been elected president of the village council at twenty-nine, which meant he was responsible for those papers, which meant he was responsible for Anenecuilco itself, for the memory it carried and the argument it could still make.

He dug up the box. He took the documents to the state capital of Cuernavaca. The governor told him the courts were the proper venue. He took the documents to the courts. The courts told him the land had been properly transferred. He took the men of Anenecuilco to the fields and they planted the disputed land while the harvest season still permitted it and waited to see who would come to stop them.

No one came that season.

The revolution was already in the air by then. Francisco Madero had published his plan — the Plan de San Luis — the previous year, calling for free elections and the overthrow of the Díaz dictatorship. Díaz had been president since 1876. He had stabilized the country, which is to say he had built railroads and telegraph lines and invited foreign capital and suppressed every movement that threatened the order he called progress. The rurales — his rural police — dealt with banditry and dissent with equal efficiency. The hacienda system had expanded under his protection until a few thousand families owned the productive land of Mexico and the people who worked it did so under conditions that the men who built those haciendas understood as the natural order. Díaz called this modernization. The men who owned the sisal plantations of Yucatán, where Maya workers were bound by debt peonage they could never repay, agreed with him.

What Madero offered was elections. What Zapata offered was something else.

He rode out of Morelos in the spring of 1911 with an army of campesinos who owned machetes and, when they could find them, rifles. They were not fighting for a constitution. They were fighting for the milpa — the cornfield, the bean rows, the squash vines growing between them in the pattern their grandmothers had taught. They were fighting for the right to plant on ground their ancestors had planted for generations before a surveyor arrived with a transit and a document in Spanish that reclassified community memory as private property. When Zapata's men took a town, they returned the land records to the village authorities. That was the revolution they were making.

Porfirio Díaz went into exile in May 1911, sailing from Veracruz to Europe on a ship called the Ypiranga. He is said to have told a companion: Mexico needed a dictatorship, and Mexico will learn that. He was wrong about the first part.

Madero became president. He asked Zapata to disarm. Zapata refused until the land was returned. Madero asked him again. Zapata released the Plan de Ayala, which called Madero a traitor to the revolution and placed the land question at the center of any politics that could call itself revolutionary. The Mexican Revolution — which would go on for another nine years, through Madero's assassination, through the rule of Victoriano Huerta, through Villa from the north and Zapata from the south, through Carranza and Obregón and the Constitution of 1917 and Zapata's own assassination in 1919 — was many things. It was a power struggle between regional strongmen. It was a conflict over the nature of the state. It was a war between an old order and several possible new ones.

But Zapata knew what he was fighting for, and he wrote it down, and the villages of Morelos knew what they were fighting for, and they kept fighting for it after Zapata was dead.

The Constitution of 1917 put it in Article 27: the ejido was restored as a legal category; communal land could not be alienated; Mexico's subsoil — its oil, its minerals — belonged to the nation, not to the foreign companies that had extracted it under Díaz. It was the most progressive constitutional document of its era, drafted by men who had made a revolution and were trying to write down what they had promised.

The villages of Morelos, in 1920, were still waiting for the land.

They are still waiting.
