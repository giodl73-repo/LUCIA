# Al-Ayyūbiyyūn — Saladin's Dynasty

We are the house of Ayyub — *Al-Ayyūbiyyūn* — the Kurdish dynasty that Salah al-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub founded when he took Egypt from the last Fatimid caliph and turned its resources toward the Sunni restoration. Saladin understood the strategic situation with the clarity that made him the most celebrated commander of his age: the Islamic world was fighting on two fronts simultaneously, the Fatimid Shia caliphate in Cairo was undermining Sunni unity from within, and the Crusaders on the Levantine coast were holding Jerusalem and the surrounding territory that the *umma* regarded as its own. His solution was to reunify Egypt and Syria under a single Sunni power — his own — and then direct that unified power against the Crusaders.

The reconquest of Jerusalem in 1187 CE was the moment that defined the Ayyubid dynasty in Islamic memory. Eighty-eight years after the Crusaders had massacred the city's Muslim and Jewish population when they took it, Saladin's army rode through the gates and Saladin gave the order that there would be no massacre. The Christians who could pay ransom would be released; those who could not would be enslaved; the poor would be freed by Saladin's own donation. This was not naïve clemency. It was deliberate policy, designed to demonstrate the moral contrast between his conduct and the Crusaders' conduct in 1099, and to establish the terms on which Muslims and Christians could coexist in the region after the battle.

## What Defined Them

**The *jihad* as political organization.** Saladin's genius was to understand that *jihad* — the effort on behalf of God's community — was not merely a religious obligation but a political technology. By framing the struggle against the Crusaders as *jihad*, he gave the unified command he sought over the rival Muslim powers of Syria and Egypt a religious legitimacy that mere ambition could not have provided. The *jihad* framing also mobilized resources — the scholars who issued fatwas, the preachers who stirred the cities, the fighters who came from across the Islamic world to participate — that a purely dynastic campaign could not have accessed. The reconquest of Jerusalem was the military culmination of this political-religious strategy.

**Chivalric conduct as strategic tool.** Saladin's reputation for clemency — ransoming poor Crusader prisoners, releasing widowed women with honor, returning Richard I's horse when the English king was dismounted — was not simply personal virtue. It was the strategic use of clemency to build a reputation that made subsequent negotiations possible and that demonstrated the moral contrast with the Crusaders' conduct. The Islamic tradition preserved these stories as examples of Saladin's piety and justice; the Crusader tradition preserved them as examples of a worthy adversary. Both traditions agreed that Saladin had behaved in a way that transcended the ordinary conduct of war.

**The Ayyubid division of territory.** Saladin understood that the Ayyubid family's governance of Egypt and Syria could not be maintained as a single unified state after his death without the personal authority he provided. He distributed the territories among his sons and brothers — Egypt to al-Adil, Syria to al-Afdal and al-Zahir — while attempting to maintain family coordination on matters of common concern (above all, the Crusaders). This system produced a generation of Ayyubid princes who were competent administrators but constant rivals, capable of defending their individual territories but unable to coordinate sustained offensive action. The Fifth and Sixth Crusades found this fragmented Ayyubid world easier to negotiate with than to defeat militarily.

**Cairo's Citadel and the institutional inheritance.** The Citadel of Cairo — built by Saladin on the Muqattam hills overlooking the city — was the military-administrative heart of Ayyubid Egypt and its Mamluk successors. Saladin built it not as a palace but as a fortified command post: the place from which Egypt's military power was organized, the army fed and paid, the defense coordinated. The institutional infrastructure he built — the *iqta* system of military grants, the administrative apparatus inherited from the Fatimids — was so effective that the Mamluks who displaced the Ayyubids used it unchanged for two more centuries.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Sunni Restoration:** Saladin stands at the pulpit of a mosque in Cairo and the Friday sermon is said in the name of the Abbasid caliph for the first time in two centuries. The last Fatimid caliph died without an heir. The Ismaili *da'wa* network is being dismantled. Al-Azhar is being converted to Sunni teaching. In Egypt and Syria, the Ayyubid reunification of the Sunni world is proceeding.

**2 — Jerusalem:** Saladin's army rides through the gates of Jerusalem in October 1187. The contrast with 1099 is deliberate and visible: no massacre, no indiscriminate looting, the Christian shrines protected, the ransom negotiations conducted with care. Saladin himself reportedly helped pay the ransoms of poor prisoners from his own treasury. The moment is being made memorable, because it will need to be remembered.

**3 — The Dynasty After Saladin:** The Ayyubid princes are negotiating with the Sixth Crusade's Frederick II while simultaneously watching the Mongols approach from the east. The dynasty that Saladin built is managing, carefully, the twin pressures that will end it. When the Mamluk soldiers who have been the Ayyubid army's most effective element turn on the last Ayyubid sultan in Egypt, they will use the institutions and the tactics that Saladin built. The dynasty ends; its legacy continues in the hands of the men it armed.
