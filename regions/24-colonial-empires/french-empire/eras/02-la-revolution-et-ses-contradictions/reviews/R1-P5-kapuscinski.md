# Panel Review — R1 / P-5 / Ryszard Kapuściński
## French Empire Era 2: La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

I have spent my working life in the physical world of imperial collapse — in Angola, Ethiopia, Iran, the places where a system that believed in its own permanence discovered it was wrong. What I look for in an opening is whether I can feel the place. Not describe it — feel it. The heat, the smell, the texture of the particular air that hangs over this specific catastrophe.

**The opening is intellectually magnificent and sensorially thin.**

What I know after reading it: Moreau de Saint-Méry was in a library; he was cataloguing; the Declaration was being written in Paris; there were 500,000 enslaved people; on August 22, plantations burned. What I cannot feel: the heat of a Saint-Domingue night in August. The smell of burning cane — which is not like burning wood, it is sweeter and oilier and the ash catches in the throat differently. The sound of Port-au-Prince harbor at dawn when the trading ships unload. The specific quality of light on the northern plain that the insurrection lit.

"The fires could be seen from the harbor at Le Cap-Français" is the closest the opening comes to giving me a body to stand in. I want to be standing in that harbor. I want to know what it felt like to be a colonist watching those fires and understanding, for the first time, that the system you believed was permanent had a seam in it.

**What works sensorially:**

The names of the racial categories — *Sacatra, Griffe, Marabou, Quarteronne, Métis, Mamelouque, Quarteron, Sang-mêlé* — are the opening's best sensory detail. They land in the mouth with weight. The reader feels the baroque intricacy of the taxonomy through the sound of the words before understanding the categories. This is the opening's most accomplished sensory-analytical fusion.

The detail of Toussaint's copy of Épictète — "the edition Moreau de Saint-Méry had catalogued in Port-au-Prince" — is wonderful precisely because it links two worlds through a single physical object. The chapter needs more of these linkages.

**The question I'd push on:**

The chapter needs at least one scene where we are in a specific body in a specific physical place. Moreau de Saint-Méry at his desk is a start, but I want to smell his library: the damp paper, the ink, the particular mustiness of a colonial official's archive in a tropical climate. And I want, somewhere in the chapter, to be in the northern plain during the insurrection — not narrating it but feeling it. Even a paragraph. Even through Leclerc's dispatches read in Paris, the smell of those dispatches, what it was like to hold a letter that described a world you could not imagine.
