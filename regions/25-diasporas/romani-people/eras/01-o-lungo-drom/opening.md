# O Lungo Drom — The Journey from India

The word for road is drom. It comes from the Sanskrit druma, the way, the path, the thing a person walks. A thousand years after the departure from India, the word is still there, in the mouth of every Romani speaker from Andalusia to the Balkans, carrying inside it the first fact of this people's existence: they walked. They walked west out of the plains of northwestern India, through Persia, through the lands of the Byzantine emperors, and into the forests of the Balkans, and the language they carried recorded every place they passed through. The Persian word for friend entered the vocabulary: dost. The Armenian word for road joined the old Sanskrit one. The Greek words for time and flesh and the names of certain tools settled into the grammar as naturally as the dust of those countries had settled into the hems of their clothing. Every borrowing is a footprint. The language is the map.

No one wrote down why they left.

This is not a gap in the record that scholarship has failed to close. It is the condition of the record itself. The people who became the Roma were not the kind of people for whom chronicles were kept. They were not kings. They were not priests whose temples required inventories. Some scholars believe they were members of the Dom caste, musicians and metalworkers whose skills made them mobile and whose status placed them outside the structures that generate written records. Others point to the armies of Mahmud of Ghazni, who raided northern India seventeen times between 998 and 1030, and suggest the ancestors of the Roma were camp followers, smiths, soldiers swept into the current of military movement. Others simply note that northwestern India in the eleventh century was a place of upheaval, that people moved, and that no one with a pen thought them worth recording.

What remains is the language. And the skills in the hands: a smith working a portable forge at the edge of a village, shaping iron into blades and hinges for the settled people, buying with his craft the right to camp for a night, a week, a season. When the welcome thins, the forge packs onto the wagon and the road opens again.

The Romani word for mother is dej. For father, dad. For sister, phen. For horse, grast. For water, pani. For fire, yag. Linguists have traced these words back through the Indo-Aryan family to their Sanskrit roots, and the words reveal a departure point: the Punjab, the Rajasthan, the northwestern edge of the subcontinent where the plains dry and the trade routes run west toward Persia. The grammar confirms it. Romani preserves features of early medieval Prakrit that place the separation somewhere around the tenth or eleventh century. The language is not merely evidence of origin. It is the origin itself, carried forward in the mouths of children who had never seen India but who said pani when they wanted water and grast when they meant the animal that pulled the wagon and drom when they spoke of the road.

They moved through Persia. The Persian loanwords in Romani are older than the Armenian ones, which are older than the Greek ones. Each layer of borrowed vocabulary marks a place where the travelers stopped long enough for the local language to leave its mark on theirs. In Persia, they acquired words for commerce and measurement. In the Armenian highlands, words for tools and landscape. In the Byzantine lands, words for the market, for the church they encountered but did not build. The borrowings cluster around the things a traveling people need from the settled world: the words for buying and selling, for the materials of craft, for the authorities who grant or deny permission to stay.

By the thirteenth century, they were in the Balkans. The Greek chroniclers noticed them and called them Athinganoi, a word associated with fortune-telling and impurity. From Athinganoi came Tsigane, Zigeuner, Zingaro, and a dozen other European names, none of them the name the people used for themselves. They called themselves Roma. The word means people. It is a word from India, from the Prakrit rama, and it traveled the entire length of the road without changing.

The road itself is the story. Not the origin, not the destination, not the question of why they left, but the fact of having walked, and the fact of still walking. O lungo drom. The long road. It begins in the dust of the Punjab and it arrives in the language of a people scattered across every country in Europe who have never stopped moving. Everything that follows, the music and the metalwork, the persecution and the survival, happens on this road. A mother teaches her daughter to say pani for water. A smith sets up his forge at the edge of a village he has never seen before. A child learns a Greek word for bread and a Romani word for home that does not mean a building. The road is not background. It is the first word in a sentence that has not ended.
