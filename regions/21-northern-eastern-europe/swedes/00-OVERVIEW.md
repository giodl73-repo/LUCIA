# Svear och Götar — "The Swedes and the Goths"

Sweden is two peoples learning to become one. The Svear, centered on the great temple at Uppsala with its sacred grove where every nine years the sacrifices of men, horses, and other animals were hung from the trees, held the north and center. The Götar, the Geats of Beowulf's saga, held the south. Their union — achieved gradually, contested, never quite complete — produced the Swedish kingdom. But the Swear's reach went east as well as south: through the Baltic, down the rivers of what would become Russia, to the great trade routes that connected the Scandinavian world to Byzantium and the Caspian. The Swedes of the Viking age are the eastern Vikings — the Varangians — and their contribution to European history runs not through England and Normandy but through Novgorod and Kyiv and Constantinople.

The sacrifice at Gamla Uppsala — the great heathen temple that Adam of Bremen described with horrified fascination in the 1070s, when it was already giving way to Christianity — represents a spiritual worldview organized around the gift exchange between the human world and the divine: you give to the gods, the gods give back, and the prosperity of the community depends on maintaining the reciprocal relationship. The king was the ritual intermediary. A king who failed to perform the sacrifices correctly, or who failed to bring good harvests and military success, could be sacrificed himself — there are records of Swedish kings killed by their own people for failing the land. This is not tyranny; it is a theologically coherent system in which the king's body is pledged to the community's welfare.

## What Defined Them

**The Uppsala cult — sacrifice as statecraft.** Adam of Bremen's description of the temple at Gamla Uppsala — where golden statues of Odin, Thor, and Freyr stood in a hall ringed by a sacred grove, and where every nine years a great sacrifice of humans and animals was celebrated — has been questioned by historians who point out that Adam was a Christian writing with polemical intent. But the archaeology confirms a major cult site at Uppsala, and the literary tradition of the *blót* (sacrifice) as the king's central religious obligation is consistent across many sources. The Swedish king was not merely a military leader and administrator — he was the ritual guarantor of the community's relationship with the divine powers that controlled harvest, weather, and war.

**The Varangian east — Sweden's other world.** While the Danish Vikings went west and the Norwegian Vikings went west and north, the Swedish Vikings went primarily east — down the rivers of what is now Russia, through the portages between watersheds, to the Black Sea and the Caspian. This eastward expansion produced Novgorod and Kyiv (both founded or reinforced by Varangians), the Varangian Guard in Constantinople, and the commercial network that brought Byzantine silk and Arab silver to Uppsala. The eastern orientation of Swedish Viking activity is the reason Sweden's Viking age archaeology is full of Arabic coins and Byzantine jewelry — the east was where the wealth was.

**Birka and the commercial revolution.** Birka, on an island in Lake Mälaren, was from the eighth to the tenth century the largest and richest trading town in Scandinavia. Its archaeological record shows trade goods from across the known world: Byzantine silk, Rhenish glassware, Arabic silver, Frankish swords, Finnish furs. Birka was a royal trading post where the Swedish kings could tax the commerce flowing through the Baltic system. Its abandonment around 975 CE was not a collapse but a relocation — the trade moved to Sigtuna on the mainland, closer to the Uppsala power center. The Birka record demonstrates that the Viking age was simultaneously an age of raid and an age of commerce.

**The law codes and the free peasant.** Swedish medieval law codes — the *Västgötalagen* (Västergötland law), the *Upplandlagen*, and the others — reflect a society with a strong tradition of free peasant landownership. The Swedish *bonde* (free peasant farmer) had legal standing in the thing assemblies, could own arms, and could sue and be sued. The law codes were compiled in the thirteenth century as written texts, but they preserved older oral custom. This strong peasant tradition shaped Swedish politics throughout the medieval period: Swedish kings who tried to impose heavy taxation or centralized control faced organized peasant resistance that they could not simply crush.

**The Baltic empire and the Hanseatic connection.** Sweden's incorporation into the Hanseatic League's commercial orbit — Visby on Gotland was a Hanseatic city before Sweden itself was fully organized — gave Swedish merchants access to the North Sea and Atlantic trading networks. The later Swedish Baltic empire (seventeenth century, beyond this era's frame) grew from the commercial and political foundations laid in the medieval period: the control of Baltic ports, the export of Swedish iron and copper, and the military capacity to hold the sea lanes. The path from Birka to Stockholm to a Baltic empire is a continuous story.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Old Time:** A Svear king climbs the burial mound of his ancestor at Old Uppsala and surveys the plain below. The mound under his feet was built three hundred years ago. The people who built it are his people. The gods to whom they sacrificed are his gods. The obligation to continue the ritual is his obligation. He is not troubled by this weight. He is defined by it.

**2 — The Eastern Vikings:** A Varangian merchant reaches Constantinople after three months on the river systems of Rus — the portages, the rapids of the Dnieper, the negotiations at every trading post. The city he enters is larger than anything in the Norse world. He has been here before, and it still stops him. He has silk to sell and silver to buy and a route home to plan. Constantinople is not the world — but from here, you can see it.

**3 — The Christianizing:** The last priest of the Uppsala temple watches the Christian missionaries arrive with the support of the Swedish king. He knows what this means. The gold statues will be taken down. The sacred grove will be cut. The nine-year sacrifice will not be performed. He does not resist. He is very old. He has outlasted three kings already.

**4 — Kingdom-Formation:** A Swedish lawman reads the new written law code aloud at the thing assembly of Uppland. For the first time, the law is not what the assembled men remember — it is what the parchment says. Some of the older men are uncomfortable with this. The lawman reads on anyway.

**5 — The Kalmar Union:** Engelbrekt Engelbrektsson leads the Swedish peasant uprising against the Danish regent in 1434. He is a minor ironmaster from Bergslagen, not a noble. He wins. He is assassinated the following year by a Swedish noble who fears what the peasants will do if they discover that they can win. The contradiction between Swedish free peasant tradition and noble ambition will not resolve for three more centuries.
