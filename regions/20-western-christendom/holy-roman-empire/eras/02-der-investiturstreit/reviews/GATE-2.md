**Notes:**

The chapter achieves its most difficult task: making the investiture controversy feel urgent and human without reducing it to a personality conflict between Gregory and Heinrich. The five-section structure uses the doubled-authority engine to give the argument a genuine arc — from unremarked ceremony through to constitutionalized irresolution.

The standout achievement is the Canossa section: the bells marking canonical hours, audible in the outer courtyard, are the auditory experience of excommunication — the prayer rising inside the walls that the excommunicate king can hear and not enter. This is the kind of sensory-analytical fusion that the highest-scoring chapters achieve: the detail carries the argument.

The Ariald figure is the chapter's second major achievement: showing that the investiture controversy was not only a constitutional argument between elites but a popular religious movement demanding that the institution people depended on for their spiritual lives be free from commerce. This prevents the chapter from being the story of two powerful men and makes it the story of a civilization in argument with itself.
