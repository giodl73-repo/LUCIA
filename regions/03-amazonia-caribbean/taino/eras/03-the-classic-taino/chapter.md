# The Classic Taíno (c. 1200–1492 CE)

---

She has been working on this areíto for three days.

The lead lines are settled now: the opening invocation of Atabey, the earth mother who holds the island in her body, and then the passage through the four directions, and then the ancestor-naming that links the cacicazgo of Xaragua back through seven generations to the one who first emerged from the cave at Cacibajagua, when the world was still being sorted into its proper forms. These lines belong to the tradition. Anacaona has not invented them; she has inherited them, as she inherited the duho she sits on, the carved wooden seat whose slight backward tilt places the cacica in the posture of someone who is always on the verge of receiving. A cacica receives. She receives the zemís' guidance, the community's need, the ancestors' instruction. She receives the lines from those who came before and passes them forward, changed a little, charged with the particular weight of her understanding.

What she has been working on is the middle section. The new part. The part that must be hers.

She is composing in the way that all areíto composition works, which is through the body first. She has been singing the phrase to herself in the rhythm of the ceremony, moving her weight from foot to foot as the community will move it, feeling where the phrase wants to land and where it resists, where the body's knowledge says yes and where it says not yet. The words are not separate from the motion. The motion is not separate from the sound. An areíto that is only words is a lesson. An areíto that carries the right rhythm in the right body makes the zemís present, which is the whole point.

Outside the bohío, the late afternoon is cooling toward the hour when the fireflies emerge in the plantain groves along the river. Through the thatch, the light comes in as bars of amber and shadow, and the carved wooden figures on their low platforms catch the light on their cheekbones and their hollow eyes: three zemís, their faces turned toward the interior space where she works. The smallest one, the cotton figure stuffed with dry herbs that carries her grandmother's presence, has a patina from years of handling. Copal smoke has darkened the walls around it. The smell of the bohío in the late afternoon is the smell of all the ceremonies that have happened here: smoke and dried cassava and the faint sweetness of the cohoba powder in its shallow container on the carved-bone shelf. In the conucos beyond the village boundary, the manioc mounds rise in their rows, their broad leaves still holding the day's heat. The women who tend them are returning, their hands carrying the particular smell of earth and broken stems that means the ground is in good order. Everything is doing what it is supposed to do.

This is what the areíto must hold.

---

Anacaona's cacicazgo of Xaragua occupies the western end of Ayiti, the island of mountains. Its bays open to the sea on three sides, and its rivers run from the central cordillera through the most productive agricultural land on the island. This geography is not separate from Xaragua's authority. The conuco system sustains that authority from the ground up. The earthen mounds in which the manioc grows, each mound raised high enough to hold moisture and aerate the roots, represent a women's knowledge that runs from grandmother to daughter across generations, specific in its demands and non-trivial in its application.

The tipití press works like this. You cut the bitter manioc root and grate it on a board studded with sharp stones, and what you get is a wet mass that contains enough prussic acid to kill you if you eat it directly. The tipití is a woven tube, longer than a man's arm, and you pack the grated manioc in and hang it from a tree and weight the lower end with a heavy stone, and the slow squeeze over an hour extracts the poison into a bucket below. What remains in the tube is dry enough to press through a sieve and onto the budare, the clay griddle, where it cooks into casabi, the bread that is the island's staff. Knowing how to do this correctly, how long to grate, how much weight, how long to cook, is not incidental knowledge. It is the foundation of everything else. Without the women who carry this knowledge, there is no casabi, and without casabi, there is no areíto feast, and without the areíto feast, there is no community, and without the community, there is nothing for the cacique to lead.

The cacique leads the reciprocity. This is the clearest way to say what the cacicazgo is. The reciprocity runs between the living and the dead, between the human community and the zemí-inhabited forces that govern the island's fertility and weather and the outcome of births, between the cacicazgos themselves through the ceremonies that maintain relationship rather than requiring conflict. Bohechío, Anacaona's brother, led Xaragua before her, and he was the most accomplished host on the island in his generation: his areítos drew caciques from Maguana and from Marién, from the inland communities and from the coast, and the communities that came learned the ceremonies and carried them home, and this is how Xaragua's knowing extended its reach. Not through soldiers. Through song.

The batey courts at Xaragua are the largest on Ayiti. They are flat, rectangular, stone-lined spaces, some of them long enough that a ball struck at one end must travel the whole length before it is struck again. The striking is done with the hip or the knee or the shoulder, never the hand, never the foot, because the ball is not a ball in the way of children's games. The ball is the sun. The court is the sky. The players are the community's living edge, the place where the human world and the cosmic order meet and maintain their relationship through the game's action. Getting this wrong, playing without the proper preparation, playing without the zemís attended, is not poor sport. It is cosmological disorder, and the consequences are felt in the rains and the harvest and the outcome of births. Playing it correctly, with the ceremony and the preparation and the whole community watching and sustaining the players with their attention, is how the island stays in its proper arrangement.

A cacicazgo produces better areítos when it is materially secure: more people, more rehearsal time, more feasts at which the ceremony is performed and refined. Better areítos travel further. Areítos that travel further draw more communities into Xaragua's ceremonial network, which increases Xaragua's prestige, which brings more resources into the cacicazgo's reach. The loop runs in both directions. A cacicazgo that cannot feed its guests at the areíto is a cacicazgo losing its authority. Xaragua's land keeps the areíto's authority intact. This has been true for three centuries.

---

In the third year after Anacaona came to lead Xaragua alone, her husband Caonabó gone and taken by the strangers before she fully understood what taking meant in their terms, her brother Bohechío received a visitor from the north. Bartholomew Columbus came with a party of men, and what Bohechío offered him was the ceremony that Xaragua reserved for its most important guests.

Thirty women came first in the canoe procession, wearing the cotton garments of ceremony, carrying palm branches, singing. The singing was not welcome-song in the casual sense. It was an areíto section: the genealogical recitation that placed this meeting inside the ongoing history of Xaragua, made it part of the story that the community had been telling about itself since before the living could remember. Then came the cacique's procession. Then the feasting, the casabi and the chicha, the fermented beverage made from the same manioc root that feeds the island, the drink that marks the ceremonial gathering as separate from ordinary time. Then the batey game, played at full ceremony, the best players on the island performing before the most important gathering Xaragua had hosted in a generation.

Bartholomew Columbus wrote about this in his reports. He found it remarkable. He described it as a kind of theater, a show, a display of hospitality that was well-organized and even impressive. He was not wrong that it was organized. He was not able to see what the organization was for: that the procession was a political statement, that the areíto was a diplomatic instrument, that the batey game was a demonstration of the community's cosmological competence. The ceremony was not for him. He was being incorporated into Xaragua's ongoing story, made part of the archive, placed in relationship with the zemís whether he knew it or not. This is how Xaragua handled powerful strangers who arrived from outside the network of reciprocity. You did not exclude them. You performed the ceremony that brought them inside it.

What Columbus could not translate was the zemís.

The carved wooden figures, the stone faces, the cotton bodies stuffed with dry herbs: he saw these as objects of religious practice, as things to be noted and eventually removed. The Taíno understanding of the zemís was not that they were worshipped. They were inhabited. An ancestor who had died could choose to persist in the world by entering an object, usually a carved figure made for the purpose, and the ancestor's authority continued across death in the object that held them. The cacique's zemís were the lineage's accumulated presence, consultable, their guidance available through the proper ceremony. The community's zemís governed the local forces, the rain, the wind, the soil's fertility, that determined whether life was possible in this particular place.

To speak with a zemí required preparation. The duho, the carved seat tilted back so the posture opens the body to reception. The purging first, the reed down the throat and the clearing of the physical interior, because you come to the zemí empty, cleaned out, ready to receive. Then the tabaco: the forked tube, one prong in each nostril, and a companion blows the cohoba powder through the tube in a sharp breath, the dried and ground seeds of the Anadenanthera peregrina tree that hits the blood faster than anything eaten, and the room reorganizes. The zemí's carved face is not still anymore. The conversation happens in a register that is not quite sound and not quite vision. The cacique brings back what the zemí has communicated and reports it to the community assembled outside. He has been the channel. He was purged and opened and then used by the conversation itself, and what comes back through him is the zemí's own knowing, not his interpretation of it.

This is not separate from the areíto. This is its complement. The areíto is the community's collective transmission: everyone present, everyone moving and singing together, the knowledge alive in the shared body. The cohoba consultation is the individual practitioner's interface: the cacique alone, opened, receiving. Both are required. The areíto carries what is known; the cohoba ceremony establishes contact with what needs to be asked. Together they constitute the Taíno practice of knowing, the system that maintains the community's relationship with everything that governs it.

---

What the areíto carries is not doctrine. It is record, genealogy, instruction, and cosmological orientation, all in the same breath. The content of the living library is the content of the island's story as the island's people understand it.

The emergence from Cacibajagua: in the first time, before the world was fully sorted, the people were inside the earth, in the caves that opened beneath the mountains of Ayiti. The guardian of the cave where the most people waited was named Mácocael, and he was supposed to manage who could leave and when. But one day the sun caught him at the entrance, and the sun's touch turned him into stone, and while the guardian was becoming stone the people poured out into the world. Those who were slow, who paused at the threshold, were turned by the light into birds and trees and the animals of the forest. They were not punished by this. The world needed its birds and its trees and its animals, and the people who became them became necessary rather than lost. Those who made it out fully became the first people of the islands.

This narrative is not a story about long-ago. It is a story about what is still happening. Transformation between states of being is continuous. The dead become zemís. The living become ancestors. The birds in the plantain grove are the people who hesitated at the doorway of the world. The zemís in Anacaona's bohío are the grandmothers who chose to stay in the objects their families carved for them. The passage between states is not a boundary but a permeability. You are always partly of one world and partly of another, and the ceremonies are what maintain the right relationship between the states you inhabit simultaneously.

The areíto carries this. It carries the names: the genealogy that begins with the first ones who came out of Cacibajagua and runs down through the generations to the present community, each named person a link in the chain that makes the current community's existence legible. It carries the instructions: when to perform which ceremony, how to address which zemí, what the signs of cosmological disorder look like and what to do when they appear. It carries the history: the settlements that were made and unmade, the alliances through which the cacicazgos formed their current relationships, the events that required new understanding and produced new sections of the ceremony. When something new happens, a new areíto section is composed. The living library grows.

The lead singer's work is technical before it is expressive. She knows the genealogy in its entirety, hundreds of names in the order that is also an argument about who descends from whom and therefore who belongs to what. She knows which sections require which posture, which sections require which rhythm, which zemís are activated by which sequences of sound and motion. She teaches not by explanation but by performance: she sings it, the students mirror it, she refines it through the mirroring, the students absorb the refinement, and after enough repetitions the phrase lives in the body and is no longer hers but theirs. The knowledge passes through the body rather than through a surface. It cannot be copied. It can only be transmitted. This is why the lead singer's death, without a student who has received the full transmission, is the loss of what she carried.

The elder woman who teaches Anacaona's daughter the previous sections while Anacaona works on the new one has been singing since before Anacaona was born. She has the genealogy back fourteen generations on the paternal line and nine on the maternal. She knows the batey ceremony's full sequence in the Xaragua version and can perform three of the variations used in Maguana and one used in Marién. She knows which zemí governs the northern river and which governs the central mountain and which governs the batey court's own ground. She has never been to Borikén but she can recite the eastern frontier communities' genealogies because they were shared with Xaragua by marriage and the genealogies came with the marriage. She is the deepest part of the living library. When Anacaona adds her section, she is adding to an archive whose keeper has been maintaining it for sixty years.

---

The five cacicazgos of Ayiti, Xaragua, Maguana, Marién, Higüey, and Caonao, are not a federation. There is no council, no shared law, no single authority over the whole island. What holds them in relationship is the network of ceremony, marriage, and batey. A cacique who plays the ball game against a rival's cacicazgo is performing a ceremony that makes conflict between them less necessary; the game adjudicates what would otherwise require soldiers, and it does so in a form that maintains the cosmic order rather than disrupting it. The marriages between cacicazgo lineages do the same work at the level of kinship: Anacaona's marriage to Caonabó, the powerful cacique of Maguana, linked Xaragua's western authority to the mountain territory of the central island.

The areítos circulate across this network, carrying Xaragua's knowing into Maguana and Marién and being returned with whatever Xaragua needs that the other cacicazgos carry. A new areíto section from Xaragua travels to Maguana with the singers who come for the ceremony season. Maguana performs it, incorporating it into its own archive, and the singers who go back to Xaragua carry whatever Maguana has composed that season. The network is a library of libraries, each one distinct, each one in ongoing conversation with the others through the ceremonial exchange. The cacique who hosts the most communities at his areíto in any given year is the cacique building the broadest ceremonial obligation network. Obligation and ceremony are the same thing, administered simultaneously through the same gathering.

When a cacicazgo wanted to register a formal claim, over territory, over a marriage arrangement, over the resolution of a dispute about fishing grounds, the mechanism was not a court and not a written contract. It was the areíto. The lineage in question would commission a composition that embedded the claim in the genealogical record: your ancestor and my ancestor stood together at this moment, and from that standing together, the following understanding follows. If the other party attended the ceremony and sang the composition alongside the claimant's community, they had acknowledged the record. If they refused to attend, they had refused the relationship itself, which was a more serious act than refusal to sign a document, because it was a refusal visible to the zemís and the ancestors who governed both communities. The areíto was simultaneously archive, court, and witness.

This meant that the quality of a cacique's ceremonial capacity was also a measure of that cacique's political credibility. A cacique whose areítos did not work, whose zemís did not respond to the cohoba ceremony with clarity, whose batey games were performed sloppily or with the wrong preparation: such a cacique was demonstrating, publicly and in the presence of the forces that governed everything, that the reciprocity was not in good order. The community would notice. The neighboring cacicazgos would notice. Authority in this system was not held; it was continuously demonstrated.

Anacaona understood this with the precision that only those who have received the full weight of the tradition can understand it. Her areítos worked because she had learned the system completely, and because she had a particular gift for finding the phrase that made the zemís present: the precise rhythm, the precise combination of words and motion, the sequence that activated rather than merely described. This gift was not mystical in the sense of being irrational. It was technical. She had practiced it for thirty years. The gift and the practice were the same thing.

From the east, from the smaller islands where the Kalinago people maintain their own world, there is pressure of a different kind. The Kalinago are not the Taíno's enemies in the simple sense of a war being continuously fought. They are the Taíno's frontier: the condition of the eastern edge, the reason the communities of Borikén maintain a kind of readiness that the western communities do not need. Borikén faces the Kalinago wind directly. Its cacicazgos are organized with the frontier in mind. The batey courts there are also places where inter-community alliance is performed and maintained, but with the particular urgency of communities that need their internal relationships strong because their eastern relationships are uncertain. The batey ceremony is not only about cosmic maintenance; it is also about sustaining the social cohesion that makes coordinated response possible when the frontier becomes active.

What the Kalinago pressure produced in Borikén was a particular kind of political consciousness: the awareness that the ceremonial network had an edge, and that the edge required active maintenance. The cacicazgos of Borikén were more tightly organized than those of Hispaniola, their inter-community ceremonies more frequent, their marriage alliances more deliberately structured. The areíto traditions of Borikén included sections that addressed the frontier directly, ceremonial acknowledgment of the eastern sea and its conditions, the proper way to approach the zemís when the canoes from the east had been sighted. This is not fear. It is the ceremonial management of a fact. The world had an eastern edge, and the edge was alive.

The network held. The network had been holding for three centuries by the time Anacaona composed her areíto in the late afternoon of the island's last full era. It held because the reciprocity worked, because the manioc grew in its mounds and the women pressed and baked and fed the gatherings and the caciques organized the ceremonies and the zemís were properly attended and the ancestors stayed in their objects and the living and the dead maintained the relationship that kept the island in its proper arrangement. When it broke, it broke from the outside.

---

Columbus arrived at the north coast of Ayiti in December 1492. He had already been to Cuba. He had already taken several Taíno people aboard, intending to bring them to Spain as evidence of what he had found. The cacique Guacanagarí, whose territory covered the northern coast region of Marién, received Columbus with the ceremony that Taíno protocol required for powerful strangers: gifts, hospitality, the performances that incorporated the visitor into the existing network of relationships. Guacanagarí gave Columbus what the cacique gives, food, objects, access to the community's ceremony. He understood the exchange as the opening of a diplomatic relationship between his cacicazgo and this new group from somewhere far to the east. Columbus understood the exchange as confirmation that the people of the islands were generous and easily managed.

Columbus took several zemís back to Spain.

This is worth holding still for a moment. He picked up the carved wooden figures, the cotton figures, the stone faces, and he took them. To him they were curiosities, artifacts to show as evidence of where he had been. To the community he took them from, they were the ancestors, the presences that governed the local forces, the objects that the areíto was partly designed to maintain in proper relationship with the living. Taking them was not theft of decoration. Taking them was the removal of the nodes through which the living maintained contact with everything that governed the island's wellbeing.

The Santa María wrecked on the north coast of Hispaniola on Christmas Eve. Columbus built a fort from its timbers, garrisoned it with thirty-nine men, and sailed back to Spain. When he returned the following year, the fort at La Navidad was burned and the garrison was dead. The Spanish soldiers had begun taking Taíno women and demanding gold. The local community, under a cacique allied with Guacanagarí, had used force, which was not the system's preferred instrument but was the system's emergency option when the reciprocity was fundamentally violated. The garrison had violated the reciprocity completely and without acknowledgment, as if reciprocity were not the structure of reality but merely a custom that could be ignored when inconvenient.

The community had not been wrong. The force had been the correct response to that specific violation. But it had not been enough to address what was actually arriving, which was not a group of strangers who had violated a protocol but a system of expansion that did not recognize the protocol as binding. The Taíno political architecture was designed for managing relationships between parties who shared the basic premises: that reciprocity was required, that the zemís governed the forces, that the ceremonies maintained the conditions necessary for life. It was not designed for a party that refused those premises entirely.

This was the stress that the classic system had not been built to hold.

---

Return to the bohío. Return to Anacaona.

The phrase has found its landing. She stands up from the duho. She moves through it once more in the gathering dark. It is ready.

She will teach it tomorrow morning, while the dew is still on the conuco leaves and the day is fresh enough for difficult work. The community will learn it in sections, the way all areíto sections are learned: the lead singer performs the phrase, the community mirrors it back, the lead singer refines it through the mirroring, the community absorbs the refinement, and after enough repetitions the phrase lives in the body of everyone present and is no longer the lead singer's property but the community's. This is how the living library works. This is why it cannot be seized: it has no location. It is everywhere the people are.

The people scattered. Many died in the epidemics that swept the island faster than anyone could understand, diseases for which the Taíno had no accumulated immunity, moving through the yucayeques before the Spanish soldiers even arrived at those communities. Many fled: to the mountains of Ayiti, to Cuba's eastern regions, to Borikén's interior, to the smaller islands beyond the Kalinago frontier where the Spanish ships did not immediately reach. The communities that fled carried what they could carry, which was the body's archive. The areítos that lived in the bodies of the people who fled continued to exist wherever those people went. The zemís that were carried, wrapped and hidden, continued to be consulted. The manioc knowledge went with the women. The batey knowledge went with the players. The living library moved.

Scholars in the twentieth century called the Taíno extinct. This was wrong in the way that matters most: it was wrong about what the Taíno were. The Taíno were not a fixed population in a fixed location. They were a network of people who shared a ceremonial complex, a language, a material culture, and a way of understanding the cosmos, and networks do not go extinct through the destruction of any single node. In 2018, scientists at Harvard and the Max Planck Institute analyzed a 1,000-year-old genome recovered from a cave in the Bahamas and compared it to the genomes of contemporary people across the Caribbean. The match was clear. The descendants of the people who greeted Columbus were alive. They were alive in the Dominican Republic and in Puerto Rico. They were alive across the islands that were supposed to have been emptied.

The areíto Anacaona composed in the late afternoon of Xaragua's last undisturbed year was performed. It was learned. Some of the people who learned it survived. Some of what survived arrived in the bodies of people who reached the mountains or the forests or the smaller islands or the mainland coast, and those people had children, and those children had children, and somewhere in the descent that follows, unmeasured, uncelebrated, continuous, the knowledge kept moving. Not all of it. Some was lost when the last fluent singers died without transmission, when the last cohoba ceremony was performed without a student present to receive it. But enough moved that the thread held.

She composed for a world that would outlast her. She did not know by how much.

The zemís are still listening.
