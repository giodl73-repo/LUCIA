## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — PANEL-2 Review of Hammurabi's Babylon Notes

**Focus:** Structural engine and causal logic.

**The engine is correctly identified.** Law as public theology, with the self-binding loop (the king publishes the standard by which he will be judged) as the mechanism. This is sound. The terminal condition (the code survives the dynasty as monument rather than instrument) is the right endpoint.

**What strengthens the plan:**
- The arc from Amorite newcomers adopting Babylonian theology (Section 2) to the code as unification instrument (Section 4) to the code as inherited curriculum (Section 6) traces a complete lifecycle of the engine.
- The Zimri-Lim figure is well chosen. The Mari archive provides the best available window into Old Babylonian diplomacy, and the letters between Hammurabi and Zimri-Lim show the political maneuvering that preceded the conquest.

**What concerns me:**
- The plan does not yet articulate how the Enuma Elish and the code relate causally. The Enuma Elish elevates Marduk to head of the divine assembly. The code begins with Marduk named to the headship of the peoples. These are parallel claims. The chapter should make explicit that Babylon's political theology and its legal theology are the same theology: the cosmos has an order, the order flows from the gods through the king to the law, and Marduk sits at the top because Babylon sits at the top. When Babylon falls, the theology does not; it simply finds a new vessel.
- Section 5 (Household of the God) risks becoming a catalog of daily life. The naditu priestesses and the scribal schools and the breweries are vivid, but they need to serve the engine. How does daily life in Babylon demonstrate the code's publicness? The naditu women's legal records show women navigating the very cases the code describes. Make this connection explicit in the writing.

**Recommendation:** The chapter is ready to write. The engine is sound, the arc is clear, the sources are strong.
