# CLEAN-E2 — Voice Keeper — Ashoka's Conquest and Turn

**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Baseline Voice (established in opening)

Register: edict-inscriptional. Declarative, weighted, carved. Sentences that land like chisel strikes. Short sentences at moments of force. Longer sentences for enumeration and accumulation (mirroring the edicts' own enumerative style). No parenthetical interruptions. Colons and periods rather than em-dashes. The prose carries the gravity of stone inscription without becoming archaic.

## Scan for Register Shifts

1. **Section I (The Edict):** Voice consistent. The edict text itself opens the chapter, then the narrator modulates into a register that maintains the stone gravity. No textbook drift. No modern commentary.

2. **Section II (The War):** Voice modulates appropriately. Shorter, more kinetic sentences for the army on the march ("The cavalry on the flanks. The infantry behind."). The army description is embodied without becoming a military-history register. The mandala theory passage is delivered through the Arthashastra's own terminology (ari, mitra), not through modern political science language. "Whether they believed this, the stone does not record" is a high point of the register. **No violations.**

3. **Section III (The Turn):** The three-explanations passage (lines 54-59) is the highest-risk moment for register shift. The explanations are presented in the narrator's voice, not in the edict's voice. However, the register remains consistent: declarative, analytical without being academic, and the narrator returns to the edict's own voice immediately after ("The edicts do not choose among these explanations"). The passage on RE 13's address to the border peoples (line 65) recovers the inscriptional register. **No violations.**

4. **Section IV (The Policy):** Enumerative register, mirroring the edicts' own style ("Non-violence toward living beings. Obedience to parents. Respect for teachers and elders."). This is a deliberate voice choice: the list is the edict's own form. "This is not a philosophical treatise. It is a king counting carcasses and reducing the count" (line 71) is strong and in register. "The dhamma had its own ledger. The ledger was stone" (line 81) is excellent. **No violations.**

5. **Section V (The Stone):** Material-descriptive register, appropriate for a section about physical objects in landscapes. The granite/laterite/sandstone variation, the mason adjusting to different stone, the carved elephant at Dhauli. The Lumbini passage ("The pillar recorded the act. The act was the policy. The policy was the man.") is rhythmically strong and in register. The dhammaghosa/bherighosa passage is a high point: the edict's own terminology becomes the section's culminating image. **No violations.**

6. **Section VI (What the Stone Holds):** The Prinsep passage shifts to 1837, which is a temporal-register risk. The handling is careful: "he could read the stone for the first time since the last mason had set down his chisel" connects the modern reader to the Maurya mason through the act of reading stone, maintaining the inscriptional register's governing metaphor. The landing returns to the edict's own present tense ("They say what they have always said") and closes in the stone voice. **No violations.**
