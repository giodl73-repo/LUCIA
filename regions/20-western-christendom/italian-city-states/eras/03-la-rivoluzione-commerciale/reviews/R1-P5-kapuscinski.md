# R1-P5 — Ryszard Kapuściński — Panel Review, Round 1

**Text:** Opening — La Rivoluzione Commerciale
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The parchment is small enough to fold twice and slip inside a boot. I read that first sentence and I was there.

This is a writer who knows that history lives in the body. The parchment is not described as "a financial instrument" — it is described as small, foldable, concealable. The writer knows what Berto knows: that a bill of exchange must be portable, because a merchant is always moving. Everything about the physical reality of medieval long-distance trade is implied in that one detail.

The sensory work in this opening is careful and varied. The Thames carrying timber and hides. The Arno with the dyers' runoff — "blue and red and the particular yellow-green of weld." London: grey, damp, iron-tasting inn water. Florence: the smell of wool and money. These are not decorative details. The yellow-green of weld dye tells you that the Florentines have solved the problem of fixing color in wool, which tells you about their technical sophistication, which tells you about the commercial value of their finished cloth, which tells you why the Frescobaldi are in London at all. The sensory detail is doing analytical work.

**What I want the chapter to ground:**

The chapter will need to take the reader into spaces that are invisible in most histories of this era. The interior of a Venetian Senate chamber. The floor of the Florentine market where currency exchange happened — the *banchieri* sitting at their benches, the actual wooden table, the actual piles of coin being sorted. The inside of a Genoese *fondaco* in Caffa or Trebizond — the smell of spices and damp wool and foreign bodies in a stone building at the end of the Black Sea.

Abstract concepts like "credit" and "trust" and "the commercial network" need physical bodies. The chapter should find at least three moments where a financial concept is given a body: a hand writing, a table covered with sorted coin, a ship's cargo being assessed at a quayside.

**The question I would push on:**

The chapter covers 150 years and three cities. That is a lot of geography and time. The risk is that the reader loses the physical thread — the specific place, the specific body. The notes must identify, for each major section, the specific physical space the reader will inhabit. Not "Venetian trade" but the Senate chamber, the Arsenal shed, the Rialto market. Not "Florentine banking" but the Bardi company bench on Via Porta Rossa, the ledger open on the table, the clerk's hand moving from column to column.
