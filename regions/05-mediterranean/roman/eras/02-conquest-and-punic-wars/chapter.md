# The Conquest of Italy and the Punic Wars

## I. Cannae

The consul Gaius Terentius Varro rode back to Roma after Cannae, and the Senate went out to meet him at the gates and thanked him for not having despaired of the res publica.

On an August day in 216 BCE, on the flat ground near the river Aufidus in Apulia, Hannibal Barca had destroyed the largest army the Republic had ever assembled. Polybius, who wrote the account a generation later and who had access to both Roman and Carthaginian sources, puts the dead at seventy thousand. The number may be inflated, but the scale is not in dispute. The consul Lucius Aemilius Paullus was dead. Eighty senators were dead on the field. Equestrians, centurions, the men who formed the backbone of the Republic's military and political classes, lay in the August heat, and the birds came, and the ground, Polybius says, could not absorb them all. The survivors broke south and east. Some did not stop until they reached the sea.

In Roma, the Senate sat and refused to discuss terms. Hannibal sent an envoy to negotiate the ransom of prisoners, and the Senate sent him back with the message that the Republic did not ransom captured soldiers. The reasoning was not cruelty. It was calculation: a state that ransoms its captured teaches its soldiers that surrender is survivable. The Romani could not afford that lesson. The city mobilized boys who had not yet reached military age. It armed slaves, promising freedom in exchange for service. It borrowed from the temple treasuries. It stripped the matrons of their gold jewelry to coin money for the war effort. And when Varro returned, the man who had commanded on the day of the worst defeat in Roman history, the Senate went to the gates and thanked him. Because he had come back. Because the res publica needed its consul.

The thanking of Varro was not Roman virtue. It was political engineering. The Republic's machinery did not depend on any single commander's survival. The Senate's collective authority meant that a decision-making body endured even when individual senators died by the dozens. The census-based army meant that Roma could lose seventy thousand men and raise another army, because the manpower pool was not Roma alone but the entire Italian alliance system. The allies, the socii, bound to Roma by treaties that required them to furnish soldiers on Roman demand, gave the Republic a reserve of fighting men that no other Mediterranean power could match. When Cannae destroyed one army, the system built another. When one consul fell, the system produced another. The res publica was a machine for absorbing catastrophe, and Cannae proved it.

## II. The Italian Web

The machine had been three centuries in the making.

After the expulsion of the kings, Roma's reach barely extended to the hills it could see. The Latin League, a confederation of communities in the plain of Latium sharing a common language and common festivals, had included Roma as one member among many. But Roma was the largest, the best-situated on the Tiber crossing, and the most aggressive in pressing its claims. By the mid-fourth century BCE, the relationship between Roma and the other Latin communities had shifted from alliance among equals to domination by one. The Latin War of 340-338 BCE ended the League and replaced it with a system the Romani designed specifically to prevent collective resistance: each Latin community was bound to Roma separately, with its own treaty, its own terms, its own relationship to the center. The Latins could not confederate against Roma because their treaties were with Roma, not with each other.

The terms varied. Some communities received full Roman citizenship, their people becoming Romani in law. Others received the ius Latinum, a lesser status that granted commercial and marital rights with Roma but not the vote. Others were bound as allies, socii, who governed themselves internally but followed Roma in war, providing soldiers under Roman command. The gradations were deliberate. A community that served well might be elevated. A community that resisted might be punished. The ladder of status was itself a tool of governance: it gave the conquered something to aspire to, which was more effective than garrison duty for keeping the peace.

The system's logic was simple. Each new ally added soldiers to the Roman pool. The soldiers were used in the next war, which produced more allies, which added more soldiers. The cycle was self-reinforcing and, once begun, difficult to stop. The Samnites, the mountain people of the central Apennines, fought Roma in three wars spanning fifty years (343-290 BCE) across terrain so rugged that the legions had to learn a new way of fighting. The manipular formation replaced the rigid phalanx: instead of one deep block, the legion divided into smaller units, maniples of one hundred twenty men, each capable of operating independently on broken ground. The mountain warfare forced the adaptation, and the adaptation became permanent. By the time the Samnites were absorbed into the alliance system, the Roman legion had become the most flexible infantry formation in the Mediterranean.

Pyrrhus of Epirus tested the system in 280 BCE. He brought twenty-five thousand men and twenty elephants across the Adriatic at the invitation of the Greek cities of southern Italy, who wanted protection from Roman expansion. He won at Heraclea and at Ausculum, but the victories cost him so heavily that his name became the term for a success that destroys its winner. After Ausculum he said, or was reported to have said, "One more victory like this and I am lost." He left Italy in 275 BCE. The Romani absorbed the Greek cities of the south, adding them to the alliance system. The system now stretched from the Po valley in the north to the heel and toe of the peninsula.

What held it together was not love. It was the alliance system's dual promise: protection and participation. The allies fought under Roman command, but they fought. They were not subjects. They governed their own communities and maintained their own customs and spoke their own languages. A Samnite or an Etruscan or a Greek serving in the allied contingent was not a Roman, but he was part of the Roman military machine, and his community's security depended on the machine's success. The system created a community of interest. The community of interest created the reserve depth that survived Cannae.

## III. The Sea

In 264 BCE, Roma went to war with Carthago, and the war was about Sicily, and Sicily was about the sea.

Carthago was a Phoenician foundation on the coast of North Africa, a trading city whose wealth came from the sea routes that connected the western Mediterranean to the Levant and beyond. Its merchants controlled the tin and silver trades from Iberia, the grain of the North African coast, the coral and dye-works of the African littoral. Its navy was the most powerful in the western Mediterranean. Its citizens did not, for the most part, fight their own wars: Carthago hired mercenaries, professional soldiers drawn from Libya, Numidia, Iberia, the Balearic Islands, paid with the wealth the trade produced. The Carthaginian general Hamilcar and his successors, the Barcids, would make war a family enterprise, funded by the state and led by men whose loyalty was to the city whose harbors had made them.

Roma had no navy. The Romans had no experience of the sea, no shipbuilding tradition, no knowledge of naval tactics. The tradition says they captured a Carthaginian quinquereme that had run aground and copied it, training their crews to row on benches set up on land before launching the fleet. The tradition is probably exaggerated, but the underlying point is not: Roma built a navy from almost nothing and went to war with the greatest naval power in the western Mediterranean. They did it because the census-based system could mobilize the manpower, because the alliance system provided coastal communities with maritime experience, and because the Romans invented the corvus, the boarding bridge that turned a naval engagement into an infantry fight by allowing legionaries to board enemy vessels. The corvus was crude. It made the ships top-heavy and unseaworthy in rough weather. But it worked, because it transformed the battle from a test of seamanship, which the Carthaginians would win, into a test of close-quarters fighting, which the Romans would win.

The First Punic War lasted twenty-three years (264-241 BCE). It was fought primarily around Sicily and the waters surrounding it. The Romani lost fleet after fleet to storms that their heavy corvus-equipped ships could not survive, rebuilt after each loss, and kept coming. The casualties at sea were staggering. In 255 BCE, a storm after the Battle of Cape Ecnomus destroyed nearly the entire Roman fleet: hundreds of ships, tens of thousands of men. The Romani built another fleet. The system absorbed the loss and continued. By 241, Carthago was exhausted. The peace ceded Sicily to Roma, and Sicily became the first Roman provincia, a territory governed by a Roman magistrate rather than incorporated as an ally.

The provincia was a new category. It was not the alliance system. It was extraction: a Roman governor, a Roman garrison, Roman taxes. The governor held imperium in the province, unchecked by colleagues or tribunes, accountable only to the Senate on his return. The distance between Roma and the province, the weeks of travel, the absence of daily oversight, created a space where the governor's power was effectively absolute for the duration of his term. The provincia was the Republic's first experiment in ruling territory that was not Italian and not allied, and the experiment revealed what unchecked imperium could become when the tribune's veto could not reach it.

## IV. Hannibal

In 218 BCE, Hannibal Barca crossed the Alps with an army and brought the war to Italy.

He was twenty-nine years old, the son of Hamilcar Barca who had fought the first war and the brother-in-law of Hasdrubal who had built the Carthaginian position in Iberia. The ancient sources say his father made him swear as a boy, before the altar of Baal Hammon in Carthago, that he would never be a friend to Roma. Whether the oath was taken as described, Hannibal's career fulfilled it. He had grown up in Iberia among soldiers, learning war in the field the way a Roman senator's son learned law in the Forum, and by the time he took command of the Carthaginian army in Iberia, he was already the most dangerous military mind in the Mediterranean.

The Alpine crossing was not strategically necessary. Hannibal could have invaded by sea. But the crossing accomplished something no sea voyage could: it demonstrated that Roma was reachable by land, that the barrier of the Alps was not absolute, that an army with sufficient will could appear in the Po valley without warning. The crossing cost him men and animals. The elephants, which had terrified the Gauls and would terrify the Romans at Trebia, died in the mountains or shortly after. The army that emerged into the Po plain was smaller than the one that had entered the Pyrenees. But it was there, and it was led by Hannibal, and Roma had no army in the north to stop it.

Trebia, December 218. Hannibal drew the Roman consul Tiberius Sempronius Longus into a battle on ground Hannibal had chosen, in winter cold, across a river, and destroyed the Roman army with a concealed cavalry force that struck from behind. Trasimene, June 217. Hannibal ambushed the consul Gaius Flaminius on the shore of Lake Trasimene in Etruria, killing fifteen thousand and capturing ten thousand in the worst Roman ambush loss to date. Then Cannae.

The Romani responded to the first defeats by trying what had always worked: larger armies, more citizens, more allies. At Cannae, they fielded perhaps eighty thousand men, the largest force the Republic had ever assembled, and put both consuls in command. Hannibal had roughly forty thousand. The terrain was flat, open, suited to the Roman infantry's strength. Everything favored Roma except the mind directing the other army.

Hannibal set his line with the weaker Gallic and Iberian infantry in the center and his strongest African troops on the flanks. The Roman infantry advanced into the center and pushed it back, as Hannibal intended, forming a deepening pocket. The African flanks closed like doors. The Numidian cavalry, the finest light horsemen in the Mediterranean, swept around the rear and sealed the encirclement. The Roman army, compressed into a mass too dense to fight effectively, was destroyed in place. The killing lasted hours.

The Senate thanked Varro. And then the Senate made a decision that reveals the Republic's structural character more clearly than any victory. It appointed Fabius Maximus as dictator and adopted a strategy of not fighting. Fabius shadowed Hannibal's army through Italy, refusing battle, cutting off foragers, harassing stragglers, and waiting. The Romani called him Cunctator, the Delayer, and some meant it as an insult. But the strategy was sound. Hannibal could win battles, but he could not win the war unless he broke the alliance system, and the alliance system held. Most of Roma's Italian allies remained loyal, not from affection but from calculation: Roma had the reserve depth to recover, and any community that defected to Hannibal would face Roman vengeance when Hannibal eventually left. Which he would, because he could not be resupplied from Carthago while Roma controlled the sea.

## V. Zama and After

Publius Cornelius Scipio was thirty years old when the Senate gave him command in Iberia, and he had never held the consulship, and the appointment violated every principle of the cursus honorum.

He earned it by winning. In Iberia, he destroyed the Carthaginian position in four years, learning Hannibal's tactics and improving them, combining Roman infantry discipline with the cavalry mobility that had broken the legions at Cannae. He returned to Roma, won the consulship in 205, and proposed the strategy that ended the war: take the fight to Africa. Force Carthago to recall Hannibal. Fabius Maximus opposed the plan in the Senate. The debate was a collision between the old Republic's caution and the new Republic's ambition. Scipio won the debate. He sailed for Africa in 204.

At Zama, in 202 BCE, Scipio and Hannibal met on the field. Hannibal had his elephants. Scipio had studied what elephants do and arranged his maniples with gaps between them, channels through which the elephants charged harmlessly through the Roman line. The Roman cavalry, trained and augmented by Numidian allies, swept the Carthaginian cavalry from the field and returned to strike Hannibal's infantry from behind. The encirclement at Cannae was reversed. Hannibal lost.

The peace that followed made Roma the dominant power in the western Mediterranean. Carthago surrendered its fleet, its overseas territories, and its independence in foreign policy. The Romani had discovered what their system could do. The alliance system had survived the worst military defeat in its history and produced the men, the ships, the money, and the strategic patience to win a sixteen-year war against the most gifted commander of the age. The machine worked.

The working changed it. Scipio returned to Roma with a new cognomen, Africanus, the first Roman to carry a name earned in war, and the name was a problem. The Republic's system depended on no man rising above the collective. Scipio's fame, his wealth from the Iberian and African campaigns, his personal following among soldiers who had served under him, made him larger than any senator was supposed to be. The Senate suspected him of seeking what the Republic was built to prevent. The suspicion was the system's immune response. Scipio retired to his estate at Liternum and asked that his tomb not be in Roma, a gesture the Romani remembered and debated for generations.

The next half-century decided the shape of the Mediterranean. Roma defeated Macedon in 197 BCE and broke the Hellenistic kingdoms' power in Greece. It defeated the Seleucid king Antiochus III at Magnesia in 190. It destroyed Corinth in 146 BCE, the same year it destroyed Carthago. The destruction of Carthago was deliberate. The Elder Cato, the censor who had visited the city and returned with fresh Carthaginian figs to show the Senate how close and how prosperous the enemy remained, ended every speech in the Senate with the same words regardless of the topic: Carthago delenda est. Carthage must be destroyed. In the Third Punic War (149-146), the Romani besieged the city for three years and then leveled it, sold the population into slavery, and, the tradition says, plowed salt into the earth.

Whether the salting happened is debated. What is not debated is the completeness of the destruction. Carthago, which had been the wealthiest city in the western Mediterranean, a city whose harbor could hold two hundred warships and whose houses were six stories tall, was rubble. The site would be refounded as a Roman colony a century later, because the location was too good to leave empty, but the Carthago that had challenged Roma for the western sea was gone.

## VI. The Cost

The Mare Nostrum was Roman.

From the Pillars of Hercules at the western end of the Mediterranean to the coast of Syria in the east, the sea lanes that carried the grain and the wine and the oil and the enslaved were now under the authority of the Senate and the generals the Senate sent out with imperium to govern what the armies had won. The wealth that flowed back to Roma was unprecedented. The spoils of Corinth alone filled Roman houses with Greek sculpture, Greek silver, Greek paintings that the soldiers did not always know how to value. A consul named Mummius shipped crated artworks back to Roma with the instruction that the shippers would be required to replace any piece they damaged, which suggests he thought the works could be replaced, which suggests the scale of what was being moved.

The wealth changed the city. Senators who had governed a city of farmers now governed provinces that produced revenue beyond anything the Italian land could generate. The domus, the Roman house, grew from a modest structure around an atrium to a complex of rooms furnished with the goods of the conquered east: Greek couches, Anatolian carpets, Syrian glass, Egyptian linen. The old Roman diet of puls, the grain porridge, and vegetables and rough wine gave way, in the houses of the wealthy, to feasts that featured foods from every corner of the Mediterranean. The garum, still fermenting in its clay vats, was now joined by Falernian wine from Campania, oysters from the Lucrine lake, dormice fattened in ceramic jars.

The enslaved changed the economy. The wars of conquest had produced hundreds of thousands of captives, sold at auction in markets like Delos, which processed, the sources suggest, ten thousand enslaved people in a single day. They worked the land that the wealthy were buying in the countryside as small farmers sold out or were dispossessed. They worked the mines in Iberia and Sardinia. They worked the workshops and the households of the Roman elite. They carried the education of Greece into Roman houses as tutors, read Greek literature aloud to patrons who were learning to appreciate it, and managed the accounts and correspondence of men who were too busy governing to handle their own affairs.

The small farmers who had been the Republic's citizen-soldiers were the system's casualties. The men who had fought the Punic Wars returned to find their land untended, their families impoverished, and the market undercut by the slave-worked estates that the wealthy had assembled during their absence. Some sold their farms and moved to Roma, joining the urban poor. Some held on. But the trend was clear: the Republic that had been built on the small farmer-soldier who owned his land and fought for it because he owned it was becoming an empire where the land belonged to the rich, the fighting was done by the landless, and the gap between the two was widening with every generation.

This was not a moral decline. It was a structural transformation. The machinery that had governed a city of farmer-soldiers was now governing an empire of provinces and trade routes and enslaved labor, and the machinery was not designed for this. The annual magistracy, which worked when the consul's job was to lead an army for a campaign season and return home for the harvest, worked less well when the consul's job was to govern a province a month's sail from Roma. The Senate, which worked when its members were men of similar wealth and similar interests governing a community they could walk across in an afternoon, worked less well when its members included men whose provincial commands had made them richer than the treasury and more powerful than any magistrate in the city.

The geese on the Capitoline were still fed. The augures still watched the sky. The consuls still held office for a single year. The tribune still stood in the Forum and said veto when the machinery needed stopping. The mos maiorum held. But the republic it described was becoming something the mos maiorum had not anticipated, and the machinery that had survived Cannae now faced a test the machinery was not built for: the test of its own success.
