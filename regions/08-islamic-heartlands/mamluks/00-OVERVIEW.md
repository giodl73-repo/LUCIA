# Al-Mamālīk — The Soldiers Who Became Sultans

We were bought as boys and made into the finest soldiers in the world. *Al-Mamālīk* — "the owned" — is what we were called, and the name did not embarrass us. The *mamluk* system was not slavery in the sense of agricultural bondage. It was the most effective military training institution the medieval world produced: young men, purchased from the Cuman steppe or the Caucasus or the Eurasian interior, brought to Egypt or Syria, converted to Islam, trained from childhood in the arts of war — horsemanship, archery, the lance, the sword — and graduated as members of the sultan's household guard. Our loyalty was to the institution and to the corps, not to any family or clan of origin. This made us dangerous to our masters and indispensable to the state.

We stopped the Mongols. Twice. At Ain Jalut in 1260 CE, the Mamluk general Baybars defeated the Mongol army that had sacked Baghdad and destroyed the Abbasid caliphate — the first significant Mongol defeat in open battle in thirty years. At Marj al-Saffar in 1303 CE, we defeated them again. We did this by building the military system the Ayyubids had assembled into something more coherent: a professional standing army of trained horsemen, a bureaucratic administration that paid and fed and equipped them reliably, and a strategic posture that traded space for time until we could concentrate enough force to fight on ground of our choosing. We held Egypt and Syria for two and a half centuries, and when the Ottomans finally took Cairo in 1517 CE, they found an institution so embedded in Egyptian governance that they kept the Mamluk structure functioning under Ottoman supervision.

## What Defined Them

**The *faris* — the horseman as the military ideal.** The Mamluk military ideal was the *faris* — the horseman, the cavalryman whose skill with the composite bow on horseback was the pinnacle of military art. The *furusiyya* manuals — treatises on horsemanship, archery, lance work, and the care of horses — were the Mamluk curriculum, as important to their culture as the Quran was to their faith. The training regime was lifelong: *mamluks* who had risen to be senior officers continued to practice the exercises of their youth. The Mamluk cavalry was the finest light cavalry of the medieval world, and the institution maintained that standard through systematic, lifelong training rather than through natural selection or individual heroism.

**The self-perpetuating caste.** The Mamluk system was designed to prevent the development of a hereditary military aristocracy: the sons of *mamluks* could not themselves become *mamluks*, ensuring that each generation of the corps was composed of men who had come up through the system from the bottom. This design worked imperfectly — the sons of senior *mamluks* occupied a privileged but ambiguous position in Mamluk society — but it prevented the development of the kind of feudal nobility that had undermined other medieval military systems. The caste perpetuated itself through the continuous purchase and training of new boys, not through inheritance.

**The Abbasid shadow caliphate in Cairo.** After the Mongols killed the Abbasid caliph in Baghdad in 1258, the Mamluks installed a member of the Abbasid family in Cairo as a shadow caliph — a figure whose function was to provide Islamic legitimacy for the sultanate rather than to exercise political power. The Cairo caliphs issued documents confirming the sultans' authority, participated in state ceremonies, and provided the institutional link to the community's founding tradition. This arrangement — the caliph as symbol, the sultan as power — was the Mamluk solution to the problem of Islamic political legitimacy, and it continued until the Ottoman sultan Selim I took Cairo in 1517 and reportedly assumed the caliphal title himself.

**The spice trade and the Portuguese disruption.** The Mamluk sultanate's wealth rested significantly on its control of the Red Sea spice trade — the route by which spices from India and Southeast Asia reached Europe through Egypt and the Levantine ports. The Mamluk customs revenues from Alexandria and the Red Sea ports were among the most valuable in the medieval world. When the Portuguese navigator Vasco da Gama rounded the Cape of Good Hope in 1498 and opened a direct sea route from Europe to India, bypassing the Mamluk-controlled land route, the economic disruption to the sultanate was severe. The Portuguese sea route threatened to make the Egyptian entrepot economically marginal. The Mamluks responded by building a fleet and fighting the Portuguese in the Indian Ocean — a conflict they ultimately lost because they lacked the heavy cannon that the Portuguese ships carried.

## The Chapters

**1 — Ain Jalut:** Baybars watches the Mongol cavalry form on the plain of Ain Jalut. They have destroyed every army they have faced for thirty years. The Mamluk force is smaller. Baybars uses a feigned retreat to pull the Mongols into a prepared killing ground and then hits them from three sides. The Mongols break. The news reaches every Islamic city simultaneously: the Mongols can be defeated. The Mamluk sultanate has defined its historical role.

**2 — The Crusaders Expelled:** The siege of Acre is the Mamluks' last operation against the Crusader states. The city falls in May 1291. The Crusaders who survive board the last ships in the harbor. The Mamluk military machine that Baybars built has completed its primary strategic objective: the Levantine coast is cleared. The two-century Crusader presence is over.

**3 — The Portuguese and the Ottoman Horizon:** The Mamluk sultan receives the report from his agents in the Indian Ocean: Portuguese ships are raiding the spice fleets. The fleet he built to counter them has been defeated at Diu. In Anatolia, the Ottoman sultan Selim I is building his army for an eastern campaign. The Mamluk sultanate, which has governed Egypt for two and a half centuries, is facing pressures from two directions that it has never faced simultaneously. Selim's army crosses into Syria in 1516. Within a year, Cairo will be Ottoman.
