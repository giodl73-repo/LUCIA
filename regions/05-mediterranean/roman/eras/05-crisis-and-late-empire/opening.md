# The Crisis and the Late Empire

The emperor Diocletian stood in the palace at Nicomedia in the year 1057 of Roma's founding (303 CE) and decided that the empire was too large for one man to hold.

This was not a philosophical observation. It was an operational assessment from a man who had spent twenty years holding the empire together by force, intelligence, and a willingness to reorganize what could not be saved in its existing form. Diocletian had risen from nothing. He was born in Dalmatia, on the eastern shore of the Adriatic, the son of an enslaved person or a freedman, the sources differ, and he had climbed through the army to the purple through a century that had tested every assumption the principate held.

The century before Diocletian had been the worst since the civil wars of the late Republic. Between the assassination of Commodus in 192 CE and Diocletian's accession in 284, the empire had seen more than fifty claimants to the throne, most of whom died by violence. The frontiers had broken in the 250s and 260s: the Persians under Shapur I captured the emperor Valerian in 260, an event without precedent, a Roman emperor in enemy hands, and the Palmyrene queen Zenobia had carved an independent domain out of the eastern provinces while Germanic peoples crossed the Rhine and Danube simultaneously. The plague that arrived in the 250s, probably smallpox, killed millions across the empire. The coinage was debased until the silver denarius contained almost no silver. The tax system collapsed in regions where the population had fallen or fled. The army, which chose and unmade emperors with increasing frequency, had become the state's most dangerous institution: the legions on the Rhine elected one man, the legions on the Danube another, and the civil wars between them consumed the resources that should have defended the frontier.

Diocletian's assessment was accurate. The empire could not be governed as Augustus had governed it. The principate's fiction, that one man held the powers of the Republic's magistracies, had been functional when the one man could maintain the fiction's performance: the Senate's dignity, the emperor's restraint, the army's loyalty. The third-century crisis had stripped the fiction to its wires. The Senate was irrelevant. The emperor's restraint was a luxury no one fighting for survival could afford. The army's loyalty belonged to the commander who paid it, and the payment came from the taxes, and the taxes came from the population, and the population was shrinking.

Diocletian did not restore. He rebuilt. He divided the empire into an eastern and western half, each ruled by an Augustus with a Caesar as junior colleague and designated successor. The Tetrarchy, the rule of four, was designed to solve the succession problem by making succession automatic: the Augustus retired, the Caesar stepped up, a new Caesar was appointed. The system was elegant. It worked for exactly one generation. After Diocletian's voluntary abdication in 305, the first in Roman history since Sulla, the system collapsed into civil war, because the men who held military power would not accept a succession that did not reward their armies, and the principle that had chosen emperors since the Year of the Four Emperors was not constitutional design but armed force.

From the wreckage, a single man emerged. Constantine. And Constantine brought with him a decision that would reshape the Roman world more completely than any military victory or constitutional settlement: the decision to favor the God of the Christians.
