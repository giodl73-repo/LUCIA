## The Editorial Frame (LUCIA)

This project tells cultures' stories from inside their own worldview, not from outside. The following principles guide all work:

1. **Present from within** — The text is written from the perspective of people living through the experience, not from a scholar observing
2. **No historical judgment** — The text avoids verdicts ("this was wrong," "this was inevitable," "this was justified"). It shows what happened and lets the reader form their own understanding
3. **Story-first** — The narrative leads through real people and real moments, not through historical exposition
4. **The Achebe Test** — Could a thoughtful member of this culture read this and feel their story was told with dignity — from inside — regardless of whether they agreed with every detail?

## The Specific Questions for the Board

### 1. Historical Accuracy & Chinese Experience

**For a China historian / sinologist:**

- Does this chapter accurately represent the Chinese experience of 1949–1957?
- Are the major events (Tiananmen, land reform, Korean War, Five-Year Plan, Hundred Flowers) characterized accurately?
- Are there significant omissions or mischaracterizations?
- Does the chapter honor the genuineness of the "standing up" — that it was real to millions of people — while also acknowledging the systemic problems that were being built?

**For a specialist in this period:**

- Is the portrayal of performance legitimacy (the Party's claim to rule based on ending humiliation and delivering prosperity) accurate?
- Does the chapter correctly understand the quota system and how it generated false reporting?
- Is the Hundred Flowers campaign and its reversal portrayed accurately?

### 2. Authenticity & Voice

**For an anthropologist or cultural specialist:**

- Does the voice sound like it comes from inside Chinese understanding of this period, or does it sound like an outside observer?
- Are the figures (Wang Mingyu the peasant, Peng Jian the soldier, Liu Shu the factory worker, Lin Mumin the intellectual) portrayed in ways that would be recognizable to someone who lived through this period?
- Does the chapter respect the people's own understanding of their experience, or does it impose an outside framework?

### 3. The Structural Engine

**For a historian of revolutions / systems thinker:**

- The chapter argues that the quota system (used in land reform) became the template for all later campaigns, including the Great Leap Forward. Is this causal chain plausible and well-rendered?
- Does the chapter show how "performance legitimacy" created systemic pressure to falsify information? Does this mechanism seem historically sound?
- Is there any indication that this interpretation contradicts what mainstream scholars would argue about the period?

### 4. Strengths & Weaknesses

**Open questions:**

- What are the chapter's greatest strengths?
- What are its greatest weaknesses?
- Where should the author know more before proceeding to Era 2 (The Great Leap Forward)?
- Are there concepts or framings that would benefit from deeper research?

## Author's Understanding of the Period

The author has done the following research:

**Primary sources:**
- Mao Zedong's selected works (official texts)
- Xinhua News Agency archives
- CCP party congress resolutions
- Some declassified US State Department / CIA documents on the period

**Secondary sources:**
- Frank Dikötter, *Mao's Great Famine* (2010) — detailed account of the Great Leap Forward famine
- Frank Dikötter, *The Cultural Revolution* (2016)
- Yang Jisheng, *Tombstone* (2012) — Chinese journalist's account of famine
- Ezra Vogel, *Deng Xiaoping and the Transformation of China* (2011)
- William Hinton, *Fanshen* (1966) — ethnographic account of land reform in Shanxi Province
- Perry Link — multiple works on Chinese intellectual life and censorship
- Ian Johnson, *The Souls of China* (2017)
- Elizabeth Economy, *The Third Revolution* (2018)

**Research gaps the author acknowledges:**
- Limited access to ordinary people's diaries or oral histories from 1949–1957 (most records were destroyed or remain inaccessible)
- Limited primary documentation of how quotas were actually implemented at village level
- No direct engagement with Chinese-language primary sources (author reads Mandarin at intermediate level)
- Limited access to PRC State Archives (access is restricted; some documents were available through Hong Kong before 2020)
