## P-4 — Wade Davis — Review of Meroe Era 3 Opening

**Overall:** The opening treats Meroe as what it was: a state of genuine depth and sophistication, an African kingdom operating on equal terms with Rome. The intellectual parity is maintained throughout. I find no hidden hierarchy, no subtle ranking. What I want more of is the knowledge system itself.

**What works:**
- The Meroitic script is mentioned not as a curiosity but as a functional system: "twenty-three signs adapted from Egyptian forms, which scribes used for royal inscriptions, temple records, and funerary texts." This treats literacy as practice, not as a marker on a civilizational checklist.
- Apedemak is introduced as a deity with "no Egyptian equivalent," which correctly positions Meroitic religion as its own intellectual achievement rather than a derivative of Egyptian theology. The opening does not measure Meroitic gods against Egyptian ones. It states their independence.
- The iron industry is positioned as "a different kind of power" from the gold-based wealth of Napata. This is structural thinking about knowledge systems: the distinction between extraction (gold) and production (iron) represents a different relationship to the material world.

**What doesn't:**
- The Meroitic script is mentioned but its intellectual significance is underexplored. This is not merely a writing system; it is an independent invention of alphabetic writing adapted from a logographic system. The cognitive leap from hieroglyphic to alphabetic within the same cultural tradition is remarkable. The chapter should engage with this as an intellectual achievement, not just as a functional fact.
- Iron smelting at Meroe is described in terms of output (slag heaps, tools, weapons, trade) but not in terms of knowledge. What did the smelters know? How was the knowledge transmitted? The furnace technology at Meroe is distinctive. The chapter should treat this as a knowledge system, not just as an economic activity.
- The elephant culture at Musawwarat es-Sufra is absent from the opening. The Great Enclosure, the largest ancient structure in sub-Saharan Africa, and the elephant domestication/training that may have occurred there, represents a knowledge system (animal management, architectural engineering, ritual practice) that deserves attention.

**The question I'd push on:**
The chapter is positioned as a story of military-diplomatic encounter with Rome. But beneath that encounter lies a question about knowledge systems: how does a state that produces iron, writes in its own script, builds its own temples, and trains elephants perceive an encounter with an empire that believes itself to be the only civilization in the room? The chapter should let us see Rome through Meroitic knowledge, not just through Meroitic politics.
