# BOARD-3 — James Sweet

**Domain:** African religious and cultural practices in the Portuguese Atlantic world; Candomblé and Afro-Brazilian religion; African diaspora
**Key work:** *Recreating Africa: Culture, Kinship, and Religion in the African-Portuguese World* (2003); *Domingo Álvares, African Healing, and the Intellectual History of the Atlantic World* (2011)
**Chapter reviewed:** Brazil, Era 2 — A Colônia — Açúcar, Ouro, e Escravos

---

## Factual Accuracy Assessment

**The axé and initiatory transmission:** The chapter's description of axé as sacred force and its transmission through initiation is accurate for the Yoruba/Nagô tradition as it survived in Brazil. The specific mechanics described — the babalorixá knowing "which deity claimed which person at birth," the initiatory period of reclusion, learning the songs in Yoruba — are consistent with the ethnographic record and the historical reconstruction of Candomblé's origins.

**Orixá-saint correspondences:** Xangô/São Jerônimo; Iemanjá/Nossa Senhora; Ogum/São Jorge. These are the standard correspondences in the Nagô Candomblé tradition. The chapter presents these as camouflage rather than genuine syncretism, which is one position in the scholarly debate. My own work suggests the picture is more complex — there are dimensions of the orixá-saint correspondence that are genuine theological synthesis, not only protective camouflage. However, the chapter's position is defensible and is consistent with the dominant scholarly interpretation. This is a scholarly debate, not a factual error.

**The Engenho Velho terreiro founders:** Iyá Detá, Iyá Kalá, Iyá Nassô are the three names consistently given in the scholarship on the Casa Branca (Engenho Velho) terreiro. The founding is dated "in the 1830s," which is within the range of scholarly opinion (some sources say 1830, others say the tradition extends to the early nineteenth century). Acceptable.

**"African religious traditions in the diaspora" — the transformation issue:** The chapter says "Candomblé in Bahia is not the religion of Lagos. It is something new, something that happened in Brazil, under specific conditions of suppression and synthesis. The opening is careful not to claim perfect continuity; it says the axé arrived 'intact,' which is a defensible claim about the core transmission, not about the forms." This is exactly right, and I am glad the chapter holds this distinction. Many popular accounts either overclaim continuity or ignore it entirely. This chapter navigates correctly.

## Cultural Representation Assessment

The composite woman figure in figures.md is where I want to comment most carefully. The chapter gives her Xangô's knowledge — the batá rhythms, the colors, the offerings. This is specific and historically grounded. But the chapter should be more explicit in figures.md about the distinction between Bantu and Yoruba initiatory traditions. The woman is from the Mina coast (Yoruba/Fon background), carrying Xangô (Yoruba). The Bantu peoples who made up much of Palmares's population had different religious traditions — the Bantu minkisi system, for example, is distinct from the Yoruba orixá system. The chapter correctly holds these as separate in the narrative; the figures.md composite woman should explicitly note that she is drawn from the Nagô/Yoruba tradition, not from the Bantu traditions that were also present.

This is a minor clarification, not a factual error.

## Corrections Required

**One correction (confirming Board-1's finding):** The Movimento Negro Unificado established the Dia da Consciência Negra commemoration in 1978, but the chapter as written implies this became a national holiday in 1978. The national federal holiday designation came later. The sentence should read "established the commemoration of November 20 as the Dia da Consciência Negra in 1978; the date was recognized as a national holiday in 2003."

**One minor addition for figures.md:** Explicitly note that the composite quilombola woman is drawn from the Nagô/Yoruba tradition and that Bantu traditions (also present in Palmares) are distinct and carried by other figures in the community.

## Verdict

**Approved for final with the two minor additions noted.** The chapter is the most historically and theologically careful treatment of the axé transmission I have seen in a narrative history format. The distinction between the continuity of the core transmission and the transformation of its forms is handled with scholarly precision while remaining fully inside the tradition's own worldview. This is what this kind of history should do.
