# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman — Panel Review, Round 1

**Text reviewed:** opening.md — The Lueji-Chibinda Foundation
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

I read this twice. The second time with a pencil, which I rarely need for an opening that works.

What works: the opening image. Lueji with the bracelet in both hands — that is a scene, not a survey. The writing earns the moment by establishing the material object before it establishes the political meaning, and I appreciated the restraint. Too many historical openings would have told me immediately what the lukanu was and what it meant. This one trusts me to want to know.

The prose rhythm is right. The short declarative sentences at key moments — "She ruled." "Then the hunter arrived." "The kingdom began." — carry weight without announcing it. This is the technique of someone who knows when to let a full stop do the work.

What I want pushed further: Chibinda Ilunga.

He arrives in paragraph five and he remains a description until the end. "Skilled with the bow in the way that some men are skilled." That is promising, but then he is summarized — his lineage, his intention, his skill — without being placed. Where exactly does Lueji see him? Does he come into a village, into her presence, into a clearing? What does he look like standing there?

The scene of recognition and decision is the beating heart of this story — a woman who holds a kingdom looking at a man who carries a different kind of authority, and deciding what those two things together could make. That scene is told, not shown. "She saw in him something that the sources call capability, or worthiness, or the right kind of strength." That sentence is honest about the tradition's indirectness, and I respect the honesty — but a reader needs at least one moment of scene before the summary.

I am not asking for romance. I am asking for a scene. Where are they standing? What does the meeting look like?

The closing two lines are correct. Do not touch them.

**The question I'd push on:** Can you stage the encounter between Lueji and Chibinda Ilunga as a scene — even two or three sentences — before moving into the summary of what the tradition says about it? The tradition's indirectness can be honored, but the reader needs to be in the room.
