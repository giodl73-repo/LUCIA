# Pagan — The Thousand Temples

We are Pagan — Bagan — and we covered the plain of the Irrawaddy with monuments. Over eleven thousand religious structures were built in the Pagan kingdom between the ninth and thirteenth centuries; approximately three thousand survive today, rising from the flat red-dust plain in every direction as far as the eye can carry. This is not exaggeration or metaphor. The plain of Bagan, at the bend of the Irrawaddy River, holds the greatest concentration of Buddhist monuments anywhere in the world. The Ananda Temple, the Dhammayangyi, the Shwezigon — each one is a complete theological statement in brick and plaster, and there are thousands of them.

Anawrahta, who unified Burma in 1044 CE and converted the kingdom to Theravada Buddhism, is the founder in every sense. Before Anawrahta, the Burmese plain was governed by competing lords and the Theravada Buddhist tradition was present but not dominant; a syncretic tradition mixing Theravada with animist and Mahayana elements (the Ari cult) was the established practice. Anawrahta conquered the Mon kingdom of Thaton, brought back thirty-two sets of the Pali canon, thirty learned monks, and the Mon king himself. He was converting his kingdom and his neighbors simultaneously, and he used the Theravada monastic institution as the organizational spine of the new state.

## What Defined Them

**The Theravada revolution.** Anawrahta's conversion of Burma to Theravada Buddhism was the most consequential religious event in the country's history. The Theravada tradition — with its emphasis on the Pali canon, the monastic vinaya (rules), and the direct transmission of the ordination lineage from the time of the Buddha — provided the organizational and legitimacy framework for the new state. The king was the patron of the sangha; the sangha was the king's instrument for organizing the population through Buddhist education and the monastery's social functions. The two were inseparable.

**Mon-Burman synthesis.** The Pagan kingdom was a fusion of the Burman-speaking majority of the upper Irrawaddy plain and the Mon-speaking civilization of the lower plains and delta. Anawrahta's conquest of Thaton brought Mon literacy, Mon Buddhist learning, and Mon artistic traditions north, and the Pagan cultural achievement — its temples, its inscriptions, its painting — was a synthesis of both. The bilingual inscriptions of Pagan (in Burmese, Mon, Pali, and Sanskrit) express a court that held multiple traditions in productive coexistence.

**Temple as cosmic statement.** Each of the major Pagan temples is a complete cosmological environment: the temple structure represents Mount Meru; the four Buddha images facing the four cardinal directions represent the Buddha's omnipresence; the murals inside narrate the Jataka tales (the Buddha's previous lives); the stupa at the center houses the relics that make the structure a living sacred presence. A person who entered the Ananda Temple was entering the cosmos, not just a building. The king who built it was demonstrating that his patronage of the dharma was worthy of Heaven.

**The manuscript tradition.** Alongside the massive temple construction, Pagan produced one of the earliest Burmese literary traditions: religious texts, chronicles, and poetry in the Burmese and Pali languages. The lacquered manuscript boxes that housed the Pali texts were works of art in themselves. The tradition of manuscript production and library endowment — supported by royal grants and accumulated at the great monasteries — preserved the Buddhist textual tradition through the political disruptions that followed Pagan's fall.

**The Mongol end.** The Mongol invasions of Pagan (1277, 1283, 1287) effectively ended the kingdom, though the dynasty lingered in reduced form for decades. The description of the Battle of Ngasaunggyan (1277) in Marco Polo's account — Burmese war elephants scattering the Mongol horses, until the Mongol archers dismounted, tied their horses to trees, and shot the elephants with arrows until they fled — is one of the most vivid battle descriptions in medieval travel literature. The Mongols didn't annex Burma permanently, but Pagan never recovered from the disruptions.

## The Chapters

**1 — Anawrahta's Conversion:** He has heard about the Theravada texts in the Mon kingdom to the south. He sends a request to the Mon king for the canon. The Mon king refuses. Anawrahta goes himself, with an army. He returns with thirty-two sets of the Pali texts, thirty monks, the Mon king, and the relics of the Buddha.

**2 — The Ananda:** Kyanzittha surveys the completed temple. It has taken years. The four standing Buddha images face the four directions. The murals narrate the Jataka tales. He writes the inscriptions in four languages. He is making a statement that all peoples can read.

**3 — The Ordination Reform:** The Burmese monks are traveling to Sri Lanka to be re-ordained in the pure Theravada lineage. The Sinhala ordination lineage is the most pristine in the world. They will bring it back. The Burmese sangha will be reformed at its root.

**4 — Marco Polo's Account:** He is describing the battle of Ngasaunggyan. The Burmese elephants are enormous and terrifying. The Mongol horses panic. The Mongol generals order their men to dismount and tie the horses to the trees. They use their bows from the forest. The elephants are wounded and flee. The Burmese cavalry is then easily defeated. He writes this as an eyewitness would — he was not there, but someone told him exactly.

**5 — After the Mongols:** The plain of Bagan still has its temples. The kingdom is fragmented. The Shan lords control the hills; the Mon lords control the delta; the Irrawaddy plain has no single master. The monks continue in the monasteries. The temples stand. The thousand-temple plain will be this way for centuries.
