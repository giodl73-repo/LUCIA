# Northern & Eastern Europe — Landscape

## Geography

The northern and eastern reaches of Europe form a world shaped by cold water, dark forests, and the long rhythm of seasons that divide the year into starkly different halves. From the fjords of Scandinavia to the river systems of the Dnieper and Volga, from the Baltic littoral to the birch forests of the Ural approaches, this is a region where distance itself is a force. Settlements cling to waterways because water is the only road that does not freeze into impassability or dissolve into mud.

The Scandinavian peninsula is a spine of mountain and fjord, deeply cut by the sea. Norway's western coast is a wall of rock and inlet, where the ocean reaches miles inland and the farmland is measured in narrow strips between cliff and shore. Sweden opens eastward into more forgiving country: lakes, river valleys, arable plains in the south. Denmark is low, flat, maritime, a bridge between the North Sea and the Baltic. The Baltic Sea itself is the interior ocean of this world, connecting Scandinavia to the eastern shores where the Slavic and Finnic peoples built their settlements.

East of the Baltic, the great rivers run south: the Dnieper, the Volga, the Don. These rivers are highways. The peoples who controlled them controlled the trade between the Baltic and the Black Sea, between the forests of the north and the markets of Constantinople and Baghdad. The steppe begins where the forest thins, and the steppe connects this world to the Turkic and Mongol worlds of Central Asia.

## How They Connect

The Norse are the connective tissue of this region. Their ships moved east along the rivers to Constantinople and Baghdad, west to the Atlantic islands and beyond, south into Frankish and Anglo-Saxon territory. The Kievan Rus was founded (the sources debate how) by Norsemen moving along the Dnieper trade route. The Baltic and Finnic peoples were neighbors, trading partners, and sometimes targets. The Slavic peoples of the interior built their own polities in the spaces the Norse ships could not easily reach, and the interaction between Norse traders, Slavic agriculturalists, and steppe nomads created the political landscape of eastern Europe.
