# BOARD Review — Robert S. Lopez
## La Rivoluzione Commerciale

**Date:** 2026-04-17
**Reviewer role:** Medieval economic history; the commercial revolution

---

The chapter is accurate in its essentials, and the structural argument — that the commune's encoding of mutual obligation is what makes the commercial instruments work across distance — is historically correct. I am the person who named this revolution, and I am pleased to see the name applied to a chapter that understands what the revolution actually was.

**Corrections required:**

1. **The bill of exchange and interest.** The chapter states: "A firm that sets the exchange rate in London and pays out at a different rate in Firenze has, in effect, charged interest on the credit it extended, without calling it interest, which canon law forbids." This is accurate as far as it goes, but the scholarly consensus is more nuanced. The concealed interest in the bill of exchange was not simply a matter of different exchange rates — it was built into the structure of the *cambio* as a four-party transaction (drawer, payer, remitter, payee) that allowed a profit to appear as an exchange service rather than a loan. The chapter's formulation is a simplification that is not wrong but may mislead a reader into thinking the concealment was crude. **Recommendation:** Add one sentence acknowledging that the exchange mechanism was more elaborate than a simple rate difference; the four-party structure was the key. This is a P3 correction — the existing text is not false, but a sentence of clarification would serve the reader.

2. **The Frescobaldi in England.** The chapter describes Berto as a representative of the Frescobaldi "twelfth year of his firm's English operations" in 1296. The Frescobaldi's English presence dates from the 1270s, so "twelfth year" would be inaccurate if taken as starting from 1284. The firm was active in England by 1270. A more conservative phrasing — "the firm's decades of English operations" — would be safer without being vague. **Recommendation:** Revise to "the twentieth year of his firm's English operations" (1296 - 1276, approximate start) or use the vaguer phrase. P2 correction — the current phrasing implies a specific chronology that may not be accurate.

3. **Double-entry bookkeeping chronology.** The chapter treats double-entry bookkeeping as an established practice in the 1200-1350 period. The earliest surviving double-entry accounts date from the 1290s in Genoa, and the practice was not widespread in Florence until the 14th century. The chapter's framing is correct for the later part of the era but may overstate its prevalence in the 13th century. **Recommendation:** Add a phrase like "by the early 14th century" to the double-entry section. P3 — the chapter does not explicitly claim the practice was universal in 1200; this is a clarification, not a correction.

**What the chapter does well:**

The Pegolotti handbook as a window onto the commercial network is exactly right. The *commenda* description at method level is the best brief account I have seen in a narrative history. The three-city constitutional comparison is analytically correct: Venice did build commercial coordination through state apparatus, Florence through the guild commune, Genoa through distributed individual enterprise. This typology has been present in the scholarship but never stated this cleanly.

The landing is historically prescient: the Bardi's exposure to Edward III is documented, the theoretical framing of his non-recognition of commercial premises is my own language applied to the crisis (though I never stated it quite so cleanly). The chapter correctly does not narrate the collapse; it seeds it.

**Recommendation:** APPROVE with two corrections (one P2, one P3). The chapter is historically sound and analytically strong.
