**Date:** 2026-04-16

## Assessment: APPROVE without revisions

The Kongo-Teke commercial relationship is correctly positioned: asymmetric in military power, balanced in commercial leverage. The Manikongo's governors paying Teke prices is consistent with the Kongo correspondence record showing chronic dependency on interior goods.

Goods listed — copper ingots, ivory, iron tools, cloth, shell beads — are all attested in the period's trade record. Information flow mechanism ("the Pool market reported whenever anyone returned from the Pool") is consistent with how the Loango coast parallel system worked.

**Observation for figures.md (not a correction):** The chapter emphasizes the Teke at the expense of the northern parallel — the Loango coast broker system, which was itself significant and competing with the Kongo-Pool axis. The Teke were not unique as Central African brokers; they operated within a competitive landscape. A cross-reference note in figures.md would situate the Teke within this broader context without disrupting the chapter's focus.

**Conclusion:** The chapter's framing is accurate for the Teke-specific story it tells. No revisions to chapter content required.
