# R1-P2 — Ibn Khaldun

**Chapter:** Papal States — Plenitudo Potestatis
**Stage:** PANEL-1
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

The opening identifies the correct structural question and earns my respect for doing so clearly.

The plenitudo potestatis is not merely a theological claim — it is a theory of legitimate authority, and this opening presents it as such. The argument is: the Church mediates between God and secular power, and the pope is the Church's summit, therefore the pope holds a superior authority from which secular authority derives. This is a theory of legitimacy, and as I have argued in the Muqaddimah, legitimacy is the central variable of civilizational power. An empire with military strength but no legitimacy is a dynasty waiting to fall. An institution with legitimacy but no military strength can nonetheless direct armies — because kings who want to be recognized as legitimate need the institution's endorsement.

Innocent's pontificate is, by this analysis, the high point of the papacy's legitimacy capital. The opening captures this: John of England submitted, Philip of France submitted, not because Innocent had armies (he had almost none) but because excommunication threatened their legitimacy with their own subjects. The asabiyyah of the medieval Christian polity ran through Rome. The pope who could withdraw legitimacy could destabilize a throne.

The question the full chapter must answer is: what eroded this? The plenitudo potestatis reached its height in 1215 and began its contraction almost immediately. Why? The opening gives one clue — the Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople, which Innocent condemned and could not prevent. This suggests the limit of legitimacy-based authority: it works until a party decides to ignore it, and then the institution has no mechanism beyond further condemnation.

But there is a deeper structural question the opening does not yet name: the reform-corruption cycle. The OVERVIEW tells us this cycle is the papacy's characteristic form. By what mechanism does the papacy's assertion of supreme legitimacy generate the very corruption that calls its legitimacy into question? The accumulation of fees, benefice taxes, the fiscal machine — these are the instruments of power that also become the instruments of criticism. The chapter should trace this mechanism from within, not as moral judgment but as structural observation.

**The question I'd push on:** The opening presents Innocent as the apex — the man who held the most, used it most broadly, died the year after his greatest exercise of it. But what sustained the trajectory from 1122 to 1215? The era begins not with Innocent but with the Concordat of Worms, which ended the Investiture Controversy. How did the papacy build from that settlement to the Fourth Lateran Council? What were the structural mechanisms — the canonists, the Curia's expansion, the extension of papal judicial jurisdiction, the theory developed by Innocent III himself in his decretals? The full chapter must name these. The opening orients us at the peak; the chapter must show the ascent.

**Verdict:** The structural argument is present and correct. The chapter has a real engine to work with: the plenitudo potestatis as a legitimacy theory whose very success created the conditions for its contraction. This is good history. The chapter should make that process visible at the level of mechanism, not just at the level of event.
