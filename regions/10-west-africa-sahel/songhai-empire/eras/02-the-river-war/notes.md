# Notes — The River War (c. 1464-1492 CE)

## Production Bible

### Sources

**Primary:**
- Al-Sadi, *Tarikh al-Sudan* (c. 1655): principal narrative source for Sunni Ali's campaigns, the siege of Djenne, the relationship with Timbuktu scholars. Written from a pro-scholarly, pro-Islamic perspective; treat with awareness of its bias against Sunni Ali.
- Mahmud Kati, *Tarikh al-Fattash* (c. 1519+): second major chronicle. More sympathetic to the Songhai state's military and administrative achievements. Provides details on the river navy and provincial organization.
- Leo Africanus, *Description of Africa* (c. 1526): eyewitness account from a generation after Sunni Ali. Descriptions of Timbuktu, Djenne, Gao, and the Niger River system. Material culture details.

**Academic:**
- Hunwick (1999): annotated translation of al-Sadi; critical apparatus for dating and interpretation.
- Cissoko (1975): the standard French-language treatment of the Songhai Empire.
- Gomez (2018): *African Dominion*; latest synthesis of western Sudanic history.
- Levtzion (1973): *Ancient Ghana and Mali*; background on the Mali decline that created the power vacuum Sunni Ali filled.

### Structural Engine

**The River Loop:** river control enables conquest, conquest enables taxation of trade, taxation funds naval maintenance, naval maintenance enables further conquest. The Niger is not setting; it is the mechanism. The river simultaneously serves as:
1. **Highway** for military projection (the war-canoe fleet)
2. **Food system** (inland delta flood-retreat agriculture feeds the cities and the army)
3. **Trade corridor** (gold-salt exchange, Saharan routes converge at river cities)
4. **Political spine** (whoever holds the river holds the three critical nodes: Gao, Timbuktu, Djenne)

The structural engine is the self-reinforcing loop of river control. The chapter tracks this loop from its first expression (taking Timbuktu, 1468) through its full extension (the seven-year siege and capture of Djenne, c. 1468-1473) to its mature state (Sunni Ali's reorganization of the river corridor) to the question the engine generates: what happens when a military river state must govern a scholarly, commercial civilization it conquered but does not share the values of?

### Voice Register

**River voice:** Forward-moving, current-driven prose. Longer sentences that gather momentum like a river gathering tributaries, punctuated by short declarative stops where the current hits a bend. Not the spare steppe voice (too short, too dry). Not the maritime voice (too oceanic, too rolling). The river voice flows in one direction, carries weight, and moves steadily. It is the voice of a current, not a tide.

Zero em-dashes. The river voice does not pause parenthetically; it carries everything downstream.

Sensory palette: water on wood, heat building over the day, the smell of wet clay and river vegetation, the sound of paddles in unison, the visual contrast between the brown river and the pale Sahel banks.

### Key Figures

1. **Sunni Ali Ber** (r. 1464-1492): The central figure. Military genius, river strategist, contemptuous of urban scholarly culture. Al-Sadi presents him as tyrannical; the *Tarikh al-Fattash* is more balanced. The chapter must render him from INSIDE Songhai military culture, where his achievements were respected, while honestly acknowledging the scholars' hostility and its sources. He is not a hero or a villain. He is a man who understood what power meant on a river.

2. **The scholars of Timbuktu** (collective): Not a single figure but an institution. The ulama of Sankore, Djinguereber, Sidi Yahia. They represent Islamic legal and intellectual authority. Their tension with Sunni Ali is structural (two forms of legitimacy competing for primacy), not personal.

3. **Akil ag Malwal**: Tuareg chief who held Timbuktu before 1468. His withdrawal represents the displacement of desert-based power by river-based power. Brief but important.

4. **The Sorko and Bozo fishermen**: The river peoples whose knowledge of the Niger was the foundation of the Songhai navy. The Sorko were the traditional masters of the river. Sunni Ali drew on their expertise. They represent the knowledge system that made the navy possible.

5. **Sunni Baru** (r. 1492-1493): Sunni Ali's son. Brief reign. Overthrown by Askia Muhammad. His fall marks the transition from the military river state to the administrative Islamic state. The chapter's terminal figure.

### Narrative Arc (Section Plan)

**I. The Canoes at Dawn (~800 words, opening integrated)**
The arrival at Timbuktu, 1468. Establish the river, the navy, the man, the tension with the scholars. The structural engine's first expression.

**II. The River's People (~700 words)**
The Sorko and Bozo fishermen. The knowledge system behind the navy: how the river peoples read the current, built the canoes, understood the seasonal flood cycle. The Niger as a living system, not just a highway. Sunni Ali's genius was not inventing the river knowledge but militarizing it.

**III. The Siege of Djenne (~1000 words)**
The seven-year siege (traditional dating). Djenne's position in the inland delta: a city on islands, surrounded by channels, impossible to take by land. The navy's role: blockade and patient pressure. The fall of Djenne as the completion of the river-control loop. Trade implications: Djenne was the commercial heart of the western Sudan. With Djenne and Timbuktu both in hand, Sunni Ali controlled the entire middle Niger trade corridor.

**IV. The Government from the River (~800 words)**
How Sunni Ali governed: provinces along the river, appointed officials, taxation of river trade. His relationship with the land: seasonal campaigns against Mossi raiders from the south, against Tuareg incursions from the north. But always returning to the river. The river as the organizing principle of the state.

**V. The Scholar's War (~800 words)**
The tension between Sunni Ali and the ulama of Timbuktu. Al-Sadi's chronicle presents Sunni Ali as an enemy of learning. The structural reason: Sunni Ali's legitimacy came from military conquest and river control, not from Islamic legal authority. The scholars wanted a ruler who governed THROUGH them. Sunni Ali governed OVER them. The tension is between two forms of authority, not between knowledge and ignorance.

**VI. Death on the Water (~700 words)**
Sunni Ali's death in 1492, crossing a river (the traditional account). Sunni Baru's brief succession. Askia Muhammad's coup (1493). The chapter lands on the transition: the river war is over, the river has been won, and the question now is whether the state that the river built can be governed by a different principle. The landing should find the river's persistence: the river does not care who governs from its banks. The current is the same.

### Panel Pressure Points (Anticipated)

- **Achebe**: Will push on Songhai cosmological relationship with the river, not just strategic. Must show the river as sacred, not just useful. Section II (Sorko/Bozo) is the place.
- **Ibn Khaldun**: Will push on the structural engine's completeness. Must show the loop explicitly: river control -> conquest -> taxation -> navy -> more river control. Section IV (governance) is the place.
- **Davis**: Will push on the river navy as a knowledge system with intellectual depth. Must show canoe-building, current-reading, seasonal adaptation at the level of practice, not summary. Section II is the place.
- **Kapuscinski**: Will push on sensory immersion in/on the water. Must put the reader in the canoe, on the river, feeling the current. Throughout, but especially Sections I and III.
- **Tuchman**: Will push on the middle sections (IV, V) maintaining narrative momentum. Governance and scholar-tension sections risk becoming expository. Must stay in scene.

### Open Questions

1. The seven-year siege of Djenne: how reliable is this duration? Al-Sadi gives seven years, seven months, seven days (formulaic). Hunwick notes the number may be traditional. The chapter should use the duration as the tradition gives it, with honest acknowledgment.
2. Sunni Ali's relationship with indigenous Songhai religion vs. Islam: the sources are biased (written by the Muslim scholars he antagonized). The chapter must handle this with the honest-uncertainty technique, not by choosing one side.
3. The Sorko as the knowledge-system foundation of the navy: this is reconstructed from later ethnographic accounts and from the chronicles' references. The epistemological status should be disclosed in figures.md.
