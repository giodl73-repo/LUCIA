# The Pacific Century (Modern East Asia)

Something decisive happened in East Asia in the century and a half after 1850. Three ancient civilizations — Japan, China, and Korea — each confronted the same challenge: a world reorganized by European industrial power and the threat of colonial subjugation. Each responded differently, and the responses remade not just those three nations but the entire global order. Japan's Meiji Restoration (1868) produced the first non-Western nation to industrialize on its own terms and then to build an empire on the European model — a path that led to the Pacific War and the atomic bombings of 1945. China's collapse of the Qing dynasty (1911) opened a century of revolution, civil war, foreign invasion, and communist transformation that ended with the People's Republic as one of the two most powerful states on Earth. Korea's colonization by Japan (1910–1945), partition into two states (1945), and fratricidal war (1950–1953) produced one of the modern world's most striking contrasts: the South became one of the twentieth century's great economic success stories; the North became one of its most sealed and surveilled states.

These are not the ancient civilizations of Region 17 (East Asia). That region tells the long story — Shang bronze and Tang poetry and Song ceramics and Joseon scholarship. This region tells the modern story: the encounter with industrial modernity, the decision about what to keep and what to transform, the wars that came from those decisions, and the societies that emerged. The thread connecting Japan, China, and Korea here is not shared culture — though they share more than any of them always acknowledges — but shared confrontation with the same historical force. How a civilization answers the question "what must we become in order to survive?" is one of the deepest questions history can ask.

Key features:
- The 38th Parallel: the line drawn in 1945 that divided Korea into Soviet and American occupation zones, and that the Korean War (1950–53) made permanent; one of the world's most militarized borders
- Taiwan Strait: the 180-km channel between the People's Republic of China and the Republic of China on Taiwan; the site of ongoing geopolitical tension and the question left unresolved since 1949
- Manchuria: the northeastern Chinese territory that Japan seized in 1931; the zone where Japanese imperial expansion and Chinese nationalist resistance first came to open war
- Hiroshima and Nagasaki: the cities destroyed by American atomic bombs in August 1945; the event that ended the Pacific War and opened the atomic age; the wound Japan has carried and meditated on ever since
- The Yellow Sea and Sea of Japan: the maritime corridors linking the three nations; the routes of medieval tribute missions and modern trade alike
- The Yangtze River: China's central artery; the line the Japanese advance reached in 1937; the corridor along which the Great Leap Forward famine fell heaviest

## How They Connect

Japan's modernization and Japan's imperialism are the same story, inseparable. The Meiji oligarchs looked at the Western powers colonizing Asia and decided Japan must either colonize or be colonized. They chose to colonize. Taiwan fell in 1895, Korea in 1910, Manchuria in 1931, and most of coastal China by 1938. Japan's empire was organized around the logic of racial hierarchy it borrowed from the European powers, with Japanese at the apex and Chinese, Koreans, and Southeast Asian peoples ranked below. The comfort women system, Unit 731's human experimentation in Manchuria, the Rape of Nanjing, the forced labor of Korean workers in Japanese mines and factories — these events remain contested between Japan and its neighbors. Japan's official acknowledgments have repeatedly been challenged as insufficient by South Korea and China; Japanese domestic politics periodically reopens the question of how to characterize the wartime period. Contemporary relations among all three countries cannot be understood without this unresolved history.

China and Korea experienced Japanese imperialism as the defining catastrophe of the twentieth century, just as the century of humiliation (1839–1949) — the series of defeats by Western powers and Japan that stripped China of sovereignty — is the emotional substrate of modern Chinese nationalism. The People's Republic of China under both Mao and his successors has used the memory of Japanese aggression and Western imperialism as a legitimizing narrative: the Communist Party that defeated the Nationalists and ended the century of humiliation is the party that restored China's dignity. This framing is not purely cynical; it is also believed, and it organizes the political imagination of hundreds of millions of people.

The Korean War (1950–1953) froze a division that has now lasted longer than it was supposed to. The two Koreas share a language, a classical cultural heritage, a memory of Japanese colonization, and an inability to reunify that is as much a function of great-power competition (China, the United States, and Russia all have stakes in the current arrangement) as of the ideological gap between the systems themselves. South Korea's democratization (1987) and economic rise, and North Korea's development of nuclear weapons, have made the two sides progressively more unlike each other at the same time that the question of unity remains officially open in both countries' constitutions.

## Cross-References

- East Asia (Region 17) — the long roots: Tang China, Heian Japan, and Goryeo Korea are the civilizational foundations from which these modern transformations grew; the Confucian social structure and Buddhist religious tradition that Meiji Japan and revolutionary China both tried to reform are products of Region 17
- Southeast Asia (Region 18) — the primary zone of Japanese imperial expansion during World War II; the Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere was the ideological frame; the Philippines, Indonesia, Malaysia, Vietnam, and Burma bore the weight of Japanese occupation
- Colonial Empires (Region 24) — the Western colonial powers whose presence in Asia forced the Meiji and Qing responses; the unequal treaties, the treaty ports, and the opium trade are the colonial system's direct impacts on this region
- Central & Inner Asia (Region 19) — Manchuria, Mongolia, and Tibet are the contested borderlands where Chinese sovereignty, Russian interest, Japanese expansion, and Mongolian and Tibetan identity have all met and collided
- Diasporas (Region 25) — the Chinese overseas communities of Southeast Asia, the Americas, and Africa maintain cultural and commercial connections to mainland China and Taiwan; the Korean diaspora (Zainichi Koreans in Japan, Koreans in the United States) carries its own version of the modern Korean story
- Oceania & the Pacific (Region 22) — Japan's Pacific War (1941–1945) brought Japanese military power to Micronesia, New Guinea, and the Solomon Islands; the Pacific theater of World War II is the meeting point of these two regions
