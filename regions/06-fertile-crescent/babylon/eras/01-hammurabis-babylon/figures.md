# Figures — The First Dynasty: Hammurabi's Babylon (Era 1)

## Hammurabi (r. c. 1792-1750 BCE)

Sixth king of the First Dynasty of Babylon. Son of Sin-muballit. King of Sumer and Akkad.

**What we know:** Hammurabi is one of the best-documented rulers of the ancient world before the first millennium BCE. His year-names survive for all forty-two years of his reign (Horsnell 1999), providing a year-by-year record of his building activities, military campaigns, and religious dedications. His diplomatic correspondence survives in the Mari archive (ARM series), where letters between Hammurabi and Zimri-Lim of Mari and between Babylonian and Mariote officials illuminate the political world of the Old Babylonian period. His administrative letters to subordinates survive in collections from Larsa and other southern cities (AbB series), revealing a hands-on ruler who demanded specificity from his officials.

The code itself (Roth 1997, Richardson 2000) provides the prologue and epilogue in which Hammurabi describes his own role: shepherd of the people, chosen by Marduk, appointed by Anu and Enlil to cause justice to prevail. The stele (Louvre Sb 8) preserves the image of Shamash extending the rod and ring.

**What we cannot know:** Hammurabi's personal character is recoverable only through his official correspondence and his public inscriptions, both of which present the royal persona rather than the private man. The Mari letters show him as a shrewd negotiator; the administrative letters show him as an exacting administrator. Whether these qualities extended to his private life, his personal relationships, or his inner motivations is not accessible. The chapter presents him through his own records and his public acts.

**Sources:** Charpin, *Hammurabi of Babylon* (2012); Van De Mieroop, *King Hammurabi of Babylon* (2005); Roth, *Law Collections from Mesopotamia and Asia Minor* (1997); Horsnell, *The Year-Names of the First Dynasty of Babylon* (1999); the Mari archive (ARM series); the AbB series (administrative letters).

## Zimri-Lim of Mari (r. c. 1775-1761 BCE)

King of Mari. Son of Yahdun-Lim. Hammurabi's ally and eventual victim.

**What we know:** Zimri-Lim reclaimed his father's throne after the death of Shamshi-Adad of Assyria, who had displaced his family. His palace at Mari, excavated by Andre Parrot beginning in the 1930s, was one of the largest structures in the ancient Near East, with over 260 rooms, painted murals, and an archive of more than 20,000 tablets. The archive preserves his diplomatic correspondence with Hammurabi, his administrative records, his intelligence reports, and the daily business of a major Near Eastern court.

Hammurabi destroyed the palace around 1761 BCE. The fire that burned it baked the tablets in the archive rooms, preserving them for excavation. Zimri-Lim's fate after the destruction is unknown; he disappears from the record.

**What we cannot know:** Whether Zimri-Lim anticipated Hammurabi's betrayal of their alliance. The Mari letters show the two kings as close allies, exchanging gifts and intelligence. The transition from alliance to conquest is not explained in the surviving correspondence. The chapter presents the alliance through the letters and the destruction through the year-names and the archaeological evidence, without inventing a motivation for the betrayal.

**Sources:** Charpin and Ziegler, *Mari et le Proche-Orient* (2003); Sasson, "The King and I" (1995); the Mari archive (ARM series); Parrot excavation reports.

## The Merchant in the Karum

Unnamed. A type representing the commercial class of Old Babylonian Babylon.

**What we know:** The Old Babylonian period produced one of the densest archives of commercial law in the ancient world. Thousands of contract tablets survive from Babylonian trading houses, documenting silver loans, overland caravan partnerships, shipping agreements, sales of real property, and the associated litigation. The merchants operated in the karum, the walled commercial district associated with the main temples, and their contracts were sealed, witnessed, and filed according to standardized legal forms.

**What we cannot know:** No individual merchant from the karum at Babylon is followed through multiple transactions in the chapter. The merchant who reads the stele in the opening and closing is a type, not a composite: he represents the profession as attested by the contract archive. His presence at the stele is a narrative device that tests the code's central claim (publicness) against the practice of the people the code governed.

**Honest limit:** The chapter uses the merchant as the figure who bridges the theological argument (the code as cosmic order) and the practical reality (the code as commercial regulation). He is rendered through the material evidence (seal impressions, contract language, trade goods) rather than through invented biography.

**Sources:** Veenhof, "The Old Babylonian Period" in *A History of Ancient Near Eastern Law* (Westbrook, ed., 2003); Charpin, *Writing, Law, and Kingship in Old Babylonian Mesopotamia* (2010); the Old Babylonian contract corpus (CAD, AbB).

## The Naditu Women of Sippar

Unnamed individually. A class of priestesses consecrated to Shamash at Sippar.

**What we know:** The naditu women are attested in hundreds of legal documents from the gagum (cloister) at Sippar, excavated by multiple campaigns from the late 19th century onward. They were women of elite families who entered the cloister, took vows of celibacy, and managed substantial estates. Their legal records document property purchases, silver loans, adoptions, inheritance disputes, and litigation. The code addresses their inheritance rights specifically (sections 178-184). Rivkah Harris's study *Ancient Sippar* (1975) remains the foundational analysis of the gagum and its inhabitants.

**What we cannot know:** The chapter does not name a specific naditu because no single naditu's full biography is recoverable from the fragmentary archive. The naditu whose transactions are described in Section V is a type drawn from the pattern visible across many documented women: property owner, silver lender, adopter of heirs, litigant. The specific transactions listed (house purchase, silver loan, adoption, field dispute) are all attested in the archive.

**Sources:** Harris, *Ancient Sippar* (1975); Roth, *Law Collections from Mesopotamia and Asia Minor* (1997), sections 178-184; Stone, "The Social Role of the Naditu Women in Old Babylonian Nippur" (1982).

## Marduk

Patron deity of Babylon. Head of the divine assembly. Not a human figure; the divine presence that organizes the chapter's theological world.

**What we know:** Marduk's rise from minor local deity to head of the Mesopotamian pantheon is traced through the Enuma Elish (Lambert, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, 2013), the code's prologue, and the Akitu festival liturgy. His temple, the Esagila, was the religious center of Babylon for over a millennium. His statue was the object of the Akitu ritual, and its presence or absence in the city determined the legitimacy of the king. The statue was taken by the Hittites (c. 1595 BCE), later by the Elamites, and each time its return was a political and theological event of the first order.

**Sources:** Lambert, *Babylonian Creation Myths* (2013); George, *Babylonian Topographical Texts* (1992); the Akitu ritual texts (various editions).

## A Note on Sources and Figures

This chapter benefits from an unusually rich documentary base for a second-millennium culture. The code itself, the year-names, the Mari archive, the administrative correspondence, the naditu legal documents, and the scribal school exercises together provide a multi-layered record of Old Babylonian life. The chapter draws on all of these. No figure is invented. The named figures (Hammurabi, Zimri-Lim) are presented through their own documentation. The unnamed figures (the merchant, the naditu) are types drawn from well-attested categories.

The primary uncertainty concerns the stele's original placement. The stele was found at Susa, not at Babylon, having been carried off by an Elamite king in the twelfth century BCE. The chapter places it in the Esagila precinct based on the prologue's language and scholarly consensus (Charpin 2012), but this is inference, not archaeological fact. The chapter notes the stele's journey to Susa in the landing section.
