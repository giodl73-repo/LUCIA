# Teotihuacan — "Where the Gods Were Made"

Teotihuacan is the name the later Nahuatl-speaking peoples gave it: "the place where men become gods," or "the place where time began." The city's own name for itself is lost. Its people's name for themselves is lost. What remains is the city itself — the largest urban complex in the ancient Americas — and the traces of its presence across a region it never needed to conquer in order to shape. At its height around 400 CE, perhaps a hundred to two hundred thousand people lived in the city's planned apartment compounds, organizing their lives along the great central axis of the Street of the Dead (which may not have been what its builders called it either; that name too comes from those who walked it after the builders were gone).

What the city's inhabitants knew was this: they lived at the center of the cosmos, at the place where the current age of the world had been created. The Pyramid of the Sun rose over the cave beneath it — the cave where, in their understanding, the sun had been born into the current age. The Pyramid of the Moon at the avenue's northern end and the Feathered Serpent Temple at its southern end completed a sacred geography that organized the entire city around the relationships between rain, maize, time, and solar power. You could not live in Teotihuacan and not feel that you were at the world's axis. That was the point.

Key places:
- **Pyramid of the Sun** — the city's largest structure; built over a natural lava-tube cave understood as the place of solar creation
- **Pyramid of the Moon** — northern terminus of the Street of the Dead; human sacrifice deposits found at its stages of construction
- **Feathered Serpent Temple (Ciudadela)** — the Rain/Water deity complex; mass sacrificial burials at its dedication
- **Apartment compounds** — the city's residential fabric; each compound housed 60–100 people in a self-contained unit with its own temple and courtyard

## What Defined Them

**The planned city as cosmological argument.** Teotihuacan was not an organic city that grew from a village. It was designed — the Street of the Dead and its cross-axis established as the organizing grid before most of the population arrived. This grid was oriented to the point on the horizon where the Pleiades rose at the beginning of the agricultural year, which was also (by astronomical coincidence or deliberate alignment) oriented toward the center of the sacred cave beneath the Pyramid of the Sun. The city's geometry was an argument about how the cosmos was organized, made visible in stone and street.

**The apartment compound as the unit of social life.** Unlike most ancient cities, where wealth concentrated in palace complexes and the majority lived in dispersed huts, Teotihuacan organized its population in dense, multi-room apartment compounds — walled enclosures housing extended families and craft-specialized work groups. Compounds for obsidian workers, for potters, for merchants were located together in distinct quarters. A compound from the Oaxaca barrio was built in Zapotec architectural style; a compound from the merchant quarter may have been home to Maya-speaking traders. The city was multinational before anyone had a word for the concept.

**Obsidian as the city's economic spine.** The Pachuca obsidian source, in the mountains northeast of the city, produced a green-tinted glass that was the finest cutting material available in Mesoamerica. Teotihuacan controlled this source, and obsidian from Pachuca appears in every corner of the Mesoamerican world during the city's florescence. The obsidian blade was medicine, warfare, ritual, and craft tool in one. The city that controlled its production controlled something the whole region needed, and the political geography of Mesoamerica organized itself accordingly.

**The Feathered Serpent and Tlaloc as paired powers.** Throughout the city's art — in the murals of apartment compounds, on the facades of the Feathered Serpent Temple, in the pottery that Teotihuacan produced and exported — two deities appear together: the Feathered Serpent (later called Quetzalcóatl in Nahuatl) and the Rain God (later called Tlaloc). This pairing expressed a cosmological claim about how the world worked: rain and solar power, water and sky, the serpent that moves between earth and air. These figures traveled with Teotihuacan's traders and emissaries to every corner of the region and took root in the sacred traditions of the Maya, the Zapotec, and every culture that followed.

**The burning question.** Around 550 CE, the civic and religious core of Teotihuacan was burned in a fire that archaeological evidence suggests was deliberate and focused on elite structures — palaces, temples, administrative buildings — while leaving the apartment compounds of ordinary residents largely intact. This is not the pattern of a foreign conquest. It looks like an internal event: a political revolution, a rebellion against the priestly and ruling class, an act of cosmological revision. The city continued to exist and be inhabited for centuries. But the institution that had organized it was gone. Why remains the central question this city leaves open.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Cave Beneath:** Before the pyramid, there is the cave. Someone discovers what is under the hill and understands what it means. The city that will house two hundred thousand people begins with a hole in the earth.

**2 — The Street of the Gods:** The surveyors lay out the axis. The Feathered Serpent Temple rises. Hundreds of people are killed at its dedication and buried in its foundations. A city that will never call itself an empire is establishing the terms on which it will organize the world.

**3 — The Height of the World:** A Maya diplomat in the apartment quarter of the foreign merchants watches the annual festival from the Street of the Dead. Back in Tikal, his king will soon commission a monument that shows him dressed as a Teotihuacan warrior. The city's presence is everywhere; its name is spoken in languages it does not speak itself.

**4 — The Interior Fire:** The fire moves along the Street of the Dead, building by building, from south to north. The apartment compounds are untouched. The temples and palaces burn. Someone has made a decision about what the city should become.

**5 — The Sacred Ruin:** A young Mexica man walks the Street of the Dead two hundred years after the fire. The city is inhabited but transformed. He is told — and he believes — that this is where the gods sacrificed themselves to make the sun move. He will carry that story home and build upon it.
