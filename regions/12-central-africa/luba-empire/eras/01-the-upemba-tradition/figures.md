# Figures — Era 1: The Upemba Tradition

**Note on sources:** The Upemba tradition is an archaeological culture. No written records survive; no oral tradition reaches reliably into this period (the Luba oral tradition's recoverable layers begin with the Kalala Ilunga foundation, c. 1500 CE, Era 2). All figures in this era are composite or anonymous, drawn from the archaeological record, from ethnographic analogy with neighboring lacustrine cultures, and from the material evidence of the Lake Kisale burials. The compositions are disclosed here; they are not disclosed in the chapter text, which presents them as particular human beings rather than typological constructions.

---

## The Fisher-Woman (unnamed, composite)

The chapter's primary figure. She is not a single individual but a composite drawn from:
- Archaeological evidence of lakeshore fishing practices in the Upemba Depression (de Maret 1985)
- Ethnographic studies of lacustrine fishing communities in the Central African Great Lakes region
- The fishing technology visible in the archaeological record: dugout canoes, weighted nets, the seasonal exploitation of tilapia schools

Her fishing practice is historically grounded. Tilapia schooling in thermoclines — the cold layers below the warm surface water — is a documented behavior in Central African lakes; fishing communities that understood this behavior had access to a more reliable and abundant catch than those that did not. The seasonal cycle of deep-water fishing (cold season) and shallow-water fishing (warm season) is attested in ethnographic sources from comparable lacustrine environments.

Her awareness of the iron exchange is historically grounded in the trade network visible in the archaeological distribution of iron tools and copper ornaments at Lake Kisale sites. The specific transaction described in the chapter (dried tilapia traded for an iron spearhead, mediated through an exchange specialist) is a plausible representation of the exchange mechanisms that the archaeological distribution implies, though no specific transaction is attested.

Her knowledge of the lake — the birds' behavior, the papyrus margin in current, the resistance of a net in the cold layer — is drawn from the deep ecological knowledge that lakeshore communities necessarily develop through generations of fishing. This is not attributed to a specific source but is an inference from the material record: communities that fished Lake Kisale successfully for centuries necessarily held sophisticated ecological knowledge.

**What we cannot know:** Her name. Her specific lineage. Her individual biography. Whether she was a real individual or only the type of person the archaeological record implies existed. The chapter presents her as particular; the historical record makes her composite. Both are true.

## The Iron-Smelter (unnamed, composite)

The chapter's specialist figure. He is composite, drawn from:
- The iron-smelting technology visible in the Upemba Depression archaeological record. The furnace design described in the chapter (shaft furnace, four tuyere pipes, forced-draft bellows) is based on Central African iron-smelting traditions documented archaeologically and ethnographically in the region.
- Pierre de Maret's stratigraphic analysis of the Upemba deposits, which shows iron metallurgy as an established practice from at least the fifth century CE.
- Studies of Central African iron-smelting knowledge systems, which document the multi-generational transmission of smelting knowledge through apprenticeship and the specific expertise required to read ore quality, manage charcoal burn, and assess bloom readiness.

His ore-reading knowledge is historically grounded in what the scholarship tells us about Central African iron-smelting traditions: the knowledge of laterite ore seams, the selection of specific wood species for charcoal quality, the reading of the bloom through color and texture. These are confirmed as components of Central African smelting knowledge, though the specific sensory details in the chapter (pressing a thumbnail into the ore, wetting with the tongue) are plausible reconstructions based on documented practice in comparable traditions.

The exchange mechanism — the iron specialist's tools moving through an intermediary to the lakeshore — is based on the distribution evidence at Lake Kisale (iron tools from plateau ore appearing in lakeshore burial sites) and on the general model of exchange specialist mediation in Central African trade networks documented by Vansina (1966).

**What we cannot know:** His name. His specific lineage. The exact furnace design he used (the chapter draws on the dominant Central African design; regional variation is possible). The precise terms of his exchange relationships with the lakeshore communities.

## The Copper-Rings Woman (unnamed, historical)

This figure is not composite. The Lake Kisale burials — excavated by Nenquin (1963) and further analyzed by de Maret (1985) — contain a burial with elaborate copper ornaments including rings on multiple fingers. The specific description in the chapter ("copper rings on every finger") is grounded in the archaeological record; the quantity and placement of copper objects in the richest Kisalian-phase burials is documented.

What the chapter adds is interior presence: the question of what the community knew about her that they arranged her burial this way. This is inference from the burial record itself, not from any direct testimony. The burial arrangement (tools at the right hand, ornaments on the body, vessels at head and feet) is a plausible reconstruction of the deliberate arrangements the Kisalian burials show; the specific arrangement of the richest burials documents intentional positioning of objects.

Her role in the exchange network — the inference that people who appear in the richest graves were nodes in the exchange network rather than simply passive recipients of wealth — is based on the correlation between grave-goods richness and the presence of goods that required long-distance exchange (copper from Katanga, beads from distant sources). This correlation is documented in the scholarship and is the basis for the chapter's characterization of her as a person who held recognized authority.

**What we cannot know:** Her name. Her specific role — whether she was a lineage head, an exchange specialist, a ritual practitioner, or some combination. Whether the recognition that the burial represents was voluntary (the community's gift to her memory) or required (an obligation the community owed to her lineage). The chapter presents her with the ambiguity the record demands.

## Kalala Ilunga (mentioned but not present)

The founding hero of the Luba kingdom is named once in the chapter's final section, as the horizon toward which the Upemba tradition is moving. He is not developed here. His story belongs to Era 2 — The Kalala Ilunga Foundation.

The historical status of Kalala Ilunga is complex. The oral tradition records him as the culture-hero who arrived from the west, married a local chief's daughter, and brought bulopwe (the transferable principle of sacred kingship) to the Luba world. Whether Kalala Ilunga was a historical individual, a legendary composite of early Luba rulers, or a mythological figure whose story was developed to explain the kingdom's origin is a question the scholarship does not resolve with certainty. Reefe (1981) treats him as representing a historical process of political integration rather than a single individual; Vansina (1966) notes that such founding-hero narratives in Central African oral tradition typically encode real political processes in symbolic form.

For the purposes of this project, Kalala Ilunga is the founding figure of Era 2. His mention at the close of Era 1 is accurate in the sense that the Upemba tradition's terminal condition — the readiness of the lakeshore society for a named legitimizing ideology — is historically prior to the kingdom's constitution, and the kingdom is historically constituted through the founding narrative's events.
