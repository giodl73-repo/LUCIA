# 中华人民共和国の起立 — The Standing Up of the People's Republic of China

## October 1, 1949 — Tiananmen Square

The gate rose before them in the autumn light, its red walls holding the afternoon sun as if they had been waiting for this day. Mao Zedong stood at the microphone in a loose-fitting grey tunic, his Hunanese accent carrying the countryside inside it, and he looked out across the square and spoke without ceremony.

"The Chinese people have stood up."

*Zhongguo renmin zhanzqi lai le.*

The announcement moved through the crowd like a current, and what it moved through was the end of something — the wars, the treaties, the occupation that had entered the language as a single concept: *bainian guochi*. The unequal treaties that had opened China to foreign goods and foreign guns. The treaty ports where Chinese were not permitted to enter certain streets. The Japanese occupation of Manchuria and then all of coastal China, and the wars that came from that occupation. The warlords in between. The corruption of the Nationalists. The sense — not abstract, but felt in the body, in the school curriculum, in the inability of China to control its own coast — that China was no longer its own.

He said it plainly. Not "China is strong" or "we have won a victory" or "the people's revolution has triumphed." He said: *Zhongguo renmin zhanzqi lai le* — the Chinese people have stood up.

The crowd filled the square. How many? The official count would say hundreds of thousands. The actual number will never be known, because many came from the surrounding villages and returned home when night fell. What was certain was that they filled the space in front of the gate, a sea of caps and raised arms, and when Mao spoke they could feel what was happening — not the future, which no one could see — but the present moment, the instant of the standing up, the release of something that had been held for so long that many people wept without understanding why they wept.

In the crowd stood Gao Rong, a math teacher from Chengdu. Gao Rong was not a revolutionary. He had spent the war years teaching in schools, watching the Japanese planes flatten the city, watching the Nationalist government lose the country and then leave. He had not fought in any war. When the voice came through the square, something in his chest moved, and his eyes burned, and he could not explain why. Around him, other people were crying too, but no one could say exactly what they were crying for — the ending of the waiting, or the beginning of something, or the relief of the burden, or the fear of what came next, all of it at once and none of it separate.

In the ministries around the square, the new officials were bent over desks, their hands moving across paper in the failing light. They were inventorying: a country in ruins, warlord holdouts, an economy on the edge of collapse. Each man wrote what he was assigned to write, and as he wrote, he felt the weight of it — not just the revolution he had won, but the weight of actually having to run it. One man paused, his pen above the page, and wondered if anyone in the square had understood what the standing up would require. Then he put the pen down and continued writing.

What they did with that responsibility would be inseparable, later, from catastrophe. The land reform that followed would be swift and brutal — landlords tried, executed or driven out, their property divided among peasants, the whole transaction driven by quotas and the knowledge that slowness would be read as rightism. The Korean War would begin within months, and China would throw an army at it, not because the border was threatened but because the revolution could not admit to the world that Korea mattered less than staying out. The Great Leap Forward was already being designed in the ministries while Mao spoke, the mad logic of it — the backyard steel furnaces, the production quotas that bore no relationship to reality, the suppression of accurate information about the harvest — all of it already being constructed as policy before anyone understood that it would kill tens of millions.

But on October 1, 1949, none of that was visible. What was visible was the gate, the square, the man at the microphone, and the people gathered to hear him say that the century was over. One era had ended. The next had not yet revealed what it would cost.

The autumn light held. The red walls held the sun. Mao finished speaking. The crowd remained, not moving, as if the standing up was so new that leaving would break it.

## The Gate of Heavenly Peace — What It Means

*Tiananmen* — the Gate of Heavenly Peace. The gate itself was five centuries old when Mao stood at its feet, built in the Ming Dynasty when Beijing was the center of the world, or so it seemed to the people inside it. The gate was painted red, and that red — the red of the imperial workshops, the red that belonged to the Chinese cosmology as the color of the south and of transformation — had become the color of revolution. The Party had chosen it deliberately. The gate was not new. What was new was that the gate now belonged to the people, or so the pronouncement claimed.

Inside the gate, or rather on top of it, the Forbidden City — the palace that had been closed to all but the imperial family and the officials who served them. After 1911, when the last emperor abdicated and was removed from the Forbidden City, it had remained a closed place, a museum of the old world. The Communists had left it standing, unburned. The revolution was not destroying the past; it was inheriting it, and that inheritance — that claim — was itself a kind of violence. We are not breaking the gate, the gesture said. We are walking through it.

The October 1 ceremony had been planned for weeks. The Nationalists had fallen so rapidly that there had been time to arrange the sound system, to send out word through party channels, to gather the crowds. But there had not been time to arrange much else. The government was new. Most of its members had never run anything. The oldest ones were communist intellectuals who had spent the previous decade in caves in Yan'an, waiting for the revolution to arrive. The revolution had arrived faster than anyone expected. Victory had moved like an avalanche, and now the question was what to do with the country that had been buried beneath it.

The people in the square did not know this. They felt the gate and the sun and the voice at the microphone, and they felt the weight of the century lift, or thought they felt it, and that feeling was the reality of the moment, regardless of what happened later.

China has stood up.

It was true. It was also the beginning of something that would not be fully visible for decades.
