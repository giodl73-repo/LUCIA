# Figures — Aimsir na Manach (The Age of the Monks)

## A Note on the Record

The primary record for this era is heavily monastic: the vitae, annals, and law texts that survive were written by monks, in Latin or early Irish, for monastic purposes. The fili's practice is reconstructed from later manuscripts — the bardic grammar tracts, the mythological and genealogical compilations — which were themselves written down by monks preserving what had once been oral. This means the surviving record is inevitably shaped by the institution that produced it. The following figures are drawn from that record with this asymmetry acknowledged.

---

## Ferdomnach — Composite Figure

No single named fili from the opening decades of the era (c. 400–500 CE) survives with documentary detail sufficient to reconstruct a scene. The fili Ferdomnach in the chapter's opening is a constructed composite: built from what the brehon law texts and later bardic grammar tracts tell us about the class of practitioners, what the mythological texts tell us about the fili's social function, and what the feast-hall scenes preserved in early Irish narrative tell us about the procedure of praise. The name Ferdomnach (literally "the man of poetry") is historically attested in later centuries — there is a Ferdomnach of Armagh, a ninth-century scribe, whose name suggests the continuing prestige of the bardic designation — and is used here as a representative figure for the class.

The composite is justified because the fili tradition was transmitted by class, not by individual genius. What Ferdomnach does in the opening scene — reading the hall, calculating éraic, composing in the deibide meter, placing Áed before Conall for reasons of community repair — any fili of his grade would have done. The specific name anchors a specific scene; the practices described are historically attested as class practices.

## Pátraic (Patrick), c. 415–493 CE

The historical Patrick is accessible primarily through two texts he wrote himself: the *Confessio* (a spiritual autobiography and defense of his mission) and the *Epistola ad Milites Corotici* (a letter of condemnation written after a group of his newly baptized converts were killed or enslaved by a British chieftain). Both are in Latin, both are clearly the work of a man who considered his Latin inadequate, and both reveal a figure of remarkable psychological complexity: certain of his calling, uncertain of his worthiness, aware of how he was seen by the educated clergy who had not had to learn their faith while herding sheep in Connacht.

He was captured at sixteen from a family estate in Britain — Romano-British, probably Christianized, his father a deacon and his grandfather a priest — and spent six years as a slave before escaping. He returned to Britain, received some form of clerical training (he is vague about this), and came back to Ireland as a bishop. He worked in the north and west, founded communities at Armagh and elsewhere, and died, by the most credible reconstruction, sometime in the second half of the fifth century.

What the hagiographic tradition (Muirchú's *Vita Patricii*, Tírechán's *Collectanea*) added to this was a narrative of triumph over druids, miraculous interventions, and the systematic conversion of the Irish kings. The historical figure resists this narrative. He was one of several missionaries working in Ireland in the fifth century — Palladius had been sent by Pope Celestine in 431 CE — and his primacy in the tradition reflects the later political needs of Armagh rather than historical dominance. He was also, by his own account, in some tension with the Irish church structures that existed before and alongside his mission.

The Patrick worth preserving is the one in the *Confessio*: the man who was afraid, who felt God had chosen him for reasons that seemed unfair, who loved the Irish people with a specificity — "even to death, if God should grant it" — that had clearly grown from those six years on the hillside with the sheep.

## Columcille (Columba), 521–597 CE

Born at Gartan in Donegal, of the Cenél Conaill, a branch of the Uí Néill. His secular rank at birth was close to the highest in Ireland, which is why his decision to take religious vows was politically significant: he removed himself from the succession, but he never stopped thinking and acting within the logic of the fine. Adomnán's *Vita Sancti Columbae* portrays a man of enormous social intelligence — capable of blessing a whale, composing poetry, mediating between kings, and managing the complex politics of a community that stretched from Ireland to the Scottish coast.

The manuscript-copying dispute — whether historical in its specific details or not — reflects something true about how Columcille operated: he moved within the brehon tradition's categories even when those categories worked against him. He accepted the judgment (however bitterly), underwent a form of penitential exile that was also a political calculation, and established at Iona a community that remained formally attached to Irish ecclesiastical structures rather than to Rome or to the Scottish church. Iona was Irish-administered from its founding until the Synod of Whitby in 664 CE changed the calculations.

He composed poetry in Irish that survives in fragments — "My hand is weary with writing, my sharp quill is not steady, its slender beak spits a dark-blue stream of blue-dark ink" — and this alone makes him unlike most historical founders of institutions: a man who could articulate what the physical act of writing felt like in his own body, from the inside.

He died on Iona in 597 CE, the same year Augustine of Canterbury arrived in England on Gregory the Great's mission. Two missions to the British Isles in the same year, from different directions, working by different methods, would spend the next century in productive and sometimes uncomfortable encounter.

## Adomnán, c. 627–704 CE

Ninth abbot of Iona; the most intellectually ambitious figure in the Irish church of the seventh century. He produced three major works: the *Vita Sancti Columbae* (biography of the founder, based on earlier materials and living memory from the community); *De Locis Sanctis* (a description of the Holy Land based on the testimony of a Frankish bishop who had been to Jerusalem and was stranded in Ireland on his way home — Adomnán turned his visitor's account into a geographic text that circulated across the Continent); and the *Lex Innocentium*, the Law of the Innocents, which he promulgated at the Synod of Birr in 697 CE.

The assembly at Birr was the largest gathering of authority the Irish world had yet convened for a legal purpose: ninety-one guarantors from both sides of the Irish Sea. The law's textual record includes the list of those guarantors, which is also a map of the political landscape of the Irish world in 697 CE: who had authority, where that authority was recognized, and which monasteries had established sufficient prestige to command attendance.

Adomnán was seventy when he promulgated the *Lex Innocentium*. He died seven years later, on Iona, having never resolved the paschal controversy (the Irish and Roman churches calculated Easter differently, and Iona's community was divided). His community's eventual adoption of the Roman method came after his death. He is not a figure of simple triumphs. He is a figure of sustained, careful, politically intelligent work in a world where every legal instrument required a community of trust to function.

## Brigid of Kildare, c. 451–525 CE

The historical Brigid is partly irrecoverable, because the textual tradition that recorded her life was also the tradition that deliberately managed the overlap between the Christian saint and the pre-Christian goddess of the same name. The fire that burned perpetually at Kildare is in both traditions. The miraculous healing of wounds, the transformation of water to ale for unexpected guests, the governance of the smith's craft — all of these appear in both the sacred biography of the saint and in the mythological record of the goddess. The monks who wrote Brigid's life down were not confused about this. They were maintaining a continuity that the community needed.

What is recoverable: Kildare was a major foundation by the sixth century, a double monastery (men and women, under a shared abbess) with jurisdiction over a bishop, which tells us something specific about Brigid's prestige within the Irish system. The abbess of Kildare held more honor-price than the bishop she administered, and the law recognized this. The foundation's location at the ridge above the Curragh — the great plain of Kildare — was not accidental: the Curragh was a place of assembly, of horse-racing, of seasonal gathering, and the monastery at its edge functioned as a center of legal, spiritual, and economic exchange in the same way a tuath center did.

The Brigid who matters for this chapter is the one who held both fires simultaneously — the smith's fire of the goddess and the Christian fire of the saint — and whose community did not require a resolution of the paradox, because in the Irish theological landscape of the fifth century, the paradox was not a problem to be solved but a truth to be maintained.

## The Anonymous Illuminators

The Book of Kells was not made by one hand. Art historians have identified at least three distinct artists in its production — distinguishable by their handling of the drapery folds in the evangelist portraits, the precision of the zoomorphic interlace in the decorated pages, and the color choices in the canon tables. They are known as the Goldsmith (responsible for the most technically demanding pages, including the Chi Rho), the Illustrator (responsible for the narrative scenes), and possibly a third artist responsible for some of the simpler decorated initials.

None of their names are known. This is not unusual in the tradition — the *Lindisfarne Gospels* also have an attribution only because a later scribe recorded it: "Eadfrith, Bishop of the Church of Lindisfarne, originally wrote this book." The Kells artists left no such record. They left the pages.

What can be said about the Goldsmith: the Chi Rho page required a mastery of at least six distinct pigment media (ultramarine, red lead, orpiment, kermes, verdigris, and the lead white used for highlighting), a sustained physical steadiness across a surface covered with forms no larger than a pinhead, and the ability to hold an image in the mind over the weeks or months required to complete a single page. The darkness of the scriptorium in winter. The rush light too close to the vellum. The breath controlled. The hand moving in a stillness that was also a form of thought.

They were making an argument about the Word. The interlace was the argument. That it required months of their lives to complete was not incidental to the argument — it was part of it.
