# Uyghurs — The Civilization of the Oasis

We are the Uyghur people — Türkic-speaking, steppe-born, and yet the Inner Asian people who most thoroughly made the transition from pastoral nomads to urban merchants and administrators. We defeated the Göktürk Second Khaganate in 744 CE and established our own khaganate in the Orkhon Valley, ruling the Mongolian steppe for nearly a century. Then the Yenisei Kirghiz drove us south in 840 CE, and what happened next is the part of our story that does not fit the usual steppe narrative: we did not dissolve. We moved to the oasis cities of the Tarim Basin and Ganzhou corridor, built sedentary kingdoms, converted to Manicheanism and then Buddhism, developed our script into the most important administrative alphabet in Inner Asian history, and became the sophisticated urban culture that the Mongols would eventually use as their administrative backbone.

The Uyghur script — derived from Sogdian — became the script in which the Mongol khans wrote their decrees, the script from which the Mongolian script descended, the script from which the Manchu script descended. When the Qing dynasty governed its empire in Manchu, the writing system that made that governance possible was ultimately Uyghur. Our contribution to Inner Asian civilization was not military power, though we had that; it was the bureaucratic, commercial, and scribal culture that allowed steppe empires to administer sedentary populations. We were the people who made the Inner Asian empire legible on paper.

## What Defined Them

**The Manichaean conversion.** In 762 CE, the Uyghur Khagan Bögü converted to Manichaeanism after encountering Manichaean missionaries during a campaign in China. This was not a casual religious choice: it was a political statement and a civilizational orientation. The Manichaean church provided the Uyghur court with a literate administrative class, a cosmopolitan religious network that connected to Sogdian merchants and the wider Iranian world, and a sophisticated intellectual tradition. Manichaeanism made the Uyghur court one of the most literate in Inner Asia. When the khaganate fell, Uyghur Manichaeanism survived in the Turfan oasis kingdoms for centuries, alongside Buddhism and Nestorian Christianity — the Turfan cave archives contain texts in all these traditions.

**The script that outlasted the empire.** The Uyghur script — adapted from the Sogdian alphabet, written vertically in the Mongolian tradition — became the most important administrative writing system in Inner Asian history. The Mongol khans adopted it for writing the Mongolian language; it became the Mongolian script that the Yuan, and later Qing, dynasties used for official communications; and a slightly modified form of it became the Manchu script. In the long arc of Inner Asian history, the Uyghur scribal tradition was the connective tissue of steppe administration.

**The Tang alliance and the silk exchange.** The Uyghur khaganate's relationship with the Tang dynasty was the most extensive commercial relationship between a steppe power and China until the Mongol period. The Uyghurs supplied cavalry that proved decisive in suppressing the An Lushan Rebellion (755–763 CE) — cavalry that saved the Tang dynasty from internal collapse. In return, the Tang paid in silk: hundreds of thousands of bolts per year, at prices that were increasingly unfavorable to the Chinese side. The Tang court resented the arrangement and the Uyghur leverage; the Uyghurs understood that they were the indispensable military ally and extracted accordingly.

**The sedentary transformation.** The most remarkable thing about the Uyghurs is what happened after the khaganate fell: they did not dissolve or assimilate into adjacent populations but reorganized as oasis-city kingdoms. The Turfan kingdom (Kingdom of Qocho) produced sophisticated Buddhist art, extensive manuscript collections in multiple languages, and a court culture that was simultaneously steppe-Türkic and Chinese-influenced and Indian-Buddhist. The Uyghur merchant networks extended across the Silk Road; Uyghur merchants and administrators were the commercial and literate class that the Mongol empire recruited to run its conquered territories.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Manichaean Mission:** The Manichaean priests arrive at the Uyghur court in 762 CE. The Khagan Bögü is not converting from nothing; there are already Sogdian merchants at court who practice Manichaeanism. But the formal conversion creates obligations: the Khagan must now protect Manichaean temples, patronize Manichaean priests, and present himself as a ruler of a civilized, book-religion polity rather than a simple steppe war-leader. The transition from warrior to patron begins.

**2 — The Tang Crisis:** The An Lushan Rebellion has burned the Tang capital. The Tang emperor needs cavalry. The Uyghur Khagan has cavalry, and he has a price: silk, at rates the Tang court finds extortionate, and the sack of Luoyang when the city is recaptured. The Tang emperor accepts. Uyghur cavalry retakes the capital. The Tang survives. The silk flows north for another eighty years.

**3 — Karabalghasun:** The Uyghur imperial capital on the Orkhon is a genuine city — walls, permanent buildings, Manichaean temples, a cosmopolitan population of Sogdian merchants, Chinese artisans, and Türkic warriors. An Arab traveler describes it with admiration. Then the Kirghiz come from the north and burn it. In 840 CE, the khaganate is over.

**4 — Turfan's Caves:** In the oasis kingdom of Turfan, the Uyghur scribes are copying texts: Buddhist sutras in Uyghur translation, Manichaean hymns, Chinese administrative manuals, fragments of the Sogdian commercial correspondence. Some of these manuscripts will be sealed in a cave at Dunhuang and not found again until 1900. They constitute one of the richest archives of Inner Asian civilization.

**5 — Uyghur Service to the Mongols:** Chinggis Khan's Uyghur advisors bring their script and their administrative experience to the new empire. Uyghur secretaries write the decrees that govern the Mongol conquests. The Uyghur script becomes the Mongolian script. The Uyghur administrative tradition becomes the Mongol administrative tradition. The people who were defeated by the Kirghiz have outlasted the Kirghiz by providing the Mongols with the tools of governance.
