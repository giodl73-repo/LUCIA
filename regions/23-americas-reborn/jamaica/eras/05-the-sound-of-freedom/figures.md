# Figures — Era 5: The Sound of Freedom

## Bob Marley (Nesta Robert Marley, 1945–1981)

He was born in Nine Mile, Saint Ann Parish, to a white English father he barely knew and a Jamaican Black mother who raised him. The mixed parentage was a source of specific pain: in the yard, some boys called him half-caste. Among Jamaicans who had internalized the colonial skin-color hierarchy, the lighter-skinned child of a white man was ambiguous in ways that straightforward status could not resolve. This did not produce in Marley a man of uncertain identity. It produced a man who understood from childhood that the racial categories Babylon constructed were both real in their effects and false in their premises. He knew the inside of both sides. This made him a better theologian than someone who had only known one.

He was not a gentle man in all respects. He was demanding of his musicians, often paid poorly, was a serial philanderer who fathered children by multiple women outside his marriage. He was also capable of extraordinary generosity — free concerts, food distributions, money given without accounting. He was a man, not a symbol, and the history of making him a symbol has done his music a disservice by suggesting that saints make prophets. In the Rastafari tradition, the prophet speaks what Jah requires regardless of the prophet's personal character. The message is not guaranteed by the messenger's virtue.

What he was certain of: the music was not his. It was Jah's. He was the instrument through which it moved. This conviction — held not as humility but as factual description — gave the music its character. A man promoting himself sounds different from a man transmitting. Marley was transmitting.

His body: compact, athletic, the footballer's build he maintained through every tour with daily matches before the soundcheck. His hands on the guitar: not technically brilliant in the classical sense, but completely authoritative in the rhythmic sense — he played the skank with the same certainty with which he stated theology. His voice: a tenor that sat in the chest before it rose to the head, warm in its lower register, urgent in its upper one. When he reached for the top of his range, it cost him something, and the cost was audible. That was the point.

He died at thirty-six. He had not seen Africa as a home — only as a concert destination. Zion was still where it had been in 1963: on the horizon, orienting, not yet arrived. He would have said: this is how prophecy works. You point at the horizon. Someone else arrives there.

## Rita Marley (Alpharita Constantia Anderson, born 1946)

Born in Santiago de Cuba to Jamaican parents, raised in Trench Town. She was performing with a group called the Soulettes when she met Bob through the Wailers' circle. They married in 1966, the day before Bob left for a nine-month stay in Delaware with his mother, who had remarried and moved to the United States. Rita stayed in Kingston and held the yard.

She was shot in the head on December 3, 1976. A bullet entered at the back of her skull and traveled along the outside of the bone without penetrating the brain. She drove herself and the other wounded to the hospital. She sang at the Smile Jamaica concert two days later with the wound still fresh, a bandage at her temple, and Bob's wound not yet fully healed.

The I-Threes — Rita, Judy Mowatt, Marcia Griffiths — are the harmonic architecture of the Wailers' major albums. "No Woman, No Cry." "Exodus." "Jamming." The voices that answer Marley's voice, that hold the harmonic space he moves through, that represent the community speaking back to the prophet: these are the I-Threes. Without them the albums are incomplete in a structural sense. The music requires the call and the response.

After Marley's death in 1981, Rita became the executor of his estate, the administrator of his legacy, the person who determined how the music would be used and by whom. This was not a smooth process — there were years of legal disputes over the estate. She built the Bob Marley Foundation and the Bob Marley Museum at 56 Hope Road. She raised the children. She continued to record and perform. She is the reason the yard has remained legible as a site of meaning rather than merely a tourist attraction.

The bullet is still in her head. The doctors decided the risk of surgery exceeded the risk of leaving it. She has carried it for fifty years.

## Peter Tosh (Winston Hubert McIntosh, 1944–1987)

The most political of the three. Where Marley's message was universal in its reach — Babylon will fall; all the suffering people of the world are his people — Tosh was specific. He named colonialism. He named white supremacy. He named the governments and systems that maintained the arrangement. He did not soften this for international audiences. His solo album Equal Rights (1977) opened with a track called "Get Up, Stand Up" (which he had written with Marley) and proceeded through "Equal Rights," "African," "Downpressor Man" — not metaphor, not allegory. The downpressor is the oppressor. Named.

He was beaten by police in Kingston in 1978 — a confrontation over a traffic stop that became a sustained beating in a police station. He wrote about it. He performed about it. He was not broken by it.

He was shot in his home in Westmoreland on September 11, 1987, by men who said they were robbing him. The political dimensions of the killing were discussed in Jamaica and never resolved. He was forty-two. He had made seven studio albums. He had refused every compromise the international music market offered.

His guitar: a Flying V, white, associated with hard rock in the American context, used by Tosh to play reggae with a sharpness that the rounder guitar tones of the era did not provide. The sharp attack matched the sharp politics. He is the small axe, honed past what the song metaphorizes — the actual cutting edge of the tradition, the part that drew blood.

## Bunny Wailer (Neville O'Riley Livingston, 1947–2021)

He left the Wailers in 1973 rather than tour internationally. This was not withdrawal. It was commitment: the yard was where the reasoning happened, and the yard required presence. He remained in Jamaica. He made Blackheart Man (1976), widely considered one of the greatest reggae albums recorded, and then continued to record and perform within Jamaica and the Caribbean for the rest of his life. He outlived both of his original bandmates by decades.

He was the deepest in the theology and the quietest about it publicly. Where Marley explained and Tosh declared, Bunny Wailer reasoned — in the original sense, the sitting-in-a-circle sense. The music he made was for the community that made the music, not primarily for export. This was not a failure of ambition. It was a different understanding of what the music was for.

He died in Kingston on March 2, 2021, of a stroke. He was seventy-three. He was the last of the three. The yard was his all along.

## Lee "Scratch" Perry (Rainford Hugh Perry, 1936–2021)

The producer who made the sound. He worked at Studio One under Coxsone Dodd before establishing his own studio — the Black Ark — in Washington Gardens, Kingston, in 1973. At the Black Ark he produced the records that defined roots reggae: the Wailers' early recordings, Junior Murvin's "Police and Thieves," the Congos' Heart of the Congos, Max Romeo's War Ina Babylon. The list is the canon.

His method: he spoke to the equipment. He inscribed symbols on the walls of the studio. He poured rum on the mixing desk as offering. He believed — or enacted the belief, the distinction may not have mattered — that the studio was a spiritual instrument and that the recordings it produced were transmissions from Jah through him into the vinyl. The reverb he applied was not effect. It was the dimension behind the dimension.

He burned the Black Ark in 1979 or allowed it to burn. He said different things about it at different times. Some accounts say he set the fire deliberately when he felt the studio had been spiritually compromised. Others say it was an accident. Perry himself, in interviews across the following decades, told stories that contradicted each other and may all be simultaneously true in the way that spiritual events are sometimes true: not in the factual record but in what they reveal about the person who experienced them.

He died in Lucea, Hanover, Jamaica, on August 29, 2021, five months after Bunny Wailer. He was eighty-five. The last surviving architect of the sound.

## Haile Selassie I (Tafari Makonnen, 1892–1975)

He did not ask to be the center of a theology organized by people he had never met, in a country he had not visited until 1966. When a delegation of Rastafari elders arrived in Ethiopia that April for a state visit — hundreds of thousands of Jamaicans gathered at the airport — accounts say he wept when he saw the crowd and understood what had been built around him. He was a monarch, educated in European courts, who governed through a court system that combined modernization with imperial autocracy. He was not a simple symbol of liberation. He was a complex political figure who had survived the Italian occupation (1936–1941), led Ethiopia through the post-war period, and maintained power through methods that included repression of his own people.

None of this contradicts what Rastafari made of him. The theological figure and the historical figure exist in different registers. Selassie the historical monarch was deposed by the Derg in 1974 and likely murdered in 1975. Selassie the theological figure — Jah, the Conquering Lion, the returned Messiah — does not die. The tradition adapted. He became more fully Jah by dying, which is what Jah does: transcends the body's end. The reasoning communities worked this out in the yards, without a council, without a pope, through the distributed method that the Rastafari tradition had always used to produce theology. The decentralized system produced a coherent answer: Jah does not lose. The king dies. Jah lives.

His League of Nations speech of June 30, 1936, after the Italian invasion of Ethiopia, is one of the foundational documents of anti-colonial rhetoric: "It is us today. It will be you tomorrow." Marley set his words to music in "War" — not paraphrase, but the speech itself, verbatim, as song. The speech was already music. It had been waiting for the instrument.
