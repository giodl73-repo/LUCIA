# BOARD Review — D.T. Potts
## Near Eastern Archaeology, Proto-Elamite and Elamite Studies
## University of Sydney / Institute for the Study of the Ancient World, NYU

---

The chapter handles the proto-Elamite scribal system with commendable accuracy and appropriate epistemic caution. Several specific points to note and one factual clarification required.

**Factual clarification required:**

The chapter states that proto-Elamite "ran for six hundred years, from approximately 3200 to 2600 BCE." This is broadly accurate for the core period of use, but the terminal date should be softened slightly — proto-Elamite does not simply stop in 2600 BCE; the transition is more gradual and some sites show later use of elements of the system. The phrasing "the system was in active use from approximately 3200 to 2600 BCE, after which it falls from the administrative record" would be more precise. This is a minor calibration, not a fundamental error.

**On the geographic extent claim:**

The chapter calls proto-Elamite "the widest geographically distributed writing system of its era." This is defensible as of current scholarship — the sites range from Susa to Shahr-i Sokhta (in modern Afghanistan) to sites in the Kerman region. However, it should be acknowledged as a contextual claim: we are comparing it to early Sumerian writing, which in the same period was more geographically concentrated in southern Mesopotamia. The claim is accurate but contextually specific; the chapter would benefit from the small addition "in comparison to the contemporary Sumerian system, which remained concentrated in the southern Mesopotamian alluvial plain." This prevents the reader from forming a misleading impression about writing systems generally.

**On the dual numerical bases:**

The chapter correctly notes the two-base counting system. To be precise for the record: proto-Elamite appears to use a bisexagesimal system for certain commodities and a sexagesimal system for others — the two bases are not simply "discrete objects vs. measured quantities" in all cases, as the system's internal logic is still being worked out by researchers including Dahl and Englund. The chapter's formulation ("one sign for grain counted by the unit; a different numerical base for grain measured by volume") is a reasonable simplification that is defensible at the level of narrative history. I would not change it but wanted it noted.

**On Puzur-Inshushinak and Linear Elamite:**

The treatment is accurate. The dating (c. 2100 BCE) is appropriate given the uncertainty. The characterization of Linear Elamite as a prestige script is consistent with current interpretations. The reference to Desset et al.'s 2022 decipherment work is correctly flagged as contested — this is the appropriate level of epistemic caution for a claim that remains under active scholarly debate.

**On Kindattu and the destruction of Ur:**

The chapter correctly avoids attributing the fall of Ur III to Elamite action alone. The dual pressure of Amorites and Elamites is historically accurate. The name "Kindattu" is taken from Sumerian sources — it is an Elamite name written in Sumerian script, which is the only form in which it survives. The chapter's treatment of this source asymmetry is not just editorially honest; it is archaeologically accurate.

**On the sukkalmah system:**

The tripartite structure (sukkalmah / sukkal of Elam and Simashki / sukkal of Susa) is accurately described. The succession logic is correctly characterized: the system was designed for lateral succession through the family rather than strict patrilineal descent. Atta-hushu as a named sukkalmah with diplomatic connections to Ur is confirmed in the texts.

**Summary:**

Two minor calibrations noted (proto-Elamite terminal date, geographic distribution qualifier). Neither requires substantial revision. The chapter's archaeological foundation is sound. The handling of the source situation — multiple undeciphered scripts treated as evidence rather than obstacle — reflects the current state of the field accurately. The chapter does not overclaim on decipherment and does not underclaim on what the tablets demonstrate even without full reading.

**Verdict: APPROVED with two minor calibrations. Apply at final revision.**
