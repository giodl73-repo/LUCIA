# Era 2: The Portuguese Encounter — Afonso I
## Opening

The letter is written in Latin.

This is the first fact, and it is not a small one. It is the year 1526 of the Christian era — the sixteenth year of the reign of Manikongo Dom Afonso I, lord of the Kongo kingdom, master of Mbanza Kongo, ruler of lands whose full extent no map in Lisbon has yet accurately drawn. The letter goes to Dom João III, King of Portugal, and it is composed with care. The secretary who holds the quill has been trained in Lisbon. The Latin is correct. The arguments are precise. The king knows what he is saying, and he knows that the King of Portugal will be able to read it, because they share, Afonso believes, a faith that obligates them both.

He writes: *And we cannot reckon how great the damage is, since the mentioned merchants are taking every day our natives, sons of the land and the sons of our noblemen and vassals and our relatives, because the thieves and men of bad conscience grab them wishing to have the things and wares of this Kingdom which they are ambitious of...*

He is writing about the slave trade. He is writing to the man who profits from it.

There is nothing naive about this letter. Afonso I has been watching the Portuguese for forty years. He was born before they came. He became a Christian — not because a missionary pressed a cross into his hands and he yielded, but because he saw in Christianity something that mapped onto the Kongo world's deepest categories: the intersection of the living and the dead, the covenant made visible in sacred objects, the power that flowed between heaven and earth. The crucifix looked, to a Kongo eye, like the cosmogram — the crossed lines that the Bakongo drew on the ground to mark the place where the two worlds met. He chose baptism as a young man, took the name Afonso, fought a civil war to claim the throne from a brother who had not converted, and won. He took his victory as confirmation. God had ratified his kingship.

He then built. He built a cathedral in Mbanza Kongo, which the Portuguese called São Salvador — the Savior — and which was, when Afonso's masons finished it, among the finest stone churches in all of Africa, and one of the few built by African craftsmen to an African ruler's commission. He sent young nobles to Lisbon and to Rome to be educated — his own son, Henrique, went to Rome, studied theology, returned, and was consecrated by Pope Leo X as the Bishop of Utica and Auxiliary of Kongo, the first sub-Saharan African bishop in the Catholic church. He established schools. He corresponded with the Vatican. He maintained, in Mbanza Kongo, a court that could conduct diplomatic business in Latin and Portuguese and that knew the protocols of Christian sovereignty as well as any minor European principality.

He was not trying to become European. He was trying to use European legal and theological categories to bind the Europeans. If the covenant of Christianity was real — if Dom João was in fact a Christian king, bound by the same moral architecture that Afonso had staked his reign upon — then the covenant obligated something. It obligated the slave traders to stop seizing Kongo's free subjects. It obligated the King of Portugal to enforce the laws he had been petitioned to enforce. The letter of 1526 is not a supplication. It is an argument from first principles. Afonso is invoking the shared framework that, he believes, neither of them can repudiate without confessing that their faith is theater.

Outside the palace at Mbanza Kongo, in the year the letter is written, the trade is operating. At Mpinda, at the mouth of the Congo River, the Portuguese traders have established a presence, and the goods moving through that point are not only the copper and ivory and raffia cloth that the Kongo kingdom produces. They are people. They are Kongo subjects — people who should be, under the legal categories of both Kongo sovereignty and Christian ethics, free. They have been seized by raiders, sold by provincial governors who have learned that a captive body is worth more to a Portuguese slaver than a year of ordinary trade, passed down a chain of exchange that ends on a ship heading west.

The king knows this. He has tried to stop it. He has prohibited the traders from traveling inland without his permission. He has tried to impose a committee of inspection to regulate what the Portuguese take out of his kingdom. The Portuguese have ignored these measures, or worked around them, or simply continued as if the Manikongo's laws applied only to people who chose to obey them.

So he writes a letter.

He writes in Latin so that it cannot be misunderstood. He writes in the formal register of a sovereign addressing a sovereign. He writes in the language of the Church because the Church is the institution that both of them, in theory, answer to. He is asking Dom João to enforce the covenant. He is asking, politely and precisely, for justice — not as a subject petitioning a master, but as one Christian king asking another to honor what they both profess to believe.

The letter exists. It sits today in the Portuguese national archives, the Arquivo Nacional da Torre do Tombo, in Lisbon. Its Latin is correct. Its arguments are intact. Its grievances are documented. The reply that came, when it came, was not adequate to what had been asked.

But the letter was written. Afonso I wrote it, and in writing it, he did something that no amount of slave raiding could undo: he put the argument on record. He said, in the language of the people who would later claim that Africa had no history worth recording, that this was wrong, and that they knew it was wrong, and that their own faith condemned them for it.

The ships left Mpinda with their cargo. The letter went to Lisbon. And in Mbanza Kongo, the Manikongo waited, in his cathedral, under his mpu, for a reply that would honor the covenant he believed they shared.
