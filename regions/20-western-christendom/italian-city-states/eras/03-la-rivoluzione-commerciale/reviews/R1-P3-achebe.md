# R1-P3 — Chinua Achebe — Panel Review, Round 1

**Text:** Opening — La Rivoluzione Commerciale
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

I read this looking for the moment the author's own vantage point becomes visible. I did not find it. That is the highest compliment I give.

Berto di Iacopo Frescobaldi does not think of the bill of exchange as a revolution. This single authorial choice is the opening's greatest achievement. The writer has refused the temptation — and it is a powerful temptation in writing about the commercial revolution — to stand outside the culture and marvel at how modern these medieval merchants were. Berto does not know he is building the infrastructure of capitalism. He is solving a practical problem: how to move value across dangerous roads without carrying coin. His perspective is the text's perspective. The culture owns the story.

The Voice is right. The counting house rhythm — precise, unsentimental, transactional — belongs to the people being described. A Florentine merchant of the 1290s would recognize himself in this prose, not in the way that he would say "yes, that is accurate" but in the way that he would say "yes, that is how I think." This is the standard.

**What I would watch carefully in the full chapter:**

The three-city structure (Florence, Venice, Genoa) creates a risk. Each city had its own political culture, its own identity, its own way of understanding itself. Florentines were Florentines, not Italians; Venetians were Venetians above all; Genoese were violently Genoese. If the chapter treats them as interchangeable examples of "the commercial revolution," it will lose the particular in favor of the general — and the particular is where the dignity lives.

The commune as a political form is described from the outside in this opening ("the communes have built..."). The chapter must find its way inside that identity — not the commune as a modern scholar describes it, but the commune as a citizen of Milan or Pisa or Bologna understood it. The sworn association, the collective oath, the pride in self-governance against the emperor's claims. These people were not building capitalism. They were asserting their honor as free men against those who would make them subjects.

**The question I would push on:**

Whose voice is carrying the chapter? The opening gives us Berto's perspective. The middle sections risk drifting into the voice of a scholar explaining the commercial revolution. The chapter must find human beings — named, placed, making decisions that make sense in their own terms — for each of its major movements. The Venice section and the Genoa section need their own Bertos, their own counting-house moments.
