## The Naming Problem

The notes correctly identify this as the chapter's most sensitive issue. "Kush" is the Egyptian name. "Kerma" is what modern archaeologists call the site. Neither name belongs to the people themselves. The chapter must acknowledge this gap in the first section, and it must do so without treating the gap as a deficit. Many peoples' self-designations are lost. The loss is the record's, not the people's.

Suggested approach: acknowledge the naming problem once, clearly, early. Then proceed using "Kerma" (as the less imperial of the two names) without apology. Do not repeatedly remind the reader that we do not know their name. One honest acknowledgment is dignity. Repeated reminders become a kind of mourning that centers the author's regret rather than the culture's story.

## Section 5 Risk

The "Egypt Watches" section is the highest-risk section for voice ownership. When the Egyptian perspective enters, the chapter must not shift into the Egyptian frame. The Execration Texts and the fortress evidence should be presented as evidence of Egyptian anxiety, not as the primary narrative. The culture being described is Kerma. Egypt is a character in Kerma's story, not the other way around. If Section 5 reads as "Egypt's experience of Kerma," it has failed. It should read as "what the Egyptian evidence reveals about what Kerma was."

## Retainer Sacrifice — Final Guidance

The notes' approach is correct. One additional thought: do not use the word "sacrifice" without qualification. "Sacrifice" implies a ritual framework that may or may not apply. The archaeological term is "retainer burial." The chapter should describe what happened (people went into the mound and did not come out) and let the reader supply whatever framework they choose. The culture's own framework is not recoverable. Honoring that silence is more honest than filling it with our categories.
