# Panel Review — R2-P3: Chinua Achebe (Cultural Voice & Ownership)

**Production Bible: Iljegangjeongi (Japanese Colonization, 1910–1945)**  
**Date: 2026-04-16**

---

## The Question

Does this culture own this story? Will the chapter be told from inside Korean understanding, or will there be subtle slides into outside judgment?

---

## The Assessment

**MOSTLY STRONG. ONE CRITICAL QUESTION: WHAT IS KIM-SA'S NAME?**

Achebe notes that the opening gives us a woman without a name, and the notes promise that "the reader should have named her (internally, at least) and should feel the erasure" when the soshi-kaimei is implemented.

But the production bible *itself* does not name her. Throughout the notes, she is "the haegeum player" or "Kim-sa" — but is "Kim-sa" her name, or is it a placeholder the author created?

If it is a placeholder, the chapter will fail the authenticity test. The reader will feel that this is a *constructed character*, not a *discovered one*.

Achebe recommends: **before the chapter is written, establish Kim-sa's actual Korean name — her given name, not just her family name.** Let it be a name that means something in Korean, that carries weight, that will be felt as a loss when it is erased.

Then, in the chapter's Section 2, when the soshi-kaimei is implemented, the reader will know what is being taken.

---

## The Question I'd Push On

**The voice in Section 3: "What the Schools Cannot Teach"**

The production bible rightly says the chapter will show contrast: the sterile official schooling versus the rich secret teaching. But the section plan notes:

> "The section shows how the official schooling, sterile and Japanese; the secret teaching, rich and Korean."

Achebe hears something dangerous here: the voice might slide into *judgment* between the two systems. "Sterile" and "rich" are *evaluative words*. They tell the reader what to think.

Instead, Achebe wants the chapter to *show both systems* without judgment. Let the Japanese schoolteacher teach in Japanese with genuine care for her students. Let the secret haegeum teacher teach in Korean with genuine care. The reader should feel the difference, not be told it.

From inside Korean understanding, what would the *reader* feel? The reader would feel: *these are two languages, two ways of knowing, and I must learn to move between them*. Not: *one is sterile and one is rich*.

This is a subtle distinction, but it matters for the voice.

---

## The Question I'd Recommend

**Young-Sook's bilingual identity**

The notes say: "code-switching: one self at school, another at home."

Achebe says: *make this the spine of her characterization*. Don't just mention that she code-switches. *Show her code-switching in her own voice*.

When she is at school, teach the reader Korean through her *suppressed Korean*. When she is at home, teach the reader Japanese through her *constrained Japanese*. Let the reader feel what it is like to be a person divided across two languages.

This is not "she struggles with her identity" (outside judgment). This is "she lives in two languages" (inside experience). The voice should embody both.

---

## Strong Points

The production bible correctly identifies that the chapter should show:

1. **Park Min-jun as active resistance** — not all Koreans were passive. Some fought openly, were captured, tortured, and released broken.

2. **The comfort women system with gravity and honesty** — not sensationalized, but not minimized. The section plan says: "The chapter handles this with the gravity it requires, but without sensationalism." This is exactly right.

3. **The terminal condition (liberation through external event)** — this is exceptional. Most narratives of colonization are triumphalist or tragic. This one is *honest about contingency*.

---

## Recommendation

**PASS WITH CRITICAL CONDITION.** The production bible is strong. But before the chapter is written, answer this question: **What is Kim-sa's actual Korean name, and what does it mean?** Let the name carry weight. Let its erasure be felt.

With that one clarification, the chapter will be told entirely from inside Korean understanding.
