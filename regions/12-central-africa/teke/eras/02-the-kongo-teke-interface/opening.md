# The Kongo-Teke Interface

The man came out of the forest with copper.

He came the way forest traders always came — through the manioc gardens on the plateau's eastern edge, where the high grass thinned and the red laterite road began, his carriers strung out behind him with the loads wrapped in leaves and bound with vine. He smelled of smoke and river water and the particular green-dark smell of the forest interior that lingered on a man for days after he emerged. The broker at the road's margin watched him walk.

The copper came in cross-shaped ingots, each one a hand's span across, the weight of them settled into the wicker frames the carriers bore on their heads. The broker had seen copper before. He had seen copper from the Katanga workings in the east, copper that had traveled six hundred kilometers along the river networks, passing through a dozen hands before it reached the plateau. He had also seen copper that claimed to be Katanga and was not. He waited until the trader was close enough, then held out his hand.

The man gave him one of the ingots without being asked. This was the negotiation's first moment, and both of them knew it. A man who demanded to be asked was a man who wanted to make you wait; a man who handed over the goods without ceremony was a man who understood the terms.

The broker turned it in his hands. The Katanga copper had a particular weight to it, a particular red-brown surface color that no local smelting could exactly replicate, and a sound when struck — not hollow, not too bright — that experienced hands learned to hear. He struck it against his ring. He turned it over. The casting seam ran where it should. He passed it back.

"How many?" he said.

Twenty ingots, the trader said. And he wanted iron.

The broker already knew, in the back of his head, what twenty Katanga copper ingots would fetch at the Malebo Pool. He had been making this calculation since the man appeared on the road, the same way a man with his eyes open makes calculations — not deliberately, but as a habit of sight, the numbers running quietly underneath everything else. The iron he could source from the Kongo merchants who brought tools up from the coast and would trade them at a price that reflected the cost of transport plus their own judgment of what the plateau market would bear. The gap between what the iron cost him and what the copper would earn — that was his cut, and his cut was why the road ran through his market and not around it.

This was the broker's art. Not to move goods — anyone could move goods. To stand in the middle. To know the price on the coast before the forest man arrived, and to know the price in the forest before the coastal man appeared, and to let neither of them know what the other knew. The Teke had been doing this on the Bateke Plateau for as long as anyone's grandfather could remember, and the plateau itself made it possible: high ground between two worlds, neither forest nor coast, belonging to both exactly as much as was necessary and no more.

The trader's name was something the broker would not remember. The copper was real. That was the relevant fact.

He made the man wait while he walked to the back of the compound and counted out the iron tools — hoes, two adzes, a length of bar stock that the forest smiths could work into whatever they needed. He laid them out where the trader could see. He watched the man's eyes do the same calculation his own had done. He had priced it correctly. The man did not flinch, and he did not gloat. A man who gloated at the price told the other man the price was wrong.

They agreed. The copper came off the carriers' heads and was stacked in the compound, and the iron was wrapped and loaded. The trader's men drank water from the clay pots at the road's edge and sat in the afternoon shade, and the broker sat with them for a while in the way that was expected, and they talked about the forest roads — which river was high, which stretch of track had been disputed lately, whether the Mongo traders from up the river were pushing their prices — and the broker listened carefully to everything and said little, because information about the forest road was worth as much as copper, and it cost nothing to hear it.

He had, at that moment, buyers waiting in three directions: the Kongo provincial market two days south, where iron tools were always short and the agents of the Manikongo's governors paid in cloth and shell beads; the pool market to the southwest, where the big river canoes came and the prices moved with the water; and a buyer from the east, a Luba trader who had been passing through the plateau market for three seasons and wanted copper for reasons the broker did not need to understand. He would find the best of these. He would not tell any of them about the others.

The forest man left before sundown, his carriers moving north again with their loads lighter. The broker went inside and added twenty cross-shaped ingots to what was already stacked against the wall and thought about the river.

The pool was always the answer. But the pool was not always the price.
