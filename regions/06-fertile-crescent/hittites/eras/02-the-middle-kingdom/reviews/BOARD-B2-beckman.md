## B-2 — Gary Beckman — Board Review of Hittites Era 2

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Hittite diplomatic and legal texts; standard editions of treaties and ritual texts; specialist in the panku assembly.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The treaty form (historical preamble, obligations, divine witnesses, curses) is accurately described. The chapter correctly notes that the divine witnesses enforced, not merely observed.
- The panku's role is handled appropriately. The chapter originally named me in the text, which was removed during CLEAN; this is the correct decision. The scholarly debate about the panku's authority is real but belongs in the notes, not in the narrative voice.
- The edict's provisions (succession rules: eldest son of first wife, then second wife, then daughter's husband) are correctly summarized.
- The Hittite Laws provisions cited (compensation for homicide: four persons to the household; plow ox replacement; vineyard repair) are accurate and reflect the Middle Kingdom recension of the laws.
- The connection between blood-feud regulation in the laws and succession regulation in the edict is a legitimate analytical point. Both address the same structural problem.
- The judicial reform (capital cases reserved for the king) is correctly attributed to the Middle Kingdom period.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The treaty form is presented from inside the Hittite understanding: the covenant as a fact in the world, binding because the gods heard it. This is correct. The Hittites did not conceptualize treaties as mere political agreements. They were sacred instruments.
- The edict's adaptation of the treaty form is correctly analyzed. The historical preamble becoming confession, the curse clause becoming demonstrated precedent: these structural parallels are sound.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The central argument that Telipinu turned the treaty form inward is the chapter's strongest contribution. This is a genuine insight correctly developed.
- The edict quotations are accurate renderings of the text.
- The honest acknowledgment that we do not know whether the panku ever actually confronted a king is important and correctly placed.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter states that the edict addresses the assembly as "You are his court." The more precise translation is "you are his panku" or "you are his assembly." The word "court" introduces a slight ambiguity (royal court vs. judicial court vs. assembly). Minor; the meaning is clear from context.

**Verdict:** APPROVED
