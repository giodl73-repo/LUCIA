## Operational Accuracy — Coen and Batavia

The chapter's treatment of Coen is operationally accurate and commendably precise about what the primary sources say. The quotation "there's nothing in the world that gives a better right than power and force added to right" is an accurate paraphrase of Coen's documented correspondence, though I note it is a paraphrase rather than a direct translation. The Dutch original runs: "daer is niets ter wereld dat ons beter recht geeft als macht en cracht naest het recht" — more precisely: "there is nothing in the world that gives us better right than power and strength added to right." The distinction between "force" and "strength" is minor in context; both are accurate to Coen's meaning. If the chapter uses it as a direct quotation (presented with quotation marks), it should note it is in translation. As a paraphrase, it is accurate.

**On Batavia's founding:** The chapter describes Batavia as built "on the ruins of Jayakarta." This is accurate: Coen destroyed Jayakarta in 1619 and constructed Batavia on the same site. The chapter's description of the canal grid as a "catastrophe" for tropical conditions is also accurate and documented in the VOC's own records; European mortality in early Batavia was indeed catastrophic by any measure. The one-third mortality figure within six months is within the documented range for particularly bad years; I would note this is at the high end and the chapter should use "up to a third" or "in the worst years, a third" to be more precise.

**On Sultan Agung:** The chapter correctly identifies Sultan Agung as the Sultan of Mataram and correctly notes the 1628 and 1629 sieges. The figure of "around eighty thousand men" for the 1628 siege is within the range of scholarly estimates (some sources give sixty thousand, some one hundred thousand); the figures.md treatment is appropriately hedged. Agung's death is not mentioned; he died in 1645, which is outside the chapter's scope. No correction needed.

**On Coen's death:** The chapter states Coen "died of disease in Batavia in 1629, in office, at forty-two." This is correct: Coen died of cholera during Sultan Agung's second siege, on September 21, 1629, at age forty-two. The detail about continuing to work at his desk during the siege is consistent with contemporary accounts.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

The chapter renders Coen accurately from the inside: a man who believed what he wrote and wrote it without apology. The description of his background (born in Hoorn, trained as a bookkeeper in Rome) is accurate. The characterization of him as "a precise, impatient man, intolerant of incompetence, and absolutely certain of his analysis" is consistent with what the primary sources show, though "absolutely certain" risks sliding toward characterization the sources don't quite support — Coen's letters show uncertainty on personnel questions and logistical details. The strategic certainty is documented; the psychological portrait is a reasonable inference. I would soften "absolutely certain" to "confident" in the figures.md treatment.

The chapter's final assessment of Coen ("he is not a monster in these pages because he was not a monster in his own understanding") is the correct approach and consistent with how serious scholarship now treats him.
