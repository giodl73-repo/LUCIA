# Khazars — The Buffer Kingdom

We are the Khazars — the Türkic-speaking confederation of the northern Caspian steppe, the polity that held the boundary between the Islamic caliphate to the south and the Slavic and Bulgar worlds to the north and west for four centuries. We are famous for one thing above all others: our ruling class converted to Judaism, making the Khazar Khaganate the only Türkic state in history to adopt Judaism as its official religion. This conversion — documented in the Khazar correspondence of the tenth century, in Arab geographical sources, and in the Hebrew texts of the Cairo Geniza — has generated centuries of speculation about its extent, its motives, and its significance. We will tell you what we know: the conversion was real, it lasted, and it defined our identity for as long as the khaganate existed.

Our position on the map was our geopolitical destiny: we sat at the Caucasus gateway, the narrow corridor between the Caspian Sea and the Black Sea through which armies from the south (Arab, later Persian) had to pass to reach the north. We stopped the Arab advance at the Battle of the Ardabil Pass in 730 CE and again in the campaigns that followed, preventing the Islamic caliphate from expanding into the Pontic steppe and the Slavic heartland. The Byzantine Empire — which had its own reasons to prevent Islamic encirclement — maintained close diplomatic relations with us, sent a bishop to our court, and proposed multiple royal marriages. We were the pivot on which the northern Eurasian balance of power turned.

## What Defined Them

**The Jewish conversion.** The Khazar ruling class's adoption of Judaism — documented in the famous correspondence between the Khazar Khagan Joseph and the Spanish Jewish scholar Hasdai ibn Shaprut in the 960s — was explained in Khazar tradition as the outcome of a religious debate between representatives of Islam, Christianity, and Judaism. Whether or not this framing reflects the actual decision process, the conversion's political logic is clear: adopting neither the religion of the Islamic caliphate to the south nor the Christianity of Byzantium to the west allowed the Khazars to maintain genuine independence from both great powers while engaging commercially with both. Judaism was the religion of a third tradition — one with no imperial ambitions — that left the Khazars free to be themselves.

**The dual kingship.** The Khazar political system featured two kings: the Khagan, the sacred ceremonial ruler of divine lineage who was not permitted to exercise real power and who might be ritually killed if the realm suffered major disasters; and the Bek (or Shad), the military and administrative ruler who actually governed. This dual-kingship structure appears in other Inner Asian polities and reflects a distinction between sacral authority and practical governance that the steppe tradition developed independently. The Khagan was the Tengri-blessed figure of legitimacy; the Bek was the man who ran the army and collected the taxes.

**The commercial entrepôt.** The Khazar capital Itil at the Volga delta was one of the great commercial cities of the early medieval world. Arab geographers described it as containing separate quarters for Muslims (with mosques), Christians (with churches), and Jews (with synagogues), presided over by Khazar officials who collected duties on all trade that passed through. The Volga route — from the Baltic through the Rus river system to the Caspian and thence to Iran and Central Asia — was the main commercial artery of the northern world, and the Khazars taxed and protected it at its southern bottleneck. The furs, slaves, and honey from the north were exchanged for the silk, silver, and spices of the south.

**The Arab-Khazar wars.** The great Arab campaigns against the Khazars in the late seventh and early eighth centuries were some of the most intensive military operations of the early Islamic period. The Arab general Marwan ibn Muhammad led campaigns deep into the Khazar heartland — some accounts say he reached the Volga — but could not convert the strategic victories into lasting conquest. The Caucasus Mountain passes were too narrow for large-scale occupation, and the Khazars could simply retreat into the steppe when pressed. The Arab failure at the Caucasus gateway preserved northern Europe from direct Islamic conquest during the period when the caliphate was at its most expansive.

## The Chapters

**1 — Stopping the Arabs:** The Arab army has come through the Derbent pass and onto the Caspian steppe. The Khazar Khagan's forces meet them. The battle is bloody on both sides. The Arabs cannot sustain the campaign across the long supply lines from their base in Azerbaijan. They withdraw. The Caucasus holds. The northern world will not be Arab — at least not yet.

**2 — The Conversion:** The khagan has been listening to the religious debate for three days. The Muslim scholar, the Christian bishop, and the Jewish rabbi have each presented their case. He turns to the Muslim and asks: between Islam and Christianity, which is the better religion? Islam, says the Muslim, of course. He turns to the Christian: between Christianity and Judaism, which is better? Christianity, says the Christian. He turns to the rabbi: both your rivals have endorsed Judaism as second-best. You are the winner by default. Whether the debate happened exactly this way is less important than what it tells us about the Khazar choice: a third option, beholden to neither great power.

**3 — Itil's Markets:** The city at the Volga mouth is receiving ships from both directions simultaneously. From the north: Rus traders with furs, amber, and slaves. From the south: Persian merchants with silver dirhams and silk. The Khazar officials collect ten percent on all transactions. The synagogues, mosques, and churches are full on their respective holy days. The city is genuinely cosmopolitan in the early medieval world's most literal sense.

**4 — The Khagan-Bek Problem:** The Khagan is sacred; he cannot be seen to govern. The Bek governs everything. When things go wrong, the Khagan can be blamed. When things go right, the Bek takes credit. This arrangement works well in stable times. In the crisis of the Rus pressure from the north, the dual structure becomes a liability: no one is clearly in charge of the strategic decision between fighting and negotiating.

**5 — Sviatoslav's Raid:** The Rus prince Sviatoslav of Kiev brings his army to Itil in 968 CE. The city falls. The commercial infrastructure of the Khazar trade system is destroyed. The Khaganate does not recover. The Khazars do not disappear overnight — there are Khazar communities for another century — but as a political power, the buffer kingdom is finished. What replaces it in the northern Caspian steppe is eventually the domain of the Kipchaks and Cumans, and then the Mongols.
