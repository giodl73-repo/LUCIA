# B-2 — Robert Harms

## Domain Assessment

**Pool Market and River Trade:** The chapter's Pool scene is the most historically grounded section of a consistently well-grounded chapter. The physical description — the vast widening, the fishing canoes, the market platforms, the noise of simultaneous transactions — matches the Pool as described in missionary accounts from the 1870s-1890s and is consistent with oral testimony about the earlier period. The detail about the sound of the market carrying up the slope before the Pool is visible is taken from the experiential accounts; it is accurate.

The chapter's characterization of the Pool as the junction where river trade shifted to land transport — where goods from the upper river arrived by canoe and were transferred to plateau porters — is accurate and important. This transfer point is the economic heart of the Teke position, and the chapter conveys why the junction exists and why the Teke controlled access to it.

**Atlantic Trade Transformation:** The phasing presented in the chapter is broadly accurate. The Portuguese arrive in 1483 and the Pool market registers their goods within decades. The transition from commodity trade to slave trade dominance is correctly placed in the mid-sixteenth to seventeenth century, though I would note that the transition was more gradual and geographically uneven than the chapter's section division suggests. The 1560s Kongo wars are a reasonable starting point for marking when enslaved people became a significant Pool market commodity; the full dominance of the slave trade over the commodity trade is more accurately placed in the early to mid-seventeenth century.

**Teke Role in the Slave Trade:** The chapter is accurate in characterizing the Teke as brokers and transporters rather than primary slave raiders. The forest interior conflicts that generated captives were conducted by forest chieftaincies; the Teke moved the captives to the Pool. This is consistent with the documentary record. The chapter does not overstate Teke agency in generating the trade and does not minimize Teke participation in it. This is the correct balance.

**One factual concern:** The sentence "wars fought partly to generate the captives that the Pool market would absorb, wars funded by the European goods that the Pool market paid out in exchange" describes a feedback loop — Atlantic goods financing wars that produced captives for the Atlantic trade — that is well-documented for later in the period (eighteenth century) but is somewhat anachronistic for the early seventeenth century, when the feedback loop was still forming. The chapter's framing puts this description at "by 1700," which is acceptable, but the language ("wars fought partly to generate captives") attributes too direct an intentionality to the forest interior conflicts in some cases; many of these wars had their own internal logics that the slave trade demand then exploited rather than created. This is a nuance worth flagging but not a factual error.

**Recommendation:** Approve with the nuance noted above. The chapter's treatment of the Atlantic trade section is the most historically demanding section and it handles it with appropriate care. The "new commodity" section's refusal to editorialize while rendering the broker's calculation is exactly the right approach — it neither excuses nor condemns, which is what the historical record warrants.
