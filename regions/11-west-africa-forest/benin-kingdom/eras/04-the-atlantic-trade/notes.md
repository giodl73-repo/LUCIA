# Production Bible -- The Palace's Memory

**Era:** The Atlantic Trade Period, c. 1550-1700 CE
**Chapter target:** ~5,000 words
**Status:** Pre-draft

---

## 1. Chapter Arc

The opening establishes the world of the bronze-caster: a guild member pressing wax into a plaque on Igun Street. The full chapter must complete the arc from one plaque to the palace archive, from local commission to Atlantic encounter, from the Oba's managed trade to the moment when the archive is taken.

**The structural engine: The plaque as governance document.**

The Benin plaques are not art objects. They are the Oba's archive, visual records of the kingdom's history cast in copper alloy to outlast any single reign. The structural engine is the relationship between the Oba's authority, the guild's craft, and the metal medium that converts transient events into permanent record. Every plaque is simultaneously a political act (commissioned by the Oba), a sacred act (the guild's casting carries ritual significance), and an archival act (the image is added to the palace's visual history). This three-part logic drives the chapter.

The engine's arc: (1) The Oba commissions plaques to record the kingdom's history. (2) The Portuguese arrive and are recorded on the plaques as visitors, managed on the Oba's terms. (3) The Atlantic trade increases the copper supply, enabling the peak of plaque production. (4) The trade relationship shifts as European ambitions grow. (5) In 1897, the British take the archive itself. The plaques that recorded the kingdom's encounters with outsiders become the objects through which outsiders claim to define Benin's meaning.

The irony the chapter holds but does not state: the archive system that was designed to outlast any single reign was defeated not by time but by a military expedition that removed the physical objects. But the guild continues. The Igun Eronmwon still cast on Igun Street in Benin City today.

**The outsider-bias technique (v1.2):** Portuguese accounts describe THEIR surprise at Benin City's scale and organization. This surprise is evidence about Portugal's expectations, not about Benin's achievement. The British expedition's documentation describes what THEY valued (the objects) and what they did not understand (the archive function). The chapter uses outsider accounts as characterization of the outsiders, never as the authoritative frame for Benin's story.

## 2. Section Plan

### Section 1 -- The Palace's Memory (Opening)
**Word target:** ~800 words (drafted)
**Anchor:** Osemwede, master caster, pressing a wax plaque on Igun Street
**What it accomplishes:** Drops the reader into the workshop. Establishes the lost-wax process, the guild system, the Oba's commission, the plaque as palace archive. Seeds the Portuguese presence (they appear on the plaque) without centering them.

### Section 2 -- The Oba's Pillars
**Word target:** ~900 words
**Anchor:** The palace compound and the plaque gallery
**What it accomplishes:** The palace as archive. The approximately 900 plaques that lined the pillars, depicting the kingdom's history, cosmology, diplomacy. The Oba's court as a functioning political system: the uzama (hereditary nobles), the town chiefs, the palace chiefs, the guild system, the coral-bead regalia as political theology. The commemorative heads on the ancestor altars. The Igue ceremony of renewal.

**Scene:** Walking through the palace compound. The pillars lined with plaques. The ancestor altar with its bronze heads. The court hierarchy visible in the arrangement of figures. The coral beads that only the Oba and those he authorized could wear, their red the color of Olokun's domain, the sea that brought wealth.

**Structural engine note:** This section reveals why the plaques matter: they are not decoration but governance. The Oba's right to commission them is inseparable from his political authority. The plaques record who visited, who served, who fought, who was honored. They make the Oba's version of history permanent and visible.

### Section 3 -- The Pepper Factor
**Word target:** ~900 words
**Anchor:** A Portuguese factor negotiating at Gwatto, the Oba's Atlantic port
**What it accomplishes:** The Portuguese encounter from inside. The Oba's management of the Atlantic trade: the royal monopoly on certain goods (pepper, ivory, enslaved persons), the factors who traded only through the Oba's representatives, the Oba's right to open and close the trade. The 1514 embassy to Lisbon. The selective adoption of Christian imagery (the cruciform motif on bronzes) without adopting Christianity. The increase in copper supply and its effect on bronze production.

**Outsider-bias technique:** The Portuguese accounts of Benin describe their surprise, their comparisons to European cities, their commercial frustrations with the Oba's monopoly terms. These are rendered as evidence about Portuguese expectations, not as the story of Benin's encounter with Europe. The Oba's court managed the Portuguese the way it managed other tributary relationships.

### Section 4 -- The Archive
**Word target:** ~900 words
**Anchor:** The peak production period: the plaques at their most abundant and detailed
**What it accomplishes:** The height of the plaque tradition. The range of subjects: warriors, hunters, leopards, mudfish, Portuguese traders, court ceremonies, acrobats, musicians. The technical achievement: thin-cast plaques with fine detail, multiple figures in narrative compositions, the use of perspective conventions specific to Benin (the Oba larger, attendants smaller). The guild system at full operation: multiple workshops, hereditary specializations within the guild, the relationship between bronze-casters and other guilds (ivory carvers, bead workers, blacksmiths).

**Structural engine note:** This is the structural engine at peak expression. The copper flowing through the Atlantic trade funds the most elaborate period of plaque production. The Oba's archive grows at the rate the trade allows. The same Atlantic contact that brings the copper also brings the visitors who are recorded on the plaques.

### Section 5 -- The Earthworks and the Walls
**Word target:** ~600 words
**Anchor:** The Iya earthwork system
**What it accomplishes:** The physical scale of the kingdom. The earthworks surrounding Benin City and its satellite communities, estimated at 16,000+ kilometers of total construction. The organized labor that built them over centuries. The kingdom's territorial logic: the walls defined the Oba's domain, controlled movement, marked the boundary between the governed and the ungoverned. This section grounds the chapter in the physical landscape of Benin's power.

### Section 6 -- February 1897 and After
**Word target:** ~900 words
**Anchor:** The removal of the plaques from the palace pillars
**What it accomplishes:** The British punitive expedition. What happened: the ambush of a British party (which the Benin court said it did not authorize), the retaliatory military assault, the burning of the palace, the removal of approximately 3,000 objects. Presented without verdict. What it meant for the archive: the plaques that had recorded the kingdom's history were taken from the pillars where they had served their function and dispersed into museums where they served a different function. The Oba was exiled. The court was disrupted.

**No-verdict technique:** Present what happened. The reader judges. Do not say "tragic" or "unjust." The facts are sufficient.

**Landing:** The guild continues. The Igun Eronmwon still work on Igun Street. The casting techniques are still transmitted from master to apprentice. The Oba's court was restored in 1914 (the British allowed a new Oba to be installed). The plaques are in museums in London, Berlin, Vienna, New York, and dozens of other cities. The Edo people's demand for their return is a political and cultural claim based on the understanding that the objects are dynastic property, not art loot. The guild casts. The Oba commissions. The archive, dispersed, is being reassembled. The wax is still warm.

## 3. Key Figures

### Osemwede (composite)
**Role:** Master bronze-caster. The chapter's opening and closing anchor.
**Historical basis:** Composite figure drawn from the documented practices of the Igun Eronmwon guild. The name is Edo and conventional for the period. His specific actions (pressing wax, building a plaque showing the Oba with Portuguese visitors) are inferred from the surviving plaques themselves and from guild practices documented by Ben-Amos and Plankensteiner. This composite approach is honestly disclosed in figures.md per the v1.1 innovation.

### The Oba (unnamed, the office)
**Role:** The commissioning authority. Present throughout as the source of the guild's work and the kingdom's political center.
**Historical basis:** Multiple Obas ruled during this era (Oba Orhogbua, Oba Ehengbuda, and their successors span c. 1550-1700). The chapter treats the office rather than individual rulers because the plaque tradition serves the dynasty, not individual kings. Specific Obas are named where the record supports it.

### Duarte Pacheco Pereira (historical)
**Role:** Portuguese captain and author of Esmeraldo de Situ Orbis (c. 1506), who described Benin.
**Historical basis:** Pereira visited the Benin coast and described the kingdom's trade, the Oba's authority, and the city's scale. His account is used as evidence of Portuguese expectations and commercial ambitions, not as the authoritative description of Benin.

## 4. Structural Engine -- The Plaque as Governance Document

The engine operates at three levels:

**Political:** The Oba commissions plaques. The commission is an exercise of authority: the Oba determines what is recorded, how, and by whom. The guild's exclusive right to cast is granted by the Oba. The resulting objects serve the Oba's ancestor altars and line the palace pillars. The visual record IS the Oba's version of history.

**Sacred:** The casting process carries ritual significance. The guild's work is not secular craft production. The Oba's relationship with the ancestors (maintained through the bronze commemorative heads on the altar) is the theological foundation of his authority. The bronzes are votive objects in an ongoing political theology.

**Archival:** The plaques accumulate. Each new plaque adds to the palace's visual record. The record includes the kingdom's own history (wars, ceremonies, successions) and its encounters with outsiders (Portuguese, Dutch, British). The outsiders are recorded on the Oba's terms, in the Oba's medium, according to the Oba's compositional conventions. A Portuguese trader on a Benin plaque is not a European subject in European art. He is a visitor in the Oba's archive.

The engine's vulnerability: the archive is physical. Unlike an oral tradition (which travels in human memory) or a manuscript tradition (which can be copied), the bronze plaques exist as unique objects attached to a specific building. When the building is destroyed and the objects removed, the archive is not lost (the objects survive) but it is displaced. The meaning changes when the context changes. A plaque on a palace pillar is a governance document. The same plaque in a glass case in a European museum is an "art object." The displacement is not just physical but categorical.

## 5. Sensory Palette

### The Workshop (Opening)
- **Sight:** Firelight on wax, the detail of the plaque taking shape, the drying racks of wax models
- **Sound:** The fire, the rhythmic pounding of clay by apprentices, market sounds outside
- **Smell:** Hot wax, clay, charcoal smoke, the humidity of the forest edge
- **Touch:** Warm wax between fingers, the sweat on faces near the fire

### The Palace (Section 2)
- **Sight:** Pillars lined with plaques, bronze heads on the altar, coral-bead regalia, the palace compound's scale
- **Sound:** Palace drums marking the hours, court ceremony
- **Smell:** The palace compound: open spaces, the smell of the great trees, incense at the altar
- **Touch:** The coolness of bronze under the hand, the weight of coral beads

### The Port (Section 3)
- **Sight:** Gwatto: canoes, the river, Portuguese ships anchored off the coast, goods being unloaded
- **Sound:** Negotiation, multiple languages, the river
- **Smell:** The river, salt, pepper (Benin pepper was a major export), the forest
- **Touch:** The humid coastal air, heavier than the city's

### The Plaques at Peak (Section 4)
- **Sight:** The full range of plaque subjects: warriors, leopards, Portuguese figures, court scenes, the mudfish of Olokun
- **Sound:** The workshop at full operation: multiple fires, multiple castings
- **Smell:** Metal, wax, the heat of the pour
- **Touch:** The finished plaque: the raised surface, the sharp detail of the cast

### The Earthworks (Section 5)
- **Sight:** The walls rising above the treeline, red laterite, the forest on either side
- **Sound:** The silence of the walls, the forest sounds
- **Touch:** The packed earth, hard as stone

### 1897 (Section 6)
- **Sight:** Plaques being taken from pillars. The empty pillar afterward.
- **Sound:** The absence of what was there
- **Smell/Touch:** Not dwelt on. Restraint.

## 6. Voice Notes

### Court Culture Register
This is a court chapter. The Style Guide specifies: "Layered. Observational. Unfolds slowly... Every gesture has meaning. Every silence is political." The Oba's court is the center of gravity. Everything in the chapter radiates from the palace.

The voice should carry the weight of the guild's precision: careful, exact, attentive to detail. The casting process is the metaphor for the prose itself: each line matters, each detail is permanent. The rhythm is deliberate, not hurried.

### Voice Constraints
- No forbidden words. No "primitive," "advanced," "discovered."
- No "surprisingly sophisticated" or any variant
- Portuguese accounts describe THEIR surprise; the text does not share it
- The 1897 expedition is presented as fact, not as tragedy or injustice
- Em-dashes: no more than 8 in the full chapter
- The bronzes are not "art" in the European sense; the text does not call them art
- The Oba is never "a ruler" who "also" did cultural things; the political and sacred functions are inseparable

## 7. Panel Risks

### Tuchman (P-1)
- Demands narrative arc. The chapter cannot be a static portrait of guild life. The Atlantic trade's arrival and evolution provides the complication. The 1897 removal provides the turn.
- Demands particularized scenes, not survey.

### Ibn Khaldun (P-2)
- Demands the structural engine. Why bronze? Why this guild system? What material conditions enable it? The copper supply, the trade networks, the Oba's monopoly.
- Demands the logic of the Atlantic trade relationship: why Benin controlled the terms initially, and what shifted.

### Achebe (P-3)
- The most critical reviewer for this chapter. The entire chapter must tell Benin's story from inside.
- Portuguese accounts must be characterization of the Portuguese, not the chapter's frame.
- The 1897 expedition must not become the chapter's emotional center; Benin existed before 1897 and continues after.
- No admiration from outside. The bronzes are bronzes. They do not need European approval.

### Davis (P-4)
- The lost-wax process must be treated as a genuine knowledge system with full intellectual depth.
- The guild's transmission system (master-apprentice, hereditary) must be treated as a knowledge institution equal to any Western academy.
- The plaque compositional conventions (hierarchical sizing, narrative organization) must be treated as a visual language, not mere decoration.

### Kapuscinski (P-5)
- Every section must be sensorily grounded. The workshop must be felt.
- The palace must be a physical space, not an abstract idea of power.
- The 1897 scene must be grounded in physical detail, not in abstraction.
