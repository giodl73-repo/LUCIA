# R1-P1 — Barbara Tuchman — Review of Opening

**Chapter:** Songhai Empire / Era 2 — The River War
**Round:** 1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

This opening does what an opening must do: it makes you want to read the next page.

**What works:**

The first line is perfect in its simplicity. "The canoes came at dawn." Four words, and I know the mode: military, forward, specific. The paddle-rhythm reaching the city before the canoes do is a detail that earns its place because it creates a sequence: sound, then sight, then meaning. The reader experiences the approach the way the merchants of Timbuktu experienced it.

The structural revelation is paced well. We learn about the river navy not through a description of Sunni Ali's military reforms but through its deployment. The navy arrives before the analysis does. The scene does the work.

The Tuareg withdrawal is excellent. Akil ag Malwal's decision to flee is not narrated as cowardice or strategic retreat. It is narrated as comprehension: "Horses could not stand on water." The tactical logic drives the narrative without the author needing to explain it.

The closing pivot from Timbuktu to Djenne is genuine pull. The chapter has not yet begun and I already know where it is going.

**What I would push on:**

The middle section, where we learn about Sunni Ali's ancestry and the Za dynasty, loses forward momentum. The information is necessary, but the delivery is expository. "His father had held Gao. His grandfather had held it." This is a good rhythm, but the passage around it reads more like background briefing than scene. I would like this context woven into the action rather than paused for.

The final paragraph is strong in intent but slightly repetitive. "The paddlers were checking hulls... The soldiers were unloading provisions... And Sunni Ali was already looking west." The tripartite structure is fine, but the first two elements do not add enough to earn their space. The weight should fall entirely on Sunni Ali looking west.

**The question I'd push on:** Can the Za dynasty background be delivered THROUGH the scene rather than beside it? The opening is at its best when strategic insight emerges from action, not from exposition placed between action scenes.
