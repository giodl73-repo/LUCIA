## B-2 — David C. Conrad — Board Review of "The Great Hajj"

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** The Sundiata epic and Mande oral traditions; the social and political role of the jeli (griot) in Mande society; the relationship between oral and written sources in West African historiography. Published extensively on how the jeli tradition preserves and transforms historical memory.

**Historical accuracy:**

- The chapter opens with "Bala Faseke Kouyate" singing at the gate of Niani. Bala Faseke (also Bala Fasseke) is the legendary first jeli of the Sundiata tradition — the griot assigned to Sundiata who was captured by Sumanguru and eventually returned with the magical balafon. Placing a jeli named "Bala Faseke Kouyate" at the departure of Musa's hajj in 1324, roughly ninety years after Sundiata's time, is a deliberate literary choice. It works if understood as a representative of the Kouyate lineage carrying the ancestral name, which is how jeli lineages operate — the founding ancestor's name recurs and is carried by descendants. However, the chapter does not signal this. A reader unfamiliar with the tradition might assume this is the same individual from the Sundiata narrative, which would be a chronological impossibility. The chapter should either make explicit that this is a descendant carrying the lineage name, or use a less iconic name that avoids the confusion. The current usage sits on the edge between homage and anachronism.
- The genealogy that the jeli sings — "Sundiata, Wali, Khalifa, Abu Bakr" — is broadly consistent with the dynastic sequence as preserved in both oral and written sources. The inclusion of Abu Bakr "who went to sea and did not return" is a reference to Abu Bakr II and the Atlantic voyages tradition. This is present in al-Umari (via Musa's own reported account to Egyptian officials) and in some oral traditions. It is a legitimate inclusion, handled with appropriate brevity.
- The chapter correctly notes that "a jeli does not choose which ancestors to remember. A jeli remembers them all." This is an accurate and important statement about the jeli's obligation to completeness in genealogical recitation. A jeli who omits names risks the accusation of corruption or partisanship. Good.

**Cultural authenticity:**

- The opening scene places the jeli at the center of the departure, which is exactly where the jeli would be. The departure of a mansa on a journey of this magnitude would have been a performance event — the jeli singing the genealogy to legitimize the departure, to place the act within the continuity of the dynasty, and to establish the public record of the event. The chapter understands this. The jeli is not ornamental here; he is performing an institutional function.
- The kora is named as Bala Faseke's instrument. This is an anachronism that needs attention. The kora's presence in the Mande world in the early 14th century is not well established. The instrument is associated with the Mandinka traditions of the Gambia region and its documented history places its development somewhat later than 1324. The balafon — the instrument specifically associated with Bala Faseke in the Sundiata tradition — would be more historically appropriate. The dundun and djembe are also named; the djembe is broadly Mande and acceptable, though its specific association with jeli performance (as opposed to communal celebration) could be questioned. Recommend changing the kora to the balafon, which has the additional virtue of being the instrument named in the Bala Faseke origin story.
- The chapter's treatment of the oral tradition in Section V — the jelilu still singing, the seven-year ceremony at Kangaba, the Kouyate lineage — is broadly accurate and handled with genuine respect. The observation that "the variations between versions are not errors" (implicit here, explicit in the Overview) reflects a correct understanding of how the epic tradition works. Each performance is an authoritative version in its context.
- The phrase "the jelilu were already weaving the hajj into the genealogy" captures an important truth: the incorporation of new events into the genealogical framework is not a distortion of the tradition but its normal operation. This is well understood.
- The chapter's closing argument that "the oral tradition and the written tradition are not opposites. They are two streams of the same river" is a position I would endorse. It avoids the hierarchy (written > oral) that has plagued West African historiography since the colonial period. The statement that "the scholars who copied manuscripts in Timbuktu and the jelilu who sang the Sundiata Epic in Kangaba were doing the same work in different materials" is strong and defensible.
- The chapter correctly identifies the Kouyate as a jeli lineage. It uses "jelilu" as the plural of "jeli," which is correct Manding morphology (the standard plural). Good.

**What the chapter gets right:**

- The jeli is treated as an institutional figure, not a colorful folk performer. The opening scene establishes the jeli as the person who legitimizes the departure by placing it in genealogical context. This is exactly right.
- The relationship between the jeli's performance and political authority is correctly rendered. The jeli sings "for the jelilu who would sing of this journey" — the recursive quality, where the current act is performed with awareness that it will itself become part of the tradition, is a genuine feature of how jelilu understand their role.
- The distinction between the oral and written traditions as parallel, complementary systems of knowledge preservation is handled with scholarly accuracy and without condescension toward either.
- The 2012 manuscript rescue is accurately described and correctly connected to the continuity of the preservation tradition. The families who smuggled manuscripts to Bamako were indeed acting within a centuries-old logic of custodianship.
- The chapter does not fall into the trap of treating the Sundiata Epic as a single fixed text. The phrase "the Sundiata Epic grew and branched, each jeli adding the inflection of their time" correctly describes the tradition's dynamic character.

**What needs correction:**

- **The kora anachronism.** Bala Faseke's instrument should be the balafon, not the kora. The kora is not securely attested in the Mande heartland in 1324, and the balafon is the instrument specifically associated with Bala Faseke in the founding tradition. The balafon (bala) is, after all, the source of his very name — Bala Faseke, "Faseke of the Bala." This is a meaningful correction both for historical accuracy and for internal consistency with the Sundiata tradition the chapter invokes.
- **The Bala Faseke naming.** The chapter should signal that the jeli named "Bala Faseke Kouyate" in 1324 is a descendant carrying the lineage name, not the legendary companion of Sundiata. A brief phrase — "a Kouyate jeli carrying the name of the lineage founder" or similar — would resolve the ambiguity without disrupting the narrative flow.
- **"The seven-year ceremony at Kangaba still takes place."** This should be specified as the septennial ceremony of the Kamabolon (the sacred house). It takes place at Kangaba (specifically Kangaba/Kela) and involves the re-roofing of the sacred house and the recitation of the Sundiata narrative by Diabate jelilu (not Kouyate — the Kangaba/Kela ceremony is specifically associated with the Diabate lineage). The chapter's reference to "the Kouyate and their fellow jeli lineages" in the same passage could be read as attributing the Kangaba ceremony to the Kouyate, which would be inaccurate. The ceremony's custodians are the Diabate. Recommend clarifying this distinction.
- **Minor: "the jelilu" vs. "jelilu."** The chapter uses both "the jelilu" and "jelilu" without the article. In Manding, "jelilu" is already definite in context. The article is a translation convention and not wrong, but the chapter should be consistent.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS

The chapter's treatment of the oral tradition is serious, respectful, and largely accurate. The jeli is understood as an institution, not a decoration. The relationship between oral and written knowledge systems is handled with scholarly maturity. The kora anachronism needs correction — it is the most significant error from the oral tradition perspective. The Bala Faseke naming needs a clarifying signal. The Kangaba ceremony attribution needs precision. These are corrections, not reconceptions. The chapter's understanding of the tradition is sound.
