# Qart Hadasht — The New City

We named ourselves for our founding: Qart Hadasht — the New City. Dido came from Tyre with her followers and bargained for as much land as a bull's hide could cover, then cut the hide into strips and enclosed the hilltop called Byrsa overlooking the bay that would become the finest harbor in the western Mediterranean. This is the story we told. Whether it happened exactly that way is less important than what it reveals: we were a people who understood negotiation, who operated through intelligence rather than force when intelligence was available, and who had come from the sea and built a new world from a strip of coastline.

From Byrsa, Qart Hadasht grew into the greatest city in the western Mediterranean — a city of four hundred thousand, its harbor divided into commercial and military basins with dry docks for over two hundred warships, its council of wealthy merchants the real governing power behind a constitution that Aristotle studied and admired. We were not a monarchy and we were not a democracy. We were a mercantile republic — a city governed by its commercial elite, whose power rested on the silver mines of Spain, the grain fields of what would later be called Tunisia, and the sea-routes that connected our colonies from Sardinia to Cadiz. When Rome came for us, it took three wars and a siege of three years — and even then Rome destroyed the city building by building, street by street, because Qart Hadasht would not simply stop.

## What Defined Them

**The harbor as the city's soul.** The harbor of Qart Hadasht was not simply infrastructure. It was the city's identity — the commercial harbor through which the wealth of the western Mediterranean flowed, and the military harbor from which the fleet that protected all that wealth deployed. The commercial basin could hold the merchant ships of every partner in the network. The circular military basin, with its island in the center and its dry docks arranged around the circumference, was one of the engineering wonders of the ancient world. To control the harbors was to control the city; to control the city was to control the sea.

**The suffetes and the council — rule by the wealthy.** Qart Hadasht was governed not by a king but by two elected magistrates — the *suffetes*, a Semitic title meaning judges — and a council of wealthy citizens. Aristotle described this constitution as well-made because it balanced competing interests. The generals were appointed separately from the civilian magistrates and were held accountable for their campaigns; a general who lost could face execution. This accountability structure, which Aristotle contrasted favorably with the hereditary military command of some Greek cities, reflected a commercial elite's insistence that military power not override the council's authority.

**The sea as sovereign.** Where Rome organized itself around the army, Qart Hadasht organized itself around the fleet. The Carthaginian navy was the finest in the western Mediterranean for three centuries — a professional force of trained oarsmen serving in specialized warships, unlike the citizen rowers of Athens who also farmed and traded. This naval supremacy underwrote the entire commercial empire: the ability to protect the sea-lanes, enforce the agreements that kept Sardinia and Spain in the Carthaginian network, and deploy overwhelming force against any rival who tried to insert themselves into the western trade. When Rome built a navy and defeated Carthage at sea, it did not simply win a battle. It destroyed the structural foundation of Carthaginian power.

**Baal Hammon and Tanit — the divine pair.** The great gods of Qart Hadasht were Baal Hammon, the lord of the furnace, and Tanit, the face of Baal in her stellar and lunar form, whose symbol — a figure with arms raised, topped by a circle — appears on every sacred inscription from the city. The *tophet* — the sacred precinct where votive offerings were made — has been the source of intense scholarly debate about the nature of Carthaginian religious practice. The votive urns contain the cremated remains of infants alongside animal remains and dedicatory stelae. What exactly these deposits represent is still contested. What is clear is that Tanit and Baal Hammon were the center of Carthaginian religious life and that the *tophet* was the city's most sacred space.

**The Barcid family and the Spanish strategy.** After the loss of Sicily in the First Punic War, the Barcid family — Hamilcar and then his sons Hasdrubal and Hannibal — developed the Spanish strategy: build a land empire in Iberia to replace the lost revenues of Sicily and to provide the manpower base for a renewed challenge to Rome. Hannibal's crossing of the Alps with his elephants, his victories at Trebia, Trasimene, and Cannae, and his sixteen-year march through Italy without a decisive supply base — all of this flowed from the Spanish strategy and from Hannibal's military genius. He came within a decision of breaking Rome. The decision the Italian cities made — to stay with Rome rather than join Hannibal — is one of the pivots of Mediterranean history.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Foundation Age:** Dido's fleet enters the bay and the negotiations begin. The hide is cut into strips. Byrsa is enclosed. The harbor that will organize the western Mediterranean for seven centuries is being chosen.

**2 — The Age of Expansion:** Hanno's ship passes the Pillars of Herakles and turns south along the African coast. He passes river mouths, forests, fires on the coast at night. He reaches a place where he hears the sound of pipes and cymbals and sees great fires and figures in the darkness. He turns back. The report survives.

**3 — The Sicilian Wars:** A Carthaginian general stands at Himera and loses. The Greek cities of Sicily resist, but the Sicilian interior is Carthaginian and the harbor cities of the west are Carthaginian and the war will go on for another century. Sicily is the contested center of the western Mediterranean and neither side will let go.

**4 — The Punic Wars:** Hannibal counts his elephants at the foot of the Alps and calculates the march. He does not expect to take Rome. He expects to make Rome's Italian allies reconsider their loyalty. He is almost right.

**5 — The Final Years:** Cato ends every speech with *Carthago delenda est* — Carthage must be destroyed. The city has been at peace for fifty years, has no war fleet, and poses no military threat. Rome destroys it anyway. The survivors are enslaved. The site is plowed with salt. The harbor fills with silt. Seven centuries of civilization end in three years.
