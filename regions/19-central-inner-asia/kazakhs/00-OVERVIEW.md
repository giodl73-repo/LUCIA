# Kazakhs — El — The People and the Law

We are the Kazakhs — the people of the Great Steppe, the grasslands of what is now Kazakhstan and the adjacent steppes of Russia and Central Asia — and we are the pastoral nomadic civilization that represents the fullest expression of the steppe way of life in the historical period. Our name means "free people" or "wanderers" in the Türkic languages; we call ourselves qazaq, and we mean by it the people who are free to move, free to graze their herds on the open steppe, free from the sedentary constraints of city walls and agricultural seasons. We were not free from law, however: the Kazakh steppe was governed by the Zheti Zhargy — the Great Code of the Seven Statutes — a sophisticated customary law that regulated everything from blood feuds to marriage payments to the inheritance of herds. The steppe democracy of the Kazakh biys (judges) and their legal tradition was a civilization of process and precedent, not of stone and mortar.

We organized ourselves into three zhuzes — great divisions that we call the Elder, Middle, and Junior Zhuzes — each occupying specific territorial ranges on the steppe, each maintaining its internal leadership structure of khans and sultans and biys, and all three together constituting the Kazakh people. The zhuz system was not an obstacle to unity; it was the form through which unity was expressed. When the Dzungar Mongol confederacy threatened our survival in the early eighteenth century — the period we call the Great Disaster — the three zhuzes coordinated their resistance under Abylai Khan. But the Russian Empire's approach from the north in the same century presented a different kind of threat, one that the military organization of the zhuz system could not counter, and by 1847 all three zhuzes had formally submitted to Russian authority.

## What Defined Them

**The three zhuzes and the steppe democracy.** The Kazakh political system was organized around the three Great Zhuzes (Elder, Middle, Junior), each covering specific steppe territories and each led by khans of Chinggisid descent and biys (judge-arbiters) of merit. The biys occupied a unique political role: they were not administrators but legal authorities, settling disputes through the application of customary law (adat) with a combination of precedent, poetry, and moral persuasion. The great biys — Tolе bi, Kazybek bi, Aiteke bi — are remembered in Kazakh tradition as the founders of the Zheti Zhargy code and the embodiments of Kazakh civic virtue. This was a law-governed society without a bureaucratic state.

**The Zheti Zhargy — the Great Code.** The customary law that governed Kazakh life was compiled into the Zheti Zhargy (Seven Statutes) by the three great biys in the early eighteenth century, though its roots were older. The code regulated criminal law (with compensation payments rather than imprisonment as the primary sanction), family law (bride-price, divorce, inheritance), land use (grazing rights, water access), and inter-clan relations (feud limitation, diplomatic protocols). The Kazakh legal tradition was sophisticated and adaptive — it had to be, governing a mobile population across a vast landscape where there were no courts, no prisons, and no police forces. Social pressure, reputation, and the threat of ostracism were its enforcement mechanisms.

**The Great Disaster.** The Dzungar Mongol confederation's campaigns against the Kazakhs in the 1720s — called in Kazakh tradition Aktaban Shubyryndy (the Barefoot Flight) and Alkaköl Sülama (Drowning in Alkaköl Lake) — killed and displaced a large fraction of the Kazakh population and fundamentally disrupted the pastoral economy. The Great Disaster is the central trauma of Kazakh historical memory: the period when the steppe way of life was nearly extinguished by a military force that the zhuz system could not coordinate against quickly enough. The recovery under Abylai Khan and the eventual Qing destruction of the Dzungar confederation (1757) ended the immediate threat, but the trauma of the Great Disaster shaped Kazakh political decision-making for a generation.

**The Russian relationship.** The Kazakh submission to Russian imperial authority — zhuz by zhuz, beginning with the Junior Zhuz in 1731 and completed with the Middle Zhuz leadership in 1847 — was partly coerced and partly chosen: the Russian Empire offered protection from the Dzungar threat at a price the Kazakh khans were willing to pay in the immediate crisis, and the gradual extension of Russian control over the steppe was something they had little military capacity to resist once it accelerated in the nineteenth century. The abolition of the Kazakh khanate's formal authority (completed by 1848) and the encouragement of Russian settlement on Kazakh grazing lands transformed the steppe economy and eventually made the nomadic way of life impossible for most Kazakhs.

**Oral literature — the zhyrau tradition.** The Kazakh cultural tradition was expressed not in written texts but in oral poetry, performed by zhyrau (poet-sages) and akyns (improvising poets). The great zhyrau composed epic poetry, genealogical recitations, political satire, and elegies that embodied the Kazakh worldview and preserved historical memory. The Kazakh oral tradition was and is a sophisticated literary system: the zhyrau were not folk entertainers but intellectual authorities whose compositions were memorized and transmitted across generations. The transition to written Kazakh literature (beginning in the nineteenth century) built on this oral foundation.

## The Chapters

**1 — Janibek and Girey's Departure:** Two Kazakh sultans have separated their followers from the Uzbek confederation of Abu'l-Khayr Khan and moved east to the Chu River valley. They call themselves qazaqs — the free people. The year is approximately 1465 CE. The Kazakh people are beginning. The three zhuzes will form from this initial separation over the following century.

**2 — The Great Biys Convene:** Tolе bi, Kazybek bi, and Aiteke bi — the three greatest legal minds of the three zhuzes — have assembled in the early eighteenth century to codify the customary law. They compose the Zheti Zhargy: the Seven Statutes that will govern the steppe for the next century and a half. The code is not written down immediately; it is memorized and recited. The law lives in the minds of the biys.

**3 — The Barefoot Flight:** The Dzungar cavalry is sweeping across the steppe. Kazakh families are abandoning their yurts and their herds and fleeing on foot — no time to pack, no time to gather the animals. Children are left behind. Elders die on the march. The pastures that have been Kazakh for generations are empty. This is Aktaban Shubyryndy, the Barefoot Flight. A generation later, the Kazakhs will still be mourning it in poetry.

**4 — Abylai Khan's Diplomacy:** The Khan is receiving envoys from two directions simultaneously: the Qing Emperor Qianlong wants Kazakh submission; the Russian Empress Catherine the Great wants Kazakh submission. Abylai Khan plays both sides for twenty years, maintaining a formal relationship with Russia while secretly negotiating with the Qing, and vice versa. He dies in 1781 without having fully submitted to either. His successors will not manage this balance.

**5 — The Last Khan:** Kuchum Khan — the last formal Khan of the Kazakh Middle Zhuz — submits to Russian authority in 1847. The khanate that Janibek and Girey founded is formally over. The Russian steppe administration takes over. But the pastoral economy continues for decades, and the Kazakh people — their language, their oral tradition, their zhuz identity — survive the political collapse, as peoples always outlast their states.
