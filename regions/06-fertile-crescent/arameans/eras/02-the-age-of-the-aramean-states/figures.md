# Figures — The Age of the Aramean States

## Hadad-ezer (Ben-Hadad) of Dammasq

The Assyrian annals call him Hadad-'idri. The Israelite text calls him Ben-Hadad, "son of Hadad," though some scholars argue that the Ben-Hadad of the Israelite wars and the Hadad-ezer of the Assyrian inscription may be different men, or the same man at different stages of his reign. The name itself is theophoric: the king bore the storm god's name because the storm god's authority legitimated his own.

What is clear across both sources is his role. Hadad-ezer organized the coalition at Qarqar. He was the king to whom the other Levantine rulers looked when Assyria moved west. This was not a function of personality. It was a function of position: Dammasq was the largest Aramean state, the one with the most chariots, the most cavalry, the deepest agricultural base. The king of Dammasq led coalitions because Dammasq's resources made the coalition possible.

The Israelite sources portray him as an enemy who lost battles but survived politically: a king who was beaten at Aphek but negotiated commercial terms rather than surrender. The Assyrian sources portray him as the principal threat west of the Euphrates: the man whose chariot force anchored a coalition that checked the empire's advance. Neither source is his own voice. The Aramean view of Hadad-ezer does not survive. What survives is the evidence that he held together a coalition of independent kings for the only decisive stand the Levant ever made against Assyrian expansion.

## Hazael

A usurper. "Son of nobody," the Assyrians said. The Israelite text offers a more dramatic origin: the prophet Elisha came to Dammasq and wept when he saw Hazael, because he knew what Hazael would do to Israel. Whether the prophecy preceded or followed the events it describes is a question the text does not invite.

Hazael's own voice survives in the Tel Dan Stele, if the attribution is correct, and possibly in other fragmentary inscriptions. The voice is a king's voice: declarative, martial, addressed to the god who granted victory. "Hadad went before me." The stele was carved in basalt, the hard volcanic stone of the region, and the choice of material was deliberate. Basalt endures. The king who carved his victories in basalt intended them to last.

Hazael was the most powerful Aramean king who ever ruled. He expanded Dammasq's territory to its greatest extent, fought Assyria to a standstill, and reshaped the political map of the Levant. He was also the man who broke the Aramean network by turning a commercial constellation into something resembling an empire, provoking the inter-state conflicts that weakened the system from within. The Aramean tradition did not record his reign in its own comprehensive narrative. The record that survives is the stone, the silence, and the testimony of the peoples he defeated.

## Zakkur of Hamath and Lu'ash

Zakkur's stele is a different kind of royal voice. Where Hazael carved his victories, Zakkur carved his survival. Besieged by a coalition led by Bar-Hadad, Hazael's son, Zakkur appealed to Baal-Shamayn: "I lifted my hands to Baal-Shamayn, and Baal-Shamayn heard me." The god answered through seers and diviners, and the siege was lifted.

Zakkur represents the Aramean states' internal tensions. He was an Aramean king fighting other Aramean kings. His stele is evidence that the Aramean system, which could unite against Assyria at Qarqar, could also tear itself apart when one state (Dammasq under Hazael's dynasty) attempted to dominate the others. The network that thrived on independence could not survive centralization.

## The Merchant at the Euphrates Crossing

He has no name. He appears in no inscription. He is the figure the record cannot see because his medium was perishable: leather, papyrus, the materials that carried Aramaic across the Near East and then rotted into nothing. The merchant is a composite, drawn from the archaeological evidence of Aramean commercial activity and the later survival of the practices he represents. He wrote in Aramaic because that was the language of his trade. He used the alphabet because the alphabet was fast. He carried his accounts in a saddlebag because leather weighed less than clay. He is the figure who made the language spread, and the spread of the language is the only thing the Aramean states built that outlasted the states themselves. The kings are known because the stone survived. The merchant is unknown because the leather did not. But the merchant's work endured longer than any king's.

## Source Note

The figures in this era present a characteristic challenge: the Aramean voice is thin. The primary Aramean-language sources are the Tel Dan Stele (likely Hazael's), the Zakkur Stele, and the Sfire treaty inscriptions. The remaining evidence comes from Assyrian royal inscriptions and the Hebrew Bible, both of which view the Aramean states from outside and, in different ways, from adversarial positions. The composite merchant figure is acknowledged as such; the named figures are grounded in the inscriptional and textual record. Where the record offers competing accounts (Hazael's accession, the outcome of Qarqar), both are presented.
