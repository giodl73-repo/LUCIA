## The Smell of the Thing

I have spent my life in the presence of empire — watching it work, watching it justify itself, watching the moment when the justification and the act diverge so completely that the people doing the act stop seeing the justification and the justification continues, on paper, in offices far away. I know what this looks like from the inside of the receiving end. I know what it looks like from the inside of the giving end.

This opening knows what it is doing.

The torch in the dark. The unrolling of the document. The sound of the sea. These three details — light, paper, water — are enough. I did not need more. The notary is in a specific body in a specific night, and I am there with him, and I feel the absurdity before I am told to feel it, because the scene itself is absurd and the prose does not look away from the absurdity.

## The Sensory Success

What works in the opening is what works in the best dispatch writing: the physical fact is placed before the interpretation. The torch comes before the theology. The dark comes before the legal argument. The reader's body is placed in the scene before the reader's mind is asked to assess it.

The detail I return to: "the sound of the sea." This is not decoration. The sea is everywhere in this story — it is how the ships arrived, it is the distance between Madrid and Tierra Firme, it is the thing that made the gap between the law and its enforcement so large. The notary reading in the dark with the sea behind him is the entire structure in miniature: the performance of legality at the edge of the known world, where no one from Madrid could see it.

I also note: the best clothes of the encomenderos at Mass on the fourth Sunday of Advent. This is a sensory anchor placed in a different moment, but it carries weight. The best clothes are the colonizers performing their status in a church, while the priest is telling them they are in mortal sin. The gap between the performance (best clothes, Sunday Mass) and the indictment (you are killing innocent people) is the same gap as the Requerimiento. The opening does not say this. It does not need to.

## What Is Missing in the Sensory Plane

**The heat.** The Caribbean in 1513 is not Spain. The climate is different, the landscape is different, the smell is different. The opening is set in the dark, which is correct — but the darkness of the Caribbean is a specific darkness, with specific sounds and smells, and the opening does not yet fully land in that physical place. A sentence of landscape — the specific forest, the specific night — would anchor the reader more fully before the legal argument takes over.

**The body of the indigenous response.** The people in the forest are present as absence — which is correct and powerful. But there is a moment available: the people watching from the forest, the firelight visible from a distance, the sound of Spanish words they cannot parse. This does not need to be lingered over or explained. One sentence, from a distance, of the scene as it might have appeared from the trees — would complete the sensory circle. The chapter can develop this further, but even the opening could use one more physical anchor on the other side.

## The Question I'd Push On

**What does Tenochtitlan smell like when Cortés enters?** Bernal Díaz del Castillo is in the sources list. His account of the soldiers' first sight of Tenochtitlan is one of the most remarkable pieces of eyewitness writing in the sixteenth century — the soldiers asking whether it was a dream, the markets, the scale, the beauty of the causeways. This is where the chapter must work hardest at the sensory level: the encounter with a city that is, by any measure, extraordinary, rendered from inside the conquistador's wonder and disorientation. Not the modern scholar's wonder. The soldier's wonder, in 1519, who has never seen a city of 200,000 people and does not have a category for what he is looking at.
