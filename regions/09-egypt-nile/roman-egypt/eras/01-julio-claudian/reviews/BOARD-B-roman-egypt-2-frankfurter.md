## BOARD — David Frankfurter — Review of Julio-Claudian Egypt

**Domain:** Egyptian religion under Roman rule, temple economics, priestly practice

**Overall:** The Temple section is the chapter's strongest section from my perspective. It treats the Egyptian temple system as a living, adaptive institution rather than a fossil, and it correctly identifies the theological logic that drove the persistence: the cosmic order required a pharaoh, and the priests would provide one regardless of what the current ruler believed.

**Factual accuracy:**
- The Esna temple inscriptions depicting Claudius as pharaoh are accurately described. The inscriptions are real and the characterization of the priestly corporation commissioning them is correct.
- The kyphi incense recipe (frankincense, myrrh, cinnamon, juniper, honey, raisins) is drawn from temple inscriptions at Edfu and Philae. The specific ingredients varied by temple and period; the list given is representative but not exhaustive. Acceptable for narrative purposes.
- The Apis bull verification protocol: the tradition of 29 marks comes from Herodotus and later sources. The specific marks listed (white diamond, eagle mark, double hairs, scarab mark) are drawn from Herodotus and Aelian. The number 29 is traditional. The chapter handles this correctly.
- The Gnomon's regulation of priestly practice is accurately described. The document does specify conditions for priestly service, temple property, and endowment management.

**Corrections needed:**
1. "The priests who performed the daily rituals bathed twice in the sacred lake before entering." The number of ritual baths varied by temple and rite. Herodotus mentions twice daily; other sources indicate more frequent bathing. "Bathed in the sacred lake before entering" is safer than specifying "twice." Minor.
2. "This was not theater." I appreciate the intent, but this is the author speaking, not the culture. The Egyptian theological system did not need to defend itself against the charge of being theater; that charge comes from a modern or Roman perspective. Consider removing this sentence entirely and letting the description of the ritual's precision speak for itself.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter correctly avoids the "decline of Egyptian religion" narrative. The temples are presented as functioning institutions under new management, not as dying remnants of a lost civilization.
- The Ptolemaic precedent is correctly cited. The continuity from Ptolemaic to Roman temple practice is one of the most important features of Egyptian religious history in this period.
- The accommodation of "two systems simultaneously" is correctly framed. Roman Egypt was genuinely dual: the Roman administrative system and the Egyptian theological system operated in parallel, each recognizing the other's necessity without sharing the other's premises.

**Verdict:** One minor correction (bathing frequency), one editorial recommendation (remove "This was not theater"). The section is otherwise accurate and handles the material with the respect it deserves.
