# Figures — The River War

## Epistemological Note

The principal sources for this era are al-Sadi's *Tarikh al-Sudan* (c. 1655) and Mahmud Kati's *Tarikh al-Fattash* (c. 1519, completed later by descendants). Both were written from within Timbuktu's Islamic scholarly tradition, and both carry that tradition's perspective on Sunni Ali, who antagonized the scholarly community. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* is consistently hostile to Sunni Ali; the *Tarikh al-Fattash* is more balanced but still written from a perspective that valued Islamic scholarly authority over the older Songhai religious and military traditions. Leo Africanus, writing a generation later (c. 1526), provides external observation but not Songhai interiority.

This means: the interior lives of these figures are reconstructed from sources that have a position. The chapter handles this by presenting the structural tension between military and scholarly authority without adjudicating it, and by rendering Sunni Ali from inside the Songhai military tradition's values rather than through the scholars' judgment.

---

## Sunni Ali Ber (r. 1464-1492)

The seventeenth ruler of the Sunni dynasty at Gao. He inherited a regional power and made it an empire.

The scholarly sources describe him as cruel, impious, and contemptuous of learning. These are the judgments of men whose authority he refused to recognize and whose system of legitimacy he declined to adopt. From inside the Songhai military tradition, Sunni Ali was a builder: a man who understood that the Niger was a strategic instrument and who organized the first standing river navy in West African history to exploit it. He campaigned for twenty-eight years without a major defeat. He took Timbuktu from the Tuareg (1468), besieged and took Djenne (traditionally c. 1468-1473), and fought the Mossi and Tuareg throughout his reign.

What we cannot know with confidence: his inner relationship with Islam. The scholarly sources say he was barely Muslim, practicing a syncretic faith that drew on older Songhai religious traditions. This is plausible but filtered through a hostile lens. What we can say: he did not govern through Islamic legal authority, he did not seek the scholars' approval, and his legitimacy rested on military achievement and on the authority of the Songhai river tradition. The chapter presents all of this without verdict.

He died in 1492, reportedly drowning while crossing a river. The tradition's brevity on the manner of his death is itself a form of narrative judgment that the chapter does not adopt.

## The Sorko and Bozo River People

Not a single figure but a collective presence. The Sorko and Bozo are the river peoples of the middle Niger, fishermen and boatmen whose knowledge of the river system was the practical and spiritual foundation of the Songhai navy.

The Sorko specifically are presented in the chapter as intermediaries with the river's spiritual life (the harey, or water spirits) and as the possessors of deep environmental knowledge: current-reading, seasonal flood prediction, navigation of the inland delta's channel system. This characterization draws on ethnographic sources and on the chronicles' references to the Sorko's role in Songhai military and spiritual life.

**Disclosure:** The specific practices described in the chapter (reading water color for current speed, feeling the riverbed through the hull, launching rituals) are composite descriptions drawn from ethnographic accounts of Sorko and Bozo communities collected in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, projected backward with the assumption of long continuity in river-based knowledge traditions. The specific individuals are not named because the sources do not name them. The Sorko community is rendered as a collective figure whose knowledge made the river war possible.

## Akil ag Malwal

Tuareg chief who held Timbuktu from approximately 1433 to 1468. He took the city during the period of Mali's contraction, when the empire could no longer hold the Niger bend cities against the desert peoples pressing from the north.

His decision to withdraw from Timbuktu rather than fight Sunni Ali's river fleet is recorded in both major chronicles. The chapter presents this as tactical comprehension, not cowardice: the Tuareg's cavalry-based military system could not contest a river-based force for control of a riverine city. Akil ag Malwal read the situation correctly and preserved his forces.

## The Ulama of Timbuktu

A collective figure representing the Islamic scholarly community of the three great mosques: Sankore, Djinguereber, and Sidi Yahia. The chapter treats them as a structural institution rather than as individual characters, because their significance to the narrative is institutional: they represent a form of authority (Islamic legal and intellectual prestige) that competed with Sunni Ali's military authority for primacy in the Songhai state.

Individual scholars of this period are not well-documented in the sources relative to the later Askia period, when figures like Ahmad Baba become historically vivid. The chapter accordingly treats the ulama as a community with a collective voice and a collective interest, without inventing individual spokespeople.

---

## Sunni Baru (r. 1492-1493)

Sunni Ali's son and brief successor. The sources describe him as continuing his father's approach to governance, which is to say: military authority without Islamic legitimacy. His overthrow by Muhammad Ture (Askia Muhammad) in 1493 was facilitated by a fatwa from the scholars of Timbuktu declaring his rule illegitimate.

The chapter treats Sunni Baru as a transitional figure whose brief reign reveals the structural limit of Sunni Ali's system: the river loop could conquer and hold territory, but without the Islamic legal framework, it could not generate the kind of political legitimacy the commercial-scholarly system required. Sunni Baru inherited the power without the resolution, and the resolution came not from him but from his replacement.

---

## Muhammad Ture / Askia Muhammad (mentioned, not featured)

A general of Soninke origin in Sunni Ali's army who overthrew Sunni Baru in 1493 and founded the Askia dynasty. He appears in the chapter only at the transition point, because his full story belongs to Era 3. The chapter notes his coup as the structural resolution of the tension the river war produced, without entering into his own achievements, which are the subject of the next chapter.
