# Figures — Meroe Era 3: The Roman Encounter

## Amanirenas

Kandake of Meroe, the central figure of this era. She held the office of queen-mother, which in the Meroitic system carried executive and military authority, not merely ceremonial status. Amanirenas led the Meroitic campaign against Roman garrisons at Aswan, Philae, and Elephantine in 24 BCE, directed the seizure of statues and trophies including the bronze head of Augustus, and negotiated the Treaty of Samos, which secured favorable border terms and the cancellation of Roman tribute demands.

Strabo describes her with the Greek word "andreia," which he meant as a compliment: masculine courage. The word reveals more about Roman categories than about the kandake. The Meroites did not need to translate her authority into masculine terms. The temple reliefs at Naga and Meroe depict kandakes as physically powerful figures, shown at the same scale as kings, wearing royal regalia, performing smiting rituals against enemies. These are not portraits in the modern sense; they are institutional self-representations, and they tell us that the Meroitic state depicted its female rulers as it depicted its male ones: as holders of divine authority expressed through physical power.

Strabo also records that Amanirenas lost an eye in the fighting. This detail, whether accurate or not, entered the Roman literary record as a marker of her ferocity. The Meroites, who depicted their kandakes as large, whole, and divine, would not have represented a physical injury in temple art. The two portraits, Roman and Meroitic, stand side by side in the historical record: one sees a one-eyed warrior queen, the other sees a divine ruler. Both are evidence. Neither is the whole person.

Her dates are uncertain. She is generally placed in the period c. 40-10 BCE, ruling during and after the Roman encounter. The bronze head she ordered buried beneath the Apedemak temple threshold at Meroe was found by John Garstang in 1910, precisely where she had placed it, and is now in the British Museum.

## Akinidad

Prince and military commander, named in Meroitic inscriptions from the frontier zone and referenced in Roman accounts of the campaign. His exact relationship to Amanirenas is debated: son, co-regent, or senior military subordinate. The inscriptions that might clarify this are in Meroitic, and the relevant vocabulary remains untranslated. What can be said is that Akinidad held sufficient authority to command forces independently during the campaign and to appear in the inscriptional record as a named figure associated with the military operations in Lower Nubia. He represents the Meroitic system's capacity for delegation: a kandake who could command through subordinates as well as in person.

## Gaius Petronius

Roman prefect of Egypt, successor to Aelius Gallus. Petronius led the Roman counterattack against Meroe, retaking Aswan, pushing south through the cataracts to Qasr Ibrim, and possibly reaching Napata. Strabo's account credits him with a decisive victory, but the treaty terms tell a different story: the border was set closer to Egypt than Rome wanted, and the tribute was cancelled. Petronius was a competent military commander operating within structural constraints that limited what any Roman prefect could achieve in the upper Nile corridor. He appears in this chapter not as a protagonist but as a counterpart: the Roman instrument through which Meroe's strategic position is made visible.

## Augustus

Present in this chapter only as a bronze head and a distant authority. Augustus does not appear as a character. His presence is mediated: through the head that was seized at Aswan and buried at Meroe, through the prefects he sent south, through the treaty his envoys negotiated at Samos. This is deliberate. The chapter is told from Meroe's perspective, and from Meroe, Augustus was not a person. He was a face on a pedestal, a name on a treaty, a power that had to be managed. The bronze head that Amanirenas placed beneath the Apedemak temple threshold captures this: the most powerful man in the Mediterranean world, reduced to an object, bound beneath a floor, pressed into the earth by the feet of a god's petitioners.

## The Smelter

A composite figure, unnamed, representing the ironworkers at Meroe's furnace complex. The smelter in this chapter is drawn from archaeological evidence: the furnace technology, the slag analysis, the fuel supply logistics documented at the site. The smelter has no name because the archaeological record preserves the work but not the workers. The figure is embodied (hands, heat, the reading of furnace signs) rather than named, following the v1.4 standard for archaeological figures: a body in the room, not a name on a list. The smelter represents the knowledge system that made Meroitic military and economic independence possible, and the characterization is built from the material evidence of what the work required.
