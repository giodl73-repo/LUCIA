# BOARD Review — Robert Harms
## The Kongo-Teke Interface

**Reviewer:** Robert Harms (Central Zaire trade history; *River of Wealth, River of Sorrow*)
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

## Assessment: APPROVE with nuance noted

The Pool market scene is the chapter's most historically grounded section. The physical description matches the Pool as described in missionary accounts and is consistent with oral testimony about the earlier period. The sound of the market carrying up the slope before the Pool is visible is accurate experiential detail.

The characterization of the Atlantic trade transformation is broadly accurate in phasing. The 1560s Kongo wars are a reasonable starting point for marking when enslaved people became significant Pool market commodities.

**Finding 1 (nuance, not error):** The sentence "wars fought partly to generate the captives that the Pool market would absorb, wars funded by the European goods that the Pool market paid out in exchange" attributes too direct an intentionality to the forest interior conflicts. Many of these wars had their own internal logics that the slave trade demand exploited rather than created. The feedback loop the chapter describes is well-documented for the eighteenth century; applying it to the early seventeenth century is slightly anachronistic. The chapter's "by 1700" framing makes this acceptable, but the language could be softened: "wars whose captives the Pool market could absorb" rather than "wars fought partly to generate captives." Author decides whether the revision improves or over-qualifies.

**Teke role in slave trade:** Correctly characterized as brokers and transporters rather than primary raiders. This is consistent with the documentary record.

**Conclusion:** The "new commodity" section's refusal to editorialize is exactly the right approach. The chapter passes the domain accuracy test.
