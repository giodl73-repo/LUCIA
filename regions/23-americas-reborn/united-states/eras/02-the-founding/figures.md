# Figures — The Founding (Era 2)
## United States, 1776–1800

---

### James Madison (1751–1836)

Madison came to Philadelphia in May 1787 with his "literary cargo": two years of systematic reading on the history of ancient republics, modern confederacies, and the theory of government, conducted specifically in preparation for the Convention. He had studied the Achaean League, the Amphictyonic Council, the Swiss Confederation, the Dutch Republic. He had written a memorandum, "Vices of the Political System of the United States," that diagnosed the Articles of Confederation's failures with clinical precision. By the time the Convention opened, he was the most prepared man in the room for the task at hand.

He was slight and quiet-voiced, and he sat near the front of the Convention chamber specifically so that he could hear everything. He took notes standing up, returning to his seat only to write, standing again to listen. He was, John Adams said, the greatest man of legislative standing he had known.

Madison owned enslaved people throughout his life. He inherited them; he never freed them; he sold them when he needed money. His private letters are full of expressions of discomfort about the institution. His public career consistently protected it. He could not resolve the contradiction within his own life any more than he could resolve it within the Constitution. He spent his old age trying to find a solution — he was a founder and financier of the American Colonization Society, which proposed to solve the slavery problem by shipping free Black Americans to Liberia — that was no solution at all.

The Madison who sits at the front of the Convention, writing faster than he sleeps, is a man who contains the republic's contradictions as fully as any single person could. He believed in republican government with genuine passion. He owned other human beings with full legal consciousness of what that meant. Both things were true at the same time, without resolution.

### Thomas Jefferson (1743–1826)

Jefferson was in Paris during the Convention, serving as the American Minister to France. He was reading his newspapers with six weeks' delay, writing letters to Madison about what the Constitution should contain, watching the French Ancien Régime begin to buckle under its own contradictions. He was not in the room where it happened; he was the author of the sentence the room was trying to honor.

"All men are created equal" — Jefferson wrote this in seventeen days in Philadelphia in June 1776, in rented rooms, at a folding desk he had designed himself that he carried everywhere. The sentence is so familiar that it has lost the capacity to startle. In 1776 it was startling. No government had ever been founded on this proposition as a stated first principle. Jefferson believed it. He also enslaved 175 people at the time he wrote it, a number that would grow over the following decades.

The question of what Jefferson "really" believed about slavery has occupied historians for two hundred years. The most honest answer is that the question assumes a consistency of belief and practice that Jefferson did not have. He wrote that slavery was a "moral and political depravity." He was, simultaneously, financially dependent on it; Monticello's elegance — the wine cellar, the library, the dumbwaiters that made food appear as if by magic — was built on enslaved labor and could not exist without it. He freed two people in his lifetime. He freed five more in his will, including four of the children he had with Sally Hemings. He sold others to pay debts.

Jefferson and Banneker's exchange in 1791 is one of the founding era's most precisely documented encounters with the paradox. Banneker wrote explicitly. Jefferson replied generously and vaguely and forwarded the almanac to Paris. The gap between what Jefferson said he believed and what he did about it was not secret. It was his life.

### Alexander Hamilton (c.1755–1804)

Hamilton was born in Nevis in the Caribbean, the illegitimate son of a Scottish merchant and a Nevisian woman. He came to New York as a teenager on scholarship. He rose through the Revolution as Washington's aide-de-camp, writing so many of Washington's letters that historians still argue about which ideas were Washington's and which were Hamilton's. He was brilliant, combative, and frequently right about the wrong things at the wrong time.

As the first Secretary of the Treasury, Hamilton built the financial infrastructure of the American republic: the national credit, the Bank of the United States, the customs system that funded the government, the revenue cutters (the predecessors of the Coast Guard) that enforced it. He had a clearer vision than any other founder of what the republic would need to become a commercial and military power. His "Report on Manufactures" (1791) laid out a model of state-led industrial development that, despite being initially rejected, eventually described what the republic actually became.

Hamilton was not a slaveholder. He was not an abolitionist in any active sense, but he did not own enslaved people, and he expressed, in his private correspondence, something closer to consistent opposition to the institution than most of his peers managed. He married into a New York family that enslaved people, which complicated his position considerably.

His choice in the election of 1800 — to work behind the scenes for Jefferson over Burr, despite despising Jefferson's politics — was the act of his career that most honored the republic he had helped build. He chose the institution over his faction. Three years later, Burr, who had never forgotten or forgiven Hamilton's opposition, challenged him to a duel. Hamilton went. He fired into the air, or perhaps he simply missed. Burr did not miss. Hamilton died the next day. He was forty-nine, or thereabouts; his exact birth year is uncertain, like much of his early life.

### Richard Allen (1760–1831)

Allen was born enslaved in Philadelphia, sold with his family to a Delaware plantation owner, converted to Methodism at a camp meeting in his teens, began preaching with his master's permission, and eventually convinced his master that Methodist sermons and manual labor were not incompatible. He worked as a woodcutter, a laborer, a wagon driver, and saved the money to purchase his own freedom at twenty years old. He walked back to Philadelphia a free man.

In 1787 he and Absalom Jones founded the Free African Society. In 1792, after the incident at St. George's Methodist Episcopal Church, they organized a separate congregation. In 1794 Allen dedicated Bethel Church. In 1816 the African Methodist Episcopal denomination was formally organized, with Allen as its first bishop. He was ordained by a white Methodist bishop on the condition that Black Methodist congregations remain formally within the Methodist Episcopal structure; Allen spent years in legal battles to establish Bethel's independence and won.

Allen's account of the St. George's incident is one of the founding era's plainest documents of what the republic looked like from the other side. He does not moralize at the church that pulled his congregation from their knees. He states what happened: "We all went out of the church in a body." The action is the argument.

Allen lived until 1831, long enough to see the republic for which he had no vote produce the Missouri Compromise, the first great formal renegotiation of the founding bargain, and to see the beginnings of the abolitionist movement that would eventually force a second, more violent renegotiation. He is buried in the basement of Mother Bethel AME Church in Philadelphia, which is a National Historic Landmark and is still a functioning congregation.

### Sally Hemings (c.1773–1835)

Sally Hemings left no written record. The record of her existence comes from property inventories, account books, the testimony of her son Madison Hemings (published in an Ohio newspaper in 1873), and the DNA analysis conducted in 1998 that established a genetic link between Jefferson's male line and descendants of Eston Hemings, Sally's youngest son.

What is documented: she was the daughter of John Wayles and Elizabeth Hemings, making her the half-sister of Jefferson's late wife Martha. She was brought to Paris in 1787 to accompany Jefferson's younger daughter. She was legally free in France. She was approximately fifteen when she arrived. She returned to Virginia in 1789. She and Jefferson had a relationship that lasted approximately thirty-eight years and produced at least six children, four of whom survived to adulthood.

Her son Madison Hemings recalled, decades later, that his mother had negotiated the terms of her return from Paris: that Jefferson would free their future children when they came of age. "She was just beginning to understand the French language well," he remembered, "and in France she was free, while if she returned to Virginia she would be re-enslaved." She chose to return. Whether this was a free choice, a coerced one, or something in between that has no clean name in the republic's legal vocabulary is a question the record cannot answer.

What the record can say: she is in the chapter because she was there. She attended the founding era as closely as Madison or Hamilton or Allen. She was in Paris when the Constitution was ratified. She was in Virginia when Jefferson became vice president and then president. She was at Monticello for the election of 1800 and for Jefferson's entire presidency. She was freed informally after Jefferson's death in 1826 and died in Charlottesville in 1835. She is in the figures not because she represents the founding paradox but because she was a person who lived through it, in a room where the author of "all men are created equal" had the power to determine what her life was for.

The chapter does not know what she thought. The chapter knows she was there.

### Benjamin Banneker (1731–1806)

Banneker was a free Black man in Maryland, the grandson of a formerly enslaved African and an English indentured servant. He was largely self-taught. In his forties, he borrowed a neighbor's telescope and books on astronomy and taught himself enough mathematics to calculate the trajectories of celestial bodies. He began publishing almanacs in 1791; they were accurate, successful, and widely distributed. He was part of the surveying team, under Andrew Ellicott, that laid out the boundaries of the District of Columbia.

His letter to Jefferson in August 1791 is one of the period's most precisely aimed documents. He did not ask Jefferson for anything. He pointed out a discrepancy between what Jefferson had written and what Jefferson was doing. He quoted the Declaration. He named the practice of slavery as "fraud and violence." He sent an almanac as evidence of what the Black man's mind could produce when it was given the conditions to develop.

Jefferson's reply was, as his letters to correspondents he found inconvenient often were, gracious and noncommittal. He said he was glad to see evidence of what Banneker had produced. He forwarded the almanac to Paris. He continued to enslave people at Monticello.

The exchange between Banneker and Jefferson is not primarily a story about Jefferson's hypocrisy. It is a story about the republic's founding language being turned on itself by someone the republic had not yet included in its mechanisms. Banneker was using the Declaration — Jefferson's own words — to make a claim that the Constitution had not yet extended to him. This is what the founding paradox looked like from the outside of the founders' circle: not an embarrassment to be managed but a promise to be demanded.
