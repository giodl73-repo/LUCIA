## B-roman-2 — Keith Hopkins — Board Review of Roman Era 1

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Roman social and economic history, demography, the material basis of political power.

**Historical accuracy:**
- The salt trade as Roma's early economic basis is well established. The Via Salaria is correctly identified as pre-dating the city.
- The Tiber crossing as strategic asset is correctly framed.
- The census as the binding of property-military service-political voice is the Republic's foundational mechanism, and the chapter treats it with appropriate seriousness.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter correctly presents the Republic as an aristocratic system, not a democracy. The weighted voting is described accurately and without modern judgment. Good.
- The economic basis of the patrician-plebeian conflict (debt, land, labor) is present but could be stronger. The secession was not just about political rights. It was about debt bondage. The nexum, the debt-bond that could reduce a free man to servitude, was the material reality behind the political struggle.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The Twelve Tables' debt provisions (chains weighing not less than fifteen pounds) are the right kind of evidence: specific, material, grounding the legal abstraction in physical reality.
- The farmer in Section VI is essential. Most Roman history forgets that the Republic was a community of farmers who governed themselves between harvests.
- The garum reference is a good material detail, though it's slightly anachronistic for the 4th century BCE. Garum production at industrial scale is later.

**What needs correction:**
- The garum reference should be qualified. Fish sauce existed early, but the industrial production the chapter implies is more characteristic of the 2nd-1st centuries BCE. For the 4th century, the condiment would be simpler, more local.
- The chapter should note that the Twelve Tables' provisions on debt were eventually reformed (the Lex Poetelia of 326 BCE abolished nexum). This matters because it shows the system responding to economic pressure, which is the material version of the political struggle the chapter describes.
- "The offices were unpaid" is correct for magistracies but should note that the system also provided opportunities for enrichment through provincial governance, military command, and the spoils of war. The unpaid magistracy was sustainable because the men who held it expected returns elsewhere.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS (minor corrections above)
