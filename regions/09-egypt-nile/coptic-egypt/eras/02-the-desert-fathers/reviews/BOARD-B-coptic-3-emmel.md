## BOARD — B-coptic-3 — Stephen Emmel — Review of The Desert Fathers

**Overall:** The chapter gives Shenouda appropriate prominence, and the treatment of the Coptic language as a living continuation of ancient Egyptian is accurate in its broad strokes. The choice to foreground Shenouda's use of Coptic over Greek as a political and theological act is correct and important.

**What is accurate:**
- Shenouda described as archimandrite of the White Monastery for approximately eighty years: correct.
- Shenouda's attendance at the Council of Ephesus (431) with Cyril: correct.
- The White Monastery described as a large compound with men and women in separate enclosures: correct.
- The characterization of Shenouda's sermons as "sustained rhetorical performances, passionate, sometimes angry": this captures the Coptic literary voice accurately. Shenouda's rhetorical style was distinctive: urgent, commanding, morally intense.

**What needs correction:**
1. **Coptic dialect specificity.** The chapter correctly identifies Shenouda's Coptic as Sahidic. However, the linguistic connections claimed need care. The statement "The word for life, onkh, echoes the hieroglyphic ankh" is broadly correct but phonologically simplified. The Sahidic Coptic form is 'onkh (with initial glottal stop), and the connection to the hieroglyphic ankh is well established in Coptic philology. The grammatical claim about verb-first structure mirroring pharaonic inscriptions is also correct for the earlier stages of the language. This is acceptable for a narrative work, though a philologist would add qualifications.
2. **Shenouda's literary corpus.** The chapter describes Shenouda's sermons but does not mention his letters, which are equally important and reveal his administrative authority, his conflicts with local officials, and his role as de facto governor of the region around Sohag. A mention of the letters would round out the portrait.
3. **The White Monastery as architectural survival.** The White Monastery's limestone walls "still stand" is true. The chapter could note that the building itself, with its pharaonic-style stone construction techniques (including reused pharaonic blocks), is physical evidence of the continuity between Egyptian architectural traditions and Christian monastic building.
4. **Bohairic vs. Sahidic.** The chapter does not distinguish between the Sahidic Coptic of Upper Egypt (Shenouda's dialect) and the Bohairic Coptic of the Delta (which eventually became the liturgical standard). This distinction matters for the larger Coptic story but may be appropriately deferred to later eras.

**Factual errors:** None that would require correction in a narrative work. The linguistic claims are simplified but not inaccurate.

**Verdict:** The Coptic language and literary tradition are treated with respect and appropriate specificity. Shenouda is given the prominence he deserves. Minor additions would strengthen the account. Approved.
