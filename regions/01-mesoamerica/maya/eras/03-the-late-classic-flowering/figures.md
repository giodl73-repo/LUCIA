# Figures — The Late Classic Flowering (Era 3)

## Lady K'abal Xook (active c. 681–750 CE)

Wife and queen of Itzamnaaj Bahlam III (Shield Jaguar III) of Yaxchilan. Depicted on Lintels 24, 25, and 26 of Structure 23 in bloodletting and Vision Serpent rituals.

**What we know:** Lady K'abal Xook is one of the best-documented royal women of the Classic Maya period. Lintel 24 records her bloodletting on 9.13.17.15.12 (26 October 681 CE). Lintel 25 shows the Vision Serpent rising from the offering bowl, with a warrior emerging from the serpent's jaws. Lintel 26 shows her presenting a war helmet to Itzamnaaj Bahlam. The inscriptions name her with full titles and her own emblem glyph, indicating that she was of sufficient rank to be identified independently of her husband.

**What we cannot know:** Her personal experience of the bloodletting ritual. The chapter renders the scene through the archaeological and epigraphic record (the lintel carvings) rather than through invented interiority. Her expression, carved in stone, is the closest the record comes to her inner state.

**Sources:** Carolyn Tate, *Yaxchilan: The Design of a Maya Ceremonial City* (1992); Linda Schele and Mary Ellen Miller, *The Blood of Kings* (1986); David Stuart, various epigraphic readings.

## K'inich Janaab Pakal (603–683 CE)

K'uhul ajaw of Lakamha (Palenque). Acceded 9.9.2.4.8 (29 July 615 CE) at age twelve. Died 9.12.11.5.18 (31 March 683 CE). Reigned sixty-eight years. Builder of the Temple of the Inscriptions, the Palace complex. Buried in a jade-mask sarcophagus beneath his funerary pyramid.

**What we know:** Pakal is among the most extensively documented Maya rulers. The Temple of the Inscriptions contains 617 glyph blocks recording his dynasty and reign. His tomb, uncovered by Alberto Ruz Lhuillier in 1952, contained his skeleton with jade mask, earflares, and grave goods. Osteological analysis suggests he lived to his seventies or eighties, consistent with the inscriptional record. The sarcophagus lid depicts his descent as the Maize God into the Underworld.

**What we cannot know:** His personal motivations for the specific building program he undertook. The inscriptions record his public acts within the Long Count framework. His private thoughts, his relationships beyond what the dynastic record shows, and the nature of his daily governance are not accessible.

**Sources:** Merle Greene Robertson, *The Sculpture of Palenque* (4 vols., 1983–91); David Stuart and George Stuart, *Palenque: Eternal City of the Maya* (2008); Alberto Ruz Lhuillier, *El Templo de las Inscripciones* (1973).

## Yuknoom Ch'een II "the Great" (ruled c. 636–686 CE)

K'uhul ajaw of Kaan (Calakmul). Orchestrated a vassal network spanning the southern lowlands. Defeated Tikal in 9.6.8.4.2 (562 CE was the earlier defeat; Yuknoom Ch'een continued Kaan's dominance through the mid-seventh century).

**What we know:** Yuknoom Ch'een II is attested in inscriptions at Calakmul and at numerous vassal sites (Dos Pilas, Cancuen, Naranjo, El Peru). His name and titles appear in connection with political events across the lowlands, documenting a web of alliances that constituted the most extensive polity in Classic Maya history. The erosion of Calakmul's limestone monuments has damaged many of his inscriptions, creating an asymmetry in the record: his rival Tikal's better-preserved monuments receive more scholarly attention.

**What we cannot know:** The full extent of his alliance network. Calakmul's eroded monuments mean that his record is incomplete. His own voice, in the form of well-preserved dedicatory texts, is largely lost to weathering.

**Honest limit:** The chapter acknowledges the evidentiary asymmetry between Tikal and Calakmul. Calakmul's political importance is well established, but the details of Yuknoom Ch'een's reign are less accessible than Pakal's or Jasaw Chan K'awiil's.

**Sources:** Simon Martin and Nikolai Grube, *Chronicle of the Maya Kings and Queens* (2nd ed., 2008); Ramón Carrasco Vargas et al., excavation reports from Calakmul.

## Jasaw Chan K'awiil (ruled c. 682–734 CE)

K'uhul ajaw of Mutul (Tikal). Achieved the star war against Calakmul on 9.13.3.7.18 (695 CE). Builder of Temple I. Restored Tikal's inscriptional record after the "hiatus."

**What we know:** Jasaw Chan K'awiil's victory over Yich'aak K'ahk' of Calakmul is recorded on Lintel 3 of Temple I at Tikal. The lintel shows him seated above a bound captive. Temple I (his funerary pyramid) and Temple II (built for his wife, Lady Kalajuun Une' Mo') face each other across Tikal's Great Plaza. His reign marks the resumption of monument erection at Tikal after more than a century of silence.

**Sources:** Martin and Grube (2008); Christopher Jones, *Tikal Report No. 33A: Monuments and Inscriptions of Tikal* (1977).

## Yik'in Chan K'awiil (ruled c. 734–766 CE)

Son of Jasaw Chan K'awiil. K'uhul ajaw of Mutul (Tikal). Continued his father's campaigns. Builder of Temple IV, the tallest structure at Tikal. Defeated El Peru and Naranjo.

**What we know:** His victories are recorded on carved wooden lintels in Temples IV and VI. Temple IV's roof comb rises approximately 65 meters above the forest floor.

**Sources:** Martin and Grube (2008); Jones (1977).

## Lady Six Sky (Wak Chanil Ajaw) (active c. 682–741 CE)

Royal woman from Dos Pilas who arrived at Naranjo on 9.12.10.5.12 (682 CE) and restored its dynasty. Depicted on stelae in the posture of a sovereign, standing on a captive.

**What we know:** Her arrival is recorded in Naranjo's inscriptions. Within a few years of her arrival, Naranjo resumed monument erection and military campaigns. She appears on at least one stela in the conventional posture of a k'uhul ajaw, though whether she held the title formally or governed through her son is debated among epigraphers.

**What we cannot know:** Whether she was regent for her son or ruler in her own right. The inscriptions can be read either way. The chapter presents both possibilities.

**Sources:** Martin and Grube (2008); Schele and Miller (1986); Kathryn Reese-Taylor et al., studies on Classic Maya queenship.

## A Note on Sources

All named individuals in this chapter are attested in the epigraphic record. Long Count dates are drawn from published epigraphic readings. No composite figures are used. The primary evidence base for this chapter is the Maya inscriptional corpus itself, which records historical events, named rulers, and specific dates in a fully phonetic writing system whose decipherment, begun by Yuri Knorosov (1952) and extended by Tatiana Proskouriakoff (1960), Linda Schele, David Stuart, and others, now permits the reading of most Classic Maya texts.

The chapter acknowledges two key limitations: (1) the erosion of Calakmul's monuments creates an evidentiary bias toward Tikal's perspective; (2) the destruction of most Maya codices after Spanish contact means that the surviving written record (three codices, plus the inscriptional corpus) represents a fraction of what the Maya produced.
