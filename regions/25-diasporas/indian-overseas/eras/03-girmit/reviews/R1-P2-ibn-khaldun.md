## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of Indian Overseas Era 3 Opening

**Overall:** The opening correctly identifies the structural mechanism: the contract (girmit) as a system for converting rural distress into plantation labor. The system's logic is visible. What I want to see in the chapter is whether the system's internal contradictions will drive the arc.

**What works:**
- The zamindar system is presented as the pre-condition, not the cause. The opening understands that indenture did not create poverty; it harvested it. The zamindar's collector coming twice in one month, the failed rains, the debt that makes the arkatti's offer rational. This is structural analysis done through scene, which is the correct method.
- The transformation of the contract from London legal instrument to Bhojpuri word is a model of how institutional power degrades as it descends through social layers. Each stage "shed something." This is asabiyyah analysis: the cohesion of the colonial system requires that the contract's obligations become less legible to the people it binds.
- The 1.5 million figure at the close is well-placed. It arrives after the reader has been inside one family's experience, so it lands as scale rather than statistic.

**What doesn't:**
- The system has two sides: the push (debt, famine, zamindar extraction) and the pull (the arkatti's promises). The push is well-rendered. The pull is rendered only through the arkatti's speech. What is missing is the pull's institutional structure: who pays the arkatti? What is his commission? What is the chain from the plantation owner in Trinidad to the recruiter in Shahabad? The opening does not need to answer all of this, but the chapter must, or the system will appear as accident rather than design.
- The colonial government's role is absent. The indenture system was not a private arrangement; it was a state-managed labor migration system with regulations, inspections, and ships chartered under government authority. If the chapter treats the system as a set of individual recruiters approaching individual families, it will miss the structural architecture.

**The question I'd push on:**
What is the terminal condition of this system? The indenture was abolished in 1917. Why? Not for humanitarian reasons alone. What structural contradiction caused the system to consume itself? The chapter needs to name this.
