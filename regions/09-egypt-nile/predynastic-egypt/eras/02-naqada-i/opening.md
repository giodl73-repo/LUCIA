# Naqada I — The Amratian

The copper was cold when he pulled it from the mold, but it would not stay cold for long.

The man working in Nubt held the awl and turned it against the morning light that came through the reed screen of his workshop. The ore had come from the Eastern Desert, from the wadis where the red rock broke open to show green veins of malachite, and he had smelted it himself in a crucible no larger than a drinking bowl, blowing through a reed tube until the charcoal burned white and the metal wept out of the stone. The awl lay in his palm, slender, the color of a new wound. It was not the first copper tool anyone in the valley had made. But it was his, and it was good, and the point would pierce leather or score wet clay as cleanly as a thorn.

He did not know that three days' walk downriver, at a settlement where the wadi met the floodplain, another man was shaping the same object from the same ore.

The copper was the same because the source was the same. The wadis of the Eastern Desert held malachite in seams through the red sandstone, and the people of the upper valley had been walking those wadis for generations, bringing back the green stone to grind into pigment for the palettes they painted their eyes with. Somewhere in those generations, someone noticed that the green stone, when it sat too long in a hot fire, did something the flint and the bone and the clay did not do. It changed. It ran like water and cooled like stone but was neither. It was copper, and it could be shaped, and it held an edge, and it gleamed.

The people of Nubt did not have a word for revolution. They had copper. They had the fine-grained clay of the riverbank, which they shaped into vessels with walls so thin that a thumb behind them showed pink through the fired surface, and they painted those vessels with white lime: hippos in the current, crocodiles watching from the shallows, a figure with upraised arms that might be a woman, might be a goddess, might be both. They had ochre, the red earth, which they ground on slate palettes and mixed with fat and applied to the skin of the living and the skin of the dead. They had flint, which they knapped into blades of a precision that would not be surpassed in the valley for another thousand years. And they had the flood.

The flood was everything. It came in summer, when the far rains swelled the river past its banks, and it spread across the low ground and stood there for weeks, and when it receded it left the black earth, the kemet, the silt that meant another year of grain. When the water rose, the work of the fields stopped and the work of everything else began. Pots were made. Copper was smelted. Flint was shaped. The dead were buried. The living made things for the dead to carry with them, because the dead were not gone. They were somewhere else, and they would need what the living could provide.

In the cemeteries along the desert edge, the people of Nubt laid their dead on their left sides, facing west, where the sun went down and the world of the dead began. They bent the knees and folded the arms, as though the dead were sleeping, or waiting to be born. They placed beside each body what that person had been in life: pottery for the potter. Flint blades for the knapper. Palettes for grinding the ochre that colored the face for the gods. Copper tools, when the dead had worked in copper. Every object was chosen. Every placement was a sentence in a language the living spoke to the dead without writing it down.

The network was forming. The copper came from the Eastern Desert. The obsidian, black and razor-sharp, came from further still, from sources that may have been as distant as the southern highlands. The shells in some graves had traveled from the Red Sea coast, a hundred miles of desert in someone's pack. None of these materials moved on their own. Someone carried them. Someone traded them. Someone at Nubt had something that someone at the Red Sea wanted, and someone at the Red Sea had shells that someone at Nubt placed in a grave. Neither would have called this a network, because it had no name. You made something, and it traveled, and something came back, and the world grew larger with each exchange.

The man in the workshop finished the awl and set it beside the crucible. Outside, the sun was climbing and the river was low. The flood would come again. When it did, the fields would vanish under water, and in the weeks of waiting he would make more tools, and some of them would travel, and some of them would end up in the ground with someone who had not yet died, and the network would grow, and no one would name it for four thousand years.

He picked up the next piece of ore and blew on the coals.
