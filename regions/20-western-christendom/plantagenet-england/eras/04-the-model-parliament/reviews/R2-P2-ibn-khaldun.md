## Assessment of the Production Bible

The notes have addressed my principal concern from PANEL-1. The engine is named precisely: "consent-for-taxation as self-reinforcing institutional practice." The mechanism is clear: each Parliament creates a class of participants with vested interest in the institution's continuity. This is the institutional 'asabiyya I identified. Good.

**On the 1295 formula's generativity:** The notes plan to treat the three-estate structure (Section 4, beginning) but this is actually the most important analytical point and should appear earlier — in Section 2 or the opening's integration. The reason the 1295 Parliament became "the model" is not simply that it included knights and burgesses — previous parliaments had done that — but that it brought all three estates together in a unified assembly for unified consent to a specific tax. The unity of consent is the constitutional novelty. The notes should flag this more sharply.

**On the constituency analysis:** The notes identify knights, burgesses, and the legal profession as the Parliament's stakeholder constituency. This is correct but the legal profession deserves more attention. The common law's expansion under Edward I created a class of trained lawyers — serjeants-at-law, attorneys, apprentices at the Inns — whose livelihoods depended on the common law courts' authority. These men had professional interest in the principle that the king was under the law. They were the system's human maintenance infrastructure. The chapter should name this constituency.

**Terminal condition assessment:** The notes classify this as adjacent to "legitimacy gap" rather than "vindicating." I agree with the taxonomy. But the notes should be more precise: Edward II's deposition is not simply a failure of his kingship. It is the Parliament demonstrating that it can act as a constituent assembly — that it can make and unmake kings — which is a power it was never given and never explicitly claimed. The Parliament did not legalize the deposition; it was used to *legitimate* a deposition that had already been decided by military force. The distinction matters: Parliament gave form to Mortimer's power, not authority. The chapter should register this gap between form and substance.

**Open question 2 (year books case):** A quo warranto proceeding is the strongest choice. Edward I's campaign to audit the lords' jurisdictional claims — "by what warrant do you exercise this right?" — is the common law at maximum institutional confidence. The lords who claimed their rights by hereditary custom were told they needed a charter; those who had no charter were stripped of their jurisdictions. This is the king using the law to extend royal authority over aristocratic privilege. The dramatic irony is rich: the same legal machinery used to subordinate the lords to the king will later subordinate the king to Parliament.

**Clearance:** The causal architecture is sound. The chapter is ready to write. Flag the three-estate unity point for Section 2.
