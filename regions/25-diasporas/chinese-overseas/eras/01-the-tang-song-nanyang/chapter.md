# The Tang-Song Nanyang

## I. The Passage

The captain knew the strait by the color of the water.

Brown meant the Johor river was pushing silt into the channel, which meant rain in the hills to the north, which meant the northeast monsoon had not yet spent itself, which meant he was on time. Green, the pale green of shallow sand, meant the current was pulling south toward the Riau islands, and a captain who did not correct for it would find himself two days off course with a hold full of porcelain he could not afford to lose. He had been making this passage for twenty years, Quanzhou to Sanfoqi and back, twice a year with the turning of the winds, and the water had taught him what no chart could teach, because no chart held what the water held: the memory of every crossing, the pull of the current against the hull at each season, the way the sea changed color at the edge of the shallows off Temasek where the strait narrowed and the tide ran fast.

His name was Lin. His hands were cracked from rope and salt, the knuckles swollen in the way of men who had spent more years gripping rigging than holding a writing brush, and he stood at the tiller with his bare feet braced against the deck planking, feeling the current shift through the soles of his feet the way a blind man reads a face with his fingers. The hull of the junk creaked around him, the bulkheads groaning as the swell lifted the stern, and the matting sail caught the northeast wind with a sound like a hand slapping cloth. His crew called to each other in Hokkien, the short syllables carrying across the deck between the creak of timber and the slap of the sea.

His family had been sailors out of Quanzhou for three generations, Hokkien speakers from the district south of the Min River where the hills pushed the farms to the sea and the sea pushed the farmers onto boats. His grandfather had made the first crossing to Sanfoqi when the Tang court still held the southern ports. His father had made the crossing when Srivijaya still collected tolls at Palembang and the harbor master weighed the cargo in his own scales. Now Lin made the crossing in a different world. Srivijaya's grip on the strait had loosened, broken by the Chola raids from southern India in 1025 and by the rise of rival ports at Jambi and along the Malay coast, and the ports were competing for the trade that the old monopoly had held, and a Hokkien captain who knew which harbor masters could be trusted and which would weigh the cargo twice was worth more than his ship.

The junk carried what Quanzhou produced and what the Nanyang wanted. In the forward hold: Dehua porcelain, white as pared bone, packed in rice straw with a care that came from knowing what breakage cost, and beside it crates of Longquan celadon, the green glaze smooth as river jade. Behind the porcelain: bolts of silk heavy enough for the humidity that rotted lighter cloth within a season. Iron pans, cast in Fujian. Copper cash, strung on cords in lots of a thousand, the round coins with the square holes that passed as currency from Quanzhou to Quilon. What the junk would carry home filled a different hold: camphor from Borneo, the crystals packed tight in palm leaves because camphor sublimated in the open air and a careless trader arrived home with empty jars. Sandalwood from Timor, stacked clear of the bilge, because wet sandalwood lost its scent and its price. Pepper from the Sumatran highlands, the smell sharp enough to carry across the deck even now, mixing with the salt air and the faint sweetness of the hull timbers baking in the equatorial sun.

Lin did not carry maps. He carried the route the way he carried his dialect, in his mouth and in his hands, learned from his father and corrected by experience. What he did carry was a list. The names were written in his own hand on a sheet of paper folded into his belt: the men in each port who would buy what he sold, who would sell what he needed, who would store his goods between monsoons, who would lend him silver if a cargo went bad, and who would remember the debt. The list was his livelihood. It was also his law. In the ports of the Nanyang, where no Chinese magistrate sat and no Song court could hear a case, the relationships on that list were the only contract that held. Lin knew this because he had seen what happened when the contract broke. A Teochew trader in Champa had cheated a Hokkien merchant on a camphor shipment two seasons back, and within a single monsoon cycle the man could not find warehouse space in any port from Palembang to Quanzhou. The network remembered. The network did not forgive.

Later generations would call it guanxi, though the practice was older than the name. It ran on dialect and surname and the knowledge that the man who sold you short in one port would find every door closed in the next. A Hokkien trader could walk into a warehouse in Palembang where he had never been and find a room, a meal, and a line of credit, because the warehouse keeper spoke Hokkien and came from the same district and belonged to the same surname association, and the association held debts the way the sea held currents: silently, structurally, across distances that no single man could cross.

Lin drank his tea, lukewarm now in the cup his wife had packed in Quanzhou, the glaze chipped at the rim. The strait was narrowing. The Malay pilot he had taken aboard at Temasek stood at the bow, reading the shoals by the ripple patterns in the current, and Lin watched the man's hands, the small gestures that meant deeper water here, shallower there, a reef to port that the pilot knew and Lin did not because the pilot had been born on these waters and the strait belonged to him the way the Min River belonged to Lin. To the north, the green hills of the Malay coast. To the south, the low shoreline of Sumatra, hazy in the afternoon heat. The wind was steady, the cargo was sound, and the names on the list in his belt were waiting in ports that the monsoon would reach before the season turned.

## II. The Port

The smell reached him before the harbor did.

Rotting jackfruit and dried fish and the dense sweetness of frangipani blossoms that grew along the waterfront, and beneath all of it the mineral smell of the river mouth where the brown freshwater mixed with the salt sea and the mangrove roots held the silt together in a tangle of wood and mud. The port sat where the river widened into its delta, the Malay houses built on stilts above the tidal flats, their atap roofs dark against the green of the palms. Fishing boats crowded the river's near bank, narrow outriggers pulled up onto the mud, their nets drying in the sun. On the far bank, where the deeper water allowed the ocean-going vessels to anchor, the junks and dhows rode at their moorings, Chinese and Arab and Tamil vessels sharing the same anchorage, and the warehouses of the foreign traders lined the shore.

Lin had been coming to this port for twelve years.

The Chinese quarter occupied a stretch of the waterfront between the river and the harbor master's compound. It was not large. Thirty or forty families lived there permanently, the men who had stayed after the monsoon turned and found that staying was more profitable than returning. They had built in timber and thatch, the local way, because the local ruler had not given them permission to build in stone, and the permission mattered because the land was his and the Chinese presence was tolerated, not guaranteed. The Mazu temple stood at the quarter's center, a small structure with a red-painted lintel and a roof curved in the Fujian style, and inside, in the smoky dimness of incense and oil lamps, the sea goddess looked out from her shrine with the calm face of a woman who had watched a thousand departures and knew which ones would return.

A young man named Chen stood in front of the warehouse of the Lin surname association and waited. He had arrived three days earlier on a smaller junk from Quanzhou, carrying a cargo of iron tools and a letter from his father. The letter was addressed to the warehouse keeper, a man Chen had never met, and it said what such letters always said: this is my son, he carries goods of this value, extend him the courtesies of the association. Chen's father had been specific about one thing. Trust the association, he had said. Do not lend to anyone outside it, do not store your goods in anyone else's warehouse, and do not eat anyone else's food until you know whose salt you are eating. The advice was old, older than Chen's father, older than the association itself. It was the first rule of the Nanyang trade.

The warehouse keeper, an older Hokkien man named Tan who had lived in the port for twenty years and spoke Malay as fluently as he spoke his native dialect, came to the door and read the letter and looked at Chen and looked at the cargo and calculated. The iron tools were good quality, the sort that the upriver Malay traders would buy for resale to the farming communities in the interior. The letter was from a man Tan knew by reputation and by three prior seasons of reliable dealing. Tan opened the warehouse door. The interior was dark and cool, the air thick with the smell of camphor and raw silk and the particular dry scent of stored pepper, and the goods were stacked along the walls in an order that only the warehouse keeper fully understood: this season's arrivals near the door, last season's stock farther back, the goods awaiting specific buyers separated by wooden partitions from the goods available for open sale.

Chen carried his iron tools inside and set them down where Tan indicated, and Tan marked the entry in a ledger, and the transaction that had begun with a letter from Quanzhou was now held in the warehouse of a man in the Nanyang, secured by nothing except the shared dialect, the shared surname, and the shared knowledge that the association remembered everything and forgot nothing.

The Malay market was a hundred paces up the riverbank, and it was a different world.

The Malay merchants sold what the interior produced: rattan, beeswax, forest resins, bird nests prized for soup, and the camphor crystals that the Chinese traders valued above almost anything the Nanyang offered. A Malay trader named Ismail sat under a palm-thatch awning sorting camphor by grade, his fingers separating the clear crystals from the cloudy ones with a speed that came from a lifetime of handling the stuff. He knew which trees produced the finest camphor, and he knew which upriver collectors to trust, and he knew what the Chinese would pay for the clear grade versus the cloudy, and this knowledge was as much his livelihood as the camphor itself. When Lin arrived at the market, the negotiation between the two men was conducted in a Malay that had absorbed enough Hokkien trade-words to handle prices, quantities, and delivery dates without either man reaching for the other's language. The camphor had come from the interior forests, carried downriver by collectors who had their own arrangements with the forest communities who tapped the trees, and each hand the camphor passed through took a share that the next hand accounted for. By the time it reached Lin's junk, the camphor carried the costs of every man who had touched it, and the price Lin paid reflected a chain of negotiation that stretched from the forest canopy to the deck of his ship.

The two markets, Chinese and Malay, were separate but not sealed. The Chinese warehouse keeper bought dried fish and rice from the Malay market for the association's kitchen. The Malay traders stored their goods in Chinese warehouses when the local facilities were full, paying rent in kind. Children from the Chinese quarter played with children from the Malay kampung along the waterfront, and the languages mixed in their mouths the way the river water mixed with the sea at the delta, not yet a new thing but no longer entirely the old things either.

## III. The Song Boom

The harbor at Quanzhou in the years after the Song court moved south was louder than any harbor in the world.

The fall of Kaifeng to the Jurchen in 1127 had pushed the court to Lin'an, and the loss of the northern territories had pushed the treasury toward the sea. The Song dynasty had always taxed maritime trade, but now it needed maritime trade the way a farmer needs rain. The shibosi, the superintendent of maritime trade, sat in his office above the Quanzhou customs wharf, and the office smelled of ink and old paper and the particular anxiety of a man whose numbers had to grow. His brush moved across the manifest: one junk departing for Sanfoqi, cargo as listed, duty paid at roughly one-tenth of value in kind. He stamped the manifest with the customs seal, and the stamp was permission to sail, and the permission was revenue, and the revenue was what held the Southern Song together.

The shibosi's name was Pu, and his family had been in Quanzhou for two generations, Arab merchants who had married into the local community and whose trade connections and political relationships with the Song court had earned them the administrative post. That an Arab Muslim family administered the Chinese customs house was not unusual in Quanzhou. The port had been absorbing foreign merchants since the Tang dynasty, and the foreigners who stayed became local, and the locals who traded reached farther than any single port, and the customs house that regulated them all ran on the principle that revenue mattered more than origin. Pu counted the junks leaving the harbor in a single month and wrote the number in his report to Lin'an. The number was larger than last year's. The court would be pleased. The court was always pleased when the number was larger.

What the numbers measured was a transformation that no single official had planned.

The Song court minted copper cash in quantities that flooded the Nanyang. A string of a thousand copper coins, heavy enough that a porter could carry only a few strings at a time, became the standard currency of the southern seas not because anyone decreed it but because the coins were reliable and the supply was vast. In the ports of the Nanyang, where a dozen currencies competed and barter was the fallback, Song copper became the medium that simplified everything. A Hokkien merchant in Palembang could price his camphor in copper, sell it for copper, and remit the copper to Quanzhou through the association's accounts, and the entire transaction ran on a currency that the Song court controlled and the Nanyang could not do without. The coins with the square holes turned the Chinese traders from participants in the Nanyang economy into its infrastructure.

The scale of trade that this monetization produced was visible in the harbor. Zhao Rugua, who served as shibosi at Quanzhou in the 1220s, compiled a record of the foreign lands and their goods that remains the most detailed account of the Nanyang trade at its Song-era height. He listed the countries: Sanfoqi, Shepo (Java), Boni (Borneo), Champa, the lands of the Arabs and the Indians. He listed the goods: the pepper, the camphor, the sandalwood, the rhinoceros horn, the pearls, the coral, the incense woods. He listed the ships: the great junks that could carry hundreds of men and thousands of piculs of cargo, with multiple decks, watertight compartments, and the magnetic compass that the Chinese had been using since the eleventh century. The compass did not replace the captain's knowledge of the water. It supplemented it. Lin's sons could cross the South China Sea in the clouded months when the stars were hidden, holding a course that their grandfather had navigated by instinct and luck.

The wealth flowed in both directions. The Nanyang goods that reached Quanzhou entered a market that consumed them, refined them, and re-exported them. The camphor went to the medicine shops and the temple incense burners. The pepper went to the kitchens of the northern provinces, where the taste for southern spices had grown with the wealth of the Song economy. The sandalwood went to the carvers and the perfumers. And from Quanzhou the manufactured goods flowed back: the porcelain, the silk, the iron, the copper cash, in volumes that dwarfed the trade of Lin's grandfather's generation by a factor that Zhao Rugua could count but not explain. The shibosi counted. The merchants traded. The monsoon carried. And the Chinese communities in the Nanyang grew, because where the goods went the people followed, and where the people stayed the institutions formed, and where the institutions held the community endured.

## IV. The Settlement

The temple burned incense day and night, and the smoke settled into the timber and the thatch until the building itself smelled of prayer.

The Mazu temple at the center of the Chinese quarter in Palembang was not grand. It was a single room with a wooden altar, a painted statue of the sea goddess in red and gold robes, and a collection of votive offerings that told the history of the community better than any chronicle: model ships carved by sailors who had survived bad crossings, copper coins left by merchants who had prospered, strips of red cloth tied by women whose husbands had not yet returned. The wooden fish drum sat beside the altar, and the temple keeper, a man too old for the sea trade, struck it at dawn and dusk, and the hollow sound carried through the quarter the way the muezzin's call carried through a Muslim port, marking the day, gathering the community, reminding the living that the gods were paying attention.

The Chinese who settled in the Nanyang ports did not come as colonists. They came as sojourners, men who intended to trade for a season or two and return to Quanzhou with their profits. What kept them was the logic of the network itself. A man who stayed in the Nanyang between monsoons saved the cost and risk of two crossings. He could store his goods, monitor the local market, build relationships with the Malay and Javanese traders that a seasonal visitor could not match, and time his purchases to catch the best prices. The man who stayed had an advantage over the man who left, and the advantage compounded each season. Within a generation, the sojourners had become settlers, and the settlers needed the institutions that settlers everywhere need: a temple for the gods, a school for the children, a cemetery for the dead, and a system for governing themselves.

The huiguan was that system. Organized by surname, by dialect, or by district of origin, the huiguan was the association that held the Chinese community together in the absence of Chinese law. It settled disputes between members, using procedures that the elders knew from memory and that bore a resemblance to the mediation practices of Fujian villages but adapted for the circumstances of a community in a foreign port. It maintained the cemetery on the hill above the harbor, where the graves faced northwest toward Fujian, and where the families burned paper offerings at Qingming and swept the paths between the markers and told the dead what the living had done since the last visit. It funded the school where the children of the community learned to read and write Chinese, because a generation that could not read the account books could not operate the network, and a generation that could not write home could not sustain the connections that made the community Chinese.

The children of the settlements were becoming something new.

A trader named Huang married a Malay woman in Palembang, one of the first such marriages in a pattern that would, over centuries, produce its own culture: a ceremony at the Mazu temple, a feast at the huiguan hall, and a bride-price negotiated between the groom's association and the bride's family according to principles that neither Hokkien nor Malay custom fully owned. The wedding food was the evidence of what was happening. On one table: Hokkien noodles in a sauce of dark soy and pork, the dishes Huang's mother would have made in Quanzhou. On the other: Malay rendang, slow-cooked in coconut milk and turmeric, the spices sharp and warm. And between the tables, moving in both directions, the dishes that belonged to neither tradition and to both: a laksa of rice noodles in coconut broth with shrimp paste and lime leaf that the Hokkien women of the quarter had learned from their Malay neighbors and remade with the Hokkien instinct for noodles and the Malay knowledge of spice. The guests ate from both tables and from the space between them, and the children, who spoke Hokkien to their fathers and Malay to their mothers, moved through the feast the way they moved through their days: in two languages, in two kitchens, in a world that the people who built it had not planned but that the people who lived in it understood.

The local rulers watched the Chinese quarter grow. They watched it because the quarter paid well: the harbor tolls, the warehouse rents, the portion of trade revenue that the ruler's agents collected. They watched it because the Chinese merchants provided goods and services that the local economy wanted and the local traders could not supply as cheaply. And they watched it because the quarter was becoming a community with its own institutions, its own authority, its own loyalty to a homeland across the sea, and this was a thing that rulers noticed. In the ports where the Chinese community was small and the ruler was strong, the arrangement was simple: the Chinese paid, the ruler permitted, and the trade continued. In the ports where the Chinese community had grown large enough to be the primary source of harbor revenue, the arrangement was more complicated, because the ruler needed the Chinese more than the Chinese needed any particular ruler, and both sides knew it, and the knowledge sat between them like a cargo neither had agreed to carry.

## V. The Monsoon Turns

The wind shifted in April.

The northeast monsoon spent its last breath across the strait, and the air went still for days, heavy and wet, the heat pressing down on the harbor like a hand on the back of the neck. The crews felt it in the change of the morning: the dew heavier on the deck, the clouds building earlier, the sea flattening into a calm that meant the old wind was dead and the new one had not yet been born. Then the southwest monsoon came, a wall of warm air from the Indian Ocean, carrying the smell of rain from seas the Hokkien sailors had never crossed, and the ships that had come south with the northeast wind prepared to go home.

Lin loaded his return cargo in the still days before the monsoon shifted. The camphor crystals from Ismail, wrapped in palm leaves and stored in sealed jars. The pepper, dried and bagged. The sandalwood billets from the warehouses. In the hold, each item sat in the place Lin's twenty years had taught him to put it: the lightest goods forward, the heaviest aft, the volatile camphor sealed and stored amidships where the hull's temperature was most stable. He checked the manifest against the goods and the goods against the manifest and the numbers matched, and the numbers matching was how you knew the network had done its work, because every item in the hold had passed through hands that the association tracked and every price reflected a negotiation that the association would remember.

The young man Chen was not going home. He had stored his remaining iron tools in Tan's warehouse and bought a consignment of pepper with the credit his father's letter had earned him, and he intended to sell the pepper when the next season's ships arrived from Quanzhou and buy more iron tools with the profit. Tan had offered him a corner of the warehouse to sleep in and a place at the association's table, and Chen had accepted, because the alternative was the cost of the return voyage and the loss of the relationships he had spent three months building. He would stay through the southwest monsoon, and when the northeast wind returned and the junks came south again, he would be in the port already, his goods stored, his relationships established, his Malay improved enough to negotiate without a translator. His father would understand. His father had done the same thing, twenty years earlier, in a different port.

The community was built from decisions like this, repeated across a hundred ports over three hundred years. Each man who stayed added weight to the settlement. Each settlement that grew added a node to the network. Each node that held made the next crossing easier, the next credit line longer, the next generation's decision to stay more natural. By the time the Song dynasty fell to the Mongols in 1279 and the Yuan court's interest in the maritime south created new pressures and new possibilities, the Chinese communities of the Nanyang had been in place long enough to have their own genealogies, their own cemeteries, their own stories about the founders who had come from Fujian with a cargo and a list of names and had never gone back.

The Mongol-Yuan period did not create the Nanyang network. It found the network already built and tried to use it. Kublai Khan's attempted invasions of Champa in 1283 and of Java in 1293 sent military fleets through waters the Hokkien traders had been crossing for generations, and the campaigns, which mostly failed, had the unintended effect of increasing Chinese naval presence in the southern seas and expanding the commercial connections that the military ventures disrupted. Chinese merchants in the Nanyang found the Yuan period profitable but unstable, because the court that encouraged trade also sent armies, and the local polities that had tolerated Chinese traders were less welcoming to Chinese soldiers, and the distinction between the two was not always visible from the harbor.

In the ports where the Mongol campaigns had passed through, the Chinese quarters held. They held because the guanxi network had been in place long enough to absorb the disruption, and because the local rulers, whatever they thought of the Mongol fleets, still needed the Chinese merchants and their goods and their copper cash. But the experience left a residue. In Java, in the years after the failed Mongol invasion, the relationship between the Chinese community and the Javanese court acquired a wariness that had not been there before. The Chinese were useful. The Chinese were also, potentially, the forward edge of an empire that sent armies. The calculation that the local rulers made, the calculation between the revenue the Chinese brought and the risk the Chinese represented, was the same calculation that would be made again and again across the centuries to come, in every port where the Chinese settled and the local ruler watched them grow.

Lin stood on the deck of his junk as the southwest monsoon filled the matting sail and the harbor fell behind. The temple smoke rose above the roofline of the Chinese quarter. The Malay pilot had gone ashore. The strait opened ahead, and the sea beyond the strait was the grey-green of deep water, the color that meant the open passage to Champa and the long run north to Quanzhou. His hands gripped the tiller, the rope scars white against his salt-darkened skin. In the hold, the cargo was balanced. In his belt, the list of names was folded, the paper softened by months of sweat and handling, the ink fading but the names still legible, still binding, still the only law that held between the monsoons.

Behind him, the port went on. The temple keeper struck the wooden fish drum, and the sound carried over the water, and Chen heard it from the warehouse where he sat counting his pepper, and Tan heard it from the association hall where he was recording the season's debts, and the Malay traders in the upriver market heard it and knew it for what it was: the sound of a community that had come from somewhere else and was not leaving.

The monsoon carried the ships. The network carried everything else.
