The notes are strong on the matapi, which is what I asked for. Tirite palm fiber (Ischnosiphon arouma) is correct — this is the plant the ethnographic and botanical record confirms. The description of the press mechanism — loading the mash, twisting the basket over a peg — captures the basic technique. For the chapter to meet the v1.3 and v1.5 Evidence standards on knowledge-at-method-level, I want to push the scene one step further.

What makes bitter manioc processing intellectually remarkable is the problem it solved: the wild cassava plant produces linamarin, which hydrolyzes to hydrogen cyanide when cells are damaged. Eating unprocessed grated manioc is lethal. Someone — generations of someones, over centuries of observation and accumulated knowledge — worked out that grating the tuber, extracting the liquid under pressure, and then either extended drying or cooking at high temperature would render it safe. This is not obvious. It is a sophisticated knowledge system that required both botanical understanding (the toxin is in the liquid, not the fiber) and processing knowledge (the matapi extracts the liquid efficiently). The chapter should render the moment of grating-and-pressing as a scene that makes the reader feel the intellectual precision of the technique, not just watch the woman's hands.

The reef-approach passage I suggested is in the plan ("Kurima reading the surf patterns as a new knowledge domain"). I want to confirm that this scene should be short and specific: what he sees (the color change over coral versus sand), what he hears (surf tone changes over the reef channels, shallower breaks and deeper ones), and what he does with the knowledge he does not yet have — the moment of acknowledging that he is navigating by a system he has to learn rather than one he inherited. That gap between knowledge systems is the chapter's deepest subject. Name the gap explicitly through his body.

The Saladoid ceramic section is good in concept. One suggestion: the decorative convention to describe should be the crosshatched decoration (the "zone-incised crosshatch" style) that archaeologists use to identify Saladoid assemblages at a glance. The chapter can render this as the potter's mark — the specific pattern that identifies the pot as belonging to the network. Not every pot carries it; the ones that move through the trade network do. The decoration is the certificate of belonging.
