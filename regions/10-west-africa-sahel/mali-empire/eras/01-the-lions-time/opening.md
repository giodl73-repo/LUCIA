# The Lion's Time

The boy could not walk.

The jelilu who carry this story — the Kouyate, the Diabate, the Konde, who have carried it for thirty generations from mouth to ear to mouth again — begin here, at the lowest point, because the lowest point is where the story earns its meaning. Sogolon Kedju had come to Niani from the land of Do, a woman whose back was bent, whose appearance the court mocked, and whom the king Maghan Kon Fatta married on the word of a hunter who said she would bear a son who would rule the world. That son was born, and his name was Sundiata, and he could not walk.

Not for a year. Not for two. Not for seven. The jelilu vary on the number because the jelilu are not a single text. They are a living argument carried by families whose telling reflects the politics of the moment and the truth of the lineage. Fa-Digi Sisoko's telling says seven years. Djeli Mamoudou Kouyate, speaking to Djibril Tamsir Niane, says the boy crawled until the day he chose to stand. What the versions agree on: the son of the king dragged himself through the dust of the compound while his mother wept and the king's first wife, Sassouma Berete, laughed and said, That? That is the lion who will rule the Manden?

Sassouma's mockery is not decoration. It is the engine. In the Manden understanding, a ruler who has not suffered cannot be trusted with power. The one who has been low knows the weight of the ground. The one who has been mocked knows the cost of cruelty. The one who has crawled knows what it means to stand. Sundiata's years in the dust are not a tragedy the story must overcome. They are the qualification the story requires.

The day he stood, the iron staff bent under his hands. This is in every version. The blacksmith brought a rod of iron — some say his mother begged for it, some say Sundiata demanded it — and the boy gripped the rod and pulled himself upright, and the rod bent like a bow under the force. Then he walked. Then, in some tellings, the earth shook. In Niani the baobab trees still grow where he was said to have pulled them from the ground and carried them to his mother's door, roots and all, because she had asked for baobab leaves and he would not bring her leaves without the tree.

But standing was not enough to save him. Sassouma Berete wanted the succession for her own son, Dankaran Touman, and Sundiata's mother knew they had to leave. So began the exile. Sogolon took her son and her other children and walked from court to court across the Manden and beyond it, seeking shelter, and at each court Sundiata watched and learned how power worked and how it failed. He saw kings who were generous and kings who were afraid. He saw the difference between a court that held together through obligation and one that held together through fear. The jelilu say he spent years in Mema, under the protection of its king, and there he grew into a hunter and a warrior and something more — a man who understood that kingship was not inheritance but covenant.

Meanwhile, the Manden burned. Sumanguru Kante, the king of Sosso, had swept through the old territories of Wagadu, and now he held the Manden in a grip that the jelilu describe in language that belongs to the forge and the spirit world both. Sumanguru was a smith-king, a man whose power came not from lineage but from the forge and from the spirits of the wild places. His skin, the jelilu say, could turn to iron. Arrows could not pierce him. He was not a tyrant in the way an outside listener might use that word. He was a king whose source of authority violated the covenant between ruler and ruled, whose power came from secret knowledge held alone rather than from the public obligations the Manden demanded of its leaders.

The messengers found Sundiata in exile. Come back, they said. Only you can face what Sumanguru has become.

What happened next is the Battle of Kirina, and it is the hinge of the Manden's history, and the jelilu have sung it for eight hundred years, and the variations between their versions are not errors but the living breath of a people who understand that memory is not a recording. It is a performance. And the performance is about to begin.
