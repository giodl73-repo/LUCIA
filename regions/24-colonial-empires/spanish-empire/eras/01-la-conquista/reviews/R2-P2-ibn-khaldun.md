## On the Structural Engine

The engine is correctly named: the theological-legal requirement of justification. And the terminal condition is correct: premise non-recognition. The indigenous peoples did not fail to comply; they declined to recognize the premises as binding. This is the structural failure, not a military one.

One addition: the engine has an internal time limit that the notes do not name. The theological-legal requirement of justification was sustainable as long as the justification could be performed in good faith — as long as there were sincere believers like Palacios Rubios who thought the form was both valid and valuable. Once the performance became purely formal (the notary reading in the dark to no one), the engine's legitimacy was hollowed out from within. The gap between sincerity and performance is the engine's internal clock. By 1550, the sincere believers (Las Casas) were arguing that the performance had entirely replaced the sincerity. This should be visible in the chapter.

## On the Valladolid Section

The notes correctly identify the no-verdict as the historical fact of maximum importance. I want to push further: the council was not incompetent. The council was a body of learned men facing a genuine logical impossibility. If the indigenous peoples had rational souls, the conquest required their consent, which had not been given. If they did not, the Requerimiento was meaningless. Neither conclusion was available to the Crown, because both conclusions undermined the enterprise. The council could not verdict because a verdict would have required the Crown to acknowledge that the entire legal-theological framework was either fraudulent (if rational souls, then conquest was not just) or unnecessary (if not rational souls, then the form had been pure performance from the start). The council sat with the impossibility and produced nothing. This is not failure. This is an institution confronting the limit of its own categories.

The Valladolid section should hold this as the chapter's climax. Not Las Casas's eloquence. Not Sepúlveda's learning. The silence where the verdict should be.

## On the Reconquista Connection

Section 2 should be more than background. The Reconquista is not context for the conquest — it IS the conquest, translated. The habit of requiring theological justification for war against non-Christians was seven centuries old by 1492. The Requerimiento's form was not invented for the Americas. It was applied there from a tradition that had been refined at home. The chapter should make this lineage explicit: the same legal-theological tradition that justified the war against the Moors at Granada is the tradition Palacios Rubios was drawing from when he drafted the Requerimiento. The conquest of the Americas is the Reconquista's third act.

## The Question I'd Still Push On

The New Laws section needs to name the geography of legitimacy failure explicitly. Madrid issued the laws. Peru defied them. The viceroy was killed. The laws were suspended. This is not hypocrisy — it is the structural problem of governing territories at oceanic distance with sixteenth-century communication technology. The chapter should state this plainly: the gap between the law and its enforcement was not moral cowardice. It was eight weeks by ship. Everything that happened in those eight weeks was beyond the Crown's control, and the Crown knew it, and could not solve it, and the empire ran on that gap for three hundred years.
