# PANEL-1 Review — P-4 Wade Davis
## The Long Struggle — Qajar Era 2
**Round:** R1 (Opening)
**Date:** 2026-04-17

---

What this opening does well, it does exceptionally well: it renders the *marjaiyyat* as an intellectual and organizational system worthy of the same analytical depth we would give to any other governance structure.

The Shia legal hierarchy is not presented as "religious authority" in some vague, pre-modern sense — a category that condescends even when it tries not to. The opening shows it as a functioning network with specific properties: scholars whose qualifications entitle them to issue binding rulings, whose income is independent of the state, whose authority travels through written letters and Friday sermons and the trust of ordinary people in the cities and bazaars. This is a knowledge system. It has nodes, transmission mechanisms, and enforcement through social rather than coercive means. The opening makes the reader understand all of this through what happened — not through explanation.

**What I want to see in the full chapter:**

The *ijtihad* system — the right of qualified scholars to make independent legal judgments — is mentioned in the OVERVIEW but not in the opening. The chapter should show this system operating at method level. What qualifications entitled a scholar to this right? What was the process by which a ruling moved from scholarly opinion to general obligation? How did ordinary Iranians — merchants, farmers, craftspeople — navigate between multiple *marjas* whose rulings occasionally conflicted? This is a sophisticated jurisprudential and social system, and the chapter should honor that sophistication by showing it at work, not merely describing its effects.

**The question I'd push on:**

The opening is centered on Tehran — the palace, the shah, the court. But the *marjaiyyat*'s power was rooted in the provincial cities and the bazaar networks, not in Tehran. The chapter should take us to Najaf or Karbala — to the seminary where Mirza Hasan Shirazi sat, to the library and lecture rooms where the scholars who would issue the tobacco fatwa worked and studied. What did that world look like? What was the texture of a place that exercised more authority over Iranian daily life than the palace in Tehran?

**On the concession system as knowledge gap:**

The opening treats foreign concessions as straightforwardly bad from the Iranian perspective. But the chapter should also show that this was contested — that there were Iranians who argued for rapid modernization through foreign capital as the only way to preserve independence, and that their argument had its own internal logic. The disagreement between the modernizers (often centered at the Dar al-Funun, the Tehran polytechnic founded in 1851) and the clerical establishment over how Iran should respond to the Great Powers' pressure is one of the era's most productive tensions. The chapter should hold both sides with full seriousness.

**One note on romanticization risk:**

The opening does not romanticize the Tobacco Protest — it renders it as a structural event with structural consequences. The chapter must maintain this. The clerical establishment was not a disinterested defender of Iranian sovereignty; it had its own institutional interests in limiting the state's power. The merchants had economic grievances that predated the tobacco concession. The protest was a genuine coalition, and coalitions are always more complex than their stated purpose. Showing that complexity is not a cynical move — it is the respectful one.

**Verdict:** The knowledge system is being taken seriously. The chapter must sustain this at method level. Proceed.
