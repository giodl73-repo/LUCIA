# Figures — La Conquista y El Virreinato

## The People of This Era

---

### José Gabriel Condorcanqui / Túpac Amaru II (c. 1738–1781)

He was a man who tried every legal instrument before he tried the other kind. Born around 1738 in Surimana, educated at the Colegio San Francisco de Borja for the sons of kurakas, he had spent the decade before the uprising filing petitions, presenting evidence, citing law. He knew the colonial system from the inside of its paperwork. His petitions to the Audiencia of Lima documented the repartos with receipts. He was precise. He was patient. He believed the system could be moved by argument.

What is harder to know is the interior of his hesitation. Micaela's letters show a man who, at the moment the siege of Cusco was possible, held back. Military caution, fear of civilian casualties, a lingering hope for negotiated settlement, the recognition of his own coalition's fractures — the letters open these possibilities without closing them. We know that he waited, and that the waiting cost the uprising its best military opportunity, and that he watched his wife executed before he was killed. What a man carries in those hours is beyond what the archive can reach.

His legacy: the Spanish feared his name so completely that they banned it, destroyed images of him, prohibited Inca dress and the quipu and the harawi that carried his memory. The things they banned survived. The name traveled where the prohibition could not follow.

### Micaela Bastidas Puyucahua (1745–1781)

She was the uprising's operational commander. The colonial archive gives her minimal attention; the evidence of her role comes from the military records, the correspondence, and the execution orders that named her among the principal rebels.

Micaela was of Quechua and possibly African descent — the ancestry is partially obscured by the colonial record's indifference to her origins before she became dangerous. She married Condorcanqui and managed the financial operations of their mule trade while he pursued his legal cases. When the uprising was decided, she took charge of what made it actually possible: the correspondence network that activated the kuraka sympathizers across the southern highlands, the timing calculations, the supply logistics for an army moving through high Andean terrain in summer.

Her letters to Condorcanqui are primary sources of the highest order — not because they tell us about him, but because they reveal her. They are strategic, impatient, politically precise. She understood military timing in the way that people understand things they have thought about for years before they have to explain them. "We are losing the moment," she wrote. "If you will not march, I will march." The subordination she chafed against was not the colonial system's subordination of an indigenous woman — it was the subordination of a better military judgment to a more hesitant one.

She was executed before her husband, in the same plaza. The Spanish cut out her tongue. The colonial documents justify this as punishment for her incitement. The justification makes sense from inside the colonial archive: the tongue that organized the uprising should be silenced. The Andean understanding of what was cut out and what remained is different. You cannot cut out what has already been spoken. The network she built had already moved.

### Túpac Amaru I (Thupa Amaru, d. 1572)

The last Inca of the neo-Inca state at Vilcabamba — the redoubt in the cloudforest east of Cusco where the Inca lineage had survived for forty years after Cajamarca, under the first generations of colonial rule, maintaining something like sovereignty in the jungle. He was captured in 1572 by the forces of Viceroy Toledo after Toledo decided that a living Inca heir was a legitimacy problem the colony could not tolerate.

He was brought to Cusco and beheaded in the plaza — the Huacaypata — on September 24, 1572. The accounts say that tens of thousands of Andean people gathered in the plaza and wept when the head was separated from the body. Toledo was reportedly stunned by the scale of the response; he had not understood what he was executing.

Two hundred and eight years later, in the same province, José Gabriel Condorcanqui took the name. The taking of the name was an argument: the cord was not cut in 1572. The Inca lineage lives in whoever takes up its obligations. The wak'a of the Inca name had survived in the Andean memory — in the taki, in the oral record, in the portraits the Spanish would later ban — and Condorcanqui was declaring that the name still had the power to reconstitute the world around it.

### Tomasa Titu Condemayta (d. 1781)

Kuraka of Acos, in the highlands near Cusco. She was one of the earliest military commanders of the uprising, joining in the first months and leading her community's forces in the initial engagements. The colonial accounts give her minimal attention; the evidence of her role comes from the military records of engagements she participated in and from the fact that the Spanish considered her important enough to bring to the plaza of Cusco for the May 18 executions.

She was garroted alongside Micaela Bastidas and the other prisoners. She was a kuraka — which means she had been managing the same dual-world navigation as all the other kurakas who faced the choice when Condorcanqui declared the pachakuti. She chose the Andean cosmological claim. She understood what it would cost. The record of her death is clearer than the record of her life, which is the record's failure, not hers.

### Viceroy Francisco de Toledo (1515–1582)

He arrived in Peru in 1569 and spent five years touring the viceroyalty before implementing a reorganization that defined colonial Peru for the next two centuries. The colonial mita, as it functioned in 1780, was Toledo's creation. The reducción system — the forced consolidation of dispersed Andean communities into planned Spanish-style towns, to make them legible to colonial administration and taxation — was Toledo's system. The execution of Túpac Amaru I was Toledo's decision.

Toledo understood the Andean world well enough to be systematic in how he reorganized it. He commissioned a survey of Andean history and institutions; the results fed his reform program. He was not ignorant of what he was doing. He was doing it deliberately. The mita he created was not a misunderstanding of the Andean mit'a — it was a deliberate repurposing of the Andean labor obligation, stripped of its reciprocal dimension, retained for its capacity to mobilize forced labor at scale. He took the mechanism and removed the return.

He was recalled to Spain in 1581 after Philip II expressed displeasure with the execution of Túpac Amaru I. Philip had worried that killing the Inca heir was politically unwise, not that it was wrong. Toledo died the following year.

### Guaman Poma de Ayala (c. 1550–c. 1615)

He is not a figure of the Túpac Amaru era, but he is a figure of the era this chapter covers, and his presence haunts the chapter's structural argument. Guaman Poma was a Quechua nobleman who served the colonial administration as an interpreter and assistant, observed it from close range for decades, and spent years writing a 1,200-page illustrated letter to Philip III of Spain explaining what had been done to the Andean world and how it should be corrected.

The letter is extraordinary. It is written in a Spanish inflected by Quechua syntax, with illustrations in a style that synthesizes European drawing conventions and Andean visual traditions. It covers Inca history, the conquest, the colonial system, the abuses of the corregidores, the rapes by the priests, the destruction of the communities, and proposes a reformed colonial order based on Andean governance principles. It names names. It has receipts.

The King of Spain never read it. The manuscript arrived in Europe somehow and ended up in the Royal Library in Copenhagen. A Danish archivist found it in 1908. Philip III had never seen it.

Guaman Poma and Condorcanqui are the same structural argument in different centuries: the man who presents the evidence to the authority that should respond to it and receives only silence. The premise-non-recognition terminal condition runs through both. What you cannot make the colonial system recognize as binding, no matter how carefully you document it, no matter how correctly you address it, no matter how legibly you present it — this is the engine the chapter tracks. Guaman Poma knew it by 1615. The knowledge arrived in Cusco plaza by 1781.

### Note on the Record

The Andean women of this uprising — Micaela Bastidas, Tomasa Titu Condemayta, and the many others whose names the archive does not always preserve — commanded, organized, and died at the uprising's center. The colonial archive consistently foregrounded the men. Scholars working in the feminist historiography of the Andes (among them Sinclair Thomson and Ward Stavig) have worked to restore the women's roles from the military records, the correspondence, and the execution orders. Their restoration should be read as part of the chapter's ongoing project: the archive was partial; the knowledge was not.

The quipucamayocs whose quipus documented the colonial violations are not named here because their names were not recorded. The harawi singers who carried the memory of the uprising are not named here because the harawi was deliberately anonymous — the song had no author so it could have every voice. This anonymity was not absence. It was the survival strategy. What has no single author cannot be arrested.
