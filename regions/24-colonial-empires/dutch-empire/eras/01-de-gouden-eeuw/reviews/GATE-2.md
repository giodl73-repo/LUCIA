**Structural engine assessment:** The corporate distance from consequence engine operates in every section. The terminal condition (the ledger cannot buy political solidarity) is endogenous to the engine's logic: the VOC produced commercial asabiyyah, not martial asabiyyah, and when the Republic needed the latter, the former was insufficient. This is a correctly named terminal condition — closest to the "legitimacy gap" type (the commercial-legal authority of the VOC cannot generate the political-martial authority the Republic required in 1672), with elements of the "categorical failure" type (the mechanism encounters an event outside its categories).

**Innovations flagged:**

1. **Commercial Asabiyyah as Terminal Condition:** The chapter applies Ibn Khaldun's asabiyyah framework to a joint-stock corporation and discovers that commercial solidarity is structurally durable but categorically limited: it holds through profit and fails at political crisis. This is a new instance of terminal condition that does not map precisely to any of the ten existing types. Proposed as a candidate for "Accountability-gap" terminal condition: the mechanism's form of solidarity (financial, contractual) cannot fulfill the solidarity demand of the crisis (political, martial). Flag for CALLIOPE to assess whether this constitutes a new type or a variant of "legitimacy gap."

2. **Source Asymmetry via Colonial Archive as Structural Engine:** The chapter uses the VOC's own administrative records as both primary evidence and structural argument — the fact that the Bandanese appear primarily in VOC ledgers as commodity data IS the chapter's point. This extends the v1.5 "outsider-as-structural-evidence" technique to the case where the archive itself is the outsider's account and the asymmetry is the engine. Flag for INNOVATIONS.md.
