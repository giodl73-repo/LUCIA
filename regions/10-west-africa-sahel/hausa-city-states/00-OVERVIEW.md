# Hausa Bakwai — "The Seven True Hausa States"

The Hausa founding myth — Bayajidda, the hero from Baghdad who slew the sacred snake of Daura, married the queen, and fathered the founders of the seven Hausa states — is a story about urban origins. Each of the Bakwai (the seven true states: Daura, Kano, Zazzau, Gobir, Katsina, Rano, Biram) was a city-state with its own sarki (king), its own walls, its own market, and its own specialism. Kano specialized in leather and cloth — the famed Kano leather and the blue-dyed cotton that North African merchants knew as "Hausa cloth." Katsina was the city of scholars, where the Islamic intellectual tradition met the Saharan trade routes. Zazzau (Zaria) controlled the supply of enslaved people from the south. The Hausa world was not a centralized empire; it was a cluster of competitive, sophisticated urban states that knew each other very well and organized their competitions as much through commerce and scholarship as through war.

The Hausa city-states were among the most densely urbanized territories in pre-colonial sub-Saharan Africa. Kano's walls at their maximum extent enclosed an area larger than medieval London, and they contained not simply the palace and the market but a vast network of craft workshops: dyers, weavers, leatherworkers, blacksmiths, potters. The economy was not simply long-distance trade; it was manufacturing. The Hausa exported finished goods, not just raw materials. The dye pits of Kano — still in operation today — are among the oldest continuously functioning industrial sites in Africa.

Key places:
- **Kano** — the greatest manufacturing city; the dye pits; the central market; the chronicles
- **Katsina** — the city of scholars; the gateway for Saharan trade; Fulani intellectual center
- **Zazzau / Zaria** — the slave-raiding frontier state; Queen Amina's expansion in the sixteenth century
- **Daura** — the ritual founding city; seat of the Bayajidda origin myth

## What Defined Them

**The city-state as the unit of civilization.** The Hausa world never unified under a single ruler — not in the pre-jihad period. The Bakwai competed, allied, fought, and traded with each other in a system that looks more like the Greek poleis or the Italian city-states than the great Sahel empires. This was not a failure of political ambition; it was a different political philosophy, one in which the sarki's authority was local and the city's identity was the primary civic loyalty. The competition between cities drove economic and military innovation: Kano built better cloth to outcompete Katsina; Zaria built better walls to hold its southern trade routes. The city-state system was productive precisely because it was competitive.

**Manufacturing and the export of finished goods.** The Hausa economy was organized around craft production at a scale unusual in pre-colonial Africa. The dye pits of Kano produced blue-dyed cotton cloth in industrial quantities; caravans carrying hundreds of bales of Hausa cloth crossed the Sahara to North African markets. Hausa leather — tanned and worked in Kano — was exported as far as Europe, where it was known as "Moroccan leather" because it arrived via Morocco. The Hausa were not intermediaries in other people's trade; they were manufacturers whose finished products commanded premium prices in markets a thousand miles away. The wealth this generated funded the great walled cities with their mosques, their palace complexes, and their scholarly establishments.

**Queen Amina and the military state.** The tradition of Queen Amina of Zaria (c. 1588–c. 1610, though the dates are disputed) is one of the most vivid in Hausa oral history. Amina expanded Zazzau's territory to its greatest extent through a series of military campaigns, requiring the building of earthwork walls around every new camp — the "Amina's walls" that survive as earthworks across the Hausa countryside. She never married, taking a new consort each night and dismissing him at dawn — or so the tradition says. Whether the historical Amina matches the legendary figure exactly is less important than what the tradition tells us: Hausa political culture had a framework for female military and political authority at the highest level.

**The Islamic scholarly tradition.** The Hausa city-states developed a sophisticated Islamic scholarly culture from the thirteenth century onward. The scholars of Katsina corresponded with scholars in Cairo, Timbuktu, and the Hijaz. The Maliki legal tradition governed commercial and personal law. The question of how to govern a city of diverse religious practices — Muslims, practitioners of the Bori spirit-possession cult, members of various traditional religious establishments — was a live political and theological question in every Hausa capital. The Hausa kings navigated this pluralism with varying degrees of success, and the tensions it created were the raw material from which Usman dan Fodio's critique was built.

**The Bori cult and religious pluralism.** Alongside the mosque and the Islamic court, the Bori cult — the spirit-possession religion of the Hausa world — persisted through the entire period of Islamic political authority. Bori specialists, predominantly women, mediated between the human world and the spirits (iskoki) through possession ceremonies. The Bori was not simply a survival of "older" religion; it was a contemporary institution that served psychological, social, and healing functions that the mosque did not. The relationship between Islam and Bori in the Hausa world is one of negotiation and coexistence rather than replacement — until the Fulani jihad attempted a more thorough Islamic reform.

## The Chapters

**1 — Bayajidda's Children:** Seven cities have been founded. Seven sarkis have been crowned. The snake has been killed; the queen has been married; the origin story has been told. The competition can begin.

**2 — The Kano Chronicles:** A scribe in Kano is writing the *Kano Chronicle* — a history of the city's kings. He is not an Arab geographer looking in from outside. He is a Hausa Muslim writing about his own city, in his own tradition. He has opinions about which kings were good.

**3 — Amina's Campaign:** Queen Amina of Zaria has taken another town. The walls go up overnight — her soldiers have done this before. Tomorrow she moves on. She is not done.

**4 — The Scholar's Critique:** Usman dan Fodio is writing a poem about the corruption of the Hausa rulers. He is a Fulani scholar in Gobir. The sarki is listening, uncomfortably. The poem is circulating. The jihad is still ten years away, but everyone can feel it coming.

**5 — After the Jihad:** The Hausa sarki has fled. A Fulani emir sits in the palace. He is using the same administrative machinery, the same market infrastructure, the same dye pits. Some things change; some things are too embedded in the city's fabric to change at all.
