# Figures — The War of Brothers (Era 5)

## Atahualpa, r. c. 1532–1533 CE

Last effective Sapa Inca. Son of Huayna Capac. Won the civil war against his brother Huascar. Captured by Francisco Pizarro at Cajamarca on November 16, 1532. Offered and delivered a ransom of gold and silver. Executed by garrote on July 26, 1533.

**What we know:** Atahualpa is the best-documented Inca ruler because the Spanish witnesses at Cajamarca wrote extensively about the encounter. Betanzos, whose wife Angelina Yupanqui had been Atahualpa's consort, provides the closest thing to an Inca-perspective account. Pedro Pizarro (a cousin of Francisco), Miguel de Estete, and other Spanish witnesses provide detailed descriptions of the events at Cajamarca. Guaman Poma de Ayala's illustrated chronicle, written c. 1615, provides an Andean perspective on the conquest.

**What we cannot know:** Atahualpa's understanding of who the Spanish were and what they represented. The chapter renders him through decisions (inviting the strangers to the plaza, the ransom offer, the choice of garrote over burning) because these are attested, while his inner deliberation is not. His examination of the book — holding it to his ear — is described in the Spanish sources and is interpreted in the chapter as consistent with a worldview in which information-bearing objects make sounds, but the interpretation is necessarily speculative.

**Honest limit:** The account of Cajamarca is told overwhelmingly from Spanish sources, because the Spanish wrote it down and the quipucamayoc who might have recorded it differently did not survive to have their accounts preserved. The chapter maintains the Inca perspective by rendering the events through what Atahualpa and his court would have seen, heard, and understood, while acknowledging that the factual details come through the colonial filter.

**Sources:** Betanzos (1557); Pedro Pizarro, *Relacion del Descubrimiento y Conquista del Peru* (1571); Estete, *Relacion* (1534); Guaman Poma (c. 1615); Hemming, *The Conquest of the Incas* (1970); Wachtel, *The Vision of the Vanquished* (1977).

## Huascar

Son of Huayna Capac. Claimed the succession from Qusqu. Defeated and captured by Atahualpa's generals. Killed during the confusion of the conquest, possibly on Atahualpa's orders.

**What we know:** Huascar's claim to legitimacy through the traditional succession framework is consistent across sources. His capture and death are attested but the circumstances vary between accounts.

**Sources:** Cieza de Leon (1553); Sarmiento (1572); Hemming (1970).

## Chalcuchima and Quisquis

Atahualpa's principal generals. Led the military campaign that defeated Huascar and captured Qusqu. Among the most capable military commanders Tawantinsuyu produced.

**What we know:** Both are named across multiple colonial sources as the architects of Atahualpa's military victory. Their campaigns through the highlands are described in sufficient detail to reconstruct the general course of the civil war.

**Sources:** Cieza de Leon (1553); Betanzos (1557); Hemming (1970).

## Francisco Pizarro (c. 1478–1541)

Leader of the Spanish expedition that captured Atahualpa. From Trujillo, Extremadura. Had been in the Americas since 1502. The chapter renders him from the Inca perspective — as one of the strangers from the coast — rather than centering his biography.

**What we know:** Pizarro's biography is extensively documented in Spanish sources. The chapter deliberately limits his presence to what the Inca would have seen and understood, consistent with the inside-voice principle.

**Sources:** Hemming (1970); Lockhart, *The Men of Cajamarca* (1972).

## Fray Vicente de Valverde

Dominican priest who presented the Bible (or breviary) to Atahualpa in the plaza at Cajamarca. The encounter between the priest, the book, and the Sapa Inca is one of the most described moments in the chronicle of the conquest.

**What we know:** The scene is described by multiple Spanish witnesses. The details vary (was it a Bible or a breviary? did Atahualpa throw it or set it down?) but the essential structure is consistent: a Christian text was offered, Atahualpa examined it, and its rejection served as the stated justification for the attack.

**Sources:** Estete (1534); Pedro Pizarro (1571); Hemming (1970).

## A Note on the Conquest Narrative

This chapter tells the conquest from Atahualpa's side, not from Pizarro's. This is a deliberate choice, consistent with LUCIA's inside-voice principle. The Spanish conquest of the Inca Empire has been told thousands of times from the Spanish perspective. It has been told far less often from the Inca perspective, in part because the Inca record-keeping system (the quipu) was destroyed along with the people who could read it, and in part because the written record is overwhelmingly Spanish.

The chapter uses Spanish sources for factual detail while maintaining the Inca perspective for framing and interpretation. The goal is not to reverse the moral polarity (making the Spanish "villains" and the Inca "victims") but to tell the story from inside the world that was lost, so that the reader understands what the loss meant to the people who experienced it, rather than what the gain meant to the people who caused it.

No verdicts are offered on the conquest. The events are presented. The reader is the jury.
