- Ahmad Shah's own Persian poetry — approximately 30 ghazals survive in the *Divan-i Ahmad Shah*. Central to Achebe's request for lived bilingual culture. Will use at least one fragment to ground the Persian court register.
- The *Tarikh-i Ahmad Shahi* by Mahmud al-Husaini — a contemporary chronicle written in the court's own Persian; eyewitness or near-contemporary accounts of the Loya Jirga and the early campaigns.

### Academic / Tier 2
- Ganda Singh, *Ahmad Shah Durrani: Father of Modern Afghanistan* (1959) — foundational biography, Tier 2; strong on the founding and the Panipat campaigns.
- Olaf Caroe, *The Pathans: 550 BC – AD 1957* (1958) — on Pashtunwali, the qawm, and the Pashtun political form. British colonial perspective to be used critically; strong on mechanism.

- Mountstuart Elphinstone, *An Account of the Kingdom of Caubul* (1815) — written within living memory of Ahmad Shah's reign; used as outsider-as-structural-evidence (v1.5 rubric technique): Elphinstone's surprise reveals his assumptions about what a "non-European" court could accomplish.

## 2. Key Figures

### Ahmad Shah Durrani (c. 1722–1773)
Born into the Sadozai clan of the Popalzai Pashtuns, probably in Multan or Herat. Served as a young military commander under Nader Shah's Abdali cavalry — probably 10,000 Abdali horsemen that formed the crack strike force of Nader Shah's campaigns into India (1739 Mughal conquest of Delhi). At Nader Shah's assassination (June 1747), Ahmad Khan was in Khorasan; he moved quickly to Kandahar before any rival could organize. At the Loya Jirga he was the youngest serious candidate. His claim: Sadozai royal bloodline + military reputation + control of the Abdali cavalry that would be the empire's first army.

Ahmad Shah was also a poet — composing in both Pashto and Persian. His Persian ghazals show genuine mastery of the classical forms, not courtly performance. This is load-bearing for Achebe's concern: his bilingual capacity was not an administrative convenience but a form of personal authority.

### Hajji Jamal Khan Mohammadzai (historical figure)
Chief of the Mohammadzai branch — the rivals of the Sadozai. His eventual acquiescence to Ahmad Shah's selection is crucial: the Loya Jirga's consensus required the rival branch's buy-in. The Mohammadzai-Sadozai tension will resurface in Era 2 (the succession wars). Plant it here.

### Sabir Shah (historical figure)
The religious figure who placed his walking stick before Ahmad Shah. His role: spiritual sanction that made the consensus decision no single khan's responsibility. Present from within the assembly's logic, not as superstition.

### Ahmad Shah's Persian secretary (unnamed)
The chancery culture had human beings operating it. An unnamed secretary — working from historical reconstruction of the Durrani court's administrative practices — gives Davis's knowledge-system concern a body. He is the person who translates Ahmad Shah's Pashto intentions into Persian administrative formulas.

## 3. Structural Engine

**Engine:** The Loya Jirga's consensus-distribution mechanism.

**Mechanism:** Ahmad Shah's authority is legitimate only while two conditions hold: (1) the campaigns continue to produce distributable rewards — plunder, land grants, administrative positions — for the clan chiefs who acknowledged him at Kandahar; (2) the distribution is seen as fair by Pashtunwali standards, which have their own accounting for what each clan is owed based on contribution and precedent. The empire is not a top-down structure; it is a franchise in which the franchisees' continued buy-in is conditional on the franchisor's performance.

**Terminal Condition (v1.6):** This is a **legitimacy gap** type (modified). The Durrani system can extract military and political performance from the Pashtun confederacy, but it cannot build the administrative infrastructure needed to govern the non-Pashtun populations of Punjab, Sindh, and Khorasan without undermining the clan-franchise model. Ahmad Shah's choice at Delhi in 1761 — not to hold the city — is the terminal condition made visible: the engine cannot convert military dominance into permanent administration. The empire remains a Pashtun confederacy that wins campaigns rather than an administered state that holds territory. When Ahmad Shah dies, the mechanism requires another demonstrated leader; his successors cannot provide one; the franchise breaks.

**Seed in Era 1:** The Panipat victory and withdrawal — show it as a rational choice within the engine's logic (the army needs to go home; the plunder is shared; the mechanism has worked) that simultaneously plants the question of what the empire cannot do.

## 4. Narrative Arc / Section Plan

### Section 1 — The Jirga (opening, integrated)
The 1747 Loya Jirga at Kandahar. Ahmad Khan receives the green-wheat crown. Establish the physical setting of Kandahar, the assembly's structure, the Pashtunwali logic, the Sadozai-Mohammadzai tension (Hajji Jamal Khan's acceptance). Ahmad Shah's dual identity as soldier and poet seeded here.

**Panel pressure point addressed (Kapuściński):** Add one more sensory layer inside the assembly — the heat, the sounds, the physical arrangement of the khans.

### Section 2 — The First Campaigns: Khorasan and the West
Ahmad Shah's first military operations: securing Kandahar and Khorasan, establishing the western border against Iranian rivals. 1748: victory at Manupur against Mughal forces begins the empire's Indian reach. Show the Abdali cavalry in motion — road, crossing, terrain. The army as a Pashtun social institution: clan structure visible in the military organization.

**Panel pressure point addressed (Tuchman + Kapuściński):** One figure other than Ahmad Shah visible in the campaigns — show a specific khan's calculation of loyalty and reward. Show the army on the road.

### Section 3 — The Loya Jirga in Operation: The Campaign Distribution Mechanism
After the first Indian campaigns: how the rewards are distributed. Show the mechanism operating — the share calculations, the land grants (jagirs) given to clan chiefs, the administrative appointments. Show Pashtunwali's accounting system at work. An unnamed chief who weighs his return against his alternatives and finds the franchise still worth joining.

**Panel pressure point addressed (Ibn Khaldun + Davis):** Pashtunwali as operating system, visible in a specific distributive act.

### Section 4 — The Persian Court: Ahmad Shah at His Chancery
Ahmad Shah's court at Kandahar: the Persian-language administration, the chancery, the diplomatic correspondence with the Ottoman, Mughal, and Iranian powers. A fragment of his own Persian poetry. The bilingual world as lived reality, not descriptive label. His Persian secretary rendering the khan's intentions into the language of civilization.

**Panel pressure point addressed (Achebe):** Ahmad Shah's bilingual authority rendered from within his own consciousness.

### Section 5 — Panipat: The Empire at Its Furthest Reach
The Third Battle of Panipat (14 January 1761/5 Jumada al-Awwal 1174 AH): the Maratha Confederacy defeated. Ground the section in the march from Peshawar: the Punjab plains in winter, frost on the battlefield at dawn, the specific cold of that morning. The Maratha commander Viswasrao's death as the physical anchor of the battle — when the center breaks, what the breaking looks like. The choice not to hold Delhi: not failure of nerve but the engine's rational self-knowledge. Show the return to Kandahar, the distribution of plunder, the mechanism completing its cycle. Note consequences: the Maratha Confederacy wounded but not finished; the Mughal rump at Delhi preserved. Ahmad Shah read his engine correctly; the chapter acknowledges the structural costs of the reading without judging the decision.

### Section 6 — The Landing: The Pearl and the Grain
Return to the green wheat. The empire Ahmad Shah built did not collapse on his death by accident — it revealed the terminal condition of its own engine. But in 1747–1772, the engine was running. Ahmad Shah died in 1772, probably of cancer, in Kandahar. His tomb is there still, beside the Shrine of the Cloak of the Prophet. He was returned to the city that made him a king, in the year 1186 AH. The green grain had ripened. The chapter closes on him in Kandahar — the circle that closed around him in 1747 and opened into a kingdom closing again at the end, tighter, quieter. What held and what the holding cost.

## 6. Voice

**Register:** Court-military Persian — the voice of the Durrani chancery filtered through the Pashtun camp. Measured, formal when addressing power, kinetic when describing movement. The prose should be able to shift between the deliberate weight of the jirga circle and the forward motion of cavalry on the road to the Punjab — two rhythms, one governing register.

**Specific techniques:**
- The jirga sections: deliberate, circling, the rhythm of debate — sentences that return and settle.
- The campaign sections: forward motion, terrain-specific, the prose moves with the army.
- The court sections: layered, observational — the Persianate register, slow and ornate at the margins, precise at the center.
- Em-dash limit: 0 target, maximum 8. Zero from the opening maintained.

## 7. Open Questions

1. The exact Pashtunwali term for the plunder-distribution customary share — *ghanimat* is the Arabic-Islamic term; what is the Pashto-specific term? The chapter will use both registers.
2. Ahmad Shah's poetry: the specific ghazal fragment most relevant to the founding period — likely one addressing the relationship of power and dust (*khak*), a common trope in Persian political poetry.
3. The physical description of Kandahar in 1747 — population estimates, bazaar layout, the specific terrain between the city and the outlying assembly ground. Elphinstone (1815) is useful; archaeology fills some gaps.
4. The Battle of Panipat (1761): the Maratha commander Viswasrao's death — that specific moment when the Maratha center broke — to ground the battle as human event rather than strategic summary.
