## Testimonial Methodology Assessment

The Rosa composite figure is handled with appropriate transparency. The chapter names her as composite in figures.md, identifies the CNMH archive as the source base, and cites *¡Basta Ya!* specifically. This is consistent with the CNMH's own methodological practice: our published accounts use composite and anonymized figures drawn from the testimonial record, with the archival basis acknowledged.

The specific details attributed to Rosa — the pre-dawn arrival, the absence of stated charges, the cast-iron pot, the saint, the backward look — are consistent with the testimonial record as documented in the CNMH's regional studies of Tolima. These are not invented; they represent recurring phenomenological elements in displacement testimony across the coffee zones. The chapter's choice to render them through a single named composite figure rather than as statistical enumeration is legitimate and, in my view, more faithful to the testimonial tradition itself, which values the specific account over the aggregated fact.

## Gender Dimension Assessment

The GATE-2 scorecard flags the question of sexual violence during La Violencia. The chapter does not address this directly. María Victoria Uribe's *Matar, rematar y contramatar* (1990) documents sexual violence as a systematic element of partisan massacres in specific zones, and the CNMH's more recent work has developed this further.

The chapter's current approach — Rosa's displacement is rendered through the objects she carried, the man's silence, the one backward look — does not exclude the full dimension of what women experienced but does not explicitly include it either. This is an editorial judgment the chapter has made. It is defensible: the chapter's register (matter-of-fact, accumulative) is suited to addressing sexual violence without sensationalizing it, but the current text does not attempt this. A future revision could add one sentence in the Rosa section — specific, factual, in the chapter's register — to acknowledge this dimension of the testimonial record. This is a recommendation, not a required correction.

## Displacement Estimate Accuracy

The chapter states "the CNMH estimates one to two million people were displaced from their land during La Violencia." The *¡Basta Ya!* report's estimate is approximately two million internally displaced persons, with a range reflecting methodological uncertainty. The chapter's "one to two million" is within the range of the scholarly literature. Some scholars cite higher estimates (up to three million) and some lower. The chapter's phrasing is appropriately cautious.

## Cultural Representation of Campesino Experience

The chapter correctly characterizes the displacement experience from inside the campesino's own relationship to the land: not as victims of an abstract political conflict but as people with a specific material relationship to a specific piece of earth, whose loss was experienced in concrete terms (the objects, the smell of the coffee flowers, the rooster in the yard). This is consistent with how the CNMH testimonial literature characterizes the experience.

The emphasis on the land question as primary — rather than on the partisan ideology — is historically correct and culturally appropriate. For the campesinos of Tolima and the Quindío, La Violencia was, at the most intimate level, a land conflict expressed through partisan language.

## Recommendation

Chapter approved for FINAL. Optional recommendation for future revision: one sentence in the Rosa section acknowledging the sexual-violence dimension of displacement testimony, in the chapter's own register. Not a required correction for this version.
