## Source Notes (Era 3)

**The Breton dictionary's gender asymmetry:** Taylor (1977) establishes that Island Carib as Breton recorded it differentiated men's and women's speech vocabulary. Breton's primary interlocutors were men; the dictionary is more complete for men's speech domains (navigation, warfare, maritime vocabulary) than for women's speech domains (agricultural practice, healing plants, domestic knowledge). The chapter's use of the dictionary for women's knowledge domains (the garden, plant preparations) is reasonable but is extrapolation from the men's-speech record and adjacent vocabulary.

**The European word for Europeans:** The chapter implies Breton recorded a Kalinago word meaning "those who have come to stay without being asked" for Europeans. This is the chapter's rendering of Breton's vocabulary for distinguishing Europeans from other peoples in the Kalinago's world. The specific gloss is a scholarly reconstruction consistent with the dictionary's evidence, not a direct Breton translation. The distinction between Breton's documented vocabulary and the chapter's interpretive rendering should be held in mind.

**Columbus account reliability:** The number "eleven" captives in the 1493 Guadeloupe encounter comes from secondhand accounts (Peter Martyr's reconstruction of the lost second-voyage journal). The exact number is uncertain. The encounter itself is documented.

**Breton's presence on Dominica:** Breton arrived in the Lesser Antilles in 1635 with the first French colonization fleet at Martinique. His most sustained contact with Dominica Kalinago communities occurred in the 1640s. The dates given in the chapter reflect this chronology.
