# Yuan — The Empire of All Under Heaven

We are the Yuan — the "original" dynasty, as Kublai Khan named it, claiming the legitimacy of Chinese dynastic succession while governing an empire that connected China to the Persian Il-Khanate, the Golden Horde, and the Chagatai Khanate from a capital in modern Beijing. We are the dynasty that proves the Mongol Empire was not only destruction: our China was the eastern node of the most commercially connected world in history, the terminus of the Pax Mongolica trade routes that brought Marco Polo to Kublai Khan's court and carried plague, silk, and ideas across the continent in both directions.

We governed through a hierarchy that placed the Mongols first, then the Semu (Central Asian and Muslim peoples), then the northern Chinese (Hanren), then the southern Chinese (Nanren) — the former Song subjects who had resisted the longest. This was not simply ethnic discrimination; it was a governance structure that maintained Mongol dominance while recruiting the skills of each group. The southern Chinese ran the local administration they had always run; the Mongols commanded the armies; the Semu managed the commercial taxation that funded it all. It was efficient and it was resented. The rebellions that drove us out after a century were fueled by that resentment.

## What Defined Them

**The global connectivity.** The Yuan dynasty governed the eastern node of the world's most connected commercial network. The Mongol relay system (yam) made it possible to travel from China to Persia in weeks; the Pax Mongolica's relative security made long-distance trade profitable. Marco Polo, Rabban Sauma, Ibn Battuta — travelers from different worlds who visited the Yuan court and left records that are the most vivid cross-cultural documents of the medieval world. The bubonic plague also traveled this network.

**Kublai Khan's Chinese statecraft.** Kublai Khan was the Mongol ruler who most fully committed to governing China in Chinese institutional forms. He maintained the examination system (though he reduced its relative weight), employed Chinese officials, used Chinese administrative vocabulary, and patronized Chinese Buddhist and Daoist religious institutions. He was simultaneously the Great Khan of the Mongol Empire (a title his cousins contested) and the Son of Heaven of the Yuan dynasty — holding both identities was the central project of his rule.

**Phagpa and Tibetan Buddhism.** Kublai Khan's relationship with his Tibetan Buddhist teacher Drogön Chögyal Phagpa established the patron-priest model for the Mongol relationship with Tibetan Buddhism that shaped the subsequent history of both peoples. Phagpa created a new script (the Phagpa script) for writing Mongolian and was appointed Imperial Preceptor of the Yuan. The Mongol adoption of Tibetan Buddhism — encouraged by Kublai — created the connection between the Mongol and Tibetan Buddhist worlds that the Dalai Lama institution would later formalize.

**The failed conquests.** The Yuan's failed invasions of Japan (1274 and 1281) and Vietnam (three times) and Java (1293) are as revealing as its successes. The Japanese invasions were destroyed by typhoons — the kamikaze, "divine winds," that the Japanese credited to the gods. Vietnam's guerrilla tactics defeated three Mongol armies. Java's conditions and resistance made occupation impossible. These failures show the limits of the steppe military system when applied to maritime and tropical environments.

**Drama and the despised art.** Because the Yuan reduced the weight of the examination system and restricted Chinese scholars from the highest offices, many educated men turned to writing vernacular drama — plays in spoken Chinese rather than classical literary Chinese. The Yuan drama (zaju) that emerged from this displacement produced some of the greatest works in Chinese dramatic literature, including "The Injustice Done to Dou E" and "The Romance of the Western Chamber." The cultural exclusion of Chinese scholars from political power produced a literary flowering in a form that the elite had previously dismissed.

## The Chapters

**1 — The Final Song:** The Mongol general crosses the Yangtze. The Song commander knows that the river, which has held the Mongols for two decades, has been crossed. He will fight as long as he can. At Yamen, the last Song emperor will die at sea. The Yuan has begun.

**2 — Marco Polo:** He walks into Kublai Khan's court at Khanbaliq. He is the son of a Venetian merchant. He will stay seventeen years. He will return to Venice with stories no one will entirely believe. He will dictate them from a Genoese prison. His account becomes the most influential description of China in the medieval West.

**3 — Kublai's Governance:** The Chinese official sits across from the Mongol administrator. They are both managing the same province. The Mongol has the military authority; the Chinese has the administrative knowledge. The system depends on their cooperation. It works, mostly.

**4 — Decline:** The Yellow River is flooding again. The conscripted laborers digging the new canal are dying at a rate that makes the foreman stop counting. The Red Turban rebels have declared their leader a reincarnation of Maitreya. The White Lotus Society is organizing in every flooded village. The Yuan has a year.
