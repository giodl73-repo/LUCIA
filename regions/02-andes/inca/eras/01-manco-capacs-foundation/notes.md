# Production Notes — Manco Cápac's Foundation (Era 1)

## Structural Engine

**The ayllu as foundation.** The structural engine for this chapter is the ayllu — the kinship group that is simultaneously a social unit, a land-holding unit, a ritual unit, and the building block of everything Tawantinsuyu will become. The chapter's arc follows the ayllu from its pre-Inca form (independent kinship groups in the Cusco valley) through its incorporation into the Inca state as the mechanism of reciprocal obligation. The mit'a emerges from ayllu-level communal labor practices. The vertical archipelago emerges from ayllu-level ecological strategies. The quipu records ayllu-level information. Everything begins here.

**Book-level arc seed:** This chapter plants the three structural threads that will run through all five eras:
1. **Mit'a (reciprocal obligation)** — introduced as ayllu-level communal labor, not yet state-level extraction
2. **Vertical archipelago** — introduced as ecological practice, not yet political strategy
3. **Quipu** — introduced as the memory system that makes the state legible to itself

By Era 5, all three will be under stress: the mit'a exhausted by civil war, the vertical archipelago disrupted by epidemic, the quipu silenced by conquest.

## Chapter Arc

1. **The Emergence** (opening material, revised) — Origin myth at Titicaca; the golden staff; descent to Qusqu
2. **The Valley** — The Cusco valley as a competitive landscape; multiple ayllus; early Sapa Incas navigating alliances and conflicts; the ayllu's internal logic (collective land, ancestor worship, reciprocal labor)
3. **The Vertical World** — How the ayllu learned to work multiple ecological zones; the llama caravan as the technology of vertical control; the exchange relationships between coast, valley, and puna; the seed of the vertical archipelago
4. **The Quipu's Memory** — The quipucamayoc and their craft; what the knots hold; the ceque system's early form; the relationship between record and recorder
5. **The Weaving** — Textile as the primary medium of Andean communication; women's labor as structural; cumbi cloth as the highest expression; the ayllu's textile obligations
6. **The Reciprocal Seed** — communal labor within the ayllu; the chicha ceremony; the obligation cycle that will become the mit'a; the closing: what the valley held before Pachacuti shook the earth

## Key Figures

- **Manco Cápac** — Mythic founder; emerges from Titicaca; the golden staff; honest treatment as myth-that-authorizes
- **Mama Ocllo** — Co-founder; bearer of the weaving arts in some traditions; structural presence, not ornamental
- **The quipucamayoc** — Composite figure (acknowledged in figures.md); a reader of knots in the early period; hands on strings as embodied practice
- **An ayllu kuraka** — Composite figure; the headman who mediates between the ayllu and the wider world; the voice of reciprocal obligation at the local level
- **A weaver** — Composite figure (female); the textile tradition as knowledge system; the act of encoding meaning in thread

## Figures Strategy (Honest Limits)

The pre-1400 Inca period has almost no individually documented figures beyond the mythic genealogy. The chapter will use:
- Manco Cápac as mythic figure (treated honestly as myth, per opening)
- Composite figures for the quipucamayoc, kuraka, and weaver (per v1.1 Honest Limits innovation)
- The genealogical names (Sinchi Roca, Lloque Yupanqui, etc.) as a list the quipu held, not as characters the chapter pretends to know

All composites disclosed in figures.md with sources.

## Sensory Palette

- **Sight:** Indigo sky, brown altiplano, green valley floor, the colors of dyed wool (cochineal red, indigo blue, the natural browns and whites of llama/alpaca fiber)
- **Sound:** Wind in ichu grass, llama caravan (soft footfalls, herder's calls), the click of the backstrap loom's heddle, chicha pouring from a clay vessel
- **Smell:** Mineral cold of lake water, shea equivalent = quinoa roasting on stone, llama dung fires (the smell of altitude cooking), wet earth after rain in the valley
- **Touch:** The cold at altitude, the texture of cumbi cloth (so fine the Spanish compared it to silk), the rough fiber of ordinary ahuasca cloth, the weight of a quipu in the hands
- **Taste:** Chicha (corn beer), chuño (freeze-dried potato, the taste of cold and starch), quinoa

## Voice Notes

**Vertical.** The prose should move up and down through altitude zones the way the Inca moved through their world. Sentences that ascend from valley to puna. Paragraphs that descend from sky to earth. The rhythm is not steppe-fast or court-layered. It is mountain rhythm: deliberate, weight-bearing, shaped by thin air and long distances.

**Layered like ecology.** Multiple things operating at multiple altitudes simultaneously. When describing the ayllu, show the coast, the valley, and the puna in the same breath.

**The quipu's silence.** The voice should carry an awareness that the record is incomplete. Not apologetically, but as texture. The gaps in what we know are part of the world's character, not its failure.
