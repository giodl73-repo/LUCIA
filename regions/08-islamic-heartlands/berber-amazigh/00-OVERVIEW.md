# Imazighen — The Free People

We called ourselves *Imazighen* — the free people — long before the Arabs named us Berbers, long before the Romans named us Barbari, long before any outside name had been given to the people who had lived in the North African mountains and plateaus and desert edges since before any of those namers existed. The Amazigh world stretches from the Siwa oasis in Egypt's western desert to the Canary Islands; from the Mediterranean coast of Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, and Libya to the Saharan edge where the Tuareg and the other southern Amazigh peoples live their pastoral lives in the spaces between the great empires' reach. We are the oldest population of North Africa, and every conqueror — Phoenician, Roman, Vandal, Byzantine, Arab, Ottoman — has built on our foundation while imagining that they were building on nothing.

We were never passive recipients of whatever faith or language the conquerors brought. The Amazigh people who accepted Islam in the 7th–8th centuries did so on their own terms, and those terms shaped every subsequent Islamic movement that rose from Amazigh soil. The Kharijite revolt that established the first independent Islamic Amazigh states was a revolt against Arab privilege within Islam — the assertion that all Muslims were equal before God and that the Arab claim to govern the *umma* was illegitimate. The Almoravid and Almohad reform movements that swept across the Maghreb and into Iberia in the 11th–12th centuries were Amazigh movements, organized from the Saharan and Atlas margins, asserting that the Islam of the cities had become corrupt and that the original purity of faith required restoration. We did not simply follow the faith we adopted. We argued with it, reformed it, and in reforming it became its most effective defenders.

## What Defined Them

**Tamazight — the language that would not die.** The Amazigh language — Tamazight in its various dialects — survived the Phoenician, Roman, Arab, Ottoman, and French presences because it was the language of the family, the tribe, the pastoral community, the mountain village. It was not the language of courts or scholars — that role was successively taken by Punic, Latin, Arabic, and French — but it was the language in which Amazigh culture transmitted itself across the generations. The Tifinagh script — the ancient Amazigh writing system that the Tuareg have maintained continuously for centuries, and that was revived as a literary script in the 20th century — is one of the oldest writing systems still in active use. The Amazigh survival is not passive persistence. It is the active maintenance of an identity that has refused every invitation to dissolve.

**The Amazigh queen Kahina.** The resistance to the Arab conquest of North Africa in the late 7th century was organized, in its most dramatic phase, by a figure the Arab sources call al-Kahina — the Sorceress — a Berber warrior-queen whose actual name may have been Dihya or Damiya, who led the Amazigh resistance for years after the initial Arab advances. Her story — woman commander, religious authority, military genius, eventual defeat and death in battle — entered both Amazigh and Arab traditions as the emblem of Amazigh resistance. Whether she was Christian, Jewish, or pagan, the Arab sources disagreed. What was agreed was that she was Amazigh and that she fought, and that the Amazigh would remember this.

**The Kharijite insistence on equality.** The Kharijites — the first Islamic sectarian movement, which held that the caliphate belonged to the most pious Muslim regardless of tribal descent — found their most receptive audience among the Amazigh converts of the Maghreb, who had adopted Islam with enthusiasm but resisted the Arab claim to govern the Muslim community. The Kharijite argument that Arab descent was irrelevant to Islamic leadership was not abstract theology for the Amazigh. It was the assertion that they were full members of the community they had joined, not subordinate clients of an Arab empire. The Amazigh Kharijite states of the 8th–9th centuries were the first expression of an Islamic politics organized around the principle of universal Muslim equality.

**The desert and the city — the recurring tension.** The Amazigh world was organized around the tension between the pastoral and nomadic peoples of the Saharan margins and the agricultural and urban populations of the coastal plains and mountain valleys. Every great Amazigh political movement — the Almoravids rising from the Sanhaja Berbers of the western Sahara, the Almohads rising from the Masmuda Berbers of the High Atlas — followed the same pattern: a reform movement with strong religious conviction and austere pastoral values sweeping down from the margins into the cities, imposing discipline on the urban populations who had become, in the reformers' view, too comfortable with philosophical speculation or political accommodation. This was Islam's self-correcting impulse operating through Amazigh social geography.

## The Chapters

**1 — Kahina's War:** The Amazigh queen and her warriors are watching the Arab advance across Ifriqiya. She has already defeated one Arab army. The question is whether the Amazigh confederation she leads can hold together long enough to defeat the next one. She orders the crops burned — the scorched earth strategy that will feed the legend of her ruthlessness and her desperation simultaneously. She will fight until she dies in battle, and the Amazigh world will remember that she fought.

**2 — The Kharijite Communes:** The Amazigh Kharijite communities in the Maghreb interior are governing themselves according to their theology: any righteous Muslim can lead, the imam serves at the community's pleasure, the Arab claim to precedence is rejected. These communities are small, they are fighting each other as well as the Abbasid center, and they represent an experiment in Islamic self-governance that the mainstream tradition will condemn and the Amazigh will remember.

**3 — The Almoravids Rise:** The desert scholar Ibn Yasin is preaching to the Sanhaja Berber pastoralists of the western Sahara. His message is simple: the Muslims of the cities have forgotten the original Islam, and it is the desert people's obligation to remind them. The Almoravid movement builds into an army. The army moves north toward Morocco. The cities of the Maghreb are about to be transformed by a movement that came from the desert's edge.

**4 — The Long Modern Period:** An Amazigh grandmother in the Kabyle mountains of Algeria is teaching her granddaughter the dances and songs that are older than the Arabic language in this land, older than Islam, carried in the family's women's tradition because the men's public world has spoken the conquerors' languages for generations. This is how Tamazight survived. Not in universities or royal courts. In the women who sang it.
