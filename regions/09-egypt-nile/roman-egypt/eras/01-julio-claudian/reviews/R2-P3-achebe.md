## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — PANEL-2 Review of Notes

**Focus:** Egyptian voice as dominant register

**What the notes resolve:**
The section plan correctly moves FROM Roman perspective (Section 1) TO Egyptian perspective (Section 4, temple; Section 6, landing with Apis bull return). This arc addresses my concern. The plan to show nilometer readings and priestly bull-verification at method level will give the Egyptian knowledge system its own intellectual authority.

**What remains unresolved:**
Section 2 (granary) and Section 3 (India trade) are still described primarily in terms of what Egypt produces FOR Rome. The farmer in the Fayyum is planned as someone who embodies "the system's human cost." Human cost is a judgment from outside. The farmer in the Fayyum has a life that is not defined by Rome's extraction. He has a field, a family, a relationship with the flood, a set of obligations to his village and his temple. The extraction is part of his life, but it is not the frame of his life.

**Recommendation:**
The farmer should enter the chapter as a person in his own world, not as a person in Rome's system. Let the reader meet him in the field, with the mud, with the flood, with the calendar that tells him when to plant. Then let the tax collector arrive. The extraction becomes an event in an Egyptian life, not the frame around an Egyptian life.

Similarly, the Periplus merchant should be encountered as a practitioner of his trade, not as a Roman commercial agent. His knowledge of harbors and monsoons and local rulers is his expertise, his livelihood, his world. The trade is his life. Rome's treasury is a destination for his customs payments, not the purpose of his voyage.

**Verdict:** The notes show awareness of the problem. The execution must ensure that Sections 2 and 3 enter through Egyptian and merchant experience, not through the Roman administrative lens. The shift from Rome's perspective to Egypt's should begin earlier than Section 4.
