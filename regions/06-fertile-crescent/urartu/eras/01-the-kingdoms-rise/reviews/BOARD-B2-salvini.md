# B-2 — Mirjo Salvini — Board Review of The Kingdom's Rise

**Expertise relevant to this chapter:** Urartian epigraphy, cuneiform adaptation, the royal inscriptional corpus. Author of *Corpus dei testi urartei* and *Geschichte und Kultur der Urartäer*.

**Historical accuracy:**
- Sarduri I's inscription is accurately described as Akkadian-language, using Assyrian titulature formulas. The quoted titulature is a reasonable paraphrase of the actual text (UKN I, 1). The chapter correctly notes that this is the earliest datable Urartian royal inscription.
- The characterization of the Akkadian-to-Urartian shift is broadly correct. Ishpuini did produce bilingual texts, and Menua's inscriptions are predominantly in Urartian. However, the transition was not perfectly linear. Some later kings occasionally reverted to Akkadian for specific purposes (diplomatic inscriptions, boundary markers intended for Assyrian readership). The chapter's narrative of a clean generational shift should be slightly qualified.
- The Meher Kapisi inscription (Ishpuini and Menua joint text) and its sacrifice list are correctly described. Haldi receiving seventeen bulls and thirty-four sheep is consistent with the published text. The hierarchy of the pantheon as reflected in sacrifice quantities is a valid reading.
- The inscriptional formula "by the greatness of Haldi" (Haldini ushmashinni) is correctly identified as standardized under Ishpuini and maintained by subsequent kings.

**Cultural authenticity:**
- The chapter's treatment of the inscriptions as the kingdom's self-voice is exactly right. These are not neutral records. They are the official memory, crafted, formulaic, and deliberately silent about defeats. The chapter's acknowledgment of this in the landing section ("the gaps in the record are not silence; they are the sound of a kingdom choosing what to carve") is a sophisticated observation.
- The linguistic isolation of Urartian (not Semitic, not Indo-European, unrelated to known language families) is correctly handled. The chapter avoids speculation about genetic relationships.

**What the chapter gets right:**
- The inscription as a political act, not merely a record. The chapter understands that carving in Akkadian was a choice with political meaning, and that the shift to Urartian was equally political.
- The Haldi theology as expressed through the inscriptional formula, not through speculative reconstruction of beliefs.
- The honest handling of the inscriptions' silence: no defeats, no interior life, no voices other than the king's.

**What needs correction:**
- The chapter states that the highland peoples "had no writing system" before Sarduri's inscription. This is true for the Urartian royal inscriptions but should note that hieroglyphic Luwian was in use in parts of eastern Anatolia during this period. The Urartian choice of cuneiform over hieroglyphic Luwian is itself significant and could be briefly acknowledged.
- The chapter attributes the Urartian language as "belonging to the highland and to no one else." While its genetic isolation is real, the Hurrian language (attested in Mitanni texts) is now recognized as related to Urartian. The chapter should acknowledge the Hurro-Urartian language family, even briefly, to avoid implying total linguistic isolation.

**Verdict:** APPROVED-WITH-CONDITIONS. Two corrections needed: the hieroglyphic Luwian context and the Hurro-Urartian relationship. Both are brief additions that strengthen rather than alter the chapter's argument.
