# R1-P4 — Wade Davis
## Panel Round 1 — Opening Review

**Era:** Teke / The Kongo-Teke Interface
**Date:** 2026-04-16

---

The broker examination is showing me a knowledge system. I want to name that carefully.

When the broker takes the ingot and strikes it against his ring and listens, he is performing what the chapter calls — correctly — "a habit of sight, the numbers running quietly underneath everything else." This is not instinct. This is trained, inherited, transmitted knowledge: how to read copper quality by sound and weight and surface color, how to calibrate that quality against the Katanga source and the Pool destination, how to simultaneously assess a trading partner's commercial sophistication from the way he hands over the goods. This is a complete epistemology of commercial judgment built without instruments, without written records, without any of the formal institutions that Western commercial theory would consider necessary for a market to function.

The chapter honors this by showing it operating, not by labeling it. That is the correct move.

The question I would push on:

The Teke knowledge system has several layers that the chapter should eventually reach. The first is the commercial layer — the broker's art — which is beautifully established here. The second is the Makoko's ritual authority — the onkani, the spiritual jurisdiction over the land that gives the Makoko political leverage without armies. The third is the environmental knowledge of the plateau itself: the Teke understanding of the road systems, the river crossing points, the seasonal patterns of the forest traders' movements, the geographic knowledge that made the broker position possible. The chapter should show at least two of these three layers operating at method level — not "the Teke had sophisticated knowledge of their territory" but what that knowledge looked like when it was being used.

I am also watching for the chapter's treatment of the onkani concept. The Makoko's authority rested on being the land's spiritual representative, not its owner. This is a different ontological category from Western property rights or political sovereignty, and the chapter must render it from inside its own logic. A Teke chief granting or denying access to the plateau roads was not performing a political act in the way a Kongo governor would understand politics. He was enforcing the land's own requirements. The chapter needs to make this felt, not explained.

One concern I want to name: the Atlantic slave trade section of this era will involve the Teke as participants in moving enslaved people to the Pool and thence to the coast. I am not asking the chapter to judge this — the No Verdicts principle is clear and I support it. I am asking the chapter to render it through the broker's calculus in the same voice it uses for copper and iron, without flinching and without editorializing. The worst failure here would be a chapter that treats commercial goods with full intellectual seriousness and then softens or blurs when the traded commodity is human. The broker's logic does not make that distinction. The chapter must hold the same voice throughout.

The opening has earned the chapter. Proceed with confidence.
