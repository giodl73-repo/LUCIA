## P-2 — Ibn Khaldun — Review of The Age of Equals (Opening)

**Overall:** This opening has what most narrative history lacks: a mechanism. The system of reciprocal gift-exchange and diplomatic marriage is not background. It is the argument. I am satisfied.

**What works:**
- The structural logic is embedded in the narrative, not appended to it. The reader understands the chariot-based equilibrium not because it is explained but because it is *shown* in operation: the gifts calibrated, the gold counted, the shortfalls recorded. This is how analytical writing should work.
- The connection between chariot forces and diplomatic standing is the right causal spine. The opening correctly identifies that the "brotherhood" system rests on military parity, not sentiment. This is the asabiyyah of a multi-state system: solidarity maintained by mutual need, dissolved when the balance shifts.
- "The equality was the fiction that made the system work" is the opening's thesis, and it arrives at the right moment, after the reader has seen the fiction in operation.

**What doesn't:**
- The opening identifies the chariot as the foundation of the system but does not yet show its fragility. Every equilibrium has a point of failure. For the Mitanni system, the fragility lies in the fact that the chariot advantage was *diffusing*: as all five powers mastered the technology, the edge that any single power held narrowed. The opening should at least plant the seed that a technology-based equilibrium is inherently temporary.
- The economic dimension is thin. What sustained the chariot system materially? Horses, copper, tin, wood, leather, trained labor. The opening mentions these in passing but does not show the supply chains that connected them. A brotherhood that depends on bronze depends on tin, and tin came from very far away.

**The question I'd push on:**
What is the terminal condition of this system? Every equilibrium fails. The opening should make the reader curious about *how* this one will fail, not just marvel at how it works. The chariot system, the marriage diplomacy, the gift exchange all require sustained resources and sustained will. Where does either run out?
