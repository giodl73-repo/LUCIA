## Domain Accuracy Assessment

**On the *correctio* program and its sources:**

The chapter correctly identifies the *Admonitio Generalis* of 789 as the
central reform document. The quotation from the text is accurate in substance.
The chapter accurately describes the *missi dominici* system: pairs of a
layman and a clergyman, authorized by royal seal, conducting circuit inspections.
The description of what the missi found — corrupt judges, illiterate clergy,
monasteries-as-family-estates — is grounded in Theodulf's *Contra Iudices*
and in the capitulary literature.

**One factual clarification:** The chapter states that the palace school was
"quiet at this hour" (at night, when Alcuin is working). This is accurate to
Einhard's description of Charles working late. However, the chapter should
note, somewhere in the notes if not the chapter, that the palace school was
not a single fixed institution but a more fluid gathering that operated in
the palace's various spaces. The "palace school" as a concept is established
in the sources, but its physical arrangement was not the fixed institution
the modern image suggests. The chapter's rendering (scholars meeting in
evenings in the palace halls) is historically accurate; no revision needed,
but the notes should flag the institution's fluidity.

**On Alcuin's arrival date:**

The chapter states Alcuin arrived "around 782." Modern scholarship (Bullough)
places Alcuin's move to the Frankish court as permanent around 782–786, with
earlier visits possible. "Around 782" is defensible and accurate.

**On Theodulf's circuit:**

*Contra Iudices* is correctly identified as Theodulf's poem about his
experience as a missus. The chapter's characterization of what he found is
accurate. The poem's tone is correctly described as having "precision and some
despair." One note: the exact dating of his circuit in southern Gaul is not
certain; scholarship places it in the 790s, possibly around 798. The chapter
does not date the circuit, which is the correct choice.

**On the Carolingian minuscule:**

The chapter accurately describes the minuscule's functional properties (legible,
portable, standardized) and correctly avoids overclaiming Alcuin's sole
authorship. The script's development was gradual and collaborative. The
association with Tours under Alcuin is well established. The claim that "most
of what we have of classical Latin literature survives in Carolingian copies"
is accurate, if slightly simplified: many classical texts survive in exactly
one Carolingian manuscript, which is the point the chapter is making.

**On the Verden massacre:**

The chapter states "4,500 Saxon prisoners." The Royal Frankish Annals give
a number (the *Annales Regni Francorum* state 4,500); the revised version of
the Annals omits the passage. Modern scholarship treats the number as possibly
accurate but potentially rounded or rhetorical. The chapter should note this
uncertainty; the figure is a reasonable scholarly consensus but not certain.

Recommended addition to the chapter (a brief hedge in the body): "The Royal
Frankish Annals record 4,500 Saxon prisoners executed — a number that later
revisions to the official record quietly removed."

**On Christmas 800:**

The chapter correctly characterizes the coronation as a surprise to Charles
(Einhard's testimony) without endorsing the claim uncritically ("Einhard sets
this down without comment, which is itself a comment"). This is exactly the
right treatment of a complex primary source. Well done.

**On Einhard:**

Correctly described as a palace school alumnus whose biography of Charles
should be read as argument-from-affection. The biographical details (Charles's
height, his voice, his preference for educated company) are from the *Vita
Karoli Magni* and are accurately rendered.

**On the script that became modern lowercase:**

"The lowercase letters you are reading now are the descendants of the script
that Alcuin corrected into existence" is broadly accurate, with the important
caveat that the connection runs through Renaissance humanists who revived
Carolingian scripts. The chapter correctly notes this path. The claim is
defensible and is a powerful way to materialize the chapter's stakes.

---

## Verdict

**Historically accurate.** The chapter uses its primary sources correctly, handles
uncertainty appropriately (the coronation, Einhard's partiality), and renders
the *correctio* program with the sophistication the scholarship supports.

**One required addition to the chapter:**
Add brief uncertainty hedge on the Verden death toll:
"The Royal Frankish Annals record 4,500 Saxon prisoners executed; later revisions
to the official record omitted the passage."

**One note for figures.md:**
The characterization of Einhard as having "an exceptional skill at mechanical
arts" is accurate — he designed the Saint-Servatius reliquary and other
metalwork. Add a mention of this in figures.md if space permits; it deepens
the portrait of what the palace school produced.

**Cultural authenticity:** The chapter represents the Carolingian reform
program as it understood itself, not as modern historians have sometimes
framed it (as proto-European nationalism, or as the precursor to Christendom's
authoritarianism). This is the right choice.
