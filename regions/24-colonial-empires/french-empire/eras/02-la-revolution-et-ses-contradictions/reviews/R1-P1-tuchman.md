# Panel Review — R1 / P-1 / Barbara Tuchman
## French Empire Era 2: La Révolution et Ses Contradictions
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

The opening is doing something architecturally interesting: it is building toward an event we know is coming (Bois Caïman, August 1791) while simultaneously constructing the cognitive world — Moreau de Saint-Méry's world — that the event will shatter. That is the right structure. The scene is specific: a man, a library, a year, a project. The pull is genuine. The close lands with the precision of a legal judgment.

**What works:**

The tension between the enumeration of the system (128 categories, their names in French, their fractions) and the statement that "something no category could hold" was approaching — this is the chapter's engine and it's visible from the first pages. The pacing is controlled. The material is dense but not crowded. "The ledgers were very good at absorption" is the best sentence in the piece — it earns its weight by waiting until after the system has been rendered fully before delivering the verdict.

The final three lines are exceptional. The compression there — "The Declaration of the Rights of Man had not failed. It had, in fact, worked exactly as its words required. / Someone had read it." — is structurally brilliant and earns the reader's desire to continue without a single trick.

**What I'd push on:**

The middle passages — paragraphs 5 and 6 — carry a great deal of analytical freight (Raynal, Montesquieu, the contrast between Enlightenment universalism and colonial practice) and do it through summary rather than scene. The argument is sound, but it runs at a slightly higher altitude than the opening scene and the closing lines. If the chapter can bring Toussaint's encounter with Raynal into a scene even briefly — a specific moment of reading, a specific passage underlined — that would anchor the analytical weight in the same sensory register as the rest.

The fire at Le Cap-Français is mentioned but not felt. "The fires could be seen from the harbor at Le Cap-Français" is true and efficient, but a single sensory detail — the smell of burning cane, the color of the sky — would ground what is otherwise the opening's only abstracted moment.

**The question I'd push on:**

The chapter ahead will need to decide: who is narrating the whole? This opening is written from inside the French administrative vantage, which is right for a French Empire chapter — but for 5,000 words, that vantage needs to remain coherent even as Toussaint and Leclerc and Napoleon enter. The chapter cannot slip into omniscience. The challenge will be to render Napoleon's restoration of slavery (1802) and the 60,000 soldiers sent to suppress Haiti from inside the logic that generated them, not from the outside moral position that condemns them. That is the chapter's hardest structural task.
