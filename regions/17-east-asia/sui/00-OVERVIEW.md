# Sui — The Canal and the Code

We are the Sui, the short dynasty that reconnected north and south after four centuries of division and paid for it with our existence. We reunified China in 589 CE, ending the era of the Northern and Southern Dynasties that had fractured the realm since the Han's fall. We built the Grand Canal — the engineering project that connected the Yellow River to the Yangtze, allowing the agricultural wealth of the south to feed and supply the military power of the north. We compiled the legal code that the Tang would inherit and perfect. We rebuilt the examination system. We launched three catastrophically costly invasions of Goguryeo in Korea that bled the state white and triggered the rebellion that destroyed us.

We lasted thirty-seven years. The history books call us the dynasty that prepared everything for the Tang. This is accurate and also unjust: we paid the cost of unification, of the Grand Canal's construction, of the legal foundation, and the Tang received the benefit. The Sui emperor Yang, our second and last emperor, was not simply a tyrant — he was a ruler who attempted too much too fast. The Goguryeo wars were strategic mistakes of catastrophic scale. The canal was a genuine achievement. Both are true.

## What Defined Them

**Reunification after four centuries.** The Northern and Southern Dynasties period (220–589 CE) had divided China into competing kingdoms, Buddhist and Daoist courts, northern steppe-influenced regimes, and southern Chinese aristocratic polities. The Sui reunification was not merely political; it was the re-establishment of the administrative and ideological premise that China was a single realm. Yang Jian (Emperor Wen Di), who came to power as a regent for a child emperor he then deposed, reunified the south through a military campaign and then set about creating the institutional framework for a unified empire.

**The Grand Canal.** The construction of the Grand Canal was one of the greatest engineering projects of the pre-industrial world. The canal system, connecting the Hai River in the north to the Qiantang River in the south via the Yellow and Huai rivers, required the conscript labor of millions of workers, large numbers of whom died in the construction. The canal made it possible for the rice surplus of the Yangtze delta to provision the armies and court of the north; it was the material infrastructure that made the Tang dynasty's wealth possible. It is still in use.

**The legal code.** The Sui legal code — revised and elaborated as the Tang Code — was the foundation of Chinese legal administration for centuries and influenced the legal codes of Korea, Japan, and Vietnam. It systematized the principles of governance across all aspects of administrative life: official conduct, taxation, family law, criminal punishment. The Tang Code that descended from it is the best-preserved early Chinese legal document and the foundation of our understanding of how the Tang state actually governed.

**The Goguryeo catastrophe.** Emperor Yang Di's three invasions of the Korean kingdom of Goguryeo (612, 613, 614) were among the most costly military failures in Chinese history. The 612 expedition alone reportedly mobilized over a million soldiers. The Sui armies were cut off, destroyed, and defeated; the generals who fled were executed; the treasury was exhausted; the peasants were ruined by the conscription. The Goguryeo king's decisive defense — including a devastating ambush at the Salsu River — destroyed the Sui army. The rebellions that followed came from men who had nothing left to lose.

**The compressed dynasty.** The Sui pattern — rapid unification, ambitious projects, overextension, rebellion, collapse — became the model against which the Tang deliberately positioned itself. The Tang learned from the Sui's mistakes: instead of three Goguryeo invasions, one strategic campaign. Instead of forcing canal construction with corvée labor, phase the projects. The Sui was the experiment from whose failure the Tang derived the rules for success.

## The Chapters

**1 — Wen Di:** He has just declared the Sui dynasty. He is fifty years old. He has spent his career as a Northern Zhou official. Now he governs China. He writes the legal code himself, in consultation with scholars. He reviews every recommendation personally. He is building something that will outlast him — but not in the way he imagines.

**2 — The Grand Canal:** The work gangs are in the water. The canal is being dug by conscripts from across the empire. Men die of exhaustion and disease. The supervisor reads the quota: this section must be finished by spring. The work continues through the night.

**3 — Goguryeo:** The Sui army stands at the Liao River. They have crossed it before. They will cross it again. Across the river is the Goguryeo fortress of Liaodong, which has not fallen in two campaigns. The emperor orders the crossing. The Goguryeo defenders are ready.

**4 — The Collapse:** The rebels in the north have cut off the supply roads. Emperor Yang Di is in Yangzhou, in the south, surrounded by his palace court, refusing to acknowledge that the empire is falling. His generals strangle him in his bath. The Tang is already beginning.
