## Domain Assessment

### Historical Accuracy

**The Parliament of 1295 and the *quod omnes tangit* formula:**
The chapter's treatment is accurate. The formula appears in the 1295 summons and was derived from Justinian's Code via Hostiensis and other canonists. The chapter correctly attributes it to the chancery context rather than to Edward's personal invention. The characterization of the 1295 Parliament as constitutionally generative because of the unified three-estate consent is supported by the secondary literature (Maddicott, pp. 153–165; Richardson and Sayles take a more skeptical view of the 1295 Parliament's significance, which the chapter might acknowledge).

**The statutes:**
The chapter accurately characterizes the Statute of Westminster I (1275) as codifying rather than replacing common law customs. The *quo warranto* proceedings are correctly described: the campaign ran from 1278 and forced lords to produce charters for their jurisdictional claims, with those who claimed prescription from time immemorial told that this was insufficient — "the Devil himself had been in continuous possession since time immemorial." The chapter does not include this particular legal formulation, which is vivid and within the culture's own register. Consider adding.

**Robert Burnell:**
The characterization is accurate. Burnell's illegitimate children and rejected archiepiscopal candidacies are documented. His central role in drafting the major statutes is the consensus of the secondary literature. The claim that he died "three years before" 1295 is correct (d. 1292).

**De Montfort and Evesham:**
The military description is accurate. The battle of Evesham (4 August 1265) was a trap — de Montfort caught in the Avon's loop. The mutilation of his body is documented by the chroniclers (notably the Flores Historiarum). The chapter's treatment is restrained and historically grounded.

### Factual Queries

**1.** The chapter names Henry de Cobham as a knight elected for Kent in 1306. Parliamentary returns for this period are fragmentary. "Henry de Cobham" is a plausible name for a Kentish knight of this period (the Cobham family held lands in Kent). However, the chapter identifies him as "composite, drawn from the records of county elections and parliamentary returns" in figures.md. This should be maintained — the figures.md disclosure is adequate and the method is sound.

**2.** The chapter states that the Statute of Rhuddlan (1284) "did not acknowledge the Laws of Hywel Dda." This is broadly accurate, though the statute's relationship to Welsh customary law was more complex than complete replacement — certain Welsh practices in property law survived in the new county courts for decades. The chapter's treatment is within acceptable compression for narrative purpose.

**3.** The Gaveston execution: "captured and executed by the earl of Lancaster in 1312 without trial." Technically accurate — Gaveston was captured at Scarborough, taken to Warwick Castle by the earls, and executed on Blacklow Hill. The chapter might specify that it was the earl of Warwick, not Lancaster, who physically ordered the execution, though Lancaster was the senior figure in the coalition. Minor.

### Cultural Authenticity

The chapter's reading of Edward I's relationship to the law — using it instrumentally while being subject to it — is the correct scholarly consensus (Prestwich's own characterization in the 1988 biography). The irony that the legal machinery Edward built for royal purposes became the machinery for constraining his successor is historically accurate and is the chapter's best analytical insight.

The treatment of Wales and the Laws of Hywel Dda is appropriate in scope. The chapter does not pretend the Welsh legal tradition was less sophisticated than English law — it describes two legal systems meeting under unequal power, which is the historical situation.

---

## Recommended Revisions

1. **The Gaveston execution:** Specify "the earls of Lancaster and Warwick" rather than "the earl of Lancaster" alone.
2. **The *quo warranto* formulation:** Consider adding the "Devil himself" phrase as a vivid instance of the royal legal culture's own dark humor.
3. The Declaration of Arbroath revision (applied in CLEAN) is correct. Approved.

## Board Verdict

**Historical accuracy:** High. Minor corrections noted.
**Cultural representation:** Strong. The Plantagenet legal world is represented from within its own categories.
**Source use:** Appropriate. The secondary literature is correctly reflected.
