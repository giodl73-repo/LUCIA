# BOARD Review — Historical Scholarship
## Mexico Era 4 — El Porfiriato y La Revolución

**Reviewer profile:** Specialist in Mexican Revolution historiography; expertise in the Porfiriato's political economy, the agrarian movements, and the constitutional period 1910–1920. Draws on Knight (1986), Womack (1969), Katz (1998), and Warman (1980).

---

## Historical Accuracy Assessment

**Plan de Ayala:** Correctly characterized. The Plan was indeed issued at Ayoxustla (not always correctly identified in popular accounts) in November 1911, after Zapata's break with Madero over the failure to address the agrarian question. The twenty-seven signatories count is accurate per the documentary record. The three-part demand (one-third expropriation with compensation, full expropriation for resisters, Zapata as commander) is correctly summarized.

**Anenecuilco:** The 1607 documents are real. Womack's definitive study opens with this archive; the colonial-era land grants and maps are documented in the AGN. Zapata's election as council president at age twenty-nine (1909) is accurate. His horse-training reputation is documented in local Morelos sources and in Womack.

**Ley de Baldíos (1883):** The characterization is correct. The one-third surveying company compensation is accurate. The legal mechanism — classifying communal tenure as vacant because it lacked individual cadastral registration — is the standard scholarly account (Warman, Gilly). The chapter correctly connects the railroad's arrival in Morelos (1881) to hacienda expansion through the sugar commodity chain. This is Knight's argument in Volume 1; the chapter faithfully represents it.

**Celaya (April and June 1915):** The trench warfare, barbed wire, and machine gun tactics are accurately described. Obregón did study the European Western Front reports. The "three charges" is a slight compression (there were multiple engagements across April and June at both Celaya and Trinidad/León), but accurate in effect. The loss of Villa's military dominance is correctly characterized as the result.

**Article 27 radicalization:** Accurately described. Carranza's draft was more moderate; the radical delegates from Sonora, Tabasco, and Yucatán (notably Francisco Múgica and Heriberto Jara) inserted the more sweeping provisions. The chapter correctly notes that Carranza "accepted the final text because he needed to" — this is the scholarly consensus (Knight, vol. 2).

**Chinameca (April 10, 1919):** Accurate. The stratagem (Guajardo's fake defection, execution of Zapatista prisoners as proof), the date, the location. The chapter accurately notes that Zapata was warned. Womack documents the warnings.

**One factual refinement recommended:**
The chapter states Carranza "had him arrested" regarding Madero; strictly, Huerta arrested Madero — Carranza was not present at the coup. The chapter actually says this correctly ("generals loyal to the old Porfirista order rose against Madero...General Victoriano Huerta switched sides"), so no correction needed there.

The figure of "one to two million dead" for the revolution's casualties is within the range of current scholarly estimates (the range in recent historiography is approximately 1 to 2 million, with population impact from emigration to the United States also significant). The hedging ("between one and two million...in a population of fifteen million") is appropriately honest.

**Minor factual note:** The chapter describes Díaz as dying "having outlived the regime he built but not the era he named." Díaz died July 2, 1915 in Paris. The revolution was ongoing. The Porfiriato as an era name applied retrospectively — Díaz did not know that is what his period would be called. The phrase is poetic rather than strictly historical, but it does not misrepresent the facts.

**María de la O:** She existed and gave testimony; her role as coronela in the Zapatista forces is documented, though the documentary record is thinner than for the major figures. The chapter's cautious treatment in figures.md — naming her role without over-detailing a thin record — is the right approach.

## Cultural Authenticity Assessment

The chapter successfully distinguishes between the revolutionary movement's own politics (ejido restoration, communal land tenure as a Nahua social technology) and the nationalist-muralist appropriation of Zapata's image that came later. The corrido tradition is correctly identified as a distinct form of memory from the murals.

The Nahua specificity of Anenecuilco is appropriate and necessary — this is not a generic "peasant" revolution; it is a specific community with specific documents defending specific land rights under a specific legal system.

**Board verdict: PASS — no corrections required; one minor amplification noted but not mandatory**
