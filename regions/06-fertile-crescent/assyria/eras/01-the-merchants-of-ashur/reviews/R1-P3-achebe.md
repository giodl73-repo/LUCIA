## P-3 — Chinua Achebe — Review of The Merchants of Ashur (Opening)

**Overall:** The voice is nearly right. The opening letter places us inside the merchant's world without apology, and the culture's commercial logic is presented as self-evident. But there are moments where the author's hand is visible, and one paragraph steps outside the world entirely.

**What works:**
- The opening sentence is a letter, and the letter is the world. No one is explaining Ashur to us. No one is contextualizing the trade for a modern reader. The tin, the cloth, the debts, the donkey caravan, the karum assembly: these are presented as the ordinary furniture of a life, not as artifacts to be admired. This is how you present from within.
- Lamassatum's voice in the final paragraph is owned. She argues about debts, manages agents, hears her daughter at the loom. She is not a specimen of "women's roles in ancient Near Eastern commerce." She is a person managing a business and a household, and the text lets her be that.
- The letters' content in the fourth paragraph is rendered without commentary. The wives' complaints, the fathers' instructions, the legal disputes: these are not interpreted. They are presented. The reader forms their own understanding.

**What doesn't:**
- The fifth paragraph breaks the contract. "This was Ashur before the empire. Before the deportations, before the flayed skins of enemies displayed on city walls, before the lion hunts..." This is the author speaking TO the reader about the larger Assyrian story. It is not the merchant's voice. It is not the karum's voice. It is a historian who knows the ending, positioning the beginning as a contrast. The merchants of Ashur did not define themselves against a future they could not imagine. They defined themselves by their ledgers, their donkeys, their contracts. The paragraph must be rewritten or removed.
- "In the reckoning foreigners would later impose on his world" is a subtle violation. The phrase is clever, but it belongs to the author's critical framework, not to the merchant's world. The merchant did not think in terms of "foreign reckonings." He thought in eponym years. State the eponym year, parenthesize the common-era date, and move on.

**The question I'd push on:**
Whose story is the fifth paragraph telling? It is not the merchants'. It is the author's, framing this era as the "before" to a more famous "after." If the merchants' world is rich enough to stand on its own terms, it does not need the later empire to make it interesting. If it is not, that is a problem the chapter must solve by making the commercial world more vivid, not by borrowing dramatic weight from the future.
