# BOARD-2 — John Thornton

**Domain:** African history, the Atlantic slave trade, African military and political culture in the diaspora, Palmares specifically
**Key work:** *Africa and Africans in the Making of the Atlantic World* (1992/1998); *A Cultural History of the Atlantic World* (2012)
**Chapter reviewed:** Brazil, Era 2 — A Colônia — Açúcar, Ouro, e Escravos

---

## Factual Accuracy Assessment

**African origins and Palmares:** The chapter correctly identifies the primary population of Palmares as coming from Angola, the Congo, Dahomey, and the Mina coast. This reflects the actual demographics of the Atlantic trade to northeastern Brazil in the seventeenth century. The chapter's use of "Bantu-derived title structures" for Palmares's governance is consistent with the scholarly consensus.

**The gangazumba title:** My research supports the interpretation of Ganga Zumba as a Bantu royal title. The chapter's note that "The Portuguese transliteration — Ganga Zumba — stripped the meaning from the words while preserving their sound" is accurate and well-put.

**African military organization in Palmares:** The chapter does not make explicit claims about the military structure of Palmares's resistance, which is wise — the evidence is thin and contested. What it does claim is that Palmares survived because it "knew the land and the Portuguese did not," which is consistent with my own analysis of the quilombo's tactical advantage.

**Capoeira:** The chapter's treatment of capoeira as "epistemological camouflage" — looking like dancing to the Portuguese while functioning as combat training — is historically defensible. The exact origins of capoeira are debated (some scholars trace it to Angola, others argue it developed in Brazil), but the camouflage function in the colonial context is well-documented.

**The Mina coast composite woman:** The specific details — Xangô, the batá rhythms, the red and white colors — are accurate for the Nagô/Yoruba tradition. The chapter correctly notes that Xangô is "the orixá of thunder and justice and the Yoruba royal lineage." All accurate.

## Cultural Representation Assessment

The chapter's central claim — that the axé survived the Middle Passage because it was embodied rather than material — is historically sophisticated. This is the argument that my own work on African cultural continuity in the diaspora has made (though in different terms): the cultural forms that survived were those held in bodies, performances, and initiatory chains rather than those requiring physical objects or texts. The chapter arrives at this claim from inside the tradition, not as an external anthropological observation, which is the right approach.

The Ganga Zumba section is the chapter's most impressive historical achievement. The argument that he was "right about different things" than Zumbi — that his calculation about accommodation was not cowardice but a reading of what Palmares was for — is historically defensible and morally serious. Most popular accounts of Palmares present Ganga Zumba as a traitor. This chapter does not, and it is right not to.

## Corrections Required

None. The chapter is historically accurate within the range of scholarly debate and handles the genuine uncertainties honestly.

One observation that the author may wish to consider but is not a required correction: the chapter does not mention the role of indigenous Tupi and Tapuia peoples in Palmares's survival beyond "Tupi agricultural knowledge." The relationship between Palmares and the indigenous peoples of the Pernambuco interior was more complex than this — there were alliances, tensions, and sometimes the incorporation of indigenous people into the quilombo. This is a genuine gap in the chapter's account, but it is not an error. It is a choice about what to include at 5,000 words. The chapter's focus on the African transmission is its thesis, and including the full indigenous dimension would require a longer chapter. The gap should be acknowledged in the notes but does not require revision in the chapter.

## Verdict

**Approved for final.** No corrections required. One observation (indigenous dimension) noted for the author's awareness. Historically accurate, culturally sophisticated, and representative of the best current scholarship on Palmares and the Atlantic African diaspora.
